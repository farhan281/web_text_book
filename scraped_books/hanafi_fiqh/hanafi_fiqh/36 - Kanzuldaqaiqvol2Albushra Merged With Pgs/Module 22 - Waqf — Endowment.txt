Definition and Rulings of Waqf
Definition of waqf with Hanafi positions and comparison with other schools

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of waqf, distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the two companions regarding its binding nature, and explain the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
Waqf in the Hanafi school is defined as detaining the asset while retaining ownership — this makes it non-binding according to Abu Hanifa.

The chapter on waqf (endowment) opens with a definition that is deceptively simple: 'detaining the asset under the ownership of the waqif and giving charity of the usufruct.' But this definition hides a fundamental disagreement within the Hanafi school. Abu Hanifa's view that ownership is retained makes waqf revocable, while his two companions argue that ownership ceases, making it binding. This lesson unpacks the definition, the proofs, and the practical implications of this difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition components
Break down the definition: 'habs al-ayn' (detaining the asset), 'ala milk al-waqif' (under the waqif's ownership), and 'al-tasadduq bil-manfa'a' (giving charity of usufruct). Recognize that this is Abu Hanifa's definition.
The definition explicitly includes retention of ownership, which is key to the dispute.
2
Locate the dalil
The hadith of 'Umar: 'Detain its origin and give charity with its fruit.' Note that Abu Hanifa interprets 'detain' as retaining ownership, while the companions interpret it as detaining from ownership (i.e., as waqf).
The same hadith is used by both sides; the difference lies in interpretation of 'habs'.
3
Map the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa: waqf is valid but not binding (lazim) — like a loan; the waqif can revoke. Abu Yusuf: valid and binding; ownership ceases by mere utterance. Muhammad: binding only after handing over to a trustee.
The most correct view (al-ashah) according to the text is that it is valid but not binding, but later practice favored binding.
4
Compare with other schools
Shafi'i and Ahmad: ownership transfers to the beneficiary (if they can own) or to Allah. This is a different conceptualization of waqf.
The Hanafi definition leads to different rulings on sale, inheritance, and revocation.
5
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Abu Hanifa's view, the waqif can sell the property later; under the companions' view, it is irrevocable and cannot be sold or inherited.
Judicial rulings can make a non-binding waqf binding if a qadi decides.

Commentary
This passage introduces the book of waqf (endowment) from Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It begins with the definition: 'detaining the asset under the ownership of the waqif (endower) and giving charity of the usufruct.' This is Abu Hanifa's definition and the basis for the ruling that waqf is not binding (lazim) because the usufruct is non-existent. The commentary then discusses the linguistic origin, the occasion for placing the chapter after partnership (both involve benefit, but partnership for this world, waqf for the next). Then the conditions, pillars, and subject are listed: the cause (desire for reward), the pillar (explicit phrases: waqaftu, habastu, sabbaltu; metaphorical: tasaddaqtu, harmatu, abadtu), the subject (valuable property), and conditions (no interdiction, immediate, owned, known, no option condition, not time-limited). The passage then details the intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that waqf is not valid in the sense of being binding—the endower retains ownership and can revoke it. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that it is valid and ownership ceases (to Allah). Imam Shafi'i and Ahmad hold that ownership transfers to the beneficiary. There is further difference within the Hanafi school: Abu Yusuf says ownership ceases by mere utterance, while Muhammad says it requires handing over to a trustee. The basis is the hadith of 'Umar, where the Prophet instructed him to 'detain the origin and give charity with its fruit,' which Abu Hanifa interprets as detaining in his own ownership, while the companions interpret as detaining in Allah's ownership. The passage concludes with the text of the hadith.

Key Terms
وقف (waqf): Detaining an asset in the ownership of the endower (or of Allah) and giving away its usufruct for charitable purposes.
حبس العين (habs al-'ayn): Detaining the asset itself, preventing its sale, gift, or inheritance.
منفعة (manfa'a): Usufruct or benefit derived from the asset, such as rent or yield.

Definition of Waqf and Dispute over Ownership
Defining waqf and presenting ownership disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definitions of waqf, compare the ownership positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and explain the practical consequences of the dispute.

Core Idea
Who owns the waqf asset? The answer changes whether you can revoke it.

Waqf is a powerful institution for perpetual charity, but its legal nature is hotly debated within the Hanafi school and across madhahib. In this lesson, we unpack the definition of waqf from the classical Hanafi texts and examine the fundamental dispute over ownership — a disagreement that has real consequences for whether an endowment can be undone.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi definition
The text defines waqf as 'detaining the asset under the ownership of the endower and giving the benefit in charity.' This is the view of Abu Hanifa. Note that the definition already embeds the ownership position.
2
Compare with the Sahibayn's view
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say waqf is detention under the ownership of Allah. This changes the ruling from revocable to irrevocable.
3
Examine the hadith proof
The Prophet said to 'Umar: 'Detain its origin and give its fruit in charity.' Assess how each side interprets the imperative 'ihbis' (detain).
Abu Hanifa reads 'ihbis' as retaining ownership; the Sahibayn read it as detaining for Allah.
4
Trace the intra-Hanafi sub-dispute
Abu Yusuf says ownership ceases upon declaration; Muhammad says only after delivery to a custodian. Despite this, both agree on the validity of waqf.
5
Identify the practical outcome (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Abu Hanifa's view, the endower can revoke, sell, inherit the waqf; under the Sahibayn's view, the waqf is binding forever and cannot be revoked.
The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's view, so in practice waqf is irrevocable.

Commentary
This passage is an inter-madhhab and intra-Hanafi dispute unpacking the definition of waqf. The author opens with the standard Hanafi definition of waqf as 'detaining the asset under the ownership of the endower and giving the benefit in charity'. He then clarifies that this definition reflects the position of Abu Hanifa, supported by the hadith where the Prophet (pbuh) instructs 'Umar to 'detain the origin and give the fruit in charity'. According to Abu Hanifa, the endower retains ownership, so the waqf is revocable like a loan. In contrast, the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that waqf transfers ownership to Allah, making it irrevocable. The Shafi'i and Hanbali schools transfer ownership to the beneficiary. The passage also notes an intra-Hanafi sub-dispute: Abu Yusuf rules that ownership ceases upon mere declaration, while Muhammad requires delivery to a custodian. The evidence from the hadith is presented to support the Sahibayn's view on validity, but Abu Hanifa contends that the hadith does not prove irrevocability; rather, 'Umar himself later considered selling it after the Prophet's death. The author concludes that the fatwa is on the view of the Sahibayn (that waqf is valid and binding).

Key Terms
الوقف (al-waqf): Detaining an asset under the ownership of the endower (Abu Hanifa) or under the ownership of Allah (Sahibayn) and giving the benefit in charity.
حبس العين (habs al-'ayn): Restricting the asset itself from being transferred; it remains in the ownership of the endower (according to Abu Hanifa) or is dedicated to Allah (according to the Sahibayn).
تصدق بالمنفعة (tasaddaqa bil-manfa'ah): Giving the usufruct or yield (e.g., rent, produce) in charity to the beneficiaries.

When Does Waqf Become Binding? Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explains the intra-Hanafi dispute on removal of ownership in waqf and conditions for its completion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on when ownership is removed in waqf and explain why the fatwa relies on judicial decree.

Core Idea
Waqf ownership is removed only by a judge's decree; mere utterance does not bind the founder.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqaiq presents the classical Hanafi debate on the moment a waqf becomes binding. The matn lays down the rule that ownership is removed by judicial decree, not to an owner. The commentary unpacks the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad, each with distinct proofs. Understanding this ikhtilaf is crucial for correctly applying waqf law in practice.

Guided Path
1
State the basic ruling of the matn
Identify the core rule: ownership is removed by judicial decree (qada'), not to an owner. The waqf is not completed until possession (qabd), segregation (farz), and a perpetual destination (ta'bid) are present.
The matn uses the phrase 'يزول بالقضاء لا إلى مالك'.
2
Identify the three Hanafi positions on removal of ownership
Abu Hanifa held that ownership is not removed until a judge decrees; Abu Yusuf said it is removed immediately upon utterance (like itq); Muhammad said it is removed only upon delivery to the trustee (like zakat).
Note that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad agree that judicial decree is needed for bindingness (fatwa on their view), but differ on the moment of removal.
3
Understand the proofs for each position
Abu Yusuf analogizes to emancipation (itq); Muhammad analogizes to zakat where ownership transfers through delivery; Abu Hanifa relies on the hadith 'la habsa an fara'id Allah' and the incident of Ibn Umar.
The hadith of Ibn Umar is cited as evidence that waqf is not automatically binding.
4
Note the conditions for completion according to Muhammad
Muhammad requires: (1) appointment of a trustee, (2) delivery to the trustee, (3) segregation from the founder's property, (4) indication of perpetuity. Additionally, the founder must be free, sane, and adult.
The condition of segregation (farz) leads to the issue of musha' (undivided share).
5
Apply the tarjih – which position is mufta bihi
The text states 'والفتوى على قولهما' – the fatwa is on the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad that judicial decree is necessary for bindingness. For the removal issue, later Hanafi practice often adopts Abu Yusuf's view of immediate removal, but bindingness requires a judge.
This means that without a judge's decree, the founder can still revoke or sell the property.

Commentary
This passage is an intra-Hanafi dispute on when the ownership of the founder is removed from the waqf property and the conditions for its completion. The matn states that waqf is removed by judicial decree, not to a specific owner, and it is not completed until three conditions are met: possession by the trustee (qabd), segregation from the founder's property (farz), and stipulation of a perpetual destination (ta'bid). The commentary then presents the ikhtilaf. Abu Yusuf holds that ownership is removed immediately upon uttering the waqf, as it is akin to freeing a slave (itq). Muhammad insists that the waqf is not effective until the property is handed over to the trustee, analogizing it to zakat which only becomes effective upon delivery to the poor. Abu Hanifa's position is inferred from the hadith 'la habsa 'an fara'id Allah' – that the property should not be withheld from the heirs, implying that waqf is not binding unless adjudicated. The commentary adds that according to Muhammad, there are four conditions: appointment of a trustee, delivery, segregation, and indication of perpetuity, plus the founder must be free, sane, and adult. The issue of musha' (undivided share) is discussed: Muhammad disallows waqf of a share that can be divided, while Abu Yusuf allows it. For indivisible property like a bathhouse, there is consensus that common ownership does not prevent validity. The text also notes that the fatwa is according to the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (i.e., the two disciples) on the core issue of removal by judicial decree. Perpetuity (ta'bid) is a condition by consensus, but the disagreement is over whether it must be explicitly mentioned. The correct view is that it need not be mentioned; the waqf is valid even without the word 'perpetual'.

Key Terms
وقف (waqf): In this passage, waqf is the act of endowing property by removing it from one's ownership for the benefit of a charitable or perpetual purpose.
القضاء (al-qada'): The judicial decree of a judge that makes the waqf binding.
التأبيد (al-ta'bid): The condition that the waqf must be perpetual, i.e., it should not be time-limited.
المشاع (al-musha'): Undivided share in property that is jointly owned.

Waqf of Real Estate with Accessories: Validity by Istihsan
Ruling with proof: istihsan overrides qiyas for accessories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why waqf of real estate with its accessories (oxen, plowmen) is valid by istihsan and distinguish the subsidiary (taba') ruling from the primary qiyas.

Core Idea
Accessories to real estate in waqf are subsidiary and thus valid by istihsan, even though movables alone would not be.

The matn declares the validity of endowing real estate together with its oxen and plowmen. This appears to conflict with the general rule that movables cannot be permanently endowed. In this lesson, we unpack the Hanafi reasoning—how the accessories are treated as subsidiary to the land, making the waqf valid through istihsan (juristic preference).

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling and its evidence
Note that Abu Hanifa holds waqf of real estate alone valid based on the practice of the Companions. This sets the asl (original case) for qiyas.
Companion practice is a recognized source in Hanafi usul.
2
Understand the qiyas objection
The qiyas would analogize the oxen and plowmen (movables) to independent movables, which are not valid for waqf due to lack of perpetuity. This is the opposing argument.
Perpetuity (ta'bid) is a condition for waqf; movables are not perpetual by nature.
3
Apply istihsan: the subsidiary (taba') principle
The text explains that the accessories are subsidiary (taba') to the land in achieving the objective. A ruling may be established for the subsidiary even if it would not be established when independent – like the right to watering in the sale of land.
Istihsan overrides qiyas here because the accessory is inseparable from the land's benefit.
4
Confirm the ruling and its scope
The ruling is valid by istihsan according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the two). It applies to all plowing implements, and by extension, buildings enter as subsidiary.
The fatwa is on this view.

And 'ukaratihi' (plowmen) is the plural of akkar, with shaddah on the kaf, meaning his slave plowmen, and similarly all plowing implements. (Fath). Common ownership in what cannot be divided does not prevent validity of waqf without disagreement. The disagreement is in what can be divided. If the musha' waqf has been adjudicated permissible i.e. the judge ruled its permissibility, then it is permissible according to Abu Yusuf because division is part of possession, and possession in his view is not a condition, so likewise its completion. According to Muhammad, possession is a condition, so waqf of musha' is not valid in his view. He qualified with 'adjudicated its permissibility' because waqf of musha' is not valid without a judicial decree according to Muhammad, and upon this is the fatwa. Waqf of musha' that cannot be divided like a bathhouse and a mill is valid by agreement and does not need a judicial decree for validity. (Mukhtasar, Fath). 'Its permissibility' i.e. the permissibility of waqf in it, because the judge's decree cuts off disagreement in ijtihadi matters. ('Ayni). 'And movable in which there is custom etc.' [i.e. also valid is waqf of a movable intentionally. The qiyas is that it is not permissible because perpetuity is a condition of waqf and there is no perpetuity in movables. The basis of istihsan is his statement peace be upon him: 'Indeed Khalid has detained his armor and horses in the way of Allah,' and similarly Talhah did. According to Abu Yusuf: it is not permissible except in horses and weapons. Al-Shafi'i allowed waqf of everything that can be sold and from which benefit can be taken while preserving its substance, by analogy to horses and weapons. We say: The principle is impermissibility of waqf of movables, so it is restricted to what the Law has specified, i.e., real estate and horses; so what remains beyond that is upon the original qiyas except that which is customary, so it becomes like dirhams and dinars. Commentary
This passage discusses the validity of three specific types of waqf: (1) real estate with its accessories like oxen and plowmen, (2) undivided shares (musha'), and (3) movable property. The matn states: 'And the waqf of real estate with its oxen and plowmen is valid, and an undivided share (musha') whose permissibility has been adjudicated, and movable property in which there is custom (of waqf).' The matn mentions 'and an undivided share (musha') whose permissibility has been adjudicated'. This points to a key dispute within the Hanafi school: how much possession (qabd) is necessary for waqf? This passage discusses the validity of three specific types of waqf: (1) real estate with its accessories like oxen and plowmen, (2) undivided shares (musha'), and (3) movable property. Ruling on validity of waqf of various property types with conditions

Core Idea
Waqf of immovable valid; undivided share needs decree; movables need custom.

This lesson covers three key property types in waqf: immovable, undivided shares, and movables. The Hanafi school has detailed conditions for each, balancing the requirement of perpetuity with practical custom. We will explore the rulings and the proofs behind them.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling statement from Kanz al-Daqa'iq on the validity of waqf. The matn states that waqf of immovable property (al-'iqar) is valid, including with its accessories like cows and tillers. It also states that waqf of an undivided share (musha') is valid if a decree is issued by a judge, and that waqf of movable property (manqul) is valid if there is customary practice ('urf) of such waqf. The commentary expands on each part. First, regarding waqf of immovable alone: Abu Hanifa holds it valid based on practice of the Companions. Waqf of movable is invalid by analogy (qiyas) because waqf requires perpetuity (ta'bid), which movables lack. However, the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) rule that waqf of immovable with its accessories (cows, tillers) is valid by istihsan (juristic preference) because they are subordinate to the land in achieving the purpose. Similarly, all agricultural tools enter the waqf as accessories. Al-Walwaliji mentions a case of a cow waqf for a ribat based on custom. The commentary then defines 'iqar and akarat. For undivided shares: there are two types—those that cannot be divided (e.g., bathhouse, mill) and those that can (e.g., land, house). For the latter, there is disagreement: Abu Yusuf permits it without qadi's decree because possession is not a condition, while Muhammad requires a decree because possession is a condition. The fatwa is on Muhammad's view. For movables: by analogy they are invalid due to lack of perpetuity, but by istihsan based on the hadith of Khalid and custom, they are valid. Abu Yusuf restricts to horses and weapons, while Muhammad allows any movable that is customarily waqf. The fatwa is on Muhammad's view. Shafi'i's broader position is mentioned but rejected because the original principle limits waqf to what is specified by revelation (immovable and horses), except where custom prevails.

Waqf Cannot Be Owned or Divided
Rulings on prohibitions of waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two key prohibitions regarding waqf property (ownership and division) and explain why they are prohibited based on the founder's removal of ownership.

Core Idea
A valid waqf removes property from ownership like freeing a slave; it cannot be sold, gifted, or divided among beneficiaries.

When a waqf is valid and irrevocable, the property enters a special legal state: it belongs to no one as a possessory entity, yet its benefits are directed to the beneficiaries. This lesson unpacks the two primary consequences: the prohibition of transferring ownership and the prohibition of dividing the corpus. Understanding these rules is essential for anyone managing or benefiting from waqf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two prohibitions
Read the first phrase: 'ولا يُملك ولا يقسم' (it is not owned and not divided). Identify that these are permanent prohibitions after the waqf becomes valid and irrevocable.
The prohibitions apply even if the waqf is for the founder's own children.
2
Understand the reason for non-ownership
The commentary explains: waqf removes ownership from the founder without transferring it to anyone else, similar to manumission ('itq). Thus, the property cannot be sold, gifted, inherited, or used as collateral.
This is a core Hanafi doctrine distinguishing waqf from mere endowment.
3
Distinguish division among beneficiaries vs. segregation
Division (qisma) of the corpus among beneficiaries is prohibited because it implies a transfer akin to sale. However, segregation (ifraz) of the waqf from the founder's other property to distinguish it is permissible according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn), as it is merely separating rights.
The prohibition is on tamlik (transfer of ownership), not ifraz (segregation).
4
Recognize the beneficiaries' right in produce only
Beneficiaries have a right to the ghalla (produce/income), not the 'ayn (corpus). Therefore, dividing the corpus would be equivalent to selling it, which is invalid.
This is why maintenance must be from the produce – to preserve the corpus.

Maintenance of Waqf: Obligation and Procedure
Rulings on maintenance of waqf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why maintenance of waqf is obligatory from its produce even without stipulation, and describe the procedure when the resident refuses or is unable to maintain.

Core Idea
Maintenance of waqf is wajib from its income because perpetual benefit depends on it; if a resident cannot maintain, the judge may lease the property and use rent for upkeep.

After establishing that waqf property cannot be owned or divided, the text turns to the practical necessity of maintenance. The waqf's produce must first be used to preserve the corpus. This lesson explains the obligation of maintenance, its scope, and the special case when a residential waqf beneficiary fails to maintain. It also covers the use of debris from demolition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation of maintenance
The matn says: 'ويبدأ من غلته بعمارته بلا شرط' — maintenance is begun from its produce without condition. The commentary explains this is wajib (obligatory) on the trustee, even if the founder did not stipulate it.
The basis is the founder's implicit intention: perpetual benefit requires maintenance.
2
Understand the scope of maintenance
Maintenance should not exceed the original capacity and state of the property. If the waqf is for a specific person, no increase beyond original. For waqf on the poor, there is a difference: some say no increase (the more correct view), some allow it.
The more correct view (الأصح) is no increase beyond original state.
3
Learn the procedure for a residential waqf
If the waqf is a house for a specific resident, the resident is responsible for maintenance because 'loss accompanies gain' (الغرم بالغنم). If the resident refuses or cannot maintain, the judge may lease the house to him or to someone else and use the rent to maintain.
The resident is not forced to spend his own money; the judge intervenes.
4
Understand the use of debris
Debris (naqd) from demolished waqf must be used for repairs if its original material can be reused. If not, it is sold and the proceeds kept for future maintenance. The debris or its price is not divided among beneficiaries.
This ensures the waqf property is preserved.

Waqf: Inalienable and Indivisible
Core ruling on waqf inalienability and indivisibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two core rulings on waqf in the Hanafi school and explain why division among beneficiaries is prohibited.

Core Idea
Waqf removes ownership entirely, making the corpus inalienable and indivisible among beneficiaries.

This lesson examines the foundational Hanafi principle that once a waqf (endowment) is valid and irrevocable, the endowed property is no longer owned by anyone and cannot be divided among its beneficiaries. The text explains the rationale: waqf is like emancipation—it transfers ownership to no one—and division (qismah) is legally a sale, which is impermissible in waqf. However, a key intra-Hanafi dispute arises regarding division between the waqif and the waqf property itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two rulings
Read the matn: 'ولا يملك ولا يقسم' — the waqf is not owned and not divided. Determine what these negations mean in legal terms.
The commentary expands on both.
2
Understand the analogy for 'not owned'
The commentary compares waqf to freeing a slave: ownership is removed to no owner, so the property cannot be sold, gifted, pawned, or loaned.
This analogy (qiyas) is central to the Hanafi proof.
3
Grasp the reason for 'not divided'
Division (qismah) is legally considered a sale (mubaya'ah) because it exchanges shares. Since waqf cannot be sold, division is impermissible among beneficiaries.
Beneficiaries only have a right to the usufruct (ghallah), not the corpus (ayn).
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi exception
The commentator notes that dividing waqf property from the owner's other property (to separate them) is permissible according to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), as it is mere separation (ifraz), not a sale.
This exception does not apply to division among beneficiaries.
5
Apply to a practical scenario
Consider a waqif who endows a portion of his land but the boundaries are mixed with his own property. Can he divide to distinguish? According to the Sahibayn, yes.
Imam Abu Hanifa is reported to disagree; the mufta bihi is not specified here.

This passage is a ruling statement from the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq, expanded by the commentator. It establishes two fundamental principles of waqf: (1) The waqf property itself is not owned by anyone after the endowment becomes valid and irrevocable—it cannot be sold, gifted, pawned, or loaned. The reason given is that waqf removes ownership from the owner and does not transfer it to any other owner, analogous to freeing a slave. Therefore, any transaction that would transfer ownership is void. (2) The waqf property cannot be divided among its beneficiaries. Since division (qismah) legally constitutes a sale (it entails exchange of shares), it is impermissible. Beneficiaries only have a right to the usufruct (ghallah), not the corpus (`ayn). Division would give each beneficiary ownership of a part of the corpus, which contradicts the nature of waqf. However, the commentator clarifies an important intra-Hanafi distinction: dividing the property between the original owner (who still retains some rights if the waqf is not fully detached?) and the waqf property itself—to separate the waqf portion from the owner's other property—is permissible according to Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (the Sahibayn). This is because such division is mere separation (ifraz) and identification of rights, not a transfer of ownership. Imam Abu Hanifa's position on this specific point is not mentioned, but the phrase 'according to the two companions' implies he disagreed, holding that any division is impermissible. The relied-upon (mufta bihi) position is not specified here.

Waqf Proceeds: Maintenance, Preservation, Not Distribution
Ruling on disposition of waqf proceeds

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on the priority of waqf proceeds and identify the three obligations: maintenance, preservation, and prohibition of distribution.

Core Idea
Waqf proceeds belong to the endowment, not the beneficiaries—upkeep comes first.

This lesson introduces a foundational rule in Hanafi waqf law: the proceeds (ghalla) of an endowed property are not the personal income of the beneficiaries. Rather, they are a trust that must first secure the endowment's own survival. The trustee's primary duty is to maintain the property; any surplus must be saved for future needs, never handed out as profit. This principle preserves the perpetual nature of the waqf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Commands
The passage contains three distinct directives: (1) use proceeds for maintenance if needed, (2) preserve any surplus for future need, (3) do not divide proceeds among beneficiaries. Locate each in the Arabic text.
The phrase 'إلى عمارته إن احتيج وإلا حفظه' covers the first two; 'ولا يقسمه' covers the third.
2
Understand the Legal Hierarchy
Maintenance (tarmim/icmar) is the first priority because without the corpus, no benefit can be generated. Preservation (hifz) is secondary—surplus is saved to cover future maintenance or unexpected losses. Distribution is absolutely prohibited because beneficiaries have no ownership in the proceeds; they only have a right to usufruct (manafi').
This hierarchy ensures the waqf's perpetuity (ta'bid), a core Hanafi condition.
3
Apply the Ruling to a Concrete Scenario
Imagine a waqf rental property that generates £10,000 annually. The roof needs £2,000 in repairs. The trustee must spend £2,000 on the roof, save the remaining £8,000 for future needs (e.g., next year's repairs if income drops), and cannot distribute any amount to the beneficiaries (e.g., the local mosque or the descendants named in the waqf deed).
Beneficiaries only receive the usufruct (e.g., right to live in the property if it is a residential waqf), not cash income.
4
Recognize the Authority of This Ruling
This ruling is a straightforward statement from the matn of Kanz al-Daqa'iq. It represents the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school. There is no known disagreement among the three imams (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) on this basic principle.
The ruling is part of Zahir al-Riwayah and is the foundation for further details in longer works.

Seven Exceptions to the Waqif's Conditions in Waqf
List of seven exceptions to the binding nature of waqf conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the seven Hanafi exceptions where a waqif's condition is not binding and explain the rationale for each.

Core Idea
Not every condition of the waqif is binding – seven exceptions protect higher legal objectives.

The general rule in Hanafi waqf law is that the conditions set by the founder (waqif) must be followed. However, Hanafi jurists have identified seven situations where a condition may be overridden for reasons of necessity, public interest, or prevention of harm. This lesson presents these exceptions clearly so you can recall them and understand their underlying principles.

Guided Path

State the general rule
The default rule is that the waqif's conditions are binding. This is the starting point.
The exceptions are carved out from this rule.

Learn the first exception (treacherous trustee)
If the trustee (qayyim) is untrustworthy (kha'in), he must be removed even if the waqif forbade removal. This protects the waqf from embezzlement.
Based on the principle of preventing harm.
3
Learn the second and third exceptions (rent restriction and grave recitation)
Second: If waqif restricts rent to one year but longer term benefits the poor, the judge may override. Third: A condition to recite at the waqif's grave is void as it may lead to bid'ah or waste.
Both prioritize public interest over specific conditions.
4
Learn the fourth and fifth exceptions (surplus charity and fixed food)
Fourth: Specifying surplus to beggars of a particular mosque is too restrictive; condition ignored. Fifth: If food items are specified, the administrator may give cash value instead.
These allow flexibility for efficient administration.
5
Learn the sixth and seventh exceptions (imam's salary and substitution)
Sixth: Judge may increase imam's salary if insufficient and he is qualified. Seventh: Judge may substitute waqf property if it is more beneficial, even if waqif forbade substitution.
The judge has authority to act for the waqf's benefit.

Making a Place a Mosque: Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which a place becomes a mosque according to the various Hanafi positions and distinguish between them.

Core Idea
Ownership of a place ceases when it is effectively dedicated for prayer, but the Hanafi imams differ on what act suffices.

This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the requirements for a place to become a mosque (masjid). The core ruling is that the owner must intend to dedicate it and it must be used for prayer. However, the imams differ on whether a single prayer suffices, or a congregation with adhan and iqama is required, or even mere utterance suffices. Understanding these differences refines our grasp of waqf law and its practical application.

When a Mosque Can Be Sold: The Effect of Attached Private Rights
Ruling on saleability of mosque with private annexes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that a mosque with attached private property can be sold and inherited, and identify the underlying reason (the mosque is not purely for Allah).

Core Idea
A mosque remains ownable if the owner retains a private right, e.g., basement or upper room not for mosque use.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq tackles a subtle question: can a mosque be sold and inherited? The answer is surprising: yes, if the mosque is built over a privately owned basement or with an upper room belonging to someone, or if it is located in the middle of one's house. The reason is that the mosque is not fully dedicated to Allah because the owner still has rights that prevent the mosque from being exclusively His. This lesson unpacks the rule and the intra-Hanafi differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The passage lists three situations: (1) mosque over a basement, (2) mosque with an upper room, (3) mosque in the middle of a house with permission to enter.
Each scenario shares the feature that the owner retains some private right.
2
Note the ruling
In all three scenarios, the mosque can be sold and inherited (له بيعه ويورث عنه).
This is counterintuitive because mosques are normally inalienable.
3
Understand the rationale
The mosque is not khalis (exclusively) for Allah because the owner's private right (e.g., right to prevent harm or to use the basement) persists.
The Quranic verse 'And the mosques are for Allah' implies total exclusivity, which is absent here.
4
Recognize the exception
If the basement or upper room is for the mosque's benefit (masalih), then it is a proper mosque and cannot be sold.
Examples: storage, imam's residence; these serve the mosque, not private ownership.
5
Compare the intra-Hanafi views
Abu Hanifa allows lower mosque/upper property; Muhammad allows upper mosque/lower property; Abu Yusuf permits both due to necessity.
The disagreement hinges on whether permanence (Abu Hanifa) or dignity of the mosque (Muhammad) is prioritized.

When Does Ownership Cease? The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Public Charitable Buildings

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Ownership of a public charitable building ceases only by a judge's ruling—unless you follow Abu Yusuf or Muhammad.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses an important waqf question: When does a person lose ownership of a building they have dedicated for public use? While the base rule seems simple, the Hanafi school has a three-way internal difference, each based on a different analogy. Understanding this dispute helps clarify the nature of charitable endowments in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the default ruling
The act is building a sabil, khan, ribat, or cemetery for public use. The default ruling is that ownership does not cease until a judge rules.
2
Locate Abu Hanifa's position and his analogy
Abu Hanifa likens waqf to a loan ('ariya) which requires a judge's ruling to become binding. Therefore, ownership remains until such a ruling.
3
Identify the positions of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
Abu Yusuf says ownership ceases by the mere statement (qawl). Muhammad says ownership ceases when people actually use the facility (istifada), with one person's use sufficient.
4
Understand the underlying proofs
Abu Hanifa uses qiyas on 'ariya; Abu Yusuf on itaq (emancipation); Muhammad on other endowments where delivery is a condition.
5
Determine the mufta bihi and the thamarah al-khilaf
The fatwa is on the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad due to common practice. The practical consequence: until a judge rules, Abu Hanifa allows the owner to revert; the others do not.
