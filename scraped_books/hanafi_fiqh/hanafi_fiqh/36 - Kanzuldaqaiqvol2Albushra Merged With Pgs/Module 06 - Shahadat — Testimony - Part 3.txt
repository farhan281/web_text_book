Sequential Retraction and the Collective Loss Principle
Ruling on sequential retractions by two men (marriage) and by two men and two women

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to calculate liability when multiple witnesses retract one after another, using the Hanafi rule that the loss is attributed to the group but its effect appears only when the remaining proof is insufficient.

Core Idea
Earlier retractions may not trigger liability if enough witnesses stay; later retractions activate the loss retroactively.

When witnesses retract sequentially, the first retraction often causes no immediate liability because the remaining witnesses still provide full proof. Only when a later retraction reduces the proof below the full threshold does the earlier retraction become effective. This lesson examines the paradigmatic case of two men testifying in a marriage case, and then extends it to mixed panels of two men and two women.

2
Apply the principle to the marriage case: two men retracting sequentially
Two men testify. If one retracts first, no one pays because the remaining witness still holds half the proof, and there is hope of completing the proof? So why no payment? However, the retractor does not pay immediately; he will pay if the other also retracts. So both pay when the second retracts. The text says: 'فلما رجع آخر، ظهر أثره؛ إذ لم يبق إلا من يقوم به نصف الحق، فيغرمان النصف' meaning when the second retracts, the effect appears, and they both guarantee half. So the first retractor also pays half now. This is because the loss is ultimately caused by both retractions taken together.
3
Extend to mixed panel: two men and two women
If two men and two women testify, and a man and a woman retract, they guarantee one quarter divided in thirds (two-thirds of the quarter on the man, one-third on the woman). If two men retract, they guarantee half. If two women retract, they guarantee nothing because the two men still provide full proof.
The fractions are based on the proof contributions: each man 1/2, each woman 1/4.

Retraction of Marriage Testimony: No Liability for Witnesses
Special ruling: non-liability for retraction in marriage testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that witnesses are not liable upon retracting marriage testimony, distinguish it from retracting testimony about receipt of mahr, and explain the rationale based on the non-compensable nature of marital benefits.

Core Idea
Witnesses who retract marriage testimony do not guarantee because the benefits of marriage are not compensable property.

This passage addresses a specific scenario in the law of testimony (shahadat): when witnesses testify about a marriage with a proper mahr and then retract, they are not liable to pay damages. This might seem surprising because retraction often leads to liability. However, the Hanafi school explains that the subject of the testimony—the marriage contract itself—does not involve a direct loss of a tangible property. The ruling changes when the witnesses testify about the actual receipt of the mahr, which is property. Let's unpack the legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its scope
The rule states that witnesses who testify about a marriage with a proper mahr and then retract do not guarantee (no liability). This is restricted to testimony about the marriage itself, not about the receipt of mahr.
Proper mahr means mahr al-mithl, the customary dower for a woman of her status.
2
Contrast with testimony about receipt of mahr
If the witnesses testify about the receipt of the mahr (or part of it) and later retract after judgment, they become liable because they caused a direct loss of property.
The liability is for the amount of mahr they testified about.
3
Understand the 'illa (rationale)
The reason for non-liability in marriage testimony is that the benefits of the marital relationship (manafi‘ al-bud‘) are not considered compensable property (ghayr mutaqawwim) in tort law. Thus, no property loss is caused by retraction.
The Arabic text states: لأن منافع البضع غير متقومة عند الإتلاف
4
Apply to a variant: testifying for less than proper mahr
If the witnesses testify for a mahr less than mahr al-mithl, they still do not guarantee the deficiency. The same rationale applies: the benefit of marriage is not compensable.
This reinforces that the rule is not about the exact amount but the nature of the subject.
5
Note the underlying principle
This ruling is consistent with the Hanafi principle that only actual property loss triggers damages; abstract rights or non-compensable benefits do not give rise to liability.
This principle appears in other areas of fiqh, such as damages for loss of consortium.

Liability of Mixed-Gender Witnesses Upon Retraction
Ruling on liability when male and female witnesses retract

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules to calculate the liability when male and female witnesses retract, and distinguish Abu Hanifa's view from that of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Liability distributes according to the evidentiary weight of each witness: two women equal one man.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqā'iq deals with a specific scenario: after a judgment based on mixed-gender testimony, some or all witnesses retract. Who owes what? The answer is not a simple equal split—the gender ratio and the sequence of retractions matter. The Hanafi school bases its calculation on the principle that a woman's testimony has half the probative value of a man's. We'll work through the rulings step by step, noting where Abu Hanifa differed with his disciples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule of collective attribution of loss
The commentary states that when witnesses retract, the loss (tلف) is attributed to the whole group. However, the liability only becomes enforceable when retraction reduces the remaining evidence below the threshold that supports the judgment.
This is the core legal theory: liability is conditional on the retraction actually undermining the proof.
2
Apply the gender weight: two women equal one man
The Hanafi rule for testimony is that two women are equivalent to one man. Use this to convert mixed groups into an equivalent number of male witnesses. For example, a man and two women = 1 + (2/2) = 2 male equivalents.
This ratio is the key to all calculations in this passage.
3
Work through each scenario in the text
The passage covers several cases: (1) one man + two women; (2) one man + ten women; (3) two men + two women. For each, determine the total male equivalents, then compute the share of liability for each retracting witness based on the proportion that retracted.
Use the fractions mentioned: quarter, half, sixths.
4
Compare Abu Hanifa with the Sahibayn
In the scenario of one man and two women, Abu Hanifa says the retracting woman pays 1/4; if both women retract, they pay 1/2. The 'three' (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, Zufar) say the retracting woman pays 1/3 and both pay 2/3. In the scenario with ten women, Abu Hanifa divides liability into sixths; the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) split equally between the man and the women.
The text does not resolve which is stronger, but later commentators prefer Abu Hanifa's method.
5
Confirm the 'apparent' ruling for two men and two women
When two men and two women testify, and a man and a woman retract, they pay 1/4 of the subject matter, divided three ways (man pays 2/3 of that quarter, woman 1/3). If two men retract, they pay 1/2. If two women retract, they pay nothing. The text marks this as 'al-ẓāhir' (the apparent/relied-upon view).
This is the mufta bihi position for this scenario.

The key principle, stated in the commentary, is that loss (tلف) is attributed collectively to all witnesses, but its effect only appears when the retraction removes the legal support for the judgment. The first scenario: a man and two women testify; if one woman retracts, she guarantees one-quarter of the subject matter. If both women retract, they guarantee half. This is because the man alone stands for half the right (as his testimony is worth half of two men, but with two women he still completes the evidence). Liability of Witnesses Upon Retraction: Marriage and Sale Testimony
Rulings on liability when witnesses retract in marriage and sale cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on witness retraction to determine liability in marriage and sale scenarios, distinguishing between property loss and non-compensable rights.

Core Idea
Witnesses guarantee only the monetary loss they cause by their testimony — marriage rights are not property.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq details the financial consequences when witnesses retract after testifying in marriage and sale cases. The key distinction is whether the testimony caused loss of property (māl) without compensation. By working through the scenarios, you will understand the Hanafi principle of damān (liability) in testimonial retraction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case type and the object of testimony
Determine whether the testimony was about marriage (including dower or receipt) or sale (including price and payment). Each has distinct rules based on whether the subject matter is compensable property.
Marriage involves mahr, which is a right in exchange for marital rights; sale involves goods and money.
2
Assess whether the testimony caused a loss of property without compensation
Liability attaches only if the retraction caused a net financial loss to a party. For marriage, the dower itself is a legitimate exchange, so only the excess over the proper dower (mahr al-mithl) is compensable. For sale, the loss is the decrease from the market value if the price was below value.
The benefits of the vulva (manāfiʿ al-buḍʿ) are not considered compensable property in Hanafi law.
3
Apply the specific rule for marriage testimony
If witnesses testify to a marriage with a dower equal to mahr al-mithl, they owe nothing upon retraction. If they testify to receipt of dower, they guarantee that amount. If the dower is less than mahr al-mithl, the shortfall is not guaranteed. If it is more, they guarantee the excess. In divorce before consummation, they guarantee half of the named dower (or its excess over mahr al-mithl).
The rationale: in marriage, the dower is a quid pro quo; only unjust enrichment is recoverable.
4
Apply the specific rule for sale testimony
If witnesses testify to a sale at market value or higher, they owe nothing upon retraction. If the price is below market value, they guarantee the decrease. Special sub-rules apply: if they testify separately to sale and receipt of price, they guarantee both the price and the excess; if they testify to both in one statement, they guarantee only the value of the item. The same logic applies symmetrically to purchase.
When separating testimonies, the loss is compounded; combining them simplifies liability to the item's market value.

Retraction in Divorce and General Principle
Ruling on retraction in divorce before consummation and the basis of liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling when witnesses retract from testifying to a divorce before consummation and explain the Hanafi reasoning using qiyas.

Core Idea
Witnesses guarantee half the dower because their testimony fixed an obligation on the verge of falling.

This lesson examines a specific case in testimony: two witnesses testify that a husband divorced his wife before consummation, then retract. The Hanafi school holds they guarantee half the dower, not the full amount. The ruling is built on a nuanced understanding of contingent obligations and an analogy from Hajj penalties. Let us walk through the legal logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and ruling
State the scenario: two witnesses testify to a divorce before consummation, then retract. The hukm is they guarantee half the dower to the husband.
The ruling is conditional on the divorce being before consummation.
2
Understand the rationale
The text gives two possibilities that could have removed the dower entirely: the wife's apostasy or her suckling the husband's son. Either would nullify the marriage and dower. The witnesses' testimony confirmed the dower on the husband when it was on the verge of falling completely.
These possibilities are not hypothetical; they are juristically significant contingencies.
3
Follow the qiyas (analogy)
The analogy is drawn to a muhrim (pilgrim in ihrām) who captures game (asl). If someone else slaughters it in his hand, the muhrim owes a penalty (jazā') but can recover it from the slaughterer. The illa (effective cause) is that the slaughterer confirmed an obligation that was about to fall (if the muhrim released the game). Similarly, the witnesses confirmed a half-dower obligation that was about to fall.
This is qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
4
Note the thamarah (practical consequence)
The outcome is half dower, not full. If the divorce were after consummation, the dower would be fully stable, and retraction would lead to full liability. The ruling highlights that liability is proportionate to the certainty of the obligation at the time of testimony.
The Shafi'i school differs, holding full liability.

Liability of Witnesses upon Retraction in Various Cases
Fiqh ruling with proof on witness retraction liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for witness liability upon retraction and apply it to sale, divorce, manumission, and qisas, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument in qisas.

Core Idea
Witnesses guarantee only uncompensated loss; a compensating exchange nullifies liability.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi ruling on witness retraction, a topic rich with nuance. The central principle is that witnesses are liable only for the portion of loss not offset by a compensating exchange (iwad). The text from Kanz al-Daqa'iq and its commentaries applies this rule to sale, divorce, manumission, and qisas, each with distinct outcomes. You will also engage with the Shafi'i disagreement on qisas, understanding the proof and counter-proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
Extract the overarching rule: if the testimony leads to a loss that is compensated by an exchange (e.g., price in a sale), no liability; if no exchange, liability for the deficiency.
Key phrase: 'destruction with a compensating exchange is like no destruction'.
2
Apply to sale cases
Analyze when witnesses guarantee the deficiency (testimony below market value) vs. no guarantee (at or above market value). Consider subcases: testimonies combined with price receipt.
The distinction hinges on whether the seller receives full value.
3
Apply to divorce before and after consummation
Before consummation, witnesses guarantee half the mahr because they confirmed what was at risk of falling (e.g., apostasy). After consummation, no guarantee because mahr is secured by intercourse.
Analogy to the muhrim who takes game and another slaughters it.
4

For 'itq, witnesses guarantee the full value. For tadbir, they guarantee the decrease (half value). For kitabah, full value but slave must pay. For istilad, difference between slave value and umm walad value.
Each subcase has specific rules about recourse and emancipation.
5
Analyze the qisas dispute with al-Shafi'i
Hanafi: witnesses guarantee blood money, not qisas, because voluntary acts of guardian and judge cut causation. Shafi'i: they caused death and are like coercers, so qisas. Evaluate the proofs.
Difference leads to practical consequences in criminal law.

Commentary
This passage from the sharh of Kanz al-Daqa'iq, authored by Imam Abu al-Barakat al-Nasafi, is an expansion on the rulings of testimony retraction. The commentary by 'Ayni and Fath al-Qadir explains the legal principles behind the liability of witnesses who retract their testimony. The core rule is that witnesses guarantee only the portion of loss that is not offset by a compensating exchange ('iwad). For example, if they testify to a sale at market value, the buyer already paid the price, so no loss; but if they testify to a sale below value, they guarantee the deficiency. In divorce before consummation, they guarantee half the mahr because the husband would have been at risk of losing it entirely (e.g., if the wife apostatizes), so their testimony confirmed it. After consummation, no guarantee because the mahr is fully established by intercourse. For manumission, they guarantee the slave's value because they deprived the owner of his property. For retribution (qisas), they guarantee the blood money and are not subject to qisas themselves, unlike al-Shafi'i's view, because their causal role is cut by the independent actions of the guardian and judge, similar to indicating a thief or opening a cage. The text also covers subcases: tadbir (half value), kitabah (full value, but slave must pay then be freed), and istilad (difference between slave value and umm walad value). The Hanafi position is opposed by al-Shafi'i on some points and by Ashhab al-Maliki on divorce. Throughout, the commentary emphasizes the principle of equivalence in compensation and the necessity of direct or effective causation for liability.

Key Terms
ضمان (daman): Liability or guarantee of compensation for loss caused by wrongful testimony.
مهر المثل (mahr al-mithl): The dower of a woman of similar status, used as a benchmark to determine excess when witnesses testify to a higher amount.
الرجوع عن الشهادة (al-ruju' 'an al-shahada): Retraction of testimony by witnesses after a judgment has been passed, leading to potential liability for damages.

Witness Liability Upon Retraction in Divorce and Manumission
Explaining guarantee when witnesses retract after divorce or manumission testimony

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on witness liability when retracting testimony in divorce and manumission cases, and distinguish it from the Shafi'i position on post-consummation divorce.

Core Idea
Witnesses are liable only when their retraction directly causes financial loss.

When witnesses retract after the court has acted on their testimony, are they financially liable? This lesson explores the Hanafi answer: liability depends on whether the testimony caused the loss. In divorce after consummation, the dower is already confirmed by consummation, so witnesses are not liable. In manumission and similar cases, they are liable for the value. We also examine the Shafi'i counter-position.

Liability of Retracting Witnesses: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Explaining the Hanafi ruling on witnesses' liability for diyah after retraction from qisas testimony, with inter-madhhab and intra-madhhab differences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on retracting witnesses in qisas cases, explain the role of shubha in avoiding qisas, and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i position as well as the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn concerning recourse.

Core Idea
Retracting witnesses pay diyah, not qisas, because shubha severs causation for fixed punishments.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq addresses a critical fiqh scenario: witnesses testify to intentional murder, the convict is executed, and then the witnesses retract. The Hanafi school holds them liable for diyah (blood-money) over three years, with no qisas. This differs sharply from the Shafi'i position, which requires their execution. The text also explores an intra-Hanafi difference on whether the witnesses can seek recourse from the wali (heir). Understanding the reasoning requires appreciating how voluntary actions by the wali and judge interrupt causal chains, creating a shubha (doubt) that blocks qisas but still triggers financial compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Scenario
The base case: two witnesses testify that a man committed intentional murder; the judge orders qisas and the convict is executed; then the witnesses retract. Note that the retraction occurs after execution, not before.
2
State the Hanafi Position
The Hanafi school rules that the witnesses are liable for diyah (blood-money) payable from their wealth over three years. They are not executed, nor do they forfeit inheritance, and no expiation is required.
3
Compare with the Shafi'i Position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the witnesses should be executed in qisas because they caused the death, analogous to coercion (ikrah). Understand the opposing view to appreciate the Hanafi response.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Dalil and Response
The Hanafi argument: the voluntary acts of the wali (claimant) and the judge sever the causal link to the witnesses. Their testimony is only a cause (sabab), not direct action (mubashara). Additionally, a shubha (doubt) arises from the lesser degree of involvement, which prevents qisas but does not negate financial liability. The response to the Shafi'i analogy of ikrah is that coercion involves compulsion, whereas the wali and judge act voluntarily.
The text also gives analogies: the indicator of a thief, opening a birdcage, untying a slave – these are indirect causes where the principal actor's choice cuts off liability for the cause.
5
Note the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf on Recourse
When the wali himself retracts or the victim appears alive, the claimant may choose to hold either the retracting wali or the witnesses liable. Abu Hanifa says no recourse between them (whoever is held liable bears the loss). The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) agree if the wali is held liable, but if the witnesses are held liable, they can recourse against the wali because they acted as his agents in testifying. This difference rests on whether the witnesses are considered agents of the heir.
The mufta bihi position often follows the Sahibayn's view, but the text presents both.

Liability of Subsidiary and Character Witnesses Upon Retraction
Explaining liability upon retraction of subsidiary and character witnesses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi positions on liability of subsidiary (furū') and original (aṣl) witnesses upon retraction, and state the dispute regarding character witnesses (muzakkī).

Core Idea
Only subsidiary witnesses are directly liable; character liability depends on intentionality and school position.

This lesson examines the rules of witness retraction in Hanafi fiqh. The key distinction is between direct testimony (subsidiaries) and indirect endorsement (originals and character witnesses). The passage presents the muftā bihī view of the two companions (al-Shaykhayn) that subsidiaries are liable and originals are not, and that character witnesses are not liable unless intentional according to Abu Hanifa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of witnesses
Distinguish subsidiaries (shuhūd al-furū'), originals (shuhūd al-aṣl), and character witnesses (muzakkī). Understand that subsidiaries testify based on originals' tashhīd; originals initiate the testimony chain; character witnesses endorse credibility.
2
State the general rule for subsidiaries
When subsidiary witnesses retract after the court has ruled, they are liable (ḍāmin). The loss is directly attributed to their testimony given in the court. This is the agreed-upon rule.
Rationale: al-talf muḍāf ilā shahādatihim (the loss is attributed to their testimony).
3
Examine the original witnesses' liability
According to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybāni in the dominant view), original witnesses are not liable by simply denying they gave testimony (e.g., saying 'lam nashhad al-furū''). Their denial is hearsay and does not overturn the judgment. However, Imam Muhammad holds that if originals admit agency and retract, they are liable because the subsidiaries are their agents.
The text sides with the two companions: originals not liable.
4
Analyze the character witness dispute
If a character witness (muzakkī) intentionally retracts his tazkiyah after the court has acted, Abu Hanifa holds him liable because his recommendation enabled the court to accept the testimony; it is like a cause of the cause. The two companions say he is not liable because tazkiyah is mere praise (thanā'), similar to witnesses of iḥṣān (chastity).
If the character witness says he made a mistake (unintentional), there is consensus that he is not liable.
5
Apply the tarjīḥ
The relied-upon (muftā bihī) position in the Hanafi school is that of the two companions: only subsidiaries are liable; originals are not liable by denial; and character witnesses are not liable even if intentional. This is based on the direct causation rule: the direct witness (mubāshir) bears liability even when an indirect cause (mutasabbib) exists.
The text refutes Muhammad's agency argument using the maxim: when direct and indirect causes combine, liability falls on the direct cause.

Liability of Original vs Subsidiary Witnesses upon Retraction
Ruling with proof on witness retraction liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on liability when original and subsidiary witnesses retract, and explain the ikhtilaf between Imam Muhammad and the two shaykhs on the nature of ishhad (delegation vs loading).

Core Idea
Whether the original witness is liable depends on whether his act is delegation or mere loading.

This lesson examines a key dispute within the Hanafi school about who bears liability when witnesses retract after a judgment. The distinction hinges on the legal nature of the act of making others testify (ishhad). By understanding the direct and indirect causation rule, you will be able to identify the liable party in various retraction scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of witnesses
Distinguish between original witnesses (shuhud al-asl) who directly witnessed the event and subsidiary witnesses (shuhud al-far') who testify based on the originals' testimony.
Subsidiaries are also called witnesses of testimony (shuhud al-shahada).
2
Recognize retraction scenarios
There are three scenarios: only subsidiaries retract; only originals retract with various statements; both retract. Different rulings apply to each.
The originals' statement 'we made them testify and we erred' is the key point of dispute.
3
Understand the ikhtilaf on ishhad
Imam Muhammad considers ishhad (making others testify) as delegation (inaba/tawkil), so the subsidiaries are agents. The two shaykhs consider it as mere loading (tahmil), so the subsidiaries are direct witnesses.
Al-Shafi'i and Malik agree with the two shaykhs.
4
Apply the rule of direct vs indirect causation
When direct and indirect causes combine, liability rests on the direct cause. According to the two shaykhs, the subsidiaries are the direct cause; according to Muhammad, the originals are also indirect causes, giving the defendant a choice.
The mufta bihi is the view of the two shaykhs.
5
Note the consequences of the dispute
Under the preferred view, only subsidiaries are liable. Under Muhammad, the defendant may choose to hold either group liable.
If the originals say 'we did not make them testify,' they are not liable because it is a report that could be true.

(معدن)] لا شهود الإحصان والشرط، فيه لف ونشر غير مرتب فصورة اليمين والشرط أنهما شهدا بتعليق العتق بشرط، أو تعليق الطلاق بشرط قبل الدخول، ثم شهد آخران بأن الشرط الذي علق عليه العتق أو الطلاق وجد، وقد نزل المعلق، فحكم بذلك، ثم رجع جميع الشهود، فالضمان على شهود اليمين خاصة أي على شهود التعليق؛ لأن اليمين هو السبب، والتلف إنما يضاف لمن أثبت السبب دون الشرط المحض.
وقال زفر : على الفريقين؛ لأن التلف حصل بشهادة الفريقين جميعا، قلنا: شهود اليمين شهود العلة؛ إذ التلف إنما حصل بالإعتاق وهم الذين أثبتوه والتعليق بالشرط كان مانعا ، فعند وجود الشرط أضيف التلف إلى علته، لا إلى زوال المانع، وصورة الزنا مع الإحصان أن يشهد أربعة بالزنا، ويشهد آخران أنه محصن، ثم رجعوا، فالضمان على شهود الزنا؛ لأنه علة، ولا ضمان على شهود الإحصان؛ لأنه علامة وليست بشرط حقيقة، وقال
زفر : يجب عليهم أيضاً، وإن رجع شهود الشرط وحدهم، اختلف المشايخ، والصحيح: أنهم لا يضمنون بحال. وكذا إذا شهد شاهدان بالتفويض وشهد آخران بالإيقاع ثم رجعوا كان الضمان على شاهدي الإيقاع؛ لأنه علة، لا على شاهدي التفويض؛ لأنه سبب، ثم اعلم أن الشرط عند الأصوليين ما يتوقف عليه وجود الشيء وليس بمؤثر في الحكم ولا بمفض إليه والعلة المؤثرة في الحكم والسبب هو المفضي إلى الحكم بلا تأثير، والعلامة: ما دل على الحكم وليس الوجود متوقفا عليه. (عيني، فتح)
Book of Testimony. Chapter on Retraction from Testimony. And the witnesses of oath and adultery, not the witnesses of ihsan and condition. The witnesses of oath etc. [meaning within the witnesses of adultery in retraction from testimony] not the witnesses of ihsan and condition, in it there is a non-ordered wrap and spread. So the scenario of oath and condition: that they testified regarding the suspension of emancipation upon a condition, or suspension of divorce upon a condition before consummation, then two others testified that the condition upon which emancipation or divorce was suspended has occurred, and the suspended matter has been executed, so judgment was given accordingly, then all witnesses retracted. So the liability is specifically on the witnesses of the oath, i.e., the witnesses of the suspension, because the oath is the cause (sabab), and loss is attributed only to the one who established the cause, not the mere condition. Zufar said: on both groups, because loss occurred by the testimony of both groups. We said: the witnesses of the oath are witnesses of the effective cause ('illa); since loss occurred by the emancipation and they are the ones who established it, and the suspension by condition was a preventer (mani'), so upon occurrence of the condition, loss is attributed to its cause, not to the removal of the preventer. And the scenario of adultery with ihsan: that four testify to adultery, and two others testify that he is muhsan, then they retract. So liability is on the witnesses of adultery because it is the cause, and no liability on the witnesses of ihsan because it is a sign ('alama) and not a condition in reality. Zufar said: it is also obligated upon them. And if the witnesses of the condition alone retract, the scholars differed; and the correct view is that they are not liable at all. Similarly, if two witnesses testify to delegation (tafwid) and two others testify to execution (iqa'), then they retract, the liability is on the witnesses of execution because it is the cause, not on the witnesses of delegation because it is a cause (sabab). Then know that according to usul scholars, the condition (shart) is that on which the existence of a thing depends, but it is not effective in the ruling nor leading to it; the effective cause ('illa) is what is effective in the ruling; the cause (sabab) is what leads to the ruling without being effective; and the sign ('alama) is what indicates the ruling but existence does not depend on it. (al-'Ayni, Fath)

Commentary
This passage from al-'Ayni's commentary on Kanz al-Daqa'iq discusses the rules of liability when witnesses retract their testimony. It distinguishes between witnesses who establish the cause ('illa or sabab) and those who merely establish a condition (shart) or sign ('alama). The passage presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: the majority view (Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn) holds that liability falls only on witnesses of the cause, while Zufar argues for liability on both groups. The author rules in favor of the majority. The passage clarifies the usul terminology: shart (condition) is something on which existence depends but is not effective; 'illa (effective cause) directly affects the ruling; sabab (cause) leads to the ruling without direct effect; and 'alama (sign) merely indicates the ruling. Examples include oaths suspending emancipation or divorce, and adultery with ihsan. The key principle: loss is attributed to the one who established the cause, not the one who established a mere condition or sign.

Key Terms
علة ('illa): The effective cause that directly produces the ruling; loss is attributed to it.
شرط (shart): A condition on which the existence of the ruling depends but which is not effective in producing it.
سبب (sabab): A cause that leads to the ruling without being directly effective (e.g., the oath in suspension cases).
علامة ('alama): A sign that indicates the ruling but existence does not depend on it.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi law, upon retraction, only witnesses who established the direct cause of the loss are liable; witnesses of mere conditions or signs are not.
