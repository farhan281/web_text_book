Timing of Acceptance in 'Itq 'ala Juz' After Death
Explains the timing of acceptance in 'itq 'ala juz' after death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the timing of acceptance in 'itq 'ala juz' after death and explain why it is treated as a compensatory contract despite its conditional wording.

Core Idea
The slave's acceptance after death is binding; the master is compelled to accept the payment.

Imagine a master says to his slave: 'You are free after my death for a thousand dirhams.' When can the slave accept? The Hanafi ruling clarifies that acceptance must occur after the master's death. The explanation lies in the dual nature of this transaction: it is a conditional statement (ta'liq) in wording but a compensatory contract (mu'awada) in meaning. This protects both parties and prevents deception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the ruling
Locate the specific scenario: master says 'You are free after my death for a thousand.' The ruling is that the slave's acceptance is valid only after death.
This is the central legal issue.
2
Understand the dual nature
Recognise that the transaction is initially treated as a conditional suspension (ta'liq) to protect the master, but ultimately as a compensation (mu'awada) to protect the slave. This duality is the key to the ruling.
The text says: 'جعلناه تعليقاً في الابتداء... وجعلناه معاوضة في الانتهاء'.
3
Analyse the qiyas with hiba bi-shart al-'iwad
The text analogises to a gift with a condition of compensation. Identify the asl (hiba), the far' ('itq 'ala juz), and the illa (both involve exchange). Note that the master is compelled to accept, just as the donee must give compensation.
Qiyas is the primary proof cited.
4
Contrast with tadbir
Compare with tadbir (postponed emancipation without payment). In tadbir, the offer is immediate, so acceptance is immediate, but the payment is not obligatory. This highlights the unique compensatory nature of 'itq 'ala juz.
The text explicitly distinguishes tadbir.
5
Summarise the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that acceptance after death is valid, and the master is compelled to accept the slave's payment. This is from Zahir al-Riwayah.
No intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf reported.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof from the chapter on emancipation for a consideration (kitab al-'itaq, bab al-'itq 'ala juz'). It addresses a specific scenario where a master says to his slave: 'You are free after my death for a thousand dirhams.' The text clarifies that the slave's acceptance must occur after the master's death, not before, because the offer is conditioned on the future event of death. The commentary explains the dual nature of this transaction: verbally it is a conditional statement (ta'liq), but in meaning it is a compensatory contract (mu'awada). This duality serves to protect interests: initially as a conditional suspension to protect the master (so he can still sell the slave, the slave's earnings remain the master's, and it does not affect children born before payment), and finally as compensation to protect the slave (so the master cannot revoke). The ruling is analogized (qiyas) to a gift with a condition of compensation (hiba bi-shart al-'iwad). The proof from analogy extends to the case of conditional divorce (talaq mu'allaq) and contrasts with tadbir (postponed emancipation). In tadbir, the offer is immediate, so acceptance of the consideration must be immediate; however, the money is not obligatory because a master cannot owe debt to his own slave. The slave's acceptance in tadbir is only to confirm his status as mudabbar, but he does not actually become free upon acceptance after death unless the heir emancipates him. This distinction highlights the Hanafi school's nuanced approach to contractual elements in emancipation.

Key Terms
عتق على جعل ('itq 'ala juz'): Emancipation of a slave in exchange for a consideration (juz') provided by the slave, treated as a compensatory contract.
تدبير (tadbir): A form of emancipation where the master declares the slave free upon the master's death; the slave does not have to pay anything.

Emancipation for Service: The Ruling and Dispute on Death
Details the ruling on emancipation for service, focusing on the legal effect of the particle 'ala and the intra-madhhab dispute on death.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on emancipation in exchange for service, distinguish between 'ala and 'in, and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute on compensation upon death.

Core Idea
Emancipation for service is immediate because 'ala indicates compensation; death triggers a dispute over what is owed.

This lesson examines the fiqh of emancipation in exchange for a year of service. We will see how the choice between the particles 'ala and 'in determines whether the slave is freed immediately or conditionally. We will also follow a dispute within the Hanafi school about the financial consequences when either party dies during the service period.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Look at the two formulations: one with 'ala (on condition of) and one with 'in (if). Understand that 'ala creates a compensation contract, while 'in creates a conditional suspension.
2
Understand the immediate effect of 'ala
When the master says 'I have emancipated you on condition that you serve me for a year' and the slave accepts, emancipation occurs immediately. This is because 'ala indicates a mu'awada (compensation) that is perfected by acceptance.
Compare with the conditional 'in, where freedom is postponed until the service is completed.
3
Follow the dispute on death
The text presents two views: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold that the full value of the slave is due when either party dies. Imam Muhammad and Zufar hold that only the wage equivalent of the remaining service is due.
The dispute hinges on whether the service is considered the price of the slave's freedom or a separate debt.
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference appears when the slave's value differs from the service wage. For example, if the slave is valuable, the first view gives the master's estate more; if the service wage is high, the second view may give more.
This difference also applies to analogous cases like the dhimmi emancipating for wine.
5
Apply to analogous cases
The passage extends the same dispute to similar scenarios: a master selling a slave-girl to the slave himself, and a dhimmi master emancipating for wine. Recognise how the same principle is applied analogically.

Third Party Offering Emancipation with Marriage Condition
Ruling on third-party emancipation with marriage condition and effect of wording 'anni'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling when a third party offers consideration for emancipation with a marriage condition, and distinguish it from the khul' scenario.

Core Idea
Stipulating compensation from a third party in emancipation is invalid; she becomes free free of charge.

This lesson delves into a nuanced ruling from the chapter on emancipation for a consideration. A third party wishes to free a slave-girl and marry her, but the legal effect depends on his exact wording. We will see how the Hanafi school rejects the validity of such a condition in emancipation, contrasting it with the rule for khul' (divorce for compensation), and then explore the variant where the third party says 'for me' ('anni), which changes the outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the master emancipating his slave-girl upon the request of a third party who promises to pay a sum and condition that the master marries her to him. Without any addition, the Hanafi ruling is: she becomes free free of charge – no one owes anything.
Focus on the absence of 'anni' or 'alayya'.
2
Locate the dalil (proof)
The proof is a rational distinction (qiyas) from khul'. In khul', a third party can validly undertake the compensation because the wife has ownership of herself for the purpose of divorce. In emancipation, the slave does not have such ownership; instead, the slave has a 'legal force' (quwwa hukmiyya) akin to a buyer, so consideration is only due from the one who receives the benefit.
This is linguistic reasoning based on legal capacity.
3
Trace the wajh al-dalalah (manner of proof)
The key term is 'فرق' (difference) explicitly stated in the text. The author uses this to demonstrate why the ruling differs from khul'.
The distinction hinges on the nature of the right: ownership vs. legal force.
4
Analyse the variant with 'anni'
If the third party says 'anni' (for me), it implies a purchase by way of iqtida' (requirement). The thousand is then divided proportionally between the slave-girl's value and her mahr. The portion corresponding to the value is due; the mahr portion is dropped if she refuses the marriage.
This shows how wording changes the legal outcome.
5
Note the ikhtilaf and tarjih
The Shafi'i school would allow the third party's undertaking by analogy with khul'. The Hanafi tarjih is based on the fundamental difference between 'itq and khul', and the text affirms that the correct view is that 'alayya' is required for third party liability.
The thamarah al-khilaf is practical: in Hanafi, the third party pays nothing unless he says 'anni'; in Shafi'i, he would be liable.

Recognize that duration can be attributed to continuous actions (e.g., 'I rode a full day').
2
Locate the proof
The author cites Quranic verses (Al-Ma'idah 5:89 and Al-Nahl 16:91) commanding to guard oaths and not break them. The proof is used to argue that requiring immediate cessation of a continuous action is not a burden (haraj) and is necessary for fulfilling the oath.
The dalil is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalalah in the Hanafi view.
3
Understand the principle: dawam equals initiation
The text states that continuity (dawam) has the same ruling as initiating the action. Therefore, continuing a continuous action after the oath breaks it, just as starting it would.
This is a key legal maxim: 'al-dawam lahu hukm al-ibtida'.'
4
Apply the exception: immediate cessation prevents breach
If the person immediately stops the action (removes garment, dismounts, moves out), the oath is not broken. This avoids burdening the oath-taker beyond capacity.
Zufar disagrees, arguing any amount of the action after the oath is sufficient for breach.
5
Note the disputes (intra-Hanafi and cross-madhhab)
Zufar (Hanafi) holds that even a moment of the action after oath breaks it. Some Shafi'is analogize staying after oath not to enter as breach. The Hanafi view refutes both using the principle of dawam and the Quranic proof.
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Hanafi, staying inside after oath not to enter does not break; under Shafi'i analogy it might.

Definition and Conditions of Apostasy
Defines apostasy, its pillar, and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of apostasy, identify its pillar (rukn), and list the three conditions required for its legal validity.

Core Idea
Apostasy is valid only when uttered by a sane, conscious, and willing person — reason, sobriety, and volition are its gatekeepers.

Apostasy is a grave matter in Islamic law, but not every expression of disbelief is legally effective. This lesson unpacks the precise Hanafi definition and the conditions that must be met for an act to constitute valid apostasy. Understanding these conditions is crucial for distinguishing between actual riddah and other forms of disbelief that carry no legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish original vs. incidental disbelief
The author first separates rulings of original disbelief (kufr asli, e.g., a non-Muslim) from incidental disbelief (kufr 'aridi, i.e., apostasy). This sets the context for the chapter.
2
Define murtadd linguistically and legally
Linguistically, murtadd means 'one who returns.' Legally, it is 'one who returns from the religion of Islam.' This shift from literal to technical meaning is typical in fiqh definitions.
The definition implies leaving a previous state of belief.
3
Identify the pillar (rukn) of apostasy
The rukn is: uttering a word of disbelief on the tongue after having faith. No specific action is required beyond a clear verbal expression of kufr.
Other schools may include actions, but Hanafis focus on verbal utterance.
4
List and explain the three conditions
Valid apostasy requires: (1) reason ('aql) – sanity, (2) sobriety (sahw) – consciousness, not intoxicated, (3) volition (taw') – free will, not compulsion. If any is missing, the apostasy is not effective.
Insanity, imbecility, muttering, non-discerning childhood, intoxication, and coercion all negate validity.
5
Recognize what is NOT a condition
Puberty (bulugh) and masculinity (dhukura) are not required. A discerning minor or a female can commit valid apostasy.
This differs from some other legal consequences (e.g., execution for male apostates requires puberty).

Establishes foundling's freedom and explores paternity claims

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A foundling is presumed free; a slave's paternity claim with owner's confirmation sparks an intra-Hanafi dispute.

This passage establishes the foundational rule that a foundling found in a land of free people is presumed free unless proven otherwise. It then delves into a nuanced intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding the effect of a slave's paternity claim supported by his owner. Understanding this dispute illuminates how Hanafi jurists balance presumptions of freedom with the realities of slave status.
