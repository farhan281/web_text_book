Buying One of Two Slaves: When Does an Agent's Deviation Become Valid?
Ruling on buying one of two slaves with half the money and exceeding permission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain and apply the Hanafi ruling on an agent's purchase of one slave for more than half the total price, and evaluate the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
An agent's deviation from implied equal division is cured if the principal's purpose is achieved, per the Sahibayn's istihsan.

This case from the Hanafi school examines agency transactions where the principal instructs the agent to buy two items with a lump sum. The implied equal division of the price can be violated, but the ruling depends on whether the principal's ultimate purpose—ownership of both items—is fulfilled. We explore the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, as recorded in the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principal's Instructions
Recognise that the principal instructs to buy two slaves for 1000 dirhams, which implies each at 500. This is the baseline.
2
Locate the Two Positions
Abu Hanifa holds any excess invalid unless the second is bought before dispute. The Sahibayn allow excess within customary tolerance (taghabun) if enough remains for the second slave.
The dispute is about the strength of the implied condition.
3
Analyse the Proofs
Abu Hanifa uses strict analogy: the implication is like an explicit stipulation. The Sahibayn rely on istihsan: the principal's purpose overrides the implication.
Compare qiyas vs. istihsan reasoning.
4
Determine the Mufta Bihi
The relied upon position is that of the Sahibayn because it facilitates transactions and is more practical.
Later Hanafi authorities like Al-Marghinani prefer this view.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling on agency (wakalah) where a principal instructs an agent to buy two slaves with a thousand dirhams, implying each should cost five hundred. The ruling states that if the agent buys one slave for half the money (five hundred) or less, the purchase is valid and binding. However, if the agent buys one slave for more than five hundred, the purchase is not binding on the principal unless the agent then buys the second slave with the remaining money before any dispute arises. This exception is based on istihsan, because by doing so, the agent fulfills the principal's explicit goal of acquiring both slaves for a thousand, even though he violated the implied equal division. The analogical reasoning (qiyas) would reject this because of the clear violation. The two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, further qualify: if the increase is within the range of what people commonly overlook (taghabun), the purchase is still valid as long as enough money remains to buy the second slave. Abu Hanifa, however, maintains that the implied equal division is as strong as an explicit stipulation, so any increase is invalid. This is an intra-Hanafi dispute based on how strictly the implied condition is treated.

Key Terms
الانقسام بالتسوية (al-inqisam bi al-taswiyah): The implied equal division of the purchase price between items when the principal specifies a total price for multiple items.
التغابن (al-taghabun): The customary tolerance in pricing where a slight difference is overlooked in transactions.
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Preferring a ruling that departs from strict analogy to achieve a more equitable result, based on implicit approval in the sources.

Agent Buying a Specific Slave with Debt from Principal
Ruling on a debtor buying a specific slave as agent for the creditor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a debtor buying a specific slave with the debt and explain the intra-madhhab difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
A debtor buying a specific item with the debt is valid; the Sahibayn generalise it to any item.

This lesson examines a scenario where a creditor instructs his debtor to buy a specific slave using the debt. The Hanafi school holds that such a purchase is valid and binding on the creditor. However, an internal dispute arises when the item is unspecified. Understanding this nuance clarifies the role of specificity in agency contracts with debt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and ruling
The scenario: creditor instructs debtor to buy a specific slave with the owed money. The debtor buys that slave. The ruling: the purchase is valid (sahih) and binding on the creditor; the creditor must take possession.
This is the default position in the matn.
2
Understand the condition of specification
The ruling changes if the item or seller is unspecified. Abu Hanifa says it is not valid for the creditor (the purchase binds the debtor). The Sahibayn say it is valid for the creditor absolutely.
The dispute hinges on whether currency becomes determinate in agency.
3
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa’s view: requires specification of seller or item. Sahibayn: allows it generally. The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn’s view due to practical need.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the school.
4
Understand the dalil (qiyas)
The reasoning: the seller acts as the creditor’s agent in collecting the debt, then transfers ownership. This is derived from general principles of agency and debt collection.
The dalil is qiyas, zanni in transmission and meaning.
5
Identify the practical consequence
If the slave dies before possession, the loss is on the creditor (the principal). This underscores that the risk transfers to the creditor upon valid purchase.
This contrasts with cases where the purchase is not valid for the creditor.

This dispute hinges on whether money becomes specific in agency contracts.

Price Dispute Between Principal and Agent
Rulings on whose word is taken when principal and agent dispute the price paid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule for whose word is taken when a principal and agent dispute the price of an item purchased through agency, and apply it to the three scenarios given in the text.

Core Idea
The agent's word is taken when he is a trustee and the item's value supports his claim; otherwise, the principal's word prevails, and if no evidence, both take oaths.

In an agency purchase, if the principal and agent disagree on the price paid, who is believed? The answer depends on whether the principal provided the funds and the market value of the item. This lesson unpacks three scenarios from the Hanafi school, showing how trust and value determine whose statement is accepted, and when mutual oaths lead to rescission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Agency Scenario
Determine whether the principal gave money to the agent for the purchase or not, and whether the purchased item matches the claimed price in value.
The ruling changes based on these two factors.
2
Apply the Trustee Principle
If the principal gave money, the agent is a trustee (amin). His word is taken if the item is worth the amount he claims, because he is trusted with the funds.
If the item is worth less, the agent's claim seems false, so the principal's word is taken.
3
Handle Non-Delivery of Funds
If the principal did not give money, the agent is not a trustee. The principal's word is taken, and the agent becomes liable for the item at the higher price claimed by the agent.
The agent must pay for the item from his own pocket if he acted without funds.
4
Interpret the Unspecified Price Case
When no price is set in the agency, and the agent claims a price with seller confirmation while the principal claims a lower price, both take oaths (tahaluf). The contract is rescinded, and the item becomes the agent's property.
This is a mutual oath leading to cancellation of the sale for the principal.

These rulings are based on principles of trust and the burden of proof.

Agent's Word vs Principal's Word in Price Disputes
Ruling on whose word is taken in price dispute between principal and agent when money is given or not given.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state when the agent's word is accepted over the principal's in a price dispute and identify the condition that triggers the agent's liability.

Core Idea
The agent is a trustee; his word is taken unless he violated instructions.

When a principal gives money to an agent to purchase an item, and they later disagree on the price paid, who is believed? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rule: if money was entrusted, the agent's word prevails, but if the agent deviated from the order by buying a lower-value item, he becomes liable. Understanding this distinction is key for resolving disputes in agency transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify whether money was given
Check if the principal handed over the purchase money to the agent before the purchase. This is the foundational condition that determines whose word is taken.
If money was given, the agent is a trustee (amin); if not, the agent is merely a messenger.
2
Determine who is claiming what
The agent claims to have bought at a certain price; the principal claims a different (usually lower) price. Identify which party is asserting a breach of trust.
Example: Agent says 1000, principal says 500.
3
Apply the trust principle
An agent who receives money is an amin (trustee). When he claims to have fulfilled the trust, his word is accepted unless the principal proves otherwise. Therefore, the agent's statement about the price is taken.
The burden of proof falls on the principal to show the agent acted improperly.
4
Check for violation of instructions
If the agent bought an item worth less than the amount specified (e.g., ordered to buy for 1000 but bought a slave worth 500), he violated the order and becomes liable for the difference. In that case, the principal's word is taken.
Liability arises only if the agent's action caused a loss or deviation from the principal's benefit.

Mutual Oath in Unspecified Price Agency and Effect of Seller's Confirmation
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether mutual oath is required when seller confirms agent's price.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of mutual oath when principal and agent dispute the price after an unspecified price purchase, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi disagreement on whether the seller's confirmation renders the oath unnecessary.

Core Idea
When the seller confirms the agent's price, the dispute is resolved and no oath is needed, according to the preferred view.

When a principal orders an agent to buy an item without specifying a price, and they later disagree on what was paid, the default Hanafi rule is mutual oath (tahalf). But what if the seller confirms the agent's version? This lesson examines the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: some scholars hold that the seller's confirmation eliminates the need for oath, while others insist on it. The relied-upon position is the former, as it resolves the dispute amicably.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the scenario: price unspecified
The principal tells the agent to buy a specific item but does not name a price. The agent buys it, and later their statements about the price differ.
This is distinct from the prior scenario where the principal specified a price.
2
Default rule: mutual oath
Since there is no evidence (beyond their statements) and they disagree on the price, they are considered like buyer and seller in a sale contract, so they perform mutual oath (tahalf). The consequence is that the sale is rescinded and the item goes to the agent.
The oath is a legal mechanism to resolve the conflict when neither party can prove their claim.
3
Consider the seller's confirmation
If the seller is present and confirms the agent's stated price, some scholars say the disagreement is resolved because the seller's word acts as a third-party confirmation. The mutual oath becomes unnecessary.
The seller's confirmation is considered like a new contract between the principal and seller, as if the agent's role is eliminated.
4
Evaluate the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Faqih Abu Ja'far and Qadhi Khan hold that no oath is needed when the seller confirms; other Hanafi scholars (including the initial matn) require oath anyway. The preferred view (mufta bihi) is that the oath is not required.
The tarjih is based on the principle of avoiding unnecessary oaths and resolving disputes quickly.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When a slave says 'I bought it for myself' he becomes free; if he says only 'I bought it' he becomes the buyer's property.

This lesson covers a fundamental ruling in Islamic commercial law regarding a slave purchasing himself from his master. The key lies in the wording of the slave: does he specify 'for myself' or not? The distinction determines the legal consequences, and the proof is based on the principle that a slave's earnings belong to the master. We will also examine the dispute among Hanafi scholars regarding the mutual oath.

Identify the two scenarios

State the ruling for each scenario

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An agent cannot transact with relatives at market price according to Abu Hanifa; the Sahibayn permit it except when the agent contracts with himself.

This lesson explores a key dispute in Hanafi muamalat: can an agent (wakil) sell the principal's property to his own father or brother at the market price? The answer depends on whether we follow Abu Hanifa, who forbids it due to suspicion (tuhma), or his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, who generally allow it. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your ability to identify the role of intention and relationship in commercial agency.

Agent's Sale at Any Price and for Credit
Ruling on the scope of an agent's authority in an absolute sale agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the scope of an agent's authority in an absolute sale agency and compare the Hanafi positions on credit sales and sales for goods.

Core Idea
An absolute agent may sell at any price and on credit, but the school limits this by custom and the purpose of the agency.

This lesson explores the extent of an agent's authority when given an unrestricted mandate to sell. While the general rule allows the agent to sell at any price, for goods, or on credit, there are important exceptions and scholarly differences within the Hanafi school. We will examine the reasoning of Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions, and see how custom and the purpose of the agency shape the ruling.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
Identify the baseline ruling: an agent with absolute authority to sell may sell at any price, whether low or high, and may sell for goods (barter) or on credit.
This is the default position from the text 'Wa saha bay'uhu bi ma qalla aw kathur, wa bil-'ardi wa al-nasi'ah.'
2
Note the exceptions and disputes
Recognize that for currency exchange (sarf), the agent cannot sell below the market rate. Then focus on the main dispute: selling on credit and for goods. Abu Hanifa allows credit for trade but not for personal need; the Sahibayn require customary terms and sale for dirhams/dinars.
The Sahibayn also prohibit selling at a loss that people do not normally overlook (ghabn fahish).
3
Compare the reasoning
Analyze the rationales: Abu Hanifa argues that the agent for sale is not suspect of self-dealing, so the absolute authorization stands. The Sahibayn and al-Shafi'i argue that absolute agency is restricted by custom (urf), otherwise the principal may be harmed.
This is a classic usul al-fiqh debate on the interaction of text (ittilaq) and custom.
4
Identify the practical consequence
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf: if an agent sells on credit for a non-customary long term, the sale is valid according to Abu Hanifa (if for trade) but invalid according to the Sahibayn.
The fatwa often follows the Sahibayn, especially for need-based agencies.
5
Apply the rule to a new scenario
Given a scenario (e.g., a woman gives yarn to an agent to sell for her daily expenses), determine whether credit is allowed. According to the fatwa, it is not; the agent must sell for cash.
This distinction between tijarah (trade) and hajah (need) is key.

Restrictions on Agent's Purchase and the Concept of Ghabn
Defines permissible ghabn and agent's purchase limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which an agent's purchase is valid when the price exceeds the market value, and identify the Hanafi thresholds for permissible ghabn.

Core Idea
An agent may buy at a slight increase over market, but excessive ghabn invalidates the purchase—and the threshold depends on the type of property.

In an absolute agency, the agent must not exceed the market price excessively. But what counts as 'excessive'? The Hanafi school provides clear numerical thresholds for different asset classes. This lesson defines permissible ghabn and the limits of an agent's authority, based on expert valuation and common practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is the agent's purchase under an absolute agency (no price restriction). The ruling: permissible if bought at mithl al-qimah (market value) or within the range that people normally accept as slight deception (yutaghaban fihi).
If the price exceeds this range, the purchase is not permissible and is considered fasid.
2
Understand the concept of ghabn
Ghabn means deception (khida') in price. It is divided into slight (yasir) and excessive (fahish). Slight ghabn is tolerated because contracts are seldom free of it; excessive ghabn raises suspicion of self-dealing.
The text emphasizes that the restriction to market value is by consensus (ijma).
3
Determine the threshold via expert valuation
Permissible ghabn is what falls within the range of valuations by experts (taqwim al-muqawwimin). If two just valuators differ (e.g., one says 10, another 7), the range between them is permissible. Anything outside that range is excessive.
This is the primary method; numerical thresholds are a practical guideline.
4
Apply the specific numerical thresholds
When expert valuation is not available, the Hanafi school sets fixed thresholds: goods (عروض) – half a tenth (1/20 = 5%); animals – a tenth (1/10 = 10%); real estate – a fifth (1/5 = 20%); dirhams – a quarter of a tenth (1/40 = 2.5%).
These thresholds apply only when the price is not commonly known among people.
5
Note the exception for known prices
When the price of an item is commonly known (e.g., bread, meat, bananas, cheese), no ghabn is excused, even if it is only one fils. This is the relied-upon position (mufta bihi).
In such cases, the agent must pay exactly the known market price.

Agent Selling Half a Slave: Validity and Intra-Hanafi Dispute

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An agent selling half a slave is invalid unless the other half is sold before dispute, because partnership is a defect.

The Hanafi school has an internal dispute about whether an agent authorized to sell a slave may validly sell only half. While Abu Hanifa considered the sale valid due to the absolute agency, his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) argued that splitting ownership harms the slave's value and thus is unauthorized. Their view, based on istihsan, became the operative position in the school. This lesson walks you through the arguments, the exception, and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1

Identify the exact case: an agent appointed to sell an entire slave sells only half. What does each Hanafi authority rule?
Note that the dispute only applies when the object is harmed by partnership (e.g., a slave).
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa uses qiyas: the agency is absolute, so any partial sale should be valid. The literal word 'sell' includes selling half.
This is the qiyas jali (clear analogy).
3
Understand the Sahibayn's proof
The Sahibayn argue that selling half creates a partnership, which is a defect ('ayb) that diminishes the slave's value. Therefore, it is not included in the authorization. This is based on istihsan (juristic preference).
They also allow an exception: if the agent later sells the other half before any dispute, the first sale becomes valid as a means to the complete sale.
4
Identify the settled (mufta bihi) position
The school prefers the Sahibayn's view because of the principle of avoiding harm (darar). The mufta bihi is that the sale of half is invalid unless followed by sale of the other half before a dispute.
This is recorded in Zahir al-Riwayah and endorsed by later Hanafi authorities like al-Marghinani in al-Hidayah.
5
Extend the ruling to other objects
The passage notes that for items not harmed by partnership (e.g., land, goods), selling half is valid by agreement. Understand the scope: the dispute only applies when partnership is a defect.
This is a key distinction: not every partial sale by an agent is invalid.

Agent's Partial Purchase: Suspension and Completion
Ruling on agent buying half a slave with suspension

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an agent buying half a slave, explain the reasoning based on tuhma, and apply the condition that buying the remainder lifts the suspension.

Core Idea
An agent's purchase of half a slave is suspended until the remaining half is bought, due to the suspicion of self-dealing.

What happens when an agent appointed to buy a slave only purchases half? Is the principal bound, or is the agent left holding the bag? The Hanafi school introduces the concept of tawaquf (legal suspension) to address the inherent suspicion (tuhma) in purchase transactions. In this lesson, we explore the ruling, its rationale, and the condition that converts a suspended purchase into a binding one for the principal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is an agent buying half of a slave. The ruling is that the purchase is suspended (mawquf) — not immediately effective.
Suspension is a unique Hanafi concept; the contract is pending.
2
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The distinction from sale: in purchase, the agent may be buying for himself (tuhma), unlike sale where the principal's ownership is already established. The principal's command to buy encounters the property of another, so absoluteness is not assumed.
The key is the element of suspicion (tuhma) which is stronger in purchase.
3
Apply the condition for lifting suspension
If the agent buys the remaining half before any dispute arises, the suspension is lifted and the purchase becomes binding on the principal.
Buying the remainder shows the agent intended to fulfill the agency, not act for himself.
4
Note the consequence if condition is not met
If the agent does not buy the remainder, or if a dispute occurs first, the agent is personally liable for the half he bought.
The principal can reject the transaction and hold the agent responsible.
5
Extend to other scenarios
The rule applies whether the slave was specified or not. It also covers cases where buying in parts is necessary (e.g., inheritance), so the purchase of the remainder before rejection clarifies the agent's intent.
The ruling balances the principal's protection with practical necessity.

Return of Defective Goods to Agent: Evidence and Effect
Ruling on buyer returning defective goods to agent and its effect on principal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three methods of proving a defect for return to the agent and explain why the return binds the principal.

Core Idea
A defect proven by evidence, refusal of oath, or confession binds the agent to return the item to the principal.

When a buyer discovers a defect in a purchased item, he may return it to the agent who sold it. But the return is not final until the judge orders it. This lesson explains the three methods of proof—evidence (bayyinah), refusal of oath (nukul), and confession—and how each leads to the principal being held liable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defect and its nature
Determine whether the defect is of a type that could have occurred after the sale (like wounds) or not (like extra finger). This classification determines the burden of proof.
Defects that do not typically occur after sale may be accepted by the judge without formal proof if the judge is certain.
2
Choose the method of proof
Three methods: evidence (bayyinah), refusal of oath (nukul) by the agent, or confession (iqrar) by the agent. Each establishes the defect's pre-existence before the sale.
Confession is strongest; it directly shows the defect existed with the principal.
3
Apply the rule: return to agent = return to principal
When the judge orders the return based on valid proof, the agent returns the item to the principal. The agent is not personally liable; the defect is attributed to the principal.
This is because the agent acted on behalf of the principal.
4
Differentiate defects requiring proof vs judge's knowledge
If the judge personally knows the defect existed before sale (e.g., extra finger), no formal proof is needed. Otherwise, proof is required.
The condition for proof in the text is to resolve ambiguity about the date of sale.

Agent Selling Half of a Slave: The Ikhtilaf Between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Ikhtilaf on agent selling half of a slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding an agent selling half of a slave and explain the practical consequence.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa permits an agent to sell half of a slave unconditionally; the Sahibayn require the other half to be sold later to avoid devaluation.

The text also examines a specific scenario: an agent sells half of a slave. Abu Hanifa allows it based on the general authorization. The Sahibayn, however, object because selling half creates a partnership that reduces the slave's value. This lesson explores their proofs and the resolution in Hanafi fatwa.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling of Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa holds that the agent's sale of half the slave is valid and binding on the principal, because the general authorization (al-tawkil al-mutlaq) includes selling any portion.
The principle is 'al-amr al-mutlaq' – absolute command indicates permissibility.
2
State the ruling of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad)
They argue that selling half creates a shirkah (partnership) which is a defect ('ayb) that reduces the slave's value, so it is not covered by the general authorization.
They consider the partnership a harm (darar) that the principal did not intend.
3
Analyze the exception: incomplete sale before dispute
If the agent sells half but then sells the remaining half before the principal objects, the sale is effective by consensus. The first half is considered a means to fulfill the order (wasilah).
This exception applies when selling the whole is difficult, e.g., no buyer for the whole.
4
Contrast with the case of buying half
In purchase (shira'), the agent buying half of a slave is suspended until he buys the other half, due to suspicion of collusion (tuhmah).
Abu Hanifa distinguishes: in sale, the agent acts on his own property (the principal's); in purchase, he deals with another's property, raising suspicion.

Whose Word is Taken: Principal vs Agent, Investor vs Mudarib
Ruling with proof on dispute over cash/credit in agency and mudaraba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state whether the principal or agent (or investor or mudarib) is believed in a dispute over cash vs credit sale, and explain the legal reasoning (talil) that distinguishes agency from mudaraba.

Core Idea
In agency, the principal's word prevails; in mudaraba, the mudarib's word prevails—because each contract's basis differs.

When a buyer claims the agent sold on credit while the principal ordered cash, whose statement is accepted? The Hanafi school gives a nuanced answer depending on the underlying contract. This lesson unpacks the rule and its rationale, showing how the default nature of each contract—restrictive in agency, absolute in mudaraba—determines credibility in disputes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contract types
Recognize that the passage treats two distinct relationships: wakala (agency) and mudaraba (partnership). In agency, the agent acts under specific instructions; in mudaraba, the mudarib has broad authority to trade for profit.
The text also mentions analogous contracts: marriage, kitaba, hire, manumission for money.
2
State the default presumption for each contract
Note the core principle: agency is built on restriction (al-taqyid), so the principal's command is the reference. Mudaraba is built on absoluteness (al-itlaq wa al-umum), so the mudarib's account is trusted.
The passage contrasts the agent's inability to delegate further (unlike mudarib) as evidence.
3
Apply to the dispute scenario
When the principal says 'I ordered cash only' but the agent says 'you gave me absolute authority', the principal's word is taken. When the investor says 'I ordered cash only' but the mudarib says 'you gave me absolute authority', the mudarib's word is taken.
The ruling differs because the default in mudaraba is absolute; the investor must prove restriction.
4
Understand the ratio legis (talil)
The reasoning: in agency, the principal is the source of the command, so his statement is authoritative. In mudaraba, profit is the objective, and profit rarely comes from cash-only sales; thus the contract implies flexibility.
The text also provides a hadith-like argument: 'Do you not see that he has the authority to deposit and send goods?' (indicating broad discretion).
5
Recognize exceptions
If the investor claims a specific type of mudaraba (e.g., in cotton only) and the mudarib claims another, the investor's word is taken because the absoluteness is dropped by mutual agreement. Also, if the principal explicitly says 'do not sell except for cash', the agent must comply; a mere 'sell for cash' still allows credit if customary.
This shows that the ruling is not absolute but depends on the nature of the command.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for whose statement is accepted when principal and agent disagree on sale terms, explain the reasoning based on the nature of wikalah and mudaraba, and apply the rule to analogous contracts.

Core Idea
The principal is believed in agency; the mudarib in mudaraba—because agency implies restriction, mudaraba implies absoluteness.

When an agent sells goods on credit but the principal claims he ordered a cash sale, who is believed? This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq sets out the Hanafi rule: it depends on the nature of the contract. In ordinary agency (wikalah), the principal's word prevails because agency is inherently restrictive. In mudaraba (silent partnership), the mudarib's word prevails because mudaraba is inherently open-ended. Let us walk through the reasoning and its applications.

The principal says he ordered a cash sale; the agent says he was given absolute authority. This is the core factual disagreement.
The dispute is about the terms of authorization, not about the fact of sale.

The principal's statement is taken because the basis of wikalah is restriction (taqyeed). The principal knows his own instructions, and agency is built on limits set by the principal.

Here the mudarib's statement is taken because mudaraba is based on absoluteness and generality (itlaq wa 'umum). The mudarib has wide authority to act for profit, which often requires credit sales.

5

Mudaraba aims for profit, and profit is often attained through credit sales. Also, the mudarib can deposit and delegate, showing broad authority. In wikalah, the agent cannot delegate without permission.
The talil is based on the objectives (maqasid) and customs of each contract.

Non-Liability of Selling Agent for Substitutes of Price
Ruling on non-liability of selling agent for lost security

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the ruling that a selling agent who takes a pledge or guarantor for the price is not liable for their loss, and explain the distinction between an agent as principal (asil) and as agent (na'ib).

Core Idea
The selling agent's inherent right to collect the price makes him asil; loss of a substitute does not trigger liability.

In Hanafi fiqh, an agent appointed to sell goods is considered a principal (asil) regarding the collection of the price. This means his authority is broader than a mere debt collector. In this passage, we examine a key consequence: if he takes a pledge or guarantor for the price and that security is lost, he is not liable. The reasoning draws on a qiyas with collecting cash. We'll also explore the boundaries of agency by comparing with an agent for debt collection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling
State the specific ruling: an agent for sale who takes a pledge (rahn) and it is lost, or takes a guarantor (kafil) who becomes insolvent, is not liable for the loss.
The ruling applies when the agent acts within his mandate to sell and collect the price.
2
Distinguish the Agent's Capacity
Understand why the selling agent is considered a principal (asil) in collecting the price: the right to collect is inherent in the sale contract itself, not delegated as a separate agency. Contrast this with a debt collection agent who is a mere representative (na'ib).
The Hanafi fix the capacity based on the nature of the transaction, not the label.
3
Understand the Proof (Qiyas)
The proof is qiyas: if the agent collects cash and it is lost, he is not liable. Taking a pledge or guarantor is a substitute for cash; analogically, the same non-liability applies.
The illah is that the right of collection (haqq al-istifa) belongs to him inherently; substitutes follow the same ruling.
4
Contrast with Debt Collection Agent
If an agent appointed solely to collect a debt takes a pledge or guarantor, it is invalid (la yajuz) because he exceeds his authority. He acts purely as a na'ib; his mandate is limited to receiving the debt, not to taking additional securities.
This contrast highlights the critical distinction between asil and na'ib in Hanafi agency.
5
Note the Ikhtilaf on 'Kafalah'
Some scholars interpret 'kafalah' here as hawalah (assignment of debt) to make sense of loss (tawa). However, the preferred view is that it is actual kafalah, and loss occurs when both original debtor and guarantor die insolvent, or when a judge (e.g., Maliki) releases the original debtor upon the guarantor's insolvency.
This subtlety shows how later jurists reconciled the term with practical outcomes.

Joint Agents: General Prohibition and Exception for Litigation
Ruling on joint agents with exception for litigation and dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for two joint agents acting alone, identify the exception for litigation, and explain the Hanafi proof based on necessity.

Core Idea
Two joint agents must act together; only litigation permits one to act alone because joint speech causes hardship and opinion is needed before court.

In Hanafi agency law, when two agents are appointed jointly by a single statement, they must act together in all matters requiring opinion. However, litigation (khuṣūmah) forms an exception: one agent may represent the principal alone in court. This lesson explores the general rule, the reasoning behind the exception, and the legal dispute with other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Two agents appointed together by one statement cannot act independently in any matter requiring opinion. This applies even if one is a slave or minor, and continues even if one dies or loses sanity.
The rule extends to executorship, mudaraba, judgeship, and waqf appointments.
2
Recognize the exception
The exception is litigation (khuṣūmah). One agent may speak alone in court without the other.
This exception is limited to court proceedings; matters requiring prior opinion (like strategy) still need joint action.
3
Understand the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof is based on practical necessity: requiring both agents to speak in court leads to difficulty, confusion, and harshness. Moreover, the opinion needed for litigation is formed before entering the court, so one agent can act with that prior opinion.
If an agent acts without prior opinion, it is not permissible even in Hanafi law.
4
Compare with opposing views
Zufar and the three (Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and al-Shafi'i) require both agents to speak in court. Their reasoning: litigation requires opinion, and the principal entrusted both, not one, with that role.
The Hanafi response is that the hardship outweighs the need for joint opinion; the practical benefit of smooth court proceedings permits the exception.
5
Apply the distinction: agent as principal vs. deputy
An agent for sale collects the price as a principal (aṣīl), so he may take substitutes (like a guarantor) and is not liable for loss. But an agent for debt collection acts as a deputy (nā’ib); he cannot take a pawn or guarantor without explicit permission.
This distinction affects liability: the sale agent is not liable if the price perishes after collection, while the debt agent might be.

Agent Cannot Appoint Sub-Agent Without Permission
Ruling that an agent cannot delegate his authority to another without the principal's permission, with ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on an agent's ability to delegate authority, identify the exceptions (permission or tafwid), and compare the Hanafi position with that of Ahmad, Shafi'i, and Malik.

Core Idea
An agent cannot delegate unless authorized because trust is personal — the principal chose the agent, not the agent's agent.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq presents the Hanafi position on a key agency question: can an agent pass his authority to someone else? The answer hinges on the principal's consent. We will examine the ruling, its proof, how other schools differ, and the practical consequences of each view.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling and its exceptions
Identify the default prohibition and the two exceptions: explicit permission from the principal or a broad authorization such as 'act according to your opinion'.
The Arabic phrase 'اعمل برأيك' (act according to your opinion) is a form of tafwid (wide discretion).
2
Understand the dalil (proof) behind the ruling
The proof is aqli (rational): the principal was satisfied with the agent's own opinion, not another's; therefore, the agent does not have the power to delegate unless given that authority.
The passage also uses a qiyas (analogy) to a judge appointed by the caliph: the judge cannot appoint a successor without the caliph's permission.
3
Note the opposing views
Ahmad ibn Hanbal allows delegation unconditionally. Shafi'i and Malik allow it only if the agent is unable to perform the task himself.
These differences stem from varying interpretations of the nature of agency (wakalah).
4
Examine the consequences (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Hanafi law, if the principal did permit sub-agency, the second agent becomes the principal's agent directly — the first cannot dismiss him, nor does he die with the first's death. Both are dismissed by the principal's death.
This contrasts with other schools where the first agent may retain some responsibility.
5
Apply to a scenario
Consider a real case: A appoints B to sell his car. B is busy and wants to delegate to C. Under Hanafi law, B cannot do so without A's permission or unless A said 'act as you see fit'.
If A had said 'act according to your opinion', then B can delegate, and C becomes A's agent directly.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the judge does not order return of a defective item when the seller claims the buyer was satisfied, and identify the intra-Hanafi dispute on the oath procedure.

Core Idea
Seller's claim of buyer's satisfaction blocks return until the buyer swears; a judge's ruling is final even if given without oath.

In the Hanafi school, khiyar al-'ayb (option of defect) gives the buyer a right to return a defective item. However, that right is lost if the buyer was satisfied with the defect after knowledge. When the seller claims such satisfaction, doubt arises. This lesson examines the role of an agent in such a case and the procedural requirement of the buyer's oath, including a difference of opinion between Abu Hanifa and his disciples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling

2
Understand the obstacle: seller's claim creates uncertainty
The seller's claim of satisfaction raises doubt about the buyer's right to return. Since the right to return depends on the absence of satisfaction, the judge needs certainty.
3
Recognize the requirement: buyer's oath
The judge demands that the buyer personally swear that he was not satisfied with the defect. The agent cannot provide this oath, so the return is blocked.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Hanifa holds that if the judge has already ruled for return without the oath, the ruling is effective and binding; a subsequent oath is useless. The majority (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) require the oath before the ruling.
5
Understand the effect of a judicial ruling
The ruling of faskh (annulment) is a substantive judicial act that changes ownership. Once issued, it is final and cannot be undone, unlike a procedural act like collection.

Commentary
This passage presents the ruling on agency in litigation for a defect claim. If a buyer purchases a slave-girl and finds a defect, he may appoint an agent to handle the litigation with the seller while the buyer is absent. If the seller claims that the buyer was satisfied (rida) with the defect after knowledge, the agent cannot proceed with the return. The reason is that the right to return due to defect (khiyar al-'ayb) is a substantive right that requires certainty. The seller's claim of the buyer's satisfaction raises doubt; the agent cannot exercise the right without the buyer's personal oath that he was not satisfied. However, according to Abu Hanifa, if the judge initially ruled for return without the buyer's oath, the ruling is effective both apparently and internally, so a subsequent oath is useless. The practical difference: in the debt case (previous lesson), the agent's collection is not a judicial ruling, so if error appears, the money can be reclaimed. Here, the judicial annulment (faskh) is final, so the agent cannot undo it. The ruling emphasizes the distinction between procedural acts (collection) and substantive rulings (annulment).

Key Terms
عيب ('ayb): Defect in goods; grounds for return (khiyar al-'ayb).
رضا (rida): Satisfaction with the defect after knowledge; it nullifies the right to return.
فسخ (faskh): Annulment of a sale due to defect; a judicial ruling that reverts ownership.

Key Takeaway
The buyer's oath is essential to confirm the absence of satisfaction before a judge can order return for defect; the binding nature of a judicial ruling means it cannot be reversed even if an error is found.
