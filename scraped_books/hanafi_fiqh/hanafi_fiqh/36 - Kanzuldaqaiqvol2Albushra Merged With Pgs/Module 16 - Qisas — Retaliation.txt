The Imam’s Authority in Homicide Cases Without an Heir
Explains the diyah and qisas for killing a Muslim without wali or a musta'min who converted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four scenarios of homicide without a wali and explain the imam's options for each, including the restriction on pardoning.

Core Idea
When a homicide victim has no known heir, the imam acts as wali with the power to demand qisas or accept diyah, but cannot pardon.

This passage from Kanz al-Daqa'iq outlines rulings when a Muslim is killed without a wali, or when a musta'min (a non-Muslim with temporary protection) converts and is then killed. The imam steps in to protect the public right to justice. The passage also clarifies that the blood of a musta'min is not inherently protected ('isma) but only protected by the aman contract, so diyah is still due if killed after conversion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four scenarios
List them: (1) Muslim kills Muslim by mistake with no wali; (2) Muslim kills Muslim intentionally with no wali; (3) Muslim kills a musta'min who converted, by mistake; (4) Muslim kills a musta'min who converted, intentionally.
The passage groups them as 'mas'ala rubaiyya' (fourfold issue).
2
Determine the ruling for each scenario
For scenario (1): diyah on the 'aqila, given to the imam. Scenario (2): imam chooses between qisas or diyah via reconciliation, but cannot pardon. Scenario (3): diyah like scenario (1). Scenario (4): imam chooses qisas or diyah.
The imam's choice in intentional cases is based on public interest.
3
Understand why the imam cannot pardon
Pardon without compensation is not allowed because the right to justice belongs to the public (the general body of Muslims). The imam is a trustee who must act in the public interest, not for personal motives.
This distinguishes from cases where a known wali can pardon for diyah or even forgive completely.
4
Address the musta'min's protection
A musta'min's life is protected by the aman contract, but this is not 'isma (permanent inviolability). Thus killing him regardless of conversion requires diyah or qisas. The diyah of a musta'min is like that of a dhimmi according to al-Zayla'i.
The inviolability of property and life from aman does not negate the need for compensation when life is taken.
5
Recognize the imam's role as substitute wali
The imam takes the place of the missing wali. This is supported by the general principle that the imam is the guardian of those without guardians, analogous to inheritance when there is no heir.
Qiyas on inheritance: just as the imam inherits when there is no heir, he acts as wali for qisas/diyah.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof covering four scenarios of homicide: (1) A Muslim kills a Muslim by mistake with no wali — diyah on the 'aqila, collected by the imam. (2) Intentional killing with no wali — imam chooses between qisas or diyah via reconciliation, but cannot pardon. (3) A Muslim kills a musta'min who converted by mistake — diyah on the 'aqila. (4) Intentional killing of such a musta'min — imam chooses qisas or diyah. The imam acts as wali because the public has a right. The proof: the general principle that the imam is the guardian of those without guardians. A question is raised: if the musta'min's property is inviolable due to aman, why is diyah not due for killing him? Answer: the inviolability is from the contract, not from inherent 'isma; thus diyah is still required. Al-Zayla'i states that the diyah of a musta'min is like that of a dhimmī. There is also an intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf denies qisas for killing a foundling because there is likely an unknown heir; the two (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) allow it because an inaccessible heir is not a wali.

عاقلة (āqila): The male agnatic relatives of the killer who are responsible for paying diyah in cases of manslaughter
ولي (walī): The legal guardian or heir who has the right to demand qisas or diyah for a homicide victim
لقيط (laqīt): A foundling; an abandoned child of unknown parentage

The Foundling’s Wali: A Hanafi Dispute on Qisas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas for a foundling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Yusuf and the majority (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) on whether qisas can be applied for killing a foundling, and identify the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
Qisas can be applied for killing a foundling with no known wali because an inaccessible heir is not a wali; the imam takes authority.

This lesson focuses on the specific case of a foundling (laqit) from the same passage. Abu Yusuf argues that since there is likely some inheritor somewhere, qisas should be suspended due to doubt (shubha). Abu Hanifa and Muhammad argue that an heir who cannot be found is effectively nonexistent, so the imam becomes the wali.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
A foundling (laqit) is killed either by mistake or intentionally. The issue is whether qisas applies when the victim has no known wali.
The passage links this to the previous discussion of killing without wali.
2
Identify the two positions
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (the majority) say qisas is allowed. Abu Yusuf says qisas is not allowed because there is shubha due to a possible hidden heir.
The text says 'عند الطرفين' for Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, and 'وقال أبو يوسف'.
3
Analyze each side's dalil
Majority: an heir who is inaccessible is not a wali because the deceased derives no benefit from him. They analogize to inheritance: when no heir is known, the imam inherits. Abu Yusuf: the existence of some heir is probable, so qisas is doubtful and must be averted.
The hadith principle: 'Idra'u al-hudud bi al-shubuhat' (avert hudud by doubts) may be invoked for qisas.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If Abu Yusuf's view is followed, the killer only pays diyah. If the majority view, qisas is possible and the imam can choose between qisas and diyah.
The practical consequence affects whether the killer faces execution or only financial compensation.
5
Identify the mufta bihi
The text gives weight to the majority view with 'ولهما' (and for them). Later scholars have preferred the majority view as mufta bihi.
The tarjih authority is al-Marghinani, who comments that the majority view is stronger.

Qisas After Recapturing Rebel Territory
Ruling with proof on qisas after rebel control

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for applying qisas when rebels previously controlled a Muslim territory and explain why rebel implementation of laws cuts off the Imam's jurisdiction.

Core Idea
Qisas after recapturing rebel territory requires that rebels had NOT yet implemented their own laws — the Imam's authority endures until legally displaced.

This passage addresses a nuanced scenario: intentional murder committed while rebels are in control of a Muslim city. The ruling hinges on whether the rebels had implemented their own legal system. Understanding this condition reveals the Hanafi principle that legitimate jurisdiction is continuous unless effectively supplanted by an opposing legal order.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Ruling
The ruling concerns qisas for intentional killing. The act is the killing, and the ruling is that qisas becomes wajib if the just party recaptures the territory. Identify that the hukm level is wajib, not merely permissible.
Wajib because the killing meets conditions for qisas (intentional, same city).
2
Locate the Key Condition
The passage presents a conditional: qisas applies 'if the rebels had not yet implemented their laws'. This is the decisive factor. Determine the state of rebel legal administration.
Implementation means they actively enforced their own penal code.
3
Trace the Legal Reason (Talil)
The reason is that the Imām's authority (wilāyah) over the territory continues until it is effectively replaced. If rebels implement laws, the Imām's jurisdiction is cut off. Explain how this reasoning leads to the ruling.
This is a talil based on the concept of wilāyah.
4
Note the Ikhtilaf
The passage implies that other schools may hold qisas applies always. Identify the mukhtalaf fih: whether rebel implementation matters. State that the Hanafi view is based on the continuity of Imām's authority.
Other schools might consider the killing itself sufficient for qisas regardless of rebel laws.
5
Apply to a New Scenario
Given a scenario where rebels controlled a city for a month but never set up courts, and a murder occurred, then the just army recaptures it. State the ruling and justify using the condition.
Practice applying the condition to ensure mastery.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof concerning qisas when rebels temporarily control a Muslim territory. The ruling clarifies that if a person commits intentional murder while rebels are in control, and then the just party recaptures the city, whether qisas is applied depends on whether the rebels had implemented their own legal system. If they had not yet implemented their laws, the just Imam's authority technically continued, so qisas must be carried out. However, if the rebels had fully implemented their own laws, the just Imam's authority is deemed interrupted, and no qisas or blood money is due; the killer will be punished in the afterlife. The reasoning is that the rebels' implementation of laws severs the jurisdiction of the just Imam. This ruling follows the Hanafi principle that the authority of the legitimate Imam is continuous until effectively displaced.

ولاية الإمام (wilāyat al-imām): The legitimate authority of the just Imam over a Muslim territory, including the power to enforce Islamic penal law.

Executing Qisas in Absence of the Principal
Inter-madhhab dispute on executing qisas in absence of principal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on executing qisas in the principal's absence and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the principle of shubha aversion.

Core Idea
Qisas is a punishment, not merely a right; doubt of forgiveness suspends execution until the principal is present.

This lesson examines a key inter-madhhab dispute: can an agent execute qisas (retaliatory punishment) when the victim's heir (principal) is absent? The answer hinges on whether qisas is classified primarily as a personal right or as a punishment. Imam al-Shafi'i permits immediate execution, while the Hanafi school suspends it based on the principle that punishments are averted by doubts (shubha). We will dissect both arguments and understand the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi Position
Identify the Hanafi ruling: qisas is not executed in the absence of the principal. The agent must wait until the principal is present to demand execution or explicitly forgive.
2
Identify the Opponent and Their View
Recognise that Imam al-Shafi'i holds the opposite view: qisas can be executed immediately by the agent because it is a right of the victim (haqq al-'abd).
The opposing school is the Shafi'i madhhab.
3
Follow the Opponent's Proof
Examine the Shafi'i dalil: since qisas is a right of the victim, the agent, as a representative, can execute it on behalf of the absent principal, similar to claiming a debt.
They argue that personal rights do not require the principal's presence for enforcement.
4
Analyse the Hanafi Response
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal: qisas is fundamentally a punishment (uquba), not merely a personal right. Punishments are averted by doubts (shubha). The possibility that the principal might forgive (since forgiveness is recommended) constitutes a valid doubt that suspends execution.
The principle 'al-hadd yudra'u bi al-shubuhat' (penalties are averted by doubts) is applied here.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: a Hanafi judge delays execution until the principal's presence; a Shafi'i judge permits immediate execution by the agent.
This difference affects the timing and validity of qisas execution in real cases.

Commentary
This passage presents the inter-madhhab dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding executing qisas (retaliatory punishment) when the principal is absent. Imam al-Shafi'i argues that qisas is a right of the victim (the servant, i.e., the person wronged), and thus it can be executed by the agent even if the principal is absent, because the principal's presence is not necessary for claiming a personal right. The Hanafi response is that qisas is a punishment (uquba), and punishments are averted by doubts (shubha). In this case, there is a valid doubt that the principal may choose to forgive the offender, since forgiveness is a recommended act (mandub). Therefore, qisas cannot be executed until the principal is present and either demands it or explicitly forgoes forgiveness. This ruling reflects the Hanafi principle of avoiding punishment in the presence of doubt.

Key Terms
القصاص (al-qiṣāṣ): Retaliation in kind for murder or injury, a penal right of the victim or their heirs.
شبهة (shubha): A legal doubt that prevents the application of a penal ruling, such as the possibility of forgiveness.
