The Gradual Revelation of the Quran (Nazzala al-Ayat)
Commentary on the phrase 'نزل الآيات' explaining gradual revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul method of distinguishing lexical and terminological meanings to interpret Quranic phrases like 'nazzala al-ayat'.

Core Idea
The Quran was revealed gradually over 23 years, not all at once — 'nazzala' implies piecemeal descent, countering the apparent sense of 'anzalna' in Qadr.

This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary explains the phrase 'نزل الآيات' (He revealed the verses), which the author includes after discussing the divine essence. The commentator clarifies that 'نزل' (nazzala) indicates gradual, piecemeal revelation, and defines 'آیات' (ayat) in two ways: as cosmic signs (linguistic) and as Quranic verses (terminological). This lesson will walk you through the commentator's reasoning, showing how careful attention to language and context resolves apparent contradictions in the Quranic text — a core skill in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key term: 'nazzala'
Focus on the word 'نزل' (nazzala). The commentator notes that it is the past tense of 'تنزيل' (tanzil), which means sending down gradually. Contrast this with the form 'أنزل' (anzala), which could imply a single event. Recognize that the lexical form itself carries the meaning of gradualness.
In Hanafi usul, the form of a word (sighah) can indicate the nature of the action.
2
Distinguish the two meanings of 'ayat'
The commentator provides two definitions of 'آية' (ayah): lexically, it means a manifest sign (like natural phenomena); terminologically, it means a segment of Quranic words with a beginning and a break. Understand that both are possible in the phrase 'نزل الآيات'.
This dual meaning allows the phrase to refer either to cosmic signs or to Quranic verses, depending on context.
3
Locate the Quranic evidence cited
The commentator cites two verses: 'إنا أنزلناه في ليلة القدر' (We sent it down on the Night of Power) and 'إنا نحن نزلنا عليك القرآن تنزيلا' (We have sent down the Qur'an to you gradually). Note how the first might suggest a single descent, while the second emphasizes gradualness.
The apparent contradiction is resolved by explaining a two-stage revelation: first to the lowest heaven at once, then gradually to the Prophet.
4
Understand the commentator's resolution
The commentator argues that the gradual meaning of 'نزل' overrides any misunderstanding from 'أنزل'. He reconciles by stating that the initial descent to the lowest heaven was at once (as in 'أنزل'), but the revelation to the Prophet was gradual (as in 'نزل').
This demonstrates the Hanafi principle of harmonizing conflicting evidence (ta'wil) when possible.
5
Apply the lesson to usul al-fiqh
Reflect on how this analysis shows the importance of linguistic precision and contextual cues (qara'in) in deriving rulings. In Hanafi usul, the lexical meaning of a word can serve as evidence for interpreting the revealed text.
This skill is foundational for understanding Quranic commands and prohibitions.

His saying 'nazzala al-ayat': After explaining the name of the Essence, the author intended to mention some attributes that are appropriate to the discipline and also benefit from the excellence of independence. So he said 'Wa nazzala al-ayat'. 'Nazzala' is the past tense of 'tanzil', which means sending down gradually and piecemeal. In this there is an indication that the revelation of the Quranic verses from the lowest heaven to the Messenger, peace be upon him, did not occur all at once, as some people misunderstood from verses like 'Inna anzalnahu fi laylat al-qadr', but rather gradually according to need and the dictate of wisdom over approximately twenty-three years. 'Ayat' is the plural of 'ayah'. Lexically, it means a manifest sign, and terminologically, it applies to every segment of the words of the Quran that has a beginning and a break. Here both meanings are possible. If 'ayah' is taken in the sense of a sign, then it refers to created things like the heavens and the earth, the alternation of night and day, etc., which indicate the existence of the Creator and His knowledge and power. In that case, it would allude to the verse 'Inna fi khalq al-samawati wa al-ard...' to 'ayat li qawmin ya'qilun'. And if 'ayat' is taken in the terminological sense, i.e., Quranic verses, then all of the Quran is meant, by way of naming the whole by the part. Thus, the clear context of 'tanzil' also indicates this meaning, as Allah says: 'Inna nahnu nazzalna 'alayka al-qur'ana tanzilan...' and 'Tilka ayatu Allahi natluha 'alayka bi al-haqq'.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the author's phrase 'نزل الآيات' (He revealed the verses). The commentator explains that the author, after discussing the name of Allah (the Essence), now turns to the attributes. The word 'نزل' (nazzala) indicates gradual revelation, countering the misconception that the Quran was sent down all at once (based on 'انا انزلناہ في ليلة القدر'). The commentator clarifies that the Quran was revealed gradually over 23 years. He then defines 'آیات' (ayat) linguistically and terminologically. Lexically, 'ayah' means a sign (like natural phenomena), and terminologically, it means a verse of the Quran. Both meanings are possible here. If signs are meant, the phrase refers to cosmic signs; if Quranic verses are meant, the phrase refers to the entire Quran by synecdoche. The context of 'tanzil' (gradual sending down) supports the Quranic verses interpretation because revelation is specific to that. The commentator cites Quranic evidence: 'انا نحن نزلنا علیک القرآن تنزيلا' and 'تلک آیات الله نتلوها عليك بالحق'. This analysis reflects the Hanafi usul approach of carefully defining terms and resolving apparent contradictions in revelation. The authority weight is from the later commentary tradition (Fatawa/Mutakhkhirin).

Preferring Ta'sis over Tawkid in Interpreting al-Bayyinat
Interpreting the phrase 'أرسل البينات' as clear signs or miracles.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify three possible interpretations of 'bayyinat' in the commentary and apply the Hanafi principle of preferring ta'sis over tawkid to determine the most plausible meaning.

Core Idea
When a word can have multiple meanings, the one that adds new information is preferred over mere confirmation.

This passage examines the commentator's analysis of the phrase 'arsala al-bayyinat'. By carefully distinguishing between three possible referents of 'bayyinat', the commentator applies a key usul principle: if an interpretation yields a new meaning (ta'sis), it is preferred over one that merely confirms what is already known (tawkid). This lesson will guide you through the commentator's reasoning and show you how this principle works in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and its linguistic form
Recognise that 'bayyinat' is the plural of 'bayyinah', derived from 'bayan' meaning manifestation. This sets the stage for understanding its possible referents.
The word carries an inherent sense of clarity and evidence.
2
List the three possible interpretations
Record the three meanings proposed: (a) clear Quranic verses (ayat muhkamat), (b) rational proofs of Allah's essence and attributes, and (c) manifest miracles of the Prophet (mu'jizat).
Each interpretation is supported by some evidence or context.
3
Examine the commentator's preference
Observe that the commentator prefers the third interpretation (miracles) because the first two meanings are already covered by earlier terms in the author's text (like 'nazzala al-ayat' and a previous mention of proofs). Thus, interpreting 'bayyinat' as miracles adds a new meaning (ta'sis) rather than mere confirmation (tawkid).
This preference is based on a Hanafi usul principle.
4
Apply the Hanafi usul principle
Understand the principle: when multiple valid interpretations exist, the one that establishes a new meaning (ta'sis) is preferred over one that merely confirms (tawkid). This prioritizes adding fresh legal content over redundancy.
This principle is often invoked in semantic analysis of texts.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the author's phrase 'أرسل البينات' (He sent clear proofs). The commentator defines 'bayyinat' as the plural of 'bayyinah', meaning something that makes clear. He then presents three possible interpretations for what 'bayanat' refers to in this context. The first: clear Quranic verses that are not abrogated or questioned (ayat muhkamat ghayr mas'ulat), citing the verse 'وانزلنا فیها آیات بینات' (And We sent down in it clear verses). The second: rational proofs that indicate Allah's existence and attributes (dala'il qahira). The third: the manifest miracles (mu'jizat) of the Prophet Muhammad that prove his prophethood. The commentator gives preference (tarjih) to the last interpretation (miracles) because the first two are already covered by other terms in the author's text (like 'nazzala al-ayat' and a previous mention of proofs), so interpreting 'bayyinat' as miracles provides a new meaning (ifadat al-ta'sis) rather than mere confirmation (tawkid). This is a standard Hanafi usul principle: when multiple interpretations are possible, the one that adds new information is preferred. The authority weight is from the later commentary tradition.

آیات محکمات (ayat muhkamat): Clear and unambiguous verses of the Quran, not abrogated or requiring interpretation.

Al-Amr: Command Belongs to Allah Alone – A Textual Analysis
Explaining the matn phrase 'walaka al-amr tahqiqan wa kullu majaz' with analysis of 'amr'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the matn phrase 'walaka al-amr tahqiqan wa kullu majaz', distinguish the three meanings of 'amr', and justify why only Allah's command is real using Hanafi usul and Quranic evidence.

Core Idea
All true command belongs to Allah alone; human authority is merely delegated.

In this lesson, we examine a commentary passage that clarifies the matn phrase 'walaka al-amr tahqiqan wa kullu majaz'. The commentator unpacks the word 'amr' (command), exploring its possible meanings and establishing that in every sense, it belongs to Allah in reality. This analysis reinforces a foundational principle in Hanafi usul: ultimate authority resides only with Allah Almighty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn phrase and the commentator's goal
Begin by locating the phrase 'walaka al-amr tahqiqan wa kullu majaz' in the passage. Understand that the commentator aims to explain why 'command' is attributed to Allah in reality, not metaphorically.
The matn phrase is part of a longer supplication.
2
List the three possible meanings of 'amr'
From the Arabic text, extract the three definitions: (1) action (fi'l), (2) request of action (talab al-fi'l, i.e., terminological command), and (3) specific speech (qawl makhsus). Note that each has a distinct legal implication.
In Hanafi usul, 'amr' is typically defined as 'request of action from a superior'.
3
Apply each meaning to the phrase
For each meaning, state that it 'belongs to You (Allah) in reality'. The commentator argues that Allah is the owner and controller of all things, so all actions, commands, and authoritative speech are His.
The phrase 'tahqiqan' means 'truly, in reality' – no metaphor.
4
Recognize the exception for created beings
The commentator acknowledges that worldly rulers, judges, and even angelic beings issue commands. But these are by Allah's permission and delegation; they are not real in the ultimate sense.
This is a key theological point: all authority is derived.
5
Link to the Quranic verses as proof
The passage cites two Quranic verses: 'Liman al-mulku al-yawm? Lillahi al-wahidi al-qahhar' (40:16) and 'Wa ila Allahi turja'u al-umur' (2:210). Analyze how these verses demonstrate that all affairs and dominion return to Allah, confirming that only His command is real.
The dalil is direct (dalil naqli) and considered qat'i in transmission and meaning.

Real vs. Metaphorical Existence in Hanafi Usul
Philosophical-theological definition of real vs. metaphorical existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the distinction between real (haqiqi) and metaphorical (majazi) existence as defined in Hanafi usul and identify its implications for the source of legal obligation.

Core Idea
Only Allah has real essential existence; all possible beings exist metaphorically, deriving existence from Him alone.

This passage from the commentary on Muslim al-Thubut delves into a foundational distinction: the difference between the real existence of Allah and the metaphorical existence of all contingent beings. Understanding this distinction is crucial for Hanafi usul al-fiqh because it establishes that the Creator is the sole source of existence and therefore the sole source of obligation and command. The text uses the analogy of sun-warmed water to illustrate how metaphorical attribution works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of existence
Read the passage and pinpoint the two kinds of existence: real essential existence (wujud dhati haqiqi) belonging to Allah, and metaphorical existence (majaz) attributed to possible beings.
Look for the terms 'وجود ذاتی حقیقی' and 'مجاز'.
2
Understand the causal relationship
The passage states that possible beings have no existence in themselves; their existence is derived from the true Creator. The cause has stronger, more rightful existence than the effect.
Consider the analogy of sun-warmed water (ماء شمس): the water is called 'sun' only because of the sun's effect.
3
Note the theological consequence
Because possible beings have no inherent existence, they cannot be sources of independent authority. Their existence is entirely contingent on Allah, the real Existent.
This sets the foundation for Tawhid in legal theory.
4
Reflect on the relevance to usul al-fiqh
Since existence is derived, anything that flows from a created being—including commands and obligations—carries only metaphorical authority. True obligation (wujub) comes from the real Existent alone.
This underpins the Hanafi position that only Allah's commands are inherently binding.
5
Analyze the quote from Fadl-i-Barelawi
The quoted scholar reinforces that only Allah has the power to bring things from non-existence to existence. No creature can do this, emphasizing the absolute dependence of all things on Allah.
This quote supports the distinction and links it to divine power.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the laqabi definition of Usul al-Fiqh and explain the distinction between a true definition (hadd) and a drawing (rasm) in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Usul al-Fiqh is knowledge of rules for deducing fiqh rulings, but its definition is a 'rasm' not a 'hadd'.

In this passage, we move from the relational definition of Usul al-Fiqh to the definition by title (laqaban). The author presents a definition that describes the discipline in terms of its purpose and content, then critically examines whether such a description qualifies as a true definition (hadd) or merely a drawing (rasm). This distinction is crucial for understanding the nature of the field and the precision required in legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the laqabi definition
Read the matn's statement: 'As for a title, it is the knowledge of rules by which one arrives at deducing jurisprudential rulings from their evidences.' This is the definition being examined.

2

The commentator notes that what follows is actually a 'rasm' (drawing) rather than a true 'hadd' (definition). A true definition captures the essence through genus and differentia, but the given definition uses universal concepts that are non-predicable parts, like the number ten.

This illustrates why the definition is not considered a hadd.

Understanding that the definition is a rasm reminds us that usul al-fiqh is a practical tool for istinbat, not an end in itself. The definition describes its function rather than its essence.
This pragmatic approach is characteristic of the Hanafi school.

The Three Considerations of Essence
Classifies essence into three considerations with consensus rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the threefold division of essence in Hanafi usul and identify which considerations exist externally by consensus.

Core Idea
Essence is considered in three ways: mixed, abstract, and absolute; only mixed exists externally by consensus.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, understanding how essences exist is foundational for relating universals to particulars. The absolute essence (al-māhiyyah al-muṭlaqah) is a key concept. This lesson introduces the three considerations of essence and the consensus regarding their external existence.

These are also called bishart shay', bishart la, and la bishart.

Understand that mixed essence includes accidents, abstract essence excludes accidents, and absolute essence disregards both.
The absolute essence is essence as such (al-mahiyyah min haythu hiya hiya).
3
Apply the consensus rule
Mixed essence exists externally by consensus. Abstract essence does not exist externally by consensus.
This consensus is among all schools of kalam.
4
Note the disputed case
The absolute essence is disputed: the verifying scholars affirm its external existence, while some philosophers deny it.
This will be explored in later lessons.

If the absolute essence did not exist, similar substances would be entirely distinct, contradicted by the similarity of water drops.

The author provides a rational proof for the external existence of the absolute essence. He first argues from the similarity of substances, then offers a geometric demonstration against indivisible atoms. This lesson examines these arguments and their role in usul al-fiqh.

State the claim
The absolute essence exists externally. This is the position defended.

2
Understand the proof from similarity
If the absolute essence did not exist, each individual (e.g., each drop of water) would have a separate, distinct reality. But we observe that substances (jawahir) are similar (mutamathilah), implying a shared essence.

This proof is attributed to Ibn Sina and refutes atomism, which is incompatible with the existence of a common essence.
4
Link to usul
The proof establishes that universals exist externally, which supports the Hanafi view that general commands (awamir) apply to all individuals uniformly.
This connection is explained in the application bridge.

Defining Dalil in Hanafi Usul: Certain vs. Probable Proof
Defining dalil as a means to reach an informative conclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of dalil, distinguish it from amara, and explain the three-part syllogism (tathlith) used in istinbat.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, dalil is certain proof (qat'i); probable proof is called amara.

This lesson unpacks the foundational definition of 'proof' (dalil) from the matn of Muslim al-Thubut. Understanding this distinction between certain and probable evidence is critical for correctly weighing sources in ijtihad. We will also see how the logical structure of a syllogism (tathlith) underpins deriving rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms in the definition
Locate the terms 'dalil', 'amara', 'qat'i', 'zanni', and 'tathlith' in the passage. Note that dalil is defined as something that leads to an informative desired conclusion (mattlab khabari) through correct consideration (sahih al-nazar).
The example given is 'like the world'—i.e., the world as a dalil for the existence of the Creator.
2
Understand the components of the definition
Break down the definition: (1) the means (dalil), (2) the condition (correct consideration), (3) the result (informative desired conclusion). Recognize that 'informative' (khabari) refers to a proposition that is either true or false, e.g., a shari ruling.
3
Distinguish qat'i from zanni
The text specifies that the term dalil is reserved for certain proof (qat'i), while probable proof is called amara. This is a key Hanafi terminological distinction—other schools may use dalil for both. Reflect on the epistemological weight: qat'i yields yaqin, zanni only probable knowledge.
Examples: Qur'an and mutawatir hadith are qat'i; ahad hadith and qiyas are amara.
4
Recognize the tathlith (three-part syllogism)
The final phrase 'al-intaj mabni ala al-tathlith' means that producing a conclusion (intaj) from a proof is built on three parts: a major premise, a minor premise, and a conclusion. This is the Aristotelian syllogism adopted by Hanafi usul for istinbat.
For example: 'Every command of the Quran is obligatory (major premise); this verse commands prayer (minor premise); therefore prayer is obligatory (conclusion).'

Definition of Dalil (Proof) and Its Categories in Hanafi Usul
Matn and commentary defining 'dalil' and its types: qat'i and zanni

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi definition of dalil to classify a given piece of evidence as qat'i or zanni, and identify the logical components (premises and conclusion) required for valid inference.

Core Idea
Dalil is the path to knowledge: certain proof (qat'i) or probable conjecture (amarah) — both require sound reasoning.

This lesson examines the foundational definition of 'dalil' as presented by Imam Muslim al-Thubut in his work 'Muslim al-Thubut'. Understanding this definition is crucial for all subsequent usul al-fiqh study, as it clarifies the nature of evidence and the structure of valid reasoning. We will also see how the Hanafi school distinguishes between certain and probable proofs, and why a proof necessarily consists of two premises leading to a conclusion. The commentary enriches this by exploring the linguistic meanings of 'dalil'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of dalil
Read the matn: 'Then the proof [dalil] is that by which sound consideration can reach a desired doctrinal matter.' Note the key components: 'nazar sahih' (sound consideration) and 'matlub khabari' (doctrinal matter).
Doctrinal matters include both creedal issues (e.g., existence of God) and legal rulings (e.g., obligation of prayer), but the focus here is on the logical structure.
2
Distinguish the two categories of dalil
The matn says 'it is specified by the certain and is called the probable [amarah].' Thus, qat'i (certain) proofs are called dalil par excellence, while zanni (probable) proofs are called amarah. Understand that both are types of evidence, but differ in epistemic certainty.
In Hanafi usul, the Qur'an and mutawatir sunnah are qat'i al-thubut, while ahad hadith are zanni al-thubut. However, even qat'i texts can be zanni al-dalala (ambiguous meaning).
3
Analyze the logical structure of a productive proof
The text explains that 'production [intaj] is based on three [premises] because a middle term is necessary, so two premises are obligatory.' This is the Aristotelian syllogism: two premises (major and minor) yield a conclusion. The logician's definition: 'two statements from which another statement follows.'
This shows that a single proposition (e.g., a Quranic verse) is not a complete dalil in the logical sense; it requires at least one other premise to produce a ruling.
4
Explore the linguistic meanings of dalil
The commentary gives three senses: (1) the one who sets up guidance (God), (2) the reminder (the world), (3) the instrument of guidance (the world itself). This shows the breadth of the term and its application in theology and usul.
In usul, the third meaning (the sign that indicates) is most relevant: e.g., the world indicates its Creator.
5
Apply the definition to classify evidence
Using the definition, classify a given evidence as qat'i or zanni. For example, the verse 'Aqimu al-salah' (Establish prayer) is qat'i in its transmission (Qur'an) but its imperative form is zanni in implying obligation (due to possibility of recommendation). Hence it is a zanni dalil in terms of dalala.
This step connects the abstract definition to actual fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute concerning the epistemological status of goodness (husn) and badness (qubh). The commentator (Sharh) responds to an objection raised by Fadil Mirza Jan against the claim that some actions are intuitively known to be good or evil. The objection argues that since reward and punishment are eschatological matters known only through revelation (sam'i), reason cannot judge them intuitively. The commentator gives two answers. First, using the Mu'tazila's own principle that justice ('adl) is rationally obligatory upon Allah, he explains that reason can independently perceive the deservingness of reward or punishment for actions, without needing physical resurrection. Spiritual resurrection is sufficient and rational. Second, he clarifies that the Mu'tazila's statement 'and some of it is necessary' is conditional: if the abode of recompense exists, then reason necessarily judges entitlement. Thus the intuitive judgment is hypothetical, not absolute. The commentator quotes Ibn Sina to differentiate between physical resurrection (known only by revelation) and spiritual resurrection (knowable by reason), showing that not all afterlife matters are exclusively revealed. The entire discussion is framed within the Hanafi usul tradition, which often accommodates rational moral evaluation (as opposed to Ash'ari occasionalism). The authority weight of this commentary is from the later fatwa/mutakhkhirin period.

Key Terms
حسن و قبح (husn wa qubh): The moral qualities of goodness and badness attributed to actions, which in this passage are claimed to be intuitively known for some actions (like beneficial truthfulness and harmful falsehood).
بدیہی (badihi): Intuitive, self-evident knowledge that does not require discursive reasoning; here applied to the knowledge of some actions' moral valences.
سمعی (sam'i): That which is known only through revealed texts (Quran and Sunnah), not through reason alone; here applied to the details of physical resurrection.
عدل (adl): Justice, defined as placing something in its proper place or delivering rights to the entitled; Mu'tazila hold it is rationally obligatory upon Allah.

The Third Category of Good and Evil: Purely Revealed Moral Values
Defines third type of husn/qubh and critiques Mu'tazilah rationalism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three categories of husn and qubh in Hanafi usul, identify the third category as purely shar'i, and refute the Mu'tazilah claim that it is rational.

Core Idea
Two identical acts cannot be distinguished by reason alone; only revelation can declare one good and the other evil.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, moral values are classified into three categories: those known by necessity (badihi), those known by rational reflection (nazari), and those known only through revelation (shar'i). The third category raises a fundamental debate: can human reason independently perceive the goodness or evil of certain acts? The Mu'tazilah claim that even these are rational after revelation unveils them. However, the Hanafi school firmly asserts that reason alone cannot differentiate between two otherwise identical acts, such as fasting on the last day of Ramadan and fasting on Eid. Only divine command determines their moral status. This passage from Sharh Muslim al-Thubut presents the Hanafi position and refutes the Mu'tazilah rationalist claim.

This passage from Sharh Muslim al-Thubut discusses the three categories of good and evil (husn and qubh) in acts. After defining the first category (known by necessity) and second (known by rational reflection), the author presents the third and final category: acts whose moral value is known only through divine revelation, not by reason. The example given is the goodness of fasting on the last day of Ramadan and the evil of fasting on the first day of Shawwal (Eid). While fasting in itself is good, the specific designation of these days as obligatory and prohibited respectively cannot be determined by reason alone; reason sees both days and both fasts as identical in essence, so any distinction must come from the Shari'ah. The author then critiques the Mu'tazilah position that even this type is rational after revelation, accusing them of bigotry. The author argues that reason cannot differentiate between these two similar acts without a preference, so the distinction is purely based on revelation. The maximum that can be said is that reason sees subduing the soul as good, which is achieved by fasting in general, but the specific connection to Ramadan is due to revelation, such as the virtue of Ramadan including the revelation of the Quran etc.

Key Terms
نظری (nazarī): Moral values that require rational reflection and deliberation to be known, such as the goodness of harmful truthfulness and the evil of beneficial lying.
شرعی (shar'ī): Moral values that cannot be known by reason at all, only through divine revelation; e.g., the goodness of fasting on the last day of Ramadan and evil of fasting on Eid.
حسن بالذات (ḥusn bi al-dhāt): Intrinsic goodness in the act itself, as opposed to goodness due to an external factor.
مقدم و تالی (muqaddam wa tālī): The antecedent and consequent in a conditional proposition; used in logical reasoning.

Commentary
This passage from the sharh of Muslim al-Thubut addresses the theological issue of jabr wa ikhtiyar (predestination and free will), which is relevant to usul al-fiqh as it underpins the validity of taklif (obligation). The author presents four madhhabs, explaining and evaluating them. The first madhhab is that of the Jahmiyyah (Jabriyyah), who deny any human power over actions, claiming complete compulsion. The author refutes this as sophistry (safsatah), arguing that it contradicts self-evident experience of choice and would nullify Shar'i obligations. The second madhhab (Mu'tazila) asserts that humans have an effective, independent power to create their own actions. The passage cuts off while explaining this view. The Hanafi position, hinted earlier, is that of kasb: the servant's created power has effect through intention, and Allah creates the act by custom. The author prefers the fourth madhhab (not fully shown), which is the Hanafi/Ash'ari via media. The discussion demonstrates the Hanafi usul principle that taklif requires a measure of human capability and choice.

کسب (kasb): The servant's direction of created power towards a firm intention; the act's acquisition.
قدرة مخلوقة (qudra makhluqa): The power created by Allah in the servant, which has effect through intention.
قصد مصمم (qasd musammam): Firm intention; the direction of power towards a specific act.
عادت (adah): Divine custom; the regular way in which Allah creates acts following the servant's intention.

The Hanafi Kasb Theory: Slave Creates the Determinate Intention
Explains the Hanafi middle path on human agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi doctrine of kasb, identify the role of the determinate intention (qasd musammam), and apply the Quranic proofs to distinguish the Hanafi position from jabr and tafwid.

Core Idea
The slave creates his own intention, not his action; this is the minimal benefit of God's creating power.

This passage from Ashraf al-Nu'ut, a commentary on Musallam al-Thubut, expounds the Hanafi position on human agency and divine power. The Hanafi school adopts a middle path between the Mu'tazili view that humans create all their actions (tafwid) and the Ash'ari/Jabri view that humans have no real power (jabr). The key is the concept of 'qasd musammam' (determinate intention), which the slave creates while Allah creates the action itself. Understanding this doctrine is essential for grasping the Hanafi foundation of moral responsibility and the validity of taklif.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
Recognize the theological-legal problem: reconciling divine sovereignty (Allah creates all) with human moral responsibility (taklif rewards/punishments). The extremes are jabr (no human power) and tafwid (full human power).
The passage begins by outlining the highest and lowest levels of efficacy from created power.
2
State the Hanafi Position
The Hanafi school holds that the slave creates his own determinate intention (qasd musammam). This is the lowest level of efficacy, avoiding tafwid (by not claiming full action creation) and jabr (by affirming real human contribution).
The author calls this an intermediary (wasita) between the two extremes.
3
Trace the Two Proofs
First proof: The benefit of God creating power (fa'idat khalq al-qudra) must be realized; if the slave had no effect, the power would be pointless. Second proof: Without the slave creating intention, taklif would be impossible or unjust (a powerless person cannot be obligated).
These are rational (aqli) proofs supported by textual evidence.
4
Analyze the Objection and Response
The author notes an objection (fihi ma fihi): how can a contingent (mumkin) create something? The response: specification of intention from all voluntary acts is not arbitrary; it is the minimal effect needed, and it is supported by Quranic verses that attribute creation to Allah and will/action to the servant.
The Quranic verses (81:29 and 37:96) are cited to show the dual attribution.
5
Synthesize the Overall Argument
The Hanafi kasb theory is a balanced theological position that preserves both divine omnipotence and human responsibility. The practical outcome is that the human's intention is his own creation, making him accountable for his choices.
This affects how Hanafi jurists understand intention in acts of worship and transactions.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Nu'ut, a commentary on Musallam al-Thubut, expounds the Hanafi doctrine of kasb (acquisition) concerning human action and divine creation. The author addresses the problem of how to reconcile divine sovereignty with human responsibility. The Hanafi position is a middle path between the Mu'tazili view of tafwid (full delegation of action creation to humans) and the Jabri/Ash'ari view of jabr (absolute compulsion). The author argues that the minimum benefit of God's creating power in the servant is that the servant creates his own determinate intention (qasd musammam). This is the lowest level of efficacy, higher levels being avoided to prevent tafwid or jabr. Two proofs are given: first, the need for a real benefit from the creation of power; second, the necessity of fair taklif (moral obligation) — without the servant creating his intention, obligation would be impossible or unjust. The author then notes an objection: how can a mere contingent (mumkin) create anything? The answer is that the specification of intention from all voluntary acts is not arbitrary but is supported by Quranic verses like 'And Allah created you and what you do' and 'And you do not will except that Allah wills', which attribute both creation to Allah and will/action to the servant. Finally, the author introduces his own preferred view (mukhtar) regarding partial volitions, but the passage is cut off before elaboration.

Key Terms
قصد مصمم (qasd musammam): Determinate, resolute intention that leads to action; the aspect of the voluntary act that the Hanafis consider to be created by the human slave.
خلق قدرت (khalq al-qudra): The creation of power (in the slave) by God — the power to act, which must yield some effect (the qasd) to be meaningful.
فائدة خلق القدرة (fa'idat khalq al-qudra): The benefit or purpose of God's creating power in the slave — identified here as the slave's efficacy in his own intention (qasd).
جبر (jabr): Compulsion — the belief that the slave has no real power or choice; all actions are directly created by God.
تفویض (tafwid): Delegation — the Mu'tazili belief that the slave has full creative power over his actions, independent of divine intervention.

The Mu'tazila Argument for Ibahah Before Revelation and Its Refutation
Explaining the Mu'tazila view of ibaha and its refutation via the purpose of creation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Mu'tazila rational proof for ibaha before revelation and evaluate the Hanafi refutation using the principle of non-futility in creation.

Core Idea
Before revelation, actions are not inherently prohibited — the default is permissibility, but creation's purpose includes testing, not just benefit.

In this lesson, we examine a fundamental usul dispute: what is the legal status of voluntary actions before any divine revelation? The Mu'tazila of Basra argued for ibaha (permissibility) based on a rational necessity to avoid absurdity in creation. We will trace their proof, see why many Hanafis initially agreed, and then analyze the Hanafi response that shifts the wisdom of creation from mere benefit to divine testing.

Guided Path
1
Understand the five-fold classification of actions based on husn and qubh
The passage opens by dividing all voluntary actions into five categories based on whether performing or abandoning involves beauty (husn) or ugliness (qubh). This framework is essential to situate the ibaha dispute.
This classification is standard in Hanafi usul; actions not comprehended by reason fall into the third category.
2
Identify the category of actions whose moral value is not perceived by reason
The second type of voluntary actions are those whose beauty or ugliness the intellect cannot ascertain. For these, before revelation, the Mu'tazila propose three views; the first is ibaha.
This category is the focus of the dispute; it includes actions like eating carrion when coerced.
3
State the Mu'tazila view: ibaha before revelation, and their rational proof
The Mu'tazila argue that all such actions are permissible before revelation. Their proof: if they were not permissible, the benefit of creation would be lost, leading to futility (عبث), which is impossible for Allah.
They assume the purpose of creation is human benefit (انتفاع).
4
Examine the Hanafi counter-argument: the implication of futility is not inevitable
The Hanafi response, cited from Sharh al-Mukhtasar, states that creation may be for testing: the servant desires the action, practices patience, and earns reward. Thus, even without permissibility, creation is not futile.
This refutation blocks the Mu'tazila's necessary implication (استلزام).
5
Evaluate the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of this dispute
The practical difference: before revelation, one may perform any action without liability; after revelation, rulings apply. The dispute affects how we understand the default legal status of unprecedent acts.
The Hanafi position is that ibaha is a default but not a rational necessity; the Mu'tazila argument is not conclusive.

Learning Objective

Why Nazm al-Quran is Not a Kashif of Divine Speech
Responds to objection that Quranic text should also be called kashif

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi argument for why the Quranic text is not considered a separate kashif (discloser) of divine speech and distinguish it from the three other sources.

Core Idea
The Quranic text is identical to the divine speech; calling it kashif would imply separation, which is impermissible.

In Hanafi usul, the three principles (Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) are called kashif — they disclose the divine address (kalam nafsi). But what about the Quranic text itself? It too discloses the divine speech. Why isn't it also called a kashif? This lesson unpacks the objection and the sophisticated Hanafi response that preserves the unique status of the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector argues: if the three principles are kashif of the divine speech, then the Quranic text (nazm al-Quran) is also a kashif, because it too discloses that speech. Why exclude it? Including it would make a fifth source.
2
Hanafi premise: kashif must be distinct
A kashif (discloser) is by definition distinct from the makshuf lahu (the thing disclosed). For example, Sunnah is distinct from the Quran it explains. So calling something a kashif implies a separation between indicator and indicated.
3
Apply to the Quranic text
The Quranic text (kalam lafzi) is so elevated and majestic that it is considered identical to the internal divine speech (kalam nafsi). There is no real separation; the indicator is essentially the indicated.
This is based on Hanafi theology: kalam lafzi perfectly reflects kalam nafsi.
4
Conclude: calling it kashif would imply separation
If we called the Quranic text a kashif, we would be implying a distinction that does not exist. That would undermine the unique, uncreated status of the Quran.
5
Note the thamarah (practical consequence)
The sources of law remain four: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas. The Quran is not a fifth source but the very embodiment of the first source. The three principles are kashif of the Quran's rulings, not the Quran itself.
