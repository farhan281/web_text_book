The Rational Obligation of Gratitude: Hanafi vs. Mu'tazila
Debate on rational obligation of gratitude with objections and responses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi argument for the rational obligation of gratitude and evaluate the Mu'tazila objection using the Hanafi responses.

Core Idea
Gratitude to Allah is rationally obligatory; sincerity negates any resemblance to mockery.

In this lesson, we delve into a foundational debate in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: Is gratitude (shukr) to Allah obligatory by reason alone, independent of revelation? The Mu'tazila raise a striking objection: human gratitude, given Allah’s infinite greatness, resembles mockery (istihza'), which is forbidden. Follow the Hanafi response, grounded in the primacy of intention and the authority of the Shari'ah itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi claim and its rational basis
Sadr al-Shariah argues that reason itself necessitates gratitude—denial of countless blessings invites punishment. Identify the logical structure: blessings + power to punish = obligation.
This is a pure rational argument (dalil aqli).
2
Extract the Mu'tazila objection and its analogy
The Mu'tazila compare the servant's gratitude to a beggar thanking a mighty king for a morsel—a gesture that actually mocks the king. Understand why they claim gratitude resembles istihza'.
The objection targets the form of the act, not the intention.
3
Analyze the first Hanafi response: intention over form
The Hanafi rebuttal rejects the major premise that resemblance to mockery is automatically forbidden. The hadith 'Allah looks at your intentions' shows that sincere gratitude is not mockery.
This is a refutation of the objection's major premise (al-kubra).
4
Analyze the second Hanafi response: impossibility of Shari'ah commanding the repugnant
Since the Shari'ah clearly commands gratitude (e.g., Qur'an 2:152, 14:7), it cannot be repugnant (qabih). How could the Lawgiver command something inherently forbidden?
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument against the objection.
5
Synthesize the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The Hanafi view affirms a pre-revelational moral framework where reason obligates gratitude; the Mu'tazila view would deny any obligation before revelation. This frames the possibility of moral knowledge outside divine commands.
This has implications for the validity of other rational duties (e.g., justice).

Position of Sadr al-Shariah: On the rational obligation of gratitude, Sadr al-Shariah argued: How can any rational person consider it permissible to deny the various types of blessings bestowed by Allah, the Owner of the Kingdom, and to show ingratitude by various denials? He who bestowed the priceless blessing of life, honored with varieties of foods, drinks, and garments, immersed in the ocean of His mercy, every moment He showers blessings beyond count. Yet if one shows extreme ingratitude and denies Him through vile denials, while He is the King of the Kingdom and Dominion, capable of severe punishment, would He not hold accountable and impose an obligation upon the servant? The conclusion is that gratitude is rationally obligatory.
His statement: 'And secondly, because it resembles mockery' – The second objection: The Mu'tazila raised two objections to the previous proof; the first has been mentioned. The second objection is expressed by the author with his words 'and secondly, because it resembles mockery'. The explanation of the objection: To express gratitude for Allah's blessings resembles mocking Allah, because relative to the true Benefactor's complete and absolute sovereignty and infinite greatness, the blessing given to the servant has no value. How can blessings given to the servant have any importance when the entire world is not even worth a mosquito's wing before the Absolute Ruler? Or because gratitude for such a blessing is not befitting the station of divinity of the true Benefactor. When the blessings upon the servant have no status relative to Allah's greatness and dominion, then the gratitude the servant offers for that blessing will not be worthy of His majesty and grandeur. In both cases, whether considering the blessing or the station of divinity, this gratitude resembles mockery. Since at that time, the rational meaning of performing gratitude is that the servant should reflect on the knowledge of Allah Almighty to know that Allah is One, Powerful, Knowing, etc. Then the servant... 
[The example of a beggar who receives a piece of bread or a drop of water from a great king, and then in grand assemblies he points to the king thanking him for that, which is not praise but blame and mockery. Similarly, gratitude to the Creator would be mockery, and mockery is forbidden, so gratitude is forbidden.]
His statement: 'And it is weak, for the consideration is...'
Response to the objection: The author gives one response to the second objection. Its conclusion is that this objection is weak and invalid. Indeed, the major premise of the objection is rejected: we do not accept that whatever resembles mockery is forbidden. Because in that court, the quantity and quality of deeds are not considered, but rather the states of the heart, sincerity, and intention are considered. Thus, in the noble hadith it is said: 'Indeed, Allah does not look at your deeds and forms, but at your intentions' or as he said. So the gratitude that emanates from the servant, if it is a valid glorification and includes sincere intention and sincerity, then it is good, not mockery. Allah Almighty says: 'And they were not commanded except to worship Allah, being sincere to Him in religion.'
His statement: 'And also, how can it be said that the Shari'ah came with obligation...'
This is the second response to the objection. Its conclusion is also that we do not accept the major premise of the proof, i.e., we do not accept that gratitude resembles mockery, because the Shari'ah came regarding the obligation of gratitude. And how can it be that the Shari'ah came with the obligation of something that resembles mockery, when resembling mockery is repugnant? And the repugnant is not obligatory but forbidden. So how could the Shari'ah make a forbidden thing obligatory? Therefore, ponder well on the soundness and precision of this discourse.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute in usul al-fiqh regarding the rational basis for the obligation of gratitude (shukr) to Allah. The Hanafi position, represented by Sadr al-Shariah, holds that gratitude is rationally obligatory (wajib aqlan). This is argued by considering that a rational person cannot deny the countless blessings of Allah and His power to punish ingratitude. The Mu'tazila object that such gratitude resembles mockery (istihza'), because Allah's greatness makes human gratitude insignificant, similar to a beggar thanking a mighty king for a morsel, which is actually derision. The Hanafi response refutes this by stating that the objection's major premise—that anything resembling mockery is forbidden—is rejected. In divine evaluation, sincerity and intention matter, not the outward form. A hadith and Quranic verse (98:5) are cited to support this. A second response argues that since the Shari'ah itself commands gratitude, it cannot be inherently repugnant; otherwise, the Shari'ah would be commanding the forbidden, which is impossible. Thus, the Hanafi position prevails.

Key Terms
شکر (shukr): Gratitude; acknowledgment of blessings with the heart, tongue, and limbs.
استہزاء (istihza'): Mockery; ridiculing someone to demean them.
وجوب عقلی (wujub aqli): Obligation based on rational necessity, independent of revelation.

Defending the Obligation of Gratitude: Refuting the Objection of Ridicule
Refuting the objection that shukr is haram because it resembles istihza.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that gratitude to Allah (shukr) is obligatory (wajib) and evaluate the objection that it resembles ridicule (istihza) by applying the usul principles of intention (niyyah) and the non-abominability of divinely commanded acts.

Core Idea
Shukr is wajib; it is not istihza because intentions matter and Sharia cannot command something qabih.

This passage from Ashraf al-Nu'ut defends the obligation of gratitude against a rational objection. The objector uses an analogy: praising a king for a tiny gift is ridicule, so praising Allah for finite blessings is also ridicule. The Hanafi response includes three arguments: actions are judged by intentions, Sharia cannot command an abominable act, and from the servant's perspective the blessings are immense.

Guided Path
1
Set up the objection
Understand the analogy: a poor man thanking a king for a morsel is ridicule. The objector analogizes this to human gratitude to Allah.
The objection is rational, not based on revealed text.
2
Analyze the first response: intention (niyyah)
The author argues that the act's moral quality depends on intention, not external form. Sincere gratitude is not ridicule.
Supported by hadith: Allah looks at your intentions.
3
Analyze the second response: Sharia's command
Since Sharia commands gratitude, it cannot be abominable (qabih). Something qabih cannot be wajib.
This is a usul principle: Sharia cannot command something inherently evil.
4
Analyze the third response: perspective of the servant
From the servant's need, the blessings are immense and sufficient, so gratitude is appropriate, not ridicule.
This counters the claim that blessings are 'tiny'.
5
Derive the usul principle
The rule: similarity to a forbidden act does not make an act forbidden if it is commanded by Sharia or done with sincere intention.
This refines the concept of 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha'.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the obligation of shukr (gratitude) to Allah. It begins with an analogy: gratitude from a creature to the Creator is compared to a poor person's gratitude to a mighty king for a small gift. If the poor person praises the king excessively for a mere piece of bread, it would be ridicule. Similarly, one might argue that our gratitude to Allah for His blessings, which are insignificant compared to His greatness, is also ridicule and thus forbidden. The author then provides three responses. First, actions are judged by intentions; if the gratitude is sincere and intended to honor Allah, it is not ridicule. This is supported by the hadith 'Allah does not look at your actions but your intentions' and the Quranic command to worship with sincerity. Second, the Sharia has commanded gratitude, so it cannot be something abominable (qabih). Something abominable cannot be made obligatory. Third, Fazil Khayrabadi argues that from the servant's perspective, the blessings are actually immense and sufficient, so gratitude is fitting and not ridicule. The key point is that the size of the blessing relative to the Giver is irrelevant; what matters is the proportion to the recipient's need and the sincerity of the gratitude.

Key Terms
شکر (shukr): Gratitude, thanking Allah for His blessings by appropriate words and deeds, here argued to be obligatory and not resembling ridicule.
استہزاء (istihza): Ridicule, mockery. The objection claims shukr is istihza because the blessing is minuscule compared to Allah's greatness.
قبیح (qabih): Abominable, ugly (in a moral/legal sense). Here used to argue that what is qabih cannot be wajib.
نیت (niyyah): Intention, which determines the moral and legal quality of an act according to the hadith cited.

Original Ruling of Actions: Ibaha vs. Hazr
Explaining the unanimous agreement on the pre-eternity of rulings but unknown specifics before revelation, and the disagreement on the default ruling of actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the default ruling of actions before revelation and explain the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Actions are permissible by default until a prohibition is proven — the Hanafi principle of ibaha.

This lesson tackles a foundational usul al-fiqh question: What is the ruling of an action before any revelation comes? The passage explains that everyone agrees there is a pre-eternal ruling in Allah's knowledge, but the debate is about the default classification for humans. The Hanafi school, followed by most, holds that actions are originally permissible (ibaha), while the Shafi'is and others hold they are originally prohibited (hazr). Understanding this principle affects how a mujtahid approaches cases where textual evidence is absent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Point of Consensus
According to the text, all schools agree that every action has a pre-eternal (qadim) ruling in Allah's knowledge, but humans may not know it before prophethood. This is explained differently by the Mu'tazila (essential rational knowledge limited) and others (kalam nafsi only manifesting through revelation).
The consensus is on the pre-eternity of the ruling itself, not on human access to it.
2
Understand the Disagreement on the Default Ruling
The main ikhtilaf among Ahl al-Sunna is: what is the original ruling (asl) of actions before revelation? Most Hanafis say ibaha (permissibility); Shafi'is and others say hazr (prohibition). Sadr al-Islam proposed a middle ground: ibaha for wealth, hazr for lives.
This disagreement is based on scriptural evidence (adilla sam'iyya), not pure reason.
3
Analyze the Evidence for Each Position
Hanafis point to general verses of permissibility (e.g., Quran 2:29, 'created for you all that is on earth') and the principle that benefit is the default. Shafi'is cite hadith on caution (e.g., 'leave what causes you doubt') and argue that the mind cannot determine permissibility without revelation.
The text does not quote the specific dalil, but the madhhab dispute map provides the typical arguments.
4
Evaluate the Thamarah al-Khilaf (Practical Consequence)
The difference affects ijtihad: for a new matter without clear text, a Hanafi jurist defaults to permissible; a Shafi'i defaults to prohibited. Example: eating an unknown animal — Hanafi says halal until proven haram, Shafi'i says haram until proven halal.
This shifts the burden of proof in legal reasoning.
5
Reflect on the Author's Comment
The passage ends with 'وفيه مافيه' (there is something in it), indicating the author finds weakness in the view that the ruling is determined after revelation by scriptural evidence alone, perhaps due to the complexity of the issue.
The author does not explicitly choose one position, leaving room for scholarly discussion.

The Agreement on Ignorance Before the Messengers
Explaining the consensus on possible ignorance of some rulings before revelation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the point of agreement among all Islamic schools regarding the knowability of rulings before the sending of messengers and identify the logical basis for this agreement.

Core Idea
All schools agree that at least some rulings may be unknown before revelation, but they differ on the scope.

This lesson opens the discussion on the epistemological status of rulings prior to the sending of messengers (ba'thah). The text establishes a key point of consensus: despite their disagreements, all Islamic legal schools—whether they see rulings as rational or purely revelatory—acknowledge that it is possible for a servant not to know some specific ruling before revelation. This agreement is derived from logical quantification: both universal and particular negations entail that at least some rulings are unknown. Understanding this shared ground is essential before exploring the deeper dispute about whether all or only some rulings are unknowable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common ground

The author uses the logical rule that negation of all (salb kulli) implies negation of some (salb juz'i). Both those who say no rulings are known and those who say some are unknown share at least the conclusion that some specific rulings are unknown.

The reason is that although the cause of the ruling is the eternal inner speech (kalam nafsi), the ruling's manifestation from inner speech occurs only when inner speech relates to the ruling or to the addressed issue. And it is clear that this relation (ta'alluq) is temporal. Since inner speech's relation to the ruling occurs only after ba'thah, and ba'thah is temporal, therefore before ba'thah, according to us, there is no ruling. So before ba'thah, there is no harm in doing or leaving actions.

So according to us, there is no restriction in doing or leaving actions.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on a matn statement from 'Musallam al-Thubut' concerning the knowability of rulings before the sending of messengers (qabla al-ba'thah). The commentator first establishes that all Islamic schools—whether they consider rulings rational (as the Mu'tazilah do) or purely shar'i (as Ahl al-Sunnah do)—agree that it is possible that some specific rulings are not known before revelation. This agreement is based on logical quantification: both universal and particular negations entail that at least some rulings are unknown. The Ash'aris and majority Hanafis hold that no rulings are known before ba'thah, while the Mu'tazilah and some Hanafis hold that only some rulings may be unknown. The passage then explains the reasons for this non-knowledge according to two groups: (1) The Mu'tazilah argue that although actions have intrinsic good and bad (husn wa qubh), reason cannot always perceive the cause of that good or bad; hence the ruling (based on good/bad) remains unknown in such cases (e.g., the obligation of specific fasts). (2) Non-Mu'tazilah (Ahl al-Sunnah) argue that the cause of rulings is Allah's eternal inner speech (kalam nafsi), but the ruling becomes specified only when this speech relates to particular actions or individuals, and this relation (ta'alluq) is temporal—occurring only after ba'thah. Therefore, before ba'thah, no individual rulings exist, so there is no legal responsibility. The commentator then elaborates on the relational nature of divine attributes (e.g., will and speech) to show that temporal relations entail temporal rulings. Finally, he begins addressing an objection regarding the dispute among Ahl al-Sunnah about the origin of actions. The passage is crucial for understanding the Hanafi-Ash'ari position on the epistemological status of rulings prior to revelation and reconciles the apparent disagreement between rational and scriptural approaches.

Key Terms
حكم (hukm): The address of the Lawgiver relating to the actions of the servants, such as obligation, prohibition, etc.
بعثة (ba'thah): The sending of messengers (rusul) by Allah to convey divine commands.
حسن و قبح (husn wa qubh): The intrinsic goodness or badness of actions, which the Mu'tazilah consider rationally knowable.
كلام نفسي (kalam nafsi): The eternal attribute of speech in Allah's essence, which is the source of rulings.
تعلق (ta'alluq): The relation of an eternal attribute (e.g., will, speech) to its object; such relations are temporal (hadith).
سلب جزئی (salb juz'i): Particular negation; negating a predicate for some members of a set.
سلب کلی (salb kulli): Universal negation; negating a predicate for all members of a set.

Rationale for Ignorance: Mu'tazilah vs. Hanafi-Ash'ari
Explaining the two rationales for why some rulings are unknown before messengers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Mu'tazilah and Hanafi/Ash'ari reasons for why some rulings are unknown before the sending of messengers and compare their epistemological foundations.

Core Idea
Mu'tazilah base ignorance on the subtlety of rational good/bad; Hanafis base it on the temporal relation of eternal divine speech to specific rulings.

Having established the shared agreement that some rulings may be unknown before revelation, this lesson explores two distinct rationales for that ignorance. The Mu'tazilah argue from the intrinsic goodness or badness (husn wa qubh) of actions: while some good/bad is rationally perceptible, the cause of goodness in certain actions is too subtle for reason, making the ruling dependent on that cause unknowable. The Hanafis and Ash'aris, by contrast, ground all rulings in Allah's eternal inner speech (kalam nafsi), which becomes particularized only when a temporal relation (ta'alluq) occurs between that speech and specific actions—a relation that only arises after the sending of messengers. Thus before ba'thah, no individual rulings exist at all, and hence no knowledge of them is possible. Understanding these two frameworks is key to grasping the deep epistemological divergence between rationalist and revelatory approaches in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Mu'tazilah reason
The Mu'tazilah hold that actions have intrinsic husn wa qubh that can be known by reason. However, for some actions, the cause (illah) of that husn or qubh is too subtle for reason to apprehend. Since the ruling is based on husn/qubh, if the cause is unknown, the ruling is unknown. Example: why fasting on 29 Ramadan is obligatory and on 1 Shawwal is prohibited.
Key phrase: 'ba'd af'aal aise hain ke uss afaal ke husn wa qubh ki illat ko aql se idrak nahi kiya ja sakta'.
2
Understand the Hanafi-Ash'ari reason
The cause (mu'aththir) of rulings is Allah's eternal inner speech (kalam nafsi). But this speech becomes a specific ruling only when it relates (ta'alluq) to a particular action or person. This relation is temporal (hadith) and occurs only after ba'thah. Therefore, before ba'thah, there is no relation, no specific ruling, and hence no knowledge of rulings. Consequently, before ba'thah, there is no legal obligation (taklif).
Key phrase: 'kalam nafsi se hukm ka zuhur us waqt hota hai jab kalam nafsi ka ta'alluq hukm se ya mukhatab bih se hota hai'.
3
Compare the two frameworks
Identify the foundational difference: Mu'tazilah rely on rational perception of good/bad (even if sometimes limited); Hanafis/Ash'aris rely on divine speech and its temporal relations. Both acknowledge ignorance, but for different reasons.
The thamarah al-khilaf: Mu'tazilah believe some acts are rationally obligatory/prohibited before revelation; Hanafis/Ash'aris say no act has any ruling before revelation.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on a matn statement from 'Musallam al-Thubut' concerning the knowability of rulings before the sending of messengers (qabla al-ba'thah). The commentator first establishes that all Islamic schools—whether they consider rulings rational (as the Mu'tazilah do) or purely shar'i (as Ahl al-Sunnah do)—agree that it is possible that some specific rulings are not known before revelation. This agreement is based on logical quantification: both universal and particular negations entail that at least some rulings are unknown. The Ash'aris and majority Hanafis hold that no rulings are known before ba'thah, while the Mu'tazilah and some Hanafis hold that only some rulings may be unknown. The passage then explains the reasons for this non-knowledge according to two groups: (1) The Mu'tazilah argue that although actions have intrinsic good and bad (husn wa qubh), reason cannot always perceive the cause of that good or bad; hence the ruling (based on good/bad) remains unknown in such cases (e.g., the obligation of specific fasts). (2) Non-Mu'tazilah (Ahl al-Sunnah) argue that the cause of rulings is Allah's eternal inner speech (kalam nafsi), but the ruling becomes specified only when this speech relates to particular actions or individuals, and this relation (ta'alluq) is temporal—occurring only after ba'thah. Therefore, before ba'thah, no individual rulings exist, so there is no legal responsibility. The commentator then elaborates on the relational nature of divine attributes (e.g., will and speech) to show that temporal relations entail temporal rulings. Finally, he begins addressing an objection regarding the dispute among Ahl al-Sunnah about the origin of actions. The passage is crucial for understanding the Hanafi-Ash'ari position on the epistemological status of rulings prior to revelation and reconciles the apparent disagreement between rational and scriptural approaches.

Key Terms
حكم (hukm): The address of the Lawgiver relating to the actions of the servants, such as obligation, prohibition, etc.
بعثة (ba'thah): The sending of messengers (rusul) by Allah to convey divine commands.
حسن و قبح (husn wa qubh): The intrinsic goodness or badness of actions, which the Mu'tazilah consider rationally knowable.
كلام نفسي (kalam nafsi): The eternal attribute of speech in Allah's essence, which is the source of rulings.
تعلق (ta'alluq): The relation of an eternal attribute (e.g., will, speech) to its object; such relations are temporal (hadith).
علت (illah): Cause; in this context, the cause of goodness/badness in actions.

The Ibahah vs Hurmah Dispute and Its Resolution
Explaining the dispute and its resolution via continuous prophethood.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi dispute on the default ruling of actions before revelation (ibaha vs hurmah), identify the key proofs, and apply the premise of continuous prophethood to resolve the debate.

Core Idea
Continuous prophethood dissolves the debate: no era was without Shariah.

This passage from 'Ashraf al-Nu'ut' tackles a foundational usul al-fiqh question: What is the default ruling for actions before divine revelation? Two positions exist among Ahl al-Sunnah: ibaha (permissibility) and hurmah (prohibition). The text presents an objection, a first response, a critique from the hashiya, and a final resolution based on the continuity of prophethood from Adam to the final Prophet.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
What exactly is the dispute about? The default ruling (hukm) of actions before the advent of Shariah—is it ibaha or hurmah? Note that the objection argues that before revelation there is no ruling at all, so claiming a default contradicts that.
The objection sets the stage for the responses that follow.
2
Trace the first response
How does the matn answer the objection? It claims that the transmitted disagreement is actually post-revelation, about actions on which the Shariah is silent. Scriptural evidences indicate permissibility or prohibition for such actions.
This response reinterprets the disagreement as about interpreting silence, not about a pre-Shariah state.
3
Examine the hashiya critique
What weakness does the hashiya point out? The scholars' own language (e.g., 'original permissibility/prohibition' and not calling its lifting naskh) indicates they consider the disagreement pre-revelation.
The author's 'fihi ma fihi' hints at this weakness.
4
Follow the final resolution
What premise resolves the issue? The premise of continuous prophethood: from Adam to Muhammad, every era had a prophet and a Shariah. Thus there never was a 'pre-Shariah' period.
Quranic verses (Fatir:24, Qiyama:36) support this.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf
What is the practical difference? If one accepts the premise, the entire dispute becomes moot—actions are always under some Shariah. The practical implication affects how a faqih approaches a new issue: there is no true 'silence', only continuity.
The final resolution does not choose ibaha or hurmah but removes the question entirely.

Commentary
This passage from 'Ashraf al-Nu'ut Sharh Muslim al-Thubut' addresses a fundamental usul al-fiqh dispute: What is the default ruling (hukm) for actions before the advent of Shariah? The author presents an objection first: if there is no ruling before revelation, then claiming permissibility (ibaha) or prohibition (hurmah) for actions before revelation contradicts that. The matn's response argues that the 'transmitted disagreement' among Ahl al-Sunnah is actually post-revelation—it is about actions regarding which the Shariah is silent. Based on scriptural evidence, some scholars say such actions are permissible, others say prohibited. So the disagreement is over interpreting the Shariah's silence, not about a pre-Shariah state. The hashiya (marginal gloss) then critiques this response: from the scholars' own words (e.g., 'original permissibility' and not calling its lifting abrogation), it appears they consider the disagreement pre-revelation. The author's 'fihi ma fihi' hints at this weakness. Finally, some great scholars provide a more robust answer with a premise: there never was a time without a Shariah (from Adam to the final Prophet). Since every era had some prophetic law, the very question of a default before revelation becomes moot—actions were always under some divine ruling. Thus the dispute is resolved by stating that the concept of a 'pre-Shariah' state is theoretical and not actual. The passage ends mid-sentence, likely continuing the argument.

Original Permissibility vs Prohibition: The Fatrat Resolution
Explaining the dispute and reconciling via the concept of fatrat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on original permissibility (ibaha asliyya) during fatrat, distinguish it from the view of original prohibition, and identify the legal basis for each.

Core Idea
Original permissibility is not about a lawless pre-Shari'ah era; it applies when ignorance excuses during fatrat.

This lesson examines a foundational dispute in usul al-fiqh: what is the original ruling of actions before Shari'ah addresses them? The passage clarifies that the disagreement is not about a time devoid of all divine guidance, but about periods of fatrat (intermission) when Shari'ah rulings become obliterated. We will explore the Hanafi/Shafi'i view that ignorance is an excuse, leading to original permissibility, and contrast it with the view that caution demands prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Dispute
Read the passage to understand that the disagreement is about the ruling of actions during a period when Shari'ah rulings are not accessible, not before any Shari'ah. Note that the author argues that there is never a complete absence of Shari'ah; rather, the discussion concerns fatrat.
Focus on the phrase 'زمان الفترة' (period of intermission).
2
Analyze the Hanafi View (Ibaha Asliyya)
Extract the Hanafi position: during fatrat, ignorance of rulings is an excuse, so all actions are permissible. Identify the proofs cited: Quranic verses about constant guidance (35:24, 75:36) and the principle that ignorance (jahl) is an excuse.
The passage mentions 'أكثر حنفية وشافعية' (most Hanafis and Shafi'is).
3
Examine the Opposing View (Hurma Asliyya)
Understand the opposing argument: when rulings are effaced, halal and haram become mixed, so caution requires assuming prohibition. Note that this view often restricts prohibition to non-essential matters.
The passage refers to 'كثير من علماء الأصوليين' (many usul scholars).
4
Explore Sadr al-Islam's Detailed Position
Learn the third view that distinguishes between actions related to lives (hurma) and those related to property (ibaha), because the prohibition of killing is an absolute that is never abrogated.
This shows a nuanced approach within the Hanafi school.
5
Synthesize the Thamara (Practical Consequence)
Apply the dispute to a scenario: During a fatrat period, if a person consumes doubtful wealth, the Hanafi view holds him blameless (ibaha asliyya), while the opposing view would consider it sinful unless proven permissible.
This demonstrates the real impact of the theoretical difference.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the dispute among usul scholars regarding the original ruling of actions before the advent of Shari'ah. The author first presents the claim that the disagreement is about the period before Shari'ah, and that the removal of original permissibility or prohibition is not considered abrogation because Shari'ah did not address those states. Then an objection is raised that the disagreement actually pertains to after the advent of Shari'ah. To resolve this, the author provides a lengthy response with a preamble. The preamble establishes that from Adam to Muhammad (peace be upon them), there has never been a time without a Prophet and a Shari'ah; every era had a divine law. Thus, all actions were always subject to Shari'ah rulings, not left without rule. Consequently, the disagreement cannot be about a period before any Shari'ah. Instead, it is about periods of fatrat (intermission) when the rulings of a past Shari'ah become obliterated due to neglect, and people become ignorant of the actual rulings. The passage then explains two positions: (1) That during such fatrat, ignorance is an excuse, so original permissibility (ibaha asliyya) applies, which is the view of most Hanafis and Shafi'is. (2) If ignorance is not an excuse, then out of caution, all things are deemed prohibited (hurma asliyya), especially for non-essential things. The author also mentions a third detailed view attributed to Sadr al-Islam, who distinguishes between matters of life (anfus) and property, ruling prohibition for the former and permissibility for the latter. Throughout, the passage clarifies that 'original permissibility' is not real but a concession due to the absence of hardship in that context. This is a classic usul al-fiqh discussion about the epistemological state of rulings before clear revelation.

Key Terms
الإباحة الأصلية (al-ibāḥa al-aṣliyya): The default ruling that things are permissible due to the absence of a Shari'ah ruling, particularly during fatrat periods when rulings are obliterated.
الحرمة الأصلية (al-ḥurma al-aṣliyya): The default ruling that things are prohibited because when Shari'ah rulings are effaced, caution requires avoiding all things until proven permissible.
زمان الفترة (zamān al-fatrat): The period of intermission between two prophets when the previous Shari'ah has become obliterated and the next Shari'ah has not yet arrived or been fully conveyed.
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a Shari'ah ruling by a later Shari'ah; not applicable to removing original permissibility/prohibition because those are not established by Shari'ah address.

Refuting Original Prohibition: Hanafi Rational Argument for Ibaha
Refutation of Mu'tazilite hazr using rational argument and analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rational argument (shukr al-mun'im) to refute the Mu'tazilite claim of original prohibition and explain the qiyas used in the refutation.

Core Idea
Before revelation, acts are permissible by default; reason and thanking the Benefactor support ibaha, not hazr.

How do we know the default ruling of actions before divine revelation? The Mu'tazilites of Baghdad argued that all acts are originally prohibited because everything belongs to Allah and using His property without permission is unauthorized. The Hanafi school countered with a powerful rational argument: just as we may benefit from a neighbor's wall for shade without explicit permission (provided no harm), so too may we benefit from created things. This lesson unpacks that rebuttal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Opposing Positions
First, clearly state the Hanafi view (ibaha asliyya) and the Mu'tazilite view (hazr asli). Understand what each school claims as the default ruling for human actions before any revelation.
The Mu'tazilite view here is from the Baghdad school; some Hanafis and Shafi'is also held it.
2
Analyze the Mu'tazilite Argument
Examine the Mu'tazilite reasoning: all things are Allah's property; acting without permission is unauthorized; therefore, original prohibition is necessary to avoid unauthorized use.
They rely on a principle of property rights.
3
Understand the Hanafi Rebuttal of Futility
The text first answers a potential objection: if actions are not originally permissible, would creation be futile? The Hanafi response: no, because trial and reward are the wisdom of creation.
This rebuttal is from Ibn Hajib and al-Qadi al-'Adud.
4
Examine the Core Hanafi Proof: Qiyas with Shade and Light
The Hanafi position is supported by a rational analogy: just as one may benefit from a neighbor's shade or lamplight without permission (since no harm), likewise one may benefit from created things without explicit permission. This shows original prohibition is not required.
This analogy is cited from the earlier discussion on shukr al-mun'im.
5
Derive the Thamarah al-Khilaf (Practical Consequence)
The dispute has a concrete impact: for the Hanafis, any action is allowed unless prohibited by revelation; for the Mu'tazilites, actions are forbidden unless permitted. This affects how jurists approach new issues (nawazil).
The Hanafi view (ibaha) is the relied-upon position in the school.

The Ijmali/Tafsili Distinction: Resolving an Objection to Mu'tazilite Rational Rulings
Usul dispute about the coherence of Mu'tazilite rational rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ijmali/tafsili distinction to evaluate and resolve apparent contradictions in rational legal rulings.

Core Idea
General rational rulings only require general knowledge, not detailed moral perception.

This passage presents a sophisticated epistemological argument. Critics argue that the Mu'tazilite claim of original permissibility (ibaha) or prohibition (hazr) for acts whose moral quality reason cannot perceive is contradictory. The author's response relies on a crucial distinction between general (ijmali) and detailed (tafsili) knowledge. While this debate belongs to kalam, its analytical structure is essential for understanding how Hanafi usul al-fiqh evaluates rational arguments and employs analogous distinctions in deriving rulings from uncertain evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Positions
First, recognize the Mu'tazilite position: for acts in the second category (where reason cannot perceive good/evil), they assign a rational ruling of ibaha or hazr. The objector claims this is contradictory because assigning a ruling implies knowledge of moral quality.
2
Understand the Objection's Logic
The objector reasons: if reason cannot know the moral aspect (jihah al-husn wa al-qubh) of an act, then no rational ruling can be given. Claiming ibaha or hazr requires that the act's moral quality be known, which contradicts the premise.
3
Distinguish Ijmali vs. Tafsili Knowledge
The answer introduces two types of knowledge: ijmali (general) knowledge that covers all acts under one ruling without distinguishing each act's moral aspect, and tafsili (detailed) knowledge that specifies each act's good or evil. The Mu'tazilites do not claim tafsili knowledge for the second category; they only have ijmali knowledge.
This distinction allows a single ruling for all acts without needing to know each act's specific moral quality.
4
Apply the Distinction
The contradiction is resolved: negating tafsili knowledge does not negate ijmali knowledge. Thus, saying 'all acts in this category are permissible' (or prohibited) is consistent with not knowing each act's detailed moral aspect, because the ruling is based on general consideration, not detailed perception.
This shows how a general rational ruling can coexist with an admission of limited moral knowledge.

Commentary
This passage presents a perceived contradiction in the Mu'tazilite position and its resolution. The objection states that Mu'tazilites claim that reason cannot determine the moral quality (jihah) of acts in the second category, yet they assign a rational ruling of ibaha or hazr. This seems contradictory because assigning a ruling implies knowing the moral quality. The answer, based on the author's explanation, distinguishes between detailed (tafsili) and general (ijmali) knowledge. The Mu'tazilites do not claim detailed knowledge of each act's moral aspect, but they can have a general ruling (ibaha or hazr) based on overall rational consideration. For example, reason can determine that all acts are either permissible or prohibited in general without specifying each act's good or evil. This is like knowing the shape of something through analogy without perceiving it directly.

Key Terms
علم اجمالی (ilm ijmali): General knowledge that encompasses all acts under a single ruling without distinguishing individual moral aspects.
علم تفصیلی (ilm tafsili): Detailed knowledge that identifies the specific moral quality (good/evil) of each individual act.
جہت حسن و قبح (jihah al-husn wa al-qubh): The aspect of goodness or evilness in an act that reason may or may not perceive.

The Mu'tazilite Objection and the Distinction Between Ijmali and Tafsili Knowledge
Presents and responds to objections regarding Mu'tazilite ibaha/nazar theory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Mu'tazilite objection to reason-based rulings for morally imperceptible acts and evaluate the Hanafi response using the distinction between total (ijmali) and detailed (tafsili) knowledge.

Core Idea
Total knowledge suffices for a general ruling; detailed knowledge is not always required.

This passage presents a key debate in usul al-fiqh: can reason declare a ruling for acts whose moral quality it cannot perceive? The Mu'tazilites argue that for such acts, reason is silent. The matn (Ibn 'Abd al-Shakur) defends the Mu'tazilite position that reason can still declare the act permissible (ibaha) or subject to consideration (nazar) by appealing to the distinction between total and detailed knowledge. However, the commentator points out that this move fails to avoid a deeper problem: the conjunction of contradictory rulings in the same act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mu'tazilite position
Recognize that the Mu'tazilites classify acts into those whose moral quality reason can perceive and those it cannot. For the latter, they say reason has no ruling, but the matn attributes to them the statements of ibaha and nazar.
This apparent contradiction is the basis of the first objection.
2
Understand the first objection
The objection states: if reason cannot perceive the good or bad aspect of an act, how can it declare it permissible (ibaha) or subject to consideration (nazar)? That would require knowledge of the moral aspect, which is assumed absent.
The objection is that ibaha/nazar and lack of moral knowledge are contradictory.
3
Analyze the matn's response: the ijmali/tafsili distinction
The matn argues that the assumption is that reason lacks detailed knowledge (tafsili) of each act's moral aspect, but it can have total knowledge (ijmali) of all such acts—a single general ruling. The syllogism analogy is given: the conclusion is implicitly known in the major premise.
Ijmali knowledge does not require specifying each item; it is sufficient for a general ruling.
4
Examine the second objection
The commentator notes that even if the first objection is resolved, a deeper problem remains: if God has a revealed ruling (e.g., obligation) for an act, and reason declares it permissible, then the same act would have two contradictory rulings (obligation and permissibility) in reality (nafs al-amr). This is impossible.
The ijmali/tafsili distinction does not remove contradiction from the act itself; it only concerns the cause of knowledge.
5
Evaluate the matn's defense
The matn's response only addresses the epistemological consistency of reason giving a ruling, not the ontological coexistence of contradictory rulings. The commentator leaves this unresolved, highlighting a limitation of the argument.
This illustrates that a valid response to one objection may not satisfy another angle.

[This is from the commentary on the passage where] an objection of contradiction arises on both mentioned statements, but the author thinks this objection will not occur. The objection and then the author's answer are as follows. 
The objection: This speech is based on the Mu'tazilite school, and their school is that the second category of voluntary actions is that reason cannot perceive their good or bad aspect. The implication is that in these actions reason has no ruling. So when rationally there can be no ruling, then how can the statement of rational permissibility or rational consideration be correct? Because the act is either permissible or considered, then its good or bad aspect must be perceived by reason, and this contradicts the assumption that its good and bad aspects are not rationally perceptible. Therefore, I say that the statement of permissibility and consideration together with the assumption is a statement combining contradictions. 
His words: 'Because the assumption is that there is no knowledge of the cause of the ruling in detail etc.' Through this expression, the author of the matn responds to the objection raised against the Mu'tazila's two statements of permissibility and consideration. 
The answer: The summary is that knowledge is of two types: total (ijmali) and detailed (tafsili). Total knowledge is that one knowledge and one ruling of all things and actions is obtained in a total manner such that the knowledge and ruling of one thing or action cannot be distinguished from another. Detailed knowledge is that the knowledge and ruling of each thing and each action is obtained in detail such that the knowledge and ruling of each thing and each action is distinguished and specified from another. 
So in the preceding it was assumed that the good and bad aspect of each thing and each action cannot be perceived by reason in a detailed way, such that reason could know each ruling of each action separately. Whereas here it is stated that reason knows only one ruling – either permissibility or consideration – of all actions in a total manner. In short, where it is said that reason cannot perceive the good and bad aspect, that is a negation of detailed knowledge; and where it is said that there is permissibility or consideration, that is an affirmation of total knowledge. And the ruling of permissibility or consideration based on total knowledge is not incompatible with not knowing the good and bad aspect of each action in detail. It is possible that something is not known in detail but is known in total, like when one forms a syllogism: the conclusion contains the major premise implicitly, as in 'The world is changing and everything changing is originated', the conclusion 'The world is originated' was already known in total from our statement 'Everything changing is originated', because 'The world is originated' is an instance of 'Everything changing is originated'. 
His words: 'I say that an objection arises against them: it necessitates the permissibility of description...' Through this expression, the author means that the mentioned objection does not occur against the statement of permissibility and consideration; however, from another perspective, an objection of necessary conjunction of contradictories arises against the proponents of permissibility and consideration, and in responding to it, the distinction between total and detailed will not be beneficial. 
The objection: The conjunction of contradictories is necessitated as follows: upon accepting the original statement of permissibility and original statement of consideration, it becomes permissible for a single act to be described with two contradictory rulings in reality, i.e., it would be permissible for a single act to have two rulings, e.g., obligation and prohibition, or obligation and permissibility, in the real state of affairs. For it is assumed that due to ignorance of the specific aspect of a particular act, the ruling of that act is unknown. In this case, it is possible that in reality and externally, for example, one ruling of obligation is established for the act; then the proponents of permissibility and proponents of consideration additionally establish a ruling of permissibility or prohibition, etc. Thus, in the real state of affairs, two rulings – a ruling of obligation and a ruling of permissibility, or a ruling of obligation and a ruling of prohibition – will coexist with the single act. This is the conjunction of contradictories because obligation and permissibility, or obligation and prohibition, are contradictory, and conjunction of contradictories is impossible. Hence the ruling of permissibility and prohibition is impossible. 
As for showing the distinction between total and detailed to deflect this objection, it is not beneficial because the difference in the cause does not eliminate contradiction. That is because the distinction between total and detailed lies in the cause of the knowledge of the ruling, not in the locus of the ruling (the act). Since here the conjunction of contradictory rulings – obligation and prohibition, or obligation and permissibility – is necessitated in the very act, which is the locus of all rulings, not in the cause of the ruling; therefore the contradiction cannot be removed by showing the distinction between total and detailed.

Commentary

The author (matn) defends the Mu'tazilite claim that for such acts, reason can still declare them permissible (ibaha) or subject to consideration (nazar). The first objection says this is contradictory: if reason cannot know the moral quality, how can it give a ruling? The matn responds by distinguishing total (ijmali) knowledge from detailed (tafsili) knowledge. Reason may not know each act's moral aspect in detail, but it can know that all such acts have a single total ruling (e.g., permissibility) by a general principle. The analogy of a syllogism is given: the conclusion is implicitly known in the major premise. The second objection argues that this distinction does not solve the problem because it allows contradictory rulings (God's revealed ruling vs. human rational ruling) to coexist in the same act. The commentator notes that the distinction between total and detailed does not remove contradiction from the act itself. This passage reflects the Hanafi usul school's engagement with Mu'tazilite thought, where Ibn 'Abd al-Shakur (the matn author) is defending a particular Mu'tazilite view while the commentator clarifies the limitations of the defense.

نظر (nazar): Consideration or reflection; here it implies a provisional ruling of awaiting revelation (tawaqquf) or rational consideration.

Resolving the Contradiction: Two Rulings on One Act
Dispute rebutting the objection of contradictory rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi position that a single act can have two contradictory rulings by distinguishing between the real order (nafs al-amr) and presumptive or summary rulings.

Core Idea
Two rulings on one act are possible when viewed from different perspectives: real order vs. presumptive assessment.

A common objection in usul al-fiqh is that a single act cannot be both permissible and prohibited simultaneously, as that would violate logic. This passage addresses that objection by revealing that the two rulings belong to different levels of analysis. One ruling is the actual divine decree (nafs al-amr), known only to Allah, while the other is a presumptive ruling (hukm zanni or hukm ijmali) based on human ijtihad or general principles before revelation. Thus, there is no real contradiction.

This objection is based on a strict logical principle.
2

The Hanafi position is that the two rulings exist from different perspectives. One is in the real order (nafs al-amr), the other is in presumption (zann) or as a summary ruling (hukm ijmali). No combination of contradictories occurs because the perspectives differ.
The phrase 'فتأمل' in the text hints at this resolution.

The passage explains that the act can be described with two rulings: one in the real order (the actual divine rule) and another in the presumption of those who rule on the act (e.g., deeming it permissible or prohibited).
This distinction avoids logical conflict.

Permissibility (ibaha) means absence of restriction (adam al-haraj), which does not contradict obligation (wujub). So even if one perspective says permissible and another says obligatory, no contradiction arises because the concepts are not opposites.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute analyzing the third Mu'tazilite position (tawaqquf) on the legal status of actions before revelation. The author begins by defining tawaqquf, stating it means either the absence of a ruling or that the specific ruling is unknown. He attributes this view not only to some Mu'tazila but also to major Ahl al-Sunnah figures like Abu Mansur al-Maturidi, the author of al-Hidayah (al-Marghinani), and even earlier Sufis. He also notes that according to some, this was the position of Abu Hanifa's companions and the school of Ahl al-Sunnah. A second interpretation reinforces this. Finally, a 'latifa' points out an apparent contradiction in the matn's reasoning: earlier he criticized the first two Mu'tazilite views for implying contradictory rulings, but now he himself uses the distinction between specific and general to allow some knowledge, which seems inconsistent. The matn's marginal note resolves this by showing the two objections are different in kind.

The Distinct Objection Against the Tawaqquf School
Defending tawaqquf by distinguishing its claim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why the objection against the Mutazilite tawaqquf school is fundamentally different from the objection against the ibahah and hazr schools, and explain how the distinction between universal and specific knowledge resolves the apparent self-contradiction in the author's argument.

Core Idea
Tawaqquf denies knowledge of the specific ruling, not the universal – thus no contradiction with earlier refutation.

This passage addresses a subtle point in usul al-fiqh: the status of actions before revelation (qabl al-wurud). The author earlier refuted two Mutazilite schools – ibahah (universal permissibility) and hazr (universal prohibition) – by showing they lead to impossible contradictions when revelation arrives. But then a third school, tawaqquf (suspension of judgment), is introduced. An observant reader might think the author is contradicting himself by using a distinction (general vs. specific) that he earlier rejected. This passage resolves that tension by clarifying exactly what each school claims.

Guided Path
1
Recall the first two Mutazilite schools and their flaw
Ibahah says all actions are permissible before revelation; hazr says all are prohibited. Both impose a single universal ruling on every action. This leads to a logical impossibility: when revelation comes, a single action would simultaneously be, say, obligatory (by revelation) and permissible (by universal rule) – a contradiction that the general/specific distinction cannot remove.
The distinction fails because the universal rule is absolute, not qualified.
2
Identify the third school's claim
Tawaqquf holds that before revelation, we have no knowledge of the specific ruling (hukm makhsus) of any action when considered in its particularity. This is not a universal rule but an admission of ignorance about the specific aspect.
They do not deny that knowledge can be attained through a universal principle (qaidah kulliyyah).
3
Understand the apparent objection against the author
The author earlier rejected the general/specific distinction as useless in refuting ibahah/hazr. Now he uses it to defend tawaqquf. This looks like self-contradiction.
The objection hinges on whether the distinction works in both contexts.
4
See why the objection against tawaqquf is different
The first two schools assign a single, universal ruling (all actions either permissible or all prohibited). This universal claim conflicts with particular rulings from revelation. Tawaqquf makes no universal claim; it only says the specific ruling of each action is unknown. There is no universal rule to conflict with revelation, so the general/specific distinction harmonizes: lacking knowledge of the specific does not undermine knowing through a universal principle.
Thus, the author's use of the distinction is consistent and necessary.
5
Appreciate the rational basis
The commentary and marginal note explain that the intellect cannot perceive the specific cause ('illah makhsusah) of each action, but can grasp universal principles of good and evil. Therefore, a real ruling (hukm waqi'i) can be derived from a universal principle without awaiting revelation, avoiding the contradiction that plagued the first two schools.
This aligns with the Hanafi position that intellect can know some rulings via universals.

The Eternity of Hukm: Hanafi Response to Mu'tazila
Refutes Mu'tazila proofs that hukm is haadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Mu'tazila argument that hukm is temporal and articulate the Hanafi response distinguishing between the eternal ruling and its temporal relation.

Core Idea
Hukm is eternal; only its relation to actions is temporal.

The Mu'tazila argue that divine rulings are temporal because they appear to change (e.g., an act becomes halal after being haram) and are caused by temporal events like marriage or divorce. This challenges the Hanafi view that hukm is eternal, like God's speech. In this passage from Muslim al-Thubut, the Hanafi scholar dissects each Mu'tazila proof, showing that temporality and abrogation apply only to the relation (ta'luq) of the ruling, not to its essence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mu'tazila arguments
List the three proofs they give: (1) Rulings are described as 'occurring' (e.g., became halal). (2) Rulings are effects of temporal causes (e.g., marriage causes halal). (3) Defining hukm as khitab (eternal speech) would mix temporal and eternal.
Focus on the claimed major premise that every ruling is temporal.
2
State the Hanafi denial of the major premise
The Hanafi response denies that hukm itself is temporal. Instead, temporality attaches to the relation (ta'luq) between the eternal ruling and the specific act.
This is the core distinction: hukm is qadeem, ta'luq is haadith.
3
Apply the distinction to abrogation (naskh)
Abrogation changes the relation of the ruling, not the ruling itself. For example, the eternal prohibition of drinking wine remains eternal; its relation to the servant changes over time.
Naskh is about the relation, not the essence.
4
Explain the role of temporal acts as manifestations
Temporal acts like marriage are not efficient causes of the ruling but manifestations (mubin) that show which relation is active. Just as verbal speech (kalam lafzi) manifests eternal internal speech (kalam nafsi), temporal acts manifest the eternal ruling.
This refutes the causality argument.
5
Conclude with the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis can define hukm as khitab (eternal address) without contradiction, while Mu'tazila must reject this definition.
This shows the impact of the dispute on usul definitions.

The Child's Actions and the Definition of Hukm
Discusses objection that child's actions have hukm without khitab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi response to the objection that child's actions have rulings without direct khitab, and apply the distinction between rational ('aqli) and shar'i validity.

Core Idea
A child's actions have rulings, but the khitab is to the guardian, and validity can be rational, not only shar'i.

The definition of hukm as khitab (divine address) faces a serious objection: a child's actions—like his recommended prayer, valid sale, and financial obligations—clearly have rulings, yet a child is not directly addressed by the Lawgiver. If hukm requires khitab, how can child's actions carry hukm? This passage presents the objection and the Hanafi answer, which reveals important layers in usul al-fiqh: the role of indirect address, rational validity, and the subtle limitations of the definition itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection

2
State the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi answer: there is no khitab to the child. Instead, the guardian (wali) is addressed with encouragement (tahrid). The guardian gets reward and is responsible for teaching and performance.
This preserves the definition by indirecting the address through the guardian.
3

For the child's sale, validity is not based on divine address but on rational conformity (mutabaqa) to contractual requirements. This is 'aqli validity, not shar'i validity derived from direct khitab.
Hanafi usul recognizes that some actions are valid by reason alone, distinguishing between shar'i (based on khitab) and rational validity.
4

Note the phrase 'wa fihi ma fihi' (and in it is what is in it). This is a subtle indication that the answer is not fully satisfactory, leaving room for further reflection on the definition of hukm.
This is typical of sharh works: the commentator signals a weakness without rejecting the school position.
5
Evaluate the Effect on the Definition
The Hanafi position defends the definition by distinguishing direct and indirect khitab, but the hint suggests that the definition may need refinement. This is part of the ongoing usul discourse.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is that the definition of hukm remains intact but its scope is nuanced.

Distinction Between Makruh Tahrimi and Haram: Evidence Strength
Explains the difference between makruh tahrimi and haram based on evidence strength

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish makruh tahrimi from haram based on the strength of evidence and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Muhammad and the Shaykhayn.

Core Idea
Makruh tahrimi is not haram; it is a prohibition proven by speculative evidence, closer to haram but distinct.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, rulings are classified based on the strength of their evidence. An act prohibited by definitive evidence (dalil qati) is haram, while an act prohibited by speculative evidence (dalil zanni) is makruh tahrimi. This lesson explores the nuanced distinction and a verbal dispute between early Hanafi authorities.

Guided Path
1
Define Makruh Tahrimi and Haram
Recall the definitions: haram is proven by qati evidence, makruh tahrimi by zanni evidence. This is the foundational distinction.
Hanafis consider ahad hadith as zanni; mutawatir and Quran as qati.
2
Explore the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Imam Muhammad sometimes called makruh 'haram' metaphorically; the Shaykhayn (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) insisted on 'closer to haram' (aqrab ila al-haram).
Both sides agree on the ruling and punishment; the disagreement is terminological.
3
Analyze the Reason for the Dispute
Imam Muhammad focused on shared punishment (istiqhaq al-‘iqab) as grounds for calling it haram metaphorically. The Shaykhayn emphasized that evidence strength (zanni vs qati) prevents equating the two.
The author indicates this is a verbal (lafzi) dispute, not real (haqiqi).
4

Both positions lead to the same ruling: the act is prohibited and punishable. The thamarah al-khilaf is thus minimal, but the terminology reflects different emphases on evidence hierarchy.
In issuing fatwas, the stronger position follows the Shaykhayn: call it makruh tahrimi, not haram.

The second objection argues that ijab belongs to the category of action (fi'l) and wujub to passion (infi'al), which are mutually exclusive categories.
These objections come from different sources: the first is from Fazil Mirza Jan, the second from a gloss on Sharh al-Mukhtasar.
2
Understand the first objection and the Hanafi response via i'tibar
Analyze the response: the same thing (wujub/ijab) can be dependent on itself under different considerations. Use the examples given in the text: essence with/without condition, and human as hot and moving. This shows that real difference is not necessary; rather, a single entity can have multiple aspects.
The key phrase is 'shay' taraqqī 'alā nafsihi bi i'tibār' — a thing's precedence over itself by consideration.
3
Understand the second objection and the response via relative vs real categories
The objector cites Aristotle's categories as mutually exclusive. The Hanafi response, via Sayyid Sharif al-Jurjani, distinguishes between real (haqiqi) categories and relative (i'tibari) categories. A single thing can fall under different relative categories from different considerations. The commentator adds that even Ibn Sina's claim of mutual exclusivity applies only to real categories, not relative ones.
This shows the Hanafi flexibility: conceptual tools like i'tibar allow resolving taxonomic conflicts.
4
Formulate the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The dispute is not merely academic. If wujub and ijab were separate, divine commands would be separate from their resulting obligations, potentially leading to multiplicity in divine attributes. The Hanafi position preserves the unity of God's will and command. In fatwa, this understanding affects how one views the binding nature of commands: the obligation is not a separate entity created after the command but is the command itself from a certain perspective.
This also relates to the Hanafi principle that the command (amr) indicates obligation (wujub) in itself.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute defending the essential unity (ittihad) of wujub (obligation) and ijab (making obligatory) as argued by Imam ar-Razi in al-Mahsul. The commentator expands on two objections and their responses. The first objection is that if wujub depends on ijab, they are different, not one. The response uses the concept of different considerations (i'tibarat), giving examples: essence (mahiyya) without condition and with condition are one essence but different in consideration; similarly, the same human can be considered from heat or movement as dependent and that upon which it depends. Thus, wujub and ijab are one in essence but distinguished by aspect. The second objection is that ijab belongs to the category of action (fi'l) and wujub to passion (infi'al), which are mutually exclusive categories (maqulat mutaba'ina) per Aristotelian logic. The response, attributed to Sayyid Sharif al-Jurjani, again invokes different considerations: a single thing can fall under different categories from different angles, as categories applied relatively (maqulat i'tibariyya) are not mutually exclusive. The commentator adds that even Ibn Sina's claim of categories being mutually exclusive applies to real categories, not relative ones. Thus, the unity of wujub and ijab stands.

Key Terms
الوجوب (al-wujub): The state of being obligatory (hukm shar'i).
الإيجاب (al-ijab): The act of making something obligatory (by the Lawgiver).
مقولات (maqulat): Categories (Aristotelian); the highest genera under which beings are classified.
اعتبار (i'tibar): Consideration or aspect; a mental distinction that does not multiply reality.

Core Idea
Ijab and wujub can unify as conventional categories; actual categorical exclusivity is not violated.

This passage tackles a philosophical objection from Mulla Mirza Jan against the Hanafi view that command (ijab) and obligation (wujub) are essentially the same. In one sense, action and passion are both two categories, two highest genera; in the other sense, they are not actually genera, though they may be called super-genera by consideration. According to the first meaning (both are two categories from highest genera), action means effective influencing, passion means being affected, like fire gradually heating water. So in this sense, action and passion are two distinct categories from mutually exclusive genera. In contrast, the second meaning of action and passion is absolute influencing and absolute being affected; thus in this sense they are not two categories. And the occasional expression of obligation (wujub) as passion and command (ijab) as action is according to the second meaning, not the first. Hence, assuming the unity of ijab and wujub, the mutual exclusivity of categories does not necessarily apply, because ijab and wujub in the second sense actually are not from the category of action and passion; they are called categories metaphorically and by consideration. In short, assuming the unity of ijab and wujub, at most the applicability of conventional categories follows, and that is not impossible; what is impossible – i.e., the unity of actual mutually exclusive categories – does not follow. His statement: 'So the objection that the Shaykh stated in al-Shifa that the categories...etc.' is subordinate to the author's previous statement 'I say the conclusion is that the mutual applicability of actual categories does not follow'. Meaning: after the statement of Sayyid is determined, the objection of Fadil Mirza Jan is not valid, which he raised after quoting Sayyid's statement in the gloss on Sharh al-Mukhtasar. Objection: Fadil Mirza Jan's objection to Sayyid's statement is that Shaykh al-Ra'is in al-Shifa explicitly stated that the categories are mutually exclusive, so both cannot be true even by consideration. Similarly, Sayyid (may his secret be sanctified) in the gloss on Sharh al-Matali' confirmed that categories are necessarily mutually exclusive; therefore, of two mutually exclusive categories, when one applies to a thing, the other cannot apply, otherwise the unity of two mutually exclusive categories would follow. Reason for non-applicability: The objection presented by Fadil Mirza Jan against Sayyid's statement will not occur because what Shaykh al-Ra'is and Sayyid said in the gloss on Sharh al-Matali' that categories are mutually exclusive means the impossibility of actual mutually exclusive categories applying, not the impossibility of conventional categories applying. And we claim that conventional categories can apply and unify. Hence objections like the unity of two genera in one thing in one rank (martaba) and difference in essence and essential attributes will not arise, because there is a great difference between reality and convention.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed response to an objection raised by Mulla Mirza Jan in his gloss on Sharh al-Mukhtasar. The objection relies on the Aristotelian principle that the ten categories (maqulat) are mutually exclusive – a single thing cannot belong to two different categories. Mirza Jan argues that if ijab (command) and wujub (obligation) were unified, they would belong to both the category of action (fi'l) and passion (infi'al), which is impossible. The commentator clarifies that there are two senses of fi'l and infi'al. In the first sense, they are actual categories and mutually exclusive. In the second sense, they are merely conventional (i'tibari) – absolute influence and being influenced. When we speak of ijab and wujub, we use the second sense, so no actual categorical conflict arises. The objection only targets actual categories, while the claim of unity concerns conventional ones. Thus, the objection does not apply. This shows the Hanafi usul position that the relationship between command and obligation can be understood without violating categorical distinctions.

Key Terms
مقولات (maqulat): Categories (Aristotelian ten categories); highest genera under which all beings fall.
اعتباری (i'tibari): Conventional; something that is not real but is considered as such for legal or conceptual purposes.
ایجاب (ijab): The act of commanding; the divine attribute of imposing obligation.
وجوب (wujub): Obligation; the quality of being obligatory that results from command.
