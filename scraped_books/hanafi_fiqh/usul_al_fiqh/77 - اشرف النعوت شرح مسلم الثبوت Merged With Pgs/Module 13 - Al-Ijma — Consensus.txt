Subject of Usul al-Fiqh and the Place of Hujjiyya
Definition of usul al-fiqh's subject and refutation of objections regarding ijma/qiyas hujjiyya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the subject of usul al-fiqh, identify the four proofs, and explain why the discussion of the authority of ijma and qiyas is included in usul despite being a kalam issue.

Core Idea
The subject of usul al-fiqh is the four proofs; their authority belongs to kalam, but usul discusses ijma and qiyas due to disagreement.

This passage lays out the very definition of usul al-fiqh: its subject is the four proofs (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) in summary. The author then defends including a key discussion—the authority (hujjiyya) of ijma and qiyas—within usul. Opponents argued it belongs to fiqh or is self-evident, but the Hanafi school places it in kalam while acknowledging usul scholars engage with it because of widespread disagreement. Understanding this sets the framework for how usul al-fiqh is organized.

Guided Path
1
State the subject of usul al-fiqh per the matn
Identify the four proofs as the subject: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas in summary.
The matn says 'wa mawdu'uhu al-adilla al-arba'a ijmalan'.
2
Identify the two objections against including hujjiyya in usul
First objection: hujjiyya belongs to fiqh because it entails obligation. Second objection: it is a religious necessity and not part of any discipline.
3
Explain the Hanafi response to each objection
First: obligation is a branch (far') of authority, not identical. Second: even if conceded as necessity, we do not concede it because there is disagreement.
4
Locate the correct discipline for hujjiyya according to the text
The truth is that hujjiyya is from kalam, just like the authority of the Book and Sunnah.
Hanafi kalam differs from the Mutakallimun in some respects.
5
Explain why usul scholars focus on ijma and qiyas rather than Quran and Sunnah
Because there is much disagreement and passion about ijma and qiyas, while the authority of Quran and Sunnah is agreed upon by the Ummah.

Hujjiyyah as Exemplary: Refuting Objections to Ibn al-Humam's Definition
Refuting objections against Ibn al-Humam's definition of hujjiyyah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Ibn al-Humam's definition of hujjiyyah as wujub al-amal is exemplary (tamthili) rather than delimiting (tahdidi), and evaluate the objections based on the concept of thamarat.

Core Idea
Hujjiyyah is broader than wujub; its mention as an example preserves comprehensiveness.

In Hanafi usul, hujjiyyah (proof authority) is a key concept that determines the binding force of evidence. Ibn al-Humam famously defined it as 'wujub al-amal' (obligation to act), but this sparked objections claiming it conflates hujjiyyah with its consequences. This lesson unpacks those objections and the clarifying response that the definition is exemplary, not exclusive.

Guided Path
1
Understand the First Objection
The first objection argues that defining hujjiyyah as wujub is circular: wujub is a branch (far') of hujjiyyah, so it cannot be the definition. Even if they are necessary concomitants, one does not prove the other.
This challenges the logical structure of defining a root by its branch.
2
Understand the Second Objection
The second objection notes that hujjiyyah produces multiple thamarat (consequences), including jawaz (permissibility). Restricting it to wujub ignores other possible rulings.
This highlights the mismatch between a single-effect definition and the multi-effect reality.
3
Grasp the Tamthili Response
Ibn al-Humam's defenders explain that his mention of wujub was tamthili (exemplary), not tahdidi (delimiting). He meant that hujjiyyah requires acting upon the ruling of the source, whatever that ruling may be (wajib, mubah, haram). Alternatively, wujub was prioritized due to its predominance in fiqh issues.
The definition is not exclusive but illustrative, preserving the comprehensive nature of hujjiyyah.
4
Apply the Tamthili Concept
Consider how the tamthili approach affects understanding of other adillah. For a source like qiyas, its hujjiyyah similarly entails acting according to its hukm, not necessarily wujub. This prevents misinterpretation of evidence authority.
The lesson applies to any source: hujjiyyah is a property that triggers the appropriate hukm, not a fixed obligation.

First refutation: From his statement 'This is a branch of hujjiyyah'. The conclusion is that the meaning of hujjiyyah of ijma' and qiyas is not to state that acting upon their requirement is obligatory at all. Because wujub al-amal is a branch of hujjiyyah, i.e., the obligation is derived from their being proofs. So as long as their being proofs is not established, the obligation to act upon their requirement cannot be established, since the branch cannot be the root itself. So their being proofs is a separate thing, and the obligation to act upon their requirement is another matter; they are not identical such that the establishment of proof can be used to infer the establishment of obligation. Rather, even if we do not consider wujub al-amal to be identical with the proof of ijma', but consider one as necessary for the other, still the existence of hujjiyyah does not lead to the existence of obligation, because from the fact that one of the two is from the category of necessity, it is not necessary that the other is also from necessity. So if wujub al-amal is a necessary concomitant, it is not necessary that the hujjiyyah of ijma' (the necessary) is also from necessity. Second refutation: The author made a second objection from his statement 'Indeed, the permissibility of action is also among its fruits'. Its summary is that the meaning of hujjiyyah of ijma' and qiyas is not only that acting upon their requirement is obligatory, so that one is considered necessary for the other. Rather, the meaning of hujjiyyah of ijma' includes both wujub and jawaz, because jawaz is also among its fruits. Thus, wujub is not necessary for hujjiyyah; rather, it is distinct and separate. On this estimation, hujjiyyah is more general than wujub, and the difference between general and specific is clear. Therefore, interpreting hujjiyyah of ijma' as wujub is to interpret it as something distinct, and that is not permissible. Response: The answer to the objection against Imam Ibn al-Humam is given that by 'obligation to act upon the requirement', Ibn al-Humam did not mean that the meaning of hujjiyyah of ijma' is solely wujub, so that the objection about jawaz is made. Rather, he presented wujub as an example (tamthila). By this he intended a general meaning, i.e., acting upon the requirement of ijma' is obligatory if the ijma' is wajib; it is permissible if the ijma' is mubah; and it is forbidden if the ijma' is haram. In short, explaining the meaning of hujjiyyah of ijma' as wujub is exemplary (tamthili), not delimiting (tahdidi). Or he gave priority to wujub over jawaz due to preponderance and predominance, because issues of obligation are fewer compared to issues of permissibility.

Commentary
This passage presents two objections against Imam Ibn al-Humam's definition of hujjiyyah and the response. The first objection argues that defining hujjiyyah as wujub al-amal is circular because wujub is a branch of hujjiyyah; thus, one cannot prove wujub from hujjiyyah without establishing hujjiyyah first. Furthermore, even if they are necessary concomitants, one does not entail the other in meaning. The second objection points out that hujjiyyah includes both wujub and jawaz, so it is broader than wujub; thus, equating the two is incorrect. The response clarifies that Ibn al-Humam's mention of wujub was exemplary (tamthili), not delimiting. He meant that hujjiyyah necessitates acting according to the hukm of the ijma', whether that hukm is wajib, mubah, or haram. Alternatively, wujub was given priority due to its predominance in fiqhi issues. This teaches us that in usul al-fiqh, definitions are often comprehensive and not restricted to a single hukm.

Key Terms
حجیت (hujjiyyah): The quality of being a proof (hujjah), entailing that its requirement must be followed according to its hukm (wujub, jawaz, etc.).
ثمرات (thamarat): The legal consequences or results (ahkam) that arise from hujjiyyah, such as wujub, jawaz, etc.
تمثیلی (tamthili): Exemplary or illustrative, not delimiting or restricting the meaning to that example alone.

Hanafi Proof of Rational Good and Evil via Consensus
Proof from consensus (ijma) of rational beings for rational good/evil, with objection and response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi proof from consensus (ijma) for rational good/evil and identify the objection (perfection/imperfection) and the Hanafi response.

Core Idea
If even atheists agree that beneficence is good and injury is evil, morality must be intrinsic.

This passage tackles a foundational usul dispute: Are moral values (husn/qubh) intrinsic to acts and knowable by reason, or are they purely determined by revelation? The Hanafi school, alongside the Mu'tazila, holds the former view. Here, the author presents a clever proof from consensus: all rational beings, including those who reject revelation, agree on the goodness of beneficence and the evil of its opposite. The passage then anticipates an objection—that this agreement might only be about perfection/imperfection—and provides a refined Hanafi response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and its proof
The Matn states: 'Our proof is that the goodness of beneficence and the evil of its opposite are agreed upon by all rational beings, even those who reject revelation.' This is the core Hanafi evidence that moral values are intrinsic.
The proof is not a revealed text but an empirical observation of universal agreement.
2
Analyze the strength of the consensus
The consensus includes deniers of revelation (Barahima, Mulahida). If morality were only from revelation, they would not agree. Thus, the moral quality must belong to the acts themselves (dhati).
This type of consensus is not the formal ijma of scholars but a rational consensus (ijma al-‘uqala’).
3
Understand the objection
The objector (an Ash'ari or similar) argues that the agreement may be about attributes of perfection/imperfection (e.g., truthfulness is perfect, falsehood imperfect), not about moral obligation or reward/punishment (the contested meaning).
The objection targets the precise meaning of husn/qubh: are they about intrinsic goodness or about deserving reward?
4
Follow the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response clarifies that they do not claim this consensus entails a divine ruling (hukm shar‘i); they only claim it shows intrinsic value independent of revelation. The objection about perfection/imperfection does not undermine this, since even that agreement shows an intrinsic quality.
Thus, the Hanafi position is moderate: reason can discern good/evil but does not necessarily know God's reward/punishment without revelation.

Presents three Mu'tazilite opinions on acts whose good/evil is not rationally known

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three Mu'tazilite opinions on acts before revelation, explain the Hanafi adoption of ibaha, and critique the argument of istilzam.

Core Idea
Before revelation, the default ruling of acts is permissibility—a principle the Hanafi school affirms.

In this passage, the author of Musallam al-Thubut discusses the second category of voluntary acts: those whose moral quality cannot be rationally known. The Mu'tazila offer three opinions; the first, ibaha, aligns with the Hanafi maxim 'al-asl fi al-ashya' al-ibaha'. We will explore the arguments, including Imam Muhammad's example, and the critique of the necessary implication (istilzam).

Guided Path
1
List the three Mu'tazilite opinions
Read the passage and identify the three distinct opinions on acts before revelation: ibaha (permissibility), man' (prohibition), and waqf (suspension).
Only the first opinion is elaborated in this passage.
2
Examine the logical argument for ibaha
Understand the rational proof: creating things would be futile if their use were prohibited, so permissibility is the wisdom behind creation.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof).
3
Analyze Imam Muhammad's example
Consider the scenario of a person threatened with death to eat carrion or drink wine; refuse and are killed. Imam Muhammad says he fears the person will be sinful because permissibility is the original state.
The example shows that ibaha does not become wajib under duress, but refusal may lead to sin.
4
Critique the necessary implication (istilzam)
The matn objects that ibaha does not necessarily follow from wisdom; the author says 'ربما يمنع الاستلزام' (the implication may be blocked).
The Hanafi school agrees with ibaha but rejects this specific argument.
5
Identify the practical consequence
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf: whether a person coerced to perform an act is sinful for not doing it affects rulings on coercion and necessity.

And as for that which is not so (i.e., acts whose good/evil reason cannot perceive), there are three opinions among them before revelation. The first opinion is that of permissibility (ibaha): before revelation, all voluntary acts are permissible for the servant, so there is no restriction in acting or leaving. The wisdom of creating all things is permissibility, so that creation be not futile. This is the opinion of the Basran Mu'tazila, and many Hanafi and Shafi'i scholars also incline to this, especially the Iraqis. Imam Muhammad indicated this meaning: if a person is threatened with death to eat carrion or drink wine, and he does not do so and is killed, Imam Muhammad said, 'I fear he will be sinful because eating carrion and drinking wine were not prohibited by a prior ruling, so permissibility is the original state.' And the author (matn) rebuts the claim of necessary implication (istilzam) with some detail in the margin.

Commentary
This passage discusses the second type of voluntary acts according to Mu'tazila: those whose moral quality cannot be rationally perceived. Here, the Mu'tazila have three opinions: 1) ibaha (permissibility) – acts are originally permissible; 2) man‘ (prohibition) – acts are originally prohibited; 3) waqf (suspension) – the ruling is unknown. The text elaborates on the first opinion, quoting Imam Muhammad's example: someone threatened with death for not eating carrion or drinking wine, who refuses and is killed, might be sinful because permissibility is the default, so the threat does not obligate the act. The author then notes that the matn (مسلم الثبوت) objects to this reasoning (ربما يمنع الاستلزام) and the commentator elaborates in the margin. Hanafi scholars generally hold that permissibility is the original state of things until prohibited by revelation (as in the Hanafi legal maxim 'al-asl fi al-ashya' al-ibaha'), so this view aligns with the Hanafi school despite its Mu'tazilite origin.

Key Terms
اباحة (ibaha): The ruling that an act is permissible and not subject to any legal restriction before revelation.
استلزام (istilzam): The claim that ibaha necessarily follows from the wisdom of creation.
