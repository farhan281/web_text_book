Why Codified Sciences Cannot Be Defined by True Definitions (Hadd)
A critique of the possibility of true definitions for codified sciences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the critique of defining codified sciences like usul al-fiqh by their specific issues and explain why such definitions are only descriptive (rasm) rather than true definitions (hadd).

Core Idea
A definition of a codified science cannot be a true hadd because its parts are external and non-predicable, making genus and differentia impossible.

This passage from Ashraf al-Nu'ut (a commentary on Musallam al-Thubut) presents a sophisticated critique of how we define codified sciences like usul al-fiqh. The author argues that the definition given in the introduction of a science—which states its object and method—cannot be a true logical definition (hadd) because the science is composed of its specific issues, which are external parts that cannot be predicated on the whole. Instead, such definitions are merely descriptive (rasm). This analysis is fundamental to understanding how Hanafi usul scholars approach the epistemology of the sciences they study.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim being critiqued
The common practice is to define a codified science in its introduction (e.g., 'usul al-fiqh is knowledge of the rules by which one derives fiqh rulings from evidence'). The claim is that this is a true definition (hadd).
This definition is universal to many introductory textbooks.
2
Understand the composition of a science
A codified science is composed of its specific issues (masa'il) or the mental affirmations of those issues. These issues are 'external parts' (ajza' kharijiyya) because they are the real content of the science and are not predicated on the science as a whole.
For example, 'al-amr lil-wujub' is an issue of usul al-fiqh but cannot be said 'usul al-fiqh is al-amr lil-wujub'.
3
Apply the principle of non-predication
Since the parts are external, they cannot be predicated on the whole. A true definition (hadd) requires that the defining components (genus and differentia) be predicable parts. Without predicable parts, no genus and differentia can be extracted.
The example of the number ten illustrates this: the units are external parts, so 'ten is one' is false.
4
Conclude the classification
Because genus and differentia are absent, the definition cannot be a hadd; it is only a descriptive definition (rasm). Accepting it as a hadd would lead to logical absurdities like multiplicity of essences (ta'addud dhati).
The passage gives two detailed reasons (one based on circularity, the other on non-predication) to reject the hadd claim.

Statement of the objection: That the realities of codified sciences like nahw, sarf, usul al-fiqh etc. are either the specific issues of that codified science or the affirmations of those issues. Whether its reality is the specific issues or the affirmations of issues, in both cases that codified science is composed of non-predicated parts (external parts). Because the specific issues or the perceptions of issues are the external parts of that codified science, they cannot be correctly predicated on the science, nor can one issue be predicated on another. For example, 'al-amr lil-wujub' cannot be predicated on usul al-fiqh, nor can 'al-nahi lil-tahrim' be predicated on 'al-amr lil-wujub'. The conclusion is that the realities and issues of sciences are external parts, not mental. Therefore, the universal concepts in the introduction, i.e. the definition of the discipline like 'usul al-fiqh is the knowledge of rules by which one arrives at fiqh rulings through evidences', that definition will be called a descriptive definition (rasm), not a true definition (hadd). There are two reasons why it cannot be a true definition. First reason: The definition (hadd) cannot serve as a premise, because the true definition of a thing is through its reality and essence, and the reality of codified sciences is its specific issues or the affirmation of specific issues. If the reality of codified sciences is the affirmation of its specific issues, then the definition mentioned in the introduction would be the very reality of the science's specific issues. So if defining the science is made a premise, it necessitates that one thing be both outside and inside the discipline. The premise is outside the reality of the discipline, so as a premise its externality is necessary, and if that premise contains the definition, then as a definition its inclusion is necessary. It has passed that definition of a science is said to be through its issues, and that is the reality of the science. So the definition of the science would become the reality of the science, indeed the very science. Rather, the self-dependency of the thing would also be necessary: the premise is that on which the beginning of the science depends, so the premise is the dependee and the science is the dependent. The premise, which is the dependee, contains the definition of the science, i.e. its reality and specific issues, and the science, which is dependent, is also issues. So both the dependee and the dependent are issues, and that is self-dependency. Then, since the dependee is the cause of the dependent, and cause and effect are the same thing, the self-causation of the thing would also be necessary. This is clearly false. Therefore, the definition mentioned in the introduction being a true definition is invalid. And if the reality of codified sciences is its specific issues [not affirmations], then on the assumption that the definition in the introduction is a true definition, beginning the discipline's issues would depend on knowing the discipline's issues. Second reason: The second reason for the invalidity of being a true definition in the introduction has been expressed by the author in his statement: 'Built on the fact that what is composed of non-predicated parts, like ten, has no genus and no differentia, otherwise multiplicity of essences would be necessary.' Its summary is that the reality of a science is its specific issues or perceptions of issues from which the science is composed, and these issues or perceptions are the external parts of that science that are not predicated on the science. For example, 'al-amr lil-wujub' is an issue of usul that is not predicated on usul al-fiqh. Because it is an external part, and an external part is not predicated, so it has no genus and differentia. And that which has no genus and differentia cannot be a true definition. Understand by example: Ten is a number composed of ten units, and these units are external parts of ten, such that no single unit is predicated on ten. Thus, 'al-ashara wahidun' and 'al-wahid ashara' are both false statements. So when the parts of ten cannot be predicated on the composite whole, it has no genus and differentia, and that which lacks genus and differentia cannot be a true definition. Similarly, the specific issues of a science are its external parts, so genus and differentia cannot be conceived in it. Therefore, the definition mentioned in the introduction will be a descriptive definition, cannot be a true definition. Otherwise, multiplicity of essences would be necessary. And multiplicity of essences is false. Multiplicity of essences would be necessary in that besides the external parts, if by way of definition a genus and differentia are assumed for that science, then for one unified determinate essence, two kinds of essential properties would be necessary. For example, for usul al-fiqh, external parts are its specific issues or perceptions of issues already established; then if for the same science, by way of definition, genus and differentia are accepted as predicated parts, then for one essence, two kinds of parts (predicated and non-predicated) would be necessary, and multiplicity of essences is necessarily false. Rather, along with multiplicity of essences, multiplicity of the essence itself would be necessary because of the two essences, one [if taken] for the constitution of that essence...

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul al-fiqh analysis of why definitions of codified sciences like usul al-fiqh cannot be true definitions (hadd) but only descriptive definitions (rasm). It begins by stating the objection: the realities of codified sciences consist either of their specific issues or the mental affirmations of those issues. In either case, the science is composed of external parts (ajza' kharijiyya) that cannot be predicated on the whole. For instance, the rule 'al-amr lil-wujub' is an issue of usul al-fiqh but cannot be predicated on the entire discipline. Hence, any definition given in an introduction (muqaddima) is only a rasm, not a hadd. Two reasons are given for this. First, if the definition were a hadd, then the premise (muqaddima) containing it would have to be both external (since it precedes the science) and internal (since a hadd defines the essence), leading to a contradiction. It would also cause circularity (tawaqquf al-shay' 'ala nafsihi) because the science depends on the premise, but the premise contains the science's essence. Second, following the author's quoted statement, since the parts of a composite like ten are external and not predicated on the whole, there can be no genus or differentia. Similarly, the specific issues of a science are external parts, so no genus and differentia exist, making a hadd impossible. Accepting otherwise would force multiplicity of essences (ta'addud dhati), which is false. The author illustrates with the example of the number ten: the units are external parts, so 'ten is one' or 'one is ten' are false. Thus, a science's definition must be rasm.

Key Terms
حد (hadd): A definition that captures the essence of a thing through its genus (jins) and differentia (fasl).
رسم (rasm): A descriptive definition that identifies a thing by its properties or accidents, without capturing its essence.
اجزاء خارجیہ (ajza' kharijiyya): The real, external parts that compose a thing, as opposed to mental parts (ajza' dhihniyya). Here, they are the individual issues that make up a science and cannot be predicated on the whole.
تعدد ذاتی (ta'addud dhati): Multiplicity of essences for a single entity, which is logically impossible.

Resolving the Objection of Self-Dependence in Definitions: Tasawwur, Tasdiq, and the Three-Fold Consideration
Resolving the objection of self-dependence in definitions using tasawwur/tasdiq and three-fold consideration.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the distinction between conceptual and judgmental knowledge (tasawwur and tasdiq) and the three-fold consideration (bishart shay', bishart la shay', la bishart shay') resolve the circularity objection in defining sciences, and apply this framework to analyze definitions in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
The same subject matter can serve as both premise and conclusion through different perspectives: conceptual vs. judgmental and conditional vs. unconditional.

Defining a science by its subject matter seems circular: if the science is knowledge of the subject matter, how can the subject matter be understood without the science? This passage from Ashraf al-Nu'ut tackles exactly that objection using two powerful tools from Islamic logic and epistemology. By carefully distinguishing between conceptual and judgmental perception, and by applying the three-fold consideration (bishart shay', bishart la shay', la bishart shay'), the commentator shows that the same set of parts can function both as premise and conclusion without contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Circularity Objection
Recognize the objection: if a science is defined by its subject matter, then understanding the subject matter requires the science, creating self-dependence.
The objection targets the logical soundness of any definition (hadd) of a science.
2
First Answer: Tasawwur vs. Tasdiq
Learn how the distinction between conceptual knowledge (tasawwur) and judgmental knowledge (tasdiq) allows the subject matter to be a premise in one sense and the intended conclusion in another.
Tasawwur is simple comprehension; tasdiq is assent. They are different modes of perception.
3
Second Answer: Three-Fold Consideration
Understand the three considerations (bishart shay', bishart la shay', la bishart shay') and how they transform non-predicate parts into predicate parts.
Predicate parts (ajza' mahmulah) are genus, differentia, etc.; non-predicate parts (ajza' ghayr mahmula) are the parts in themselves.
4
Apply to the Example of Human
Trace how 'animal' and 'rational' shift between species, matter, genus, form, and differentia depending on the consideration.
This concrete illustration shows the mechanism in action.
5
Note the Remaining Problem: Unity of Tasawwur and Tasdiq
Recognize that accepting the definition of a science leads to another issue: the unification of conception and judgment, which are normally distinct.
This is an advanced point that the commentator flags for further reflection.

Commentary
This passage from 'Ashraf al-Nu'ut' is a commentary expanding on a logical objection regarding the definition of a science. The objection is that if a science is defined by its subject matter, it leads to circularity (tawqif al-shay' 'ala nafsihi). The text presents two answers. The first answer distinguishes between conceptual perception (tasawwur) and judgmental perception (tasdiq). The subject matter in conceptual knowledge serves as a premise, while in judgmental knowledge it is the intended object. Since they are different aspects, no circularity occurs. The second answer uses the three-fold consideration (bishart shay', bishart la shay', la bishart shay'). The same parts can be considered as predicate or non-predicate depending on the consideration. For example, 'animal' can be a species (with rationality), matter (without rationality), or genus (without condition). Thus, the subject matter of a science, though non-predicate in itself, can be considered without condition to yield genus and differentia, allowing a proper definition (hadd). However, the commentator notes that if definitions are accepted, another problem arises: the unity of conception and judgment, which are distinct. This is based on whether sciences are perceptions of subject matters or the subject matters themselves. The passage is typical of the Hanafi usul tradition's engagement with logical precision and epistemological foundations.

Key Terms
اجزاء محمولہ (ajza' mahmulah): Predicate parts; components that are predicated (e.g., genus and differentia) in a definition.
اجزاء غیر محمولہ (ajza' ghayr mahmula): Non-predicate parts; components considered in themselves without being predicated.
بشرط شیء (bishart shay'): With the condition of something; a consideration where a part is taken with a condition.
بشرط لا شیء (bishart la shay'): With the condition of nothing; a consideration where the absence of something is conditioned.
لا بشرط شیء (la bishart shay'): Without any condition; a consideration without positive or negative condition.
تصور (tasawwur): Conceptual knowledge; simple apprehension without judgment.
تصدیق (tasdiq): Judgmental knowledge; assent or affirmation of a proposition.
حد (hadd): Definition consisting of genus and differentia.

The Hujjiyyah of Ijma' and Qiyas: A Dispute on the Scope of Usul al-Fiqh
Intra-Hanafi dispute on classification of hujjiyyah of ijma' and qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute between the author and Ibn al-Humam regarding whether the hujjiyyah of ijma' and qiyas belongs to usul al-fiqh or fiqh, and apply the criterion of 'essential accidents' to classify issues.

Core Idea
A proof's authoritativeness (hujjiyyah) is an essential accident of the proof, not a fiqh ruling.

This passage from Sharh Muslim al-Thubut tackles a fundamental question: where does the discussion of hujjiyyah (authoritativeness) of ijma' and qiyas belong? Is it part of usul al-fiqh, the science of legal proofs, or is it merely a fiqh issue about whether to act? The author presents Ibn al-Humam's view that hujjiyyah is a fiqh matter because it involves an action (ijma') and a ruling (obligation to act), then refutes it by arguing that hujjiyyah is an essential accident of the proof itself—a property studied in usul. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to delimit the scope of any Islamic science.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of mawdu' al-'ilm
Identify the author's definition: the subject of a science is that whose essential accidents (a'rad dhatiyyah) are investigated. For usul, the subject is the four proofs from the aspect of indicatory authority (min haythu al-isal).
Essential accidents are properties that necessarily accompany the subject, e.g., 'indicating definitiveness' for a text.
2
Analyze the implication for hujjiyyah
Since 'indicatory authority' (al-isal) is a condition (qayd) of the subject, some argue that hujjiyyah, which is that authority, is a condition, not an accident—hence not discussed in usul. But the author counters that hujjiyyah is an essential accident of the proof, not a condition.
The distinction between condition (qayd) and essential accident (arad dhati) is critical.
3
Examine Ibn al-Humam's argument
Ibn al-Humam claims hujjiyyah of ijma' and qiyas is a fiqh issue because its subject (the act of ijma'/qiyas) is an action of the mujtahid, and its predicate (obligation to act) is a shariah ruling—both belong to fiqh.
He extends the same reasoning to khabar al-wahid.
4
Trace the author's refutation
The author refutes Ibn al-Humam in two ways (indicated by 'qila' and 'pointed to weakness'). First, hujjiyyah is a property of the proof, not of the action. Second, even if it were about obligation, that obligation is a ruling about the proof's authority, not a specific action.
The refutation reasserts that hujjiyyah is an essential accident of the proof.
5
Apply the criterion to classify issues
Use the principle: if a discussion concerns an essential accident of the subject of a science, it belongs to that science; if it concerns a predicate about an action, it belongs to fiqh. Practice by classifying other disputed issues (e.g., hujjiyyah of qiyas, admissibility of istihsan).
This criterion is a tool for structuring Islamic sciences.

Because the subjects of hujjiyyah of ijma' and qiyas are also actions of the legally responsible, since ijma' and qiyas are actions of the mujtahid, and khabar al-wahid is an action of the reporter. Then their predicates become the obligation to act (wujub al-'amal) from among the shariah rulings. It is said: 'Ijma' is obligatory to act upon, and qiyas is obligatory to act upon.' Others, while presenting a second argument, write that the issue of hujjiyyah of ijma' is from fiqh. The gist is that the meaning of our statement 'Ijma' is a hujjah, qiyas is a hujjah, and khabar al-wahid is a hujjah' is that acting upon its implication is obligatory. And the obligation to act is one of the shariah rulings. So when the meaning of hujjiyyah of ijma' is obligation to act, and obligation to act is a shariah ruling, and from fiqh, then the hujjiyyah of ijma' and qiyas being from fiqh is apparent.

Definition of Nazar (Intellectual Consideration) – The Two-Movement Structure
Defines nazar and explains its components and movements.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi later scholars' definition of nazar, explain its two-movement structure, and differentiate between the definitions of Ibn al-Humam and al-Taftazani.

Core Idea
Nazar is the soul's ordered movement from premises to conclusion via a two-step process.

Nazar (intellectual consideration) is a foundational concept in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It is not merely 'looking' but a structured rational process by which the mind moves from what is known to what is unknown. This lesson unpacks the detailed definition given by later Hanafi scholars, emphasizing the two movements involved and the technical choices in wording.

Ibn al-Humam defined nazar in his Tahreer as: "Nazar is the movement of the soul from demands (matālib) regarding quality (kayf), seeking the premises (mabādi) by reviewing forms (isti'rād al-suwar), i.e., adapting itself form by form to find the appropriate (munāsib) which is the middle term (wasat), then arranging it with the two ends of the demand in a way that entails the demand." That is, the movement of the rational soul from demands of conceptualization and assent regarding quality, seeking the premises, such that the soul adapts itself one by one to the forms so that the soul obtains the appropriate matter, and the appropriate matter is the middle term; then the arrangement of the middle term with both ends of the demand is such that it entails the demand. This is called nazar. (1) The author (of Muslim al-Thubut) following Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani defined nazar as: "Nazar is the arrangement of the rational (ma'qūl) to attain the unknown (majhūl)." The only difference is that instead of "arrangement of the rational" he said "contemplation of the rational" (mulāḥaẓat al-ma'qūl), but the meaning is the same: the rational soul's attention to the known to obtain the unknown. The reasons for using ma'qūl instead of ma'lūm (known) are: (1) ma'lūm is a homonym (mushtarak) and using homonyms in definitions should be avoided. (2) Using ma'qūl indicates that nazar and fikr apply to rationals (ma'qūlāt), i.e., universal matters whose locus is the mind, not to particulars because particulars are not subject to reasoning (sabab wa maktab?). (3) For the sake of rhyme (ri'āyat al-maji') with the later word majhūl. It should be noted that this definition of nazar is according to the preferred view of the later scholars (muta'akhkhirīn), which requires a second movement (ḥaraka thāniya), i.e., the movement from premises to demands. Thus Sayyid Muhqaq Sharif said in Sharh al-Mawaqif: When in our mind some awareness of a conceptual or assented matter arises, and then we want to obtain it completely, it is first necessary that a movement occur from one known to another known among the stored information in the treasure of the mind, so that it can find the information suitable to that demand; these are the premises. Then it moves again among these premises and arranges them in a specific order that leads to the demand one wishes to obtain. Thus nazar has two movements. The conclusion: the reality of nazar, which is intermediate between the known and the unknown, is to obtain the conclusions (maḥmūlāt) by arranging the premises, which necessitates the eight-term compound (murakkab thamānī) and falls under movement in psychic qualities. For this reason, the author used the word "arrangement" (tartīb) instead of "contemplation" (mulāḥaẓa), because arrangement requires repetition from which the second movement is proven.

Definition of Essential (Dhati) and Defence against Objection
Defines dhati and responds to an objection about including accidents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'dhati' (essential) in Hanafi usul and apply the three conditions of entailment, inclusion, and predication to distinguish essential from accidental attributes.

Core Idea
An essential (dhati) is not just what is understood with the essence—it must satisfy three conditions: entailment, inclusion, and predication.

In defining 'haqiqi' (real definition), the author uses the term 'dhatiyyat' (essential things). But 'dhati' can have multiple meanings in logic. The commentator clarifies exactly what 'dhati' means in this context: it is that whose understanding is entailed by the understanding of the essence. However, a sharp objection arises: if mere co-understanding suffices, then accidental concomitants would also be essential. The answer refines the definition by adding two more conditions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the problem
The term 'dhati' has multiple meanings in logic. The author used 'dhatiyyat' in the definition of haqiqi, so the reader must know which meaning is intended. The commentator defines it to remove ambiguity.
2
Grasp the basic definition
The essential is 'what is understood in the understanding of the essence.' This means that when you conceive the essence, the essential is also conceived—either implicitly in a summary conception or explicitly in a detailed conception. The understanding of the essence entails the understanding of the essential.
Example: Understanding 'human' entails understanding 'rational animal' (genus and differentia).
3
Recognize the objection
If only entailment is required, then concomitants (lawazim) and predicates (mudafat) would also be essential, since they are understood together with the essence. But they are not essential. So the definition seems too broad.
Concomitant: e.g., 'capable of laughter' for human. It is understood with human but not part of the essence.
4
Analyze the answer
The answer adds two more conditions: inclusion (dukhul) and predication (haml). The essential must be included in the essence as a part (like genus or differentia) and must be predicable of the essence (e.g. 'human is rational'). Concomitants fail these because they are not parts and are not predicated essentially.
The word 'fi' (in) in the definition already hints at inclusion.
5
Apply the three conditions
For any attribute to be essential, check: (1) Tadammun: understanding the essence entails understanding the attribute. (2) Dukhul: the attribute is part of the essence. (3) Haml: the attribute can be predicated of the essence. If all three hold, it is essential.

Refining the Definition of Dhati: Critique of 'Uncaused'
Critical refinement of a definition of dhati

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct definition of dhati according to Hanafi usul and critique the alternative definition 'ma la yu'allal' using the example of imkan.

Core Idea
A dhati attribute is conceptually prior to the essence; 'uncaused' fails because it includes accidental attributes uncaused externally but caused by the essence.

In Hanafi usul, precision in defining terms like 'dhati' (essential) is crucial for sound legal reasoning. This passage examines a second definition attributed to Ibn Hajib — 'ma la yu'allal' (uncaused) — and shows why it is weak. You will see how the objection based on attributes like imkan (possibility) exposes the flaw, and how the correct definition preserves the exclusivity of dhati attributes.

Guided Path
1
Understand the standard definition
The author's definition: dhati is that which cannot be understood except after understanding the essence. Example: blackness cannot be understood without understanding color-ness.
2
Examine the alternative definition
Ibn Hajib transmitted another definition: dhati is uncaused ('ghayr mu'allal') — its affirmation for the essence requires no external cause.
3
Apply the objection
Attributes like imkan (possibility) and wujud (existence) are also uncaused externally, yet they are accidental, not dhati. Thus the definition would wrongfully include them.
4
Analyze the response
Ibn Hajib's actual definition is the same as the author's; he only reported the 'uncaused' version to show its weakness. Furthermore, imkan is caused by the essence itself, so it is not truly uncaused.
The key distinction: 'uncaused by an external cause' does not mean 'uncaused altogether'.

The Three Categories of Husn wa Qubh (Good and Evil)
Matn classifying types of husn wa qubh with commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three categories of husn wa qubh (daruri, nazari, sam'i) as defined by the Mu'tazila and identify the epistemological basis of each.

Core Idea
Good and evil are classified as necessary (immediate), theoretical (requiring reflection), or solely revealed (beyond reason).

The Mu'tazila argued that reason can independently know the goodness or evil of certain acts. This lesson unpacks their classification of moral judgments into three levels: necessary, theoretical, and revealed. This taxonomy is foundational for understanding the later Hanafi position that moral obligation is determined by revelation alone. We will examine the examples provided and the underlying claim that reason can perceive goodness and evil in the first two categories.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
List the three types of husn wa qubh mentioned: necessary (daruri), theoretical (nazari), and solely revealed (sam'i). Note that each corresponds to a different mode of perception by the intellect.
The categories are based on how reason apprehends the goodness or evil.

Note the examples given
For daruri: the goodness of beneficial truth and the evil of harmful lying. For nazari: the goodness of harmful truth and the evil of beneficial lying. For sam'i: the goodness of fasting on the last day of Ramadan and the evil of fasting on the first day of Shawwal.
These examples illustrate the immediate vs reflective vs revealed nature of judgment.
3
Understand the epistemological basis
Daruri goodness is perceived instantly without reflection; nazari requires rational deliberation; sam'i is only known through divine communication. The Mu'tazila hold that reason is sufficient for the first two, but not for the third.
This classification reflects the Mu'tazila epistemology of moral values.
4
Recognize the Mu'tazila claim
The Mu'tazila assert that reason can independently determine the goodness or evil of acts in the daruri and nazari categories, forming the basis for moral obligation (taklif).
This is a key point of difference with the Hanafi school.

The objection challenges the scope of rational moral knowledge.

The Rational Basis of Reward: Mu'tazila Objection and Hanafi Response
Matn with objection and response about rational knowledge of reward

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the Mu'tazila objection that the Hereafter is revealed (sam'i) and evaluate the Mu'tazila response based on rational justice, contrasting it with the Hanafi position.

Core Idea
Even if the details of the Hereafter are revealed, reason can still affirm that justice demands recompense for good and evil.

The Mu'tazila faced an important objection: if the specifics of the afterlife are known only through revelation, how can reason claim that good acts deserve reward? Their answer relies on the rational necessity of justice. This lesson examines that exchange and contrasts it with the Hanafi view that moral obligation derives solely from revelation. Understanding this dispute is key to grasping the epistemological foundations of Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
The objection holds that the matter of the Hereafter is sam'i (revealed), so reason cannot independently perceive reward and punishment. Thus, how can reason confidently judge that good deeds will be rewarded?
This objection targets the Mu'tazila claim that reason can ground moral obligation.
2
Present the Mu'tazila response
The Mu'tazila respond: Justice is rationally obligatory, and justice requires rewarding good and punishing evil. Therefore, even if the specifics of the Hereafter are revealed, the general principle of recompense is rationally known.
They distinguish between the general principle (rational) and the specific details (revealed).
3
Understand the rational necessity of justice
The text states 'al-'adl wajib 'aqlan' (justice is rationally obligatory). This is a key Mu'tazila axiom: reason alone can determine the obligation of justice, which then entails recompense.
This axiom is contested by the Hanafi school.
4
Contrast with the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school denies that reason can impose moral obligation. Even the obligation of justice is known through revelation. Thus, without revelation, we cannot know what acts are good or evil, nor that they deserve reward or punishment.
Hanafis hold that moral obligation (taklif) is entirely dependent on divine communication.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Mu'tazila may derive obligations from rational good/evil, while Hanafis require a textual basis. This affects which acts are considered obligatory, prohibited, or simply permissible.
For example, Mu'tazila might rule lying as haram based on its rational evil, while Hanafis would first seek a prohibition in the text.

The Ash'ari Challenge to Intrinsic Moral Values and the Hanafi Response
Presents and rebuts Ash'ari proof that husn/qubh are not intrinsic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Ash'ari argument against intrinsic husn/qubh, apply the Hanafi distinction between husn li-dhatihi and husn li-ghayrihi, and evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
An act's intrinsic badness remains even when necessity permits it; husn li-ghayrihi does not negate qubh li-dhatihi.

Is lying always bad? The Ash'aris argue that if lying were intrinsically bad, it could never become good. But we see that lying becomes obligatory to save a life. Therefore, they claim, moral values are determined by revelation, not by reason. Our Hanafi tradition offers a precise response: the act does not become intrinsically good; rather, the ruling changes due to necessity while the intrinsic nature remains. This distinction is captured in the maxim 'al-darurat tubih al-mahdhurat' and the concept of husn li-ghayrihi. In this lesson, we will dissect this argument and its rebuttal, strengthening our understanding of Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ash'ari Premise
The Ash'aris claim that if husn/qubh were intrinsic (dhati), they could never change. They point to lying becoming obligatory as evidence of change.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) that challenges the very definition of intrinsic.
2
Understand the Hanafi Response: Irtikab Aqall al-Qabihayn
The first response is that in such cases, one commits the lesser of two evils; lying is not 'good' but the unavoidable lesser evil.
This preserves the intrinsic badness of lying while acknowledging the obligation.
3
Unpack the Concept of Husn li-Ghayrihi
The author clarifies that 'goodness due to something else' (husn li-ghayrihi) does not negate 'badness in itself' (qubh li-dhatihi). The act remains intrinsically bad but acquires a legal goodness from the external factor of necessity.
This is the key conceptual tool to reconcile the change in ruling with the claim of intrinsic moral qualities.
4
Apply the Maxim al-Darurat Tubih al-Mahdhurat
The principle that necessities permit prohibitions is invoked to show that the ruling changes, not the intrinsic nature. The original prohibition is suspended, not cancelled.
This maxim is widely accepted, but Hanafis apply it with strict conditions.
5
Evaluate the Thamarah al-Khilaf
If husn/qubh are shar'i, then actions before revelation have no moral value; if dhati, then some acts (like justice) are universally good. Hanafis hold the latter for core moral intuitions, which affects rulings on fitra and accountability.
This has practical implications for ethics and law even in the absence of a revealed text.

Commentary
This passage is a usul al-fiqh dispute concerning whether the goodness (husn) and badness (qubh) of actions are intrinsic (dhati) or determined by divine command (shar'i). The author presents the first Ash'ari argument, the response from their opponents, and a rebuttal from the author's own school (likely Hanafi). The Ash'aris argue: if goodness and badness were intrinsic, they could never change. But we observe that the same kind of act (e.g., lying) is normally bad but becomes obligatory and thus good in certain circumstances (e.g., to save a life). Therefore, goodness and badness are not intrinsic but are assigned by divine law. The opponent responds: in such cases, one merely commits the lesser evil (irtikab aqall al-qabihayn); the act itself does not become good intrinsically. A further objection is raised: if lying is obligatory, then it enters the category of good (al-wajib hasan). The author replies that 'goodness due to something else' (husn li-ghayrihi) does not contradict 'badness in itself' (qubh li-dhatihi). This is exactly the principle 'necessities permit prohibitions' (al-darurat tubih al-mahdhurat). At most, it necessitates that we say acts can be both intrinsically bad and conditionally good. The Ash'aris might accept this to avoid problems with abrogation (naskh), but the argument is not conclusive against the Jabba'iyya (a Mu'tazili sub-school) or against the Hanafis, who maintain that some moral values are rationally knowable. The commentary elaborates that the Ash'ari argument fails because the change in moral evaluation is due to an external factor (necessity), not because the intrinsic quality changes. The Hanafi position, as implied, is that while some actions are known rationally to be good or bad (like justice and injustice), their specific moral weight in the absence of revelation may be different, but the intrinsic aspect remains; the ruling (hukm) due to other reasons is secondary.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Whether pre-eternal speech is 'khitab' depends on definition: capacity to be understood or actual understanding.

This passage from al-Muslim's usul text examines a disagreement among scholars: can pre-eternal divine speech be called 'khitab' (address)? The Unity of Ijab and Wujub: Hanafi Doctrine of Obligation by Consideration
Defines and defends the Hanafi position on ijab and wujub

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that ijab and wujub are united in essence but differ by consideration (i‘tibar), and explain the rational proof and response to objections.

Core Idea
Ijab and wujub are one reality; only the perspective changes.

This lesson delves into a foundational Hanafi usul principle: the relationship between the command (ijab) as an attribute of the Lawgiver and the obligation (wujub) as an attribute of the commanded act. While they appear distinct, the Hanafi school argues they are the same in essence, differing only in how we consider them. Understanding this unity is critical for grasping the nature of obligation in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
The passage asserts that ijab and wujub are united in essence (dhat) and distinct only by consideration (i‘tibar). The command (if‘al) is one reality; when attributed to the commander, it is called ijab; when attributed to the act, it is wujub.
2
Understand the proof via abstract meaning
Wujub is an abstract meaning (ma‘na i‘tibari) abstracted from the same source (muntaza‘ 'anhu) as ijab. Since the act is non-existent at the time of command, it cannot have a real attribute; wujub is merely the command considered in relation to the act.
This rational proof relies on the concept that non-existent entities cannot bear real attributes.
3
Analyse Mirza Jan's objection
Mirza Jan objects that if wujub is merely credal, it does not prove real unity with ijab. He implies that a real attribute of the act is needed for true obligation.
The objection challenges the sufficiency of abstract meanings for establishing legal obligation.
4
Examine the author's response
The author rebuts that wujub is not a real attribute of the external act; it is an inseparable concomitant of ijab, preceding the act. The unity lies in the source of abstraction (the command itself), not in the act.
The response reinforces that the command's existence suffices for obligation.
5
Evaluate further arguments from the matn
The matn adds that one thing can follow itself under different considerations, and categories (action/passion) can apply to one reality under different aspects, as supported by al-Sayyid al-Jurjani.
This addresses the Aristotelian categories objection and solidifies the Hanafi position.

Summary: The summary of the marginal note is that the research of the muhaqqiq, the commentator of Mukhtasar al-Usul, is that ijab and wujub are united in essence and distinct by consideration. Because the address (khitab) and ijab are an attribute of the ruler, and the act is related to the obligated person. So, by consideration of its attribution to the ruler, it is called ijab, and by consideration of its attribution to the act, it is called wujub. The reality of both is one, and the difference is a matter of consideration. The objection of the learned: But the learned Mirza Jan has objected to this view of the muhaqqiq commentator of Mukhtasar al-Usul, which the author stated with 'wa ad‘illahu bimadhkur'. The gist of the objection is that from the aforementioned research, the most that appears is that the act cannot be described with a real attribute, i.e., wujub, but rather an wujub is attained for the act which is a credal attribute, and the desired proof is not established by it, because the desired and claimed unity from the negation of the real attribute is established, and wujub which is a credal thing for the act does not necessitate unity. Answer: The author replied to the objection of the learned Mirza Jan with his statement: 'Aqulu inna al-wujuba laysa sifatan lil-fi‘li al-kharijiy'. The summary of the answer is that wujub is not a real attribute of the external act that subsists in it, because wujub is an inseparable concomitant of ijab, and there is no doubt that ijab precedes the act of the obligated one, so necessarily wujub also precedes the act of the obligated one. In short, the non-existent act has no role in the attribute of wujub, because the non-existent is not described with a real attribute. Since wujub is an abstract entity (amr i‘tibari), and an abstract entity's existence is with the existence of that from which it is abstracted (muntaza‘ 'anhu). So the existence of 'do' is ijab, and it is wujub, but by consideration. Its further elaboration is that wujub is not an attribute of the actual external act in the state of the act's existence; rather, it is called its accidental attribute before the existence of the act. And since the act is non-existent, and a non-existent cannot be described with any attribute in the state of non-existence, therefore the existence of ijab and similarly wujub, which is an attribute, subsists with its source and referent. Because ijab and wujub are both abstract meanings (ma‘ani i‘tibariyya) that are abstracted from their source of abstraction, and that source of abstraction itself is the attribute of 'do' issued from the ruler. So, in terms of source and referent, both ijab and wujub are united. And by the unity of ijab and wujub, what is intended is that they are united in essence in terms of source and referent, although they are distinct in terms of concept and meaning. Thus, 'do' which subsists in the agent, when it is related to the object, reason abstracts the concept of ijab from the aspect of its subsistence in the agent, by which the agent is attributed, and from the aspect of its relation to the supposed object, reason abstracts the concept of wujub. Therefore, the meaning of 'do' is both ijab and wujub in the sense that their source of abstraction is one. But from the aspect that it is an address that subsists in the ruler, it is the attribute of the ruler called ijab; and from the aspect that it is an address related to the act, it is called the attribute of the act, called wujub. The conclusion of the discussion is that the ruling is an attribute of the ruler, but not in every respect and all considerations; and similarly, the ruling is an attribute of the act, yet not in every respect and all considerations. So the division of the ruling into ijab and prohibition, and into wujub and prohibition, is valid. The text of al-Muslim: It was objected that wujub follows from ijab, so how can they be one? And it is answered by the permissibility of a thing following itself under a different consideration, and its reference is to the sequence of one consideration over another. Al-Sayyid said: And by this is answered that which was said: 'Ijab belongs to the category of action and wujub to the category of passion.' And the claim of the impossibility of the categories applying to one thing under one consideration is open to discussion. I say: The conclusion is that the application of real categories is not necessary, and the application of credal categories under different considerations is not impossible. So it is not refuted by what was said that the Shaykh in al-Shifa' declared that the categories are distinct and do not apply, even by consideration.

Commentary
This passage is a dense usul al-fiqh discussion regarding the relationship between ijab (obligation as an attribute of the commander) and wujub (obligation as an attribute of the commanded act). The author defends the position that ijab and wujub are identical in essence (al-mutahid bi al-dhat) but differ in consideration (i‘tibar). The passage follows an objection-response structure and is directly based on the matn (Musallam al-Thubut) and its commentary. The commentator first summarizes the view of the muhaqqiq (the author of Mukhtasar al-Usul) that ijab is the attribute of the ruler (haakim) while wujub is that attribute when considered in relation to the act (fi‘l); the reality is one but the difference is i‘tibari. An objection by Mirza Jan is raised: if wujub is merely a credal attribute (sifah i‘tibariyyah), it does not prove real unity. The author answers by stating that wujub is not a real attribute of the external act; rather, it is an abstract entity (amr i‘tibari) that subsists with ijab, which is the commander's attribute. Since the act is non-existent when commanded, it cannot have a real attribute; thus wujub is simply ijab considered in relation to the act. The unity is in the source of abstraction (muntaza' 'anhu), i.e., the commander's command (if‘al). The passage then quotes the matn: 'It was objected that wujub follows from ijab, so how can they be one?' and gives the response that one thing can follow itself under different considerations. Al-Sayyid (Sharif al-Jurjani) is cited to refute the objection that ijab is an action (fi‘l) and wujub a passion (infi‘al) and thus different categories; the response is that categories can apply to one thing under different considerations, and this does not entail real multiplicity. The commentator emphasizes that the division of the ruling into ijab/tahrim (as commands of the ruler) or wujub/hurmah (as attributes of the act) is both valid. This discussion is foundational to understanding how Hanafi usul conceptualizes obligation, differentiating between the source of legal address and its effect on the subject.

Key Terms
الإيجاب (al-ijab): The attribute of the commander (hakim) that is the command itself, considered as subsisting in him.
الوجوب (al-wujub): The same command attributed to the act of the obligated person, i.e., the obligation as it relates to the act.
الاعتبار (al-i‘tibar): The mental consideration or perspective that distinguishes one aspect from another while preserving essential unity.
المعنى الانتزاعي (al-ma‘na al-intiza‘i): An abstract meaning that has no independent existence but is derived from a source (muntaza' 'anhu).
