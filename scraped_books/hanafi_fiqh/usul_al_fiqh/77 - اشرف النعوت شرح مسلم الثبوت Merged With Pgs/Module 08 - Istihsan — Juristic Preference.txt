The Reins of Principles: Understanding 'Ainnat al-Mabadi'
Defines and expands a metaphorical phrase from the matn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the metaphor of 'ainnat al-mabadi' and identify its implications for the principles of usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
All causes and principles are under Allah's control; knowledge of usul al-fiqh is a divine gift.

This passage comments on the phrase 'Ainnat al-mabadi bi-yadik' from the matn. It unpacks the metaphor of reins to express that the foundational issues and rules of every science, especially usul al-fiqh, are entirely in Allah's grasp. Understanding this theological grounding is essential for grasping the Hanafi view of legal causality and the ultimate dependence of legal knowledge on divine will.

Guided Path
1
Define key terms
Identify the two plural nouns: 'ainnah' (reins) and 'mabadi' (principles). Understand their literal meanings and how they are used metaphorically.
'Ainnah' implies control; 'mabadi' refers to foundational causes or issues.
2
Analyze the metaphor
Explain how reins signify mastery and direction. The phrase means that the reins (control) of all principles are in Allah's hand.
This is a kinayah (metaphorical expression) asserting divine sovereignty over causes.
3
Apply to usul al-fiqh
The commentator specifies that 'mabadi' here most likely means the principles of usul al-fiqh. Recognize that the rules and maxims of usul al-fiqh are divinely ordained.
This underscores that legal theory is not merely human convention but rooted in divine will.
4
Connect to Quranic proof
The passage references the verse 'fa''alun lima yurid' (Qur'an 85:16). Understand that this verse supports the idea that Allah's will is absolute over all causes and commands.
The verse is cited as a proof (dalil) for the statement.
5
Draw epistemological implication
Conclude that acquiring knowledge of usul al-fiqh is a gift from Allah; no one can attain it without His bestowal.
This fosters humility and reliance on divine guidance in legal study.

Commentary
This passage comments on the phrase 'Ainnat al-mabadi bi-yadik' (The reins of the principles are in Your hand). The commentator first defines 'ainnah' as the plural of 'inan' (rein), and 'mabadi' as the plural of 'mabda'' (beginning or cause). He explains that all causes and commands are created solely by Allah, referencing the Qur'anic verse 'fa''alun lima yurid' (He does what He intends). The commentary then offers an interpretation specifically for the field of usul al-fiqh: the 'mabadi' here refer to the foundational issues and rules of the science, which are entirely under Allah's control. This emphasizes that knowledge of usul al-fiqh is a divine gift; no one acquires it without Allah's grant. The author also notes the literary device of metaphorical expression (isti'ara bilkinnaya) in the matn.

مبادی (mabadi): Principles; plural of 'mabda'', meaning beginnings or causes. Here specifically refers to the foundational issues of a science.

Istinbat from Jawami' al-Kalim: Deriving Fiqh Rulings from Quran and Hadith
Explains istinbat from Quran and hadith with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of istinbat to derive rulings from Quranic verses and Prophetic hadith, and identify how different schools derive opposite rulings from the same text.

Core Idea
Jawami' al-kalim are comprehensive texts from which mujtahids derive rulings; the same text can yield different rulings depending on usul principles.

This passage explains the concept of jawami' al-kalim (comprehensive words) and illustrates how Hanafi jurists derive rulings from Quran and hadith. The author presents two examples: one from the tayammum verse, where Hanafis derive that wiping one-fourth of the head is obligatory in wudu, and another from the hadith on two qullahs, where Hanafis argue that less than that amount becomes impure. These examples highlight the role of istinbat and the differences among madhahib.

Guided Path
1
Identify the comprehensive text
Find the Quranic verse or hadith that serves as the source for istinbat. These are the 'jawami' al-kalim' referenced in the passage.
In the first example, the text is Quran 5:6 (تَيَمَّمُوا صَعِيدًا طَيِّبًا فَامْسَحُوا بِوُجُوهِكُمْ وَأَيْدِيكُمْ). In the second, it is the hadith 'إِذَا بَلَغَ الْمَاءُ قُلَّتَيْنِ لَمْ يَحْمِلِ الْخَبَثَ'.
2
Classify the text's linguistic category
Determine whether the text is mutlaq (unrestricted), amm (general), or another category. This classification triggers a specific Hanafi usul principle for interpretation.
For the tayammum verse, the Hanafis treat 'فَامْسَحُوا' as mutlaq regarding the extent of wiping. For the hadith, they treat it as amm and use mafhum al-mukhalafa.
3
Apply the Hanafi istinbat tool
Use tools like qiyas, mafhum, or taqyid to derive the ruling. The passage shows two operations: qiyas from tayammum to wudu, and mafhum al-mukhalafa for the hadith.
Hanafi: from the mutlaq command in tayammum, they use qiyas to conclude that wiping part of the head suffices, and then taqyid specifies the amount as one-fourth based on other evidence.
4
Note cross-madhhab differences
Observe how other schools read the same text differently due to different usul principles. The passage contrasts Hanafi with Maliki and Shafi'i for the verse, and Hanafi with Shafi'i for the hadith.
Maliki: amm → takhsis → entire head. Shafi'i: mutlaq → qiyas → three hairs. Shafi'i on hadith: takes mantuq (explicit meaning) to argue purity of two qullahs.
5
Extract the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence of the differing interpretations. This shows why usul matters for actual fiqh rulings.
For wudu: Hanafi wipes 1/4 head, Maliki wipes full head, Shafi'i wipes three hairs. For water: Hanafi says less than 2 qullahs becomes impure, Shafi'i says 2 qullahs remain pure.

Commentary

Key Terms
جوامع الكلم (jawaami' al-kalim): Words that encompass various types of rulings, such as the Quran and Prophetic hadith.
استنباط (istinbat): Derivation of legal rulings from textual sources.
مفهوم المخالفة (mafhum al-mukhalafa): The opposite implied meaning of a conditional or qualifying phrase; e.g., 'if X then Y' implies 'if not X then not Y'.
منطوق (mantuq): The explicit wording of a text, as opposed to its implied meaning.

Why Logic Became a Part of Kalam: The Hanafi Response to Ibn al-Humam
Debate on whether logic should be counted among Kalam's introductory sciences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that logic is an integral part of Kalam, evaluate Ibn al-Humam's objection, and identify the historical necessity that justified this inclusion.

Core Idea
Logic became integral to Kalam not by equal relation but by historical necessity against philosophical attacks.

In this passage, we encounter a critical refinement of an earlier objection by Ibn al-Humam in al-Tahreer. He argued that counting logic among the preludes of Kalam is far-fetched because logic is equally needed for all sciences. The Hanafi response, citing later scholars and the historical development of Kalam, demonstrates that logic became a part of Kalam itself due to the rise of philosophical skepticism. This dispute reveals how Hanafi usul adapts its methodology to preserve the integrity of Islamic belief.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two opposing positions
First, locate Ibn al-Humam's claim that logic has equal relationship to all sciences (istiwa' nisbatiha ila kull al-'ulum). Then find the response that later scholars made logic a part (juz') of Kalam, citing al-Sayyid al-Sharif.
The opposing position is not a different madhhab but an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between earlier and later scholars.
2
Analyse the Hanafi response
The response refutes the idea of equal relationship by showing that historically, logic was incorporated into Kalam as an essential component to refute the philosophers. This transformation changed the subject matter of Kalam to 'absolute existence'.
The key proof is the statement of al-Sayyid al-Sharif in Sharh al-Maqasid and the practice of the mutakhkhirin.
3
Trace the historical context
Read the quote from Sharh al-'Aqa'id al-Nasafiyyah which explains how philosophy was translated, Muslims engaged in refutation, and gradually logic and philosophical topics were mixed into Kalam until it became nearly indistinguishable from philosophy except for its foundation in revelation.
This context explains why the mutakhkhirin expanded the scope of Kalam and included logic.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider the practical consequence: if logic is only a prelude common to all sciences, it is not unique to Kalam. If it is a part of Kalam, then Kalam's methodology is fundamentally logical and its subject matter includes rational proofs alongside revealed ones.
This affects how theology is taught and how Kalam interacts with other disciplines.

Critiquing the Proof for Tamathul among Jawāhir
Refuting a proof for tamathul using logical analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical fallacy of circular reasoning (dawr) in a theological proof and explain why tamathul among jawahir does not necessarily imply unity of essence according to the Hanafi critique.

Core Idea
If a proof for tamathul presupposes the very thing it aims to prove, it collapses into circular reasoning (dawr) and is invalid.

This lesson examines a refined critique of a common theological argument: that the similarity (tamathul) among atomic substances (jawāhir) proves they share a single universal essence (mahiyyah mutlaqah). The author dissects the proof, showing either the premise is ambiguous (tamathul may only mean sharing a description) or the argument is logically circular. This analysis is a classic example of using rational argumentation (dalīl ʿaqlī) in uṣūl al-fiqh to refute a position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Opposing Claim
The opposing position is that tamathul among jawāhir implies unity of reality—that all jawāhir share the same essence.
This view is likely held by some mutakallimūn who affirm absolute essence.
2
Understand the First Critique: Tamathul as Shared Description
The author notes that tamathul could be interpreted as sharing the most specific derived description, not unity of essence. Thus the proof does not establish a single essence.
This critique shows the proof is insufficient even if tamathul is accepted.
3
Understand the Second Critique: Circularity (Dawr)
If tamathul is taken to mean unity of reality, then establishing tamathul depends on proving the existence of absolute essence. But if the proof of absolute essence itself relies on tamathul, then the argument is circular (dawr).
Circular reasoning is impossible (mustahīl), so any proof leading to it is invalid.
4
Recognize the Two Forms of Circularity
The author outlines two circular scenarios: (a) tamathul depends on absolute essence, and absolute essence depends on tamathul (dependence of a thing on itself); (b) the proof of tamathul is identical to the proof of absolute essence (proof of a thing by itself).
Both are forms of dawr, which is logically untenable.
5
Conclude the Refutation
Since the opposing proof either fails to establish unity (first critique) or leads to impossibility (second critique), the claim of tamathul among jawāhir as intended by the opponent is impossible (mustahīl).
The critique does not necessarily deny tamathul altogether, only that it implies a single essence.

Ash'ari and Mu'tazila on How Reasoning Leads to Knowledge
Dispute between Ash'ari and Mu'tazila on nazar's mechanism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Ash'ari and Mu'tazila positions on the mechanism of nazar and explain the epistemological consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of the dispute.

Core Idea
For Ash'aris, knowledge follows reasoning by divine custom; for Mu'tazila, reasoning directly generates knowledge by tawlid.

This passage from Sharh Musallam al-Thubut presents a foundational dispute in usul al-fiqh: how does correct reasoning (nazar) produce knowledge? The Ash'ari school holds that knowledge is created by Allah after reasoning as a matter of His established custom, while the Mu'tazila claim that reasoning itself causes knowledge by a process of generation (tawlid). Understanding this debate is crucial for appreciating the epistemological foundations of Hanafi legal theory, which generally aligns with the Ash'ari view.

Guided Path
1
State the Ash'ari position
Identify the school and its core claim: knowledge after correct reasoning is by divine custom ('adah), not by any causal power of reasoning. Allah creates knowledge following reasoning without any necessitation.
This view emphasizes Allah's sole efficacy and rejects any inherent causal link.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
The Mu'tazila argue that knowledge is generated by reasoning through tawlid: the reasoner's act of reasoning directly produces knowledge, analogous to a hand movement causing a key to move.
Tawlid means one act of an agent giving existence to another act.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Mu'tazila support their view with analogies like the hand and key, and they base it on their belief that humans create their own actions.
This is rooted in their doctrine of human free will and creation of acts.
4
Analyse the Ash'ari response
The Ash'ari refutation (mentioned at the end) rejects the analogy between reasoning and recollection, noting differences in matter and form that prevent the desired conclusion from being necessitated.
The text cuts off, but the idea is that the comparison is invalid because the conditions for necessitation differ.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: whether knowledge from reasoning is necessary (Mu'tazila) or contingent on Allah's custom (Ash'ari). This affects the certainty of rational proofs in theology and jurisprudence.
Hanafi usul generally adopts the Ash'ari view, making rational proofs reliable but not absolute.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute between the Ash'ari and Mu'tazila schools on how correct reasoning (nazar) leads to knowledge. The commentator explains that the author of Musallam al-Thubut mentioned four schools, but begins with the Ash'ari and Mu'tazila. 

First, the Ash'ari position: Benefit is by custom ('adah). According to Imam al-Ash'ari, after correct reasoning, Allah creates knowledge as a matter of His established custom. There is no necessary causal connection; reasoning does not produce knowledge by its own power. Allah is the sole efficient cause. Just as fire does not really burn but Allah customarily creates burning after contact with fire, similarly knowledge appears after reasoning only by Allah's custom. This refutes the philosophers who claim necessity and the Mu'tazila who claim obligation on God. 

Second, the Mu'tazila position: Benefit is by causation (tawlid). They hold that the act of reasoning directly produces knowledge, analogous to how the hand's movement causes the key's movement. The reasoning is a direct act, and knowledge is an indirect act of the servant. This is rooted in their belief that humans create their own actions. 

At the end, the commentator notes a refutation by the Ash'aris using an analogy with recollection (tadhakkur), but the text cuts off. The point is that reasoning and recollection are different in terms of what constitutes a valid basis for necessitating the desired conclusion. This is a rational argument (dalil 'aqli) about the mechanism of knowledge acquisition.

Key Terms
نظر (nazar): Correct rational reasoning aimed at acquiring knowledge of a desired object.
بالعادة (bil-'adah): By custom; meaning that knowledge follows reasoning according to Allah's established custom, not by any inherent causal power.
توليد (tawlid): Causation or generation; the production of one act by another act of the same agent.
مباشرت (mubasharat): Direct performance of an act by the agent without an intermediary.

Hanafi Critique of Rational Proofs for Good and Bad
Refuting two rationalist proofs for inherent good/bad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi refutation of Proof 4 (equality of truth and falsehood) and Proof 2 (preference without preponderator) regarding the rationality of good and bad.

Core Idea
Good and bad are not inherent in actions; they are determined by the Sharīʿa, not by reason alone.

In this passage, the Hanafi commentator refutes two key arguments from the Muʿtazila that claim goodness (ḥusn) and badness (qubḥ) are rationally knowable. The first argument relies on the intellect's preference for truth over falsehood even when both achieve the same goal; the second argues that divine commands would be arbitrary without intrinsic moral qualities. You will learn how the Hanafī school undermines both proofs by appealing to the limits of reason and the primacy of revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the Hanafi position
Understand the core dispute: Are ḥusn and qubḥ intrinsic to actions (Muʿtazila) or determined by revelation (Hanafīs)? The Hanafī position is that acts are good because Allāh commands them, not because of inherent qualities.
2
Analyze Proof 4 and its refutation
Proof 4 claims intellect prefers truth over falsehood even when both accomplish the same goal, proving truth is inherently good. The Hanafī refutation shows that truth and falsehood are never equal in all aspects (differing consequences), and even if partially equal, preference may be due to habit, not intrinsic goodness.
Key concept: iṣṭiwāʾ (equality) in reality is impossible; the arguer's assumption is counterfactual.
3
Analyze Proof 2 and its refutation
Proof 2 argues that if actions were not intrinsically good/bad, Allāh's choice of which to command would be arbitrary (tarjīḥ bilā murajjiḥ), which is impossible. The Hanafī response: Allāh's will itself is the preponderator; His essence does not necessitate specific rulings—He chooses freely by His will, not due to qualities in the acts.
Key term: tarjīḥ bilā murajjiḥ – preference without a preponderator.
4
Contrast the Hanafī alternative: iṭlāq ʿāmm (general permissibility)
The Hanafī school holds that actions are originally permissible (ibāḥa aṣliyya). Good/bad are attached by revelation; reason can perceive benefits but does not impose obligation. This is the foundation of the Hanafī uṣūl position.
This aligns with the principle al-aṣl fī al-ashyāʾ al-ibāḥa.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
If good/bad are rational, humans can judge morality independently of revelation. If sharʿī, then only revelation determines right/wrong. The Hanafī position limits the role of reason in establishing legal rulings, emphasizing the need for scriptural evidence.

Commentary
This passage presents a Hanafi critique of two rationalist proofs for the inherent goodness (ḥusn) and badness (qubḥ) of actions, a key issue in uṣūl al-fiqh between the Hanafī school and the Muʿtazila. The commentator refutes this by showing that truth and falsehood are never truly equal in all respects due to their differing consequences (maṣlaḥa vs. mafsada). Even if partial equality is conceded, the intellect's preference might stem from habit, not inherent goodness. The second proof (Proof 2) argues that since Allāh commands and prohibits actions, if good and bad were not rational, divine preference would be arbitrary (tarjīḥ bilā murajjiḥ). The Hanafī response, concluding the passage, affirms that good and bad are not intrinsic to acts but are based on general permissibility (iṭlāq ʿāmm) — i.e., they are determined by the Sharīʿa, not pure reason. This aligns with the Hanafī position that ḥusn and qubḥ are شرعی (legal) rather than عقلی (rational).

The Obligation of Lying Under Duress: Husn li Ghayrihi vs. Qabih li Dhatihi
Response to Ash'ari objection that obligatory lying must be intrinsically good

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi argument that an act can be essentially evil but accidentally good due to external necessity, and evaluate the Ash'ari objection against this position.

Core Idea
Lying remains essentially evil but can become good accidentally when obligatory for a higher purpose.

This passage addresses a key theological dispute between the Hanafi and Ash'ari schools over the moral nature of actions. The question is: if lying becomes obligatory to save a prophet or an innocent person, does lying become intrinsically good? The Hanafi scholar responds by introducing the distinction between essential ugliness (qabih li dhatihi) and accidental goodness (husn li ghayrihi), a crucial concept in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. Understanding this distinction is vital for correctly applying the principle that 'necessities permit prohibitions' (al-darurat tubihu al-mahzurat).

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on lying under duress
Identify the initial Hanafi claim: lying remains intrinsically ugly (qabih li dhatihi), but the act of choosing the lesser evil (lying to save life) is praiseworthy due to the external objective, not because lying itself becomes good.
The Hanafi position relies on distinguishing between the essence of the act and its accidental attributes.
2
Identify the Ash'ari objection
Recognize the objection raised by Mirza Jan: if lying is obligatory, it must be good (hasan) because every obligatory act is good. This would make lying both essentially ugly and essentially good, a contradiction.
The objection is based on the premise that moral qualities are intrinsic to acts.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response using husn li ghayrihi
Examine the response: lying is ugly in its essence (dhat) but good due to an external attribute (ghayr), i.e., saving life. Goodness and ugliness attach from different aspects (jihaat), so no contradiction arises.
This is the core move: the attribute of goodness is accidental, not essential.
4
Apply the principle to the drowning/fire example
See how the jurists' ruling on drowning to avoid fire illustrates the same point: drowning remains forbidden in itself, but is permitted due to necessity. The permissibility does not change its intrinsic prohibition.
This reinforces that 'necessities permit prohibitions' does not mean prohibitions become intrinsically permissible.

Commentary
This passage is a response to an Ash'ari objection concerning the nature of lying when it becomes obligatory to protect a prophet or an innocent person. The author (the commentator of Muslim al-Thubut) argues that lying remains intrinsically evil (qabih li dhatihi) but can become good due to an external factor (husn li ghayrihi) such as necessity. This does not entail a contradiction because the attribute of goodness attaches to the act from a different perspective (saving life) than the intrinsic evil. The text supports this with the prophetic hadith 'choose the lesser of two evils' and the fiqh principle 'necessities permit prohibitions' (al-darurat tubihu al-mahzurat), citing the example of drowning to escape fire. The commentator clarifies that permissibility due to necessity does not alter the intrinsic prohibition; the act remains prohibited in essence but is allowed due to the overriding necessity. This is a key Hanafi position on the distinction between essential and accidental attributes of actions.

Evaluating the Mu'tazili Argument on Wujub Nazar and Wujub al-Imtithal
Reformulation of Mu'tazili argument and Hanafi response to avoid regress.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between 'aqli (rational) and shar'i (revealed) obligations to evaluate the Mu'tazili argument on wujub nazar and wujub al-imtithal.

Core Idea
If wujub al-imtithal is rational, it depends on nazar, not on obeying the command — no regress.

The Mu'tazila reformulate their argument to escape an earlier objection, now claiming that the mukallaf can suspend obedience until he knows wujub al-imtithal. This appears to lead to an infinite regress, silencing the Prophet. The Hanafi response, however, dissects the nature of wujub al-imtithal: if it is 'aqli (rational), it is theoretical (nazari) and known through reflection, not through obedient action, so no regress occurs. If it is shar'i (revealed), the regress is real, but that shows the incoherence of the Mu'tazili position. The key is to distinguish the source of obligation.

Guided Path
1
Restate the Mu'tazili Reformulation
Identify the new phrasing of the argument: the mukallaf says 'I will not obey unless I know wujub al-imtithal', and knowledge of wujub al-imtithal depends on obeying, creating a loop that seems to silence the Prophet.
This avoids the earlier objection that wujub nazar in nafs al-amr is independent of nazar.
2
Analyze the Hanafi Response: Split the Cases
The Hanafi analysis examines two possibilities for wujub al-imtithal: if it is 'aqli (rational) or shar'i (revealed). The ruling changes based on this distinction.
Hanafi usul treats rational and revealed obligations differently.
3
Case 1: 'Aqli Wujub al-Imtithal
If wujub al-imtithal is rational, it is a theoretical (nazari) obligation—it is known through reflection (nazar), not through performing the act of obedience. Therefore, it does not depend on imtithal bil-shar', and the regress is avoided.
Rational obligations are known by reason, not by prior obedience.
4
Case 2: Shar'i Wujub al-Imtithal
If wujub al-imtithal is revealed, then knowing it depends on prior obedience (imtithal bil-shar'), which indeed leads to a regress. But this regress shows the impossibility of the Mu'tazili position, not a problem for the Hanafi.
The regress is a reductio ad absurdum of the Mu'tazili claim.
5
Conclude the Dispute
In neither case does the Prophet get silenced. The Mu'tazili argument fails because it conflates rational and revealed sources of obligation. The Hanafi position upholds the possibility of commanding nazar to the initial skeptic.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf: the validity of imposing nazar on a skeptic.

An act is good due to an external purpose (wasita) beyond the servant's control—like need, soul, or the Kaaba.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and expansion of the usul term 'al-husn li-ghayrihi' (goodness due to another). The author explains that in acts of worship like zakat, fasting (sawm), and hajj, the goodness is not intrinsic to the physical actions themselves but derives from a purpose (wasita) external to the servant's choice. The passage then presents two interpretations of the matn's phrase 'كالزكوة والصوم والحج' (like zakat, fasting, hajj). The first interpretation, supported by Sadr al-Shari'ah and al-Taftazani, holds that the intermediaries are 'relieving the need of the poor' (دفع حاجة الفقير), 'subduing the soul' (قهر النفس), and 'honoring the House' (شرف البيت). According to this view, the attribution of legislation is to the core element (need, soul, House) rather than the action of relieving, subduing, or honoring, because those actions would conflate the intermediary with the act itself. The second interpretation prefers the simpler reading: the intermediaries are merely 'need,' 'soul,' and 'House' without the verbal nouns, avoiding the problem of identity between intermediary and act. The passage concludes that the preferred view is that the intermediaries are the simple entities (need, soul, House) because they are not under human control and serve as the basis for the goodness of the acts. This exemplifies the Hanafi use of istihsan (juristic preference) to identify the underlying rationale (illa) for legislation.

حسن لغیره (ḥusn li-ghayrihi): Goodness of an act derived from an external factor, not intrinsic to the act itself.
واسطہ (wāsiṭa): The intermediary or external purpose that confers goodness upon the act.

Consistency in Selecting Intermediaries: Critique of the Author's Choices
Critique of author's choice of distant vs. close intermediaries

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify distant and close intermediaries in a causal chain and evaluate an author's consistency in selecting them according to the Hanafi principle that the intermediary need not be good.

Core Idea
Intermediaries need not be good; only the outcome matters, but authors must be consistent in their selection.

This passage from the commentary on *Muslim al-Thubut* presents a detailed critique of the author's classification of intermediaries (wasa'it). The commentator argues that the author mixes distant and close intermediaries inconsistently, leading to unnecessary metaphorical omission and conflict with most usul scholars. Understanding this critique will sharpen your ability to critically evaluate usul arguments and apply the key principle that the intermediary's own moral quality is irrelevant to the moral value of the action it serves.

Differentiate Distant vs. Close Intermediaries

Commentary
This passage is a critical commentary on the author's (the matn writer's) choice of intermediaries (wasa'it) in actions. The commentator begins by clarifying a marginal note: the intermediary in an action does not need to be good in itself; only the outcome matters. Then he presents a detailed critique (muhakamah). He argues that the author has inconsistently used distant intermediaries in some categories and close intermediaries in others, which leads to disagreement with most usul scholars and forces him into metaphorical omission (majaz bi al-hadhf). The commentator prefers the view of the authors of al-Tawdih and al-Talwih, who are more consistent. The underlying principle is that the intermediary's own moral quality is irrelevant; what matters is whether it leads to good or evil.

Key Terms

وسائط بعیدہ (wasāʾit baʿīdah): Distant intermediaries, indirect causes for the moral qualification of an action.
وسائط قریبہ (wasāʾit qarībah): Close intermediaries, direct causes for the moral qualification of an action.
