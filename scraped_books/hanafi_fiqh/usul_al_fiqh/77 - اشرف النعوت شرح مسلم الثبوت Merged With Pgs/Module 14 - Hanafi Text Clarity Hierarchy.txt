Defining Nazar: Purposeful Reasoning in Usul al-Fiqh
Defining nazar and discussing scholarly definitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the technical definition of nazar in Hanafi usul and distinguish it from general reflection (fikr) by identifying its key components.

Core Idea
Nazar is purposeful reasoning aimed at acquiring knowledge or probability, not aimless thought.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational term 'nazar' (reasoning) as used in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The passage from the commentary on Muslim al-Thubut provides the matn definition and then expands it with scholarly variations. Understanding nazar is essential because it is the primary tool for ijtihad and istinbat. We will see how Hanafi scholars define it, what makes it obligatory, and how it differs from ordinary reflection.

Guided Path
1
Parse the matn definition
Identify the three key components: (a) arrangement of known matters, (b) purpose of obtaining the unknown, (c) obligatory status. The simple being (Allah) is neither kasib nor muktasab because He does not acquire knowledge.
The matn connects nazar to wajib because it is a premise for knowing obligatory matters.
2
Distinguish nazar from general thought
Nazar is purposeful; stray thoughts (hadith al-nafs) are not nazar. The definition requires intentionality (qasd).
Imam al-Haramayn specifically excludes aimless mental transitions.
3
Compare the scholarly definitions
Qadi Abu Bakr defines it as reflection seeking knowledge or probability; Ibn Hajib similarly; the author of Abkar al-Ahkam adds composition and suitability; Imam al-Haramayn emphasizes intentional transition among meanings.
These are differences in emphasis, not substance.
4
Relate to Hanafi usul
Nazar is wajib because it is the means to know shar'i rulings. Hanafis use it in istinbat as the process of arranging premises to derive hukm.
This ties nazar to the practical methodology of ijtihad.
5
Apply the legal definition dissector
Map the components: mahall (nazir), sabab (need to know unknown), shart (proper arrangement and intention), mani (stray thoughts), athar (knowledge or probability).
This tool systematizes the definition.

Text of al-Muslim: "Nazar is the arrangement of known matters to obtain the unknown. It is obligatory because it is a premise of the obligatory. And the simple being is not a 'kasib' because it does not accept action, nor 'muktasab' because an accident does not inform of the essence."

Translation: Nazar is to arrange known matters in order to obtain the unknown. It is obligatory because it is a premise of the obligatory. The simple being is not a 'kasib' because it does not accept movement/action, nor is it 'muktasab' because an accident does not inform of the essence.

Commentary: According to the author, logic is part of kalam, and kalam is among the preliminaries of usul al-fiqh. Being a preliminary requires that logic also be mentioned alongside kalam. However, the author has already thoroughly discussed logic in his book Ifadat wa-Sullam, so there is no need now. But he mentions a few necessary terms that are important to mention in this science. So he said: "Nazar is the arrangement of the intelligible..." etc.

His statement: "Nazar is the arrangement of the intelligible to obtain the unknown." In language, nazar is used in several meanings: waiting (looking with the eye), mercy, reflection, and consideration. But here the last meaning, i.e., consideration, is intended.

There are also various scholarly definitions of nazar in terminology:
(1) Qadi Abu Bakr said: "Nazar is the reflection by which the person engaged in it seeks knowledge or probability," as al-Amidi mentioned in al-Ahkam. That is, nazar is called the thought by which the thinker obtains knowledge or probability, as al-Amidi said in al-Ahkam.
(2) In Abkar al-Ahkam, nazar is defined in these words: "Nazar is an expression for the operation of the intellect upon prior matters known or probable, suitable for the desired goal, with a specific composition, intending to obtain what is not yet present in the intellect." That is, the name nazar is given to the operation of the intellect, with a specific composition and in accordance with the means, upon prior known or probable matters appropriate to the desired goal, for the purpose of obtaining what is not yet obtained in the intellect.
(3) Allamah Ibn Hajib said in al-Mukhtasar: "Nazar is the reflection by which knowledge or probability is sought." That is, nazar is called such reflection by which knowledge or probability is sought.
(4) In Sharh al-Mukhtasar of Qadi 'Adud: "Nazar is the intentional transition of the soul among meanings; sometimes it is for seeking knowledge or probability, then it is called nazar; sometimes it is not like that, such as most inner thoughts, then it is not called nazar." This is explicitly stated by Imam al-Haramayn in al-Shamil. Al-Amidi's statement that nazar is reflection and then its interpretation as 'that by which probability or knowledge is sought' is far-fetched.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and discussion of the term 'nazar' (reasoning/reflection) as an introductory topic in usul al-fiqh. The author of the sharh explains that the matn from Muslim al-Thubut defines nazar as 'the arrangement of known matters to obtain the unknown.' He then notes that nazar is obligatory because it is a premise for knowing what is wajib. The matn also mentions that the simple being (the Necessary Being, Allah) is neither a 'kasib' (one who acquires) nor 'muktasab' (acquired) because it does not accept action and an accident does not inform of the essence.

Following the matn, the sharh elaborates on the linguistic and technical meanings of nazar. Linguistically, nazar can mean waiting, mercy, reflection, or consideration. Here the intended meaning is 'consideration' (i'tibar). Technically, different scholars have given definitions: Qadi Abu Bakr (via al-Amidi) defines it as reflection that seeks knowledge or probability; the author of Abkar al-Ahkam defines it as the intellect's operation on prior known or probable matters suitable for the desired goal; Ibn Hajib defines it as reflection seeking knowledge or probability; and Qadi 'Adud (citing Imam al-Haramayn) defines it as the intentional transition of the soul among meanings — if it seeks knowledge or probability it is called nazar; otherwise it is just inner thought. The sharh notes that al-Amidi's equation of nazar with fikr and then interpreting it as 'that by which probability or knowledge is sought' is considered far-fetched by some.

This discussion is placed here because logic is a preliminary to kalam, and kalam is a preliminary to usul al-fiqh. The author had already covered logic in his earlier works, so he only mentions essential terms like nazar.

Authority: This is from a later commentary (sharh) on Muslim al-Thubut, which is a matn in usul al-fiqh. The definitions cited are from recognized scholars of usul and kalam. The Hanafi context is that nazar is considered a means to obtain shar'i rulings, and its study is necessary for ijtihad. The emphasis on the definition of nazar reflects the Hanafi approach of grounding usul in rational epistemology.

Key Terms
النظر (al-nazar): The arrangement of known matters (or operation of the intellect upon prior known/probable matters) to obtain the unknown.
الفكر (al-fikr): Reflection; some scholars equate it with nazar, others consider it more general (any mental movement).

Definition of Nazar and Critique of Mulahazah
Critique of defining nazar as observation of simple meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi usul definition of nazar requires tartib and distinguish it from mere mulahazah, and identify the proof that a simple meaning cannot be acquired.

Core Idea
Nazar is not mere observation; it is an ordered mental process (tartib) that is acquired (kasb), whereas simple meanings are self-evident and not acquired.

In Hanafi usul, the term nazar (speculation or reflection) is carefully distinguished from simple awareness (mulahazah). This passage from a classical sharh critiques a definition that equates the two, using a rational argument that simple meanings cannot be acquired — they are known through self-evident concomitants. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping how legal reasoning (istinbat) must be an active, ordered process.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Definition Under Attack
The passage states that some define nazar simply as mulahazah (observation) of a simple meaning. Recognize that this is the target of criticism.
2
Understand the Objection
The objector argues that mulahazah is not kasb (acquired knowledge) because the known essence (mahiyyah ma’lumah) is a necessary concomitant (lazim bayyin) of the simple meaning. Concomitants are not acquired — they are self-evident.
3
Analyze the Proof
The commentator then proves that a simple entity cannot be defined or acquired, either through itself or through a composite. This reinforces why nazar must involve multiple steps.
The key phrase: 'lāna al-ʿāriḍ lā yufīdu al-kunh' (because the accidental does not give the essence).
4
Accept the Alternative
The correct definition of nazar is as tartib (arrangement) that requires two movements (i.e., discursive thought). This ensures nazar is kasb.
5
Apply the Lesson
In practical usul reasoning, any legal inference must be based on ordered reflection (tartib) — not mere intuition. This distinguishes valid istinbat from subjective opinion.

The conclusion of the objection is that although the presence and observation of a simple meaning is possible and from it the known essence is obtained, nonetheless this presence and observation of the simple meaning is not called acquisition (kasb), because the known essence is among the self-evident concomitants of the simple meaning, and it is obvious that the concomitants of a thing are not acquired, even if they are obtained after the presence of the simple meaning. In summary, the conception of the simple meaning entails the conception of its clear concomitants, and concomitants are not acquired through kasb. Therefore, the definition of nazar as observation is rejected. The correct definition is that nazar must be taken as arrangement (tartib) which necessitates two movements; in that case the simple cannot be the definition (hadd) or the means of proof (sabab). And his statement 'and non-acquired because the accidental does not give the essence' means that just as the simple cannot be a definition, it also cannot be acquired. The meaning is that knowledge of a simple thing cannot be obtained, because if knowledge of the simple is obtained, it would either be through the simple itself or through something composite. The first possibility, knowledge of the simple through the simple, is invalid as previously mentioned that the simple cannot be a definition. The second possibility, knowledge of the simple through the composite, is also invalid, because its definition would be composed either of essential parts or accidental parts or both. All three forms are invalid. The invalidity of the first (composition from essential parts) is obvious because the subject is simple; if the definition becomes composite, the defined (which is simple) would no longer remain simple but become composite. The second (composition from accidental parts) is also invalid because in that case it would not give knowledge of the essence (kunh), since knowledge of reality is not obtained from accidentals. As for the third (composition from essential and accidental parts), its invalidity is also apparent because the combination of essential and accidental is accidental, just as the combination of inside and outside is outside, and the result follows the lower. Thus the third is reducible to the second. Therefore, just as knowledge of the essence (kunh) cannot be obtained from accidentals, similarly it cannot be obtained from the combination of essential and accidentals. Pointing to the last two possibilities, the author (matn) said: 'because the accidental does not give the essence (kunh).'

Commentary
This passage is an advanced critique by the commentator (sharh) of a definition of nazar (speculation) as mulahazah (observation). The objection is that mulahazah of a simple meaning is not kasb (acquisition) because the known essence (mahiyyah ma'lumah) is a necessary concomitant (lazim bayyin) of the simple meaning; concomitants are not acquired. Therefore nazar must be defined as tartib (arrangement) which involves multiple steps. The commentator then explains why a simple entity cannot be a definition (hadd) and cannot be acquired, since if it were definable, the definition would either be through itself or through a composite, both impossible.

Key Terms
النظر (al-nazar): Speculation or reflection that is an arrangement of thoughts leading to knowledge
الملاحظة (al-mulahazah): Observation or direct awareness of a simple meaning without discursive effort
الكسب (al-kasb): Acquired knowledge through effort and reasoning
