Taklif bil-'Aql: The Obligation of Reason
Explaining legal responsibility through reason and the excuse before reflection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on taklif bil-'aql and distinguish the role of reason versus prophetic call in obligation.

Core Idea
Reason itself obligates belief after the period of reflection, even without a prophet's call.

This passage from Sharh al-Usul explains a foundational Hanafi principle: that legal responsibility (taklif) is not solely dependent on receiving revelation; rather, the capacity for rational reflection granted by Allah can itself obligate a person to believe in the Creator and avoid disbelief. The author clarifies the concept of 'muddat al-ta'ammul' (period of reflection) and its role in establishing accountability, citing Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling on the safih at age 25.

Guided Path
1
Identify key terms
Locate 'yukallafu bil-'aql' and 'muddat al-ta'ammul' in the passage. Understand their definitions as given.
These terms are central to the Hanafi theory of obligation.
2
Distinguish reason vs. revelation
Note that the Hanafi view holds that reason can obligate after the period of reflection, even without a prophetic message. This contrasts with the Ash'ari position.
The passage explicitly states: 'even if the call did not reach him... he will not be excused.'
3
Analyze the two scenarios
Examine the first scenario: a person who does not believe at the beginning of intellect (before reflection) is excused. The second scenario: after reflection, if he chooses disbelief, he is not excused.
The key is whether the period of reflection has passed.
4
Connect to the safih analogy
Imam Abu Hanifa set age 25 as the period of experience for a safih, showing that a specific age can be a benchmark for the completion of reflection.
This demonstrates that the period of reflection is not indefinite but has a practical limit.
5
Compare with Ash'ari view
The thamarah al-khilaf: under Ash'ari, no obligation without revelation; under Hanafi, obligation arises by reason after reflection.
This difference has real implications for the fate of people who never received the message.

Commentary
This passage from the sharh explains the Hanafi understanding of the phrase 'yukallafu bil-'aql' (obligated by reason). The author clarifies that legal responsibility is not solely dependent on receiving the prophetic message; rather, it is tied to the capacity for reflection (ta'ammul) that Allah grants to every rational being. Once a person reaches the age where they have the ability to reflect on the beginning and end of things, and they are given the opportunity to do so, they become accountable to believe in the Creator and avoid disbelief—even without the arrival of a messenger. The passage draws an analogy to Imam Abu Hanifa's ruling that a safih (foolish person) becomes legally competent to manage his wealth at age twenty-five, as that is deemed the age of experience. The key point is that the period of reflection is essential; before that period, a person is excused for not having faith, but after it, they are not excused, especially if they actively choose disbelief.

Key Terms
يكلف بالعقل (yukallafu bil-'aql): One is obligated (to believe and act) by virtue of reason, i.e., the ability to reflect which leads to knowledge of the Creator without needing revelation.
مدة التأمل (muddat al-ta'ammul): The period of reflection necessary for a rational person to come to knowledge of the Creator through reasoning and experience.

The Hanafi Doctrine of Kasb: A Middle Path Between Jabr and Tafwid
Defining khala and kasb and defending the existent qasd musammam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi distinction between khala (creation) and kasb (acquisition) and identify how the concept of qasd musammam resolves the dilemma of human action.

Core Idea
Acquisition (kasb) is real but does not create existence; the determinate intention (qasd musammam) avoids both compulsion and delegation.

In this passage from Ashraf al-Nu'ut, the author defends the Hanafi doctrine of kasb against the objection that it either entails human creation or collapses into pure compulsion. By introducing the determinate intention (qasd musammam) as an existing attribute (or state) of the servant, the Hanafis establish a middle path that upholds both divine omnipotence and human accountability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four definitions of khala and kasb
Read the initial distinctions: creation is independent by eternal power; acquisition is dependent on instrument and temporal power. Trace how each definition emphasizes the exclusivity of creation to Allah.
The fourth definition ties creation to the effect of eternal power and acquisition to the effect of temporal power, using the example of Zayd's movement.
2
Understand the criticism: the dilemma of acquisition
The commentator (quoting al-Taftazani) argues that the distinction is not beneficial: if acquisition produces existence, it implies human creation; if not, it is mere compulsion. Recognize this as a classical objection against kasb.
This is the core challenge: how can acquisition be meaningful without either creating or being coerced?
3
Locate the Hanafi response: acquisition as attribute, not existence
According to the Hanafis, acquisition does not give existence to the act but rather refers to the attribute of the act that subsists with the acquirer. The act's existence is from Allah alone; its attribution to the servant is via intention (qasd).
This preserves divine creation while giving real, though dependent, role to the servant.
4
Analyze the role of qasd musammam as a middle path
The matn states that qasd musammam (determinate intention) is either among ahwal (neither existent nor non-existent) or existent. It is specified from general texts of creation by reason, serving as a middle path between jabr and tafwid.
The author concludes that the servant is free in partial corporeal perceptions but compelled in universal rational sciences, referring to another treatise for details.
5
Appreciate the consequence for taklif
Without a real role for the servant, obligation (taklif) would be unjust. The Hanafi doctrine of kasb, via qasd musammam, provides the minimal ground for the servant's accountability.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf: validity of religious obligation depends on this doctrine.

Commentary
This passage is a theological discussion within Hanafi usul al-fiqh on the nature of human acts and divine power. The author presents four distinctions between 'khala' (creation) and 'kasb' (acquisition). Creation is an attribute of Allah by which He brings things into existence independently, without any instrument. Acquisition is an attribute of the servant that does not give existence independently but is an effect of temporal power. The movement of Zayd illustrates: Allah creates the movement in Zayd's essence, but Zayd's acquisition relates to that movement. The critic, citing Taftazani in al-Talwih, argues that Sadr al-Shari'ah's distinction is unsatisfactory because if acquisition produces something, it becomes creation, and if not, it is mere compulsion. The matn then presents the position that 'qasd' (intention) is among the states (ahwal) — neither existent nor non-existent, or some say existent. The determinate intention (qasd musammam) is then specified from general texts of creation by reason as a middle path between jabr (compulsion) and tafwid (delegation). The author concludes that the servant is free in partial, corporeal perceptions but compelled in universal rational sciences, referring to a treatise for full explanation.

Key Terms
خلق (khala): Attribute of Allah by which He creates things independently without need of acquisition; effect of eternal power
كسب (kasb): Attribute of the servant by which he cannot give existence independently; effect of temporal power on the attribute of the act
قصد مصمم (qasd musammam): Determinate intention of the servant that is existent (or from ahwal) and is the locus of acquisition
أحوال (ahwal): States that are neither existent nor non-existent, used to describe the nature of qasd by some
جبر (jabr): Compulsion, the view that humans have no real choice in actions
تفويض (tafwid): Delegation, the view that humans create their own actions independently

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi concept of kasb preserves human accountability without compromising divine omnipotence by placing the servant's real role in the attribute (not existence) of the act through the determinate intention.
