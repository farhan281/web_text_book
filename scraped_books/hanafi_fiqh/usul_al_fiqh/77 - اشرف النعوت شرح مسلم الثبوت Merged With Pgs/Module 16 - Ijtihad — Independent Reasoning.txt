Intellect's Independence in Knowing Some Divine Rulings
Matn quote on reason's role in obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the claim that reason can independently know certain divine rulings and evaluate the Hanafi-Maturidi position on reason's role in ethical obligation.

Core Idea
Reason alone can obligate belief in God and forbid disbelief, even for a sane child.

This lesson examines a nuanced position within Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the belief that human intellect, without relying solely on revelation, can independently arrive at some divine rulings. The passage from Muslim al-Thubut asserts that reason itself can make faith obligatory and disbelief forbidden, a view attributed to some Hanafis and consistent with the Maturidi theological tradition. Understanding this helps clarify the Hanafi stance on the relationship between reason and revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
Locate the assertion that 'the intellect can be independent in perceiving some of His rulings.' This is a claim about reason's capacity in the absence of textual guidance.
The phrase 'some Hanafis' indicates it is not the unanimous view.
2
List the rulings reason can know
The passage specifies two rulings: obligation of faith (iman) and prohibition of disbelief (kufr). It adds 'everything that is not befitting His majesty' as a general category.
This includes ethical judgments about God's attributes.
3
Determine the scope of application
Note that this applies 'even for the sane child' (al-sabi al-aqil). This indicates the ruling is not conditional upon adulthood or receipt of revelation.
This expands accountability beyond the usual age of taklif.
4
Locate the supporting narration
The passage quotes Imam Abu Hanifa: 'No one has an excuse for ignorance of his Creator.' This undergirds the claim that knowledge of God is accessible to reason.
This narration is a transmitted proof (riwayah) used to support the position.
5
Situate the ikhtilaf
Recognize that other Hanafis and Ash'aris hold that reason alone cannot impose binding obligations; revelation is necessary. This is a legitimate divergence in usul al-fiqh.
The practical difference affects accountability of those unreached by revelation.

Commentary
This is a short passage from the matn of Muslim al-Thubut, presenting a position attributed to some Hanafis (not the majority). It asserts that human reason, without relying solely on revelation, can independently arrive at certain divine rulings. Specifically, reason can determine that belief in God is obligatory, and disbelief and anything incompatible with divine majesty is forbidden. This applies even to a sane child who has not yet reached the age of taklif. The author then quotes a narration from Imam Abu Hanifa that no one has an excuse for ignorance of the Creator, implying that some basic knowledge of God is accessible through reason. This stance aligns with the Maturidi theological tradition within Hanafism, which grants reason a role in ethical judgments, although it is not the unanimous Hanafi position.

Key Terms
عقل (aql): Intellect, the faculty of reasoning that can independently derive some rulings.
استقلال (istiqlal): Independence in perceiving rulings without reliance on revelatory texts.
ايمان (iman): Faith in God, which reason obligates.
كفر (kufr): Disbelief, which reason prohibits.
