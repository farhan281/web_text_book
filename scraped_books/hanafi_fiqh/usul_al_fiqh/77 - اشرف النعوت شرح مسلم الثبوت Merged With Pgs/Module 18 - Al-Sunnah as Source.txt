The Three Sources as Revealers of the Divine Address
Objection and answer regarding inclusion of Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas in definition of hukm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas are included in the Hanafi definition of hukm as divine address, using the concept of kashif.

Core Idea
Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas reveal the same divine address; they are not independent legislators.

In the fourth discussion of Muslim al-Thubut, an objection is raised: if hukm is defined as divine address (khitab), then only Qur'anic rulings qualify, excluding most rulings from Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas. The Hanafi answer is that these three sources are kashifa (revealers) of the same divine address, so rulings from them are ultimately from Allah. This lesson unpacks that answer and its usul implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
State why a definition of hukm as divine address excludes Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas: they are not direct divine speech like the Quran.
The objection assumes the three sources produce independent rulings.
2
Understand the Hanafi response
The three sources are kashifa (revealers) of the same divine address, so rulings from them are ultimately from the khitab.
Kashif means 'revealer' or 'manifestor', not creator.
3
Define the concept of kashif
A kashif uncovers the divine address that already exists in principle; it does not legislate independently.
This distinguishes kashif from independent sources.
4
Differentiate direct and indirect revelation
The Quran is the direct divine address; the three sources are indirect revealers of that same address.
The composite Quran (nazm) is also a kasihf of internal speech, but it is treated as the indicated itself.
5
Apply to the definition of hukm
Thus, rulings from Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas are included under hukm because they are manifestations of the divine address.
This preserves the unity of the Shari'ah's source.

Similarly, in this sense, the three sources are called the proofs of rulings or the establishers of rulings, not in the sense that rulings are established from them independently.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth and final discussion regarding the definition of hukm. The objection is that if hukm is defined as a divine address (khitab), then only rulings from the Quran are included, excluding those from Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas, which are the majority. The answer, as explained in the 'tashrih' (clarification), is that these three sources are not independent creators of rulings but rather kashifa (revealers) and mazahir (manifestations) of the divine address. Rulings derived from them are ultimately from Allah's address. The text adds a nuance: although the Quran's composition (nazm) is a revealer of internal speech (kalam nafsi), it is not called a kashif because the indicator (dalil) is considered like the indicated (madlul) in the sense of being part of the same reality. Regarding the Hanafi view that qiyas is a mazhar (manifestation) whereas Sunnah and Ijma are not, the reason is that qiyas is more clearly derivative (far'i), so its manifesting role is more apparent. The author ends with 'ta'mmal' (reflect), indicating careful consideration is needed.

Key Terms
كاشف (kāshif): A source that reveals or uncovers the divine address; it does not create a new ruling independently.
مظهر (mazhar): A manifestation; something that makes apparent the ruling of the divine address, especially used for qiyas.
خطاب (khitāb): Divine address, specifically the Quran as direct speech of Allah.

The Unique Opinion: Are Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas Equal to the Quran as Proofs?
Presents a minority view on equality of four sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the unique opinion that ranks Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas as independent proofs equal to the Quran, and evaluate the standard Hanafi position on this issue.

Core Idea
A minority view holds Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas as independent proofs; standard Hanafi view maintains Quran as the root.

This lesson examines a unique opinion presented by some researchers in Usul al-Fiqh, who argue that the Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas are not merely subordinate proofs but are on equal footing with the Quran as 'positive proofs' (muthbit li al-ahkam). They support this claim by citing Quranic verses that command following the Prophet, adhering to the way of the believers, and using reason. Although this view challenges the conventional hierarchy, it is important to understand its reasoning and why the majority Hanafi position differs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the unique opinion
What is the claim? The opinion that Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas are all independent proofs of Shariah, equal to the Quran in establishing rulings (muthbit li al-ahkam).
2
Locate the Quranic evidence cited
For Sunnah: 'Whatever the Messenger gives you, take it...' (59:7). For Ijma: 'And follow the way of those who turn to Me' (31:15) and 'Whoever follows a path other than that of the believers...' (4:115). For Qiyas: 'Those who derive it' (4:83) and 'So take heed, O people of insight' (59:2).
3
Understand the argument's structure
The argument holds that these verses independently command adherence to each source, making them primary. The Quran itself legislates their authority, so they are not mere extensions of the Quranic address.
4
Contrast with the standard Hanafi position
The standard Hanafi view sees the Quran as the fundamental source. Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas are proofs but subordinate; they derive their authority from the Quran, which is the primary source. The verses cited are seen as commands to follow established proofs, not as creating independent sources.
5
Evaluate the ikhtilaf
This is a legitimate scholarly difference. The practical consequence (thamarah) is in case of conflict: if all are equal, no preference; but in standard Hanafi view, Quran overrides others. The majority position is mufta bihi.

Commentary
This passage presents a unique opinion of some researchers regarding the four sources (usul arba'ah) of Islamic law. The claim is that the Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas are on equal footing with the Quran as proofs and principles of Shariah, without any distinction in their status as positive proofs (muthbit li al-ahkam) and manifestors of Islam (mazhar al-Islam). The evidence provided is that the Quran itself contains verses that independently command following the Prophet, adhering to the way of the believers (interpreted as Ijma), and using analogy (Qiyas). The verses cited include 'Whatever the Messenger gives you, take it' for Sunnah, 'And follow the way of those who turn to Me' and 'Whoever follows a path other than that of the believers' for Ijma, and 'Those who derive it' and 'So take heed, O people of insight' for Qiyas. The argument holds that since these three sources are directly attested to by the Quran, they are not merely subordinate but are themselves primary sources. The opinion challenges the view that these sources are only within the scope of the Quranic address and not independent. This is a minority view; the standard Hanafi position maintains a hierarchy where the Quran is the fundamental source, and the others are proofs but not equal in rank. However, the passage does not provide the mainstream rebuttal, only presents this argument.

Key Terms
اصول اربعہ (usul arba'ah): The four sources of Islamic law: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas.
مثبت للاحکام (muthbit li al-ahkam): That which establishes or confirms the rulings of Shariah.
