The Geometric Proof Against Indivisible Parts
Geometric proof against indivisible parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the geometric proof against indivisible parts and explain why it supports the Hanafi position that naskh (abrogation) is possible.

Core Idea
The hypotenuse must be a fraction, proving parts are divisible — thus the world is contingent and subject to abrogation.

This passage presents a geometric demonstration that the hypotenuse of a right triangle cannot be composed of an integer number of parts if the legs are, disproving the existence of indivisible parts. This rational proof (dalil 'aqli) is used in Hanafi usul to establish that matter is divisible, hence created, and therefore capable of being abrogated (naskh).

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of an indivisible part
Define 'juz' alladhi la yatajazza' as the smallest unit that cannot be further divided. This is a key concept in Islamic theology (kalam) regarding the composition of matter.
Hanafi mutakallimun often accept atoms; this proof challenges that view.
2
Follow the first geometric argument (donkey shape)
Assume each leg of a right triangle is composed of three indivisible parts. The triangle inequality states the sum of any two sides is greater than the third. If the hypotenuse were also three parts, equality would occur, which is false. Therefore the hypotenuse must be less than three parts.
This shows the hypotenuse cannot be an integer multiple of the assumed part.
3
Follow the second geometric argument (bridal shape)
Now assume each leg is composed of two parts. It is known that the hypotenuse is longer than each leg. If the hypotenuse were also two parts, it would equal the leg, which is false. Thus the hypotenuse must be more than two parts.
Combined with the first, the hypotenuse must be between two and three parts.
4
Conclude divisibility of the part
Since the hypotenuse requires a fractional number of parts (between 2 and 3), the part itself must be divisible. This contradicts the definition of an indivisible part.
Therefore, the concept of an indivisible part (juz' la yatajazza) is logically impossible.
5
Connect to usul al-fiqh: naskh
If matter is divisible, it is subject to change and creation. This supports the contingency of the world (huduth), which is a prerequisite for the possibility of divine abrogation (naskh). Without this, eternalists would deny naskh.
Thus, the geometric proof contributes to establishing a foundational principle in Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage is a geometric proof refuting the existence of indivisible parts (al-juz' alladhi la yatajazza). The commentator uses the example of a right triangle to show that if the two legs are composed of three parts each, the hypotenuse cannot be three parts (by the triangle inequality) nor two parts (since the hypotenuse is longer than each leg). Therefore it must be a fraction between two and three, implying that the parts are divisible. This disproves the claim that there exists an indivisible part, which is a key philosophical point in Hanafi usul regarding the nature of atoms and contingency of the world.

The Maturidi Doctrine of Husn and Qubh and Its Implication for Naskh
Usul al-Fiqh dispute: defense of absolute generality in moral valuation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Maturidi position on husn and qubh, explain how it permits naskh, and evaluate the Mu'tazila counter-argument.

Core Idea
Moral good and bad are not inherent in actions but are absolutely attributed, allowing abrogation.

This lesson delves into a fundamental usul al-fiqh dispute between the Maturidi (Hanafi) school and the Mu'tazila over the nature of moral valuation. The Maturidi doctrine of 'itlaq al-amm' (absolute generality) asserts that actions are not good or bad in themselves; rather, their moral status depends on various considerations, including Sharia rulings. This directly impacts the possibility of naskh (abrogation). If moral values were inherent, abrogation would be impossible—yet naskh is a well-established feature of Islamic law. The passage from Ashraf al-Nu'ut Sharh Muslim al-Thubut presents and defends this view, using examples like jihad and unjust killing to illustrate how the same act can be good or bad depending on context and Sharia intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maturidi position (itlaq al-amm)
Locate the statement that husn and qubh are not due to essence, real attribute, or added attribute, but are absolute and general. Understand that this means moral valuation is not fixed to an action's intrinsic nature.
The phrase 'al-itlaq al-amm' is key—it means the attribution is unrestricted.
2
Contrast with Mu'tazila positions
The text mentions three Mu'tazila views: early (essence), later (real attribute), and others (added attribute). Note that all share the idea that husn/qubh are inherent and unchanging.
This essentialism is what the Maturidis reject.
3
Understand the naskh objection
If husn/qubh were essential, then a good action would always be good and a bad action always bad, making naskh impossible. Since naskh is known to occur, essentialism must be false.
The logical structure: modus tollens from actual naskh to non-essentialism.
4
See how Maturidi view escapes the objection
Since moral status is not essential but based on changing Sharia considerations, naskh simply changes the consideration, altering the moral valuation. The goodness or badness was 'for something else' (li-ghayrihi), not inherent.
Example from text: jihad is good because it exalts Allah's word; unjust killing is bad for destroying life.
5
Analyze the marginal note's rebuttal
The hashiya addresses a potential Mu'tazila counter: even inherent attributes can be overcome (water's coldness by heat) or suspended (carrion in necessity). It also criticizes the idea that killing unjustly and killing in qisas are different realities, implying that the Mu'tazila defense is weak.
This shows the depth of the debate: both sides refine their arguments.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute in usul al-fiqh between the Maturidi (Hanafi) school and the Mu'tazila regarding the nature of moral good (husn) and bad (qubh) and its implication for naskh (abrogation). The author begins by stating the Maturidi position: goodness and badness of actions are not inherent in the essence of the act, nor in a real attribute, nor in an added attribute, but are attributed absolutely and generally based on various considerations. This is called 'itlaq al-'amm' (absolute generality). For example, jihad is good because it uplifts Allah's word, while unjust killing is bad because it destroys life without right – even though both involve killing, the moral valuation depends on the intention and circumstance.

Then the author addresses a potential objection from the Mu'tazila: if goodness and badness are not essential, then naskh (abrogation) would be impossible because the essence cannot change. But the Maturidi view avoids this because it allows the moral status to change when the underlying consideration changes. For instance, the prohibition of fighting during certain months was abrogated, so the same act became permissible. This is possible because the previous goodness/badness was not essential but relational (li-ghayrihi).

The marginal note (hashiya) provides a further defense: even inherent attributes can be overcome by other factors (like water's coldness overcome by heat) or can be suspended (like carrion becoming permissible in necessity). Thus the Mu'tazila's objection doesn't hold. The note also mentions that the Mu'tazila might respond by distinguishing between the realities of actions (e.g., killing unjustly vs. killing as punishment are different in Sharia), but the author implies this response is problematic.

In summary, the Maturidi position that husn and qubh are not essential allows for naskh, which is a necessary feature of Islamic law. This distinguishes the Hanafi/Maturidi approach from the Mu'tazila and affirms the possibility of abrogation.

Key Terms
الحسن والقبح (al-husn wa al-qubh): Moral goodness and badness attributed to actions based on Sharia and rational considerations, not inherent to the action's essence.
النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation of a Sharia ruling by a later revealed ruling, indicating that the previous ruling's temporal applicability has ended.
الإطلاق الأعّم (al-itlaq al-'amm): The principle that attribution of husn and qubh to actions is absolutely general, not restricted to essence, attribute, or specific consideration.
ذات الفعل (dhat al-fi'l): The essence or intrinsic nature of an action considered independent of its circumstances.

The Maturidi Refutation of the Mu'tazili Objection to Naskh
Explaining the Maturidi response to the Mu'tazili objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Maturidi position on husn and qubh and explain how it refutes the Mu'tazili objection that naskh is impossible.

Core Idea
Husn and qubh are not essential (dhātī); thus naskh can change them without contradiction.

The Mu'tazila argue that if goodness and evil are essential to actions, then they can never change, making naskh impossible. However, we observe naskh in the Shari'a. The Maturidi school resolves this by denying essentiality: husn and qubh are due to something else (li-ghayrihi), so they can be abrogated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Mu'tazili claim
The Mu'tazila claim that husn and qubh are essential attributes (dhātiyya) of actions, meaning they are inseparable from the act. Therefore, naskh would require changing the essence, which is impossible.
2
State the Maturidi counter
The Maturidi school responds by denying that husn and qubh are essential. They hold that these attributes are due to something else (li-ghayrihi), such as the divine command or external circumstances.
3
Identify the key concept (li-ghayrihi)
The attribute 'good' or 'evil' is not rooted in the action's essence but in factors outside it. This allows the attribute to change when those factors change, making naskh possible.
This is a core Maturidi principle in usul al-fiqh.
4
Conclude the possibility of naskh
Since husn and qubh are not essential, the occurrence of naskh (as observed in revelation) does not entail a contradiction. The objection is thus refuted.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute about the nature of husn (goodness) and qubh (evil) of actions and its relation to the possibility of naskh (abrogation). The author, commenting on Musallam al-Thubut, presents the Maturidi position that rejects the Mu'tazili claim that goodness and evil are essential attributes (dhātiyya) of actions. The argument begins by stating that if goodness were essential, it could never be abrogated; but we observe that some acts become good or evil after being otherwise, proving naskh occurs. The Maturidis, unlike the Mu'tazila, avoid this contradiction by holding that goodness and evil are not essential but rather 'due to something else' (li-ghayrihi). Thus naskh is possible because the attribute is contingent on external factors that can change.

The author then notes a marginal comment explaining that the objection of naskh only applies to those who consider husn/qubh essential. A possible rebuttal for the Mu'tazila is that an essential attribute can sometimes be overridden (like water's coldness removed by heating) or its consideration dropped (like carrion's prohibition lifted in necessity). This shows that 'essential' may be understood in two ways: as an inseparable requirement (e.g., evenness from four) or as a requirement that can be separated due to conditions. If the Mu'tazila adopt the second meaning, their position is not refuted.

Finally, the author mentions another answer attributed to Mirza Jan, which argues that an act can have different rulings under different aspects (e.g., killing unjustly vs. killing as retribution are different realities legally). However, the author disapproves of this answer, signaling weakness by using the passive voice. The lesson focuses on the first answer and the Maturidi principle, emphasizing that the Hanafi- Maturidi tradition does not predicate husn/qubh on the essence of actions, preserving the possibility of naskh.

Key Terms
ذاتی (dhātī): Essential property, inseparable from the essence of an action.
حسن (ḥusn): Goodness of an action, which can be abrogated.
قبیح (qubḥ): Evil or badness of an action, subject to change via naskh.
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a legal ruling, implying change in husn/ qubh of an action.
مقتضائے ذات (muqtaḍā al-dhāt): Requirement of the essence; can be direct and inseparable or conditional and separable.

Two Meanings of 'Requirement of the Essence' and Its Application to Naskh
Expanding the notion of essentiality and its implications for naskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two meanings of muqtada al-dhāt and apply them to evaluate the Mu'tazili response to the naskh objection.

Core Idea
The essential can be understood as either inseparable or separable due to conditions, allowing naskh even if husn/qubh are essential in a qualified sense.

In the previous lesson, we saw the Maturidi position. Now, the text explores a deeper analysis: even if one insists husn/qubh are essential, the term 'essential' can mean two things. One meaning is strictly inseparable (like evenness from four); the other allows separation under conditions (like coldness of water). This second meaning provides a possible defense for the Mu'tazila, but also shows that naskh remains possible. Additionally, the analogy of carrion's prohibition lifted in necessity illustrates that even essential rulings can be suspended.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings of muqtada al-dhāt
The text explains that 'requirement of the essence' (muqtada al-dhāt) can refer either to a requirement that is direct and inseparable (e.g., evenness from the number four) or to a requirement that is conditional and can be removed by external factors (e.g., coldness of water).
2
Examine the examples provided
First meaning: evenness is inseparable from four – it cannot be removed without altering the essence. Second meaning: water's coldness is a natural property but can be removed by heating, showing that essential properties can be overridden.
3
Apply to husn and qubh
If the Mu'tazila adopt the second meaning, then even if husn/qubh are essential, they can be removed under certain conditions (like a new divine command). Thus naskh is not impossible.
4
Analyze the carrion example
The prohibition of carrion is essential (dhātī), but in extreme hunger (makhmasa), the essentiality is temporarily suspended, making it permissible. This shows that essential rulings can be overridden by necessity, analogous to naskh.
This is a separate point: the consideration of essentiality can fall away.
5
Conclude the possibility of naskh
Both the two-meanings analysis and the carrion example demonstrate that naskh is conceptually possible without changing the essence of the action. The objection is resolved.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and analysis of haqiqah shar'iyyah (shari' reality) and its relationship to naskh (abrogation). The author explains that when the Lawgiver assigns a new legal meaning to a word (haqiqah shar'iyyah), this reality can change through abrogation. For example, the act of fasting 'Ashura had one shari' reality before abrogation (when it was obligatory or recommended) and a different reality after abrogation (when it became merely permissible or still recommended but with changed ruling). The key point is that the moral quality (husn/qubh) of an act is tied to its shari' reality, not to an unchanging essence. Therefore, abrogation can change the moral value of an act without contradiction. The marginal note criticizes Mirza Jan's contrary view, arguing that time differences do not create multiple realities; the same act at different times has the same reality but the ruling changes according to the Lawgiver's command. This defends the Hanafi position that husn and qubh can be attribute changes through naskh while remaining 'essential' in the sense of being intrinsic to the act's legal reality.

Key Terms
حقیقت شرعیہ (haqiqah shar'iyyah): The legal meaning assigned by the Lawgiver to a word or act, differing from its linguistic or type reality.
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation: the removal or replacement of a legal ruling by a later revelation, which changes the haqiqah shar'iyyah of the act.
ذاتی (dhati): Essential attribute (here, husn/qubh) that is intrinsic to the act's legal reality.

The Qibla Abrogation Objection: Essential Evil Cannot Be Obligatory
Raises a strong objection to the essential/accidental theory via qibla naskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the objection that the distinction between essential and accidental moral qualities cannot account for the abrogation of the qibla, and evaluate its implications for the Hanafi usul position.

Core Idea
An essentially evil act can never be wajib in any sharia — the qibla abrogation refutes the essential/accidental distinction.

In this passage, the commentator presents a powerful objection to the Hanafi/Mu'tazila theory that acts can be essentially evil but accidentally good. The objection uses the abrogation of the qibla: if facing Bayt al-Maqdis was essentially evil, it could not have been obligatory in earlier sharias. This forces us to re-examine whether the essential/accidental distinction truly resolves all cases of naskh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Theory Under Attack
The passage first summarizes the Hanafi/Mu'tazila position that goodness and evil can be either essential or accidental. This allows an act to change its ruling without contradiction.
Essential qualities never change; accidental ones do. The example given is marrying a sister.
2
Locate the Strong Objection
The objection states that the theory fails for the abrogation of the qibla. The command to face Bayt al-Maqdis was continuous in the sharias of Musa and 'Isa, and the command to face the Ka'bah was continuous in Muhammad's sharia. If we consider one essentially evil, how could it be wajib?
The objection is that an essentially evil act cannot be wajib in any sharia.
3
Understand the Logical Force
If the previous sharia commanded an act that is 'essentially evil', that would mean Allah commanded something inherently evil, which is impossible. Thus, the theory that essential evil can be overridden by accidental goodness fails here.
The objection does not deny the distinction entirely, but shows its incompleteness.
4
Consider the Hanafi Response Scope
The commentator does not fully resolve the objection but affirms the general validity of the essential/accidental distinction. This leaves an open question for further usul investigation.
This is a critical refinement: the theory needs additional nuance.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion and a strong objection. The author first suggests that the Mu'tazila could avoid an accusation by accepting that moral qualities can be both essential and accidental. He gives the example of marrying a sister: it was permissible earlier due to accidental goodness (procreation) and became forbidden when that accidental goodness ceased. Then he raises a strong objection: the earlier explanation (that an act can be essentially evil but accidentally good) fails for cases of abrogation (naskh) like the change of qibla. In the case of the qibla, both the command to face Jerusalem and the command to face the Ka'bah were continuous in their respective sharias. If we say that facing Jerusalem was essentially evil (because it was later abrogated) but accidentally good in the time of Musa and 'Isa, then that would mean an essentially evil act was commanded, which is impossible. No Muslim can claim that an essentially evil act was wajib in any sharia. Thus, the distinction between essential and accidental goodness seems correct in general, but the objection from naskh shows that the previous answer is not comprehensive. The commentator leaves the issue open, affirming the distinction but acknowledging the force of the objection.

Husn Dhati and 'Aradi: Defending Naskh Against the Mu'tazila
Refuting Mu'tazilite objection using husn dhati/'aradi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the distinction between intrinsic (dhati) and accidental ('aradi) goodness/evil, and apply it to defend the possibility of abrogation (naskh) against the Mu'tazilite objection.

Core Idea
Intrinsic goodness never changes; accidental goodness allows acts to be commanded or prohibited in different laws.

In this passage from Ashraf al-Nu'ut, the commentator defends the Hanafi view on naskh by engaging with the Mu'tazilite theory of intrinsic moral value. He shows that even if an act is intrinsically good, it can become accidentally evil when it conflicts with a commanded act. This resolves the key objection that abrogation would require an intrinsically evil act to become good.

Guided Path
1
State the Mu'tazilite position
Identify that Mu'tazila hold husn and qubh to be intrinsic (dhati) to actions, independent of revelation.
This is the 'adherent' view in the dispute.
2
Identify the objection to naskh
If acts are intrinsically good/evil, they cannot change; yet naskh requires a good act to become evil or vice versa. The Mu'tazila argue that this is impossible.
The objection is that naskh would imply that an intrinsically evil act becomes obligatory, which is absurd.
3
Explain the Hanafi distinction
The commentator proposes that acts can have both intrinsic (dhati) and accidental ('aradi) attributes. An act can be intrinsically good but accidentally evil at a specific time due to conflict with a command.
This allows the ruling to change without changing the intrinsic nature.
4
Apply to the qibla case
Both Bayt al-Maqdis and the Ka'ba are intrinsically good. When one is commanded, the other becomes accidentally evil because it prevents the commanded act.
Thus, the earlier command to face Bayt al-Maqdis is abrogated without requiring the Ka'ba to be intrinsically evil.
5
Conclude the argument
The distinction preserves the possibility of naskh without needing to attribute change to intrinsic qualities, thereby rebutting the Mu'tazilite objection.
The Hanafi view remains consistent: husn and qubh are ultimately determined by Shari'ah, but even within a Mu'tazilite framework the problem is resolved.

There is multiplicity of aspects: in terms of essence it is evil, and in terms of attribute and accident it is good. So goodness and evil are related by consideration to two loci. His statement: 'Perhaps they will adhere to it, and thereby it becomes possible for them, etc.' Through this expression, the author praises and approves his own answer, saying: The Mu'tazila who believe in the intrinsic nature of goodness and evil—if they also adopt affirmation that an act's goodness and evil can be both intrinsic and accidental, since there is no contradiction between intrinsic and accidental—then the accusation of being obviously false that was leveled against them can be eliminated. Because if they only hold intrinsic goodness/evil, then the objection remains that when the evil of a prohibitive act and the goodness of a commanded act are intrinsic, then the act that is good will always be good, and that which is evil will always be evil; change and deviation cannot occur, because deviation and change from the essence of what is intrinsic is impossible. But if they adopt the doctrine of intrinsic and accidental goodness/evil, then that objection will not arise. Because that which was intrinsically evil at one time can become obligatory by another and good by another at another time. For example, in a previous time, marrying one's real sister was permissible due to accidental goodness, which was the increase of the human race. But when the accidental goodness ended—in that the increase of progeny was no longer dependent on marrying a sister—then marrying a sister became forbidden due to original intrinsic evil. In short, obligation was due to accidental goodness, and prohibition was due to intrinsic evil. A strong objection: There is a strong objection here, namely that the mentioned answer does not encompass all cases of abrogation. Because the mentioned explanation is not possible in the case of the abrogated command to face Bayt al-Maqdis and the abrogating command to face the Ka'ba, and similarly in the opposite scenario. For then it would be necessary that each of the two qiblas is evil in itself and good by another, whereas investigation shows that something evil in itself can never be obligatory in any revealed law. No Muslim who is sane and mature can dare to claim that. And how can he dare, given that the command to face the Ka'ba was continuous in one law, and then in the law of our Prophet, the last of the prophets, peace be upon him, the command to face the Ka'ba became continuous? Similarly, the command to face Bayt al-Maqdis was continuous in the laws of Moses and Jesus, peace be upon our Prophet and upon them. Therefore, the distinction between intrinsic goodness and accidental goodness is not valid; otherwise, it would necessitate that something evil in itself becomes obligatory. The answer: The answer to the objection is that Bayt al-Maqdis and the Ka'ba are in themselves good intrinsically. But in whichever law, at whatever time the obligatory command to face Bayt al-Maqdis existed, facing Bayt al-Maqdis at that time was good due to that goodness that accrued to the act of facing through the essence, without an intervening accident. So when at that time facing Bayt al-Maqdis is good, albeit through the essence, it is clear that facing the Ka'ba would be accidentally evil, because at that time facing the Ka'ba prevents facing Bayt al-Maqdis, which has goodness mediated by essence. Exactly the same reasoning applies to the goodness of facing the Ka'ba. When the noble law of the master of the worlds (peace be upon him) was revealed and the obligatory command to face the Ka'ba was issued, at that time the goodness of facing Bayt al-Maqdis ceased and this goodness transferred to facing the Ka'ba. So when the command to face the Ka'ba is in effect, in that case facing the Ka'ba is either intrinsically good or accidentally good, and facing Bayt al-Maqdis becomes accidentally evil, because at that time facing Bayt al-Maqdis prevents facing the Ka'ba, which has goodness.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute about whether acts can have intrinsic (dhati) or accidental ('aradi) goodness (husn) and evil (qubh). The author (commentator) is expanding on a line from the matn (Muslim al-Thubut). He begins by clarifying that a thing can be evil in its essence but good in terms of a description or accidental attribute. The Mu'tazila (rationalist theologians) held that good and evil are intrinsic to the act. The author argues that if they also admit accidental goodness/evil, the objection that intrinsically evil acts can never become obligatory (since intrinsic qualities are immutable) is resolved. For example, marrying one's sister was once permissible due to accidental benefit (increase of progeny), but when that benefit ceased, it returned to its intrinsic prohibition. A strong objection is raised: this explanation does not work for the abrogation of the qibla from Bayt al-Maqdis to the Ka'ba, because both qiblas were commanded by God and are intrinsically good, not evil. The author answers that each qibla is intrinsically good, but at the time when facing one is commanded, the other becomes accidentally evil because it prevents fulfilment of the commanded act. Thus, the intrinsic goodness of the other remains, but the ruling changes due to accidental evil of conflict. This preserves the idea that intrinsically good acts can be commanded or prohibited based on accidental considerations. The example of naskh (abrogation) is used to show the applicability of the husn/qubh distinction. The Hanafi position here is that actions have no intrinsic moral value independent of divine command; rather, goodness and evil are determined by Shari'ah, but the discussion here engages with Mu'tazilite views. The passage defends the consistency of ascribing both intrinsic and accidental aspects to acts.

Key Terms
حسن (husn): Goodness or beauty of an act; the property that makes an act praiseworthy or commanded.
قبح (qubh): Evil or ugliness of an act; the property that makes an act blameworthy or prohibited.
ذاتی (dhati): Intrinsic; belonging to the essence of the thing, inseparable from it.
عرضی ('aradi): Accidental; contingent, not essential, can change with circumstances.

Key Takeaway
The distinction between intrinsic (dhati) and accidental ('aradi) goodness/evil allows naskh to be rationally defensible, even within a framework that acknowledges some acts as intrinsically good.
