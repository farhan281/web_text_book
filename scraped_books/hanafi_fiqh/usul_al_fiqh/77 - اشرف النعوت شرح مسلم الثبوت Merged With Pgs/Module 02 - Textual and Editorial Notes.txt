Grammatical Analysis of the Basmalah: A Usul Perspective
Grammatical analysis of the basmalah with four probabilities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four probabilities for the ta'alluq of the ba in the basmalah and explain how grammatical analysis (i'rab) is used in usul al-fiqh to interpret sacred texts.

Core Idea
The basmalah's grammar is ambiguous; both constructions are valid, making pure grammatical preference insufficient for legal interpretation.

This passage from Sharh Muslim al-Thubut examines the grammatical construction of the basmalah. While seemingly a grammatical exercise, usul al-fiqh relies on precise language understanding. Here, the author explores whether the basmalah is a nominal or verbal sentence and whether the implied element precedes or follows. This debate illustrates how linguistic analysis is critical for interpreting texts, a foundational skill in usul.

Guided Path
1
Interpretation as nominal vs. verbal sentence
Recognize that the Basrans view the basmalah as a nominal sentence (mubtada wa khabar) while the Kufans see it as a verbal sentence (fi'l wa fa'il). This affects whether the implied phrase is 'Bismillah' as a starting point or 'I begin with Bismillah'.
This is a grammatical (nahw) dispute, not a theological one.
2
Four probabilities for ta'alluq al-ba
Understand that the ta'alluq of the preposition 'ba' can be to a verb or a noun, and that verb or noun can be either antecedent (muqaddam) or postponed (mu'akhkhar). The author lists four examples.
Each probability leads to a different implicit meaning.
3
Compare the arguments of Imam Razi and Abu Bakr Razi
Imam Razi favors postponing the muta'allaq, citing Allah's priority, glory, and the example in Surah Fatiha. Abu Bakr Razi favors the antecedent implied verb, using the same verse to argue that the implied speech is 'Say: You alone we worship...'.
Both use the same Quranic evidence but derive opposite preferences.
4
Evaluate the author's conclusion
The author dismisses the disagreement as out of place because the Quran itself uses both constructions. The real criterion is eloquence (fashahah).
This illustrates a principle in usul: linguistic possibilities are not automatically legal evidence.
5
Apply to usul al-fiqh
In usul, the grammatical analysis of a text (matn) is the first step in extracting a ruling. Ambiguities in grammar can lead to different interpretations, but they must be resolved by considering the operative word (sabab) and context (siyaq).
This passage shows that even the basmalah's grammar is debated, so usul scholars must be aware of linguistic subtleties.

Commentary: His statement 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim'. The statement of the matn 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim' is a nominal sentence according to grammatical analysis if the implied initial phrase is 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim' is not present, as the Basrans say. Or it is a verbal sentence if the implied phrase is 'I begin with Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim' as the Kufans think. In both estimations, the implied element is antecedent. But some people consider the implied phrase to be postponed from 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim'. Therefore, regarding the ta'alluq of the ba of 'Bismillah', there are four probabilities. The ta'alluq of the ba is either an ism or a fi'l; then each of them is either antecedent or postponed. The four probabilities are exemplified as follows: (1) The ta'alluq of the ba is a postponed verb, as in 'Bismillah I begin performing acts of obedience.' (2) The ta'alluq of the ba is an antecedent verb, as in 'I begin with Bismillah in recitation.' (3) The ta'alluq of the ba is an antecedent noun, as in 'My beginning of speech is with Bismillah.' (4) The ta'alluq of the ba is a postponed noun, as in 'Bismillah is the beginning of my speech.' According to Imam Razi, considering the ta'alluq of the ba as postponed is preponderant. The reasons for preponderance are: (1) Allah, exalted is His status, is eternal and necessary of existence; His existence is prior to the existence of others, so what is prior in essence should also be prior in mention. (2) Allah's statement 'He is the First and the Last, and to Allah belongs the command before and after' requires priority. (3) Mentioning first is more impactful in glorification. (4) In 'You alone we worship and You alone we seek help', the verb is postponed, so in 'Bismillah' it should be postponed. In contrast, Abu Bakr Razi deemed prioritization of the implied verb as preponderant; his reason is the same verse 'You alone we worship and You alone we seek help' because the implied phrase is 'and say: You alone we worship and You alone we seek help', so similarly in 'Bismillah' the implied speech should be antecedent. Imam Razi's view is supported by the noble hadith, where it is stated: 'with Your name, my Lord, I place my side' — the ta'alluq in it is mentioned as postponed. I say: this disagreement among scholars regarding the antecedence and postponement of the ta'alluq of the ba of 'Bismillah' is out of place, because in the Holy Quran the ta'alluq of the ba appears both as antecedent and postponed. His statement 'Bismillah its sailing and its anchoring' has it postponed, while 'Read in the name of your Lord' has it antecedent. At most, it can be said that the verbal and semantic beauty of speech sometimes comes from making the 'amil and muta'allaq antecedent, and sometimes from postponing the muta'allaq, as in 'with Your name my Lord I placed' if we say 'I placed with Your name my Lord and my side' similarly 'Read in the name of your Lord' if we delay 'Read' and say 'in the name of your Lord read' — the resulting disruption in verbal and semantic beauty is not hidden from those of understanding.

Key Terms

مقدر (muqaddar): An implied or omitted word that is understood from the context to complete the grammatical structure.

The Position of the Related Word of the Ba in Bismillah
Analyzing the grammatical relation of the ba in bismillah and scholarly preferences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four possible positions of the related word of the ba in bismillah and critically evaluate the arguments for precedence and delay using Quranic evidence.

Core Idea
The basmala's syntax is flexible: both orders of the related word are valid, and the choice is rhetorical, not doctrinal.

In this passage from Ashraf al-Nu'ut, we explore a grammatical dispute that has implications for how we understand a foundational phrase of Islam—bismillah. While the debate might seem purely linguistic, it reflects deeper principles about the relationship between divine attributes and human actions. We will analyze the four possible positions of the related word, examine the arguments of two leading scholars, and see why the author considers the entire debate baseless, pointing instead to Quranic flexibility and rhetorical beauty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four possibilities
List the four combinations: verb/noun and preceded/delayed. This sets the stage for the analysis.
Use the examples given in the passage.
2
Understand Imam Razi's arguments for delay
Imam Razi prefers delaying the related word based on four reasons: Allah's eternity, Quranic verses about priority, precedence increases exaltation, and analogy with 'Iyyaka na'budu'.
3
Understand Abu Bakr Razi's counterargument
Abu Bakr Razi argues for precedence of the implied verb, citing the same 'Iyyaka na'budu' verse but interpreting the implied structure differently.
4
Evaluate the author's conclusion
The author rejects the dispute as baseless by showing that the Quran uses both orders, thus both are permissible.
The author emphasizes rhetorical beauty over fixed rules.
5
Apply the principle to other Quranic phrases
Consider how this grammatical flexibility might apply to other phrases in the Quran, such as 'Bismillah majraha wa mursaha' and 'Iqra' bismi rabbika'.
This reinforces the idea that language serves the meaning.

Commentary
This passage is a grammatical analysis of the basmala, focusing on the position of the word related to the letter ba. The author first enumerates four possibilities: the related word can be a noun or a verb, and each can be placed before or after the ba. تأخیر (ta'khīr): Placing the related word after the prepositional phrase.

The Significance of al-Hamd and Its Grammatical Nature
Explaining why author began with al-hamd and the khabar/insha debate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the reasons for beginning a work with al-hamd, distinguish between negative and positive praise, and apply the Hanafi usul principle of khabar vs insha to the phrase 'al-hamdu lillah'.

Core Idea
Al-hamdu lillah is not a statement of fact but a performative act of praise.

This passage explains why the author began his book with 'al-hamdu lillah' and delves into a key linguistic debate: is the sentence declarative or performative? Understanding this debate is crucial for grasping the author's intention and the Hanafi approach to language in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three reasons
List the three reasons the author gives for starting with al-hamd: following the Quran and Sunnah, following the scholars, and acknowledging divine grace.
These reasons reflect the author's methodology.
2
Distinguish negative and positive praise
Understand the two forms of praise: negative (Subhanahu) and positive (al-hamdu lillah). The author used both in different works to cover both types.
This distinction is theological but also relates to linguistic expression.
3
Analyze the objection and response
The objection asks why the author started another book with 'Subhanahu'. The response clarifies that he used both forms to fulfill both ways of praising Allah.
4
Understand the khabar/insha debate
The main dispute: is 'al-hamdu lillah' declarative (khabar) or performative (insha)? Compare the two positions: Abd al-Qahir says khabar; Sahib al-Kashshaf says insha. Identify the hanafi position (insha) and its evidence.
The thamarah al-khilaf affects whether the sentence reports a fact or enacts praise.
5
Apply the tarjih
The author favors the performative view. State why: context of praise and analogy with contractual and supplicatory speech acts.
This tarjih aligns with Hanafi usul principles on speech acts.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis explaining why the author of the matn began with 'Alhamdu lillah' rather than 'Ahmadu lillah'. The discussion begins by clarifying that the sentence 'Alhamdu lillah' is not a declarative (khabariyya) sentence in the technical sense because it does not report an external reality independent of itself. Then it raises the question directly: why did the author not use a verbal construction? The answer presents three reasons. First, the Quran itself begins with the nominal 'Alhamdu lillah', establishing it as the most virtuous form. Second, and more importantly, the verbal form 'Ahmadu lillah' implies temporality (huduth), suggesting that the praise comes into existence only when the servant utters it. This weakens the concept of Allah's inherent eternity of praise. The nominal form, however, explicitly indicates that praise is an eternal attribute of Allah, independent of any creature's action. This argument draws on the usul principle that nouns indicate stability, verbs indicate renewal. Third, the nominal form safeguards against potential falsehood. If a person says 'Ahmadu lillah' (I praise Allah) but his heart is heedless, he is lying because he claims to be doing an action of praise while his heart is unaware. But if he says 'Alhamdu lillah' (Praise belongs to Allah), even if his heart is heedless, his statement remains true because he is not claiming an action from himself but attributing the quality of praise to Allah as His right. This is similar to the superiority of 'La ilaha illallah' over 'Ashhadu an la ilaha illallah' — the latter includes a claim of bearing witness that can be false, whereas the former is a pure declaration of tawhid. Thus, the author's choice is based on linguistic precision, theological soundness, and practical protection from falsehood. This analysis reflects the Hanafi emphasis on understanding the meanings and implications of words in legal and theological texts.

Preference of 'Alhamd' over 'Ahmad' in Praise
Explaining why 'alhamd' is chosen over 'ahmad' in praising Allah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why 'alhamd' is preferred over 'ahmad' in praising Allah based on the principle that definitive statements are immune to falsehood.

Core Idea
Alhamd is absolute praise; Ahmad depends on the speaker's truthfulness. Definite statements preclude falsehood.

When praising Allah, the author of Muslim al-Thubut chose 'alhamd' over 'ahmad'. This choice is not arbitrary but rooted in a profound linguistic and usul principle: the form of the utterance itself must secure the intended meaning irrespective of the speaker's internal state. In this lesson, we unpack the commentator's reasoning and the analogy with the kalima that clarifies why a nominal, definitive phrase ('alhamd') trumps a verbal, testimonial one ('ahmad').

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contrasting phrases
Note that 'alhamdulillah' is a nominal sentence (ismiyyah) stating a fact: praise belongs to Allah. 'Ahmad' is a verbal form (fi'l or ism tafdil) implying an act of praising or 'more praiseworthy', which depends on the speaker's sincerity.
2
Analyze the condition for truthfulness
The commentator argues that with 'alhamd', the utterance is true regardless of the speaker's heart — even a heedless speaker declares the objective fact. With 'ahmad', the speaker's testimony (shahada) could be false, as in the case of hypocrites (Quran 63:1).
This is the key usul point: some statements are self-verifying (qat'i al-dalala) while others are contingent on the speaker's integrity.
3
Apply the analogy with the kalima
Compare 'la ilaha illallah' (absolute negation of falsehood) with 'ashhadu an la ilaha illallah' (testimony subject to lies). The former is always true even from a disbeliever; the latter can be false. The same logic applies to 'alhamd' vs 'ahmad'.
This analogy is a form of qiyas used in tarjih al-alfaz (preference of wordings).
4
Evaluate the author's choice
Given that the goal of praise is to affirm Allah's perfection without depending on the imperfect human condition, the definitive nominal form is superior. The author thus selects 'alhamd' because it is more preponderant (arjah) in meaning and immune to takdhib.
This reasoning reflects the Hanafi emphasis on the definitive nature of certain linguistic forms in usul.

Hikmah in the Definition of Fiqh
Explaining the meaning of hikmah in the definition of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three components of the Hanafi definition of fiqh (hikmah, far'iyyah, shar'iyyah) and support each with evidence from the Qur'an and the interpretation of Ibn Abbas.

Core Idea
Fiqh is divinely revealed wisdom (hikmah), not mere human intellect; it is a branch derived from foundational Islamic sciences.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies that when fiqh is defined as 'hikmah', it is not philosophical wisdom but knowledge of divine laws, as confirmed by the Qur'an and the Companion Ibn Abbas. This understanding distinguishes fiqh from other disciplines and roots it in revelation. The commentator further unpacks the terms 'far'iyyah' (branching) and 'shar'iyyah' (legal) to complete the definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term 'hikmah' in the definition
Read the passage and note that 'hikmah' is the first qualifier of fiqh. Find the Qur'anic verses and Ibn Abbas's statement that define hikmah as knowledge of halal and haram.
The commentator uses both Qur'an (2:269) and a companion's tafsir to establish this meaning.
2
Distinguish between the two interpretations of the author's phrase
The commentator presents two probabilities: either the phrase is the author's own definition of fiqh, or it is a description of fiqh's characteristic to prepare for a later definition. Weigh the evidence from context.
The second probability is supported by the fact that a later definition uses 'qalu' (they said).
3
Explain the meaning of 'far'iyyah'
Analyze why fiqh is called 'far'iyyah' (branching). Identify the foundational sciences from which fiqh branches, such as theology (kalam) and usul al-fiqh.
This reflects the Hanafi view that fiqh is a derivative discipline.
4
Explain the meaning of 'shar'iyyah'
Understand that 'shar'iyyah' restricts fiqh to rulings established by Shariah evidence (Qur'an, Sunnah, consensus, qiyas). Contrast with purely rational rulings.
This qualifier excludes philosophical or customary norms unless supported by revelation.
5
Synthesize the full definition
Combine the three qualifiers: fiqh is a branch of wisdom that is established by Shariah evidence. State the definition in one sentence.
The full definition guards against reducing fiqh to mere intellect or tradition.

It is apparent that in the noble Qur'an, in most places, by 'wisdom' is meant the knowledge of divine laws. Allah Most High says: 'Whoever is given wisdom has been given abundant good.' It is narrated from Abdullah ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him) that in the Qur'an, 'wisdom' means knowledge of the lawful and unlawful. Similarly, in the verse 'Call to the way of your Lord with wisdom and goodly exhortation,' 'wisdom' means fiqh and shariah. Thus, the author's intended meaning is that in the custom of Shariah, it is wisdom that is called fiqh, because it is generally beneficial for creation. Since the fact that fiqh is wisdom is among the specific characteristics of fiqh in the custom of Shariah, as came in the interpretation of Ibn Abbas (may Allah be pleased with him). Therefore, the author's statement 'al-fiqhu hikmatun far'iyyah' can have two probabilities: one, that it is the author's own definition of fiqh, as is apparent from the context, and later the definition of fiqh is mentioned with the word 'qalu' (they said) attributed to the people (the scholars), which supports that it is the author's personal definition. The second probability is that it is not a definition but a mention of the characteristic of fiqh at the beginning, so that for the definition to be mentioned later, this characteristic would produce complete distinction and perfect insight. His word 'far'iyyah' means 'branching off' i.e., that knowledge which branches off from the knowledge of essence and attributes, necessities of religion, principles of religion, or the science of usul al-fiqh and its introductory disciplines like kalam. His word 'shar'iyyah' - shar' in language refers to a path and a way, and in terminology it refers to that way which Allah has prescribed for His servants in religion. The meaning is that fiqh is the name of such a branch of wisdom that is established by a legal proof that speaks with truth and correctness.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the author's definition of fiqh as 'hikmatun far'iyyatun shar'iyyatun' (a branch of legal wisdom). The commentator first establishes from the Qur'an and the interpretation of Ibn Abbas (RA) that 'hikmah' (wisdom) in the Qur'an refers to knowledge of divine laws, specifically fiqh. Thus, describing fiqh as 'hikmah' is rooted in Qur'anic usage. The commentator then notes two interpretations of the author's phrase: either it is the author's own definition, or it is a description of fiqh's distinguishing characteristic. The word 'far'iyyah' indicates that fiqh is derivative—it branches off from foundational sciences like theology (kalam) and usul al-fiqh. The word 'shar'iyyah' restricts it to what is established by Shariah evidence. This passage guards against misunderstanding fiqh as mere rational wisdom; it is specifically wisdom grounded in divine revelation.

Key Terms
حكمة (hikmah): In this passage, hikmah means knowledge of divine laws (sharia rulings), synonymous with fiqh as understood in the Qur'anic usage.
فرعية (far'iyyah): Branching off from fundamental sciences like theology and usul al-fiqh.
شرعية (shar'iyyah): Established by Shariah evidence, i.e., from the Qur'an, Sunnah, consensus, or analogy.
