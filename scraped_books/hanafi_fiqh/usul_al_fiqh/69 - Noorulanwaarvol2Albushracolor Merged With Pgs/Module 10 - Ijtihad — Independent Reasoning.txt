Conditions of Ijtihad in the Hanafi School
Stating the conditions and obligation of ijtihad for a mujtahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for ijtihad and apply them to determine when a jurist may derive a ruling by qiyas on the imam's principles.

Core Idea
A Hanafi jurist must know core texts and general principles to perform ijtihad; without a direct narration, he may derive rulings by qiyas on the imam's method.

Ijtihad in the Hanafi school is not boundless creativity but a disciplined exercise rooted in the transmitted principles of the imams. This lesson extracts from the commentary of Nur al-Anwar the precise conditions that qualify a jurist to perform ijtihad within the madhhab, emphasizing the need for foundational textual knowledge and the ability to extend rulings analogically from the imam's own method.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central rule
Pinpoint the statement that defines the condition for ijtihad in the Hanafi school. The text says: 'شرط الاجتهاد حفظ المبسوط وظاهر الرواية' (the condition of ijtihad is memorization of al-Mabsut and Zahir al-Riwayah). This is the core legal rule.
This is a condition for ijtihad within the madhhab, not for independent ijtihad.
2
Understand the scope of the condition
The condition includes knowledge of the general ijtihadi principles (الكليات الاجتهادية). This means the jurist must be able to apply the imam's method to new cases, not merely recite previous rulings.
The phrase 'على قياس الإمام أبي حنيفة' shows the result is attributed to the imam's method.
3
Recognize the permission for qiyas
If no direct narration from the imam exists, the qualified jurist may perform qiyas on the imam's principles and state the ruling 'on the qiyas of Imam Abu Hanifa'. This is distinct from qiyas on a branch (qiyas on a furu'), which is invalid according to most usul scholars.
This is a form of ijtihad within the madhhab, not absolute ijtihad.
4
Distinguish from independent ijtihad
The Hanafi jurist does not claim to be a mujtahid mutlaq; he works within the framework of the imam's usul. The condition restricts ijtihad to those who have mastered the core books and principles.
This ensures consistency and authority within the school.

Commentary
This passage transitions from the discussion of istihsan to the conditions of ijtihad. It begins by stating that qiyas and istihsan can only be properly applied through ijtihad. It then quotes a matn line about an incident where the property is safe in the buyer's hand, and the commentary repeats the earlier discussion about the dispute after delivery, confirming the positions of the two shaykhs and Muhammad. The key part for usul is at the end, where it quotes 'the greatest of scholars' stating that the conditions for ijtihad in the Hanafi school include memorization of 'al-Mabsut' and 'Zahir al-Riwayah' (the core books). If a Hanafi jurist does not find a direct narration from his imam, but knows the general ijtihadi principles, he may perform qiyas based on the imam's established principles and attribute the ruling to the imam's method. This is distinct from qiyas on a branch, which is invalid according to most usul scholars.

Conditions of Ijtihad: The Mujtahid's Required Knowledge
List of minimum knowledge and conditions for a mujtahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the minimum Quranic and Sunnah knowledge required for a mujtahid and explain why only the verses and hadiths of ahkam are necessary.

Core Idea
A mujtahid need not know the entire Quran and Sunnah, only the 500 verses and 3000 hadiths of legal rulings.

Ijtihad is the engine of legal derivation, but who is qualified to drive it? This passage from Noor al-Anwaar lays out the precise qualifications. You will learn that the mujtahid’s knowledge is not boundless but sharply focused on the verses and hadiths that bear legal rulings, along with the tools of qiyas and Arabic sciences. We will also explore how this standard applies within the Hanafi madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Define ijtihad and its obligation
Identify the definition of ijtihad as given in the text and note the two types: wajib 'ayn (individual) and wajib kifaya (communal) depending on the circumstances. Recognize that ijtihad is the precondition for applying qiyas and istihsan.
The text says ijtihad is 'bazl al-faqih taqatahu' – the full exertion of the jurist's capacity.
2
Extract the knowledge conditions for the mujtahid
List the conditions explicitly stated: knowledge of the Book (Quran) with its linguistic and legal meanings, knowledge of the Sunnah with its chains and divisions, and knowledge of the aspects of qiyas. Note the quantitative thresholds: 500 verses and 3000 hadiths related to rulings.
The author mentions that ijma' is not listed among the conditions following the salaf, but the mujtahid still needs it to know established rulings.
3
Analyze the scope of required Quranic and Sunnah knowledge
Understand why only the 'ayat al-ahkam' and 'ahadith al-ahkam' are required, not the entire scripture. The text clarifies that the mujtahid needs only the portion from which rulings are derived.
The author references his own work 'al-Tafsir al-Ahmadi' which collects the 500 verses.
4
Identify additional conditions from the commentary
The commentary adds more: sound faith, justice ('adala), correct intention (ikhlas), knowledge of Arabic sciences (lexicon, grammar, rhetoric), and ability to distinguish sound from weak hadith and correct from corrupt qiyas.
The commentary also mentions that for a Hanafi, ijtihad within the madhhab requires memorization of al-Mabsut and Zahir al-Riwayah.
5
Distinguish between absolute ijtihad and madhhab-bound ijtihad
The text explains that a Hanafi jurist who does not find a narration from his Imam may still perform ijtihad by applying analogy to the Imam's principles, not to a subsidiary case. This is 'takhrij' or derivation within the madhhab.
This is a key nuance: the mujtahid fi'l-madhhab derives rulings based on the Imam's usul.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the conditions required for a mujtahid (one who performs ijtihad). It opens by defining ijtihad as the complete exertion of a jurist's capacity to derive a theoretical (zanni) ruling, such that he feels incapable of further effort. It then discusses the obligation of ijtihad: it is individually obligatory (wajib 'ayn) when a specific incident occurs and no previous ruling exists from an earlier mujtahid. If a previous ruling exists, the questioner may follow it. Before an incident occurs, ijtihad is a communal obligation (wajib kifaya) if there are multiple mujtahids; if only one exists, it becomes individually obligatory even before the incident, unless the rulings of the earlier mujtahid are preserved and actionable. The text then addresses an important point: the condition of ijtihad within the Hanafi madhhab is the memorization of al-Mabsut and Zahir al-Riwayah (the core transmitted books). A Hanafi jurist who finds no narration from his Imam but knows the general principles (kulliyat ijtihadiyya) may derive rulings by analogy from the Imam's principles, not from a subsidiary case (far'). The main focus shifts to the knowledge requirements: the mujtahid must have comprehensive knowledge of the Quran with its linguistic and legal meanings, including its divisions such as general/specific, command/prohibition, etc. However, he need not know the entire Quran, only the approximately 500 verses that relate to legal rulings (ayah al-ahkam). The author mentions his own compilation, 'al-Tafsir al-Ahmadi'. Similarly, he must know the Sunnah with its methods (chains of transmission, divisions like mutawatir, ahad, etc.) and the approximately 3000 hadiths that pertain to rulings. He must also know the methods and conditions of qiyas (analogy). The text notes that the author did not mention ijma' (consensus) following the early scholars (salaf), but the mujtahid nevertheless needs it to know the rulings established by consensus. The commentary adds further details: the condition of faith, knowledge of Arabic sciences (lexicon, morphology, grammar, rhetoric) to understand meanings, knowledge of hadith narrators to distinguish sound from weak, and knowledge of the divisions of qiyas to differentiate correct from corrupt analogy. Additionally, the mujtahid must be 'adil (just), and his intention must be for Allah, not for fame or partisanship. Piety (wara') is recommended. The passage emphasizes that ijtihad requires a strong foundation in usul al-fiqh.

The Ruling of Ijtihad: Predominant Opinion and the Possibility of Error
Ruling on ijtihad with proof from ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on ijtihad (ghalib al-ra'y) and identify the ijma' proof from the incident of Ibn Mas'ud that supports the possibility of error.

Core Idea
Ijtihad yields predominant opinion, not certainty; the mujtahid can err, as proven by the Companions' consensus.

In this passage, the author explains the fundamental nature of ijtihad: it is an effort that leads to predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y), not certainty. This has direct implications for the status of mujtahids and the validity of multiple opinions. Commentary
This passage is a ruling (hukm shar'i) regarding the nature of ijtihad and the status of the mujtahid. The author first distinguishes matters of consensus (ijma'iyyat) from those of the Book and Sunnah, stating that ijtihad is not allowed in the former. For the latter, every mujtahid has his own scope for interpretation (ta'wil) in ambiguous and general texts. Qiyas is identified as the core of ijtihad. The ruling (hukm) of ijtihad is that it yields 'predominant opinion' (ghalib al-ra'y) rather than certainty. Consequently, a mujtahid can be correct or mistaken, but the truth in any given dispute is singular in Allah's knowledge, though humans cannot access it with certainty. This is why the four schools are considered valid. The author then presents the proof from the action of Ibn Mas'ud regarding the 'muffawada' case. The Companions' tacit approval constituted a consensus (ijma') that ijtihad can err. The discussion then shifts to the opposing view of the Mu'tazila, who claim every mujtahid is correct and that truth is multiple. Some Ash'aris also held a similar position. The author refutes this by arguing that contradictory rulings cannot both be true in reality. The passage also addresses a report attributed to Abu Hanifa that every mujtahid is correct, explaining that this refers to correctness in practice (fi al-'amal), not in reality. Finally, the passage distinguishes between transmitted matters (naqliyyat, i.e., fiqh rulings) and rational matters ('aqliyyat, i.e., beliefs), stating that error in beliefs can lead to disbelief or sin depending on the nature of the error. The commentary from (al-Qamari) and (al-Sanbali) further clarifies these points, emphasizing that in practice a mujtahid fulfills his duty by correctly applying evidence and conditions, even if his conclusion is factually wrong.

Key Terms
اجتهاد (ijtihād): The maximal effort of a jurist to derive a ruling from the sources by predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y), not certainty.
مصيب (muṣīb): One who attains the truth (in ijtihad) by predominant opinion, but can still be mistaken in the actual divine ruling.
المفوضة (al-mufawwaḍah): A woman whose marriage contract was concluded without stipulating a dower, or on condition of no dower.

The Hanafi Refutation of Mu'tazila on the Infallibility of Mujtahids
Theological-jurisprudential dispute on the infallibility of mujtahids

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi refutation of the Mu'tazila claim that every mujtahid is correct and evaluate the distinction between error in transmitted (fiqh) matters and error in rational (belief) matters.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school refutes the Mu'tazila claim that every mujtahid is correct by showing it leads to logical contradiction and violates the unity of Sharia.

The Mu'tazila argued that every mujtahid is correct (musib) because truth in disputes is multiple. The Hanafi school, while acknowledging that all mujtahids are correct in practice ('amal), insists that truth in reality is singular. This passage presents the Hanafi response, employing both logical arguments and reliance on consensus. It also delineates the boundaries of this debate: it applies only to transmitted rulings (fiqh), not to matters of creed, where error can lead to disbelief.

They argue that contradictory rulings apply to different persons, so no logical problem. The author refutes by citing the universality of Sharia.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling (hukm shar'i) regarding the nature of ijtihad and the status of the mujtahid. The author first distinguishes matters of consensus (ijma'iyyat) from those of the Book and Sunnah, stating that ijtihad is not allowed in the former. For the latter, every mujtahid has his own scope for interpretation (ta'wil) in ambiguous and general texts. Qiyas is identified as the core of ijtihad. The ruling (hukm) of ijtihad is that it yields 'predominant opinion' (ghalib al-ra'y) rather than certainty. Consequently, a mujtahid can be correct or mistaken, but the truth in any given dispute is singular in Allah's knowledge, though humans cannot access it with certainty. This is why the four schools are considered valid. The author then presents the proof from the action of Ibn Mas'ud regarding the 'muffawada' case. The Companions' tacit approval constituted a consensus (ijma') that ijtihad can err. The discussion then shifts to the opposing view of the Mu'tazila, who claim every mujtahid is correct and that truth is multiple. Some Ash'aris also held a similar position. The author refutes this by arguing that contradictory rulings cannot both be true in reality. The passage also addresses a report attributed to Abu Hanifa that every mujtahid is correct, explaining that this refers to correctness in practice (fi al-'amal), not in reality. Finally, the passage distinguishes between transmitted matters (naqliyyat, i.e., fiqh rulings) and rational matters ('aqliyyat, i.e., beliefs), stating that error in beliefs can lead to disbelief or sin depending on the nature of the error. The commentary from (al-Qamari) and (al-Sanbali) further clarifies these points, emphasizing that in practice a mujtahid fulfills his duty by correctly applying evidence and conditions, even if his conclusion is factually wrong.

Ruling on Ijtihad: Are All Mujtahids Correct?
Explains the ruling on ijtihad and the dispute over whether every mujtahid is correct.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on ijtihad and distinguish between correctness in action vs. correctness in reality, applying the rational argument against the Mu'tazila.

Core Idea
A mujtahid can be correct in action even if wrong in reality—the actual divine ruling is singular.

This passage addresses a foundational dispute in usul al-fiqh: when a mujtahid exerts his best effort, is his conclusion necessarily the actual divine ruling? The Hanafi school, following the majority of scholars, distinguishes between correctness in action (fulfilling the duty of ijtihad) and correctness in reality (matching Allah's eternal decree). The Mu'tazila argue for multiple truths, but the Hanafis maintain the unity of Sharia. This lesson unpacks the rational proofs and clarifies the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed claim
The claim that every mujtahid is correct in reality (i.e., his ijtihad matches the actual divine ruling) is attributed to some, including a reported view of Abu Hanifa. First, understand what this claim entails and why it is problematic.
The text states this claim is 'false' and explains why.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi position is that every mujtahid is correct in action (muṣīb fī al-ʿamal) but not necessarily in reality. This means the mujtahid fulfills his obligation and is rewarded, even if his conclusion differs from the actual ruling in Allah's knowledge.
The commentator clarifies that the report from Abu Hanifa is interpreted as correctness in action.
3
Examine the Mu'tazila argument
The Mu'tazila argue that Allah has no specific ruling before ijtihad; truth becomes multiple relative to each mujtahid. They claim no contradiction because the rulings apply to different persons.
This is the opposing school in this dispute.
4
Apply the Hanafi critique
The Hanafi response uses two rational proofs: (1) The Sharia of the Prophet is universal, so contradictory rulings cannot both be correct even for different persons. (2) If a mujtahid changes his ijtihad, the old ruling cannot remain correct (contradiction for one person) nor be abrogated (ijtihad cannot abrogate).
These proofs rely on the impossibility of contradictory divine rulings.
5
Note the practical consequence
The dispute affects how we view differing rulings: Hanafis consider them valid for practice but potentially incorrect in divine reality; Mu'tazilites consider both equally true. The Hanafi view upholds the unity of Sharia.
The thamarah al-khilaf is clear: a Hanafi does not claim absolute truth for his mujtahid's ruling.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion within the Hanafi usul tradition regarding the status of a mujtahid who errs in his ijtihad. The text clarifies that the disagreement here is among Ahl al-Sunnah (Ash'aris and Maturidis) and not with the Mu'tazila, who hold that the mistaken mujtahid is sinful. The focus is on the difference between the view of Abu Mansur al-Maturidi (and some others) and the preferred (mukhtar) view associated with Fakhr al-Islam and the scholars of Samarkand. According to the first view, the mujtahid is mistaken from beginning to end, meaning both in arranging the premises and in extracting the result. The mukhtar view holds that he is correct in arranging the premises (because he did what he was required to do) but mistaken in the final ruling; he is excused and even rewarded. The text then explores various interpretations of what 'error from the beginning' and 'error from the end' mean. Some interpreted it as reward or punishment: the mistaken mujtahid has no reward but is not punished. The text rejects this because it is agreed that the mistaken mujtahid is rewarded. Another interpretation relates to the validity of the action: if the mujtahid is mistaken from the beginning, his action is invalid and must be compensated; if mistaken only at the end, his action is valid but compensation is due if error becomes apparent. The text criticizes attributing the latter interpretation to al-Maturidi because he permitted following any view in ijtihad, which would contradict the invalidity of the action. The majority interpretation is that the error is in the premises or the result. The preferred view is that the mujtahid is correct in premises but mistaken in result. The author of the commentary seems to question this preferred view because if premises are correct, the result should be correct; but he concedes that with speculative evidences, correctness of premises does not guarantee correctness of result. The passage then uses the Quranic story of Dawud and Sulayman (AS) to illustrate that both were given wisdom (correct in premises) but Sulayman was given the correct understanding (the right ruling). The judgments in the story are explained: Dawud ruled to give the sheep to the crop owner, Sulayman ruled to give the sheep's benefits to the owner while the shepherds restore the crop. This shows that ijtihad can err, but the errant mujtahid is not sinful.

Key Terms
المجتهد (al-mujtahid): A scholar who exerts effort to derive legal rulings from the sources, and in this context, one who may err or be correct in his ijtihad.
الخطأ (al-khata'): Error in ijtihad, which can be either in the premises (arranging evidence) or in the final ruling (result).
الإصابة (al-isabah): Correctness in ijtihad, also applicable to both premises and result.
المختار (al-mukhtar): The preferred view in the Hanafi school, attributed to Fakhr al-Islam and the scholars of Samarkand.
ابتداءً وانتهاءً (ibtida'an wa intiha'an): From the beginning (ibtida'an) and from the end (intiha'an), i.e., both in premises and result.

Ijtihad: Error and Correctness of the Mujtahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the different Hanafi positions on whether a mistaken mujtahid errs in premises, in result, or both, and apply the chosen view to practical scenarios of ijtihad.

Core Idea
The mistaken mujtahid is rewarded but his ruling may be erroneous; the Quran shows correctness in premises, error in conclusion.

This passage delves into the nature of ijtihad, using the story of Prophets Dawud and Sulayman to explore whether a mujtahid can be mistaken in his premises, in his conclusion, or both. The text presents and critiques multiple interpretations, ultimately settling on a view that is both nuanced and intellectually challenging. By working through this analysis, you will gain a deeper understanding of the epistemological status of ijtihadi rulings and the reward structure for mujtahids.

Types of Ignorance in Hanafi Jurisprudence
Explaining the categories of ignorance as legal excuses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between excusable and inexcusable ignorance in Hanafi usul and apply the distinction to sample cases.

Core Idea
Ignorance is only an excuse in Hanafi law when it occurs in matters open to valid ijtihad or genuine ambiguity — not when it contradicts definitive texts.

This passage from Noor al-Anwaar delineates the Hanafi classification of ignorance (jahl) as a legal excuse. It presents two categories: ignorance that opposes clear textual rulings (invalid) and ignorance that arises in areas of valid ijtihad or ambiguity (excusable). Through examples involving Imam al-Shafi'i and others, it sharpens the distinction and reveals the underlying usul principles.

Types of Ignorance and Their Legal Effects in Hanafi Usul
Explaining three types of ignorance and when they excuse penalties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given case of ignorance under one of the three Hanafi categories and determine whether it excuses legal penalties.

Core Idea
Only ignorance in a valid place of ijtihad or shubha excuses; ignorance of definitive texts does not.

This passage from a commentary on Hanafi usul al-fiqh presents a crucial classification: not all ignorance (jahl) carries the same legal weight. Under the Hanafi school, whether ignorance excuses penalties such as hadd or kaffarah depends on the context. The author identifies three types: ignorance contradicting a definitive text (unexcused), ignorance in a place of valid ijtihad or shubha (excused), and ignorance in Dar al-Harb for a non-immigrant Muslim (excused). Through illustrative examples—cupping during Ramadan and adultery with a father's slave-girl—the passage demonstrates how the Hanafi school applies these categories.

Carefully read the passage and extract the three types of ignorance mentioned: (a) ignorance that contradicts a definitive text (nass qat'i) — not an excuse; (b) ignorance in a place of valid ijtihad or shubha — excuse; (c) ignorance in Dar al-Harb for a non-immigrant Muslim — excuse.
The first type is implicit in the gloss: ijtihad that contradicts a definitive text is invalid ignorance.
2
Analyze the ruling for each category
For each type, determine the consequence: (a) penalty remains; (b) hadd/kaffarah are dropped; (c) no qada required.
The passage explicitly states for type (b) that the person is not required to offer kaffarah, and for type (c) that missed acts are not made up.

Examine the first example: cupping and fasting
The passage presents a person who undergoes cupping (hijama) while fasting, then intentionally eats, thinking the cupping broke his fast. According to al-Awza'i, cupping breaks the fast. The Hanafi school holds that cupping does not break the fast. However, because the person's belief was based on a valid ijtihad (al-Awza'i's view), his ignorance is excused, and no kaffarah is required.
This illustrates ignorance in a place of ijtihad. Note that the Hanafi school does not adopt al-Awza'i's view, but the follower is excused.

A man commits adultery with his father's slave-girl, thinking she is permissible for him. Because ownership between father and son is interconnected (shubha), the hadd penalty is dropped. If he thought she was not permissible, the penalty applies. Compare with the son's slave-girl (always permissible) and the brother's slave-girl (never permissible).
This illustrates shubha that repels hadd. The doubt arises from the close relationship of property.
5

The passage states that a dhimmi who converts to Islam in Dar al-Islam is not excused for ignorance of Islamic rulings because he can ask. This contrasts with the Muslim in Dar al-Harb who could not have known.
The key factor is the ability to acquire knowledge without excessive hardship.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary on the types of ignorance (jahil) and their legal effects. It opens by stating the topic: 'Explanation of ignorance and its types.' The first type (implicitly mentioned) is ignorance that contradicts a definitive text (nass qat'i); this is not an excuse. The second type is ignorance in a place of valid ijtihad or shubha (doubt), which does excuse penalties. An example is given of a fasting person who undergoes cupping (hijama) and then intentionally eats, thinking the cupping broke his fast. He is not required to offer kaffarah because his belief was based on the valid ijtihad of al-Awza'i, who held that cupping breaks the fast. The commentary then clarifies that this example does not contradict a definitive text, as the verse about not eating meat without mentioning Allah's name is speculative and has been specified. The glosses discuss that ijtihad must not contradict clear texts and define 'place of ijtihad' and 'place of shubha'. The second type also covers a person who commits adultery with his father's slave-girl thinking it permissible; the hadd penalty is dropped due to shubha (interconnected ownership between father and son). The third type is ignorance in Dar al-Harb for a Muslim who never emigrated; missed acts of worship are not required to be made up if the da'wah did not reach him, unlike a dhimmi in Dar al-Islam who can ask.

Key Terms
الجهل (al-jahl): Lack of knowledge; in this passage, categorized into types regarding its effect on legal responsibility.
موضع الاجتهاد (mawdi' al-ijtihad): A situation where valid ijtihad (independent reasoning) can be applied, i.e., not expressly ruled by a definitive text.
الشبهة (al-shubha): Doubt or resemblance; a situation where the halal and haram are ambiguous, repelling hadd penalties.

The Excuse of Ignorance (Jahl) in Hanafi Usul: Non-Negligence is Key
Ruling on ignorance as an excuse in Hanafi usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that ignorance excuses only when the person is non-negligent, distinguishing cases from the text.

Core Idea
Ignorance excuses only when the person had no reasonable means to know; negligence nullifies the excuse.

This lesson explores the foundational Hanafi rule that ignorance (jahl) can serve as a legal excuse (udhr) but only when the person is not negligent (muqassir) in acquiring the necessary knowledge. The text presents several scenarios—from expiation for breaking a fast to missing prayers in Dar al-Harb—each illustrating when ignorance is excused and when it is not. By studying these, you will learn to identify the key factor: the possibility of learning without undue burden.

Guided Path
1
Extract the general rule
From the passage, identify the principle that determines whether ignorance is an excuse. The text states that if a person did not consult a mufti and broke his fast based on his own assumption, expiation is obligatory; but if he followed a mufti's fatwa, expiation is dropped. Why? Because his assumption was misplaced vs. he acted on reliable guidance.
The key is whether the person had reasonable access to correct knowledge and used it.
2
Analyze the Dar al-Harb vs Dar al-Islam distinction
A Muslim in Dar al-Harb who never learned the obligations due to the call not reaching him is excused and not required to make up missed prayers/fasts. In contrast, a dhimmi who converts in Dar al-Islam is not excused because he could have asked about the rulings. Determine the factor: the possibility of learning.
This shows that the excuse depends on the environment and the person's ability to know without excessive hardship.
3
Identify negligence (taqsir) as the dividing line
The text explicitly says that in Dar al-Harb, the person is not negligent (laysa muqassir) because the evidence is hidden; in Dar al-Islam, the convert is negligent (huwa muqassir) because he could ask. This confirms that the excuse of ignorance is negated by negligence.
The term 'muqassir' is central: one who fails to seek knowledge when possible is not excused.
4
Apply the principle to the intra-Hanafi dispute
The passage notes a difference between Fakhr al-Islam and Khaherzadeh: Fakhr al-Islam holds that expiation is dropped by mere assumption (dhann), while Khaherzadeh requires seeking a fatwa. This dispute hinges on whether the layman is considered negligent if he does not consult a scholar. Determine which view aligns with the mufta bihi.
This shows that the principle is debated even within the Hanafi school, but the later relied-upon position is that the layman must act on a mufti's fatwa.
5
Summarize the conditions for jahl as udhr
Based on the above, list the conditions: (1) the person must be genuinely unaware of the ruling or fact; (2) he must not have been negligent (i.e., he had no reasonable opportunity to learn); (3) the ignorance must relate to a hidden evidence or inaccessible knowledge. Compare with the later cases of pre-emption and options to confirm consistency.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul text discusses when ignorance (jahalah) serves as a legal excuse (udhr) that removes liability or preserves rights. The author begins with a ruling from Shaykh al-Islam (probably Khaherzadeh): a person who breaks his fast based on his own opinion without consulting a qualified jurist, even if he believed it was permissible, must pay expiation (kaffarah) because his assumption was misplaced. But if he consulted a reliable mufti and followed his fatwa, no expiation is due, as the layman must act on the mufti's instruction. The text then gives a parallel case of shubha (doubt) regarding a father's slave girl: if a man fornicates with his father's slave girl assuming she is permissible due to the close connection of property between father and son, the hadd penalty is dropped because of the doubt. However, if he assumes she is not permissible but does it anyway, the hadd is enforced. This is contrasted with the son's slave girl (always permissible for the father) and the brother's slave girl (always impermissible). The commentary cites the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father' to explain that the father virtually owns the slave girl, removing the hadd entirely. Next, the third type of ignorance: a Muslim in dar al-harb who never learned the obligations of Islam (prayer, fasting, etc.) due to the call not reaching him is excused; he is not required to make up missed acts. But a dhimmi who converts in dar al-Islam is not excused because he could have asked about Islam. The author then attaches several other cases to this ruling: (1) a pre-emptor who does not know of the sale is excused for not claiming his right; (2) a married slave girl who is emancipated but unaware of her option to annul the marriage is excused until she learns; (3) a minor married by a guardian other than father/grandfather has an option upon puberty, but ignorance of the marriage itself is excused, whereas ignorance of the legal option is not if she knew of the marriage; (4) an agent who does not know the scope of his authorization is similarly excused. Throughout, the text emphasizes that the key factor is whether the person was negligent (muqassir) in seeking knowledge. If the evidence was hidden or the circumstances prevented learning, ignorance is an excuse; otherwise, it is not. The marginal notes (al-Qamar, al-Sunbuli) provide additional clarifications, such as the conditions for the virgin's option.

Key Terms
الجهل (al-jahl): Lack of knowledge of a legal ruling or fact that can affect liability.
المقصر (al-muqassir): One who is negligent in seeking knowledge, thus his ignorance is not excused.
العارء (al-udhr): A legal excuse that exempts a person from a ruling that would otherwise apply.
