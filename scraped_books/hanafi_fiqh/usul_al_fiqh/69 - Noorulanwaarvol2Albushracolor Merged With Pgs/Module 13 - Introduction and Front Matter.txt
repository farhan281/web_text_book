The True Cause (Sabab Haqiqi): Definition and Distinctions in Hanafi Usul
Defining sabab haqiqi with examples and exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a true cause (sabab haqiqi) in a given scenario, distinguish it from the 'illa and sign, and evaluate the liability rule based on whether the cause is pure or involves a breach of duty.

Core Idea
A true cause leads to a ruling without effecting its existence; liability only follows from direct causation or a breached duty.

This passage from Nur al-Anwar defines the sabab haqiqi (true cause), a foundational concept in Hanafi jurisprudence. Understanding it is crucial for correctly attributing legal consequences to actions. The author distinguishes the true cause from the 'illa (effective cause), the sign ('alama), and the condition (shart), using the example of directing a crime to illustrate that a mere cause does not create liability. However, exceptions arise when the actor has a pre-existing duty, such as the depositary's custody or the muhrim's security.

Guided Path
1
Define the true cause
Identify the definition: sabab haqiqi is a path to the ruling that has no effect on its existence and to which neither obligation nor existence is attributed.
2
Distinguish from 'illa and 'alama
Note how the 'illa is attributed both obligation and existence; the 'alama indicates but does not lead. The sabab haqiqi leads but without attribution.
The 'illa is the efficient cause; the sabab is a means.
3
Analyze the example of direction
A person directing another to steal is a pure cause. Theft results from the thief's free choice (the 'illa). The director is not liable.
The intervening 'illa is not attributed to the cause.
4
Examine the exceptions
The sa'a (informer to a tyrant) and the muhrim directing game: these incur liability because they abandon a duty (aman, custodial security).
The later jurists' fatwa on sa'a is based on corruption of time, not on causation.
5
Evaluate the effect of attributing the 'illa to the cause
If the intervening 'illa is attributed to the cause, the cause takes the ruling of an 'illa, imposing liability.
This is a further nuance: the cause becomes like a cause of the cause.

Chapter on Rulings: Explanation of the cause (sabab) and its divisions. The true cause (sabab haqiqi) is that which is a path to the ruling, i.e., leading to it in general, unlike the sign (alama), for it indicates it but does not lead to it without the obligation of the ruling being attributed to it as it is attributed to the 'illa (ratio legis), nor existence as it is attributed to the condition (shart), and no meanings of 'illa are understood in it in any way such that it has no effect in the existence of the ruling at all, neither indirectly nor directly; because if it were so, it would not be a true cause, but rather a cause that has an aspect of 'illa, or a cause in which there is a meaning of 'illa. Rather, it is that between it (the cause) and the ruling there intervenes an 'illa that is not attributed to the cause; for if it were attributed to the cause and the ruling were attributed to it, the cause would be the 'illa of the 'illa, not a true cause, as will come. An example is a person's indication of another's property or person to steal or kill it; for it is a true cause for theft and murder because it leads to them without itself necessitating or creating the theft or murder, and it has no effect in the act of theft at all. Rather, between the indication and the theft there intervenes an 'illa not attributed to the indication, which is the free act and intention of the thief. For it is not necessary that whoever is directed by someone to an evil deed will do it at all; perhaps God grants him success to abandon it despite the direction. If the theft or murder occurs, the director is not liable for anything because he is a possessor of a pure cause, not a possessor of an 'illa. On this basis, it is appropriate that the one who incites (sa'a) to a tyrannical ruler against someone unjustly until he fine him money should not be liable because he is a possessor of a pure cause; but the later jurists gave a fatwa of liability due to the corruption of the time through false incitement and the abundance of inciters. As for the one in ihram who directs to game, he is liable for its value because he abandoned the security (aman) he undertook by his ihram through the act of direction, like a depositary who directs a thief to the deposit: he is liable because he abandoned the custody he undertook. If the intervening 'illa between the cause and the ruling is attributed to the cause, then the cause takes the ruling of the 'illa regarding the obligation of liability, because then the ruling is attributed to the 'illa, and the 'illa is attributed to the cause.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and exposition of the 'true cause' (sabab haqiqi) in Hanafi legal methodology. The author begins by defining the true cause as something that is a path leading to a ruling in general terms, distinguishing it from three other concepts: the sign (alama), the 'illa (ratio legis), and the condition (shart). The sign indicates a ruling but does not lead to it; the 'illa is that to which the existence and obligation of the ruling are attributed; the condition is that to which existence is attributed but not obligation. In the true cause, neither obligation nor existence is attributed to it, nor are the meanings of 'illa understood in it at all. It has no effect on the existence of the ruling, either directly or indirectly. If it had an effect, it would not be a true cause but rather a cause with an aspect of 'illa or a cause that contains a meaning of 'illa. The key point is that between the true cause and the ruling there must intervene an 'illa that is not attributed to the cause. If that intervening 'illa were attributed to the cause, then the cause would be the 'illa of the 'illa, not a true cause. The example given is a person who directs another to steal or murder. The indication is a true cause for the theft or murder because it leads to it, but it does not necessitate or create the act. The act is caused by the free choice and intention of the thief/murderer, which is an 'illa not attributed to the indicator. Therefore, the indicator is not liable because he is only a possessor of a pure cause. This is the general principle: a person who merely sets a cause without being the direct agent of the harm is not liable. However, the later scholars (mutakharin) made an exception for the slanderer (sa'a) who incites a tyrant against someone unjustly, ruling that he is liable due to the corruption of the times, even though he is also a pure cause. Another exception is the person in ihram who directs a hunter: he is liable because by directing he abandons the security (aman) that he undertook by his ihram, just like a depositary who directs a thief to the deposit is liable for abandoning the custody he undertook. These exceptions show that liability can arise from the breach of a duty rather than from causation alone. The passage then discusses that if the intervening 'illa is attributed to the cause, then the cause takes the ruling of an 'illa regarding liability, because the ruling is then attributed to the 'illa and the 'illa is attributed to the cause. This is a nuanced discussion on the distinctions between cause, 'illa, and condition in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Key Terms
سبب حقيقي (sabab haqiqi): A true cause: something that leads to a ruling in general without having any effect on the existence of the ruling, without attribution of obligation or existence, and without containing any meaning of 'illa.
علة ('illa): The effective cause (ratio legis) to which the ruling is attributed both in obligation and existence.
علامة ('alāmah): A sign that indicates a ruling but does not lead to it or cause it.

Navigating a Classical Usul al-Fiqh Text: The Index as a Roadmap
Index of topics from Qiyas to Taqlid and beyond

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major topics in a classical Hanafi usul al-fiqh text and trace the logical progression from Qiyas to conditions of ijtihad and legal capacity.

Core Idea
A classical usul text is structured to guide the mujtahid from the sources of law to the conditions for applying them.

This lesson introduces the table of contents of a foundational Hanafi usul al-fiqh work. While an index may seem trivial, it reveals the deep structure of the discipline: starting with analogical reasoning (qiyas), then moving to istihsan, ijtihad, the conditions of rulings, legal capacity, and finally the principles of preference (tarjih). By understanding this flow, you gain a mental map of how a mujtahid constructs legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main sections
Scan the index to find the major headings: 'Bab al-Qiyas', 'Fasl fi al-Ahkam', 'Fasl fi Bayan al-Ahliyya'. These mark the main parts of the book.
The book is divided into 'bab' (chapter) and 'fasl' (section).
2
Trace the logical progression
Notice that Qiyas comes first, then Istihsan, then Ijtihad, then the rulings (ahkam), then legal capacity (ahliyya). This mirrors the process of ijtihad: first understand analogy, then use istihsan as an exception, then learn who can perform ijtihad, then know the types of rulings and who can be obligated.
Hanafi usul often begins with the rational proofs (qiyas) before the textual proofs.
3
Observe the sub-topics within each section
Under Qiyas, there are topics like definition, pillar, cause, istishab, and unsuitability of certain attributes. This shows the analytical depth: not just stating qiyas but discussing its conditions and limitations.
The index mentions 'عدم صلاحية' (unsuitability) multiple times, indicating critical discussions.
4
Relate to the overall discipline
The index ends with tarjih (preference). This is the final stage of ijtihad: when proofs conflict, how to choose. The structure shows a complete method for deriving rulings.
Classical usul texts often conclude with tarjih and ethical debates.

Key Terms
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning: extending a ruling from an original case (asl) to a new case (far') due to a shared effective cause ('illa).
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Juristic preference: departing from a clear analogy in favor of a different ruling due to a stronger consideration (e.g., necessity, public interest).
الأهلية (al-ahliyya): Legal capacity: competency to acquire rights and obligations. Divided into ahliyyat al-wujub (capacity for rights) and ahliyyat al-ada' (capacity for performance).

This lesson examines the table of contents of Nur al-Anwar, a classic Hanafi usul text. Use this map to plan your reading
