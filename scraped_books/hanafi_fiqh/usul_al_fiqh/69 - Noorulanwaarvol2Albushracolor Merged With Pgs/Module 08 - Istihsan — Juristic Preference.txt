Istishab al-Hal: The Presumption of Continuity in Hanafi Usul
Defines istishab and explains Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on istishab al-hal, distinguish it from the Shafi'i view, and apply it to the cases of shuf'a and the missing person.

Core Idea
Istishab is a shield, not a sword: it repels claims but does not impose obligations.

This passage defines istishab al-hal and presents the key dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. While both accept istishab as a proof, they differ in its probative force: the Hanafis consider it a defensive proof (hujja dafi'a) only, while the Shafi'is consider it binding (hujja mujiba). The practical consequences are illustrated through the well-known cases of pre-emption (shuf'a) and the missing person (mafqud). Understanding this distinction is essential for anyone applying Hanafi usul to real legal questions.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition
Identify what istishab al-hal is: a presumption of continuity of a previously established ruling when doubt arises about its continuation, after reflection and ijtihad, and with no evidence of change.
The ruling must have been known by a proof; the doubt is about its continuance.
2
Identify the dispute
Note the two positions: Hanafi says istishab is a defensive proof (hujja dafi'a) only; Shafi'i says it is a binding proof (hujja mujiba).
The Hanafi position is the focus of this passage.
3
Examine the Hanafi argument
The Hanafi rationale: the preserver (muthbit) does not necessitate continuance; a separate proof is needed for permanence. Acting on istishab out of necessity is like tahrī (estimation), but it is a lower level of proof.
The text quotes: 'al-muthbit laysa bi-mubqī' – the one who establishes does not make permanent.
4
Apply to shuf'a (pre-emption)
Work through the example: a co-owner demands pre-emption; the buyer denies the co-owner's ownership of another share. The Hanafi rule: the buyer's word is taken; pre-emption is not granted without evidence. Istishab here only repels the buyer's claim against the co-owner's possession, but does not obligate the buyer to hand over the share.
The thamarah al-khilaf: Shafi'i would grant pre-emption without evidence based on binding istishab.
5
Apply to mafqud (missing person)
The missing person is considered alive regarding his own property (repelling heirs from dividing it) but dead regarding inheriting from others (cannot obligate his testator's estate). This dual treatment illustrates the defensive vs. binding distinction.
Ibn al-Humam rejects even this defensive use, holding that istishab is not a proof at all.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of Istishab al-Hal (presumption of continuity) as a legal proof. It begins by defining istishab: when the obligation of a ruling is known by a proof, then doubt arises about its continuation or removal, without any evidence indicating either, after reflection and ijtihad. The central dispute is whether this presumption is a binding (mujiba) proof that obligates a ruling, or merely a defensive (dafi'a) proof that repels claims. According to Imam al-Shafi'i, it is a binding proof (hujja muzima) — it forces acceptance. According to the Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad), it is not binding but defensive: it can be used to repel an opponent's claim but cannot impose a new obligation. The practical difference is illustrated with the case of shuf'a (right of pre-emption). If a share of a house is sold, and the co-owner (shafi') demands pre-emption, but the buyer denies that the pre-emptor actually owns the other share (which he claims is given on loan or rent), the Hanafis rule: the buyer's word is taken, and shuf'a is not established without evidence (bayyina). This is because the pre-emptor relies on the original ownership (istishab) and the fact that possession is apparent evidence of ownership, but this apparent evidence only serves to repel claims against him, not to obligate the buyer to give up the property. The Shafi'i rules the opposite: pre-emption is established without evidence, because the pre-emptor's istishab of ownership is a binding proof. The text then discusses the case of a missing person (mafqud): he is considered alive regarding his own property (so it is not divided), but dead regarding inheriting from others (so he does not inherit). This is because istishab can defend his property from heirs but cannot impose inheritance rights on his relatives. The commentary (by Qamar) adds that some later Hanafis like Ibn al-Humam reject istishab even as a defensive proof, holding that there is no presumption at all. The passage also mentions that the principle applies to many fiqhi issues, such as a conditional emancipation: if a master says to his slave 'If you do not enter the house today, you are free,' and the day passes doubtfully, the Hanafi rule is that the former master's word is taken and the slave is not freed, because the slave's istishab of non-entry is only defensive, not binding.

Key Terms
استصحاب الحال (istishab al-hal): Presumption of continuity of a previously established ruling in the absence of evidence of change.
حجة موجبة (hujja mujiba): A proof that obligates a ruling and is conclusive against an opponent.
حجة دافعة (hujja dafi'a): A proof that only repels or defends against claims, without establishing a new obligation.

Definition and Sources of Istihsan
Defining istihsan and enumerating its four categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define istihsan, identify its four sources, and apply the concept to the example of salam.

Core Idea
Istihsan is not arbitrary preference but the adoption of a stronger evidence over a manifest qiyas.

Istihsan (juristic preference) is a key Hanafi source of law often misunderstood as personal caprice. In this passage, we learn its precise definition: it is the abandonment of a manifest qiyas in favor of a stronger evidence from text, consensus, necessity, or hidden qiyas. The classic example of the salam contract (advance payment for future delivery) illustrates how a textual permission overrides the general prohibition of selling non-existent goods. By studying this lesson, you will grasp not only the definition but also the legal mechanism that makes istihsan a disciplined, evidence-based tool.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition
Read the opening sentence: 'Istihsan is based on text, consensus, necessity, and hidden qiyas.' This is the foundational classification. Note that istihsan is not a single type but has four distinct sources.
The term 'athar' here means text (Qur'an or Sunnah).
2
Understand the mechanism
The passage explains that manifest qiyas indicates one ruling, but a stronger evidence (text, consensus, necessity, or hidden qiyas) indicates the opposite. The stronger evidence takes precedence, and the manifest qiyas is set aside. This is the essence of istihsan.
The condition for istihsan is that the contrary evidence must be preponderant.
3
Analyze the example of salam
Salam (advance payment for future delivery) is presented as a case of istihsan by text. Manifest qiyas would prohibit it because it involves selling something non-existent. However, a textual permission (the hadith permitting salam) overrides the qiyas. This shows how text can override analogy.
The text further explains that the liability (dhimmah) of the seller substitutes for the physical existence of the object.
4
Explore the terminological nuance
The commentators note that when 'istihsan' is used without qualification, it often refers specifically to hidden qiyas. This is because hidden qiyas is the most common form, but the term also encompasses the other three sources.
Hidden qiyas is a stronger analogy that is less apparent than manifest qiyas.
5
Recognize the cross-madhhab difference
While the Hanafi school accepts istihsan as a source, the Shafi'i school generally rejects it, arguing that qiyas cannot be abandoned without a direct text. However, both schools permit salam, though on different theoretical grounds.
This difference is a classic point of usul al-fiqh debate.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and categorization of istihsan (juristic preference). It states that istihsan operates through four sources: text (athar), consensus (ijma), necessity (darura), and hidden qiyas (qiyas khafi). The mechanism is that manifest qiyas indicates one ruling, but these four proofs indicate the opposite, so the manifest qiyas is abandoned and istihsan is adopted. The author then gives a concrete example: the salam contract (advance purchase of goods for future delivery). Manifest qiyas would prohibit it because it involves selling something non-existent, which violates the condition of a valid sale (existing, owned, deliverable object). However, a textual proof (hadith and the practice of the Prophet) permits it, so istihsan overrides the qiyas. The commentary clarifies technical points: manifest qiyas is perceived by apparent matter; istihsan is called such because scholars deemed it good to abandon the manifest qiyas; the unrestricted term 'istihsan' often refers specifically to hidden qiyas. The passage also notes that a textual deficient cause (like one specified by the Lawgiver) provides knowledge and is not subject to this discussion. The example of salam is expanded: the permissibility is based on transferring the contract to the liability of the seller (dhimmah) instead of requiring an existing object at the time of contract. This is a classic Hanafi illustration of istihsan.

الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Juristic preference: abandoning a manifest qiyas for a stronger evidence (text, consensus, necessity, or hidden qiyas).
القياس الخفي (al-qiyas al-khafi): Hidden analogy: a qiyas that is less obvious than manifest qiyas, and when preponderant, it is called istihsan.

Bayan al-Istihsan: Definition and Types
Explaining istihsan, its definition, four types, and illustrative fiqh examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define istihsan, identify its four types (athar, ijma, darura, qiyas khafi), and explain how each type overrides manifest analogy with a concrete fiqh example.

Core Idea
Istihsan is abandoning manifest analogy for a stronger proof—athar, ijma, darura, or qiyas khafi.

Istihsan is a distinctive Hanafi usul principle that allows a jurist to set aside an obvious analogy in favor of a more compelling indicator. This lesson unpacks the definition, the four recognized bases for istihsan, and illustrates each with a classical fiqh example. Understanding istihsan is key to appreciating how Hanafi jurists derive rulings that appear to conflict with straightforward analogy but are grounded in stronger textual, consensual, practical, or subtler analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of istihsan
Locate the opening sentence: 'Istihsan is by textual evidence, consensus, necessity, and hidden analogy. That is, manifest qiyas requires something...' Understand that istihsan is the departure from qiyas jali to a stronger indicator.
The definition is operative: it tells you when istihsan applies.
2
List the four indicators for istihsan
From the text, extract the four types: athar (textual evidence), ijma (consensus), darura (necessity), and qiyas khafi (hidden analogy). Each is a valid basis to override qiyas jali.
Memorize these four categories; they are comprehensive.
3
Match each indicator to its example
The author gives four examples: salam for athar, istisna' for ijma, purifying vessels for darura, and leftover water of birds of prey for qiyas khafi. For each, note how qiyas jali would rule and why istihsan changes the ruling.

Istiḥsān: Definition and Three Categories

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Istiḥsān is not a fifth source but a stronger type of Qiyās.

Istiḥsān (juristic preference) is one of the most distinctive principles of the Hanafi school. It allows a jurist to depart from a manifest analogy (qiyās jalī) when a stronger consideration – such as consensus, necessity, or a more subtle analogy – calls for a different ruling. In this lesson, you will learn the Hanafi definition of Istiḥsān and its three main categories, as illustrated by concrete examples from the text.

Commentary
This passage from the Chapter on Qiyās explains Istiḥsān (juristic preference), a disputed but essential principle in the Hanafi school. The author, after citing the hadith on Salam, presents three categories of Istiḥsān with examples: (1) Istiḥsān by consensus (e.g., Istisnā', or contract for manufacture), where the explicit Qiyās forbidding sale of a non-existent item is abandoned due to universal practice since the Prophet's time, showing that consensus can override unusual Qiyās. (2) Istiḥsān by necessity (e.g., purifying impure vessels that cannot be wrung), based on the hardship (ḥaraj) and common affliction that would arise otherwise. (3) Istiḥsān by hidden Qiyās (e.g., the water left by predatory birds), where the manifest Qiyās (based on impurity of flesh) is outweighed by a subtler analogy: the beak is pure bone, contrasting with beasts that use tongues. The commentary then clarifies that the ranking is not simply between manifest and hidden, but depends on the strength of effect (quwwat al-athar). The Hanafi position on 'illah is that it must be effective (mu'aththira) rather than merely correlational (tardiyyah), which leads to preferring a hidden Qiyās with strong effect over a manifest Qiyās with weak effect. This shows that Istiḥsān is not a fifth source but a stronger form of Qiyās. Finally, there is a rare case where manifest Qiyās is preferred due to its inner soundness, but this occurs only in a few issues; in most cases, Istiḥsān prevails.

The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute on 'Illah and the Preference for Istiḥsān
Analyzes the epistemological dispute about 'illah and its impact on choosing Qiyās or Istiḥsān

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi requirement of an effective cause (al-'illah al-mu'aththira) and contrast it with the Shafi'i correlational cause, and describe how this leads to preferring Istiḥsān over Qiyās.

Core Idea
Strength of effect, not apparentness, determines preference between Qiyās and Istiḥsān.

One of the most profound methodological differences between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools concerns the nature of the legal cause ('illah). While the Shafi'is accept any attribute that correlates with the ruling as a valid cause, the Hanafis require the cause to be effective – its effect must be established by text or consensus. This difference directly impacts which analogy is preferred: a hidden Qiyās with a strong effect (Istiḥsān) may override a manifest Qiyās with a weak effect. In this lesson, we dissect this dispute and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Locate the dispute about the cause ('illah)
Find the part of the text that contrasts the Hanafi and Shafi'i views on the 'illah. Look for phrases like 'العلة عندنا علة بأثرها لا بدورانها' and 'الشافعية من أهل الطرد'.
The dispute is introduced after the three examples of Istiḥsān.
2
State the Shafi'i position (ahl al-ṭard)
From the text, identify that Shafi'is (ahl al-ṭard) consider an attribute a valid 'illah if the ruling correlates with it – present when the attribute is present, absent when absent. This is called al-'illah al-ṭardiyyah.
They do not require the effect to be proven by text or consensus.
3
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafis require the 'illah to be effective (mu'aththira) – its effect must be established by text (nass) or consensus (ijma). Mere correlation is insufficient. This is al-'illah al-mu'aththira.
The text says: 'since the cause for us is a cause by its effect, not by its correlation.'
4
Apply the rule to the Su'r example
Show how this principle works in practice: manifest Qiyās (flesh is impure, so Su'r is impure) has a weak effect because it is merely correlational. Hidden Qiyās (beak is pure bone, so contact is pure) has a strong effect because it is based on a proven principle (pure + pure = pure). Therefore, Istiḥsān (hidden Qiyās) is preferred.
The text explicitly states: 'we prefer Istiḥsān, which is hidden Qiyās, when its effect is strong.'
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Identify the actual difference in fiqh rulings that results from this methodological dispute. In the Su'r case, Hanafis rule the water pure, while Shafi'is would likely rule it impure. This practical difference is the fruit of the ikhtilaf.
The passage also mentions that Istisnā' is valid by Hanafi Istiḥsān but may be invalid by strict Shafi'i Qiyās.

Commentary
This passage from the Chapter on Qiyās explains Istiḥsān (juristic preference), a disputed but essential principle in the Hanafi school. The author, after citing the hadith on Salam, presents three categories of Istiḥsān with examples: (1) Istiḥsān by consensus (e.g., Istisnā', or contract for manufacture), where the explicit Qiyās forbidding sale of a non-existent item is abandoned due to universal practice since the Prophet's time, showing that consensus can override unusual Qiyās. (2) Istiḥsān by necessity (e.g., purifying impure vessels that cannot be wrung), based on the hardship (ḥaraj) and common affliction that would arise otherwise. (3) Istiḥsān by hidden Qiyās (e.g., the water left by predatory birds), where the manifest Qiyās (based on impurity of flesh) is outweighed by a subtler analogy: the beak is pure bone, contrasting with beasts that use tongues. The commentary then clarifies that the ranking is not simply between manifest and hidden, but depends on the strength of effect (quwwat al-athar). The Hanafi position on 'illah is that it must be effective (mu'aththira) rather than merely correlational (tardiyyah), which leads to preferring a hidden Qiyās with strong effect over a manifest Qiyās with weak effect. This shows that Istiḥsān is not a fifth source but a stronger form of Qiyās. Finally, there is a rare case where manifest Qiyās is preferred due to its inner soundness, but this occurs only in a few issues; in most cases, Istiḥsān prevails.

Key Terms
العلة المؤثرة (al-'illah al-mu'aththira): The effective cause: a cause whose effect (athar) is established by text or consensus, not merely by correlation.
العلة الطردية (al-'illah al-ṭardiyyah): The correlational cause: an attribute whose only connection to the ruling is that they co-occur; no independent effect is required.
أثر (athar): The effect or impact of a cause, which must be demonstrable by text or consensus for the cause to be valid in Hanafi usul.

Types of Istihsān and Their Transmissibility
Classifies istihsān and explains transmissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the types of istihsān according to Hanafi usul and identify which types can be extended to other cases.

Core Idea
Istihsān by hidden qiyās extends; istihsān by text, consensus, or necessity is case-specific.

Istihsān (juristic preference) is a distinctive Hanafi source that sometimes overrides manifest qiyās. This passage classifies its bases and explains a crucial limitation: only istihsān grounded in hidden qiyās can be extended to analogous cases, while those resting on text, consensus, or necessity remain confined to their original context. Understanding this taxonomy is essential for applying istihsān correctly in istinbāṭ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the classification
Locate the four bases of istihsān mentioned: hidden qiyās, text (athar), consensus (ijmā'), and necessity (ḍarūra). Note which is singled out for transmissibility.
The text explicitly contrasts 'mustaḥsan bil-qiyās al-khafī' with 'al-aqsām al-ukhar' (the other categories).
2
Understand the reasoning for transmissibility
The hidden qiyās istihsān remains a form of analogy—concealed but still qiyās—so its ruling can be transferred to other cases sharing the same effective cause.
The author says: 'ghāyatuhu annahu khafī yuqābil al-jalī' (its limit is hidden in contrast to manifest).
3
Analyze the example of the sale dispute
The passage presents a disagreement over price before possession. Compare the manifest qiyās (seller delivers, buyer swears) with the istihsān ruling (mutual oaths, annulment). Confirm that the istihsān here is based on hidden qiyās—the illa is mutual claim and denial.
This ruling 'ḥukm ma'qūl' (rational) extends to heirs and lease because the cause is present.
4
Contrast the post-possession case
After possession, the ruling reverts to manifest qiyās (buyer alone swears) and is based solely on text. The author states it 'lam taṣiḥḥ ta'diyatuhu' (its extension is invalid) because it is not reasoned by hidden analogy.
This shows the boundary: only istihsān with a discernible cause (ʿilla) can be generalized.
5
Apply the principle to new scenarios
Using the example of lease (ijāra) and heirs (wārithān), confirm that extension is valid only when the hidden-illa basis is present. Test a hypothetical: could the post-possession ruling be extended to a similar contract? No, because its basis is textual, not analogical.
The glosses (Al-Qamar, As-Sunbali) emphasize the cause-based limitation.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary explains the categories of istihsān (juristic preference) and their transmissibility to other cases. The main text begins by distinguishing istihsān that is based on hidden qiyās (analogy) from other types based on text (athar), consensus (ijmā'), or necessity (ḍarūra). The hidden-qiyās-based istihsān is valid to extend to other cases because it remains a form of qiyās, albeit concealed. In contrast, istihsān from text, consensus, or necessity is turned away from analogy entirely and cannot be extended.

The first example given is the ruling that bowing (rukū') outside prayer can substitute for the prostration of recitation (sajdat al-tilāwa) by way of istihsān, even though manifest qiyās would reject it because bowing is not prostration. This is permitted because the hidden qiyās recognizes the shared purpose of glorification. The commentator clarifies that this extension is only valid for hidden qiyās.

Then the passage examines a contract dispute: if the seller and buyer disagree about the price before possession (qabḍ), manifest qiyās would require the seller to deliver the goods and have the buyer swear, but istihsān (based on hidden qiyās) applies mutual oaths because both are claimants and deniers. This ruling extends to the heirs (if both parties die) and to lease contracts (ijāra) before possession, because the same logic applies.

However, after possession, the ruling changes: only the buyer swears because he is the denier, and this is based solely on textual evidence (athar), not hidden qiyās, so it cannot be extended to other situations. The commentaries (Al-Qamar, As-Sunbali, etc.) provide detailed glosses on the reasoning, including a discussion by Muhammad ibn Salama on preferring qiyās over istihsān in this context. The key lesson is that the basis of istihsān determines its applicability: hidden qiyās allows extension, while non-qiyās sources restrict the ruling to that specific case.

Istihsan in Sales Disputes Before Delivery
Explaining istihsan through sale dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of istihsan to a buyer-seller price dispute before delivery and explain its extension to ijarah and heirs using qiyas khafi.

Core Idea
Istihsan overrides qiyas when deeper reasoning demands mutual swearing and rescission of a disputed sale before delivery.

This lesson explores the Hanafi concept of istihsan (juristic preference) through a classic sales dispute. The passage shows how the apparent analogy (qiyas) is set aside for a more just ruling requiring both parties to swear and the sale to be rescinded. We will see how this ruling extends to heirs and rent contracts, demonstrating the methodology of qiyas khafi (hidden analogy).

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute scenario
Examine the case: buyer claims a lower price, seller claims a higher price, before delivery of the sold item.
Both parties have conflicting claims, each being both a claimant and a denier.
2
State the qiyas ruling
Apparent analogy (qiyas jali) would require the seller alone to swear and deliver on the buyer's claim, treating the buyer as the sole claimant.
This is the default ruling if only qiyas were applied.
3
Explain the istihsan ruling
Istihsan requires both parties to swear (tahaluf) and then the judge rescinds the sale, because each is both claimant and denier.
This is derived from a deeper analysis of the parties' positions.
4
Analyze the reasoning (qiyas khafi)
The underlying 'illah (effective cause) is that both parties have dual roles; mutual oaths are necessary to prevent injustice.
This hidden analogy forms the basis for extending the ruling.
5
Extend to heirs and ijarah
The same 'illah applies when heirs dispute after death, or when lessor and lessee dispute rent before possession of the house.
The contract must be susceptible to rescission (as ijarah is).

Istihsan After Delivery: Athar-Based and Limited
Explaining limitation of istihsan based on athar that is not rationally understood

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a ruling based on an athar that is not rationally understood (ghayr ma'qul al-ma'na) and apply the Hanafi principle of textual limitation to prevent its analogical extension.

Core Idea
A ruling based on an athar that is not rationally understood cannot be extended by qiyas; it remains confined to its specific textual context.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi application of istihsan where a prophetic athar overrides qiyas in a sale dispute after delivery. The key usul principle is that when a textual ruling lacks a rational meaning (ghayr ma'qul al-ma'na), it cannot be analogically extended to other cases. We will see how Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf limit the mutual oath ruling to the sale context, while Imam Muhammad analogically extends it to heirs and ijarah.

Guided Path
1
Establish the qiyas baseline
Without the athar, qiyas would only require the buyer's oath since he denies the seller's claim and the commodity is in his possession. Understand the default ruling.
2
Identify the athar and its apparent generality
The hadith says 'if two transactors differ and the commodity is still in existence, they both swear and rescind.' It is absolute (mutlaq), covering disputes before and after delivery.
The hadith text is given in the passage.
3
Classify the athar's epistemological status
The ruling in the athar is not rationally understood (ghayr ma'qul al-ma'na) – we cannot deduce a reason why both must swear. This is crucial for limitation.
If it were ma'qul al-ma'na, it could be extended by qiyas.
4
Apply the Hanafi principle of limitation
Since the athar is ghayr ma'qul al-ma'na, the ruling is confined to the exact textual scenario: a sale dispute while the commodity exists. It does not extend to heirs or ijarah.
The two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) hold this view.
5
Compare the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Imam Muhammad extends the ruling by qiyas, arguing each party is both claimant and denier. The mufta bihi is the position of the two shaykhs.
The thamarah al-khilaf is that under Muhammad, both heirs swear and the sale is rescinded; under the two shaykhs, only the buyer's heir swears.

The Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) holds that earth is a direct substitute for water, causing tayammum to remove ritual impurity (hadath) absolutely. Muhammad and Zufar argue the substitution is between the acts of wudu and tayammum. Al-Shafi'i limits tayammum to a mere concession that permits prayer without removing impurity. Understanding these positions reveals how usul principles (like the interpretation of textual sequence) generate conflicting rulings.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion probe from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary discussing the classification of legal rights (huquq) into an original (asl) and a substitute (khalaf) that takes its place when the original is impossible. The commentator opens by stating that rights, whether belonging to Allah or to individuals, are divided into these two categories. He then illustrates with the example of iman (faith): the original in the sight of Allah is both tasdiq (heartfelt belief) and iqrar (verbal confession). However, for worldly legal rulings (such as protection of life and property), iqrar alone suffices and acts as a substitute for tasdiq, because tasdiq is internal and unknowable. This is demonstrated by the case of a person coerced into pronouncing the shahada: iqrar alone establishes the worldly rulings of Islam even if inner belief is absent. The commentator then extends the concept to a minor child: a parent's performance of faith on the child's behalf serves as a substitute for the child's own performance, so the child becomes Muslim by one parent's Islam. Similarly, for a captive child taken into dar al-Islam, affiliation to Muslim inhabitants (dar) substitutes for affiliation to parents. A potential objection is raised: 'this would mean a substitute of a substitute (khalaf 'an khalaf), which is invalid.' The commentator resolves this by clarifying that there is no chain of substitution; rather, each is a direct substitute for the minor's own performance, with some ordered upon others. The discussion then shifts to tayammum as a substitute for wudu/ghusl. The commentator notes that all agree tayammum is a substitute, but there is a divergence: The Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) hold that the substitution is absolute (mutlaq), meaning tayammum removes ritual impurity (hadath) entirely, allowing multiple prayers until water is found. Evidence is the Quranic verse (4:43) making earth a substitute for water. Muhammad and Zufar, however, hold that the substitution is between the acts of wudu and tayammum, not between the substances. Al-Shafi'i considers tayammum a mere concession for necessity: it does not remove impurity but permits prayer, so each obligatory prayer requires a separate tayammum. The commentator ends by preferring the view that substitution is between wudu and tayammum, using the sequence of the Quranic verse as evidence.

خلف (khalaf): A substitute that stands in for the original when the original is impossible; e.g., tayammum is khalaf for wudu.
تصديق (tasdiq): Belief in the heart, the inner component of iman.
إقرار (iqrar): Verbal confession of faith, which becomes a substitute for tasdiq in worldly matters.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and illustration of the concept 'cause of the cause' (علة العلة) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author explains that when a ruling is attributed to a distant cause through an intermediate cause, the distant cause still functions as a cause in name and meaning, but not necessarily in ruling. Three examples are provided:
1. Purchase of a relative: The purchase causes ownership, which in turn causes emancipation. Thus, the purchase is a cause of the cause for emancipation. It is a cause because it is effective, but it resembles a means because there is an intermediary.
2. Terminal illness: The illness causes the attachment of heirs' rights, which in turn restricts the patient from donating beyond one third. The illness is a cause in name (the ruling is attributed to it) and in meaning (it is effective), but not in ruling because the restriction only takes effect upon death.

The Concurrency of Legal Cause and the Principle of Substitution
Explaining the principle of cause and evidence standing in place of effect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that the legal cause must be concurrent with its effect and apply the principle of cause/evidence standing in place of effect to cases like istibra' and khalwa.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, a legal cause is an accident that must be simultaneous with its effect; evidence may substitute the effect to prevent hardship.

This passage from Nur al-Anwar delves into a foundational usuli dispute: can the legal cause (al-‘illah al-shar‘iyyah) precede its effect in time? The Hanafi school insists on concurrency, arguing that legal causes are accidents (a‘rad) that do not persist. The discussion then introduces a practical principle—cause or evidence may stand in place of the effect (qiyam al-sabab wa al-dalil maqam al-madlul)—to avoid hardship in cases like istibra' and valid seclusion (khalwa). We will unpack both the theoretical debate and its real-world applications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key issue
The passage begins with a claim: the true legal cause must accompany its effect. Note the opposing view that causes can precede. Recognize that this is a dispute about the nature of legal causality.
This is a rational (dalil aqli) argument, not based on a revealed text.
2
Analyse the Hanafi argument
The Hanafi position relies on the categorization of legal causes as accidents (a‘rad). Accidents do not endure for two moments; therefore, cause and effect must be simultaneous. Reject the analogy to substances (jawhar) that persist.
This logical move prevents the absurdity of an effect existing without a cause.
3
Examine the practical principle
The principle 'qiyam al-sabab wa al-dalil maqam al-madlul' is introduced: when it is impossible to know the actual effect (e.g., pregnancy), the cause or evidence that usually leads to it is given legal weight. This is to alleviate hardship (daf‘ al-darurah).
The principle is applied only when the cause/evidence leads to the effect in most cases (ghalabah).
4
Apply to istibra' and khalwa
For istibra': the occurrence of ownership and possession (cause) is taken as evidence of potential pregnancy, making istibra' obligatory even if certainty of no pregnancy exists. For khalwa: valid seclusion stands in place of actual intercourse to impose mahr and 'iddah.
The hadith about 'watering another's crop' is the scriptural basis for istibra'.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of an important usul principle: the legal standing of a cause or evidence in place of its effect. The author begins by distinguishing between different types of causes. The true legal cause (al-‘illah al-shar‘iyyah) must accompany its effect in time—it does not precede it. Some scholars (like Abu Bakr ibn al-Fadl) argued that legal causes can precede their effects, analogizing them to physical substances that endure. The Hanafi response (via the marginal commentator al-Qamar) is that legal causes are accidents (a‘rad), like rational causes, and accidents do not persist for two moments; hence cause and effect must be simultaneous. This is a key difference: if a cause could precede, the effect might occur without a cause. The example of capability (istita‘ah) is given: the capability that is a condition for obligation is not the physical ability that precedes, but the ability that accompanies the action with all conditions met. The discussion then turns to the principle of ‘qiyam al-sabab wa al-dalil maqam al-musabbab wa al-madlul’—a cause or evidence can stand in place of the effect to prevent hardship (daf‘ al-darurah wa al-‘ajz). This is applied in two examples: istibra' (waiting one menstrual cycle after acquiring a slave girl) and valid khalwa (seclusion). For istibra', the cause is the suspicion that the womb may be occupied with another man's child. Since certainty is impossible, the occurrence of ownership and possession—which indicate the possibility of intercourse—are made to stand in place of actual knowledge. Thus, istibra' is obligatory upon every acquisition of a slave girl, even if she is a virgin (where certainty of no pregnancy exists). For khalwa, valid seclusion between spouses stands in place of actual intercourse, making the mahr obligatory and ‘iddah necessary. The passage grounds these rules in the hadith of Ruwayfi‘ and Abu Sa‘id, emphasizing that Islamic law builds rulings on prevalent causes rather than rare exceptions.

Key Terms
العلة الشرعية (al-‘illah al-shar‘iyyah): The legal cause that necessitates a ruling; in this passage, it is defined as concurrent with its effect.
الاستطاعة (al-istita‘ah): The capability that accompanies the action with all conditions met, as opposed to mere physical ability.
قيام السبب والدليل مقام المدلول (qiyam al-sabab wa al-dalil maqam al-madlul): A legal principle allowing a cause or evidence to stand in place of the effect or indicated matter to prevent hardship.
الاستبراء (al-istibra'): The waiting period for a newly acquired slave girl to ensure she is not pregnant; one menstrual period.
الخلوة الصحيحة (al-khalwah al-sahihah): Valid seclusion between spouses without any legal impediment (illness, menses, ihram, obligatory fast).

Substituting Inciters for Precaution (Ihtiyat)
Explanation of substitution of inciters via ihtiyat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule of ihtiyat by identifying contexts where inciters (dawa'i) are prohibited as substitutes for actual intercourse.

Core Idea
To block sin, Hanafi law prohibits inciters as substitutes for the act itself.

This passage introduces a key Hanafi usul principle: extending the prohibition of an act to its inciters as a precaution (ihtiyat). It applies to five contexts—istibra', musahara, ihram, zihar, and i'tikaf—where looking, kissing, and touching are treated like intercourse to prevent falling into sin. Understanding this rule helps you see how Hanafi law blocks the means to sin without requiring explicit textual evidence for each inciter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
State the Hanafi rule: inciters (dawa'i) to intercourse are prohibited in place of the act itself as a precaution.
The rule is an application of sadd al-dharai (blocking the means).
2
List the five contexts
The passage specifies: istibra', musahara (prohibition of marriage relations), ihram, zihar (before expiation), and i'tikaf. Memorize these.
Each context has its own ruling on intercourse; inciters follow that ruling.
3
Understand the rationale (ihtiyat)
The rationale is ihtiyat (precaution): to avoid falling into the prohibited act, the means leading to it are also prohibited.
This is a form of istihsan based on blocking harm.
4
See the substitution mechanism
The inciter (e.g., looking) is substituted for the incited (intercourse) in terms of ruling. For example, looking during ihram is haram because intercourse is haram.
This substitution is not qiyas but a direct extension via ihtiyat.
5
Note cross-madhhab differences
The Hanafi school applies this broadly; for instance, musahara is established even by inciters. The Shafi'i school restricts it to actual intercourse.
This difference has practical effects on marriage prohibitions.

Legal Capacity and Actions of a Minor: Categories and the Apostasy Dispute
Explains legal capacity categories and the apostasy dispute using istihsan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four categories of actions for a minor according to Hanafi usul, state the ruling for pure harm and pure benefit, and apply the Hanafi method of istihsan to resolve the apostasy dispute.

Core Idea
A minor's deficient capacity suffices for benefit but not for harm; thus apostasy is valid for worldly rulings by istihsan, not qiyas.

This lesson examines the Hanafi theory of legal capacity (ahliyyah) as applied to minors. The passage from Noor al-Anwar classifies all actions into four categories based on benefit and harm, and then uses the case of a minor's apostasy to illustrate the istihsan method. Understanding this classification is essential for deriving rulings on a minor's transactions and worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concept of ahliyyah
Understand the Hanafi division of legal capacity into complete (for adults) and deficient (for minors). Deficient capacity allows the minor to perform actions that are pure benefit but prevents pure harm.
The minor's capacity is sufficient for benefit but not for harm.
2
Categorize actions by benefit/harm
Identify the four categories: (1) pure benefit (e.g., accepting a gift) – valid without guardian; (2) pure harm (e.g., divorce, emancipation) – invalid; (3) mixed with benefit outweighing harm (e.g., prayer) – valid but not obligatory; (4) actions that vary with time (e.g., prayer during menstruation not prescribed).
Financial acts of worship like zakat are not valid because they involve loss of wealth.
3
Apply to specific cases
For each category, determine whether the minor may perform the action without a guardian. Pure benefit is always valid; pure harm is never valid; mixed actions are valid without liability.
The minor is not obligated to complete a prayer if started, and no compensation is required if spoiled.
4
Examine the apostasy dispute
State that Abu Hanifa and Muhammad rule a minor's apostasy valid in worldly and Hereafter rulings (via istihsan), while Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi‘i rule it invalid (via qiyas). Identify the practical consequence: under Hanafi, the minor is separated from his spouse and disinherited; under the opposing view, he remains married and inherits.
The dispute is only in worldly rulings; in Hereafter rulings, apostasy is valid by agreement.
5
Derive the usul rule
Extract the principle that actions of pure harm that are rights of Allah (like disbelief) are valid to maintain legal consistency, because they admit no excuse. Istihsan overrides qiyas when necessary.
The minor's capacity for harm is recognized in apostasy to preserve symmetry with the validity of faith.

Commentary
This passage from Noor al-Anwar explains the legal capacity (ahliyyah) of a minor (sabi) and categories of actions based on benefit and harm. It begins with the ruling on a minor's Islam and apostasy. According to the Hanafi school (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad), a minor's Islam is valid in both worldly and religious rulings because it is pure benefit, while apostasy is also valid (causing separation and loss of inheritance) but killing is not required since no warfare is proven. Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi‘i analogize that apostasy is pure harm and invalid for a minor. The passage then divides actions into four types: (1) pure benefit like accepting gifts—valid without guardian; (2) pure harm like divorce and emancipation—invalid; (3) mixed like prayer—valid without liability; and (4) actions involving both benefit and harm depending on time, like prayer during menstruation. The commentary clarifies that financial worship like zakat is not valid because it involves harm (loss of wealth). Bequest is considered pure harm because its benefit is deferred to the afterlife while immediate harm (depriving heirs) is deemed greater. The discussion is based on Qur'anic principles (like family ties) and istihsan over qiyas.

Key Terms
أهلية (ahliyyah): Legal capacity: the quality in a person by which he is fit for accountability and legal actions.
ردّة (riddah): Apostasy: returning from Islam to disbelief.
ضرر محض (darar mahd): Pure harm: an action with no worldly benefit, like divorce or emancipation.
نفع محض (naf mahd): Pure benefit: an action with no worldly harm, like accepting a gift.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference: abandoning analogical reasoning for a more suitable ruling.

Unconsciousness (Ighma'): Definition and Rulings
Defining unconsciousness and its legal effect on prayer and fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define unconsciousness (ighma') according to the Hanafi school, distinguish its legal effect from sleep and insanity, and explain the Hanafi ruling on qada for missed prayers using istihsan, citing the companion reports.

Core Idea
Unconsciousness is a hadath in all postures; qada for prayer depends on duration, using istihsan.

This passage from a commentary on legal capacity defines unconsciousness (ighma') and distinguishes it from sleep and insanity. The key legal effect is that unconsciousness is considered a hadath (ritual impurity) in every posture, unlike sleep which only breaks wudu in certain positions. The text also addresses the obligation of making up missed prayers (qada) based on the duration of unconsciousness, introducing the usul principle of istihsan (juristic preference) supported by companion practices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and key characteristics
Locate the definition of ighma' in the text: a type of illness and loss of strength that weakens faculties but does not remove intellect. Note how it is distinguished from insanity (which removes intellect) and from sleep (which is less severe in loss of control).
The definition avoids ambiguity by contrasting with similar states.
2
Analyze the hadath ruling
Understand that unconsciousness is a hadath in all cases (lying, sitting, etc.) due to intense relaxation of limbs, increasing the likelihood of emission of a nullifier. Contrast with sleep, which only breaks wudu in reclining postures.
This is a stricter ruling than sleep based on the severity of loss of control.
3
Determine the qada ruling for prayer
The text states that if unconsciousness does not extend beyond a day and night, it is attached to sleep and qada is required; if it extends longer, it is like insanity and no qada is required. This is derived from the practice of the Companions Ammar ibn Yasir and Ibn Umar.
The duration threshold is a day and night (24 hours approximately).
4
Examine the usul principle: istihsan
The Hanafi school uses istihsan to differentiate based on duration, overriding the strict qiyas (analogy) that would treat unconsciousness like sleep. The companion reports serve as the basis for this preference.
Istihsan is a valid method of derivation in Hanafi usul, allowing exception from qiyas when stronger evidence exists.
5
Compare with the opposing school
Note that Imam al-Shafi'i holds that all missed prayers due to unconsciousness require qada, analogizing unconsciousness to sleep. The Hanafi response uses the companion reports to distinguish extended unconsciousness as akin to insanity.
This ikhtilaf illustrates how different usul principles lead to different rulings.

Jest in the Type of Price: Istihsan and the Distinction
Ruling on jest in the type of the price with istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of istihsan to distinguish between jest in the amount and jest in the type of the price in a sale contract, and state the ruling in each case.

Core Idea
Istihsan validates sale with named type despite jest; hidden type cannot be given effect.

This lesson examines a subtle distinction in Hanafi fiqh: the effect of jest (hazl) in the type versus the amount of the price. The text from Noor al-Anwaar explains that when parties collude privately on dirhams but publicly name dinars, the sale is always valid for the named dinars by istihsan. This ruling differs from jest in the amount, where the hidden price can be given effect. We will unpack the reasoning and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Recognize the difference between hazl in the amount (qadr) and hazl in the type (jins) of the price.
In the source, the amount case is where they collude on a hidden lower price; the type case is where they collude on a different currency.
2
Note the ruling for hazl in amount
In the amount case, the hidden price can be given effect because the named price includes it.
Example: collude on 1000 but name 2000; the sale is for 1000.
3
Note the ruling for hazl in type
In the type case, the publicly named price prevails because the hidden type is entirely different.
Example: collude on dirhams but name dinars; sale is valid for dinars.
4
Understand the basis: istihsan and qiyas khafi
The ruling is by istihsan, preferring practical validity over strict qiyas. The hidden analogy (qiyas khafi) with marriage supports the rule.
Marriage analogy: jest in dower amount does not invalidate the marriage; similarly, jest in price type does not invalidate the sale.

Coerced Actions Are Valid Unless Modified: A Usul Principle
Usul rule: coerced actions are legally effective unless a modifier exists

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that coerced actions are legally valid unless a specific modifier (mughayyir) exists, using the analogy to voluntary acts and examples like divorce and hudud.

Core Idea
Coerced actions are valid because ahliyyah remains intact; only a mughayyir can nullify them.

This lesson introduces a foundational usul principle from the Sanbuli commentary: the actions and statements of a coerced person are generally valid and carry legal consequences, just like those of a voluntary agent. However, if a modifying element (mughayyir) such as an exception, condition, or legal impediment is present, the ruling may change. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly applying contractual effects and hadd penalties in situations of coercion.

Guided Path
1
State the General Rule
Read the first part: 'Coerced actions and statements are considered and valid because they proceed from intellect and legal capacity.' Identify that the default ruling is validity.
The rule applies to both statements (e.g., divorce) and actions (e.g., drinking wine).
2
Identify the Modifier (Mughayyir)
Look for the exception: 'except when a modifier exists.' A modifier is something that changes the legal effect, such as an exception (istithna') or condition (ta'liq). In the examples, dar al-harb or shubha also act as modifiers.
The modifier must be a recognized legal factor that alters the normal consequence.
3
Apply the Analogy to Voluntary Acts
The passage compares coerced acts to voluntary acts: both are valid unless a modifier attaches. For a voluntary divorce, an exception or condition can prevent its effect; similarly for coerced acts.
The underlying commonality is the presence of ahliyyah (legal capacity).
4
Analyze the Examples
Examine the examples: divorce utterance is effective unless followed by istithna' or ta'liq. Drinking wine or zina incurs hadd unless in dar al-harb or with shubha. Coerced acts follow the same pattern.
The examples illustrate that the presence of a modifier negates the default ruling.
5
Conclude the Principle
Synthesize: The default is validity; the burden is on showing that a modifier exists. If no modifier, the act is legally effective.
This principle underlies many fiqh rulings on coercion.
