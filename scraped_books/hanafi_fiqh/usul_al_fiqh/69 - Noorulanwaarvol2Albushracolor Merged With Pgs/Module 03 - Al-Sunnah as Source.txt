Proving Qiyas by Hadith and Ijma
Shows that qiyas is established by hadith and consensus of the community.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hadith and consensus proofs for qiyas, and explain how the hadith's requirement of equality establishes the validity of qiyas.

Core Idea
The hadith of the six commodities proves qiyas by requiring equality in exchange, showing that rulings are transferred between analogous cases.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar explains how legal analogy (qiyas) is proven as a source of Islamic law. The author cites a famous hadith on riba transactions and argues that the principle of equality (tamathul) embedded in the hadith demonstrates that rulings can be extended to similar cases. Additionally, the consensus (ijma) on linguistic borrowing is used as evidence for the validity of qiyas in legal matters. We will unpack this argument step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two analogies
The passage states that qiyas is analogous to reflection on punishments (to avoid their causes) and linguistic borrowing (to extend meanings). Both are accepted as valid human reasoning. Recognize that qiyas is being compared to these two established forms of reasoning.
This shows qiyas is not an innovation; it is similar to accepted rational methods.
2
Analyze the hadith of the six commodities
Examine the hadith: 'Wheat for wheat, barley for barley... like for like, hand to hand, and the excess is riba.' Note that the command (amr) is for obligation (wujub) according to Hanafi usul. But since the sale itself is permissible (mubah), the command applies to the condition of equality, not to the act of sale. This establishes that equality (tamathul) is a necessary condition for valid transactions of like commodities.
The grammatical analysis of 'misl' as a circumstantial accusative (hal) reinforces that equality is a condition.
3
Connect to consensus (ijma)
The passage argues that linguistic borrowing (isti'arah) is a form of transposition (ta'diyah) in language that is agreed upon by consensus. Since qiyas is a transposition in legal rulings, it is also valid by consensus, not by a separate qiyas that would cause circular reasoning. This is a rational proof (dalil 'aqli) based on consensus.
This avoids the circularity of using qiyas to prove qiyas.

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary on the proof of qiyas. It first states that legal qiyas is analogous to two types of reflection: reflection on punishments to avoid their causes, and reflection on linguistic realities for metaphorical borrowing. Both are accepted as valid, so qiyas is also valid by consensus (ijma), not by a further qiyas that would cause circular reasoning. The commentary then cites the famous hadith of the six commodities (wheat, barley, dates, salt, gold, silver) where the Prophet (peace be upon him) commanded that they be exchanged like for like, hand to hand, and any excess is riba. This hadith demonstrates the principle of equality (tamathul), which is the basis of qiyas: when two things share the same effective cause (illah), they must be treated equally. The commentator then discusses grammatical points: the word 'misl' (like) is interpreted as a condition; the command is for obligation, but the sale itself is permissible, so the command pertains to the condition of equality. This shows that qiyas is a necessary tool to determine equality of rulings. The passage concludes that the hadith, combined with consensus on the validity of linguistic borrowing, establishes qiyas as a legitimate source of law. The hadith is from Muslim (no. 4063).

Key Terms
قياس (qiyas): Legal analogy: regarding a new case (far') under the same ruling as an original case (asl) due to a shared cause (illah).
إجماع (ijma): Consensus of the mujtahids of the ummah after the death of the Prophet on a legal ruling.
نظير (nazir): Counterpart or equivalent; used here to state that qiyas is the counterpart of the two forms of reflection (punishments and linguistic) whose validity is accepted.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A hadith's apparent generality can be restricted by a contextual qarina, changing the ruling.

This passage presents a classic usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over the interpretation of a hadith where the Prophet ﷺ gave a boy a choice between his parents. The Shafi'i school takes the hadith as a general ruling, while the Hanafi school argues that the context (the Prophet's supplication) specifies it to that particular case. Understanding how each school handles textual generality is key to grasping their different fiqh positions on child custody.

Commentary

This illustrates the Hanafi method of restricting general texts (takhsis) based on context and purpose of the Lawgiver.

Effect of Fainting on Qada: Hanafi Rule and Istihsan
Explains rulings on fainting and sleep regarding prayer and fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that fainting exceeding a day and night removes the obligation of qada, identify the istihsan-based distinction, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Hanafi: Fainting beyond a day+night cancels qada; Shafi'i: Any complete prayer time fainting cancels qada.

In this passage from Noor al-Anwar, the author explains a nuanced Hanafi ruling: missed prayers due to fainting must be made up only if the fainting lasts a day and night or less. If longer, qada is waived. This rule is derived through istihsan (juristic preference) based on the actions of Companions, and it contrasts with the Shafi'i position that any fainting covering a complete prayer time lifts qada entirely. The passage also distinguishes fainting from sleep, which always requires qada.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi rule and its threshold
State the Hanafi ruling on qada for missed prayers due to fainting, noting the critical duration of one day and night.
The rule applies specifically to fainting (ighma), not sleep.
2

Examine the athar of Ammar ibn Yasir (fainted one day+night, made up qada) and Ibn Umar (fainted more than one day+night, did not make up qada). Determine how these establish the distinction.
These athar are the basis for the Hanafi use of istihsan.
3
Explain the istihsan reasoning
Understand why the Hanafi school prefers istihsan over qiyas in this case: qiyas would require qada for all fainting, but the Companion examples show a distinction based on duration, leading to a concession for extended fainting.
Istihsan is a key Hanafi principle that allows departing from qiyas for a stronger proof.
4
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Describe the Shafi'i view (no qada for any fainting covering a complete prayer time) and their proof. Then articulate the Hanafi response, highlighting the role of istihsan and Companion practices.
The practical difference (thamarah) is when qada applies: Hanafi requires it for ≤1 day fainting; Shafi'i does not.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that slavery limits legal capacity (ahliyyah) to derive rulings on Hajj, marriage, and blood inviolability for a slave.

Core Idea
A slave's Hajj is nafl, not obligatory, because his physical and financial benefits belong to his master, so he lacks full qudrah.

This passage examines the partial legal capacity of a slave in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It addresses three specific areas: concubinage (tasarrī), the obligatory Hajj, and marriage, as well as the slave's status regarding blood inviolability. The rulings are derived from the fundamental principle that a slave's benefits (manāfi') belong to the master, except for purely bodily acts like prayer and fasting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the usul principle
Recognize that the slave's benefits (manāfi') – both physical and financial – are owned by the master. This is the core premise from which all subsequent rulings flow.
Purely bodily acts like prayer and fasting are exceptions because they require no financial outlay.
2
Apply the principle to concubinage
Concubinage (tasarrī) requires purchasing a female slave, which is a financial transaction. Since the slave's earnings belong to the master, he cannot own a concubine.
The mukatab is mentioned to clarify that even though he has some rights to his earnings, he still lacks full ownership.
3
Apply the principle to Hajj
Hajj requires provision and a mount (zād wa rāḥila). The slave's physical labor and financial resources belong to the master, so he lacks the necessary qudrah (ability). Even with the master's permission, his benefits remain the master's property, so the Hajj counts only as nafl.
Contrast with a free poor person who later becomes wealthy: his Hajj counts because he had qudrah at the time.
4
Apply the principle to marriage
Marriage involves a dowry (mahr), which becomes a debt on the slave's person (raqabah). Selling him to pay it would harm the master, so the master's consent is required. However, the slave does have the capacity to own marriage because satisfying sexual desire is a necessity.
The master's consent is a condition (shart) for validity, not a pillar.
5
Apply the principle to blood inviolability
The slave's blood (life) is not owned by the master; the master cannot kill him. The slave has full capacity to acknowledge qisas (retaliation) because his life belongs to himself and he needs to survive.
This shows that slavery does not strip all legal capacity; the slave retains ownership over his life.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Aman is valid only from one who has a right to drop — partnership in spoils establishes that right.

This passage from Nur al-Anwar delves into the usul basis for the Hanafi position on aman (safe conduct). It distinguishes between a slave authorized for combat and one authorized only for trade, tying validity to partnership in spoils. The text also records a disagreement with Muhammad al-Shaybani and al-Shafi'i, providing an opportunity to analyze how a general proof (hadith) is qualified by a specific legal consideration (the existence of a right).

Guided Path
1
Identify the key distinction
Find the two categories of slave mentioned: al-madhun bi al-qital (authorized for combat) and al-mahjur (restricted). Note which aman is valid and which is disputed.

The text says the combat-authorized slave becomes a partner in spoils (sharikan fi al-ghanimah). Why does that matter? Aman is a disposition that waives one's own right to kill or capture. Without a right, one cannot drop it.
The partnership gives him a right (haqq) in the spoils, which includes the right to waive it via aman.

The Hanafi position relies on the principle that aman is a disposition (tasarruf) in one's own right. Therefore, only one who has a right can perform it. The authorized slave has a right because of his partnership; the restricted slave has none.
This is a talil (causal reasoning) linking the ruling to the existence of a right.
5

The text implies the Hanafi position is stronger by conditioning aman on authorization. The commentary quotes al-Qamar and al-Sunbuli supporting the restriction. Consider the practical consequence: if a restricted slave grants aman, it is invalid according to Abu Hanifa, so Muslims may still attack that enemy.
The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is the position of Abu Hanifa.

Explanation of legal capacity, section on illness. Worldly rulings involve obligation such that zakat and all other devotional acts become void for the deceased. Zakat was specified first to refute the illusion of one who thinks it is a financial worship not related to the act of the deceased, so the guardian may pay zakat as al-Shafi'i claimed, but without wealth, it equals prayer and fasting in being void. Only sin remains upon him; if God wills, He pardons by His grace, and if He wills, He punishes by His justice and wisdom. This is the situation regarding the rights of God. As for the rights of people, either it is a right of others upon him, or a right of his upon others. He pointed to the first with his saying: 'What is legislated upon him for the need of others.' If it is a right attached to a specific object, it remains with its permanence, like a mortgage where the right of the mortgagee attaches, a lessee where the right attaches to the object (not to the act of the deceased), a sale where the right of the buyer attaches, a deposit where the right of the depositor attaches. These objects are taken by their owners first without entering the estate and are divided among creditors or heirs. If it is a debt, it does not remain merely upon the liability until joined to it—i.e., to the liability—wealth or something that reinforces liabilities, which is the liability of the surety. That is, as long as he did not leave wealth, etc., because death destroys the foundation of obligation; because it is complete inability to perform acts of worship in the present or as make-up, and because he went from the abode of trial to the abode of recompense. (al-Sunbuli) 'Until it became void' means zakat has fallen from the deceased and it is not obligatory to pay it from his estate, and all other devotional acts like prayer, pilgrimage, and fasting. (al-Qamar) 'And the purpose' etc.: Do you not see that if a poor person obtains zakat wealth, he is not allowed to take it nor does it fall due? (al-Qamar) So it: i.e., zakat equals prayer and fasting in being void. And Bahar al-Ulum Mawlana Abd al-Ali said: This is if he did not bequeath; but if he bequeathed, then financial devotions like zakat, ransom for fasting, and prayer are performed from one-third of his wealth. (al-Qamar) 'If it is a right' etc.: This is the second division of the divisions of worldly rulings, divided into several sections: the first is this, and the second is what he explained with his saying: 'And if it is a debt' etc., and he omitted some that are explained in other books of principles. (al-Sunbuli) 'And if it is' i.e., the right of another is a debt, it does not remain etc. Because the liability of obligation has become void by death. (al-Qamar) Or a surety from his presence, his debt does not remain in this world, so his children are not pursued for it, but he takes it in the Hereafter. I.e., at the time of his presence and life, the creditor. (al-Qamar) For this reason—i.e., because there is no debt on his liability—Abu Hanifa said: Indeed, suretyship for a debt of a deceased insolvent is not valid if he has no surety from his lifetime, because suretyship is joining liability to liability, i.e., in claim. So if the deceased has no recognized liability, then how can the surety's liability be joined to it? Unlike if he has wealth or a surety from his lifetime, for his liability is complete, then suretyship is valid from him at that time. And unlike if someone gratuitously pays, for then his liability is complete by discharging the debt without suretyship, and it is valid. And the two said: Suretyship for the deceased insolvent is valid because death was not legislated to cancel the debt; if it were cancelled, then taking from a gratuitous payer would not be allowed, nor would it be claimed in the Hereafter. And al-Shafi'i also agreed with them unanimously. Unlike the restricted slave who confesses a debt and then a man becomes surety for him, for it is valid even if the slave is not subject to a claim before emancipation, because his liability is complete for his life and intellect, and the claim is also established in general—i.e., the restricted slave who is not permitted—because it is based on the claim, since it is conceivable that his master might believe him or emancipate him, so he would be claimed immediately. So when his being subject to claim is established, suretyship for him is valid. However, the surety is taken immediately even if the principal (the restricted slave) does not have the debt. (al-Qamar) 'Or a surety from his presence' i.e., a surety whose suretyship was from the presence of that deceased i.e., during his lifetime. (al-Qamar) 'It does not remain' etc. because the contract does not remain in reality or in effect, unlike if he dies leaving sufficient assets, for the contract remains in effect for the attainment of the purpose, which is the substitute, even if not remaining in reality. And the two said: etc. I said: Ahmad and Malik also said it; indeed Ibn Qudamah attributed it to most scholars, as in al-Taqrir. And they adduced the hadith of Jabir: The Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) would not pray over a man who died while in debt. A deceased was brought and he asked: 'Is he in debt?' They said: 'Yes, two dinars.' He said: 'Pray over your companion.' Then Abu Qatada al-Ansari said: 'They are upon me, O Messenger of Allah.' So the Messenger of Allah prayed. Narrated by al-Nasa'i and Abu Dawud. (al-Sunbuli) 'And the two said' etc. The response from the Imam is that his liability is free from worldly claim, so the meaning of suretyship is not realized. As for the Hereafter claim, it remains, and it is part of the rulings of the Hereafter. As for taking from the gratuitous payer, its validity is based on the debt remaining in the right of the creditor. For the falling of the debt from the debtor is due to necessity, so it is estimated to the extent of necessity, so the effect of its falling appears in the right of the one upon whom the debt lies, not in the right of the one to whom the debt is due. So the debt in the right of the creditor remains, so taking from the gratuitous payer is valid. Thus it was said. (al-Qamar) 'So he is claimed immediately' i.e., upon the assumption of the master's confirmation, and he is claimed after emancipation upon the assumption of emancipation. So when his being subject to claim is established, i.e., immediately or in the second instance, suretyship for him is valid because joining liability to liability in the claim is realized. (al-Qamar)

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from Noor al-Anwar explaining the effects of death on legal capacity and obligations. The author begins by stating that death voids all acts of worship that require choice, such as prayer, fasting, and zakat, because the deceased has left the abode of trial. However, the sin of neglecting these obligations remains subject to God's will. Zakat is specified because some might think it can be paid by the guardian from the estate, but the Hanafi position is that it becomes void like other devotions, contrary to Shafi'i. The passage then distinguishes between rights of God and rights of people. For rights of people, if the right is attached to a specific object (like a mortgage), it remains and the object is taken before distribution. If it is a debt, the mere liability (dhimmah) does not suffice after death; there must be assets or a surety from the lifetime. This leads to the central dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that suretyship (kafala) for a deceased insolvent is invalid because the liability is no longer existent for worldly claims. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and al-Shafi'i argue it is valid because death does not cancel the debt, citing the hadith of Abu Qatada who guaranteed the Prophet's debt. The commentary provides a detailed response: the liability is free from worldly claim but remains for Hereafter accountability; taking from a gratuitous payer is valid because the debt remains in the creditor's right. The ruling on a restricted slave is contrasted: suretyship for him is valid because his liability is complete and claim is possible upon emancipation. This passage illustrates Hanafi usul principles regarding the effect of death on obligations and the nuances of suretyship, with the relied-upon position being that of Abu Hanifa for the deceased insolvent without assets or prior surety.

The Dispute on Suretyship for the Deceased Insolvent
Explaining the dispute on the validity of suretyship for a deceased insolvent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute on the validity of kafala for a deceased insolvent and evaluate the opposing proofs.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: No liability to join, so no valid suretyship; Sahibayn and Shafi'i: debt survives death, so suretyship is valid.

This passage delves into the second category of rights: rights of people. It explains that after death, rights attached to specific objects remain, but unsecured debts require either assets or a surety from the deceased's lifetime. The central dispute among the Hanafi imams and with al-Shafi'i revolves around whether suretyship (kafala) for a deceased insolvent is valid. Abu Hanifa holds it invalid because the deceased's liability (dhimmah) for worldly claims is extinguished, while his two students and al-Shafi'i argue that death does not cancel the debt, citing the hadith of Abu Qatada. The passage provides a robust Hanafi response to the opposing proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the categories of rights after death
What are the two types of rights of others? Rights attached to specific objects (mortgage, lease, etc.) and unsecured debts.
Only the first category remains automatically. For debts, the liability does not survive death without assets or a prior surety.
2
State the Hanafi ruling on kafala for a deceased insolvent
What is Abu Hanifa's position? He holds that kafala is invalid because the deceased's dhimmah is no longer recognized for worldly claims.
Suretyship requires joining two liabilities in a claim; if the principal's liability is gone, there is nothing to join.
3
Analyze the opposing proofs (Sahibayn and Shafi'i)
What evidence do they use? The hadith of Abu Qatada, where the Prophet accepted a guarantee for a deceased debtor. They also argue that taking from a gratuitous payer is valid, showing debt remains.
The hadith is from Sunan al-Nasa'i and Abu Dawud.
4
Examine the Hanafi response
How does Abu Hanifa rebut the hadith? He distinguishes worldly claim from Hereafter claim: the fiqhi claim is gone, but accountability remains in the Hereafter. The gratuitous payer case is explained by the debt remaining in the creditor's right, not on the debtor.
The response uses the principle of necessity (darurah) limited to the debtor's side.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
What is the practical difference? Under Abu Hanifa, a person cannot become surety for a deceased insolvent; under the two students and al-Shafi'i, the surety is bound.
This affects modern cases where a deceased person leaves debts and no assets.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from Noor al-Anwar explaining the effects of death on legal capacity and obligations. The author begins by stating that death voids all acts of worship that require choice, such as prayer, fasting, and zakat, because the deceased has left the abode of trial. However, the sin of neglecting these obligations remains subject to God's will. Zakat is specified because some might think it can be paid by the guardian from the estate, but the Hanafi position is that it becomes void like other devotions, contrary to Shafi'i. The passage then distinguishes between rights of God and rights of people. For rights of people, if the right is attached to a specific object (like a mortgage), it remains and the object is taken before distribution. If it is a debt, the mere liability (dhimmah) does not suffice after death; there must be assets or a surety from the lifetime. This leads to the central dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that suretyship (kafala) for a deceased insolvent is invalid because the liability is no longer existent for worldly claims. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and al-Shafi'i argue it is valid because death does not cancel the debt, citing the hadith of Abu Qatada who guaranteed the Prophet's debt. The commentary provides a detailed response: the liability is free from worldly claim but remains for Hereafter accountability; taking from a gratuitous payer is valid because the debt remains in the creditor's right. The ruling on a restricted slave is contrasted: suretyship for him is valid because his liability is complete and claim is possible upon emancipation. This passage illustrates Hanafi usul principles regarding the effect of death on obligations and the nuances of suretyship, with the relied-upon position being that of Abu Hanifa for the deceased insolvent without assets or prior surety.

Definition of Safar and Its Legal Character
Defining safar and establishing it as a cause of concession.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of safar according to the Hanafi school and explain why travel itself is considered a cause of legal concession.

Core Idea
Safar is a prolonged departure with intention; it itself grants concessions even without actual hardship.

Travel (safar) is not merely a physical departure but a legal category with specific conditions. Understanding its definition helps clarify why the law eases certain obligations during travel, even when no hardship is felt. In this lesson, we dissect the Hanafi definition and its underlying rationale.

Guided Path

The definition combines spatial and intentional elements.
2
Determine the minimum duration

The text says 'جعل نفس السفر قائماً مقام المشقة' (travel itself is placed in the position of hardship). This means the license applies whether hardship is felt or not.
This is a key distinction from illness.
4
Distinguish from illness
Unlike travel, illness only grants a license if fasting would actually cause harm. The object of license is not illness itself but the harm it causes.
This distinction is crucial for the rulings on fasting later.

Fasting during Travel and the Distinction from Illness
Ruling on breaking fast during travel vs. illness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a traveler who begins a fast before traveling may not break it, while a sick person may, and explain the rationale based on volition.

Core Idea
Travel is voluntary, so breaking a fast already begun is not allowed; illness is involuntary, so breaking is allowed.

Fasting during travel is permissible to defer, but the situation changes if one has already started the fast. The Hanafi school makes a critical distinction between travel and illness: the former is chosen, the latter is imposed. This lesson explores the reasoning behind this distinction.

Guided Path
1
State the basic rule for fasting during travel
Travel permits deferring the obligation of fasting to other days, but does not drop the obligation entirely.
The text says 'لا في إسقاطه' – it does not drop the fast.
2
Understand the exception for breaking a fast already begun
If one intends and begins a fast while at home (or already traveling), then travels (or continues), he may not break his fast.
The text says 'إذا أصبح صائمًا وهو مسافر أو مقيم فسافر لا يباح له الفطر'.
3
Contrast with illness
If one begins a fast healthy and then falls ill, he may break his fast. Similarly, a sick person who intended to fast may break if needed.
The text says 'بخلاف المريض' and gives examples.
4
Identify the underlying principle
Travel is a voluntary action (amr ikhtiyārī), so the obligation established by starting the fast remains. Illness is a heavenly matter (amr samāwī) beyond human control, so necessity overrides.
The text explicitly contrasts the two: 'السفر من الأمور المختارة، المرض أمر سماوي'.

The Nature of Qasr: Original Obligation or Concession?
Analysis of dalil and dispute on the nature of qasr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds qasr to be the original obligation for the traveler and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the hadith of Aisha.

Core Idea
The hadith 'Prayer was prescribed as two rak'ats' proves shortening is the traveler's original duty.

This lesson examines a classical usul dispute about the nature of shortened prayer (qasr) during travel. The Hanafi school argues that qasr is not a concession but the original obligation, based on a clear hadith. The Shafi'i school analogizes with fasting, treating it as a concession. We will analyze the textual evidence and the reasoning behind each position.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the Hanafi position on qasr
State clearly what the Hanafi school holds: that the traveler must pray two rak'ats, and praying four is not permissible because the obligation itself is two.
2
2. Locate the primary proof
Find the hadith quoted from Aisha (رضي الله عنها) in the passage: 'فرضت الصلاة ركعتين ركعتين فأقرّت صلاة السفر وزيد في الحضر'. Note its source (Bukhari and Muslim).
The hadith is the core textual evidence.
3
3. Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
Explain how the hadith proves the Hanafi position: the original prescription was two rak'ats; the increase (to four) was for residents. Thus the traveler's prayer remains at the original two, making it the rule, not an exception.
The words 'أقرّت' (confirmed) and 'زيد' (added) indicate two rak'ats are fixed for travel.
4
4. State the opposing Shafi'i position and its proof
The Shafi'i school holds that the obligation is four rak'ats, and shortening is a concession. Their proof is analogy (qiyas) with fasting: travel does not cancel fasting but permits concession to break it.
The passage mentions 'اعتباراً بالصوم'.
5
5. Evaluate the Hanafi response and tarjih
The Hanafi response argues that the explicit hadith (nass) takes precedence over analogy (qiyas). Additionally, prayer involves physical actions (bowing, prostrating) done in pairs, so travel directly affects their structure, unlike fasting which is abstention.
The text concludes 'ولنا' (our proof) and the tarjih is the Hanafi position.

فيؤثر السفر في قصر ذوات الأربع، وفي تأخير وجوب الصوم إلى عدّة من أيام أخر لا في إسقاطة، لكنه لما كان من الأمور المختارة، جواب عما يتوهّم أنه لما كان نفس السفر أقيم مقام المشقة، فينبغي أن يصح الإفطار في يوم سافر أيضًا؟ فأجاب بأن السفر لما كان من الأمور المختارة الحاصلة باختيار العبد. أي السفر و لم يكن موجباً ضرورة لازمة مستدعية إلى الإفطار كالمرض، فقيل: إنه إذا أصبح صائمًا وهو مسافر أو مقيم فسافر لا يباح له الفطر؛ لأنه تقرر الوجوب عليه بالشروع، ولا ضرورة له تدعوه إلى الإفطار، بخلاف المريض إذا نوى الصوم، وتحمّل على نفسه مشقة المرض، ثم أراد أن يفطر حلّ له ذلك، وكذا إذا كان صحيحًا من أول النهار ناويًا للصوم، ثم مرض حلّ له الفطر؛ لأنه أمر سماوي لا اختيار للعبد فيه، والمرخص للفطر موجود، فصار عذرًا مبيحًا للفطر. أي المرض ذوات الأربع إلخ : أي يسقط السفر النصف الأخير من ذوات الأربع كالظهر والعصر والعشاء حتى لم يبق الإكمال مشروعًا أصلاً عندنا، وقال الشافعي له فرضية الأربع والقصر رخصة اعتبارًا بالصوم، فمن صلّى أربعًا عمل بالعزيمة، ومن قصر اختار الرخصة ولنا ما روى الشيخان عن عائشة ما قالت: فرضت الصلاة ركعتين ركعتين" فأقرّت صلاة السفر وزيد في الحضر. Statement of legal capacity: Definition of travel and its ruling. So travel affects shortening of four-rakat prayers, and postpones the obligation of fasting to other days, not its cancellation. But since it is among chosen matters, it is a response to the misconception that since travel itself is placed in the position of hardship, breaking the fast on the day of travel should be valid? So he answered: since travel is among chosen matters that occur by the choice of the servant. That is, travel. And it is not a necessary cause demanding breaking the fast like illness. So it is said: if one becomes a fasting person while traveling or resident and then travels, breaking the fast is not permitted for him, because the obligation is confirmed upon him by beginning, and he has no necessity calling him to break the fast. Unlike the sick person if he intends fasting and endures the hardship of illness, then wants to break the fast, it is allowed for him. Likewise if one is healthy at the beginning of the day intending to fast, then becomes ill, breaking the fast is allowed for him, because it is a heavenly matter, no choice of the servant in it, and the concession for breaking the fast exists, so it becomes an excuse permitting breaking the fast. That is, illness. 'Four-rakat prayers etc.': meaning travel removes the second half of four-rakat prayers like Zuhr, Asr, and Isha, so that completion is not legislated at all according to us. Al-Shafi'i said: four is obligatory and shortening is a concession, by analogy with fasting; whoever prays four acts with resolve, and whoever shortens chooses the concession. Our proof is what the two shaykhs narrated from Aisha that she said: 'Prayer was prescribed as two rak'ats, so the prayer of travel was confirmed and extra was added for residency.' Because travel only becomes a complete cause if three days with ease have passed. So analogically before that the concession should not be established by mere leaving, but it is established for the realization of concession for all. Commentary
This passage addresses the rulings of travel (safar) concerning prayer and fasting from a Hanafi perspective. It begins by stating that travel affects the shortening (qasr) of four-rakat prayers (Zuhr, Asr, Isha) by halving them, and it delays the obligation of fasting to other days without canceling it entirely. The author distinguishes travel from illness: illness is a divine ordeal beyond human choice, so breaking a fast is permissible even if one has started it; travel, being a chosen act, does not permit breaking the fast after one has begun fasting, unless there is a genuine necessity (such as fear of illness). A key proof is the hadith from Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) that prayer was originally two rak'ats and the additional rak'ats were added for residents, confirming that the traveler's prayer remains at two. The passage also addresses a scenario where a traveler or resident breaks the fast after starting it while traveling. According to the Hanafi school, if the traveler breaks the fast in the two scenarios (becoming fasting while traveling or becoming fasting while resident then traveling), the presence of travel as a permissible cause creates a doubt (shubha) that prevents the obligation of expiation (kaffarah). However, if a resident breaks the fast while still at home, then travels afterward, the expiation remains binding because the permissive travel did not coincide with the act of breaking. The text further explains that the concessions of travel are established from the moment one leaves the inhabited area of the city, based on the practice of the Prophet (peace be upon him) as transmitted by Anas. Even though the complete cause of travel (three days' journey) may not have been fulfilled, the concession is granted immediately to provide relief for all travelers. Finally, the passage defines 'error' (khatā') as the occurrence of something contrary to intention, giving the example of shooting at game but hitting a person, where the intention is incomplete.

Travel, Fasting, and Shubha: Distinguishing Voluntary and Involuntary Excuses
Analysis of travel vs. illness, shubha in fasting, and definition of khatā’

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between voluntary (travel) and involuntary (illness) excuses in fasting, and apply the concept of shubha to determine the obligation of kaffarah.

Core Idea
Travel is voluntary so breaking a started fast requires necessity; illness is involuntary so breaking is permitted without necessity.

This lesson explores the distinction between travel and illness regarding the obligation of fasting and the expiation (kaffarah) for breaking it. Travel, being a chosen act, does not permit breaking a started fast unless there is a genuine necessity (such as fear of illness). Illness, being involuntary, allows breaking even without necessity. The concept of shubha (doubt) plays a key role in determining whether kaffarah is obligatory. The lesson also defines the term 'khatā’' (error) as a related concept in usul.

Guided Path
1
1. Differentiate between travel and illness as excuses
Travel is a chosen act (ikhtiyār al-‘abd), while illness is a heavenly matter (samāwī). This distinction affects the permissibility of breaking a started fast.
The passage states: 'المرض أمر سماوي لا اختيار للعبد فيه'.
2
2. Determine the ruling when a traveler/resident starts fasting and then travels
If one becomes a fasting person while residing or traveling, and then travels later, breaking the fast is not allowed unless there is a necessity (e.g., fear of illness). This is because the obligation has been confirmed by starting.
Passage: 'إذا أصبح صائمًا وهو مسافر أو مقيم فسافر لا يباح له الفطر'.
3
3. Analyze the effect of shubha on kaffarah
If a traveler breaks the fast in the two scenarios (starting while traveling, or starting while resident then traveling), the presence of travel as a permissible cause creates a doubt (shubha) that prevents the obligation of expiation (kaffarah).
Passage: 'كان قيام السفر المبيح شبهة فلا تجب الكفارة'.
4
4. Contrast with the case of a resident who travels after breaking the fast
If a resident breaks his fast while at home and then travels, the kaffarah remains obligatory because the permissive travel did not coincide with the breaking act.
Passage: 'وإن أفطر المقيم الذي نوى الصوم في بيته، ثم سافر لا تسقط عنه الكفارة'.
5
5. Apply the definition of khatā’ (error)
The passage defines error as the occurrence of something contrary to intention, due to incomplete intention or lack of caution. This is linked to the idea that shubha and error affect liability in fiqh.
Definition: 'الخطأ وقوع الشيء على خلاف ما أريد'.

Legal Definition of Travel and Its Effect on Kaffarah
Defining travel as a legal excuse and its effect on kaffarah

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Travel creates a doubt that prevents kaffarah from becoming obligatory.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar defines the legal category of travel as a license-granting excuse. It then applies this definition to the issue of fasting kaffarah, showing how the presence of a permissible cause (travel) creates a legal doubt that removes the obligation of expiation. The text also contrasts this with illness, where the loss of control leads to a different ruling.

Establishment of Travel Licenses: Qiyas vs. Istihsan
Explaining istihsan in establishing travel licenses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school establishes travel licenses immediately upon departure using istihsan overriding qiyas, and evaluate the sunnah evidence.

Core Idea
Travel licenses begin upon departure, not after three days, to achieve the purpose of facilitation.

This passage continues the discussion of travel by examining when the legal licenses for a traveler commence. The text cites the Prophetic practice of granting concessions immediately upon leaving Medina, and then addresses a potential qiyas that would delay the licenses to the completion of the full cause (three days).
