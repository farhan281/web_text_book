The Minor's Utterance: Shafi'i vs Hanafi on Bequest and Conversion
Comparison of Shafi'i and Hanafi views on minor's utterance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on a minor's bequest and identify the usul principle (consideration of harm) that underpins it, as well as contrast it with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
A minor's bequest is void in the Hanafi school because it is pure harm; the Shafi'i school allows it when the guardian cannot act, as it is pure benefit.

This passage presents a classic usul al-fiqh dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on the validity of a minor's utterances (عبارة الصبي). At issue is whether a minor can make a valid bequest (وصية) or independently embrace Islam. The Shafi'i school distinguishes between matters the guardian can handle (like sale or conversion through the father) and those he cannot (like bequest after death), allowing the minor's utterance in the latter. The Hanafi school, however, voids all gratuitous transfers by a minor, including bequests for pious works, because they involve pure harm and the minor's capacity is deficient. Understanding this disagreement reveals how each school prioritizes different aspects of the Shafi'i hadith on the minor's acts and the principle of preventing harm (darar).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contested Issue
Determine the exact legal question: Under what conditions is a minor's utterance (عبارة) considered valid for legal acts like bequest and conversion?
The source explicitly compares Shafi'i and Hanafi views.
2
State the Shafi'i Position and Its Basis
Articulate the Shafi'i view: If the guardian can achieve the benefit directly, the minor's utterance is not needed; otherwise, it is valid. For bequest, the guardian cannot act posthumously, so the minor's utterance counts.
Al-Shafi'i considers bequest a pure benefit (ثواب) in the afterlife.
3
State the Hanafi Position and Its Basis
Articulate the Hanafi view: All gratuitous transfers (تبرعات) by a minor are void because they are pure harm (ضرر محض) and the minor's capacity is deficient.
The Hanafi school does not distinguish between pious and non-pious bequests.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Response to Shafi'i
Examine why the Hanafi school rejects the Shafi'i distinction: Even if the bequest is for pious works, it still removes ownership without immediate benefit, which is harmful. The minor's limited capacity cannot bear such harm.
The marginalia in the text highlight this reasoning.
5
Identify the Practical Consequence (Thamarah)
Recognize the real-world difference: Under Shafi'i, a minor's bequest to charity is valid; under Hanafi, it is void. Also, a minor's conversion to Islam is only effective through the guardian in Hanafi, while Shafi'i considers it valid for the afterlife.
This dispute has implications for inheritance and religious status.

Commentary
This passage presents the Shafi'i position on the validity of a minor's statements (عبارة). According to al-Shafi'i, if the benefit can be achieved through the guardian's direct action (like accepting Islam – the minor becomes Muslim through his father's Islam; or sale – the guardian handles it), then the minor's own utterance is not considered. However, if the benefit cannot be achieved through the guardian (like a bequest, because the guardian cannot make a bequest on behalf of the minor after death), then the minor's utterance is considered valid. Thus, a bequest by a minor for pious works is valid in the Shafi'i school. The Hanafi school disagrees entirely: they hold that a minor's bequest is void in all cases because it is pure harm (ضرر محض) – it removes ownership without any immediate benefit, and the minor's capacity is deficient. The marginal notes further clarify that the Hanafi school does not recognize the minor's independent conversion to Islam; he only becomes Muslim through his guardian. This contrasts with the Shafi'i view that the minor's faith is valid for the afterlife even if not for worldly rulings. The dispute centers on whether a minor can incur harm through gratuitous transfer of property.

Key Terms
عبارة الصبي (ibarat al-sabi): The verbal expression or utterance of a minor as a legal act.
ضرر محض (darar mahd): Pure harm – an act that brings no benefit to the doer in this world, such as a gratuitous transfer of wealth.
تبرع (tabarru'): Gratuitous act/donation without compensation.

Classification of Impediments to Legal Capacity
Definition and classification of impediments (awāriḍ) to legal capacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two categories of impediments (heavenly and acquired), list the specific impediments in each, and justify the inclusion or exclusion of certain conditions based on Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Impediments to legal capacity are either heavenly (beyond human control) or acquired (involving human choice) — this classification determines how Islamic law treats a person's acts.

We have already learned about legal capacity (ahliyyah) — the attribute that makes a person fit for rights and obligations. Now we turn to the impediments (awāriḍ) that disrupt that capacity. The passage classifies them into two fundamental types: heavenly (samāwī) and acquired (muktasab). Understanding this classification is essential because it affects whether an act is valid, void, or requires compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defined term
Find the phrase 'al-umur al-mu'taridah' and note its linguistic derivation from 'arada (to appear and obstruct). The commentary explains it as things that arise and prevent legal capacity from remaining in its state.
The term is derived from 'arada, meaning to obstruct.
2
Classify into two types
The impediments are divided into heavenly (samāwī) — established by the Lawgiver without human choice — and acquired (muktasab) — opposite of heavenly, involving human action.
Heavenly are beyond power; acquired are within human control.
3
List the members of each type
Count the eleven heavenly impediments: childhood, insanity, imbecility, forgetfulness, sleep, unconsciousness, slavery, illness, menstruation, postnatal bleeding, death. Then the seven acquired: ignorance, intoxication, jest, travel, foolishness, mistake, compulsion.
Memorize the lists as they reappear in fiqh discussions.
4
Rationalize inclusions and exclusions
The commentary explains why pregnancy, breastfeeding, and extreme old age are not listed separately: they are included under illness. Insanity and unconsciousness are listed despite being under illness because they have many distinct rulings.
This shows the author's method of avoiding redundancy while highlighting important subcategories.
5
Determine the legal effect
Each impediment affects either ahliyyah al-wujub (capacity for rights) or ahliyyah al-ada (capacity for action). For example, death removes ahliyyah al-wujub entirely; sleep removes ahliyyah al-ada temporarily.
The effect depends on the type and severity of the impediment.

Commentary
This passage defines and classifies the impediments (awāriḍ) that affect legal capacity (ahliyyah). The commentator explains that these are things that arise and prevent legal capacity from functioning normally. They are divided into two categories: heavenly (samāwī) impediments, which are beyond human control and directly from Allah, enumerating eleven: childhood, insanity, imbecility, forgetfulness, sleep, unconsciousness, slavery, illness, menstruation, postnatal bleeding, and death. The second category is acquired (muktasab) impediments, which involve human choice, enumerating seven: ignorance, intoxication, jest, travel, foolishness, mistake, and compulsion. The marginal notes clarify the linguistic derivation of 'mu'taridah' and explain why certain conditions like pregnancy are not listed separately—they fall under illness—while insanity and unconsciousness are listed despite being illness, because they have many distinct rulings. This classification is fundamental in Hanafi usul al-fiqh for understanding how different states affect a person's capacity to perform legal acts and bear obligations.

Key Terms
الأمور المعترضة (al-umur al-mu'taridah): The things that arise and appear upon legal capacity, preventing it from remaining in its state.
السماوي (al-samawi): That which is established from the Lawgiver without any choice of the servant.
المكتسب (al-muktasab): That which is opposite to the heavenly, acquired through human action.
