Definition of Hukm, Its Components, and Categories
Defining hukm and its components and types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of hukm, identify its three essential components (hakim, mahkum alayh, mahkum bih), and distinguish between prescriptive (taklifi) and declaratory (wad'i) rulings.

Core Idea
Hukm is a quality of the mukallaf's act; rulings are either prescriptive or declaratory, and qiyas only reveals, not establishes.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar introduces the foundational concept of al-hukm in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It defines the ruling as the quality of the mukallaf's act, explains its necessary components – the Lawgiver, the subject, and the object – and divides rulings into two broad categories: prescriptive rulings (ahkam taklifiyya) that directly impose obligation or choice, and declaratory rulings (ahkam wad'iyya) that establish a cause, condition, or preventer. The passage also addresses the epistemological role of qiyas (analogy) as a revealer (muzhir) rather than an establisher (muthbit).

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject matter
Determine what this section of the text is discussing. It introduces a new chapter on al-ahkam (the rulings) within the broader context of usul al-fiqh.
Look for the phrase 'فصل في الأحكام' and the enumeration of what is established by the proofs.
2
Extract the definition and components of hukm
From the text, identify the definition of hukm and its three essential components: al-hakim (Lawgiver), al-mahkum alayh (the mukallaf), and al-mahkum bih (the act of the mukallaf). Note that rulings are described as qualities of the act.
The text explicitly states: 'الحكم مفتقر إلى الحاكم والمحكوم عليه والمحكوم به'.
3
Distinguish the two categories of rulings
Identify the two categories mentioned: prescriptive rulings (ahkam taklifiyya) and declaratory rulings (ahkam wad'iyya). Understand that the former directly address the mukallaf's actions, while the latter deal with causal relationships like cause, condition, and preventer.
The text says: 'وبما يتعلّق به الأحكام الوضعية' as the second category.
4
Analyze the role of qiyas
Understand the Hanafi position that qiyas does not establish new rulings but only transfers an existing ruling from a known case to a new one based on a shared attribute. The text emphasizes that qiyas is a revealer (muzhir), not an establisher (muthbit).
Look for the statement: 'القياس لا يثبت شيئًا وإنما هو للتعدية' and the discussion in the marginal commentary.
5
Understand the epistemological weight of proofs
Note the marginal comment that all legal proofs, including qiyas, are identifiers and indicators that provide knowledge. This reinforces the Hanafi view that the primary sources and qiyas all function as means to know the ruling, though qiyas operates by transference.
The text states: 'إن الأدلة الشرعية كلها معرفات وأمارات'.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and taxonomic exposition typical of Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author opens a new section on the rulings (al-ahkam). He first clarifies that what is established by the primary proofs (Book, Sunnah, Ijma) is two categories: the prescriptive rulings (ahkam taklifiyya) and what pertains to them, i.e., the declaratory rulings (ahkam wad'iyya). Analogy is excluded because it does not establish rulings in the same sense but transfers an existing ruling to a new case. He then defines the constituent elements of a ruling: the Lawgiver (Allah), the subject (the mukallaf), and the object (the act of the mukallaf). Rulings are described as qualities of the act, such as obligation, prohibition, etc. The marginal comments elaborate on the nature of analogy as a revealer rather than an establisher, and define declaratory rulings as causes, conditions, signs, and preventers. The discussion firmly grounds the Hanafi view that legal proofs are all indicators, with analogy serving a transmissive function.

Key Terms
الحكم (al-hukm): The legal ruling that is the quality of the mukallaf's act, such as obligation, prohibition, etc.
الأحكام التكليفية (al-ahkam al-taklifiyya): Prescriptive rulings that directly impose a duty or give a choice on the mukallaf.
الأحكام الوضعية (al-ahkam al-wad'iyya): Declaratory rulings that establish something as a cause, condition, preventer, etc., without directly addressing the mukallaf.
الحاكم (al-hakim): The Lawgiver, Allah Almighty, who issues the rulings.
المحكوم عليه (al-mahkum alayh): The subject of the ruling, i.e., the mukallaf (legally responsible person).
المحكوم به (al-mahkum bih): The object of the ruling, i.e., the act of the mukallaf.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning that transfers a known ruling to a new case based on a common attribute; it does not establish rulings but reveals them.

The Four Types of Mahkum Bih: Pure Rights of Allah
Classification of rulings with detailed definition of pure divine rights.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'pure rights of Allah (haqq Allah khalisah)' and apply the criterion of general benefit to classify rulings.

Core Idea
Pure rights of Allah serve universal benefits, not individual interests.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar introduces the four categories of the object of ruling (al-mahkum bih) – the acts of the legally responsible person. The focus is on the first category: rights that belong exclusively to Allah. Understanding this classification is crucial for usul al-fiqh because it determines the scope of obligations and the role of human agency. We will examine the defining criterion of general benefit and see examples like the sanctity of the Kaaba and the prohibition of zina.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four types of mahkum bih
The passage states that the mahkum bih (act of the mukallaf) is divided into four types. Read the opening lines to list them: pure rights of Allah, rights of people, rulings combining both, and rulings that are neither? Actually the text only mentions the first type explicitly; the other three are referenced. So step: note the taxonomy.
The author says 'As for rulings, they are four, meaning the object of the ruling... four types.'
2
Define 'pure rights of Allah'
Extract the definition: it is a ruling whose benefit is general to all people, not specific to any individual. The passage gives two examples: the sanctity of the Kaaba and the prohibition of zina. Note the explanation that the benefit of the Kaaba is universal because all Muslims use it as a qibla; the benefit of prohibiting zina is protection of lineage for all.
The gloss clarifies: 'general benefit means purification of the soul and perfection of the afterlife for all, without regard to one servant over another.'
3
Understand the attribution of rights to Allah
The passage addresses a potential confusion: why attribute rights to Allah when He does not benefit? The answer is that it is a figure of speech; the rights are established by His command and serve His creation's welfare.
This is a key theological point: Allah's rights are for our own benefit.
4
Distinguish from rights of people
Rights of people are those attached to specific individual interests. The passage contrasts this with the universal benefit of Allah's rights. Later scholars (al-Sanbali) note that some rulings combine both aspects, e.g., theft involves a hadd (Allah's right) and restitution (individual's right).
This distinction is essential for fatwa and qada.

Commentary
This passage continues the usul al-fiqh discussion by specifying the divisions of the mahkum bih (object of ruling). The author introduces the four types of the mahkum bih, which are acts of the mukallaf. The first type is the exclusive rights of Allah, which are those whose benefit is general to all people, such as the sanctity of the Kaaba (all benefit from it as a qibla) and the prohibition of zina (all benefit from the protection of lineage). The marginal notes clarify that the definition of 'right' here is a ruling established with specification to Allah or to His servants, and that the benefit of Allah's rights is universal, not limited to individuals. The note also addresses a potential confusion: why attribute rights to Allah when He does not benefit? The answer is that it is a figure of speech. The passage also mentions the imprecision in the earlier division of rulings into two categories, as prescriptive rulings were omitted from that division but were covered elsewhere in the sections on azimah and rukhsah.

Key Terms
حق الله تعالى خالصة (haqq Allah ta'ala khalisah): A ruling that is a pure right of Allah, attached to a general benefit for the public, not specific to any individual.
حق العباد (haqq al-'ibad): A ruling that pertains to a specific individual's interest, as opposed to the general benefit of Allah's rights.
نفع العام (naf' al-'amm): General benefit that extends to all people, such as spiritual purification and societal order.
المحكوم به (al-mahkum bih): The object of the ruling, which is the act of the mukallaf, divided into four types.
