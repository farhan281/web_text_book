Default Causality of Original Texts
Stating the principle that original texts are by default subject to legal causality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that original texts are inherently causal by default and explain the necessity of dalalat al-tamyeez to distinguish the effective cause.

Core Idea
Original texts are inherently causal by default unless proven ta'abbudi, but the exact cause requires an indication of distinction.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the default assumption for all primary texts (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) is that their rulings are based on a cause ('illah) that can be extended to new cases. This principle rebuts the notion that texts might be purely devotional and not subject to analogical reasoning. However, the principle alone is insufficient; the mujtahid must also find a specific indication (dalalat al-tamyeez) to pinpoint the exact cause among the various attributes of the text.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Principle
Grasp the statement 'al-usul fi al-asl ma'lula' – original texts are inherently causal by default. This means every text from Quran, Sunnah, or Ijma is assumed to have a cause that can be identified and applied to similar cases, unless evidence shows it is purely devotional (ta'abbudi).
This is the default position; it does not mean every ruling must be analogized.
2
Identify the Evidence
The passage cites the Ijma (consensus) of the Companions as evidence. The widespread practice of the Companions in using qiyas when no explicit text exists indicates they considered original texts as implicitly causal.
This Ijma is the dalil aqli (rational proof) for the principle.
3
Recognize the Need for Tamyeez
Even though texts are inherently causal, one cannot simply assume any attribute is the cause. A specific indication (dalalat al-tamyeez) is required to distinguish which attribute can serve as the 'illah. This is a methodological safeguard.
Tamyeez does not achieve certainty; it identifies potential causes for further investigation.
4
Apply the Principle
When encountering a new case, first identify the relevant original text. Assume it has a cause. Then search for dalalat al-tamyeez to determine the specific 'illah. If no tamyeez is found, the ruling may remain ta'abbudi.
This step connects the principle to practical istinbat.

Exception to Faith in Expiation Slaves
Stating a usul rule: specific text overrides general analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a specific text (nass) can restrict a general analogy (qiyas) by identifying the source of exception and its scope.

Core Idea
A specific text overrides a general analogy, restricting the faith requirement to two expiations.

This short passage from Nur al-Anwar presents a concise usul rule: the general requirement that an expiation slave be a believer is set aside for the expiations of oath and zihar. The reason given is that these two cases are based on a specific textual ruling (nass) that modifies the general analogy. Understanding this principle helps us see how specific texts can limit the scope of general qiyas in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
The general rule in Hanafi fiqh is that a slave used for expiation (kaffarah) must be a believer (iman). This is derived from general analogy (qiyas) based on texts like Quran 4:92 which explicitly requires a believing slave.
This general rule applies to most expiations, e.g., for killing, broken fast, etc.
2
Identify the Exception
The passage states that for expiation of an oath (yamin) and zihar (ancient divorce practice), faith is not required. These two are exceptions.
The exception is limited to these two specific cases.
3
Locate the Dalil for the Exception
The reason given is 'because it is an extension to something in which there is a text that changes it.' This refers to the specific Quranic verses (5:89 for oath, 58:3-4 for zihar) that command freeing a slave without the qualification of faith.
The dalil is a specific text (nass) that is unrestricted (mutlaq) regarding faith.
4
Analyse the Usul Principle
The usul principle at work: 'Al-mutlaq yajri 'ala itlaqih' (the unrestricted remains unrestricted until a restriction is proven). Since the verses do not mention faith, the general analogy that would impose faith is overridden by the specific text.
This is an example of takhsis (specification) of a general analogy by a specific text.
5
Determine the Scope (Thamarah)
The exception applies only to expiation of oath and zihar. For all other expiations (e.g., killing, broken fast), the general rule requiring faith remains. The usul rule is that a specific text cannot be extended beyond its own context via analogy.
The thamarah is the practical ruling: faith is not required for these two expiations.

Defining istishab al-hal and presenting the Hanafi rejection of it as a proof against the Shafi'i acceptance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that existence does not imply continuance and apply it to evaluate the Shafi'i use of Quran 6:145 as a proof for istishab.

Core Idea
Istishab al-hal is not a proof because the cause of origin does not guarantee survival.

This passage tackles a foundational usul dispute: can we presume that a past ruling continues simply because no contrary evidence appears? The Shafi'i school says yes (istishab al-hal), but the Hanafi school demands a separate evidence for continuity. Let us walk through the definition, the arguments, and the Hanafi critique.

Guided Path
1

This definition is central to understanding the debate.
2

Identify why the Shafi'is consider istishab a proof. What two arguments do they give? Note the Quranic verse (6:145) and the continuity of Shari'ah after the Prophet.
3
State the Hanafi rejection and rational argument
The Hanafis reject istishab. Their core argument: 'the establisher is not the maintainer' (al-muthbit laysa bi-mubq). Why is existence different from continuance? Find this distinction in the text.
This is the critical usul principle.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i verse
The Hanafis do not accept that verse 6:145 commands istishab. Instead, they argue it points to the explicit text of 2:29. How does this shift the basis of permissibility from presumption to text?

The passage ends with a reference to al-Talwih, which suggests a further response to the Hanafi position, implying the debate is nuanced. Overall, the Hanafi position is that istishab is not an independent proof because continuity requires a specific dalil, not mere absence of change.

The Ruling of Ijtihad: Probability and the Dispute with the Mu'tazila
Explains the ruling of ijtihad and addresses the debate on error and correctness.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that ijtihad produces probable (zanni) rulings and that only one correct ruling exists in Allah's knowledge, and evaluate the Mu'tazili counter-argument using the evidence from the incident of Ibn Mas'ud.

Core Idea
Ijtihad yields probability, not certainty; the mujtahid may err, and only one ruling is true, though unknown to us.

In this lesson, we examine a foundational debate in usul al-fiqh: when a mujtahid derives a ruling, is he always correct, or can he err? The classical Hanafi position, supported by the practice of the Companions, holds that ijtihad produces probable knowledge (zann) and that only one ruling is correct in Allah's knowledge, though we may not know which one. This principle explains why all four Sunni schools are considered valid. We will explore the evidence from the incident of Ibn Mas'ud on the mufawwidah and contrast the Hanafi view with that of the Mu'tazila.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central issue: What is the nature of ijtihad and correctness?
The text states that ijtihad leads to 'isaba bi ghalib al-ra'y' (hitting the truth by predominant opinion), not certainty. The mujtahid may be correct or err. The truth in disputed matters is one, but unknown. This is the Hanafi position.
2
Recognize the Hanafi evidence from the incident of Ibn Mas'ud.
Ibn Mas'ud said: 'I exert my opinion... if I err it is from me and from Satan.' The Companions were present and did not object, forming consensus (ijma) that ijtihad may include error.
This is a dalil naqli (narrative proof).
3
State the opposing view of the Mu'tazila.
The Mu'tazila claim that every mujtahid is correct (musib) and that truth in disputed matters is multiple. They reject the possibility of error.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response.
The Hanafi response is that the incident of Ibn Mas'ud proves error is possible. Moreover, logically, contradictory rulings cannot both be true; only one can be correct.
Also, most fiqh issues are based on qiyas, which yields probability, not certainty.
5
Apply the principle to the validity of the four madhhabs.
Since the true ruling is unknown, all four schools are valid because their mujtahid may have hit the truth. This is the thamarah al-khilaf.

Chapter of Analogical Reasoning. Statement of the ruling of ijtihad. Its ruling is hitting the truth by predominant opinion, meaning the ruling of ijtihad due to its mention nearby, or the ruling of qiyas due to its mention in summary: hitting the truth by predominant opinion without certainty, until we said: the mujtahid errs and hits, and the truth in disagreement is one, but that one is not known with certainty, so for this we said the four schools are valid. And we took the narration of Ibn Mas'ud, may Allah be pleased with him, regarding the woman with deferred dowry (al-mufawwidah), which is she whose husband died before consummation and no dowry was named for her. Ibn Mas'ud was asked about her, and he said: 'I exert my opinion (ijtihad) in it; if I am correct it is from Allah, and if I err it is from me and from Satan. I think she is entitled to the dowry of her peers, neither deficient nor excessive.' And that was in the presence of the Companions, and none of them objected to him, so it was consensus that ijtihad may contain error. The Mu'tazila said: Every mujtahid is correct, and the truth in the place of disagreement is multiple. And some Ash'aris... Commentary
This passage is a ruling and dispute text. It first states the ruling of ijtihad: it leads to hitting the truth by predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y), not certainty. The mujtahid may be correct or may err; the truth in disputed matters is one specific ruling, but we do not know it with certainty. Therefore, all four madhhabs are valid. Evidence is provided from the incident of Ibn Mas'ud regarding the mufawwidah (woman without a named dowry). He exercised ijtihad and said her dowry is like her peers, and the Companions did not object, forming consensus that ijtihad may err. The Mu'tazila are refuted as they claim every mujtahid is correct and truth is multiple. Some Ash'aris are mentioned as also differing. The commentary adds that ijtihad may sometimes yield certainty, but the general ruling is probability. The passage emphasizes that most fiqh issues are derived through qiyas, and the ruling of ijtihad is that it produces a probable result.

Key Terms
الإصابة بغالب الرأي (al-isaba bi ghalib al-ra'y): Hitting the truth by predominant probability, not certainty.
المفوضة (al-mufawwidah): A woman whose marriage was contracted without a specified dowry or on condition of no dowry.
