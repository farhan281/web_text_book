Proving Qiyas from the Quranic Command 'Fai'tabiru' and Linguistic Analogy
Explaining the proof of qiyas from Quran and isti'ara

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of istinbat to derive the authority of qiyas from the Quranic verse (59:2) using dalalat al-nass (textual indication) and identify why this does not lead to circular reasoning.

Core Idea
The Quran commands reflection, and that very command, by implication, establishes qiyas—not as a qiyas itself but by indicating the principle of analogy.

This passage from a commentary on Usul al-Fiqh explains how the authority of qiyas is derived from the Quranic verse 'So take heed, O people of insight' (59:2). The author emphasizes that the proof is not from the explicit wording but from textual indication (dalalat al-nass), using the example of linguistic borrowing (isti'ara) to illustrate how a shared attribute allows transfer of meaning. The commentary also addresses a potential circularity: if the verse were used as a qiyas to prove qiyas, that would be circular; but the proof is by implication, not analogy. You will learn the precise method of deriving the hujjiyya of qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source text and its context
The verse 59:2 is cited; note that it appears in the context of punishment of the People of the Book. Recognize that the explicit command 'take heed' is directed at those of insight.
The context (siyaq) is crucial for understanding the type of indication.
2
Classify the textual indication
According to the commentary, the verse indicates qiyas by ishara (implication), not by ibarah (explicit wording). Why? Because the context is admonition, not a direct command to perform qiyas. The underlying principle of i'tibar is general, though the specific instance is punishment.
In Hanafi usul, ishara is one of the four types of dalalat al-nass.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle of dalalat al-nass
Hanafi usul holds that implication (ishara) is a valid proof. The command to 'take heed' implies that one should draw analogies in similar situations, i.e., when the effective cause ('illah) is present, the ruling applies. This yields the principle that qiyas is a source of law.
The proof is 'aqli (rational) but based on textual indication, not pure reason.
4
Distinguish from circular reasoning
A potential objection: proving qiyas by this verse seems to use qiyas itself (since the verse compares the people of insight to the disbelievers). The commentator refutes this: the proof is by textual indication, not by qiyas. The existence of a cause necessitating its ruling is known by language and intellect, not by analogical reasoning, so no circularity occurs.
Circularity (dawr) would occur if the proof of qiyas itself depended on qiyas.
5
Reinforce with linguistic analogy
Just as the word 'lion' is borrowed for a brave man due to shared courage, the command to 'take heed' is extended from the specific case of punishment to all situations where a cause is shared. This illustrates the principle of analogy in language, mirroring legal qiyas.
The linguistic analogy (isti'ara) demonstrates that transfer based on shared attribute is linguistically sound.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a matn about qiyas. It begins by stating that the authority of qiyas is not proven by its own explicit wording, but rather by rational proof based on textual indication (dalalat al-nass) from the Quranic verse "So take heed, O people of insight" (59:2). The commentator argues that this verse is not itself a qiyas, but a command to reflect and draw analogies. To avoid circular reasoning (that proving qiyas by qiyas would be circular), he explains that the verse indicates qiyas by implication (ishara) rather than by explicit wording. He then draws an analogy to linguistic borrowing (isti'ara): just as the word 'lion' is borrowed for a brave man because of shared courage, so too is the command to 'take heed' transferred from the specific case of punishment to general legal analogy. The commentator also addresses a common misunderstanding: some thought that the verse proves qiyas by itself being a qiyas, but he clarifies that the proof is by textual indication, not by qiyas. The marginal notes from 'al-Qamar' and 'al-Sunbulī' provide further clarifications on the proper understanding of the matn.

Key Terms
قياس (qiyas): The legal analogy that transfers a ruling from an original case (asl) to a new case (far') due to a shared effective cause (illah).
استعارة (isti'ara): Linguistic borrowing: using a word for something other than its original meaning due to a shared quality (e.g., 'lion' for a brave man).
دلالة النص (dalalat al-nass): Textual indication: a meaning derived from the text not by its explicit wording but by implication from its context or underlying rationale.

The Meaning of Lam in the Zakat Verse: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Interpreting the lam in 'lil-fuqara' as lam al-aqiba.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi interpretation of the lam in Quran 9:60 as lam al-aqiba, identify the Shafi'i counterposition, and evaluate the Hanafi proof using the rational argument from permissibility of using a trade slave girl.

Core Idea
Zakat is Allah's right — the lam indicates consequence for the poor, not ownership.

In this passage from Noor al-Anwar, the Hanafi school defends its distinctive view that the lam in 'lil-fuqara' denotes ultimate outcome (lam al-aqiba) rather than ownership. This is more than a grammatical nuance — it determines the legal nature of zakat as a right of Allah, which in turn affects concrete fiqh rulings such as the permissibility of using a trade slave girl before paying zakat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the grammatical pivot
The key term is the lam in 'lil-fuqara'. The Hanafi school reads it as lam al-aqiba, the Shafi'i as lam al-tamlik. Understand the two competing interpretations.
Lam al-aqiba indicates the ultimate consequence; lam al-tamlik indicates transfer of ownership.
2
Examine the Hanafi dalil
The text cites Quran 9:60 and the hadith of Mu'adh. But its main proof is a rational argument: if the poor owned zakat, then having intercourse with a trade slave girl before paying zakat would be impermissible due to co-ownership, yet it is allowed in Hanafi fiqh.
This rational proof (istidlal) is used to determine that the lam cannot be tamlik.
3
Contrast the Shafi'i position
The Shafi'i school argues that the lam indicates ownership, so the poor own the zakat directly. They rely on the default meaning of lam and other texts that suggest entitlement.
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the lam is a preposition of ownership, making zakat a joint property of the poor.
4
Apply the dispute to a branch (furu')
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is clear: in Hanafi, one may have intercourse with a trade slave girl before paying zakat; in Shafi'i, it is forbidden because the poor are co-owners.
This example shows the real-world impact of a seemingly linguistic dispute.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi tarjih
The text gives preference to the Hanafi view by showing that the rational proof supports lam al-aqiba and that the hadith 'taken from their rich and returned to their poor' implies Allah takes and gives, not direct ownership.
The Hanafi position is considered mufta bihi in the school.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary discussing the interpretation of the Quranic verse 'Indeed the charities are for the poor...' (Al-Tawbah 60) and the nature of zakat. The central issue is whether the lam in 'lil-fuqara' indicates ownership (lam al-tamlik) or consequence (lam al-aqiba). The Hanafi position, presented here, holds that zakat is a right of Allah, not a right of the poor. The lam indicates that zakat ultimately benefits the poor as a consequence of fulfilling Allah's right, not because they own it. The author supports this with rational arguments: if the poor had a right, then buying a slave girl for trade and having intercourse with her before paying zakat would be impermissible (like a jointly owned slave girl), but it is permissible in Hanafi fiqh. The hadith of Mu'adh is cited: '...a charity taken from their rich and returned to their poor.' The Hanafi interpretation is that Allah takes the zakat and gives it to the poor directly, so it is not owned by the payer or the recipient. The passage then addresses an objection: the zakat animal (sheep) cannot fulfill all the diverse needs of the poor, so the command to spend implies substitution (istibdal) with currency. The response is that zakat is the primary and universal source for the poor, unlike other obligations like 'ushr or kaffarah, which are rare. The later part clarifies that substitution is permitted because the sheep is mentioned only as a measure of value, not as a specific requirement. This is a classic Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute: Shafi'i holds that the lam indicates ownership by the poor, leading to different rulings in branches of fiqh.

The Role of Intellect in Legal Capacity: A Dispute
Analyzes the nature of intellect and its role in legal rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi-Ash'ari position on the role of intellect in legal obligation and evaluate the Mu'tazilite counter-argument using the distinction between meanings of good and bad.

Core Idea
Intellect alone cannot establish legal obligation; revelation is necessary, and the Lawgiver set puberty as the criterion for mature intellect.

This passage explores a foundational dispute in usul al-fiqh: can human intellect independently determine what is legally good (husn) and bad (qubh)? The Hanafi-Ash'ari position, as presented here, argues that intellect is insufficient without divine revelation. Understanding this debate is crucial for grasping the limits of reason in Islamic law and the basis of legal capacity (ahliyya).

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
The core dispute is whether intellect alone can determine legal obligation, prohibition, and reward/punishment. The Hanafi/Ash'ari school says no; the Mu'tazila say yes.
The text frames this within the topic of legal capacity (ahliyya).
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
The Hanafi/Ash'ari position: intellect has no authority in legal rulings without revelation (sam'). Their proof is Quran 17:15, indicating punishment only after sending a messenger.
They also argue that a rational minor's faith is invalid because revelation has not addressed him.
3
State the Mu'tazila position and its proof
The Mu'tazila say intellect is a necessitating cause for good/bad. They cite Quran 6:74 (Ibrahim's statement before revelation) and argue that a rational minor is obligated to believe.
They claim intellectual causes are above legal causes because they are not abrogable.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response distinguishes three meanings of husn/qubh: natural compatibility, perfection/deficiency, and relation to reward/punishment. Only the third is disputed; intellect perceives the first two by consensus, but the third depends on revelation.
The story of Ibrahim is interpreted as using natural intellect, not an independent legal source.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: a rational minor is not obligated according to Hanafi/Ash'ari, but is obligated according to Mu'tazila. Also, matters of the unseen (e.g., vision of Allah) cannot be known by intellect alone.
This affects when legal responsibility begins.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute between the Ash'ariyya (and Imam al-Shafi'i) and the Mu'tazila regarding the role of intellect ('aql) in determining legal good (husn) and bad (qubh). The Hanafi position, as presented, sides with the Ash'aris in affirming that intellect alone, without revelation (sam'), cannot determine legal value; rather, revelation is the sole arbiter of what is obligatory or prohibited. The text begins by noting that intellect varies among people, with prophets and saints having the highest degree, then scholars, then common people, etc. Despite this variation, the Lawgiver (Shar') has made puberty (bulugh) the criterion for the level of intellect that triggers obligation (taklif). The Ash'aris argue that without revelation, intellect has no authority in legal rulings; the Qur'anic verse (17:15) is cited: 'We do not punish until We send a messenger.' The Mu'tazila, on the other hand, claim that intellect itself is a necessitating cause (illa) for good and bad, independent of revelation. They argue from the story of Ibrahim (6:74) where he called his father a disbeliever before receiving revelation, implying that intellect alone can judge. The text refutes the Mu'tazila by distinguishing between two meanings of good and bad: one relating to natural disposition (which all agree intellect can perceive) and the other relating to legal reward and punishment (which is the point of contention). The Hanafi/Ash'ari position maintains that intellect cannot independently establish rulings that are subject to abrogation or that concern the unseen (sam'iyyat), such as seeing Allah, punishment of the grave, etc. The passage also addresses the Mu'tazila view that a rational minor is obligated to believe, which the Ash'aris reject because obligation (taklif) depends on revelation address. Overall, the text establishes that for legal capacity (ahliyya), intellect is a necessary but not sufficient condition; it must be combined with the age of maturity and the reception of divine revelation.

Key Terms
العقل (al-'aql): A varying creation (khalq mutafawwit) in people, by which one discerns good and bad in non-legal senses, and which serves as a basis for legal capacity when combined with puberty.
السمع (al-sam'): Divine revelation (the heard from the Lawgiver), i.e., the legal proofs (adilla shar'iyya) from Quran and Sunnah.
الحسن والقبح (al-husn wa al-qubh): Three meanings: (1) natural compatibility; (2) perfection vs. deficiency; (3) relation to reward/punishment in the afterlife. The third is the disputed sense regarding intellect's role.

Childhood as a Heavenly Impediment: Derivation and Comparison
Detailed analysis of childhood as a heavenly impediment and comparison with insanity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi istinbat that childhood is a heavenly impediment and apply the reasoning to distinguish it from insanity.

Core Idea
Childhood is an impediment because it is incidental, not essential, like ignorance.

This passage tackles a subtle point: why is childhood listed among impediments (عوارض) when it is a natural state? The Hanafi jurists argue that childhood is not intrinsic to human essence, as Adam was created adult, so it is an incidental attribute. They support this by analogizing to ignorance, which is also original yet considered an impediment, citing the Quran. The comparison with insanity reveals the practical legal difference: childhood has a fixed end, so rulings can be deferred; insanity does not, so immediate action is needed to avoid harm.

Guided Path
1

Read the opening: why does the author justify listing childhood among impediments even though it is natural? The answer lies in the concept of essence (māhiyya): childhood is not part of the definition of a human.

2

The text cites Surah Nahl 16:78 to show that ignorance is original yet treated as an impediment. This strengthens the case for childhood being an impediment despite being original.
The verse is used as a proof (dalīl) that original states can still be impediments.
3

The passage compares the rulings for a minor's wife vs. an insane person's wife accepting Islam. Note the different procedures: for the minor, we wait; for the insane, we act immediately. Understand why: insanity has no end, so waiting causes harm.
This is a practical example (thamara) showing how the legal capacity differs.
4

From the comparison, derive the usul principle: an impediment with a fixed end allows deferral; one without end requires immediate resolution. This is based on the maqāsid (preventing harm).

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed analysis of childhood (sughr) as a heavenly impediment to legal capacity. It begins by justifying why childhood is listed among impediments despite being inherent: it is not part of the essence of humanity (since Adam was created as an adult), so it is an incidental ('āriḍ) attribute. The passage then compares childhood with insanity, stating that in its initial stage, childhood is even lower than insanity in terms of capacity. The key difference is demonstrated through the ruling on offering Islam: if the wife of a minor becomes Muslim, we do not offer Islam to the minor's parents but wait until the minor himself can understand. If the wife of an insane person becomes Muslim, we immediately offer Islam to the insane person's parents because insanity has no foreseeable end, and delaying would harm the Muslim woman. This shows the practical effect of the difference: childhood has a definite end (age of majority), so we can wait; insanity may be permanent, so we act to avoid harm. The marginal notes further elaborate on why childhood is an impediment, citing the verse from Surah Nahl to show that ignorance is also an original state yet considered an impediment. It also discusses the subsidiary rulings: a non-discriminating child becomes a Muslim by following his parents or the Islamic land, and similarly becomes an apostate by their apostasy.

Key Terms
الصغر (al-sughr): The state of being a minor, from birth until the age of majority (bulugh).
التمييز (al-tamyiz): The ability to discriminate and understand the basic aspects of speech and actions.
الجنون (al-junun): Insanity; a mental condition that removes legal capacity of performance.
عارض ('arid): An incidental attribute that is not part of the essence, often affecting legal rulings.
