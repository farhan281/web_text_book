The Life and Legacy of Shaykh Jīwan
Biographical note on the author Mulla Jīwan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key biographical facts of Shaykh Jīwan and identify his contributions to Hanafi scholarship.

Core Idea
Shaykh Jīwan, descendant of Abu Bakr and teacher of Aurangzeb, authored this seminal Hanafi usul commentary.

This passage introduces the author of the commentary we are studying. Shaykh Jīwan was a distinguished Hanafi scholar whose life exemplifies the pursuit of knowledge and service to the Muslim community. Understanding his background helps appreciate the authority of this work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's full name and lineage
Note the name 'Shaykh Jīwan' and his lineage traced back to the first Caliph Abu Bakr al-Siddiq. This establishes a scholarly pedigree.
The name 'Jīwan' means 'life' in Hindi.
2
Recognize his education and teachers
He memorized the Qur'an, had a strong memory, and studied under Mulla Lutf Allah al-Kuruli.
His teacher's name appears as 'al-Kuruli' indicating his origin from Kura, a region in India.
3
Examine his relationship with Mughal patronage
He went to Sultan Alamgir (Aurangzeb), who honored him and became his student. The emperor's sons also respected him.
This reflects the Hanafi tradition of scholars serving in courts.
4
Recall his scholarly activities and travels
He visited Mecca and Medina (the two noble sanctuaries) and spent his life teaching and authoring works.
The biography is quoted from Sayyid Ghulam Ali Azad al-Bilgrami.
5
Understand the marginal notes' context
The marginal note from al-Sunbuli discusses concession (rukhsah) vs permissibility (ibahah), which is a usul al-fiqh topic.
This shows that the biography is embedded within a usul text.

(Margin: 'And it is granted concession' — meaning the third and fourth categories of prohibition are granted concession in complete coercion, not permissibility, because their prohibition remains as it was. Only concession is given to the coerced person in complete coercion to remove hardship. Therefore, if the coerced person endures until he is killed, he becomes a martyr and rewarded, God willing, unlike permissibility where the prohibition does not remain and the coerced person is not rewarded for refraining but rather sins. (al-Sunbuli) Regarding the category of permissibility: the difference between concession and permissibility is that in permissibility the prohibition rises, while in concession it does not rise, rather only sin is removed. Some usulis said: It is better not to mention permissibility because if there is sin in patience it is obligation, otherwise it is concession. So the sum is that it falls under obligation or concession. Therefore the commentator said: 'Since we have stated that it is either included in obligation or in concession.' (al-Sunbuli) Regarding 'Shaykh Jīwan': with a kasra on the jim, sukun on the ya, fatha on the waw, sukun on the nun, meaning 'life' in Hindi. He is my friend, his lineage traces back to the first caliph, the great Siddiq (may Allah be pleased with him). He was born in Amithi, a village in the outskirts of Lakhnau, and grew up there, memorized the Qur'an, had a strong memory, memorized book passages letter by letter. He traveled to various regions to acquire knowledge. He completed his studies under Mulla Lutf Allah al-Kuruli (related to Kura, a region of Fatehpur in India). Then he went to the Sultan Alamgir, who honored and respected him, and the Sultan became his student. The Sultan observed utmost etiquette towards him, and his sons Shah Alam and others also respected him. He had the honor of visiting the two noble sanctuaries (may Allah increase them in honor). He spent his precious life in teaching and authorship. Thus said Sahan al-Hind, al-Sayyid Ghulam 'Ali Azad al-Bilgrami. (al-Qamar)

This passage is a biographical note on the Hanafi scholar Shaykh Jīwan (Mulla Jīwan), the author of the commentary (likely 'Nur al-Anwar') that this text is part of. The note begins with the author's self-identification as 'the servant in need of Allah', then provides his full lineage, indicating he is a Hanafi. There is a marginal note from al-Sunbuli reiterating the difference between concession and permissibility, confirming the earlier classification. Biography of Shaykh Jiyun and the Provenance of Nur al-Anwar
Biography and colophon of Nur al-Anwar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author of Nur al-Anwar, describe his scholarly lineage, and explain the significance of the work's completion details for establishing textual authority in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Nur al-Anwar, a foundational Hanafi usul text, was authored by Shaykh Jiyun in Medina in 1105 AH and later elucidated through a hashiah completed in 1276 AH.

Every scholarly work carries a chain of transmission and a context that establishes its authority. This lesson introduces you to the author of Nur al-Anwar, Shaykh Jiyun, and the colophon details that connect this commentary on al-Manar to the broader Hanafi tradition. Understanding these biographical facts helps you appreciate the textual reliability and the scholarly network behind the work.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Author and His Lineage
Locate the name and title of the author: Shaykh Ahmad, known as Shaykh Jiyun al-Hanafi. Note his lineage traced back to Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, which is mentioned to affirm scholarly and spiritual connection.
The author's nisbah 'al-Hanafi' confirms his madhhab affiliation.
2
Examine the Author's Education and Career
Review the biographical clues: born in Amithai near Lucknow, memorized Quran, studied under Mulla Lutfullah, taught Sultan Aurangzeb, visited the Haramayn. This establishes his scholarly credentials and influence.
His relationship with the Mughal emperor underscores the authority of his work.
3
Understand the Completion Details
Note the dates and locations: original work (Nur al-Anwar) completed in Medina in 1105 AH when the author was 58; the hashiah (super-commentary) completed in Jaunpur in 1276 AH. This indicates a later scholar's engagement with the text.
The hashiah author prays for the work's acceptance and names his teacher and student, creating a clear isnad (chain).

Commentary
This passage is a biographical and colophon section from the hashiah (super-commentary) on Nur al-Anwar. It first identifies the author of the original commentary as Shaykh Ahmad known as Shaykh Jiyun, a Hanafi scholar from India. It details his lineage back to Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, his birthplace near Lucknow, his memorization of the Quran, studies under Mulla Lutfullah, and his relationship with Emperor Aurangzeb Alamgir, who became his student. The author then notes the completion of the original work 'Nur al-Anwar' in Medina in 1105 AH at age 58. The hashiah itself was completed later in Jaunpur in 1276 AH by the super-commentator, who names his teacher Shaykh Muhammad Imam Bakhsh and prays for the acceptance of the work and benefit for his student Muhammad Abd al-Hayy. This is typical front matter providing provenance and chain of transmission.

Key Terms
الشيخ جيون (al-Shaykh Jiyun): The author of Nur al-Anwar, a Hanafi scholar from India, original name Shaykh Ahmad
نور الأنوار (Nur al-Anwar): A commentary on al-Manar, a key text in Hanafi usul al-fiqh
الحاشية (al-Hashiyah): A super-commentary or marginal gloss on an existing commentary
