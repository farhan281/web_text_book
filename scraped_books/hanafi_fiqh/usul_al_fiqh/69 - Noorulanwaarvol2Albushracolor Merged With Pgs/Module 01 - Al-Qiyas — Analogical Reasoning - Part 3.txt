Conflict of Preponderances in Qiyās: Essence vs. Accident
Rule on conflicting preponderances with fiqhi example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that preponderance in essence (dhāt) outweighs preponderance in accident (ḥāl) when two qiyās conflict, using the example of a usurped sheep.

Core Idea
When two conflicting analogies conflict, preponderance goes to the fully subsisting attribute (essence) over the partially subsisting one (accident).

This passage from Noorul Anwar presents a crucial Hanafi tarjīḥ rule: when two qiyās conflict, preponderance in essence (dhāt) takes precedence over preponderance in accident (ḥāl). The rule is then applied to a classic fiqhi case: a usurped sheep that is cooked and roasted. Understanding this principle enables you to resolve situations where multiple analogies seem to pull in different directions.

Guided Path
1
Define iṭirād and inʿikās
Understand these two conditions for a valid ʿilla in qiyās: consistency and reversibility.
iṭirād = ruling exists when description exists; inʿikās = ruling absent when description absent.
2
Identify the tarjīḥ rule
When two preponderances conflict, the one based on essence (dhāt) outweighs the one based on accident (ḥāl).
Essence = fully subsisting attribute; accident = partially subsisting attribute.
3
Apply to the sheep case
Walk through the two conflicting analogies: owner’s right to physical item vs. usurper’s right to craftsmanship.
The craftsmanship fully subsists (essence) while the sheep exists only partially (accident).
4
Identify the Hanafi position and proof
Note how the rule leads to the owner’s right being cut off; owner takes compensation.
Proof: the craftsmanship is like essence, the physical item like accident.
5
Compare with Shafi'i view
Recognize the ikhtilaf and the tarjīḥ mechanism that prefers the Hanafi position.
Shafi'i analogizes to a case where right is not cut off; Hanafi rebuttal based on essence/accident distinction.

Commentary
This passage from Noorul Anwar, a foundational Hanafi usul text, explains an important qiyās principle: when two conflicting preponderances (tarjīḥayn) exist, the preponderance based on essence (dhāt) is stronger than that based on accident (ḥāl). The text first defines iṭirād (consistency) and inʿikās (reversibility) as they relate to the validity of a description (waṣf) in qiyās. A description is 'consistent' if the ruling exists whenever the description exists; it is 'reversible' if the ruling is absent whenever the description is absent. The example of wiping the head (mash al-ra's) illustrates: the description 'wiping' implies no repetition in sunna, but this is reversible to 'non-wiping' requiring repetition. Imam Shafi'i's view that wiping is a pillar (rukn) does not reverse because non-pillar acts like rinsing and snuffing still require repetition. Then the author introduces the core rule: when two qiyās conflict, the preponderance in the essence (e.g., a description that is intrinsic) takes precedence over a preponderance in an accident (acquired state). This is because the accident depends on the essence and cannot override it. The rule is then applied to a fiqhi case: usurpation (ghaṣb) of a sheep, where the usurper slaughters, cooks, and roasts it. According to the Hanafīs, the owner's right to the physical item (ʿayn) is cut off, and he is entitled to compensation (qīma) instead. The reasoning is that the craftsmanship of cooking and roasting, which is the usurper's right, subsists fully (from every aspect), while the original sheep exists only partially (its form changed, some benefits lost). So the craftsmanship is treated as the essence and the physical item as accident. This contradicts the apparent priority of the original item. The text contrasts this with Imam Shafi'i's view, who analogizes it to a case where the owner's right is not cut off. The Hanafīs give preponderance to their position because the existence (the craftsmanship as it is) is like the essence, while the changed physical item is like an attribute. Existence (waṣf consistent) outweighs the attribute of change. The passage concludes with an inheritance example: preferring the son's son over the uncle because his kinship (brotherhood) is essential, while the uncle's closeness in degree is accidental. This nuanced rule guides the jurist in weighing conflicting analogies.

Key Terms
اطراد (iṭirād): Consistency: the existence of the ruling upon the existence of the description.
انعكاس (inʿikās): Reversibility: the non-existence of the ruling upon the non-existence of the description.
ترجيح (tarjīḥ): Preponderance: giving weight to one of two conflicting qiyās or descriptions.
ذات (dhāt): Essence: the intrinsic, subsisting attribute of a thing.
حال (ḥāl): Accident: a contingent, dependent state of a thing.

Tarjih When Two Analogies Conflict
Resolving conflict of two analogies in usurper cooking goat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle of tarjih when two qiyas conflict and apply it to the cooked goat case.

Core Idea
When two analogies conflict, the one whose illa is more fully realized takes precedence.

In Hanafi usul, qiyas must be consistent; when two valid but conflicting qiyas arise, we apply tarjih to determine which ruling prevails. The classic example is the cooked goat case: after a usurper cooks a goat, the original owner's right to his property and the usurper's right to his labor (industry) come into conflict. This passage shows how the Hanafi school resolves the conflict by examining the strength of each analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting analogies
Recognize that the Shafi'i school analogizes the cooked goat to a case where the owner's right is unaffected, while Abu Hanifa analogizes it to a case of complete destruction where the usurper compensates. These two analogies yield opposite rulings.
The Shafi'i qiyas: owner takes goat + pays deficiency. The Hanafi qiyas: usurper takes goat + pays value.
2
Determine the illa in each analogy
For the Shafi'i, the illa is that the owner's property remains identifiable despite the cooking, so his right persists. For Abu Hanifa, the illa is that the industry (cooking) is fully realized and permanent, while the original property is partially destroyed.
The Hanafi illa: 'industry subsists from every aspect'; the Shafi'i illa: 'ownership subsists in the original property'.
3
Apply the Hanafi tarjih principle
The principle: the qiyas whose illa is more fully realized (qaim min kulli wajh) preponderates over the one whose illa is only partially realized. Here, the industry (sin'a) subsists fully, whereas the original 'ayn is destroyed in one aspect (its form and name have changed).
Thus, the industry is like essence (dhat) and the property like attribute (sifah), and essence precedes attribute.
4
Derive the ruling
Since the usurper's right (through industry) is stronger, he keeps the cooked goat and pays its value to the owner. This is the mufta bihi Hanafi position.
The owner receives financial compensation for his loss, but the actual object remains with the usurper.

Invalid Types of Tarjih in Hanafi Usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify three invalid methods of tarjih according to the Hanafi school and explain why the Hanafis reject them, contrasting with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
A descriptor's strength lies in its meaning, not its quantity.

Tarjih, or preponderance, is essential in qiyas when conflicting analogies arise. The Hanafi school rejects certain methods of tarjih that rely on superficial factors such as the number of similarities or the generality of a descriptor. This lesson examines three such invalid methods and the Hanafi rationale for rejecting them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three invalid methods

2
Understand the Shafi'i justification
Examine the Shafi'i argument: multiplicity of similarities gives stronger indication. They also accept generality and fewness as factors. Note the example of brother vs. cousin.
The Shafi'i view is based on the apparent weight of quantity.
3
Analyze the Hanafi counter-argument
The Hanafis argue that the criterion is the meaning (ma'nā) of the descriptor, not its form. Fewness of parts is like conciseness in a text, which is not preferred. Generality does not confer strength.
The essence is the strength and effect of the descriptor, not its multiplicity.
4
Apply to the manumission example

This is the thamarah al-khilaf.
5
Contrast the practical consequence
The difference in methodology leads to different rulings in specific cases. Understand that the Hanafi method prioritizes the intrinsic strength of the descriptor, avoiding arbitrary preference based on numbers.
This preserves consistency in qiyas.

Three Invalid Preferences in Qiyas: Hanafi Critique of Shafi'i Tarjeeh
Explains three invalid types of tarjeeh in qiyas with examples and Hanafi critique

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain why each of the three invalid preferences (ghalabat al-ashbah, al-umoom, qillat al-awsaaf) is rejected by the Hanafi school, and apply the criterion of effectiveness (ta'thir) to evaluate analogical reasoning.

Core Idea
Preference in qiyas depends only on the strength of the effective cause, not on the quantity, generality, or simplicity of attributes.

In this passage from Noor al-Anwar, the author presents three types of tarjeeh (preference) that Hanafis consider invalid in analogical reasoning (qiyas). The Shafi'i school accepts these types, but Hanafis reject them because they deviate from the true criterion: the effectiveness (ta'thir) of the descriptive cause ('illa). Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to evaluate competing analogies and strengthens your grip on Hanafi usul methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi criterion for tarjeeh
First, recall that Hanafis hold that the only valid basis for preferring one 'illa over another is its ta'thir (effective influence on the ruling). All other factors—quantity of similarities, generality, or simplicity—are irrelevant.
This is the key principle underlying the entire critique.
2
Examine the first invalid preference: ghalabat al-ashbah
The Shafi'is argue that if a branch resembles one root in many points of similarity, that analogy should be preferred. The Hanafi response: many similarities may be superficial; only the effective meaning matters. The example given is the brother's emancipation: Shafi'is analogize brother to cousin (many similarities) and deny emancipation; Hanafis stick to the effective cause (mahram kinship) and affirm emancipation.
Know the example: brother vs. cousin in emancipation.
3
Examine the second and third invalid preferences: al-umoom and qillat al-awsaaf
Shafi'is prefer a general attribute (e.g., taste in riba) because it covers more cases, and a simple attribute (e.g., taste alone) because it has fewer parts. Hanafis rebut: generality is not a valid criterion because Shafi'is themselves prefer specific texts over general ones; simplicity does not guarantee effectiveness—a compound cause can be stronger.
Note the internal consistency argument: Shafi'is should prefer the specific attribute by their own textual principle.
4
Trace the overall Hanafi rebuttal structure
The author uses a combination of direct counterexamples, appeal to consistency (within Shafi'i usul), and emphasis on ta'thir. The final part discusses how a mu'allil (proponent) might try to switch arguments when refuted, but this transition is also invalid if based on flawed premises.
Observe the method: you can refute an opponent by showing their own principles contradict their claim.

Commentary
This passage from Noor al-Anwar discusses invalid preferences (tarjeehat fasida) in qiyas according to the Hanafi school. The author begins by noting that al-Shafi'i followed the literal meaning in a previous example (about craftsmanship and usurped property), while Hanafis followed precision (al-diqqa). Then the main topic: three types of preference that are invalid in the Hanafi madhhab: (1) preference by preponderance of similarities (ghalabat al-ashbah), (2) preference by generality (al-umoom), and (3) preference by fewness of attributes (qillat al-awsaaf). Each is explained with examples from Shafi'i usul and refuted with Hanafi arguments.

First, 'preference by preponderance of similarities': The Shafi'is argue that if a branch (far') resembles one root in only one aspect (e.g., mahramiyya) and another root in many aspects (e.g., giving zakah, marriage of ex-wives, acceptance of testimony), then the stronger similarity should determine the analogy. Hanafis reject this because the criterion is not quantity of similarities but the effective meaning (al-ma'na al-mu'aththir). The many similarities cited by Shafi'is are not all equal; some might be purely formal. The author gives an example: a brother resembles a cousin in many legal aspects, so Shafi'is would analogize the brother to the cousin to deny emancipation upon ownership. Hanafis, however, maintain that emancipation is caused by mahram kinship—the brother is mahram, so he is emancipated. The cousin is not mahram, so no emancipation. Thus the Shafi'i preference is invalid.

Second, 'preference by generality': Shafi'is may prefer a general attribute (e.g., taste/ta'm in riba) over a specific one (e.g., measure/kayl) because it covers more cases. Hanafis argue that the Shafi'i school itself allows causation by a deficient cause (al-illa al-qasira) that doesn't extend to all branches, so generality loses its edge. Moreover, just as a specific text is stronger than a general one according to Shafi'is, a specific attribute should be stronger than a general one. Thus their own principle should lead them to prefer the specific attribute.

Third, 'preference by fewness of attributes': Shafi'is might prefer a simple cause (e.g., taste alone) over a compound cause (e.g., measure and genus combined) because it is fewer parts. Hanafis counter that effectiveness (ta'thir) is what matters, not simplicity. A compound cause can be stronger in effect than a simple one. The example contrasts the Hanafi cause for riba (measure and genus) with the Shafi'i cause (taste alone). The Hanafi cause is more comprehensive and effective.

Finally, the passage transitions to a discussion of refuting causes and the mu'allil's need to switch arguments when his causes are rebutted. The author notes that when a mu'allil (proponent of a cause) is forced to abandon his cause, he may try to shift to another argument (intiqal), but this is also invalid if based on the same flawed premises.

The text includes marginal glosses (al-Qamar, al-Sanbali, al-Muhashshi) that clarify terms and add further Hanafi perspective.

الْعِلَّةُ الْقَاصِرَةُ (al-illa al-qasira): A cause that exists in the original but not in the branch, thus not extending to branches.

Invalid Preferences in Qiyas: Generality and Fewness of Components
Refutation of Shafi'i arguments on 'illah preference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify invalid criteria for preferring one 'illah over another and apply the Hanafi principle that effectiveness (ta'thir) is the sole basis for tarjih.

Core Idea
Preference of an 'illah is based on effectiveness, not generality or simplicity.

In qiyas, when multiple 'illahs seem plausible, scholars often seek to prefer one over another. The Shafi'is argue that a general 'illah (like taste in riba) is preferable because it covers more cases, and a simpler 'illah (like taste alone) is easier to establish. The Hanafis, however, reject these criteria, insisting that effectiveness (ta'thir) is what matters. In this lesson, we will dissect the Shafi'i arguments and the Hanafi refutations as presented in Noor al-Anwar, and understand the principle that a specific text (nass) is stronger than a general one, and that effectiveness overrides simplicity.

2
Analyze the Hanafi response to generality
The Hanafi author refutes by noting that the Shafi'is themselves permit reasoning with a deficient 'illah (qasirah), so generality does not confer strength. Moreover, a specific text (nass) is always stronger than a general one, so the specific 'illah (kayl) should be preferred.
This mirrors the usul principle that a specific text takes precedence over a general one.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to fewness of components
The Hanafi author argues that preference is for effectiveness (ta'thir), not number. A cause with two parts can be stronger in effect than a cause with one part. So simplicity alone is not a valid criterion.
4

Consider two possible 'illahs for the prohibition of wine: intoxication (general) and its specific effect on the mind (specific). The Hanafi would prefer the specific 'illah if it is more effective in explaining the ruling, not just because it is general.
This illustrates the emphasis on ta'thir.

Transition of the 'Illah (Intiqal) in Qiyas Debates
Classification of transition types after 'illah is refuted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the four types of transition (intiqal) when a cause is rejected and apply them to examples from the Hanafi usul literature.

Core Idea
When an 'illah is refuted, the defender may transition to a new 'illah or a new ruling, but only within four permissible categories.

In the give-and-take of qiyas reasoning, a defender may find his original 'illah challenged. Rather than admitting defeat, he can transition to a new argument. This lesson explores the four types of such transition (intiqal) as outlined in Noor al-Anwar, with detailed examples. Understanding these moves is crucial for anyone engaging in serious usul debate.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context of intiqal
The discussion of intiqal begins after establishing that an 'illah can be refuted. The defender (mu'allil) can shift to a new line of reasoning to salvage his position. This is a procedural move, not a new source of law.
2
Identify the four types of transition
The text mentions four types, but lists two explicitly. The first is transition from one 'illah to another to establish the first 'illah. The second is transition from one ruling to another using the same 'illah. The other two are implied: transition from 'illah to ruling, and from ruling to 'illah.
Full classification: (1) 'illah to 'illah, (2) 'illah to ruling, (3) ruling to 'illah, (4) ruling to ruling. Only two are illustrated.
3
Work through the first example: minor depositor
The minor (sabi) entrusted with property is not liable if he consumes it, because he is 'authorized to consume' by the depositor. When the opponent denies this authorization, the defender transitions to a new 'illah: the depositor's knowledge of the minor's nature implies consent. This new 'illah establishes the original claim of authorization.
4
Work through the second example: mukatab's expiation
A mukatab (slave under kitaba) can emancipate himself in expiation because the kitaba contract is subject to rescission. The opponent concedes the 'illah but objects that a deficiency in slavery prevents expiation. The defender then transitions from ruling (permissibility) to a new ruling (no deficiency) while keeping the same 'illah (contract is subject to rescission).

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on qiyas in Noor al-Anwar addresses two issues: first, invalid methods of preferring one 'illah over another, and second, the concept of 'transition' (intiqal) when an 'illah is refuted. The author, Allamah Nur al-Din al-Sabuni, explains that according to the Hanafi school, preferring one analogy over another with a third analogy is invalid. The Shafi'is argue that the description of taste in riba is preferable because it is general (includes small and large quantities), while the Hanafi preference for measure (kayl) is specific to large quantities. The author refutes this by noting that the Shafi'is themselves allow reasoning with deficient 'illahs, so generality holds no weight; moreover, a specific text (nass) is stronger than a general one, so the specific description (measure) should be preferred. Similarly, the Shafi'is claim that a cause with few components (like taste alone) is better than one with many (quantity and genus). The author rejects this because preference depends on effectiveness (ta'thir), not number. A cause with two parts may be more effective. The second part discusses the 'transition' of the defender when his 'illah is invalidated. There are four types, but here two are illustrated: (1) moving from one 'illah to another to prove the original 'illah, as in the case of a minor depositor; if the opponent denies that the minor has authority to consume, the defender invokes a new 'illah (the depositor's knowledge of the minor's nature proves implied consent). (2) moving from one ruling to another with the same 'illah, as in the case of the mukatab's expiation; if the opponent grants the 'illah but claims a different preventer (the slave's right to freedom), the defender shifts from claiming permissibility of expiation to claiming that the contract does not create a deficiency in slavery, still using the 'illah that the contract is subject to rescission. This shows the procedural dynamics of qiyas debate.

The Four Types of Intiqal (Transition) in Qiyas
Explaining the four types of invalid transitions in qiyas during debate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the four types of intiqal, distinguish valid from invalid transitions, and apply the rules of debate in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Only three of the four transitions in qiyas are valid; the fourth leads to infinite regress.

In this passage from Noor al-Anwar, the author dissects the rules of debate (munazara) concerning transitions from one proof to another. Understanding which transitions are permissible is crucial for constructing sound qiyas arguments. This lesson will guide you through the four types, their examples, and why one is rejected.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context of intiqal
Intiqal occurs during a debate when the mu'allil (arguer) shifts from one proof to another to counter an opponent's objection.
2
Identify the four types
The text lists: (1) from illa to illa to prove the first illa, (2) from hukm to hukm with the same illa, (3) to a new hukm and new illa, (4) from illa to illa to prove the original hukm.
Types 1-3 are valid; type 4 is invalid.
3
Analyze the first three types with examples
Example for type 1: a minor consuming a deposit. For types 2 and 3: the mukatab and expiation case. Work through how each transition functions.
Focus on the logical movement.
4
Explain why the fourth type is invalid
Type 4 leads to infinite regress (tasalsul) because each new cause would itself need a cause to prove the same ruling, never settling.
No precedent exists in legal cases.
5
Note the exception: Ibrahim's argument
The text addresses an objection from the story of Ibrahim (AS) and clarifies that his transition was not of the fourth type but rather a valid type (likely type 3).
Understand the distinction.

It is of four divisions: either one transitions from one cause (illa) to another cause to prove the first cause, as when the mu'allil (arguer) argues in the case of a minor who is given a deposit (wadi'ah) that if he consumes the deposit he is not liable because he is authorized to consume it from the depositor. Or one transitions from a ruling to another ruling with the first cause, as when he argues for the permissibility of emancipating a mukatab (contractually freed slave) who has not paid anything of the kitabah (manumission contract) amount for expiation, that the kitabah is a contract of exchange that is subject to annulment by agreement or by the mukatab's inability to pay, so it does not prevent spending for expiation. If the opponent says: I also hold its implication; in my view the kitabah contract does not prevent spending for expiation; rather the preventer is the deficiency in slavery due to this contract, because emancipation is due to the slave due to kitabah. Then the mu'allil transitions from that ruling to another ruling with the mentioned cause, saying: This contract does not cause deficiency in slavery; if it did, it would not be subject to annulment because deficiency is established by partial freedom, and partial freedom does not allow annulment. So the mu'allil proved with the first cause the other ruling, which is that it does not cause a deficiency preventer from slavery. Or one transitions to another ruling and another cause, as in the same issue if the opponent says: In my view this contract does not prevent expiation; rather the preventer is deficiency in slavery. The mu'allil says: This is a transaction contract among servants like other contracts, so it must not cause deficiency in slavery. So this is a transition to another ruling and another cause. Or one transitions from a cause to another cause to establish the first ruling, not to establish the first cause. Commentary
This passage from the section on Qiyas deals with the rules of argumentation (munazara) concerning 'illa (cause). The author explains the four types of 'invalid preponderances' (al-tarjihat al-fasida) which are actually types of transitions (intiqal) from one proof to another during a debate. The first type is transitioning from one cause to another cause to prove the first cause itself. An example is given: if a depositor gives a deposit to a minor, and the minor consumes it, the mu'allil (prover) argues that the minor is not liable because he is authorized to consume. If the opponent disputes the authorization, the mu'allil can provide another cause (e.g., the minor's lack of accountability and the depositor's knowledge thereof) to prove the original claim of authorization. The second type is transitioning from one ruling to another ruling while keeping the same cause. Example: permitting emancipation of a mukatab for expiation, where the cause is that kitabah is a contract subject to annulment. If the opponent claims the preventer is the slave's deficiency in freedom, the mu'allil transitions to argue that this contract does not cause deficiency, using the same cause (the contract's nature). The third type is transitioning to both a new ruling and a new cause. The same example is used: if the opponent says the kitabah contract does not prevent expiation but the deficiency does, the mu'allil can argue that this is a transaction contract like others, so it should not cause deficiency — thus a new cause (general nature of transactions) and new ruling (no deficiency). The fourth type is transitioning from a cause to another cause to prove the original ruling, but not to prove the original cause. This is deemed invalid because it leads to infinite regress (tasalsul), as each new cause would require another to prove the first ruling. The text notes that this fourth type has no precedent in legal cases. The commentary adds clarifications from sources like Qamar al-Aqmar and Sanabali, including a distinction between 'mu'amalah' (transaction) and 'mu'awadah' (exchange) and a note that the three valid types are accepted by consensus because they serve to conclude debate.

Key Terms
الانتقال (al-intiqal): Transition from one proof (cause or ruling) to another during a debate to support one's position.
العلة (al-illa): The effective cause or ratio legis that establishes a legal ruling in qiyas.
المعلل (al-mu'allil): The person who provides the 'illa (cause) in a debate to prove a ruling.
الترجيحات الفاسدة (al-tarjihat al-fasida): Invalid preponderances or transitions that are not acceptable in qiyas argumentation.

The Four Types of Transition in Qiyas: Valid and Invalid
Explaining invalid transitions in qiyas arguments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of transitions (intiqaal) in qiyas and explain why the fourth type leads to infinite regress, using the Hanafi framework.

Core Idea
The fourth transition—shifting causes endlessly for the same ruling—is invalid because it prevents finality in debate.

In Hanafi jurisprudential debate (munazarah), when a mu'allil cites a cause for a ruling, the opponent may challenge him. The mu'allil may need to shift his argument. This passage categorizes four possible transitions and evaluates their validity. The key ruling: only shifts that maintain commitment to the original cause structure are valid; the fourth type—endless shifting of causes for the same ruling—is invalid because it leads to infinite regress. The commentary also clarifies why Prophet Ibrahim's (AS) shift in argument was not this invalid type.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four types of transition
From the passage, list the four ways a mu'allil can shift his argument when challenged: (1) to another ruling with the same cause, (2) to another ruling with another cause, (3) to another cause to establish the first ruling, (4) to another cause to establish the same ruling repeatedly.
The first three are considered valid; the fourth is the focus of criticism.
2
Understand why the fourth is invalid
The passage states that causes are infinite in reality. Allowing the fourth type would permit endless shifting without resolution, making it impossible to cut off debate. The rule is that transition is allowed only to cut off discussion, not to perpetuate it.
Infinite regress (tasalsul) is a key argument against the fourth type.
3
Analyze the objection from Ibrahim (AS)
A counterargument: Prophet Ibrahim (AS) seemed to shift from one proof to another when debating Nimrod (life-death argument to sun argument). Was this the invalid fourth type? The commentary explains that Ibrahim's first statement was not a proof but a claim; the real proof was the second. So the shift was from a misunderstood claim to a clear proof, not an endless cause shift.
This shows that not all shifts are transitions of the fourth kind; context matters.
4
Apply the principle to a hypothetical
Given a scenario where a mu'allil keeps proposing new causes for the same ruling when challenged, classify the transition and state its validity. For example, if asked why a contract is valid, and he says 'because of consent', then when challenged says 'because of consideration', then 'because of form'—this would be the invalid fourth type.
The mu'allil must commit to one consistent cause.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Bab al-Qiyas explains the types of transitions (intiqaal) that occur in qiyas arguments. The author discusses four possible transitions when a mu'allil (the one presenting a cause) is challenged: (1) transitioning to another ruling with the same cause, (2) transitioning to another ruling with another cause, (3) transitioning to another cause to establish the first ruling, and (4) transitioning to another cause to establish the same ruling repeatedly. The first three are valid because they allow the mu'allil to complete his proof without leaving his initial commitment. The fourth is invalid because it leads to an infinite regress of causes. A counterargument is raised using Prophet Ibrahim's debate with Nimrod: Ibrahim first argued that God gives life and death, then when Nimrod responded with a flawed demonstration, Ibrahim transitioned to the argument about the sun. The commentary clarifies that this is not the invalid fourth type because Ibrahim's first argument was actually a claim, not a proof, and the real proof was the second argument. Thus the transition was to replace a misunderstood claim with a clear proof, not to endlessly shift causes. The passage concludes by noting the transition to discussing rulings established by the four proofs.

Key Terms
المعلّل (al-mu'allil): The person who cites a cause ('illah) to prove a legal ruling in a qiyas argument.
الانتقال (al-intiqaal): Moving from one cause or ruling to another during a qiyas debate.
الترجيحات الفاسدة (al-tarjihaat al-faasidah): Invalid preponderances or transitions that do not lead to a sound conclusion in qiyas.
التسلسل (al-tasalsul): Infinite regress; an endless chain of causes that never reaches a final point.

Corrupt Preference in Qiyas: The Example of Ibrahim and Nimrod
Illustrating a corrupt preference in argumentation with the story of Ibrahim and Nimrod

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a corrupt preference (tarjih fasid) in qiyas from a valid shift in argumentation, using the Hanafi usul framework.

Core Idea
A valid shift in argument to remove doubt is not a corrupt preference; the original proof remains sound.

In this lesson, we examine a classic example from the story of Prophet Ibrahim and Nimrod to understand what constitutes a corrupt preference in qiyas. The Hanafi usul scholars highlight that Ibrahim's move to a second argument was not a corruption but a strategic clarification to overcome the audience's inability to grasp subtle meanings. This distinction is crucial for evaluating shifts in proofs during debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two arguments
First, note Ibrahim's initial proof: God gives life and causes death. Then Nimrod's counter: I give life and death (by releasing and killing). Finally, Ibrahim's second proof: God brings the sun from the east; can you bring it from the west?
The shift is from a subtle proof to a more apparent one.
2
Analyze Nimrod's objection
Recognize that Nimrod misunderstood the first proof. He took literal 'life and death' as mere release and killing, missing the deeper meaning of creation and destruction of life itself.
The first proof was valid but misunderstood by the audience.
3
Apply the Hanafi criterion for corrupt preference
A corrupt preference occurs when one abandons a valid proof for a weaker or invalid one. Here, the first proof was strong; the shift was not due to weakness but to accommodate the audience's intellectual limitations.
The shift is permissible (ja'iz) and does not invalidate the first proof.
4
Conclude the status of the shift
Thus, Ibrahim's move is a valid shift (tahawwul sahih), not a corrupt preference. The Hanafi principle: if the first proof is sound but the opponent is confused, shifting to a clearer proof is allowed to end the debate effectively.
The author clarifies this is 'not of the kind' (ليس هذا القبيل) of corrupt preference.

Definition and Sevenfold Division of Legal Cause ('Illa)
Defining 'illa and its sevenfold classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'illa and identify which of the seven categories a given cause belongs to based on the three attributes.

Core Idea
The 'illa is what directly attributes the ruling; its completeness depends on having name, meaning, and effect simultaneously.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, understanding the legal cause ('illa) is central to analogical reasoning (qiyas). This lesson introduces the precise definition of 'illa and its sevenfold classification according to three attributes: being a cause in name, meaning, and effect. You will learn to distinguish 'illa from related concepts like sabab and to recognize complete vs. deficient causes.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of 'illa
Find the phrase 'والثاني: العلة، وهو ما يضاف إليه وجوب الحكم ابتداءً أي بلا واسطة'. This gives the core: 'illa is that to which the obligation of the ruling is attributed directly without intermediary.
Note the exclusion of sabab, sign, and cause of cause.
2
Understand the three attributes
Identify the three attributes: ism (name: the cause is designated by the Lawgiver), ma'na (meaning: reason perceives its effect), and hukm (result: the ruling follows immediately).
A complete 'illa has all three; otherwise deficient.
3
Enumerate the seven logical combinations
List the seven possibilities: (1) all three, (2) only ism, (3) only ma'na, (4) only hukm, (5) ism+ma'na, (6) ism+hukm, (7) ma'na+hukm. Note that the text omits (3) and (4) and replaces them with a cause resembling an 'illa but in the domain of sabab.
This classification is systematic; each category has an example in fiqh.
4
Apply to an example
Consider sale (al-bay'): it is a prescribed cause for ownership. It is a cause in name (the Lawgiver called it a cause), meaning (reason perceives the exchange leads to ownership), and effect (ownership transfers immediately upon contract). So it is a complete 'illa (category 1).
Contrast with the time for prayer: it is a sabab, not a complete 'illa.
5
Recognize the significance
Understanding these categories helps evaluate qiyas: the soundness of a deduced 'illa depends on these attributes. A cause that is deficient may weaken the analogy.
This memorization anchor is foundational for advanced usul discussions.

Commentary
This passage from Noor al-Anwar deals with the definition and classification of the legal cause ('illa) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It begins by discussing the fourth division of sabab (cause) and clarifies that the contingent obligation (ijab mudaf) is actually a type of 'illa in reality, but by attribution it is a sabab. The text then defines the second category of things rulings are attached to: 'illa, which is that to which the obligation of a ruling is attributed directly without an intermediary. This definition excludes the sabab (which is more remote), the sign ('alamah), and the cause of the cause ('illat al-'illa). The 'illa includes both prescribed causes (like sale for ownership, marriage for usufruct) and deduced causes (like capacity and genus for riba). The commentary by al-Qamar expounds on the difference between real cause, cause in the sense of 'illa, and metaphorical cause, citing al-Tawdih and al-Sanbali. The passage then presents the sevenfold division of 'illa based on the presence or absence of three attributes: being a cause in name (ism), in meaning (ma'na, i.e., rational effect), and in result (hukm, i.e., immediate legal effect). A complete 'illa has all three; deficient ones lack one or two. The author lists seven logical possibilities: (1) all three present (e.g., absolute sale for ownership), (2) only name, (3) only meaning, (4) only ruling, (5) name and meaning, (6) name and ruling, (7) meaning and ruling. However, the author omitted cases (3) and (4) and replaced them with a cause that looks like an 'illa but is actually in the domain of sabab, which he describes as having resemblance to 'illa. The student should understand that the classification is based on objective attributes of the cause, and that not all 'illas are equally complete.

Key Terms
العلة (al-'illa): That to which the obligation of the ruling is attributed directly, without intermediary, whether prescribed or deduced.
السبب (al-sabab): A cause whose effect is not perceived by reason and may not be by the action of the obligated person, e.g., time for prayer, or a cause like purchase for usufruct where the purpose differs.
الإيجاب المضاف (al-ijab al-mudaf): A contingent obligation, e.g., divorce or manumission contingent on a future event; it is a type of 'illa in reality but considered a sabab by attribution.

The Seven Divisions of Legal Cause (Illa)
Definition and classification of legal causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three attributes of a legal cause (ism, ma'na, hukm) and classify any given 'illa into one of the seven categories based on the presence or absence of these attributes.

Core Idea
A legal cause can be complete (all three attributes) or incomplete; classification depends on presence of name, effect, and immediacy.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, not every 'illa is equal. Some causes are merely nominal, others are truly effective, and some produce immediate rulings while others are delayed. This passage from Noor al-Anwaar unpacks a seven-fold classification based on three attributes: formal designation (ism), effective influence (ma'na), and immediate legal consequence (hukm). By mastering this classification, you will better understand how qiyas operates and why certain causes are considered valid for analogical reasoning.

The passage first defines the three attributes: ism (the cause is designated by the Lawgiver as a reason), ma'na (the cause effectively influences the ruling), and hukm (the ruling follows immediately upon the cause). List them with their Arabic terms.
These are the building blocks of the classification.
2

From the three attributes, derive the seven possible combinations (e.g., all three present, only ism, only ma'na, etc.). The passage enumerates them explicitly. Write them out in order.

3
Analyze the first example: absolute sale
The passage gives the complete 'illa: absolute sale (without option). Confirm that it possesses all three attributes: it is named as a cause for ownership, it effectively transfers ownership, and ownership transfers immediately upon contract.
This is the standard for a perfect 'illa used in qiyas.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the classification of legal causes ('illa) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, specifically the seven-fold division based on three attributes: formal designation (ism), effective influence (ma'na), and immediate legal consequence (hukm). The author begins by defining a complete cause as one possessing all three attributes. Then he enumerates all possible combinations, resulting in seven categories. However, he notes that the original author (al-Muharrir? refers to the matn author) omitted two categories (meaning only, and legal effect only) and instead substituted two other types that are more practically relevant. The commentator then explains each of the three types that the author does mention:
1. Complete cause: e.g., an absolute sale (without option) for ownership. The sale is named as a cause, it effectively produces ownership, and ownership follows immediately upon the contract.
2. Cause in name only: e.g., a conditional divorce pronouncement. The phrase 'You are divorced' is a cause in name because it is legally recognized as a cause for divorce, but its effect is delayed until the condition occurs, and it has no intrinsic effect before that.
3. Cause in name and meaning but not immediate: e.g., a sale with an option clause. The sale is a cause in name and meaning because it is the effective instrument for transferring ownership, but the legal effect (full ownership) is delayed until the option period ends, so it does not have immediate legal consequence.

The commentary uses marginal notes (by al-Qamar?) to clarify technical points such as the definition of 'effectiveness' and provides examples like marriage (nikah) causing marriageability, murder causing qisas, and the case of oaths. The passage is essential for understanding the structure of qiyas (analogical reasoning) in Hanafi fiqh, as the validity of qiyas depends on identifying a proper 'illa.

علة ('illa): A legal cause that is the basis for a ruling; classified by presence or absence of three attributes: ism (name), ma'na (meaning/effect), hukm (legal consequence).

Explanation of the types of 'illah (cause) and their classifications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three dimensions of 'illah (nominal, meaningful, legal) and classify a given cause according to the four Hanafi types.

Core Idea
A cause can be nominal without being effective, or effective without being immediate — classification depends on attribution, influence, and timing.

In Hanafi usul, not every cause operates in the same way. Some causes immediately produce their ruling, others are delayed, and some are merely nominal designations. This lesson breaks down the three dimensions of 'illah — ism, ma'na, and hukm — and then classifies causes into four distinct types. By understanding these distinctions, you will be able to analyze how rulings are attached to their causes in Hanafi legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the three dimensions of 'illah
Identify the nominal (ism), meaningful (ma'na), and legal (hukm) aspects. Nominal means the term is designated for the ruling; meaningful means it influences the ruling; legal means the ruling is immediate upon its occurrence.
These dimensions are not mutually exclusive; a cause may have all three, two, or only one.
2
Understand Type 1: All three dimensions
Example: an unconditional sale. It is a cause for ownership nominally (the word 'sale' is designated), meaningfully (the contract effects ownership), and legally (ownership transfers immediately without delay).
This is the standard or complete cause.
3
Understand Type 2: Nominal only
Example: a conditional divorce (e.g., 'You are divorced if you enter the house'). The pronouncement is a cause nominally but has no legal effect and no meaningful influence until the condition occurs.
The ruling is suspended on the condition, so the cause is ineffective before it.
4
Understand Type 3: Nominal and meaningful, not legal
Example: a sale with option (khiyar). The contract designates ownership (nominal) and it influences ownership (meaningful), but ownership is not immediate; it is delayed until the option expires.
The condition of option delays the legal effect, not the cause itself.
5
Understand Type 4: In the domain of causes (asbab)
Examples include a suspended sale, deferred divorce, nisab before a year, and a lease contract. These resemble causes but share features with occasions (asbab) because the ruling is not immediately complete or is contingent on progressive realization.
Type 4 is a hybrid category for causes that are part of a process.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary that expands upon a foundational text (matn) regarding the classification of 'illah (cause) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author first establishes three dimensions of 'illah: nominal (ism), meaningful (ma'na), and legal (hukm). A cause is nominal when it is lexically or legally designated for a ruling and the ruling is directly attributed to it (e.g., the word 'sale' is nominally a cause for ownership). It is meaningful when it actually contributes to the existence of the ruling (e.g., the sale itself is the effective factor that produces ownership). It is legal when the ruling is immediately established upon the cause's occurrence without delay (e.g., an unconditional sale gives ownership instantly). The commentary then provides three categories of 'illah: (1) nominal, meaningful, and legal (like an immediate, unconditional sale); (2) nominal only (like a conditional divorce pronouncement, which affects no ruling until the condition is met); (3) nominal and meaningful but not legal (like a sale with option, where ownership is transferred but contingent on the option's lapse). Additionally, a fourth type is introduced: 'illah in the domain of causes (asbab), where the cause resembles a true cause but shares characteristics with occasions (asbab). Examples include a suspended sale (sale of another's property without permission), a deferred pronouncement (divorce fixed for a future time), the nisab of zakat before a year passes, and a lease contract (where ownership of usufruct is gradual). Throughout, the commentary clarifies the nuances of each type, emphasizing that the classification depends on the nature of the cause's relationship to the ruling in terms of time, effectiveness, and directness.

Key Terms
علة (‘illah): A cause or occasioning factor that is designated to bring about a ruling; it may be nominal, meaningful, or legal.
إيجاب معلق بالشرط (ījāb mu‘allaq bi-sh-sharṭ): A pronouncement (like divorce) that is suspended upon a future condition; it is a cause nominally but not meaningfully or legally before the condition.
بيع بشرط الخيار (bay‘ bi-sharṭ al-khiyār): A sale with an option to rescind; it is a cause for ownership nominally and meaningfully but not legally because ownership is contingent on option expiry.

Types of Illah: Cause in Name and Meaning but Not in Ruling
Classification of causes: cause in name and meaning not in ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify a cause as 'isman wa ma'nan la hukman' and explain why the ruling is delayed or gradual.

Core Idea
A cause may be valid in name and meaning yet the ruling is delayed or gradual.

In Hanafi usul, not every cause immediately yields its ruling. Some causes are designated for the ruling (name) and are effective (meaning), but the ruling itself is deferred due to conditions, options, or graduality. This lesson explores five such examples from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause and its intended ruling
Pinpoint the specific attribute or contract being considered (e.g., sale with option, nisab of zakat) and the ruling it is supposed to produce (ownership, obligation of zakat).
2
Test the cause in name (ism)
Is the cause specifically designated by the Lawgiver for this ruling? If yes, it is a cause in name.
Designation often comes from textual evidence or consensus.
3
Test the cause in meaning (ma'na)
Does the cause have a real effect in bringing about the ruling? If the ruling depends on it, it is a cause in meaning.
Effectiveness can be rational or customary.
4
Determine why it is not a cause in ruling (hukm)
Is the ruling attached immediately? If not, identify the barrier: a condition (e.g., option), a time delay, or gradual occurrence.
Key barriers: khiyar (option), ijazah (permission), time specification, gradual existence.
5
Confirm the classification
Conclude that the cause is 'isman wa ma'nan la hukman' and articulate the reason for the delay.

Cause of a Cause (Illat al-Illah) and its Legal Effect
Subdivision of causes: cause acting through an intermediary (illat al-illah).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the concept of 'illat al-illah' and apply it to determine legal liability, particularly in cases of retracting witnesses.

Core Idea
The cause of a cause is treated as a cause itself in Hanafi law, affecting liability.

Sometimes a cause operates through an intermediary. Hanafi usul recognizes that a cause that leads to another cause is legally effective. This lesson examines three examples: purchase of a relative, illness of death, and tazkiyah (witness credibility), and the resulting dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding witness liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary cause and the intermediary cause
In each example, pinpoint the first cause (e.g., purchase of relative) and the intermediate cause (ownership) that directly produces the final ruling (emancipation).
2
Establish the chain of causation
Show how the primary cause leads to the intermediary, which then leads to the ruling. The intermediary is the immediate cause, the primary is the cause of the cause.
3
Apply the principle 'causa causae est causa causati'
In Hanafi usul, the remote cause is treated as a cause for legal purposes. Verify this by checking the text's statement: the cause of the cause is considered a cause.
This principle is illustrated by the analogy of archery.
4
Examine the ikhtilaf on witness liability
Contrast Abu Hanifa's view (liable) with the Sahibayn's view (not liable) for retracting witnesses after stoning. Identify the key difference: Abu Hanifa treats tazkiyah as a cause of a cause; the Sahibayn do not.
The dispute revolves around whether the remote cause carries direct legal effect.
5

The Fifth and Sixth Divisions of Cause (Illah)
Defining the fifth and sixth types of legal cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the fifth and sixth types of legal cause (illah) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh and identify which part of a compound cause is effective in specific examples.

Core Idea
In a compound cause, only the combination is the named cause; the last-occurring part is effective, not each part alone.

This passage sharpens our understanding of compound legal causes. The author divides causes into six types; here we examine the fifth and sixth. The fifth describes a component that has the semblance of a cause but is not independently effective. The sixth describes the last-occurring component that is effective in producing the ruling but is not the named subject of the ruling. Mastering these distinctions prevents misattribution of rulings in complex cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fifth division
Read the description: a descriptor that has shubhat al-‘ilal (semblance of causes). Note that each part of a compound cause (e.g., qadr and jins for riba) alone is not a pure cause but has semblance.
Some say it is a cause in meaning only.
2
Identify the sixth division
Read the description: illah ma‘nan wa hukman la isman. The last part of a sequential compound cause is effective; the subject is the whole combination.
Example: manumission cause = ownership + kinship.
3
Understand the negation of sabab mahd
The author rejects that each part is a pure cause (sabab mahd) without effect. Instead, each part has shubhat al-illah. This is the relied-upon position.
Contrast with Imam Sarakhsi's view that each part is a pure cause.
4
Apply the sixth division to manumission
If a person buys a mahram relative, ownership is the last part → effective. If a person buys a slave and later claims kinship, kinship is the last part → effective.
The ruling (manumission) follows the last part.
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Sarakhsi vs. Fakhr al-Islam: is each part a sabab mahd or shibh al-illah? The text sides with the latter.
The thamarah: in practice, which part determines the ruling.

Substitution of Cause and Evidence for Effect in Hanafi Usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle of substituting a cause or evidence for its effect and apply the condition of predominance (ghalabah) to evaluate specific cases.

Core Idea
A cause replaces its effect in rulings only when it leads to that effect in the majority of cases.

In Hanafi legal theory, the Law often treats a consistent indicator as the actual cause for practical necessity. This lesson unpacks when a motivating cause (e.g., kissing) or evidence (e.g., travel) can stand in for the intended effect in legal rulings, and the critical condition that the connection must be predominant (ghalib). Commentary
This concluding section discusses the principle of substitution: a cause (sabab) or evidence (dalil) can take the place of the effect or indicated thing in legal rulings, provided it leads to the effect in the majority of cases. The example given is hardship (mashaqqa) being substituted by travel; travel is a consistent indicator of hardship. The great scholar Abdul Salam al-A'zami emphasizes that this substitution is only valid when the connection is predominant (ghalib). He criticizes some students in India who erroneously deemed listening to music permissible if it leads to a permitted act, arguing that such reasoning is flawed because the connection is not predominant. The text also distinguishes between a 'motivating cause' (da'i) like kissing leading to intercourse, and 'evidence' (dalil) like travel indicating hardship. This principle is a development of the earlier discussion on cause and effect, asserting that in legal theory, the Law may treat the indicator as the cause itself due to practical necessity.

الدليل (al-dalil): Evidence that indicates something else, e.g., travel indicating hardship.
قيام مقام (qiyam maqam): The substitution of one thing in place of another in legal rulings.
غالب المواد (ghalib al-mawad): In the majority of cases or materials.

Qiyam al-Dalil wa al-Sabab: Substituting Proof and Cause
Expounding the usul principle of substitution of cause/proof for the proved, with examples and commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sabab (cause) and dalil (proof) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh and apply the principle of qiyam to examples from marriage, travel, and divorce.

Core Idea
A cause necessarily affects its effect; a proof merely indicates, but Shariah may treat either as the legal basis for ease.

This passage from Noorul Anwar tackles a foundational usul principle: the Shariah sometimes treats a proof or cause as if it were the thing it proves or causes. This is done for practicality and precaution, as seen in rulings on marriage, travel concessions, and divorce. Understanding qiyam al-dalil wa al-sabab is essential for grasping how Hanafi jurists derive legal rulings from indicants rather than inaccessible realities.

Guided Path
1

Read the opening phrase 'قيام سبب الدليل مقام المدلول'. Understand that the passage explores how a cause (sabab) or proof (dalil) can stand in for the actual proved or effect (madlul) in legal rulings.

Locate the explicit definition: 'الفرق بين السبب والدليل أن السبب لا يخلو عن تأثير له في المسبب، والدليل قد يخلو عن ذلك'. A cause necessarily has an effect, while a proof may lack it.
This distinction is crucial for understanding which examples are causes and which are proofs.
3
Analyze the Examples Provided
Work through each example: marriage (cause for cohabitation), travel (cause for hardship), purity (proof for need of divorce), declaration of love (proof for love). Note how each fits the definition.
The travel example is debated; commentators note it is actually a cause, not a proof.
4
Understand the Rationales
Identify the justifications: precaution (ihtiyat) in the calls to intercourse, and removing hardship (raf' al-haraj) in travel and purity. The text explains that internal states are inaccessible, so external indicants are used.
The difference between necessity (darurah) and removing hardship is also clarified.
5
Examine the Scholarly Commentary
Note the layers: base text from Noorul Anwar, then comments from Al-Qamar, Al-Muhashi, Al-Sanbali. They refine the classification, e.g., criticizing purity as a direct proof and clarifying that travel is a cause.
This shows the dynamic refinement of usul principles within the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage from Noorul Anwar deals with the usul principle of establishing a proof (dalil) or cause (sabab) in place of the proved (madlul) or effect. The author opens by stating the principle and then provides multiple examples. 

First, marriage is established in place of actual cohabitation for establishing lineage. This is because the true cause of lineage (the husband's water) is hidden, and knowledge of intercourse is difficult to ascertain. Thus, marriage—which is a cause that calls to intercourse—is taken as sufficient proof. Similarly, the 'calls' (dawa'i) to intercourse, such as looking, kissing, and touching, are established in place of actual intercourse in several rulings: istibra' (waiting period to ensure no pregnancy), prohibition of marriage by affinity, ihram, zihar, and i'tikaf. This is out of precaution (ihtiyat) to prevent falling into the forbidden.

Second, travel (safar) is established as a proof of hardship (mashaqqa) for the concessions of shortening prayer and breaking fast, even if the traveler does not actually experience hardship. This is because hardship is an internal state that cannot be reliably measured; travel is its usual cause. The author distinguishes between cause (sabab) and proof (dalil): a cause necessarily has an effect on the result, while a proof merely indicates without causing. Travel is actually a cause, so it is an example of establishing the cause in place of the effect, not the proof.

Third, purity (tuhr) free from intercourse is established as a proof of the need for divorce. Since divorce is legislated out of necessity when marital rights cannot be fulfilled, and the need for divorce is internal, its external sign—a time when desire for intercourse renews—is used. However, some critics note that purity itself is not a direct proof; rather, the act of initiating divorce in purity is the real proof of need because it shows the husband is willing to forgo intercourse.

Finally, an example of establishing the proof (dalil) in place of the proved (madlul) is the declaration of love. When a man says to his wife, 'If you love me, you are divorced,' and she responds 'I love you,' divorce occurs because love is internal and only knowable through her statement. But this is restricted to the same sitting (majlis), because it resembles option (takhyir), which is limited to the sitting.

The passage also clarifies the difference between necessity (darurah) and removing hardship (raf' al-haraj): in necessity the truth is impossible to ascertain, while in removing hardship it is possible with difficulty.

Throughout, the principle is applied to various fiqh rulings, all within the Hanafi madhhab, demonstrating how the Lawgiver uses external indicants and conveniences to facilitate practice.

Key Terms
سبب (sabab): A cause that has a real effect on its effect (musabbab); it must precede the effect and is not devoid of influence.
دليل (dalil): A proof that indicates the existence of the proved (madlul) without necessarily having an effect on it; its function is purely epistemic.
داعي (da'i): A call or incentive that leads to an act; in this context, precursors to intercourse (e.g., looking, kissing) that are treated like the act itself out of precaution.
مشقة (mashaqqa): Hardship; an internal state that the Shariah establishes through its external cause (safar) for the purpose of granting concessions.

Liability of Witnesses: Cause vs. Condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi qiyas principle distinguishing cause and condition to determine liability of retracting witnesses, and identify the effective cause (illah) vs condition (shart).

Core Idea
In liability, the witnesses of the cause bear the loss; the condition's witnesses are irrelevant when the cause is present.

This passage from Noor al-Anwaar analyzes a nuanced case in Hanafi usul: when witnesses of both the cause (illah) and condition (shart) retract, who is liable? The Hanafis, through a qiyas on the case of witnesses of ihsan, conclude that only the cause witnesses are liable. We will trace the qiyas, examine Zufar's opposing view, and understand why the cause takes priority.

Guided Path
1

Distinguish shuhud al-illah (witnesses of the cause, e.g., the act of zina or the oath of talaq) from shuhud al-shart (witnesses of a condition, e.g., ihsan or virginity).
2
State the asl (original case) in the qiyas
The asl is the ruling on witnesses of ihsan: they are not liable when witnesses of zina (cause) exist.
The passage explicitly analogizes witnesses of condition to witnesses of ihsan.

Extract the illah
The illah is that the condition (e.g., ihsan) is not the effective cause of the harm; the effective cause is the act itself (zina/talaq).

Since the condition witnesses are like ihsan witnesses, they bear no liability when the cause witnesses also exist. If only condition witnesses retract, they do not cause the harm to be undone.

This illustrates the Hanafi prioritization of the original (asl) over the substitute.

Commentary
This passage discusses the sixth category of a minor's actions: transactions that are between benefit and harm, such as sale. Since sale can be either profitable (benefit) or loss-making (harm), the minor's act requires the guardian's opinion to ensure benefit. According to Abu Hanifa, if the guardian permits, the minor's sale with gross fraud (ghubn fahish) with strangers is effective, just as an adult's sale would be. However, the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) disagree: they hold that such a sale is not effective because the guardian's permission is given to avoid harm, and gross fraud indicates that the permission was not genuine. Furthermore, there are two narrations from Abu Hanifa regarding a sale with gross fraud conducted with the guardian himself: one says it is effective, another says it is not, due to suspicion that the guardian might have colluded to take the minor's property. The commentary elaborates on these positions, emphasizing that the guardian's permission is considered for the minor's welfare, and if gross fraud occurs, it undermines the purpose of the permission.

غبن فاحش (ghubn fahish): Gross fraud in a transaction where the price is far from market value.
رأي الولي (ra'y al-wali): The opinion or permission of the guardian, which compensates for the minor's deficiency.
تأمين الضرر (ta'min al-darar): Avoidance of harm, the purpose behind the condition of guardian's permission.

Purity from Menstruation as a Condition: Text Overriding Analogy
Explains the condition of purity for prayer and fasting, and the basis for the qada difference.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the usul principle that purity from hayd/nifas is a condition for fasting by nass (contrary to qiyas) and for prayer by a combination of nass and the rule that a condition lost prevents performance.

Core Idea
Purity from menstruation is a condition for fasting by nass, overriding qiyas; for prayer, it's a condition by nass, but qada is waived due to hardship.

This lesson explores the legal reasoning behind why a menstruating or postpartum woman cannot pray or fast. The Hanafi usul analysis reveals that while purity is a condition for both acts, the basis differs: for prayer, the condition is recognized by both analogy and text; for fasting, it is established solely by textual proof, contrary to analogy. This distinction has practical implications for whether missed acts must be made up.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its normal ruling
Determine the default ruling for prayer and fasting without impediments. Both are obligatory on a legally capable person. Then note that menstruation (hayd) and postpartum bleeding (nifas) are excuses that prevent performance.
The passage states these do not negate legal capacity.
2
Locate the nass (textual proof)
Find the hadith evidence that establishes the condition of purity. For fasting, the passage cites the hadith of Aisha where the Prophet ordered qada of fast but not prayer. For prayer, the hadith of Fatimah bint Qays distinguishes menstruation from istihadah and instructs leaving prayer during menses.
The proof is ahad hadith, zanni al-thubut but qati al-dalala regarding the ruling for menstruation.
3
Apply the usul rule: nass overrides qiyas
Analyze how the text contradicts analogical reasoning. Qiyas would suggest that fasting is valid even with impurity (since it is valid in janabah and hadath). However, the nass overrides this, making purity a condition.
Hanafi usul: al-nass muqaddam ala al-qiyas.
4
Derive the condition (shart)
Conclude that purity from hayd/nifas is a condition (shart) for the validity of performance of prayer and fasting. For prayer, this is also consistent with qiyas (purity is a condition for prayer in all states).
The passage says: 'And this is an instance where analogy agrees with the transmitted text.'
5

Note that the condition affects performance, but qada is a separate matter. The passage clarifies that for fasting, qada is required by text; for prayer, it is not, due to hardship and the principle that texts contrary to qiyas are not extended.

He means the distinction: fasting—that condition did not extend to qada, even though there is no hardship in its qada; for the qada of ten days of fasting within eleven months is not burdensome, even if it is assumed that postpartum bleeding covers the entire month of Ramadan, that is rare, and rulings are not based on it, and there is no hardship in it because the qada of one month of fasting in eleven months is not burdensome. Unlike prayer, the qada of ten days of prayer in every twenty days leads to hardship generally, so it is excluded. And death—conjoined to what preceded and it is the last of the celestial impediments—and it nullifies legal capacity: he means his statement regarding [the discussion of] childhood. The transmitted proof: what Bukhari and Muslim narrated from Fatimah bint Qays: she said, 'O Messenger of Allah, I am a woman who experiences istihadah (irregular bleeding) and I do not become pure, should I leave prayer?' He said, 'No, that is a vein and is not menstruation. When your menstruation comes, leave prayer, and when it ends, wash the blood from yourself and pray,' etc. (al-Sanbali) By textual proof: meaning that the Prophet prohibited the menstruating woman from fasting, and it is established that he also prohibited the postpartum woman from it by implication. In al-Mishkat from Adi ibn Thabit from his father from his grandfather from the Prophet - he said regarding the mustahadah: 'She leaves prayer during the days of her period that she used to menstruate, then she bathes and performs wudu for every prayer and fasts and prays.' (Narrated by Abu Dawud) (al-Qamar) By textual proof: meaning what al-Tirmidhi narrated from Aisha: she said, 'We used to menstruate at the time of the Messenger of Allah, then we would become pure, and he would order us to make up the fast and would not order us to make up the prayer.' Thus it is known that women used to fast during the time of the Prophet—[rather, they did not fast during menses], and there is no qada for prayer and qada for fasting—so it is established that purity from menstruation is a condition for fasting. (al-Sanbali) It did not extend: meaning this condition [of purity] did not extend to qada, for the texts that come contrary to analogy are not extended beyond the subject of the text. (al-Qamar) Regarding the qada of prayer, etc.: and postpartum bleeding usually lasts longer than the menstrual period, so the obligations multiply in it as well, and it entails hardship, and it is repelled. (al-Sanbali) to hardship generally: and postpartum usually lasts longer than the menstrual period, so hardship is conceivable in the qada of prayers during the postpartum state as well. (al-Qamar) And that it nullifies: meaning death destroys the foundation of obligation.

Commentary
This passage discusses the effect of menstruation (hayd) and postpartum bleeding (nifas) on prayer and fasting. It begins by noting that these conditions are excuses (udhr) like illness, but they do not nullify legal capacity—neither the capacity to have obligations (ahliyyat al-wujub) nor the capacity to perform (ahliyyat al-ada). Therefore, one might expect that prayer and fasting would still be obligatory during these states. However, purity (taharah) from these is a condition for prayer, and when the condition is absent, the performance lapse. For fasting, purity is a condition by textual proof (nass), which is contrary to analogy (qiyas) because fasting is normally valid even with minor or major impurity. The text establishes that both prayer and fasting are not performed during these states. The key issue is why qada (make-up) is required for fasting but not for prayer. The reason given is that the condition of purity for fasting is established by a text that is contrary to analogy, and such texts are confined to their specific context—they do not extend beyond what is explicitly stated. Thus, since the prohibition of performance during menstruation is textual, the qada is also treated as not required by extension of that text? Actually, the reasoning is different: for fasting, the text mandates qada, so it is done; for prayer, there is no such text, and additionally, qada of numerous prayers (especially with long postpartum) would cause hardship, so it is waived. The text also cites hadith: the famous hadith of Fatimah bint Qays and the narration from Aisha that the Prophet ordered qada of fast but not of prayer. Finally, death is mentioned as the ultimate nullifier of capacity. This passage illustrates Hanafi usul principles: texts that contradict qiyas are restricted to their explicit scope (la tata'adda 'an mawrid al-nass), and hardship (haraj) is avoided in deriving rulings.

Key Terms
الحرج (al-haraj): Hardship or difficulty, a reason for alleviating rulings in Islamic law.
مورد النص (mawrid al-nass): The specific subject or context in which the text was revealed or stated.
قضاء الصلاة (qada al-salat): Making up missed prayers.
قضاء الصوم (qada al-sawm): Making up missed fast days.

Qisas Not Inherited: Direct Right of Heirs
Hanafi usul rule on the nature of qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that qisas is not inherited and apply the underlying usul principle of direct establishment to analogous cases.

Core Idea
Qisas is a personal vengeance right, not property—thus established directly for heirs, not inherited.

This passage from Nur al-Anwar presents Abu Hanifa's unique view on the nature of qisas (retaliation). Unlike debts or other financial rights, qisas does not pass to heirs as part of the deceased's estate. Instead, it arises directly for the living guardians, because its purpose is to soothe their hearts by punishing the killer. This principle teaches us an important lesson about legal categories: rights can be established for beneficiaries without being owned by the deceased first.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its basis
Read the first sentence: qisas is 'not suitable for his need'—meaning it is not a property right of the deceased. The ruling: qisas is not inherited (ghayr mawruth).
Note the word 'ghayr mawruth' – this is the key legal term.
2
Understand the rationale (talil)
The passage explains that qisas was legislated (shuri'a) as a punishment (uquba) for exacting vengeance (dark al-tha'r), which is soothing the hearts of the guardians (tashfiyat al-sudur lil-awliya). Therefore, the benefit belongs to the living, not the deceased.
This is a teleological argument (ta'lil bi al-ghaya).
3
Contrast with other rights
The text contrasts qisas with other rights (huquq) that are first established for the deceased and then transfer to heirs. For qisas, it is 'established directly for the heirs' (yathbutu li al-warithati ibtida'an).
Direct establishment is a distinct legal category.
4
Analyze the cause (sabab)
The cause (qatl) is contracted for the deceased because the killing occurred against him. But the right (haqq) is for the heir because the vengeance belongs to them. This allows the heir to pardon before the victim dies, as the cause is connected to the deceased but the right is for the heir.
This explains why pardon by heir before death is valid in Hanafi law.
5
Examine the analogy (qiyas) to guardianship
Abu Hanifa compares qisas to wilayat al-inkah (marriage guardianship) for brothers. Just as each brother has complete authority to give in marriage (not divided shares), each heir has full right to demand qisas – it is not divisible among them.
This qiyas shows the principle: an indivisible right vests completely in each entitled person.

Commentary
This passage explains Abu Hanifa's position that the right of qisas (retaliation) is not inherited from the deceased to his heirs like other properties, but is rather established directly for the heirs. The reasoning is that qisas is a punishment meant to satisfy the vengeance of the living guardians, not a property right. Therefore, the cause (the killing) is contracted for the deceased but the benefit goes directly to the heirs. This is analogous to the right of giving a woman in marriage for her brothers, where each brother has complete guardianship, not divided shares.

غير موروث (ghayr mawruth): Not inherited; does not pass via inheritance.
السبب (al-sabab): The cause; in this context, the act of killing that gives rise to the right of qisas.
