Definition of Error (Khata) and Its Legal Effects
Definition of error and its legal consequences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-khata linguistically and terminologically, and distinguish between its effects on rights of Allah (haqq Allah) and rights of people (haqq al-'abd).

Core Idea
Error excuses from sin and punishment but not from compensation.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar defines error (khata') and establishes a fundamental distinction in Islamic law: error can lift accountability to Allah (sin and hadd) but does not remove liability to fellow humans (property, life). Understanding this dichotomy is essential for applying the concept across fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Extract the linguistic and terminological definition of khata' from the first lines. Linguistic: opposite of correctness. Terminological: occurrence of something contrary to intention due to lack of caution.
2
Note the cause of error
The text says error occurs 'by abandoning caution when directly pursuing the goal.' This implies it is unintentional but results from insufficient care.
3
Effects on rights of Allah
List examples: error in ijtihad (reward, no sin), mistaken intercourse (no hadd), mistaken killing (no qisas). Common thread: no punishment, no sin.
Error is a valid excuse (عذر صالح) for dropping haqq Allah when it results from ijtihad.
4
Effects on rights of people
Contrast: property destruction (daman), mistaken killing (diyah). Common thread: compensation required. Error does NOT lift liability.
Rationale: these are compensatory obligations (بدل المحل), not punishments.
5
Synthesize the principle
Error is a valid excuse for haqq Allah but not for haqq al-'abd. The distinction is between penal and compensatory realms.
This principle has wide application across fiqh.

والخطأ، عطف على ما قبله وهو في اللغة ضد الصواب، وفي الاصطلاح وقوع الشيء على خلاف ما أريد. وقوع الشيء: بترك التثبت عند مباشرة المقصود. وهو عذر صالح لسقوط حق الله تعالى إذا حصل عن اجتهاد، فلو أخطأ المجتهد في الفتوى بعد استفراغ الوسع لا يكون آلما، بل يستحق أجرًا واحدا، ويصير شبهة في دفع العقوبة حتى لا يأثم الخاطئ، ولا يؤاخذ بحدّ أو قصاص فإن زفّت إليه غير امرأته فظنها أنها امرأته فوطئها لا يحد، ولا يصير آلما كإثم الزنا، وإن رأى شبحًا من بعيد، فظنه صيدا، فرمى إليه وقتله، وكان إنسانًا لا يكون آلما إثم العمد ولا يجب عليه القصاص خطأً. ولم يجعل عذراً في حقوق العباد حتى وجب عليه ضمان العدوان إذا أتلف مال إنسان ووجبت به الدية إذا قتل إنسانًا خطاً؛ لأن كلها من حقوق العباد، وبدل المحل، لا جزاء الفعل. وصح طلاقه، أي طلاق الخاطئ كما إذا أراد أن يقول لامرأته: "أقعدي" فجرى على لسانه أنت طالق" يقع به الطلاق عندنا، وعند الشافعي لا يقع قياسًا على النائم ولقوله عليه السلام: "رفع عن أمتي الخطأ والنسيان".
And error, an apposition to what preceded, and it is in language the opposite of correctness, and in terminology: the occurrence of a thing contrary to what was intended. The occurrence of a thing: by abandoning caution when directly pursuing the goal. And it is a valid excuse for dropping a right of Allah Most High if it occurs as a result of ijtihad. So if a mujtahid errs in a fatwa after exhausting his effort, he is not sinful, but rather deserves one reward, and it becomes a doubt (shubha) in repelling punishment, so that the one who errs does not sin, and is not taken to account with a fixed punishment or retaliation. For if a woman other than his wife is brought to him and he thinks she is his wife and has intercourse with her, he is not subjected to the fixed punishment, and he is not sinful like the sin of zina. And if he sees a figure from afar, thinks it is game, shoots and kills it, and it is a human, he is not sinful for intentional sin, and retaliation is not obligatory for error. But it is not made an excuse in the rights of people, so that he is liable for compensation for aggression if he destroys a person's property, and blood money is obligatory if he kills a person by mistake, because these are all from the rights of people, and compensation for the subject, not punishment for the act. And his divorce is valid, i.e., the divorce of the one who errs, as when he intends to say to his wife 'Sit down' but his tongue utters 'You are divorced', the divorce occurs according to us, but according to al-Shafi'i it does not occur, by analogy with the sleeping person, and due to his saying (peace be upon him): 'My ummah is pardoned for error and forgetfulness.'

Commentary
This passage defines 'khata' (error) and its legal consequences. It begins by stating that error is the opposite of correctness; terminologically, it is the occurrence of something contrary to intention due to lack of caution. The key legal distinction is that error serves as a valid excuse for dropping the rights of Allah (such as fixed punishments and sin) when it occurs through ijtihad (juristic reasoning). For example, if a mujtahid makes an error in a fatwa after exerting his full effort, he is not sinful and earns one reward; the error creates a doubt that repels punishment. Similarly, if a man mistakenly has intercourse with a woman thinking she is his wife, he is not subject to the hadd punishment for zina. If he kills a person thinking it is game, he is not sinful for intentional murder and not subject to retaliation. However, error is not an excuse in rights of people (huquq al-'ibad). Thus, if one destroys another's property by mistake, he is liable for compensation (daman). If one kills by mistake, blood money (diyah) is obligatory. The reasoning is that these are compensatory obligations for the subject (badal al-mahall), not punishments for the act. Finally, the passage discusses divorce: according to the Hanafis, if a person intends to say something else but mistakenly says 'You are divorced', the divorce takes effect because the external statement is taken as evidence; the intent is internal and cannot be verified. Imam al-Shafi'i says it does not occur, analogizing to a sleeping person and citing the hadith 'My ummah is pardoned for error and forgetfulness.' The Hanafi response is that the hadith applies to sins and punishments, not to contractual acts like divorce.

Key Terms
الخطأ (al-khata): The occurrence of something contrary to intention due to lack of caution; a valid excuse in rights of Allah but not in rights of people.
حق الله (haqq Allah): Rights of Allah, such as fixed punishments and acts of worship; error can excuse from sin and punishment.
حق العبد (haqq al-'abd): Rights of individuals, such as property and life; error does not excuse from compensation.

Key Takeaway
Error excuses from sin and punishment (haqq Allah) but not from civil compensation (haqq al-'abd).

Divorce by Mistake: Hanafi Ruling and Shafi'i Dispute
Dispute on divorce by mistake.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on divorce by mistake, identify the Shafi'i position, and explain the Hanafi interpretation of the hadith 'رفع عن أمتي الخطأ والنسيان'.

Core Idea
The external utterance governs in divorce, not hidden intent.

This passage concludes with a specific application: a man who mistakenly says 'You are divorced' instead of 'Sit down'. The Hanafi school rules that the divorce is valid, while Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees. Understanding this difference reveals how Hanafi usul prioritizes apparent evidence over subjective intent in contractual matters.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
The person intends to say 'Sit down' to his wife but mistakenly says 'You are divorced'.
2
Identify the Hanafi ruling
The divorce takes effect (صح طلاقه). The text says 'يقع به الطلاق عندنا'.
This is the mufta bihi position of the Hanafi school.
3
Identify the Shafi'i ruling
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that divorce does not occur, by analogy with a sleeping person and due to the hadith of pardon for error.
4
Analyze the Hanafi reasoning
The external expression is the basis for judgment; internal intent cannot be verified. The hadith applies to sin and punishment, not to contractual effects like divorce.
Hanafis argue that the sleeping person analogy fails because a sleeping person has no consciousness, while the mistaken speaker is conscious.
5
Determine the practical difference
Under Hanafi, the woman becomes divorced (talāq bā'in or raj'ī depending on context). Under Shafi'i, she remains married.
This has significant consequences for the couple's marital status.
