Imamate of Mutayammim for Mutawaddi'een: The Principle of Substitution (Khilafah)
Intra-Hanafi dispute on imamate and usul principle of khilafah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the imamate of a mutayammim for mutawaddi'een and apply the condition for substitution (khilafah) to evaluate similar cases.

Core Idea
Substitution (khilafah) is established only by text, not opinion; tayammum is not a substitute for wudu but equal to it.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute about whether a person who performs tayammum (dry ablution) can lead in prayer those who have performed wudu (water ablution). The disagreement hinges on the nature of tayammum: is it a substitute (khalaf) for wudu, or is it an equal alternative? The resolution of this dispute invokes a fundamental usul principle: the concept of khilafah (substitution) and the conditions under which a substitute can stand in for an original obligation. By studying this passage, you will understand how the Hanafi school uses textual indicators to determine whether a secondary ruling is a true substitute or an independent ruling, and how this affects practical fiqh matters like congregational prayer leadership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions on the imamate of a mutayammim
Note that the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) permit it, while Muhammad and Zufar prohibit it. Identify the core reason for each side: equality vs. substitution.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, so the positions are recorded but later tarjih may settle the mufta bihi.
2
Analyze the usul principle of khilafah (substitution)
Understand that for a ruling to be a substitute (khalaf) it must be established by a clear textual indication (nass) or its implication, not by personal opinion. Also, the original (asl) must be absent at the time of need but potentially existent.
The passage uses the example of takbir al-tahrimah and its substitute 'Allahu Ajall' to illustrate how substitution works.
3
Apply the condition of 'absence of the original with potential existence'
Consider the kaffarah (expiation) for a false oath (yamīn ghamūs) vs. an oath on a future impossible matter. In the ghamus oath, the original (truthfulness) is impossible, so no substitute (expiation) is required. This shows that substitution requires the possibility of the original.
This condition is crucial: if the original is impossible, there is no need for a substitute.
4
Evaluate the dispute in light of the usul principle
Examine the Hanafi response: since tayammum is not explicitly made a substitute for wudu by text, it is equal, not a substitute. Thus the mutayammim is not 'weaker', and leading is valid. The opposing view mistakenly treats tayammum as a substitute without textual basis.
Note the condition that the mutawaddi' must not find water for the permissibility to hold.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference: according to the two shaykhs, a mutayammim can lead mutawaddi'een (with the water condition); according to Muhammad and Zufar, it is invalid unless it is a funeral prayer (where no disagreement exists).
This demonstrates how a usul principle directly impacts fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute (ikhtilaf) within the Hanafi school regarding the imamate of a person who performs tayammum (dry ablution) leading those who perform wudu (water ablution) in prayer. The two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) hold that it is permissible, while Muhammad and Zufar hold it is not. The core reasoning revolves around the nature of tayammum: is it a substitute (khalaf) for wudu, or equal to it? The two shaykhs argue that although earth is a substitute for water, tayammum itself is not a substitute for wudu; rather, both are equally valid purifications removing ritual impurity. Hence, one can lead the other. Muhammad and Zufar argue that since tayammum is a substitute for wudu, the mutayammim is weaker, and it is invalid for a weaker person to lead a stronger one. The passage then discusses the principle of substitution (khilafah), stating that it is established only by a clear textual indication (nass) or its implication, not by personal opinion. An important condition for a valid substitute is that the original (asl) is absent at the time of need but potentially existent. This condition is illustrated by contrasting the oath of ghamus (lying about the past) where the original (truthfulness) is impossible, so no expiation is due, versus an oath regarding touching the sky where truthfulness is theoretically possible, so expiation is required.

Key Terms
خلافة (khilāfah): The state of being a substitute (khalaf) in legal rulings, established only by text or its indication.
تيمم (tayammum): Dry ablution with clean earth as a purification method when water is unavailable.
وضوء (wuḍū’): Ritual ablution with water, a condition for prayer.
إمامة (imāmah): Leading others in congregational prayer.

Description with Semblance of Causality (Shabah al-Ilal)
Defining the fifth type of cause: a description with semblance of causality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define a description with semblance of causality (shabah al-‘illah), distinguish it from a pure means (sabab mahd) and a full cause (illah tammah), and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute over its causal efficacy.

Core Idea
A cause's attribute with partial effect: not pure means nor full cause, but a semblance of causality.

In Hanafi usul, causes are classified into several types. One subtle category is a description that resembles a cause without being a complete cause on its own. This lesson unpacks the fifth type, known as 'shabah al-illal' (شبهة العلل), using the classic example of riba. We will also examine a key scholarly disagreement among Hanafi authorities regarding the causal weight of such descriptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fifth category
Locate in the text the definition of the fifth type of cause: 'وصف له شبهة العلل' – a description that has a semblance of being a cause. Understand that it is not a full cause (علة تامة) nor a pure means (سبب محض).
The author places this category between pure means and complete cause.
2
Understand the riba example
The text gives the example of riba al-fadl: the prohibition is caused by a combination of two attributes, quantity (القدر) and type (الجنس). The combination is the complete cause. Each attribute alone has a semblance of causality.
This example is fundamental for grasping composite causes (علة مركبة) in Hanafi usul.
3
Distinguish composite cause from its parts
Recognize that the full cause is the composite of both attributes; each part individually is not a full cause but has some influence. If only one part exists, the ruling (prohibition) does not apply, showing that each part contributes.
This distinction is crucial for correctly applying qiyas.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
The passage records two positions: Imam al-Sarakhsi viewed each part as a pure means (سبب محض) with no independent effect; only the whole composite is effective. Fakhr al-Islam held that each part has a semblance of causality (شبهة العلية). The author follows Fakhr al-Islam.
The dispute affects whether one can base an analogy on a single attribute alone.
5
Reflect on implications for qiyas
Consider how this classification impacts legal analogy: if each part is a pure means, then qiyas cannot be performed using only one attribute; if it has semblance, qiyas on one attribute may be valid.
This shows the practical consequence of a theoretical usul distinction.

Commentary
This passage defines the fifth category of causes in Hanafi usul: a description that has a semblance of being a cause (شبهة العلل). It is illustrated by the example of riba (interest/usury), where the prohibition due to riba al-fadl is caused by two attributes together: quantity (al-qadr) and type (al-jins). The combination of the two forms the complete cause (علة تامة) in name, meaning, and ruling. However, each attribute alone has a semblance of being a cause (شبهة العلل) because it contributes to the effect but not independently. The cause is not a pure means (سبب محض) because it has some causal efficacy.

The passage then records a dispute among Hanafi scholars regarding the nature of such attributes. Imam al-Sarakhsi held that each attribute is a pure means with no independent effect; only the combination is effective. In contrast, Fakhr al-Islam argued that each attribute is not a pure means but a means with a semblance of causality. The author of this commentary sides with Fakhr al-Islam. The commentator from al-Talwih objects that this view contradicts the established Hanafi principle that parts of a cause have no effect on parts of the effect; rather, the whole cause affects the whole effect.

The passage also addresses an implied objection: if quantity and type each have some effect through the other, they would be causes of the cause, but they are not. So they should be pure means without semblance. The author rebuts that even so, they are not pure means because they have some influence. This shows the nuanced classification of causes in Hanafi usul.

Key Terms
شبهة العلل (shubhat al-`ilal): A semblance or appearance of being a cause, where an attribute has some causal influence but not independently.
علة مركبة (illah murakkabah): A cause composed of two or more attributes, such as quantity and type for riba. The combination is the complete cause.
سبب محض (sabab mahd): A pure means that has no causal efficacy; it is merely a path or indicator. Contrasted with attributes that have semblance of causality.

Substituting Proof for the Proven
Defining and exemplifying the substitution of proof for the proven

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the Hanafi usul concept of substituting proof (dalil) for the proven (madlul), distinguish it from cause (sabab), and apply it to divorce rulings.

Core Idea
Proof (dalil) stands in for the hidden reality but may have no causal effect.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar introduces a key Hanafi usul principle: when the actual legal cause (madlul) is hidden, the law may substitute a visible proof (dalil) that indicates its presence. The text distinguishes between cause (which always has an effect) and proof (which merely indicates), and illustrates this with the divorce example where a husband's need for divorce is proven by the timing of the pronouncement. Understanding this distinction helps you see how Hanafi fiqh deals with interior states that cannot be directly verified.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core concept
Read the opening statement: 'قيام الدليل مقام المدلول'. The principle is that a proof (dalil) can take the place of the proven (madlul) in legal rulings. Understand that this substitution occurs only when the madlul is hidden (like inner need or love).
2
Distinguish cause (sabab) from proof (dalil)
The passage explicitly differentiates: a cause (sabab) always has an effect on its effect (mussabbab), while a proof may have no effect and merely conveys knowledge. For example, a wife's statement 'I love you' is a proof of love but does not create love. Trace this distinction in the text.
This distinction is uniquely emphasized in Hanafi usul.
3
Analyze the divorce example
The passage gives two examples: (1) divorce based on need – need is hidden, so purity (tuhr) without intercourse is taken as proof; (2) conditional divorce on love – love is hidden, so the wife's declaration is proof. Work through how the proof substitutes for the hidden reality in each.
In the first example, some later commentators (like the Sharh Husami) refine: the proof is the initiation of divorce in purity, not purity itself.
4
Understand the temporal restriction
Both examples have a temporal limitation: the proof is confined to the session (majlis). The text explains this is because the condition 'if you love me' resembles an option (takhyir), and options are limited to the session. See how the ruling is tied to the context of the proof.
5
Note the ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school
The passage mentions a disagreement: some say that purity itself is not proof of need; the better view is that the act of initiating divorce during purity proves need. This shows that even within the school, the exact application of the principle can be refined.
This is an example of expansion and refinement in later Hanafi scholarship.

Commentary
This passage is a legal definition and rule: it explains the principle of substituting a proof (dليل) for the actual proven (مدلول) when the latter is hidden. The text begins by stating the rule and then provides an example from divorce law. The actual cause for divorce being permissible is the need to avoid harm, but need is inward and hard to know. Therefore, the law takes the presence of a pure period (tuhr) in which no intercourse occurred as an indicator of that need. The text distinguishes between ordinary necessity and hardship: necessity makes knowledge impossible, while hardship allows it with difficulty. It also distinguishes between cause (sabab) and proof (dalil): a cause has an effect on the effect, while a proof may have no effect and merely indicates. The example of conditional divorce based on love illustrates: love is hidden, so the wife's statement serves as proof, but it is confined to the session. The commentary (by al-Sanbali, al-Qamar, etc.) expands on these points, noting that divorce is disliked but permitted for necessity, and that the proof (initiation of divorce in purity) substitutes for the actual need.

Key Terms
الدليل (al-dalil): A proof or indicator that conveys knowledge of something else (the مدلول) without necessarily having a causal effect on it.
السبب (al-sabab): A cause that necessarily has an effect on its resultant (مسبب).
المدلول (al-madlul): The thing proven or indicated by the proof; often a hidden reality (e.g., need, love).
الطهر (al-tuhr): The state of purity from menstruation or lochia, specifically a period in which no intercourse has occurred.

The Role of Intellect in Legal Obligation: Hanafi vs. Mu'tazila
Explaining the dispute over intellect as a source of obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi and Mu'tazilite positions on intellect as a source of obligation and apply the Hanafi framework to evaluate the status of a person who has not received the prophetic call.

Core Idea
Intellect recognizes obligation but does not impose it; the divine law is the true obligator.

This passage from Noor al-Anwaar dives into a foundational usul al-fiqh debate: can the human intellect alone impose legal obligations, or does it merely recognize obligations that come from revelation? The Hanafi school argues that intellect is a tool for understanding, not a source of obligation, and that a person who never received the call is excused until they have the means to reflect. Let's examine the arguments.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the role of intellect ('aql) in legal obligation (taklif), focusing on the dispute between the Hanafi school and the Mu'tazila. The author first distinguishes between legal causes ('illal shar'iyyah) and intellectual causes ('illal 'aqliyyah): legal causes are mere signs that do not necessitate by themselves and are subject to abrogation, whereas intellectual causes are self-necessitating and unchangeable. The Mu'tazila hold that the intellect alone is sufficient to impose obligations—specifically, the obligation to know God and believe in Him—even without revelation. They cite the story of Prophet Ibrahim (a) who, before receiving revelation, told his father 'I see you in manifest error,' arguing that this judgment was purely rational. They claim that no one who possesses intellect has an excuse to refrain from seeking truth and belief; even a minor who is rational is obligated to faith. Conversely, the Hanafi stance, attributed to Abu Hanifa and Shaykh Abu Mansur, is that the intellect is not an independent source of obligation but rather a tool for recognizing obligations conveyed by revelation. The true obligator (al-mujib) is the divine law. For a person who has never received the prophetic call (man lam tablaghhu al-da'wa), if they have not had the opportunity to reflect—for example, if they grew up isolated on a mountain peak—they are excused for not believing. However, if they are granted time and experience to ponder the signs of creation, and they still disbelieve, they are not excused. The passage also addresses the estimation of a grace period (imhal) for reflection. Some analogize it to the three-day respite given to an apostate, but this is deemed weak because intellectual capacity varies greatly among individuals; the true measure is left to Allah. In summary, the Hanafi position is that intellect is a means of recognizing legal rulings, not a direct source of obligation. The Mu'tazila error is to treat intellect as an independent legislator. The practical outcome is that a person who never received the message is excused until they have the means to reflect, while a person who received the message and rejected it is not excused.

Core Idea
Reason can discern good and evil; a person with capacity but neglect is accountable even without revelation.

This lesson examines a fundamental usul dispute between the Hanafi and Ash'ari schools: can reason alone determine the goodness or badness of actions? The Hanafi school holds that reason is an independent source of moral knowledge, making a person accountable even before revelation reaches them. The Ash'aris maintain that only revelation defines good and evil. The practical consequence concerns the liability of someone who dies without belief but never received the call. We will explore the evidence each side presents, including the hadith 'the pen is lifted from three,' and see why the Hanafi position has been preferred.

Guided Path
1

The central question is whether good and evil are rational (Hanafi) or legal (Ash'ari). This determines accountability before revelation.

The Ash'ari school argues that without revelation, a person is excused because only the Lawgiver defines obligation. Killing such a person incurs blood money because their disbelief is pardoned.

Partial Manumission: The Divisible Nature of Ownership vs. Indivisible Freedom
Intra-Hanafi dispute on the legal nature of manumission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute over the definition of manumission and derive the ruling on partial manumission using qiyas.

Core Idea
The divisibility of manumission depends on whether it removes ownership (divisible) or establishes freedom (indivisible).

The ruling on partially freeing a slave hinges on a fundamental usul question: Is the act of manumission divisible? This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. By analyzing their definitions of i'taq and the qiyas they employ, we uncover how a single legal concept branches into different rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions of manumission
Locate the definitions: Abu Hanifa says i'taq is 'removal of ownership' (izālat al-milk); Sahibayn say it is 'establishment of freedom' (ithbāt al-‘itq). This difference is the root of the dispute.
Ownership is divisible; freedom is not.
2
State each position on partial manumission
From the definitions, deduce the ruling: For Abu Hanifa, freeing half a slave frees only that half (the rest becomes a mukatab). For Sahibayn, freeing half frees the entire slave because freedom cannot be divided.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is significant for the slave's status.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn's qiyas
They argue by absurdity: if manumission were divisible, three impossibilities follow (effect without cause, cause without effect, division of freedom). Map their qiyas: asl = complete manumission (indivisible); far' = partial manumission; illa = manumission establishes freedom (indivisible effect).
The third absurdity ('division of freedom') is contested in some manuscripts.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's response and his own qiyas
Abu Hanifa rejects their definition. His qiyas: asl = removal of ownership (divisible); far' = partial removal; illa = ownership is divisible, so its removal is divisible. The effect (freedom) follows automatically, not directly from the act, avoiding the absurdities.
He introduces the concept of 'legal power' (quwwa hukmiyya) to explain indivisibility of freedom itself.
5
Evaluate the proof structure and commentary refinements
Note that the Sahibayn's proof is complete only with the clause 'or division of freedom.' Some manuscripts omit it, and Bahr al-Ulum preferred the version with it. The commentary (al-Qamar, al-Sanbali) refines the definitions and clarifies that ownership removal indirectly causes freedom, like buying a relative.
This shows how usul scholars scrutinize proofs and prioritize certain riwayat.

Hanafi Qisas: Equality in Essence, Not Accidents

Learning Objective

Core Idea
For qisas, equality in the essential meaning suffices; extra honors do not prevent retaliation.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar explains a key Hanafi usul principle: slavery reduces blood money (diyah) but does not prevent retaliation (qisas) because qisas is based on the essential humanity, not accidental honors. The Hanafis support this with a qiyas (analogy) to gender: a man is executed for killing a woman despite differences in diyah. This lesson unpacks the argument and the Shafi'i counterposition.

The passage says: 'equality has been found in the original meaning upon which retaliation is built.' That original meaning is the inviolability of life (ismah). Other honors are additional qualities irrelevant to qisas.

The Hanafis draw an analogy: just as a man is killed for a woman despite diyah disparity, a free man is killed for a slave. The common factor is that both share the essential meaning (protection of life).

This highlights a fundamental usul disagreement about what counts as equality.

Kafala for a Deceased Insolvent: A Hanafi Usul Dispute
Usul dispute on kafala for a deceased insolvent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on kafala for a deceased insolvent debtor, apply the usul principle that kafala requires a valid dhimma, and evaluate the counter-argument based on the hadith of Jabir.

Core Idea
If a deceased debtor is insolvent, Hanafi law rejects guarantee because no valid liability exists to join.

The validity of guaranteeing a debt on behalf of a deceased person who left no assets is a contested issue between Abu Hanifa and the majority of scholars including his two companions. At the heart of the dispute lies the definition of kafala (guarantee) as joining one liability (dhimma) to another for demand. Abu Hanifa argues that death extinguishes worldly liability, making the deceased's dhimma non-existent for worldly claims, thus making guarantee impossible. The opposing view, supported by a hadith, insists that death does not cancel debts, so guarantee remains valid. This lesson will guide you through the arguments and their usul foundations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and positions
State the exact point of contention: whether kafala (guarantee) for a debt on behalf of a deceased insolvent debtor is valid. Note that Abu Hanifa rules it invalid (unless the deceased had assets or a guarantor during life), while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) and Imam al-Shafi'i rule it valid.
The dispute is recorded in the context of a longer discussion on legal capacity (ahliyyah).
2
State Abu Hanifa's proof
Abu Hanifa defines kafala as 'ضم الذمة إلى الذمة' (joining liability to liability) for the purpose of demand. If the deceased's dhimma is no longer considered valid for worldly claims (since he died insolvent), there is no liability to join the guarantor's dhimma to. Hence, the guarantee is invalid. Exceptions exist if the deceased had property or a guarantor from his life, preserving his dhimma.
This is a qiyas-based argument: kafala requires a valid original liability, analogous to a contract.
3
State the opposing proof
The Sahibayn and al-Shafi'i argue that death does not cancel debts; debts remain owed, as evidenced by the hadith of Jabir where the Prophet (peace be upon him) refused to pray over a deceased with debt until someone guaranteed it. This shows that the debt is still extant and can be guaranteed.
They also note that if debts were canceled, voluntary payment by a third party would be invalid, and there would be no accountability in the Hereafter.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond by distinguishing between worldly liability and Hereafter liability. The deceased's dhimma is freed from worldly legal demand (mutalaba dunyawiyya), but the debt remains for the Hereafter (ukhraviyya). Kafala is a worldly contract that requires a worldly liability; the Hereafter dimension does not affect the contract's validity in this world. Thus, the hadith refers to the Hereafter obligation, not a basis for a valid worldly guarantee.
This distinction is crucial: the same evidence is interpreted differently due to the usul principle that 'al-ummur bi-maqasidiha' (matters are judged by their objectives).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: According to Abu Hanifa, a person who guarantees the debt of a deceased insolvent cannot be sued for that debt in this world because the guarantee is invalid. According to the Sahibayn and Shafi'i, the guarantor can be sued and must pay. The Hanafi position is adopted in the relied-upon (mufta bihi) view of the school.
The tarjih is based on the definitional and epistemological weight of the concept of dhimma.

Spousal Washing: Resolving a Hadith-Text Conflict with Qiyas
Dispute between Hanafi and Shafi'i on spousal washing; Hanafi ta'wil of a hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of interpreting a prophetic hadith (tawil) to reconcile it with qiyas in the case of spousal washing of the deceased.

Core Idea
Hanafis interpret 'I would wash you' as arranging washing, not performing it, to preserve qiyas.

The Hanafi school rules that a wife may wash her deceased husband during her idda, but a husband may not wash his deceased wife. Imam al-Shafi'i objects, citing the Prophet's statement to Aisha: 'If you were to die before me, I would wash you.' This lesson examines the Hanafi usul response: how a hadith that appears to contradict qiyas is reinterpreted (ta'wil) to bring it in line with established principles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi ruling and its qiyas basis
State the Hanafi positions: (i) wife washes husband during idda because marital ownership continues for his needs; (ii) husband does not wash wife because her capacity as owned (mamlukiyya) ends at death. Identify the rational proof (qiyas) based on the nature of marriage as a form of ownership.
The key concept is milk al-nikah which persists during idda only for the husband's needs.
2
State the Shafi'i objection using the hadith
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the hadith 'If you were to die before me, I would wash you' proves that a husband may wash his wife, just as a wife washes her husband.
The literal wording 'ghasaltuka' suggests personal washing.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response: ta'wil of the hadith
The Hanafis reinterpret the hadith: 'I would wash you' means 'I would undertake the arrangements for your washing' (not physically wash). This ta'wil is necessary to avoid contradicting the stronger qiyas derived from the nature of marriage.
The response is supported by the principle that a hadith that contradicts qiyas must be interpreted figuratively if possible.
4
Evaluate the strength of each argument in light of usul principles
Compare the Hanafi use of qiyas (based on the underlying rationale of marital ownership) with the Shafi'i reliance on literal hadith. Note that the Hanafi interpretation is considered more consistent with the overall objectives of Shariah (maqasid) and other evidences, such as the report that Ali washed Fatima from behind a curtain.
The tarjih (preference) in the text sides with the Hanafi position.

Identify the Hanafi ruling and its ratio legis
Locate the Hanafi position: the wife washes her deceased husband during her 'iddah, but the husband does NOT wash his deceased wife. Identify the underlying principle: the husband's ownership of marriage persists during the iddah, so he is the 'owner' entitled to washing; the wife's capacity as a mamluka ends at death, so the husband becomes a stranger.
The key term is 'ahliyyat al-mamlukiyyah' — legal capacity of being owned, which ends with death.
2
State the Shafi'i ruling and its proof
Al-Shafi'i holds that the husband washes the wife just as she washes him, based on the Prophet's statement to 'A'ishah: 'If you died, I would wash you.' The Shafi'i reading takes the hadith literally as evidence for reciprocity.
The ruling is symmetric: both spouses can wash each other.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to the hadith
The Hanafi school responds that the hadith's apparent meaning is not intended literally. Instead, 'I would wash you' means 'I would undertake the arrangements for your washing' (i.e., finding someone else to do it). This reinterpretation is necessary because the hadith's literal sense would contradict a stronger qiyas based on the principle that ownership ends at death.
This is a classic case of overriding a zanni al-dalala hadith with a qiyas that has a stronger wajh al-dalalah.
4
Evaluate the epistemological weight
Note that the hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) and its meaning is also speculative (zanni al-dalala). The Hanafi qiyas is based on a clear principle (al-aṣl: ownership ends at death) which is qat'i. Therefore, the Hanafi reinterpretation is preferred.
The conflict is resolved by weighing the strength of the evidence: the qiyas is stronger in terms of both thubut and dalala.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence is: in Hanafi practice, a husband may not touch the body of his deceased wife for ghusl; in Shafi'i practice, he may. This affects funeral arrangements in different communities.
This is a real-world difference that muftis must be aware of.

Nature of the Right to Qisas: Inherited or Ab Initio?
Dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on Qisas inheritance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on qisas inheritance and explain the rational (ma'qul) derivation that distinguishes qisas from other rights.

Core Idea
Qisas is not inherited because its purpose—vengeance for the living—cannot benefit the deceased.

This passage addresses a fundamental dispute in Hanafi usul: does the right to qisas (retaliation) belong to the deceased and then pass to the heirs by inheritance, or does it arise directly for the heirs? The answer shapes several practical rulings, including the validity of pardon and the need to re-establish evidence. We will follow the reasoning of Imam Abu Hanifa and contrast it with that of his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
What is the exact point of disagreement? Is qisas inherited (mawruth) from the deceased, or established initially (ibtida'an) for the heirs?
2

Note the purpose (gharad) of qisas: it is legislated to fulfill the vengeance (darak al-tha'r) of the guardians. The deceased has no need for vengeance, so the right must belong directly to the heirs.

The two companions analogize qisas to other rights that are inherited. They see qisas as essentially a financial right because it can be converted to diyah. Hanafi response: the substitute (khalaf) may differ from the original in rulings.

4

How does this theoretical difference affect real cases? For example, when an heir is absent, does the present heir need to re-establish evidence? According to Abu Hanifa, each heir is independent; according to the Sahibayn, one heir can represent the deceased.
This step connects the abstract dispute to concrete fiqh.
5

The dispute also affects whether spouses inherit the right to qisas. Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn agree that they do, but for different reasons (ab initio vs. inheritance). Contrast with Malik's view.

Commentary
This passage is a fiqh discussion within a usul work, focusing on the nature of the right to qisas (retaliation) after the death of the victim. The central dispute is between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (al-Sahibayn). In contrast, the Sahibayn maintain that qisas is inherited, i.e., it first belongs to the deceased and then passes to the heirs by inheritance, like other rights. The author then explains the practical consequence (thamarat al-khilaf): if some heirs are absent and a present heir brings evidence to establish qisas, according to Abu Hanifa, the absent heir must re-establish the evidence upon his return because each heir is independently entitled, and qisas cannot be executed until all are present or consent. According to the Sahibayn, one heir can represent the deceased, so no re-evidence is needed. Another consequence concerns pardon: Abu Hanifa's view allows an heir to pardon before the victim's death (by istihsan), while the Sahibayn would not allow it since the right is not yet inherited. The passage also discusses conversion of qisas to diyah (blood money) by compromise or partial pardon, making it inherited property. Additionally, it addresses qisas between spouses, affirming it is obligatory, contrary to Malik who excludes spouses from inheriting diyah. The passage ends with a hadith evidence from the Muwatta' about a woman inheriting 'aql from her husband.

Commentary
This passage is a madhhab dispute (ikhtilaf) within the Hanafi school and with Imam Malik regarding the nature of qisas (retaliatory punishment) when it involves spouses. The author first presents a case: if an elder brother exacts retaliation before a younger brother reaches maturity, it is permissible. However, if one of two adult heirs is absent, the present one cannot exact retaliation alone, because the possibility of the absent one pardoning is considered likely, whereas the possibility of a minor pardoning after maturity is remote and thus disregarded. This establishes a principle: the presence and consent of all adult heirs is required for qisas. The core disagreement is between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). Imam Abu Hanifa holds that qisas is established for the heirs initially (ibtida'an), as an independent right for each heir. The two companions hold that qisas is inherited (irthan), like property, because the right to qisas passes from the victim to the heirs. The practical difference (thamarah) appears when some heirs are absent and the present heir establishes proof in court. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the absent heir must re-establish proof when he appears because each heir is independent in this matter. According to the two companions, no re-proof is needed because the present heir stands as a representative for the deceased, and the right is inherited collectively. When qisas is converted into property (mal) via a settlement (sulh) or pardon by some heirs, it becomes like any other inherited property: debts are paid from it, bequests are executed, and one heir can represent the deceased without needing to re-establish proof. The analogy is made with tayammum differing from wudu in intention: the substitute may have different rulings from the original. The passage then addresses spouses: qisas is established for husbands and wives just as diyah (blood-money) is. However, Imam Abu Hanifa says it is by initiation, while the two companions say it is by inheritance, as with diyah. Finally, Imam Malik's position is refuted: he denies that spouses inherit from diyah because marriage ends at death. The Hanafi proof is a hadith (recorded by Malik in al-Muwatta and Ibn Majah) where the Prophet ordered the inheritance of the wife of Ashim al-Dhibabi from her husband's blood-money. Thus, Hanafi law affirms inheritance of qisas and diyah between spouses.

الدية (al-diyah): Blood-money; financial compensation paid to the heirs of a murder victim as substitute for qisas.
الإرث (al-irth): Inheritance; the transfer of rights and property from the deceased to his heirs.

Commentary
This passage addresses the legal effect of jest (hazl) on contracts, specifically the sale contract. It opens by defining the scenario: two parties secretly agree to simulate a sale (muwada'a) without intending a real transaction. They then perform a formal sale in front of witnesses. Afterward, they may have one of four situations: (1) they both agree to adhere to the original jest (bina'), (2) they both agree to disregard it and treat the sale as serious (i'rad), (3) they had no intention—neither building on the jest nor disregarding it—at the time of the contract (khali al-dhihn), or (4) they disagree: one claims they were still jesting, the other claims they were serious. The ruling is that in case (1), the sale is invalid (fasad) because there was no genuine consent (rida) to the legal effect. In case (2), the sale is valid and binding because the i'rad cancels the prior jest. In case (3), the default is validity because soundness is the principle in contracts, so the sale is valid according to Abu Hanifa (and reportedly held by him), contrary to the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) who consider it invalid. In case (4), Abu Hanifa upholds validity, preferring the claim of seriousness, while the disciples side with the claim of jest. The same analysis applies to hazl in the price (qadr al-thaman). The passage then divides the scenarios similarly and discusses consequences. The commentary includes glosses from al-Qamar and al-Sunbuli explaining nuances, such as that the invalid sale of a jester does not transfer ownership even with possession, unlike other invalid sales where possession can transfer ownership if there is consent to the legal effect. The overall legal principle is that a contract made in jest is void, but if later the parties intend seriousness, the contract becomes valid as long as the formal elements are present.

Effect of Jest on Khul' - Imam vs Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on jest in khul'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish Abu Hanifa's conditional approach from the Sahibayn's voidance approach in jest khul' and apply Hanafi qiyas to derive each ruling.

Core Idea
In khul', jest suspends divorce under Abu Hanifa but is void under Sahibayn — the dispute hinges on analogizing to khiyar al-shart vs. treating jest as null ab initio.

This lesson explores an intra-Hanafi dispute on the effect of jest (hazl) in khul'. Imam Abu Hanifa views jest as akin to a condition of option (khiyar al-shart), suspending divorce until the woman accepts the payment. His two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, consider jest void ab initio, so if the parties later confirm seriousness, divorce is effective immediately. The passage from Noor al-Anwar with its commentaries clarifies the legal reasoning and practical outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal act and element of jest
Recognize that khul' is a divorce initiated by the wife for compensation. When performed in jest, the question arises whether it is valid or suspended. Note the two positions.
2
State the two Hanafi positions
Abu Hanifa: divorce does not occur until the woman chooses to accept the payment. Sahibayn: jest is void; if seriousness is later affirmed, divorce occurs immediately and payment is due.
The Sahibayn's view is considered the apparent (zahir) position in the text.
3
Trace the dalil for each position
Abu Hanifa uses qiyas to khiyar al-shart: just as an option condition suspends the sale and price, jest in khul' suspends divorce and payment. The Sahibayn rely on the principle that jest is void in serious matters like divorce, and the hadith 'The jest of divorce is serious' supports immediate effect.
Both proofs are accepted within the Hanafi school; the difference is methodological.
4
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Abu Hanifa, if khul' is jested, the woman must later choose the payment for divorce to occur. Under Sahibayn, if they later agree it was serious, divorce is retroactive from the jest utterance.
This practical difference affects when divorce takes effect and the timing of iddah.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The text indicates that the Sahibayn's view is the relied-upon position (zahir al-riwayah), but Abu Hanifa's view is also recognized as valid. In fatwa, the mufta bihi is generally the Sahibayn's position, though some later scholars prefer Abu Hanifa's conditional view for caution.

Commentary
This passage discusses the effect of jest (hazl) on a khul' contract, focusing on the disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). According to Abu Hanifa, if khul' is performed in jest, the divorce does not take effect immediately but becomes contingent on the woman's acceptance of the payment (mal). If they differ, the one claiming abandonment of jest is believed. The passage also notes that silence is interpreted as abandonment of jest, leading to the divorce being binding. The commentary includes notes from later scholars like al-Qamar and al-Sanbali clarifying reasoning and sources.

Building on a Jest Agreement in Khul'
Ruling when both parties agree to build upon a prior jest agreement in khul'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the effect of building on a jest khul' agreement and apply the hadith 'The jest of divorce is serious' to evaluate the positions.

Core Idea
Building on a jest khul' triggers immediate divorce according to Sahibayn, but remains conditional under Abu Hanifa.

We now examine a scenario where both spouses agree to treat a previous jest khul' as serious. The key dispute is whether this agreement alone makes the divorce effective, or whether it still depends on the wife's acceptance of the payment. This hinges on how we understand the relationship between payment and divorce in khul'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The spouses had previously made a jest agreement regarding khul', and now both agree to build upon that agreement while still in the same sitting. What is the legal effect?
2
State the positions
Abu Hanifa holds that divorce is conditional upon the woman's acceptance of the full payment. The Sahibayn hold that divorce occurs immediately upon agreement, with payment due after the sitting ends.
3
Analyze the dalil
The hadith 'The jest of divorce is serious' is cited. The Sahibayn apply it directly to khul' as a type of divorce. Consider whether this analogy is sound.
The hadith is about unilateral divorce, not khul' which is a contract.
4
Examine the Hanafi response
Abu Hanifa distinguishes: payment is a condition for divorce; without the wife's consent to pay, the divorce is not effective. The hadith applies only to the divorce pronouncement, not to the contractual aspect of khul'.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).
5
Consider tarjih
The text suggests that the Sahibayn's view is more apparent in one respect, but it also mentions the alternative. Reflect on which view is given more weight and why.

Hijr for the Spendthrift: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Dispute on hijr for a safih: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi dispute on hijr for a safih by comparing the proofs of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn and identifying the mufta bihi position.

Core Idea
The Hanafi fatwa restricts a spendthrift in transactions nullified by jest, despite Abu Hanifa's objection.

This passage presents a classic intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), on whether a free, sane, adult who is foolish with his wealth (safih) can be legally restricted (hijr). While all agree that the safih should not be given his wealth, they differ on whether his transactions are valid. This lesson walks through the arguments and reveals the operative fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Agreed Principle
First, note that all scholars agree on one point: the safih should not be given his wealth to manage freely. This is the ijmah mentioned in the text.
The disagreement is only about an additional issue: whether he is restricted from transacting.
2
Isolate the Point of Disagreement
The dispute is whether a safih can be placed under legal restriction (hajr) preventing his financial transactions from taking effect. Abu Hanifa says no; the Sahibayn say yes, but only in certain transactions.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's Position
Abu Hanifa holds that no restriction whatsoever is allowed for a free, sane, adult. His reasoning is that a free adult has full legal capacity (ahliyyat al-ada), and the Sharia has not legislated hijr for such a person.
He makes no distinction between transactions nullified by jest and those that are not.
4
Analyze the Sahibayn's Position
The Sahibayn distinguish between two types of transactions: those nullified by jest (like sale, hire, gift) and those not nullified (like marriage, divorce, emancipation). Only in the former do they consider the safih restricted. Their proof is qiyas: just as a minor or insane person is restricted to protect his wealth, the safih should be restricted because he wastes his wealth and becomes a public burden.
They analogize the safih to those lacking full capacity in specific contexts.
5
Determine the Tarjih (Mufta Bihi)
The text explicitly states: 'al-fatwa 'ala qawl al-sahibayn' — the fatwa is on the statement of the two Sahibayn. Later Hanafi authorities like Ibn Abidin in al-Durr al-Mukhtar confirm this. Thus the operative ruling is that the safih is restricted in transactions voided by jest.
This is a clear example of an intra-madhhab tarjih where the teacher's view is overridden by the students' view due to strength of proof and public interest.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four imams' positions on qisas in coercion and evaluate the Hanafi tool analogy as a basis for assigning liability.

Core Idea
In coercion, the coerced can be a mere tool; the real killer is the coercer, according to Abu Hanifa.

Coercion raises a critical question in Islamic criminal law: when someone is forced to kill, who bears qisas? The Hanafi school, especially Abu Hanifa, introduces a unique concept: the coerced may be like a tool (ālah), shifting liability to the coercer. This passage presents a famous ikhtilaf among the imams, each basing their ruling on different understandings of causation and intent. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to analyze legal reasoning in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed case
The case is intentional killing (qatl amd) under coercion: the coercer throws the coerced onto the victim, or the coerced is forced to strike with a sword. Identify the act and the key question: who is the killer in the eyes of the law?
The coerced is physically the direct cause, but the coercer is the ultimate cause.
2
Analyze each imam's position
List the rulings: Abu Hanifa – liabilit on coercer; Muhammad and Zufar – liability on coerced; al-Shafi'i – liability on both; Abu Yusuf – liability on neither due to doubt (shubhah).
Note that Abu Yusuf's view introduces the principle of shubhah as a barrier to qisas.
3
Examine the tool analogy
Abu Hanifa's argument: the coerced is like a knife (ālah). The coercer is the real killer because he uses the coerced as an instrument. Understand how this analogy transfers causation.
This is a talīl (rationale) based on considering the coerced's lack of free will.
4
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: who gets executed? Under Abu Hanifa, the coercer; under Muhammad, the coerced; under Shafi'i, both; under Abu Yusuf, neither (only diyah and kaffarah on coerced for accidental cases).
This shows how a seemingly abstract usul dispute has life-or-death consequences.
5
Assess the absence of tarjih
The passage states Abu Hanifa's view first but does not declare a tarjih (preferred view). It also includes Abu Yusuf's doubt-based opinion. Consider why later Hanafi scholars might adopt one view over another.
In later Hanafi fatwa, the view of Abu Hanifa is usually taken in intentional killing, but the dispute is well-known.

The second: that is, the second category of acts is that in which the coerced person can be a tool for another, like destroying life and property. For it is possible for a person to take another and throw him onto someone's property to destroy it, or onto someone's life to kill him. So qisas is obligatory upon the coercer (with kasr of ra) if the killing was intentional with a sword, because he is the killer, and the coerced is his tool like a knife. This is according to Abu Hanifa. Muhammad and Zufar said: qisas is obligatory upon the coerced, because he is the true doer, even if the other is the commander. Commentary
This passage presents the second category of coerced acts: those where the coerced can function as a mere instrument (ālah) for the coercer, such as being forced to kill someone or destroy property. The classical example: the coercer physically throws the coerced onto a victim to kill him. The ruling on qisas (retaliation) for intentional killing generates a famous ikhtilaf among the imams. Abu Hanifa holds qisas on the coercer only, as he is the real killer using the coerced as a tool. Muhammad and Zufar hold qisas on the coerced (the direct actor). Al-Shafi'i holds both liable. Abu Yusuf holds neither liable due to the doubt (shubha) of coercion. For accidental killing, the diyah is on the 'āqilah (tribal surety) of the coerced, and kaffārah is on him. The passage then transitions to a new classification: the author (of the matn) now divides the prohibited matter (ḥurmah al-mukrah bih) into four types, corresponding to obligation, prohibition, permission, and concession.

Key Terms
قصاص (qiṣāṣ): Retaliation in kind for intentional killing or injury
آلة (ālah): Tool; in this context, the coerced is likened to a weapon used by the coercer
شبهة (shubhah): Doubt that repels the fixed punishment; here Abu Yusuf argues coercion creates a shubhah

Liability for Intentional Murder Under Coercion: The Hanafi Attribution Principle
Determining who bears liability for murder under coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four positions on liability for intentional murder under coercion and apply Abu Hanifa's tool-analogy to determine the liable party.

Core Idea
The coercer is the killer; the coerced is his tool — Abu Hanifa attributes the act to the true cause.

This passage from Nur al-Anwar discusses the second category of coerced actions: those where the coerced person can serve as a mere tool (ala). The case of murder under coercion illustrates a deep usul debate about attribution of actions. When someone is forced at knifepoint to kill another, who bears the criminal liability? The Hanafi school offers multiple answers, each grounded in a distinct theory of causation and intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
Recognize that the act is intentional murder under coercion (ikrah), which falls under the second division where the coerced can be a tool.
Coercion is full (mulji') such that the coerced's will is overridden.
2
List the four positions
State the views of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad/Zufar, Shafi'i, and Abu Yusuf on who is liable for qisas.
These are intra-Hanafi (Muhammad, Zufar, Abu Yusuf) and inter-madhhab (Shafi'i) differences.
3
Analyze the causal reasoning behind each
Trace how each scholar determines the 'real doer': Abu Hanifa sees the coercer as cause, Muhammad sees the coerced as actual doer, Shafi'i sees both as causes, Abu Yusuf sees doubt (shubha) negating liability.
Abu Hanifa's analogy: coerced is like a knife (tool without will).
4
Apply the tool-analogy
Apply Abu Hanifa's reasoning: just as the wielder of a knife is the killer, the coercer is the killer; the coerced is an instrument.
This analogy is the basis for attributing the act to the true cause.
5
Note the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf)
Identify how the differing views affect who pays qisas, diya, and kaffara. Under Abu Hanifa, only the coercer pays qisas; under others, the coerced or both may be liable.
For accidental killing, diya is on the 'aagila of the coerced, and kaffara on the coerced, indicating he is the direct cause in accidental cases.

Commentary
This passage discusses the second type of coercion where the coerced person can act as a tool for the coercer, such as being forced to kill or destroy property. The ruling on intentional murder under coercion is disputed among scholars. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, the coercer is considered the actual killer and must face qisas, while the coerced is merely a tool like a knife. Muhammad and Zufar hold that the coerced is the real agent and thus bears liability. Imam Shafi'i imposes liability on both the coercer (as orderer) and the coerced (as doer). Abu Yusuf exempts both due to the presence of doubt (shubha). For accidental killing, the blood money is on the 'aagila of the coerced, and expiation is also upon him.

Key Terms
الإكراه (al-ikrah): Coercion that nullifies free choice, rendering the coerced as a tool.
المكره بالكسر (al-mukri): The one who coerces (the coercer).
المكره بالفتح (al-mukrah): The one who is coerced.
