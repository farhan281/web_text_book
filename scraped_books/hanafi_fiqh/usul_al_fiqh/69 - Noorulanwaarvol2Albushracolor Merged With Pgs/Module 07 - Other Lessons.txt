Implicit Permission for Substitution (Istibdal) in Promised Provisions
Resolving an objection regarding substituting goat for promised provisions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi principle of implicit permission (dalalat al-nass) for substituting a specified item when it cannot fulfill diverse needs, and apply it to similar cases.

Core Idea
When a specific item cannot fulfill the intended purpose, the text implicitly permits substitution to achieve the objective.

This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary tackles a nuanced issue: can a goat promised to the poor be substituted with cash if the goat alone cannot provide for their varied needs (bread, stew, clothing, firewood)? The commentator demonstrates how the necessity of fulfilling the promise implies an implicit permission for substitution, and he counters a potential objection that other sources (zakat, kaffarah, khums) already cover the poor. By analyzing the primacy and constancy of zakat, he reinforces the reasoning behind Hanafi permissibility of istibdal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core problem and the implicit permission
The passage begins by noting that the single goat cannot fulfill the diverse promised provisions (bread, stew, firewood, etc.). Therefore, the command to fulfill these promises implicitly permits replacing the goat with cash (dirhams/dinars) so that all needs can be met.
This is an example of dalalat al-nass – the text implies a ruling beyond its literal wording.
2
Analyze the objection and its basis
An objector argues that the poor receive other things from zakat al-fitr, 'ushr, kaffarah, and khums, so the goat is not their only resource. Thus, there is no necessity to substitute.
The objector assumes that these alternative sources are readily available, hence the goat alone is insufficient is not a problem.
3
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The commentator refutes the objection by showing that zakat is the only constant and ubiquitous source. Other sources are rare or conditional: booty (ghanimah) rarely occurs, expiation (kaffarah) depends on oath-breaking, 'ushr depends on cultivation, and fitr may not be paid. Thus, zakat remains the primary resource for the poor, and it must fulfill all their needs, so substitution is essential.
This response uses a comparative analysis of the reliability of different sources.
4
Refine the principle with a precision from 'al-Da'ir'
A further subtlety: if one can give the exact goat and it is valid, then how can substitution be mandatory? The answer: the goat is considered absolute valuable property (mal mutaqawwim mutlaq), not restricted to its specific form. The promise is for a value, so the goat and cash are equivalent.
This refines the understanding that the primary object of the promise is the value, not the specific animal.
5
Synthesize the usul principle
The overall argument combines necessity (dharurah) and the nature of the specified item (absolute value) to derive an implicit permission for substitution. The ruling is not based on explicit text but on an inference from the underlying objective of fulfilling the promise.
This is a classic Hanafi use of istihsan (juristic preference) based on maqasid.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed textual analysis from a Hanafi usul commentary. It begins by addressing an objection concerning the substitution of a specific goat for promised provisions to the poor. The argument is that the goat alone cannot fulfill the diverse needs (bread, stew, etc.), so the text implicitly permits substituting it with cash (dirhams or dinars) to meet all needs. An objector points out that the poor also receive other provisions from various sources like zakat al-fitr, 'ushr, kaffarah, and khums, so the goat is not the only resource. The response distinguishes zakat as the primary and ever-present resource, while other sources are rare or conditional. Thus, the necessity of fulfilling varied needs from zakat justifies substitution. The commentator further refutes the notion that the exact goat cannot be given; it can, but if it does not suffice, substitution is allowed. The principle is that the goat serves as a standard value, not as a specific item. This analysis reflects Hanafi reliance on istihsan based on necessity and the primacy of zakat.

Key Terms
الاستبدال (al-istibdal): Substitution of the specified item with another item or cash, based on implicit permission from the text.
المواعيد (al-mawa'id): The promised provisions that include various items like bread, stew, firewood, and clothing.
الشاة (al-shaat): The specific goat mentioned as the promised item, which cannot alone fulfill diverse promises.
مال متقوّم مطلق (mal mutaqawwim mutlaq): Absolute valuable property: property that can be valued monetarily in any form, not restricted to a specific type.

Understanding What is Established by Ta'lil (Causal Reasoning)
Clarification of the scope of ta'lil and correction of a common misinterpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'what is established by ta'lil' and 'the ruling of qiyas', and identify the Hanafi view on the correct category of ta'lil.

Core Idea
Ta'lil produces rulings that are either correct or erroneous; the correct category in Hanafi usul is the fourth.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar clarifies a common confusion in usul al-fiqh: what exactly is meant by 'hukm al-qiyas'? Some mistakenly think it refers to the legal status of qiyas as a proof, but the author corrects this, explaining that it refers to the ruling derived from qiyas. We will break down the categories of rulings established by causal reasoning and see why the Hanafi school prefers the fourth category.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main claim
The author says there are four categories of what is subject to ta'lil, but only the fourth is correct in our madhhab. What does that tell us about the nature of ta'lil?
This indicates that not all derivations via ta'lil are equally valid.
2
Note the error corrected
Some commentators thought this section discusses the ruling of qiyas itself. Why is that a 'gross error' according to the author?
The error is confusing the product (the derived ruling) with the tool (qiyas).
3
Distinguish two meanings of 'hukm al-qiyas'
The ruling that results from qiyas (the derived hukm) vs. the ruling on the validity of qiyas as a proof. Which one is the topic here?
The passage explicitly says it is about the effect (athar) connected to qiyas.
4
Understand the attributes of the derived ruling
The derived ruling can be wrong or correct, definitive or speculative. What does this imply about the epistemological status of qiyas-derived rulings?
They are generally zanni (speculative) unless otherwise established.

Commentary
This passage begins a new topic: the categorization of rulings that can be established through ta'lil (causal reasoning), which is the basis of qiyas. The author states that there are four categories of rulings that can be subject to ta'lil, but the correct view in our Hanafi school is the fourth category (which will be explained later). He criticizes some commentators who mistakenly think this section deals with the ruling of qiyas itself (i.e., its legal status as a proof). Instead, the author clarifies that it deals with the hukm (the ruling that is the result of qiyas) and its attributes: whether it is correct or erroneous, definitive or speculative. This distinction is important because later usul works elaborate on the epistemological status of rulings derived via qiyas. The passage emphasizes that the discussion is about the product of qiyas, not qiyas itself.

Key Terms
التعليل (al-ta'līl): The process of identifying and establishing the effective cause ('illa) of a ruling for the purpose of extending it to new cases via qiyas.
حكم القياس (hukm al-qiyās): The ruling that is the result of qiyas (the derived ruling), not the ruling on the validity of qiyas itself.

The Hanafi Classification of Cause: Between Real and Metaphorical
Explaining the types of cause with Hanafi, Shafi'i, and Zufar views

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi distinction between real and metaphorical cause in conditional pronouncements and evaluate the views of al-Shafi'i and Zufar.

Core Idea
A conditional divorce is a metaphorical cause with a semblance of reality, requiring a locus to exist.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the concept of cause (sabab) is central to understanding how rulings attach to acts. This lesson examines a key dispute: how to classify a conditional pronouncement like 'If you enter, you are divorced.' Is it a real cause from the moment of utterance, a pure metaphor, or something in between? The Hanafi school adopts a middle position, and this classification has practical consequences for the validity of subsequent divorces.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three positions
State the Hanafi, Shafi'i, and Zufar views on the nature of conditional pronouncements (ta'liq) as cause (sabab).
Hanafi: metaphorical with semblance (shubhat al-haqiqah); Shafi'i: real cause; Zufar: pure metaphor.
2
Understand 'semblance of reality'
Grasp why the Hanafi school considers conditional declarations as having a trace of reality — because they ultimately lead to the ruling when the condition occurs.
This distinguishes it from pure metaphor, which has no such effect.
3
Requirement of a locus (mahall)
Learn that a cause (even metaphorical with semblance) requires an existing locus to operate; for divorce, the marriage is the locus.
If the locus is destroyed by an immediate pronouncement (tanjiz), the conditional cause becomes void.
4
Apply to the concrete scenario
Work through the scenario: man says 'if you enter, you are divorced thrice' then immediately divorces thrice, wife remarries, returns, enters. Determine outcome under each school.
Hanafi: not divorced; Zufar: divorced; Shafi'i: not applicable since he would consider first divorce valid? (Shafi'i view on timing differs).
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence of the dispute: tanjiz invalidates ta'liq only in the Hanafi view, affecting subsequent marriage and entry.
This is the 'fruit of disagreement' explicitly mentioned in the text.

This passage is a detailed discussion from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary (sharh) on the classification of cause (sabab) and its types. Distinguishing Conditional and Temporal Causes in Talaq
Distinguishing conditional from temporal causes in divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a conditional (mu'allaq) and a temporal (mudaf) cause in Hanafi usul, and apply the rule to determine when a cause takes effect.

Core Idea
A temporal cause is effective in the present; a conditional cause only upon the condition's occurrence.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text clarifies a critical distinction: how a cause (sabab) operates when expressed as a condition (e.g., 'if you enter') versus a time specification (e.g., 'tomorrow'). Understanding this distinction affects whether a legal act (like divorce or vow) is considered to have taken effect immediately or only later. The author uses the concept of conflicting uncertainties (shubah) to explain why a conditional cause requires a locus only at condition fulfilment, while a temporal cause is a cause in the present despite the delay in its ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of causes
First, identify what the passage means by 'conditional obligation' (al-ijab al-mu'allaq) and 'temporal obligation' (al-ijab al-mudaf). The former is tied to a future condition, the latter to a future time.
Mu'allaq: 'If you enter, you are divorced.' Mudaf: 'You are divorced tomorrow.'
2
Understand the role of shubah in the conditional case
The passage states that two conflicting uncertainties (shubah) cancel each other: one suggests the consequence occurs before the condition (needing a locus), the other suggests the condition as a cause delays ruling, so no locus is needed until condition. Their cancellation eliminates the need for a locus.
This is a rational argument for why conditional causes do not require a present locus.
3
See how the temporal case lacks the barrier
The temporal obligation lacks ta'liq (conditioning), which acts as a barrier (mani') in the conditional case. Therefore, it is a cause in the present, even though its ruling is delayed.
The temporal specification attaches to the ruling, not to the cause itself.
4
Apply to practical furu'
The passage gives examples: a conditional vow cannot be fulfilled before the condition; a temporal vow can be fulfilled before the time. Also, an oath not to divorce is broken immediately if a temporal divorce is pronounced.
These furu' show the practical implications of the distinction.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed Hanafi usul analysis distinguishing between two types of causes: conditional (mu‘allaq) and temporal (mudaf). The author first explains that a conditional statement (e.g., 'If you enter, you are divorced') involves two conflicting uncertainties (shubah) that cancel each other, making it unnecessary to have a locus (mahall) for the divorce to take effect. The first uncertainty is that the consequence (divorce) might occur before the condition (analogous to absence of a locus), and the second is that conditioning upon a thing that has the ruling of a cause suggests the absence of a locus before the cause is fulfilled. The reason given is that there is no ta‘liq (conditioning) that would act as a barrier (mani‘) to the cause taking effect. The Three Types of Conditions in Hanafi Legal Theory
Classifying conditions into pure, cause-ruled, and reason-ruled

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of conditions (shart mahd, shart lahu hukm al-illah, shart lahu hukm al-asbab) and apply the Hanafi principle to determine liability.

Core Idea
A condition is not always a cause—legal liability depends on whether a free agent's act intervenes naturally or by choice.

This passage introduces a critical taxonomy in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: conditions (shart) are not all the same. Some conditions merely precede a ruling without affecting liability, while others function like causes or reasons. Understanding this classification is essential for determining legal responsibility in cases involving indirect actions.

Guided Path
1
Define 'condition' (shart) in legal theory
A condition is a prerequisite for a ruling but not the direct cause. Identify the three categories from the passage: pure condition, condition with ruling of cause, condition with ruling of reason.
Note the key difference: whether a free agent's act intervenes and whether that act is attributed to the condition.
2
Analyze each category with examples
For each category, note the intervening act: digging a well (natural act – cause-ruled), splitting a wineskin (natural act – cause-ruled), opening a birdcage (disputed – free agent or natural?), releasing a slave (free agent – reason-ruled).
Muhammad al-Shaybani treats birdcage opening as cause-ruled; the two shaykhs treat it as reason-ruled.
3
Apply the attribution principle
Liability is assigned based on whether the result is attributed to the condition. If the intervening act is natural or instructed, attribution holds; if it is a free choice, it does not, unless the condition itself compels the act.
Example: digging a well in another's property leads to liability because the fall is natural; releasing a slave who runs away does not because the flight is the slave's choice.
4
Identify the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
The birdcage case: Muhammad al-Shaybani holds the opener liable because the bird's flight is instinctual and immediate; Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf consider the bird a free agent, so no liability. This shows the subtle boundary between natural and voluntary acts.
The dispute centers on whether the bird's flight is attributed to the opening or to the bird's own will.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul work explains the classification of conditions (shart) in legal rulings. The author, commenting on the base text, identifies three categories: (1) Pure condition (shart mahd): a condition that is merely a prerequisite with no causative element, e.g., 'If you enter the house, you are divorced' — here the entry is a condition but the divorce is attributed to the utterance, so no liability for the condition. (2) Condition with the ruling of a cause (shart lahu hukm al-illah): where the condition directly leads to the result and the intervening act is natural or attributed to the condition, e.g., digging a well in another's property (the digger is liable because the fall is natural), splitting a wineskin (the liquid flows due to removal of obstacle, so the splitter is liable). (3) Condition with the ruling of a reason (shart lahu hukm al-sabab): where a free agent's act intervenes and is not attributed to the condition, e.g., releasing a slave who then runs away — the releaser is not liable because the running is the slave's choice. However, there is a dispute: Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that opening a birdcage causing the bird to fly makes the opener liable because the bird's flight is attributed to the opening (the bird's nature is to flee), while Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf consider it a condition like releasing a slave, so no liability. The author also discusses nuances: if the intervening act is attributed to the condition (like driving an animal), the liability follows the conditioner. The passage thus provides a detailed taxonomy of conditions relevant to legal liability in Hanafi jurisprudence.

Key Terms
شرط محض (shart mahd): A pure condition that is merely a prerequisite with no element of cause or reason; the legal effect is attributed to the subsequent utterance or act, not to the condition.
شرط له حكم العلة (shart lahu hukm al-illah): A condition that has the ruling of a cause; the intervening act is natural or attributed to the condition, thus the condition is treated like a cause for liability.
شرط له حكم الأسباب (shart lahu hukm al-asbab): A condition that has the ruling of a reason; a free agent's act intervenes and is not attributed to the condition, so no liability unless the act is attributed to the condition.

Fourth Type of Condition: Nominal vs Legal Condition in Divorce
Defines and illustrates the fourth type of shart with a divorce example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a nominal condition (shart isman) and a legal condition (shart hukman) and apply this distinction to determine the outcome of a conditional divorce.

Core Idea
In a sequence of two conditions, only the last condition is legally effective; the first is nominal.

This passage introduces a subtle but critical distinction in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: a condition that is a condition in name only (shart isman) versus one that is a condition in legal effect (shart hukman). The example of a conditional divorce clarifies how the last of two linked conditions determines the ruling, even if the first condition occurs outside of marriage.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions in the example
Read the husband's suspended statement: 'If you enter this house or this house, you are divorced.' The two conditions are entering the first house and entering the second house. Note that the sequence matters.
2
Classify each condition as nominal or legal
According to Abu Hanifa, the first condition that occurs is only nominal (shart isman) because the ruling (divorce) is attached to the last condition. The last condition is both nominal and legal (shart hukman).
3
Apply the rule to a scenario with interrupted ownership
Consider: wife enters first house after being divorced (no ownership), then remarries and enters second house. Since the last condition (second house) occurred during marriage, divorce takes effect. Ownership is only needed at suspension and at the occurrence of the last condition, not in between.
4
Contrast with Zufar's qiyas
Zufar analogizes the last condition to the first, arguing that if the first condition requires ownership, so does the last. Therefore, he would rule no divorce in the scenario. Identify the flaw: the ruling is not attached to the first condition; the analogy fails due to different legal attachment.
5
State the Hanafi rationale
The ruling is attached to the last condition existence-wise (idafat al-hukm ila akhir al-shartayn wujudan). Ownership is a condition for the suspension and for the effect, but not for intermediate events. This is why the first condition can occur outside ownership without affecting the outcome.

Fifth Type of Condition: Condition as a Pure Sign
Defines shart kal-'alamah and distinguishes it from true shart using the example of ihsan in zina

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a true condition (shart) and a condition that is a pure sign (alamah), and apply the dabita to identify conditional implications in legal statements.

Core Idea
A condition like ihsan in zina is a sign indicating a ruling, not a cause.

In Hanafi usul, not every description that accompanies a ruling is a true condition; some are mere signs. This lesson explores the fifth type of shart—a condition that is like a pure sign—using the classic example of ihsan. You will learn the principle (dabita) that distinguishes a true conditional statement from a mere descriptive indication, and why wording and definiteness matter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fifth type and its example
Note the definition: a condition that is like a pure sign (kal-'alamah al-khalisah). The example is ihsan (being married) as a condition for stoning in zina, but it is only a sign of the ruling, not a cause.
2
Understand the distinction between shart and alamah
A true condition (shart) is something upon which the existence of a ruling depends; a sign (alamah) merely indicates the ruling without being a cause. Ihsan is a sign that the punishment is stoning, not a condition that causes it.
The author of Al-Tawdih omitted this type from the divisions.
3
Learn the two ways a condition is recognized
First, by conditional wording (huruf al-shart) such as 'in' (إن). Second, by a description that implies conditionality when it falls on a nakirah (indefinite noun) that is not specified by pointing.
Example: 'The woman I marry is divorced thrice' is conditional because 'the woman' is a nakirah in the sense of unspecified.
4
Apply the principle: contrast with definite noun
If the description falls on a definite noun (mu'ayyan) like 'this woman', it does not imply conditionality because pointing is more definitive than description. Such a statement becomes immediate and void for a non-wife.
The description in a definite noun is redundant.
5
Analyze why ihsan is a sign, not a condition
Ihsan is a description that accompanies the ruling of stoning, but it does not cause the ruling. It is like a sign that indicates the severity of the crime. The dabita helps identify that it is not expressed with conditional wording or a descriptive clause on a nakirah.
This distinction prevents confusing legal signs with true conditions.

Ruling on Forgetfulness (Nisyan) and Its Exceptions
Defines nisyan and its exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define nisyan in Hanafi usul, explain the default rule that nisyan does not remove obligation, and apply the criterion of ghalabah to distinguish excused from non-excused forgetfulness in specific cases.

Core Idea
Forgetfulness in rights of Allah is excused only when predominantly caused; forgetfulness in rights of people is never excused.

In Hanafi usul, forgetfulness (nisyan) is a specific legal state that affects obligations and torts. This passage defines nisyan and establishes a critical distinction: while forgetfulness does not remove the obligation of acts of worship, it can excuse certain violations when it is 'predominant' (ghalib). We will examine the definition, the default rule, and the three exceptions where forgetfulness is excused, including a key intra-madhhab dispute on slaughter.

Guided Path
1
Define nisyan
Identify the definition from the text: unavoidable ignorance of what one knew, without a defect. Differentiate it from insanity, sleep, and unconsciousness.
The phrase 'without a defect' (la bi-afah) is crucial; nisyan is not due to a mental deficiency.
2
State the default rule
Nisyan does not negate obligation in the rights of Allah. If one forgets a prayer or fast, qada is still required.
This contrasts with intentional neglect; forgetfulness does not erase the duty.
3
Identify the exception: ghalabah
When forgetfulness is 'predominant' (ghalib), meaning there is no reminder (mudhakkir), it is excused (ma'fu) in certain contexts. The text lists three examples: fasting, tasmiyah on slaughter, and salam in prayer.
Ghalabah is determined by the natural circumstances (e.g., hunger, fear) that make forgetfulness overwhelmingly likely.
4
Analyze each exception
For eating/drinking while fasting, the natural inclination causes forgetfulness; for tasmiyah, fear during slaughter predominates; for salam, the similarity between first and second sitting leads to confusion. In all three, the act is not invalidated.
Note the tamarah al-khilaf with Shafi'i on tasmiyah: Hanafi holds meat is halal; Shafi'i holds it is haram.
5
Distinguish non-excused cases
Forgetfulness that is not predominant is not excused. Examples include speaking forgetfully during prayer (because the worshipper should be aware) and a pilgrim hunting forgetfully (because ihram serves as a reminder). Also, forgetfulness in rights of people (e.g., damaging property) is never excused.
The commentator adds these exclusions to clarify the boundaries of the rule.

Manumission and Third-Party Rights: Hanafi Talil and Analogy
Usul rule with talil and analogy to tadbir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that manumission affecting creditors' or heirs' rights is suspended like tadbir and explain the talil differentiating rights in possession versus person.

Core Idea
When a master's debt exceeds assets, freeing a slave is suspended until death—analogized to a mudabbar.

This passage from a usul text clarifies how the Hanafi school treats a manumission that would harm creditors or heirs. It explains that such a freeing is not immediately effective but is compared to a tadbir (conditional upon death). The text also addresses an apparent inconsistency: why is a pawned slave's freeing effective immediately? The answer lies in the nature of the right—whether it is attached to the slave's person or merely to the possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule
The text states that if a debtor frees a slave while his wealth is fully encumbered by debt or the slave's value exceeds one-third, the manumission does not take effect immediately. It is treated like a mudabbar.
2
Understand the talil (rationale)
The rule is based on the principle that the rights of creditors and heirs attach to the slave's person (raqaba). Since immediate freedom would eliminate those rights, the manumission is deferred until death.
3
Apply the analogy to tadbir
The mudabbar is a slave free only upon the master's death. Here, the situation is analogous because both involve a future contingency.
4
Contrast with the pawner's case
Unlike the debtor, the pawner's freeing is effective immediately because the pawnee's right is only in possession (yad), not in the slave's person. The pawner still owns the raqaba.
This distinction is key to the Hanafi talil.

Core Idea
Burial first, then debts, then bequests from one-third, then inheritance.

When a person dies insolvent, competing claims on the estate must be prioritized. The Hanafi school establishes a clear hierarchy based on the urgency of need: the deceased's immediate burial, then clearing liabilities, then fulfilling bequests (limited to one-third), and finally distributing inheritance. Inheritance is by succession (khilafa), which is automatic and without choice, unlike deputyship (niyaba).

Practice ensures mastery of the hierarchy.

(2) Debts – these come next because clearing the deceased's liability is more pressing. (3) Bequests (wasiyya) – these are executed from one-third of the remaining estate after burial and debts, as the need to fulfill the deceased's wishes for the Hereafter is stronger than the heirs' claim to that portion. (4) Inheritance – the remainder passes to the heirs by succession (khilafa). The passage also explains the difference between khilafa (succession without condition or choice) and niyaba (deputyship with condition and choice). The text further addresses special cases: if a creditor has a right attached to a specific property (e.g., mortgage), that takes precedence even over burial. Additionally, the contract of kitabah (manumission by payment) remains valid after the master's death, as the master's need for the wala (loyalty) and payment persists. The marginal notes clarify these points, e.g., giving precedence to burial over debt is analogous to giving precedence to a living person's clothing over debts.

Key Terms
التجهيز (al-tajhiz): Expenses for burial and shrouding of the deceased; given first priority from the estate.
الوصية (al-wasiyya): Bequest made by the deceased; executed from one-third of the estate after burial and debts.
الخلافة (al-khilafa): Succession; substitution of a person in place of another necessarily without condition or choice (as in inheritance).

Hazl (Jest) in Contracts: Hanafi Ruling on Intent and Price
Defines hazl and explains its effects in property and non-property transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between hazl in the amount versus the genus of price, and state the ruling for non-property declarations based on the hadith.

Core Idea
Jest in contract price amount is valid; jest in price genus voids the sale; divorce, emancipation, and oaths bind even in jest.

The Hanafi school carefully distinguishes between jest that affects the validity of a contract and jest that does not. The key lies in whether the jest touches the amount (qadr) of the price or its genus (jins), and whether the transaction involves property or non-property matters. This lesson unpacks the definition of hazl and its legal effects, drawing on the seminal ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of hazl
Determine whether the collusion (muwada'a) involves jest in the amount (e.g., naming 2000 but intending 1000) or in the genus (e.g., naming dirhams but intending dinars).
2
Classify the transaction type
Is the contract a property transaction (sale) or a non-property matter (divorce, emancipation, oath)? The ruling differs fundamentally.
Non-property matters are governed by the hadith: 'Three things are serious whether done in earnest or in jest.'
3
Apply the Hanafi ruling
For property: if hazl is in the amount, the contract is valid for the actual price (remaining amount). If in the genus, the contract is invalid unless a genuine price is named. For non-property: the act is effective despite hazl.
The ikhtilaf with the Sahibayn concerns the genus case: they hold the contract invalid per qiyas, but Abu Hanifa rules it valid by istihsan.
4
Note the practical consequence (thamarah)
The difference in opinion leads to different outcomes: under Abu Hanifa's view, the parties must correct the price; under Sahibayn, the contract is void and must be re-contracted.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the definition of hazl (jest) and its conditions. It first addresses the ruling when parties jokingly agree on a price but are serious about the contract. The commentary includes a discussion of the hadith's authenticity, noting that some scholars consider it weak. Four Types of Prohibitions Under Coercion
Classification of prohibitions under coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any prohibited act under coercion into one of four Hanafi categories and state the ruling for each category.

Core Idea
Not all prohibitions are equal under coercion: some remain absolute, some fall completely, some permit concession, and some remain despite possibility of lifting.

In this passage, the author (al-Sanbali) expands on the classification of prohibitions (hurmah) under coercion. Building on the earlier division of coercion into obligatory, prohibitive, permissible, and concessional, he now reclassifies the very prohibition of the coerced act. Understanding these four types is crucial for a jurist to correctly apply rulings when a person is compelled to perform a forbidden act. We will examine each type with its example and rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first type: absolute prohibition that never lifts.
This type of prohibition remains entirely intact under coercion; the act is never allowed. Example: zina with a woman. The reason is the irreversible harm to lineage.
Even under threat of death, it is forbidden.
2
Identify the second type: prohibition that completely falls away under coercion.
This type becomes permissible when coerced or in necessity, such as eating carrion or drinking wine. The Quranic verse (6:119) provides the basis: 'except that to which you are compelled.'
This falls under 'obligatory coercion' (ikrah al-fard).
3
Identify the third type: prohibition that does not fall but allows a concession (rukhsah).
The act remains prohibited in essence, but one is permitted to do it to avoid greater harm, like uttering words of disbelief under threat of death.
The concession does not remove the sin entirely but excuses it.
4
Identify the fourth type: prohibition that could potentially be lifted but is not lifted under coercion.
Unlike the second type, this prohibition remains even when coerced, such as taking another's property in dire need without permission. Although the prohibition could be lifted if the owner consents, coercion alone does not justify it.
The difference lies in the presence of an alternative or the nature of the right.

(القمر) وقيل: هذا في زنا الرجل بالإكراه، وأما إذا كانت المرأة مكرهة بالزنا يُرخص لها في ذلك؛ إذ ليس في التمكين معنى قتل الولد الذي هو المانع من الترخص في جانب الرجل؛ لأن نسب الولد عنها لا ينقطع، ولهذا سقط الإثم عنها. وقتل المسلم فإن حرمته لا تنكشف؛ لأن دليل الرخصة خوف تلف النفس والعضو، والمكره والمكرة عليه في ذلك سواء، فلا ينبغي للمكره أن يُتلف نفس أحد أو عضوه لأجل سلامة نفسه أو عضوه، فصار الإكراه في حكم العدم، فكأنه قتله بلا إكراه، فيحرم. المكره وحرمة تحتمل السقوط أصلًا بعذر الإكراه ،وغيره وتصير حلال الاستعمال، فهو داخل في الإكراه الفرض، كحرمة الخمر والميتة ولحم الخنزير، فإن حرمة هذه الأشياء إنما تثبت بالنص حالة الاختيار لا حالة الاضطرار، قال الله تعالى: ﴿وَقَدْ فَصَّلَ لَكُمْ مَا حَرَّمَ عَلَيْكُمْ إِلَّا مَا اضْطُرِرْتُمْ إلَيْهِ﴾، فحالة المخمصة والإكراه مستثناة عن ذلك. وحرمة لا تحتمل السقوط، لكنها تحتمل الرخصة كإجراء كلمة الكفر، فإنه قبيح لذاته، وحرمته غير ساقطة، لكنه يترخّص في حالة الإكراه بإجرائها، فهو داخل في قسم الرخصة. وحرمة تحتمل السقوط لكنها لم تسقط بعذر الإكراه وإن احتملت الرخصة أيضًا كتناول المضطر مال الغير، فإنه حرام بالنص، يحتمل سقوط حرمته وقت الإذن، ولكنها لم تسقط بعذر الإكراه، في التمكين: أي تمكين المرأة رجلاً بالزنا. (القمر) في الإكراه الفرض: أي في العمل بالإكراه الذي كان فرضًا. (القمر) قال الله تعالى: في قوله: ﴿ حُرِّمَتْ عَلَيْكُمُ الْمَيْتَةُ وَالدَّمُ (المائدة: ٣) الآية ﴿ إِلَّا مَا اضْطُرِرْتُمْ إِلَيْهِ ﴾ (القمر) فحالة المخمصة : هو خلو البطن من الغذاء، يقال: "رجل" خميص البطن" إذا كان طاويًا خاليًا، كذا في "معالم التنزيل". (القمر) في قسم الرخصة: أي العمل بالإكراه صار رخصة. (القمر)
Then, when the author first divided coercion into obligatory, prohibitive, permissible, and concessional, now he divides the prohibition of the coerced act into four categories under another heading, although the outcome of the two divisions is one. He said: Explanation of the types of prohibitions of the coerced act. First of the four: A prohibition that is never lifted and admits no concession, like zina with a woman, for it is never allowed under any excuse of coercion, because it involves corruption of the marital bed and loss of lineage, since the child of zina is legally lost, as the mother is not obligated to support him, nor is the fornicator required to discipline or support him. So it falls under prohibitive coercion for the coerced, and the coercer is exempted and attached to the tool due to the corruption of his choice by full coercion, as he is compelled in this act. Man is naturally inclined to love life, so when threatened with death by the coercer saying, 'Kill so-and-so and destroy his property or I will kill you,' and he seeks to save himself from destruction by committing murder or destroying property even though it is forbidden, his choice is corrupted in this manner. (al-Sanbali) According to Abu Hanifa... (al-Qamar) And it is said: This concerns the zina of a man under coercion. As for a woman coerced into zina, she is granted a concession, because there is no meaning of killing a child in her submission, which is the obstacle to concession on the man's side, for her lineage from the child is not severed, so sin is removed from her. And killing a Muslim: Its prohibition is not lifted, because the evidence for concession is fear of loss of life or limb, and the coercer and the coerced are equal in that. So the coerced should not destroy another's life or limb for the sake of his own safety, thus coercion is considered non-existent, as if he killed without coercion, so it is forbidden. The coerced [is forbidden]. And a prohibition that can altogether fall away due to the excuse of coercion and other [reasons] and becomes permissible to use, so it falls under obligatory coercion, like the prohibition of wine, carrion, and pork, for these are prohibited by text in the case of choice, not in the case of necessity. Allah said: 'He has detailed for you what He has forbidden to you except that to which you are compelled.' So the state of starvation and coercion is excepted from that. And a prohibition that does not admit of falling away but admits of concession, like uttering the word of disbelief, for it is intrinsically evil, and its prohibition is not lifted, but one is granted a concession to utter it under coercion, so it falls under the category of concession. And a prohibition that admits of falling away but does not fall away under the excuse of coercion even though it also admits of concession, like the distressed person taking another's property, for it is forbidden by text, its prohibition could be lifted at the time of permission, but it is not lifted under coercion.

Commentary
This passage categorizes the types of prohibited acts under coercion into four. The first type is a prohibition that cannot be lifted under any circumstance, such as adultery (zina) with a woman. Even under threat of death, it remains forbidden because of the severe consequences: mixing of lineage and the child being legally fatherless. For the man, no concession is given. However, if a woman is coerced into zina, there is a concession because the child's lineage from her is not lost. The second type is a prohibition that is completely lifted under coercion, such as consuming carrion, wine, or pork when forced. This falls under 'obligatory coercion' (ikrah al-fard) because the Shari'a permits it out of necessity, as indicated by the Quranic exception (Quran 6:119). The third type is a prohibition that is not lifted but allows a concession, like uttering words of disbelief (kufr) under duress. While the act remains prohibited in essence, one is allowed to utter them to save one's life. The fourth type is a prohibition that could be lifted (like taking another's property in dire need) but is not lifted under coercion alone; such taking remains unlawful even if the person is forced. This classification helps understand how coercion interacts with different legal prohibitions.

Key Terms
حرمة (hurmah): A prohibition that may be subject to different rulings under coercion.
رخصة (rukhsah): A concession that allows an otherwise prohibited act under duress.
مخمصة (makhmasah): Starvation; a state of extreme hunger that triggers necessity.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school classifies prohibitions under coercion into four types: absolute, falling away, concessional, and remaining but liftable, each with distinct rulings based on the underlying harm and textual evidence.
