Definition of 'Aql (Intellect) and its Role in Ahliyya (Legal Capacity)
Defining intellect and its role in legal capacity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'aql according to the Hanafi school and explain why it is essential for legal capacity (ahliyya).

Core Idea
Without intellect, divine address is meaningless — intellect is the key to legal capacity.

This passage from Noor al-Anwaar transitions from discussing the appurtenances of rulings to the subject of legal capacity (ahliyya). It establishes that intellect ('aql) is the foundational prerequisite for being a mukallaf. We will explore the definition of 'aql from the Hanafi and majority perspectives, and understand why it is indispensable for the obligation of shari'ah rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of legal capacity
The passage begins by discussing the mahkum alayh, i.e., the mukallaf. Understand that ahliyya attaches to persons.
The mukallaf is the one who is addressed by divine law.
2
Recognize the necessity of intellect
The text states that ahliyya is not without 'aql. This is the core premise.
3
Analyze the definition of 'aql
According to the majority, 'aql is a power for perceiving universals, located in brain or heart. The Hanafis describe it as a light that guides from the senses.
The Hanafi definition uses the metaphor of 'light' (nur).
4
Derive the legal implication
Without 'aql, the mukallaf cannot understand the divine address. Addressing one who does not understand is repugnant (qabih), so 'aql is a condition for obligation.
This is a talil (causal reasoning) linking 'aql to taklif.
5
Contrast with other views
Note the difference between the philosophers' and usul scholars' view on the locus of 'aql, but the commonality in its necessity for capacity.
The disagreement on locus does not affect the legal conclusion.

Commentary
This passage transitions from discussing the appurtenances of legal rulings (such as cause, condition, sign) to the subject of legal capacity (ahliyya). The text emphasizes that intellect ('aql) is fundamental for legal capacity because the mukallaf (legally responsible person) must understand the divine address (khitab). Without understanding, address is considered improper. The commentary (from al-Qamar and al-Sunbuli) clarifies technical points: that previous discussion about witness liability is revisited as an example, and then definitions of 'aql are provided. The majority view defines 'aql as a power (quwwa) for perceiving universals, located in the brain (philosophers) or heart (usul scholars). The Hanafi school describes it as a 'light' that guides from the senses. This lesson underscores that legal capacity depends on rational comprehension, a foundational principle in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Key Terms
الأهلية (al-ahliyya): Legal capacity; the fitness of the mukallaf for the obligation of legislated rights.
العقل (al-'aql): Intellect; a power for perceiving universals; considered a light (nur) that guides from the senses.
المكلف (al-mukallaf): The person who is addressed by divine law; the subject of legal capacity.

The Two Types of Capacity for Performance: Deficient and Complete
Defines and categorizes legal capacity for performance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define legal capacity for performance (ahlíyya al-ada') and distinguish between its deficient and complete forms, explaining the roles of intellect and body.

Core Idea
Capacity for performance is either deficient (valid acts, no obligation) or complete (acts obligatory).

This passage from Noor al-Anwar introduces the fundamental Hanafi concept of legal capacity (ahlíyya). After distinguishing capacity for obligation from capacity for performance, the focus shifts to the latter, which is further divided based on the perfection or deficiency of intellect and body. Understanding this division is essential for grasping why a minor's acts of worship can be valid without being obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of legal capacity
Recognize that Hanafi usul divides legal capacity into ahlíyya al-wujub (capacity for obligation) and ahlíyya al-ada' (capacity for performance).
This passage focuses on the latter.
2
Focus on capacity for performance and its sub-types
Note that ahlíyya al-ada' is further divided into deficient (qasira) and complete (kāmila).
3
Understand what deficient capacity is based on
Deficient capacity depends on deficient intellect and/or body. Examples include an intelligent child (weak body) and an adult imbecile (weak intellect).
Both capacities must be present for complete capacity.
4
Understand what complete capacity is based on
Complete capacity requires both complete intellect and complete body, typically attained at puberty.
5
Recognize the consequence of deficient capacity
With deficient capacity, acts performed are valid (sahih) but not obligatory. Obligation requires complete capacity.
This is key for the first category of rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the concept of legal capacity (ahlíyya) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It begins by distinguishing two types of capacity: capacity for obligation (ahlíyya al-wujub) and capacity for performance (ahlíyya al-ada'). The focus here is on the latter. The author explains that capacity for performance is further divided into deficient (qasira) and complete (kāmila). Deficient capacity depends on deficient intellect and body, as seen in an intelligent child whose body is weak or an adult imbecile whose intellect is weak. The key point is that if a person with deficient capacity performs an act (like prayer or faith), it is valid even though it wasn't obligatory. For a newborn (mawlūd), there is no obligation at all—they cannot be held liable for worship or punishments because they lack both capacities. The commentary underscores that for worship, intention is required, which a child cannot do, while for financial obligations like 'ushr and kharāj, intention is not needed. The second part of the passage discusses the six categories of rulings based on capacity. The first category is the right of Allah that is purely good and cannot be removed, such as faith (īmān). The ruling is that a minor's faith (if they are intelligent) is valid and acceptable but not obligatory. This is supported by the famous statement of Ali (RA) claiming he embraced Islam before reaching puberty. The text then addresses the obligation of performance after puberty—when capacity becomes complete, the rulings become binding. The commentary includes a discussion on the authenticity of the report from Ali, citing various narrations about his age at conversion. The overall reasoning (ta'līl) is that obligating an act before full capacity would cause hardship (haraj), which is negated in Islamic law. The principle of istihsan (juristic preference) is also invoked to validate the minor's faith despite the lack of obligation.

Key Terms
أهلية (ahlíyya): Legal capacity: the quality by which a person becomes suitable for receiving obligations and performing transactions.
أهلية أداء قاصرة (ahlíyya al-ada' al-qasira): Deficient capacity for performance: based on deficient intellect or body; allows validity of acts but not obligation.
أهلية أداء كاملة (ahlíyya al-ada' al-kāmila): Complete capacity for performance: based on complete intellect and body; makes acts obligatory and address binding.
الحسن المحض (al-hasan al-mahd): Pure good: an act that is entirely beneficial and cannot lose its goodness, like faith.

The First Category: Purely Good Acts and the Ruling on a Minor's Faith
Explains the first category of rulings: purely good acts and the validity of a minor's faith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of purely good acts (ḥasan maḥḍ) to rule that a minor's faith is valid but not obligatory, and explain the reasoning using istiḥsān.

Core Idea
A minor's faith is valid and accepted because it is purely beneficial and cannot be harmed.

This passage continues by dividing rulings into six categories, the first of which concerns acts that are purely good (ḥasan maḥḍ) and admit no bad. The quintessential example is faith (īmān). Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the concept of legal capacity (ahlíyya) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It begins by distinguishing two types of capacity: capacity for obligation (ahlíyya al-wujub) and capacity for performance (ahlíyya al-ada'). The focus here is on the latter. The author explains that capacity for performance is further divided into deficient (qasira) and complete (kāmila). Deficient capacity depends on deficient intellect and body, as seen in an intelligent child whose body is weak or an adult imbecile whose intellect is weak. The key point is that if a person with deficient capacity performs an act (like prayer or faith), it is valid even though it wasn't obligatory. For a newborn (mawlūd), there is no obligation at all—they cannot be held liable for worship or punishments because they lack both capacities. The commentary underscores that for worship, intention is required, which a child cannot do, while for financial obligations like 'ushr and kharāj, intention is not needed. The second part of the passage discusses the six categories of rulings based on capacity. The first category is the right of Allah that is purely good and cannot be removed, such as faith (īmān). The ruling is that a minor's faith (if they are intelligent) is valid and acceptable but not obligatory. This is supported by the famous statement of Ali (RA) claiming he embraced Islam before reaching puberty. The text then addresses the obligation of performance after puberty—when capacity becomes complete, the rulings become binding. The commentary includes a discussion on the authenticity of the report from Ali, citing various narrations about his age at conversion. The overall reasoning (ta'līl) is that obligating an act before full capacity would cause hardship (haraj), which is negated in Islamic law. The principle of istihsan (juristic preference) is also invoked to validate the minor's faith despite the lack of obligation.

The Legal Capacity of a Ma'tuh: Definition, Repetition, and Liability

Core Idea
A ma'tuh is like a child: his worship is valid but he lacks contractual liability; for intermittent insanity, missed prayers drop after six, fasting after a full month.

This lesson from Noor al-Anwar defines 'atah (imbecility) as an affliction causing a defect in intellect, distinguishing it from full insanity. It also explains the Hanafi rules for makeup of worship when a person suffers intermittent insanity, introducing the key concept of takrār (repetition) which can waive qada. Understanding these rulings is essential for knowing when a person is obligated to perform or make up acts of worship.

Guided Path
1
Define 'atah and its legal effect
Read the definition of 'atah: an affliction causing a defect in intellect. The person's speech and actions mix sanity and insanity. Legally, it is treated like childhood in many respects.

2
Identify what is valid for the ma'tuh
Acts of worship (prayer, fasting), acceptance of Islam, and acceptance of a gift are valid from the ma'tuh, just as from a child.
This shows that not all capacity is removed.

The ma'tuh cannot perform contractual acts that impose liability (like divorce, sale, purchase) without a guardian, because his dhimma lacks 'uhdah.
'Uhdah is the capacity to bear contractual obligations and their harmful consequences.

Despite lacking 'uhdah, the ma'tuh must compensate for property he destroys. This is not 'uhdah but jabar (compensation) for the violation of protected property.
This is derived from the inviolability of property, not from contractual capacity.
5

For a person who becomes sane after a period of insanity, the duty to make up missed prayers depends on whether the missed prayers exceed six (a day and night), which constitutes repetition causing hardship. For fasting, the full month is the threshold.
There is ikhtilaf on the exact calculation; the relied-upon view is given.

Statement of legal capacity. Statement of 'atah (imbecility) after puberty. It (the obligation of makeup) is not dropped from him, and with consideration of hours according to them (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) such that if he became insane before noon, then became sane on the second day after noon, there is no makeup upon him according to them, because in terms of hours it is more than a day and a night. The two shaykhs (?) say there is makeup upon him unless it extends to the time of 'Asr until the prayers become six, thus entering the limit of repetition. (Commentary: meaning repetition of prayer) And according to him (Muhammad?) And concerning fasting: it is (conditional) upon encompassing the month, such that if he becomes sane in any part of the month, by night or day, makeup is obligatory upon him in the apparent narration. And from Shams al-A'immah al-Halwani: if he was sane on the first night of Ramadan, then became insane at dawn, then encompassed the rest of the month, makeup is not obligatory upon him, and that is correct, because the night is not fasted in, so sanity and insanity are equal therein. And if he became sane on a day of Ramadan, i.e., at the time of intention, if it was before noon, makeup is required; if after noon, it is not required in the correct view. And concerning zakat: upon encompassing the year, because it does not enter the limit of repetition unless the second year enters. And likewise for hajj. (Commentary: i.e., zakat) And Abu Yusuf says for him: he has established most of the year in place of the whole, for facilitation and to remove difficulty for the obligated person. [Statement of 'atah after puberty] And 'atah after puberty, it is an affliction that causes a defect in the intellect, so its possessor becomes... (Commentary: i.e., his statement: minority) ... The commentator says: ... (long commentary about repetition, time, etc.) ... Statement of 'atah after puberty: His speech is mixed; some of his speech resembles that of sane people and some resembles that of insane people. He is also like a child. Likewise his actions are mixed. (Commentary: plural of majnun) In the presence of the basis of intellect and the possibility of defect as he said, he is like a child with respect to intellect in all rulings, such that it does not prevent the validity of speech and action. So his acts of worship, his Islam, his valid sale of another's property, his freeing of his slave are valid, and acceptance of a gift is valid from him as it is valid from a child. But it prevents 'uhdah (liability), so divorce is not valid (Commentary: i.e., of another's slave woman), nor freeing his slave at all, nor his sale nor purchase without the guardian's permission, nor is he demanded in agency to deliver the sold item, nor is it returned to him due to defect, nor is he ordered to litigation. Then it was raised: if that is the case, should the ma'tuh not be held liable for damages of what he consumed from wealth? He answered by saying: (Commentary: i.e., by sale) Likewise it should be. As for damages of what he consumed from wealth, it is not 'uhdah, and his being a child, slave, or ma'tuh does not contradict the inviolability of the property, meaning the protected thing, and its inviolability has not ceased because the consumer is a child or ma'tuh, unlike the rights of God. That damages of wealth is not by way of 'uhdah, but rather by way of compensation for what he wasted of wealth, i.e., the ma'tuh. (Commentary: i.e., as a recompense for actions, like adultery) For its liability is only obligatory as a recompense for actions, not for the substance, and it is dependent on completion of intellect. Commentary
This passage is a definition and ruling on the condition of 'atah (imbecility) after puberty, detailing its effect on legal capacity (ahliyyah). The author begins by stating that one who becomes insane after puberty is not absolved from making up missed prayers if the insanity lasts less than a day and a night by hours; Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider the hours, so if he was insane before noon and became sane the next day after noon, no makeup is required. The two shaykhs (?) require makeup unless the prayers become six. For fasting, covering the entire month is considered: if he becomes sane during any part, makeup is required in the apparent narration. Al-Halwani corrects this: if he was sane the first night and insane at dawn, then covered the rest, no makeup needed. For zakat, the condition is covering the whole year because it does not fall under repetition unless the second year enters. Abu Yusuf considers most of the year sufficient to ease hardship. Then the passage defines 'atah as a defect in intellect that makes its possessor like a child: his speech and actions are mixed between sanity and insanity. He has some intellect but with defect. In rulings, he is like a child in many ways: his acts of worship and Islam are valid, but he cannot beheld liable (العهدة) for contractual obligations—so his divorce, freeing of a slave, buying/selling without guardian are invalid. However, he is liable for damages because that is compensation for property destruction, not contractual liability. The address of taklif (obligation) is removed from him, so acts of worship are not obligatory, though later scholars say they are precautionary. This follows the Hanafi position that 'atah impedes 'uhdah but not all capacity.

Key Terms
العته (al-'atah): An affliction that causes a defect in the intellect, making the person's speech and actions mixed between sanity and insanity.
العهدة (al-'uhdah): Liability or responsibility for contractual obligations and their harmful consequences (مضرة). A person with a valid 'uhdah can be held to contracts, debts, etc.
التكرار (al-takrār): Repetition of an act of worship across multiple time periods causing hardship; used to determine when makeup (قضاء) is waived for a person with intermittent insanity.

Ruling on 'Atah (Imbecility) After Puberty
Defines 'atah and its effect on legal guardianship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-'atah, state its ruling on guardianship, and apply the analogy to a minor using Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
An imbecile after puberty is legally like a minor: under guardianship, not a guardian.

In Hanafi usul, the category of mahkum alayh includes persons with varying degrees of legal capacity. This passage defines 'atah (imbecility) that occurs after puberty and clarifies its effect on guardianship. It establishes that the imbecile is legally akin to a minor, requiring a guardian and unable to act as a guardian over others.

Commentary
This passage defines the legal condition of 'atah (imbecility) that occurs after puberty. The ruling is that such a person is treated like a minor: he must have a guardian appointed over him for his own welfare. He cannot exercise guardianship over others, including for marriage, discipline, or managing orphans' wealth, because he lacks complete intellectual capacity. The commentator (al-Qamar) adds that guardianship over him is established for another person, and he cannot have guardianship over anyone since he lacks it over himself.

Sleep in Usul al-Fiqh: Definition and Legal Effects
Definition and effects of sleep on the mukallaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi legal definition of sleep, explain its components, and apply its effects on obligation, legal utterances, and liability.

Core Idea
Sleep delays performance but preserves obligation; it nullifies utterances but imposes full liability for harm.

In usul al-Fiqh, precise definitions govern the application of rulings. The passage defines sleep (al-nawm) as a natural involuntary inability to use power while the intellect remains. This definition drives three key legal consequences: obligation is delayed but not removed, legal utterances are invalid due to lack of choice, and liability for damage to others remains intact. Understanding this definition helps us differentiate between sleep and unconsciousness, and clarifies the scope of the mukallaf's responsibility.

Guided Path
1
Deconstruct the Definition
Identify the four components of sleep: inability to use power, natural state, involuntariness, and remaining intellect. Note how each component shapes the legal assessment.
The commentator specifies 'natural' to exclude unconsciousness (ighma').
2
Understand the Delay of Obligation
Recognize that sleep causes ta'khir al-khitab (delay of address) but not removal of wujub (obligation). The sleeper is not sinful during sleep but must make up missed acts.
Contrast with the ruling for insanity, which removes obligation entirely.
3
Analyze the Negation of Choice
Sleep negates choice (ikhtiyar), so all legal utterances (divorce, manumission, conversion, apostasy) are void. The sleeper's statements have no effect in religion or court.
This applies even if the utterance harms someone.
4
Apply the Liability Rule
Sleep does not exempt from liability for damage to property or life. If a sleeping person turns over and destroys something or causes death, he must compensate.
The rationale: rights of people (huquq al-'ibad) are inviolable.

The Legal Definition and Ruling on Unconsciousness (Ighma')
Defines unconsciousness and distinguishes it from sleep and insanity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define unconsciousness (ighma') in Hanafi law, distinguish it from sleep and insanity, and state its effect on wudu and prayer.

Core Idea
Unconsciousness always invalidates wudu because it severely weakens choice, unlike sleep which only does so in certain positions.

Unconsciousness (ighma') is a legal state that resembles both sleep and insanity but has distinct rulings. The Hanafi school defines it as a weakening of faculties without loss of intellect, which affects the validity of acts like wudu and prayer. Understanding its precise definition is essential for deriving correct fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Definition of Ighma'
Examine the text's description: 'a type of illness and loss of strength that weakens the faculties and does not remove intelligence (hija).' Note that it retains intellect, unlike insanity.
The definition is crucial for distinguishing it from sleep and insanity.
2
Compare with Sleep and Insanity
Sleep removes choice but not intellect; insanity removes intellect. Ighma' weakens faculties and intensively removes choice, but intellect remains. This impacts rulings on wudu and the validity of expressions.
Prophets are protected from insanity but not from ighma', as shown by the Prophet's illness.
3
Determine the Ruling on Wudu
Since ighma' involves severe relaxation of limbs, the probability of emission of impurities is high. Thus, it is a hadath in all positions, unlike sleep which only invalidates wudu when lying, leaning, or supported.
The text explicitly states: 'it is an event (hadath) in every case, whether lying down, leaning, standing, etc.'
4
Distinguish the Ruling on Prayer Makeup
If ighma' is not prolonged (i.e., short duration), it is treated like sleep: missed prayers must be made up. If prolonged, it is treated like insanity: no makeup required due to complete lack of choice.
Fakhr al-Islam is cited for this distinction.
5
Note the Effect on Expressions
Like sleep, expressions of divorce, emancipation, Islam, and apostasy are invalid during ighma' because the person lacks choice. However, ighma' is more severe than sleep in loss of choice.
This is based on the principle that valid expressions require deliberate choice.

Commentary
This passage defines unconsciousness (ighma') and distinguishes it from both sleep and insanity. Like sleep, expressions of divorce, emancipation, etc., are invalid during ighma'. Regarding prayer: if ighma' is short (not prolonged), it is treated like sleep, so the person must make up missed prayers. The note also mentions that prophets are protected from insanity but not from ighma', citing the Prophet's unconsciousness in his illness.

Core Idea
Equality in the soul suffices for qisas; additional dignities do not affect the ruling.

Ignorance of the New Muslim and the Preemptor as Legal Excuses
Commentary on two cases of excusable ignorance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that ignorance is excused only when a genuine obstacle to knowledge exists, specifically for the new Muslim in dar al-harb and the preemptor unaware of a sale.

Core Idea
Ignorance excuses only when obtaining knowledge was impossible, not when accessible.

This lesson examines two specific cases of ignorance from the passage: the new Muslim living in dar al-harb who is unaware of Islamic rulings, and the preemptor (shafi') who does not know about a sale. In both, the Hanafi school treats their ignorance as a valid excuse for their silence or inaction because they faced a genuine barrier to acquiring knowledge. Understanding these foundational cases illustrates the key principle that ignorance is not automatically excused; its validity depends on whether the person had means to learn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
Read the Arabic passage and identify the two scenarios: (1) the new Muslim who converts in dar al-harb and is ignorant of Islamic rulings, (2) the preemptor who is ignorant of the sale. Note the legal consequence for each: the new Muslim must make up prayers and fasts from the time of Islam (not before), and the preemptor's silence does not invalidate his preemption right until he knows.
The new Muslim's case is mentioned first; the preemptor is said to be attached (yulhaq) to it.
2
Analyze the rationale (ta'lil)
For the new Muslim, the reason is that he was in dar al-harb where learning Islamic rulings was difficult or impossible. For the preemptor, the rationale is that he lacked knowledge of the sale, and silence before knowledge cannot be construed as waiver.
The underlying principle: ignorance is an excuse when there is an obstacle to learning.
3
Identify the condition for excuse validity
Note that the excuse holds only as long as the obstacle persists. Once the preemptor knows of the sale, silence no longer excuses and the right of preemption is lost. Similarly, once the new Muslim enters dar al-Islam or gains access to knowledge, ignorance ceases to be an excuse.
Silence after knowledge is a waiver.
4
Distinguish from negligence
Contrast these cases with situations where ignorance is not excused, such as a Muslim in dar al-Islam who fails to learn basic obligations. The passage implies that negligence negates the excuse.
The key distinction: obstacle vs. negligence.

Ignorance of the Slave Woman and the Virgin Regarding Marriage Options
Commentary on ignorance of slave woman and virgin as excuses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that ignorance of the married slave woman about her emancipation and the virgin about her marriage by a guardian are excused due to occupation or hidden dependency, and distinguish when such ignorance is not excused.

Core Idea
Occupation with service or reliance on the guardian excuses ignorance of legal options.

This lesson covers two more cases of excusable ignorance from the passage: the married slave woman who is emancipated but does not know about her right to choose to stay or leave her husband, and the virgin (minor) who is married by a guardian (other than father/grandfather) and reaches puberty unaware of the marriage. The Hanafi school excuses their ignorance because the slave woman is busy serving her master and might not be informed, and the virgin's marriage was transacted by a guardian who acts independently. However, if they knew of the underlying facts but not the legal option, their ignorance is not excused in an Islamic land.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
Find the two scenarios in the passage: (1) the emancipated slave woman who does not know of her emancipation or the option to choose; (2) the virgin who is married by a guardian other than father or grandfather and does not know at puberty that she has a choice to annul the marriage.
Both involve a khiyar (option) upon emancipation or puberty.
2
Analyze the rationale for granting the excuse
For the slave woman: the master acts independently in emancipation and may not inform her; also she is busy with service. For the virgin: the guardian acts independently (mustaqill) and the minor may not be informed. In both, ignorance is due to a real or presumed obstacle to knowledge.
The obstacle is the hidden nature of the act (khafa' al-dalil) or occupation (shughl bi khidma).
3
Identify the condition for the excuse to cease
If the slave woman knows of the emancipation but does not know the legal option, it's not excused (in dar al-Islam) because she could have learned. Similarly, if the virgin knows of the marriage but not the option, she is negligent.
Knowledge of the fact removes the obstacle; ignorance of the law is not excused when the means to learn exist.
4
Note the exception for father/grandfather
The passage mentions that if the father or grandfather marries the virgin to a compatible with proper dower, she has no option at all due to their complete compassion. This is an important boundary case.
This is not ignorance-based excuse but a substantive rule.

Ignorance of the Agent and Authorized Slave Regarding Authorization and Revocation
Commentary on ignorance of agent and authorized slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the ignorance of an agent or authorized slave about their appointment or revocation is a valid excuse, affecting whether their actions bind the principal/master.

Core Idea
An agent's action before knowledge of agency binds the principal; before knowledge of revocation, it binds the agent.

This lesson focuses on the ignorance of an agent (wakil) and a slave authorized to trade (ma'dhun) regarding their authorization or its revocation. The passage states that if they act before knowing of their agency, their actions are not binding on the principal/master – they are personally liable. Conversely, if they act before knowing of revocation (i.e., they think they still have authority), their actions bind the principal/master. This distinction protects both parties: the agent is not forced to bear loss from an unknown agency, and the principal is not harmed by actions taken after revocation but before notice reaches the agent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Two situations: (1) the agent/authorized slave acts before knowing they were appointed (ignorance of agency); (2) they act before knowing they were dismissed (ignorance of revocation).
The passage uses 'جهل الوكيل والمأذون بالإطلاق وضده' – ignorance of authorization and its opposite (revocation).
2
Determine the ruling for each scenario
In scenario 1 (ignorance of agency): their action does not bind the principal/master; the agent is personally responsible (e.g., if goods spoil). In scenario 2 (ignorance of revocation): their action binds the principal/master; the agent is not personally liable.
The reasoning is repelling harm: in the first case, the agent would suffer harm if bound; in the second, the principal would suffer if the agent's action after revocation were invalid.
3
Understand the underlying rationale
The ignorance is excused because the agent/authorized slave had no way to know of the authorization/revocation without notification. The rule balances risk: when acting without authority (unknown), the agent bears risk; when acting with apparent authority (unknown revocation), the principal bears risk.
This is based on the principle of avoiding harm (darar).
4
Contrast with other cases in the passage
Compare this to the earlier cases of ignorance (new Muslim, preemptor, slave woman). Here, the excuse operates differently: it determines upon whom the contract is binding, not merely whether a penalty is excused.
The common thread: ignorance is an excuse when there is a genuine obstacle to knowledge (here, lack of notification).

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the types of ignorance and intoxication that serve as legal excuses in Hanafi jurisprudence, specifically within the discussion of legal capacity (ahliyyah). The author lists several cases where ignorance is a valid excuse: the ignorance of a new Muslim in dar al-harb about Islamic rulings (requiring makeup of prayer and fasting from the time of conversion), the ignorance of a preemptor (shafi') about the sale of a property (his silence does not invalidate his right of preemption until he learns of the sale), the ignorance of a married slave woman about her emancipation or the option to choose (she is excused because she is busy serving her master and may not be informed), and the ignorance of a virgin about having been married by a guardian other than father or grandfather (she has an option upon puberty, and ignorance is excused if she did not know of the marriage itself, but not if she knew of the marriage but not of the option, because in an Islamic land she could have learned). The ignorance of an agent or authorized slave about their agency/permission or its revocation/interdiction is also an excuse; if they act before knowledge, their actions are not binding on the principal/master if they were ignorant of the agency, but are binding if they were ignorant of the revocation. Finally, intoxication (sukr) is treated: if it results from a permissible substance (like medicine according to early scholars, or from coercion or necessity), it is like unconsciousness – it prevents the validity of divorce, manumission, and other transactions. However, later scholars consider substances like hashish and opium problematic, with some ruling that they are prohibited and intoxication from them does not excuse. The commentary explains the reasoning behind each rule, emphasizing that ignorance is only excused when there is a genuine obstacle to knowledge, not due to negligence.

Intoxication from Permissible Sources as a Legal Excuse
Commentary on intoxication as an excuse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that intoxication from a permissible cause (e.g., medicine, coercion) renders one like an unconscious person, invalidating divorce, manumission, and other transactions, and distinguish this from intoxication from prohibited substances.

Core Idea
Permissibly intoxicated persons are like unconscious: their statements have no legal effect.

This final subsection of the passage addresses intoxication (sukr) as an excuse. The Hanafi school distinguishes between intoxication from permissible causes (e.g., drinking a medicinal intoxicant like henbane/opium according to early scholars, or wine under coercion/necessity) and from prohibited causes. In the first case, the intoxicant is treated like unconsciousness (ighma) – it prevents the validity of divorce, manumission, and all other transactions. The rationale is that such intoxication is not a form of frivolity (lahw) but akin to illness. The passage also notes a difference of opinion regarding substances like hashish and opium, with later scholars considering them prohibited and thus intoxication from them not excusing. Understanding this distinction is crucial for legal capacity (ahliyya).

Guided Path
1
Identify the governing principle
The passage states that intoxication (sukr) is attached to ignorance as an excuse. The key is that intoxication from a permissible cause renders a person like one who is unconscious – their statements are not considered legally valid.
The passage uses 'كالإغماء' (like unconsciousness).
2
Determine the permissible causes
Permissible causes include: drinking a medicinal intoxicant (like banj/hashish or afiyun according to early scholars), being coerced into drinking wine (under threat of death or limb), and drinking out of dire thirst (lack of water).
The permissibility of the cause is what makes the intoxication an excuse; if the cause is prohibited, the intoxication does not excuse.
3
Understand the legal effect
The effect is that such intoxication prevents the validity of divorce, manumission, and all other transactions. The intoxicated person's words are not considered.
This is because the person has lost discerning reason (aql tamayyiz).
4
Note the ikhtilaf on hashish and opium
The passage mentions that early scholars considered banj and afiyun as permissible intoxicants, while later scholars (including many Hanafis) ruled them as prohibited. Consequently, intoxication from them does not excuse.
This is a matter of ijtihad based on whether they are considered intoxicants (muskir) that corrupt the mind.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the types of ignorance and intoxication that serve as legal excuses in Hanafi jurisprudence, specifically within the discussion of legal capacity (ahliyyah). The author lists several cases where ignorance is a valid excuse: the ignorance of a new Muslim in dar al-harb about Islamic rulings (requiring makeup of prayer and fasting from the time of conversion), the ignorance of a preemptor (shafi') about the sale of a property (his silence does not invalidate his right of preemption until he learns of the sale), the ignorance of a married slave woman about her emancipation or the option to choose (she is excused because she is busy serving her master and may not be informed), and the ignorance of a virgin about having been married by a guardian other than father or grandfather (she has an option upon puberty, and ignorance is excused if she did not know of the marriage itself, but not if she knew of the marriage but not of the option, because in an Islamic land she could have learned). The ignorance of an agent or authorized slave about their agency/permission or its revocation/interdiction is also an excuse; if they act before knowledge, their actions are not binding on the principal/master if they were ignorant of the agency, but are binding if they were ignorant of the revocation. Finally, intoxication (sukr) is treated: if it results from a permissible substance (like medicine according to early scholars, or from coercion or necessity), it is like unconsciousness – it prevents the validity of divorce, manumission, and other transactions. However, later scholars consider substances like hashish and opium problematic, with some ruling that they are prohibited and intoxication from them does not excuse. The commentary explains the reasoning behind each rule, emphasizing that ignorance is only excused when there is a genuine obstacle to knowledge, not due to negligence.

Key Terms
سكر (sukr): A state of heedlessness or loss of reasoning due to consuming intoxicants.
إكراه (ikrah): Coercion that forces one to commit an act under threat of harm.

Definition and Three Divisions of Coercion (Ikrah)
Defines coercion and divides it into three types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of coercion (ikrah) and distinguish its three types by their effect on consent (riḍā) and free choice (ikhtiyār).

Core Idea
Coercion levels differ: mulji' negates both consent and choice; others negate only consent or neither.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar defines coercion (ikrah) and presents a tripartite classification essential for determining the validity of actions performed under duress. By analyzing how each type affects consent (riḍā) and free choice (ikhtiyār), you will grasp the foundational usul concept that not all coercion is equal in Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of ikrah
The passage opens by defining coercion: 'compelling a person to what he dislikes, and that person does not desire to directly do it were he not coerced.' Identify the three essential components: compulsion, dislike, and lack of will.
This definition distinguishes ikrah from simple persuasion.
2
Understand the classification criterion
Coercion is divided according to its effect on riḍā (consent) and ikhtiyār (free choice). Note that the three types are exhaustive: either both are negated, only consent is negated, or neither is negated.
The terminology 'mulji' (overwhelming) is used for the first type.
3
Analyze the mulji' type
Mulji' coercion involves threat to life or limb (e.g., 'I will kill you or cut off your hand'). It negates both consent and free choice completely.
This is the most severe form; acts done under mulji' coercion are generally invalid.
4
Examine the remaining two types
The second type (shackles, prolonged imprisonment, non-lethal beating) negates consent but leaves free choice intact because the person could potentially endure the harm. The third type (threat to loved ones) leaves both consent and free choice, as the coercion is indirect.
The key distinction: in the second type, the coerced person still has the option to endure; in the third, the threat is not directly against the person.

And coercion (al-ikrāh): it is a conjunction to what preceded and with it complete the interfering acquired matters, and it is compelling a person to what he dislikes, and that person does not desire to directly do it were he not coerced. And it, meaning coercion, is of three divisions: because it either negates consent and corrupts free choice—and it is the compelling type (al-mulji'), meaning compelling coercion by fear for oneself or one of one's limbs, such as saying: If you do not do such-and-such, I will kill you or cut off your hand, thus his consent is negated and his free choice is completely corrupted. Or it negates consent but does not corrupt free choice—and it is coercion by shackles or prolonged imprisonment, or it is the second division by beating that does not carry fear of death, so his free choice remains then, but he does not consent to it. Or it does not negate consent nor corrupt free choice—and it is being threatened with imprisonment of his father, son, wife, or similar, so both consent and free choice remain.

Commentary
This passage defines coercion (ikrāh) as compelling someone to do something they dislike and would not do willingly. It then divides coercion into three categories based on its effect on consent (riḍā) and free choice (ikhtiyār). The first type is mulji' (overwhelming) coercion, where the coerced person fears death or loss of a limb; this negates both consent and free choice entirely. The second type involves threats like shackling or long imprisonment, or beating that does not endanger life; here free choice remains as the person could potentially endure the harm, but consent is absent. The third type involves threats to loved ones (father, son, wife); here both consent and free choice remain because the coercion does not directly press the person themselves. This classification is crucial in Hanafi fiqh for determining the validity of acts under coercion.

Key Terms
الإكراه (al-ikrāh): Compelling a person to do something he dislikes and would not do willingly.
الملجئ (al-mulji'): Overwhelming coercion that involves threat to life or limb, negating both consent and free choice.
