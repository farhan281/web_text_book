Hanafi vs Shafi'i on Istithna' in Food Sale Hadith
Dispute over whether istithna' excludes a type or a state

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the Hanafi and Shafi'i interpretations of istithna' in the riba hadith and identify the thamarah al-khilaf in ruling on small amounts of unequal exchange.

Core Idea
The dispute is whether the exception excludes a type (Shafi'i) or a state (Hanafi), leading to different rulings on small unequal exchanges.

This lesson examines a classic usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over the interpretation of the hadith 'لا تبيعوا الطعام بالطعام إلا سواء بسواء' (Do not sell food for food except equal for equal). The ambiguity lies in the exception phrase: does it exclude a specific type of food (equal quantity) from the general prohibition, or does it exclude a specific state (equality) from the prohibition on states? The answer determines whether exchanging a handful of dates for two handfuls is permissible or prohibited. We will follow the Hanafi argument that the exception applies to the state, leaving small amounts unaddressed and thus permissible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed text and ambiguity
Locate the hadith in the source passage and note the exception phrase 'إلا سواء بسواء'. Understand that the word 'food' (طعام) appears in both the general term and the exception, creating ambiguity about what exactly is excluded.
The text is a general prohibition with an exception; the structure is 'لا تبيعوا الطعام بالطعام إلا سواء بسواء'.
2
State the Shafi'i position and its interpretation
Al-Shafi'i interprets the exception as excluding a type: 'except equal food for equal food'. In this view, the prohibition applies to all food-for-food exchanges unless both quantities are equal. Thus any inequality, even small amounts, is prohibited.
This interpretation treats 'either' as the mustathnā minhu and 'sawā'an' as the mustathnā referring to a substance.
3
State the Hanafi position and its interpretation
The Hanafis interpret the exception as excluding a state: 'except in a state of equality'. They estimate the mustathnā minhu as 'any state' and limit the prohibition to states of much (equality, excess, gharar). Small amounts are not addressed, so they remain permissible.
This interpretation requires ta'wil in the mustathnā minhu, changing 'food' to 'state' via figurative language.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Apply both interpretations to the case of selling a handful of dates for two handfuls. Shafi'i: prohibited (inequality). Hanafi: permissible (small amount not covered by text). Note the difference in ruling.
The Hanafi view allows small unequal exchanges that are not done in large quantities.
5
Compare epistemological weights and ta'wil approaches
Examine the Hanafi critique: Al-Shafi'i employs two departures from the original principle—using a figurative interpretation (the exception is disconnected) and an exception itself. The Hanafi approach uses only one ta'wil (in the mustathnā minhu) and keeps the text's coverage limited to much quantities.
The Hanafis argue that omitting the mustathnā minhu is common in Arabic, making their interpretation more natural.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute between the Hanafis and Shafi'is over the interpretation of the hadith, 'Do not sell food for food except equal for equal.' The issue is how to understand the exception (istithna') – whether it modifies the excluded term (al-mustathna) or the general term (al-mustathna minhu). Al-Shafi'i interprets the exception as attaching to 'food,' so the prohibition is on exchanging food for food unless both are equal. This makes a handful for a handful permissible because it is equal, but a handful for two handfuls remains prohibited since it is unequal. The Hanafis interpret the exception as attaching to the state or manner of exchange, meaning 'Do not sell food for food in any state except in a state of equality.' The states are equality, excess, and uncertainty (gharar). Equality is permissible, excess and uncertainty are prohibited. Small amounts are not addressed at all, so they remain on the original principle of permissibility, thus a handful for two handfuls is allowed. The Hanafis argue that interpreting the exception as excluding a state from substances is problematic, but they manage it through a figurative reading. They also note that al-Shafi'i's principle regarding ribawi goods is prohibition unless an exception is proven, whereas for all other things his principle is permissibility. The discussion includes nuanced points about the nature of states versus substances, and the role of custom in determining what is 'much' or 'small.' The key is that the Hanafis view the text as not covering small amounts, so no analogy (qiyas) or causal reasoning ('illah) is needed to exclude them; the text simply does not include them.

Key Terms
الاستثناء (al-istithnā'): An exception phrase indicating that what follows is excluded from what precedes.
المستثنى منه (al-mustathnā minhu): The general term from which the exception is made.
المستثنى (al-mustathnā): The excluded term or state.

The Specification of the Derived Cause (Takhsis al-'Illa al-Mustanbata)
Determines whether a derived cause can be specified by invoking a preventer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on specifying a derived cause and evaluate the reasoning behind the majority and minority positions.

Core Idea
Specifying a derived cause is not allowed, for it would undermine the critique of ijtihad.

This passage from Noor al-Anwaar tackles a critical usul issue: can a mujtahid restrict his derived cause (al-illa al-mustanbata) by claiming a preventer? The majority of Hanafis prohibit it, while some (like al-Karkhi) allow it. The discussion reveals how Hanafi usul guards the integrity of qiyas by preventing arbitrary exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
Understand what 'specification of the cause' means: claiming that the cause is effective but a preventer (mani') blocks the ruling in some cases, so the cause is not universal.
2
State the Majority Hanafi Position
The majority (jumhur) of Hanafis hold that specification of a derived cause is not permissible. Derive this from the passage's opening statement.
3
Examine the Dalil
The majority's proof: if allowed, any mujtahid faced with a counterexample could claim a preventer, making it impossible to deem any ijtihad wrong. This would lead to taswib (deeming every mujtahid correct).
4
Note the Opposing View
Some scholars (Iraqi mashayikh, al-Karkhi) permit specification because the derived cause is only an indicator (amara), not a necessary cause. They analogize to clouds as a sign of rain that may not always produce rain.
5
Evaluate the Response and Thamarah
The Hanafi response: even if specification were allowed, there are other ways to refute a cause (e.g., showing the cause is ineffective), so taswib does not necessarily follow. The practical consequence (thamarah) is whether a mujtahid's derivation can be challenged publicly.

Commentary
This passage addresses the issue of specifying (takhsis) a derived cause ('illa mustanbata) in qiyas. The author states that specification of a derived cause is not allowed, because if a mujtahid can simply claim that his cause is specified due to a preventer every time a counterexample is presented, then every mujtahid's reasoning would be considered correct, which is unacceptable. However, some Hanafi scholars, such as the Iraqi mashayikh and al-Karkhi, allowed it on the grounds that the cause is merely an indicator (amara) and not a complete cause necessitating the ruling, so it may be absent in some cases. The passage contrasts this with the textual cause ('illa mansusa), for which many jurists permitted specification. The commentary includes marginal notes that further clarify the argument, including a discussion of whether specification leads to deeming every mujtahid correct, and a note that some later scholars consider this difference insignificant. The passage ultimately upholds the position that specification of a derived cause is impermissible.

Key Terms
العلة المستنبطة (al-'illa al-mustanbata): A cause derived by the mujtahid through analysis, not explicitly stated in the text.
العلة المنصوصة (al-'illa al-mansusa): A cause explicitly stated in the Quran or Hadith as the basis of a ruling.
تخصيص العلة (takhsis al-'illa): The act of restricting the application of a cause to exclude some cases, often by invoking a preventer (mani').

Forgetful Eating During Fast: Proof Against Takhsis al-Illa
Debate on whether forgetfulness prevents the hukm or negates the illa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi position that forgetfulness negates the 'illa from the opposing view that it merely blocks the hukm, and apply the hadith proof to support the Hanafi stance.

Core Idea
Forgetfulness during fasting negates the 'illa itself, not just the hukm—the act is attributed to Allah.

A common misconception is that forgetfulness is a 'preventer' that blocks the ruling while the cause remains. This passage from Noor al-Anwaar dives into that very debate, using the case of eating while fasting. The Hanafi school offers a sharp counter-argument: the act is attributed to the Lawgiver, so the cause vanishes. Understanding this subtle distinction is key to grasping the Hanafi rejection of takhsis al-'illa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested ruling and questions
Determine what hukm is in dispute (the fast's validity) and what the 'illa is (the act of eating). Then articulate the two opposing views: does forgetfulness block the hukm or negate the 'illa?
The 'illa here is eating, which normally breaks the fast. The question is whether forgetfulness changes that.
2
Locate the opponent's claim and evidence
Find the statement of those who permit takhsis al-'illa. They claim the 'illa (eating) remains present, but the hukm (invalidity) is prevented by the 'preventer' of forgetfulness. They cite the hadith as evidence that the hukm is not applied, not that the 'illa is absent.
The opponent's reading: the hadith shows a ruling was suspended, but the cause remains.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response and its proof
The Hanafi rebuttal reinterprets the same hadith: the act of the forgetful person is attributed to Allah ('فإنما أطعمك الله وسقاك'), so the 'illa (the servant's act of eating) is not present. Therefore, the fast remains valid not because of a preventer, but because the 'illa is absent.
This is the key move: the opponent's 'preventer' becomes evidence that the 'illa never existed.
4
Compare the two models of reasoning
Contrast the two frameworks: the opponent's model ('illa exists + preventer blocks hukm) vs. the Hanafi model (preventer proves that the 'illa is not realized). Understand how each view treats the hadith.
This step is about seeing the different logic structures, not just the conclusions.
5
Apply the Hanafi principle to a new case
Given a scenario (e.g., a person forgetfully speaks during prayer), decide using the same principle: does forgetfulness negate the 'illa? Practice the reasoning.
This extends the lesson's tool to another ibadah, testing understanding.

Commentary
This passage from Nur al-Anwar is a legal-theoretical discussion on the nature of tayammum as a substitute (khalaf) for wudu/ghusl. The author explains that all matters of the rights of Allah involve substitution (khalaf 'an khalaf), giving the example of a minor's faith following parents then the community. Specifically for tayammum, it is a substitute for water purification. The core Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) holds that tayammum is an absolute substitute (mutlaq), meaning it fully removes ritual impurity (hadath) and thus permits prayer until water is found. Consequently, one tayammum suffices for multiple obligatory prayers. In contrast, Imam al-Shafi'i views tayammum as a necessary (daruri) substitute: it does not remove impurity but merely permits prayer out of necessity, so each obligatory prayer requires a separate tayammum. Within the Hanafi school, Imam Muhammad and Imam Zufar differ from the two shaykhs: they hold that tayammum is a substitute for wudu specifically, not for water, and thus it is weaker—so a person who performed tayammum cannot lead those who have wudu, because the weaker cannot lead the stronger. The passage further elaborates that substitution (khilafa) must be established by clear textual evidence (nass) or its indication, not by personal opinion (ra'y). A key condition for a substitute is the actual absence of the original while it remains potentially available. The discussion then applies these principles to the issue of a mutayammim leading people with wudu in prayer, noting the differences within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf allow it (provided the follower cannot find water), while Muhammad and Zufar generally prohibit it. An exception is the funeral prayer, where it is unanimously allowed. This passage carefully integrates usul principles with fiqh rulings, demonstrating how theoretical positions on the nature of a substitute directly affect practical matters like prayer leadership.

Key Terms
خلف (khalaf): Substitute that takes the place of the original (asl) in its absence.
مطلق (mutlaq): Absolute or complete, meaning the substitute fully fulfills the function of the original.
ضروري (daruri): Necessary, meaning the substitute is only permitted under necessity and does not fully replace the original.

Commentary
This passage from Nur al-Anwar is a legal-theoretical discussion on the nature of tayammum as a substitute (khalaf) for wudu/ghusl. The author explains that all matters of the rights of Allah involve substitution (khalaf 'an khalaf), giving the example of a minor's faith following parents then the community. Specifically for tayammum, it is a substitute for water purification. The core Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) holds that tayammum is an absolute substitute (mutlaq), meaning it fully removes ritual impurity (hadath) and thus permits prayer until water is found. Consequently, one tayammum suffices for multiple obligatory prayers. In contrast, Imam al-Shafi'i views tayammum as a necessary (daruri) substitute: it does not remove impurity but merely permits prayer out of necessity, so each obligatory prayer requires a separate tayammum. Within the Hanafi school, Imam Muhammad and Imam Zufar differ from the two shaykhs: they hold that tayammum is a substitute for wudu specifically, not for water, and thus it is weaker—so a person who performed tayammum cannot lead those who have wudu, because the weaker cannot lead the stronger. The passage further elaborates that substitution (khilafa) must be established by clear textual evidence (nass) or its indication, not by personal opinion (ra'y). A key condition for a substitute is the actual absence of the original while it remains potentially available. The discussion then applies these principles to the issue of a mutayammim leading people with wudu in prayer, noting the differences within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf allow it (provided the follower cannot find water), while Muhammad and Zufar generally prohibit it. An exception is the funeral prayer, where it is unanimously allowed. This passage carefully integrates usul principles with fiqh rulings, demonstrating how theoretical positions on the nature of a substitute directly affect practical matters like prayer leadership.

Substituting the Inciter for the Act Due to Necessity
Explaining legal substitution when direct knowledge is impossible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi principle of substituting the inciter for the act when direct knowledge is impossible, and apply it to the cases of istibra', valid seclusion, and marriage.

Core Idea
The inciter substitutes for the act to prevent hardship when the truth is hidden.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar introduces a fundamental Hanafi usul principle: when direct knowledge of a real situation is impossible or extremely difficult, the law substitutes the known cause (al-da'i) for the unknown effect (al-mad'u). This substitution is necessary to avoid hardship and to uphold legal objectives. The text illustrates this with three key examples: istibra' (waiting period for a slave-girl), valid seclusion (khalwah sahihah), and marriage in establishing lineage.

Guided Path
1
Understand the principle of qiyam al-da'i maqam al-mad'u
The law treats the known cause (e.g., ownership) as equivalent to the unknown result (e.g., pregnancy) for legal rulings.
The Arabic terms are: الداعي (the inciter) and المدعو (the incited).
2
Examine the first application: istibra'
A newly acquired slave-girl must wait one menstrual cycle because ownership (al-milk wa al-yad) is an inciter to intercourse. Since we cannot know the womb's state, ownership stands in for it.
Even if certainty of non-pregnancy exists (e.g., virgin), istibra' is still required.
3
Analyze the second application: valid seclusion (khalwah sahihah)
Valid seclusion substitutes for actual intercourse, triggering full mahr and 'iddah, because seclusion is an inciter to intercourse.
Impediments like illness, menstruation, ihram, or obligatory fast invalidate seclusion.
4
Consider the third application: marriage in lineage
The marriage contract itself substitutes for intercourse to establish paternity (nasab), another case of the inciter standing in for the act.
The text mentions this briefly but it follows the same principle.

The Five Types of Conditions (Shart) in Hanafi Usul
Definition and classification of shart in Hanafi usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define shart, distinguish it from 'illa and part (juz), and classify the five types of conditions in Hanafi usul al-fiqh using the examples from the passage.

Core Idea
A condition enables a ruling's existence but not its obligation, and Hanafis identify five types based on causation attribution.

In Hanafi usul, understanding the concept of 'shart' (condition) is essential for correctly attributing legal rulings. This passage not only defines shart but also refines the definition to exclude parts of the ruling itself, then classifies five types through induction. Each type has distinct legal consequences, especially regarding liability. This lesson will guide you through the definition and the classification so you can apply these categories in analyzing rulings.

Guided Path
1
Define shart and its key distinction
Identify the official definition: shart is that upon which the existence (wujud) of a ruling depends, but not its obligation (wujub). This distinguishes it from 'illa, which makes the ruling obligatory. Also note the refinement: the condition must be external to the essence of the mahall (thing conditioned) to exclude parts (juz').
See the author's addition: 'and it is external to its essence'.
2
Extract the first type: pure condition (shart mahd)
The pure condition has no causal effect on the ruling itself; it merely allows the cause to become attached. The example is the suspended divorce: 'If you enter the house, you are divorced.' Entering does not cause the divorce; it is a condition for the pronouncement to take effect.
No liability or attribution to the condition.
3
Analyze the second type: condition in the status of a cause (hukm al-‘ilal)
Here the condition is treated as the cause for liability, even though the real cause is something else (e.g., gravity). Digging a well in a public road removes the earth's prevention, so the digger is liable for damages. Similarly, cutting a waterskin releases liquid. Note exceptions: digging on own property or deliberate jumping.
The condition stands in for the cause because the real cause is not a voluntary act.
4
Examine the third type: condition with status of a cause (free agent intervention)
This type involves a free agent's action that intervenes between the condition and the result, but the action is not attributed to the condition. Example: opening a birdcage – the bird's flight is a natural reaction, and according to Muhammad al-Shaybani the opener is liable because the flight is attributed to the opening, contrary to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf who consider it a pure condition.
Notice the intra-madhhab disagreement.
5
Identify the fourth and fifth types: no legal effect
The fourth type is a mere sign (like a marker) that does not affect the existence of the ruling at all. The fifth type is a rational condition not designated by the Law (e.g., possibly the moon in a pre-Islamic custom). These are outside the scope of legal causation.
These are residual categories.

Commentary
This passage is from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary explaining the concept of 'shart' (condition) and its five types. The author first defines a condition as something upon which the existence of a ruling depends but not its obligation (wajib), thereby distinguishing it from a cause ('illa), which makes the ruling obligatory. He adds that a condition must be external to the essence of the thing, to exclude a part (juz') of it. Through induction, five types of conditions are identified. The first is a pure condition (shart mahd) that has no effect on the ruling itself but is necessary for the activation of the cause – like entering a house as a condition for the divorce pronouncement to take effect. The second is a condition that stands in for a cause in terms of attributing the ruling and imposing liability, such as digging a well in a public road. The real cause of destruction is the weight of the falling object, but the earth prevents it; digging removes the prevention, so the digger is liable for damages if he dug without ownership. However, if he dug on his own property or if the victim deliberately jumped, no liability attaches. Similarly, cutting a waterskin is a condition for the liquid to flow; the liquid's fluidity is the cause but not attributed to it, so liability goes to the one who cut. The third type is a condition that has the status of a cause (sabab) – where a free agent's action intervenes between the condition and the result, and that action is not attributed to the condition. For example, opening a birdcage: Muhammad al-Shaybani holds the opener liable because the bird's flight is a natural reaction attributed to the opening, but the two elders (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) disagree, considering it a mere condition. The fourth and fifth types have no legal effect – one is a mere sign (like a marker), and the other is a rational condition not designated by the Law, such as (possibly) the moon in an example of pre-Islamic customs. The passage uses many Hanafi examples and reflects the school's detailed analysis of causation.

D) Rational condition, because the Law does not designate cutting as a condition.

In Hanafi usul, a condition (shart) is an external factor that enables a ruling's existence without making it obligatory, and it is classified into five types that determine legal liability and attribution of the ruling.

The Fourth Type of Condition: Nominal Condition in Paired Conditions
Discusses the fourth type of condition: nominal condition in paired conditions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In paired conditions, the ruling attaches only to the last condition; the first is merely nominal.

This lesson delves into a nuanced Hanafi usul rule: when two conditions are linked by a conjunctive phrase, the first condition is considered nominal (shart isman la hukman), meaning it does not independently trigger the ruling. The ruling actually depends on the second condition. We will explore the classic example of conditional divorce, analyze the scenarios, and understand the dispute with Zufar within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fourth type of condition
Recognize that a condition can be a condition only in name (shart isman) not in ruling (la hukman) when it is the first of two paired conditions.

2
Understand the example
Work through the divorce example: 'If you enter this house, then if you enter this house, you are divorced.' The first 'if' is nominal; the second is the real condition.
The second condition is the one that triggers the divorce.

The Hanafi response: the second condition is the real condition, so marriage is required only at its time.
5

The rule is that when two conditions are conjoined with 'thumma' or 'fa', the ruling is suspended on the last condition; the first is a mere formality.
This rule is specific to Hanafi usul and is an expansion of the general principle of conditionality.

Hazl does not negate legal capacity; the contract is presumed valid unless the jest is proven.

In Hanafi fiqh, contracts concluded in jest (hazl) are a nuanced topic. This lesson clarifies what constitutes hazl, how it differs from khiyar al-shart, and the legal principle that governs its effect on contract validity. The key is understanding that the outward expression is given precedence over secret intent, and the contract is considered valid unless the parties clearly persist in the jest.

Guided Path
1
Define hazl and identify its essential elements
Extract from the text the definition of hazl: a secret agreement between the parties to simulate a contract publicly without an actual contract. Identify the key components: prior collusion (muwada'a), public performance, and absence of real intent.
The definition distinguishes hazl from khiyar al-shart, which requires explicit mention in the contract.
2
Understand that hazl does not affect legal capacity
The text states that talji'a (coercion-based pretence) is like hazl and both 'do not conflict with legal capacity.' Recognize that the contract is still considered concluded by capable parties; the validity hinges on their post-session intention, not on their capacity at the time of utterance.
Legal capacity (ahliyya) is not impaired by hazl; the focus is on the validity of the contract itself.
3
Distinguish hazl from khiyar al-shart
The author compares hazl with the option of condition: hazl does not need to be mentioned in the contract because its purpose (deceiving the public) is achieved by the secret agreement alone, whereas khiyar al-shart must be stipulated expressly to inform the public of the provisional nature of the sale.
This distinction is a usul principle about how different legal purposes determine the form of expression required.
4
Analyze the four post-session scenarios
The text outlines four possible states after the people disperse: (1) both persist in the jest → contract void; (2) both abandon the jest → contract valid; (3) both are free of mind (no intent) → contract valid; (4) they dispute (one claims jest, the other earnest) → Abu Hanifa rules valid, Sahibayn rule corrupt.
The fourth scenario is the crux of the madhhab dispute; focus on the underlying usul principle: al-asl fi al-uqud al-sihha.
5
Examine the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn
Abu Hanifa gives precedence to the apparent words and the principle of validity; the Sahibayn give weight to the secret collusion. Understand the thamarah al-khilaf: practical difference in ruling (valid vs. corrupt) and the jurisprudential reasoning behind each view.
This dispute illustrates how usul principles (e.g., precedence of explicit over implicit, origin of validity) directly influence fiqh rulings.

Explanation of Legal Capacity. Definition of Jest and its Condition: That the two parties jest in the contract, and that is not established by circumstantial evidence alone. However, it is not required to mention it in the contract, unlike the option of condition; because the purpose of the two from a jocular sale is that people believe it is a sale, while in reality it is not a sale, and this is not achieved by mentioning it in the contract. As for the option of condition, its purpose is to inform people that the sale is not final but contingent on the option, and that is only achieved by mentioning it in the very contract. Talji'a (coercion-based pretence) is like hazl, so it does not conflict with legal capacity. It is derived in language from 'ilja' meaning compulsion; its essence is that something compels one to do an act whose inner reality is contrary to its outer appearance. So one shows in front of people that they are transacting a sale between them for some benefit that prompted it, while in reality there is no sale between them. Hazl is more general than talji'a, but the ruling for both is the same in that they do not negate legal capacity. Know that the basis of hazl is that the two contracting parties agree secretly to show the contract in front of people while there is no contract between them in reality. So they contract in front of people, and after the people disperse, there are four possible states between them concerning every contract. The author detailed them. He said: 'The two contracting parties... the contract etc.' Know that the total of what hazl enters into is three categories: creation of a disposition, informing about a disposition, and what pertains to belief. Then creation is of two types: what can be rescinded like sale and lease, and what cannot be rescinded like divorce and manumission. Similarly, informing is of two types: what can be rescinded and what cannot. And what pertains to belief is also of two types: good like faith and evil like disbelief. Then hazl in the first category (i.e., creation that can be rescinded) has three aspects: either they jest about the essence of the contract, or about the amount of the consideration, or about the type of consideration. Each aspect has four types as the commentator indicated. Then after the people disperse, there are four states: (1) If they agreed to continue the hazl regarding the essence of the sale (i.e., they secretly agreed to show a sale in front of people, and there is no actual sale between them), and they contracted in their presence and the session dispersed, then later they came and agreed to build upon that prior secret agreement (i.e., they persisted in that collusion), then the hazl invalidates the sale and does not confer ownership even if delivery is effected, due to lack of consent. So even if the sold item was a slave and the buyer manumitted him after taking delivery, it is not effective, like a sale with a perpetual option condition, which prevents ownership from being established even though the sale is valid; so in a corrupt sale it is more so. (2) If they agreed to abandon (i.e., they turned away from the prior collusion and contracted the sale in earnest), then the sale is valid and binding, and the hazl is void. (3) If they agreed that at the time of the contract they had neither the intent to build upon the prior collusion nor to abandon it, i.e., they were free of mind, or they disputed about building and abandoning: one said 'we contracted on the prior collusion' and the other said 'we contracted in earnest' — then according to Abu Hanifa it is valid, contrary to the two. So Abu Hanifa made the case of validity of the offer preferable, because soundness is the principle in contracts; thus contracts are interpreted as valid as long as no altering factor exists. This applies when they agree they were free of mind; but if they dispute, the one claiming abandonment is adhering to the principle, so he is more deserving. Commentary
This passage is a detailed legal analysis of hazl (jest or pretence) in contracts, a key topic in Hanafi fiqh. It defines hazl and distinguishes it from khiyar al-shart (option of condition). The author explains that hazl does not require explicit mention in the contract; rather, it is inferred from the parties' secret agreement, whereas khiyar al-shart must be stipulated explicitly. Talji'a (coercion-based pretence) is a subset of hazl, but the ruling for both is that they do not affect the legal capacity (ahliyyah) of the parties—meaning the contract is technically concluded, but its validity depends on the parties' post-session intention. The passage then categorizes hazl into three types based on the nature of the act (creation, information, belief) and further breaks down creation into three aspects (jest about the contract itself, the amount of consideration, or the type of consideration). The core ruling is presented through an analysis of four possible scenarios after the people disperse: (1) If the parties persist in their jest (continue the collusion), the contract is void (fasid) and does not confer ownership even if delivery occurs; (2) If they abandon the jest and contract in earnest, the contract is valid and binding; (3) If they have no specific intention or are free of mind at the time of contract, the contract is valid based on the principle that 'soundness is the origin in contracts' (al-asl fi al-uqud al-sihha); (4) If they dispute (one claiming jest, the other earnest), Abu Hanifa rules the contract valid because the claim of earnestness (abandonment) is supported by the principle that contracts are presumed valid. The two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that the contract is corrupt (fasid) in the disputed case, giving weight to the prior collusion. This illustrates an important usul principle: in the absence of clear evidence, the default assumption is validity. The commentary by al-Sunbuli and al-Qamar clarifies that hazl is not established by circumstantial evidence alone—the oral expression is given precedence. The example of a sale with a perpetual option condition is used as an analogy to show that ownership is not transferred in a jocular sale.

Key Terms
الهزل (al-hazl): Jest or pretence in a contract; a secret agreement to simulate a contract publicly without actual intention to create legal effects.
التلجية (al-talji'a): A type of hazl where the pretence is due to duress or compulsion, not free choice.
المواضعة (al-muwada'a): The secret collusion or prior agreement between the parties to engage in a jocular contract.
الأهلية (al-ahliyya): Legal capacity; the fitness of a person to acquire rights and incur obligations.
الأصل في العقود الصحة (al-asl fi al-uqud al-sihha): The principle that contracts are presumed valid unless there is evidence to the contrary.
الإعراض (al-i'rad): Abandonment or turning away from the prior collusion; the parties' decision to contract in earnest.
البناء (al-bina'): Building upon or persisting in the prior collusion; the parties continue the jest intent.

Key Takeaway
Hazl does not negate legal capacity, and contracts are presumed valid (al-asl fi al-uqud al-sihha); the jester's secret intent does not override explicit words unless the jest is clearly proven.
