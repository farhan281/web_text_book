Paying Zakat Value: Text or Reasoning?
Defense that paying value in zakat is from textual indication, not 'illah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a ruling based on textual indication (nass) and one based on causal reasoning ('illah) by applying the Hanafi approach in the zakat value debate.

Core Idea
Paying zakat value is allowed by general texts, not by analogy from the specific sheep.

In Hanafi usul, the permissibility of giving the monetary value (qima) instead of the specified animal in zakat is a classic point of contention. This passage defends the Hanafi position by arguing that the extension to value comes directly from general textual indications (nusus) about providing for the poor, not from a causal reasoning (ta'lil) that would nullify the explicit restriction of the sheep. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping how Hanafi usulists balance specific commands with broader textual objectives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting positions
The objector claims that the explicit mention of 'sheep' is a restriction (qayd) that cannot be overridden by reasoning. The Hanafi position is that value is permissible by other texts.
Focus on the disagreement over the scope of the sheep directive.
2
Locate the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi scholar cites general Quranic verses about zakat and sustenance (e.g., 'the alms are for the poor…') as the basis for allowing value.
These texts are general (amm) and provide the legal basis.
3
Examine the objection and response
The objector argues that using 'illah (suitability for needs) nullifies the explicit text. The Hanafi response: the text itself, not the 'illah, gives the permission. The 'illah merely accompanies and confirms.
The Hanafi response emphasizes that the nass is self-sufficient.
4
Understand the role of the 'illah
The 'illah does not create the ruling; it only explains why the specific example (sheep) is suitable. The extension to value comes from other texts, not from the 'illah.
Distinguish between explanatory 'illah and operative 'illah.
5
Apply to a similar issue
Consider another zakat case: can cash be given in place of specified grains? Use the same principles.
Test your understanding by analogizing to other zakat items.

Commentary
This passage addresses an objection: The law explicitly requires a sheep in zakat on grazing livestock (e.g., 'in five camels a sheep'). The Hanafi scholar argued that the sheep is suitable for the poor because it is wealth that can meet needs, and by that reasoning (ta'lil), any property that meets needs can be given, including the value (qima) instead of a sheep. The objector says this nullifies the explicit restriction (qayd) of the sheep, since the text demands a sheep, not its value. The Hanafi response is that the permissibility of paying value comes from textual indication (nass) itself, not from the reasoning (ta'lil). Specifically, the Quranic promise of providing for the poor (e.g., the verses about zakat) indicates that the poor's need is paramount. The text (nass) about the sheep is not absolute; it is an example. The extension to value is based on the general texts about zakat maintenance, not on a mere analogy from the sheep's suitability. The commentary clarifies that this is the point of the earlier discussion about the food hadith – that the text itself, not the 'illah, is the basis for the ruling. The objection arises because the 'illah accompanies the text, giving the illusion that the 'illah is the cause of the extension. In fact, the text already provides for the poor, and the value is permissible by that textual indication. Some also argue that the permissibility of paying value is independently established in the Shariah, so the restriction of the sheep is not nullified; rather, the Lawgiver allowed an alternative.

Key Terms
النص (al-nass): Textual indication, specifically the general Quranic verses and hadiths about zakat and sustenance.
التعليل (al-ta'lil): Reasoning or causation, i.e., identifying the 'illah (e.g., suitability for needs).
القيد (al-qayd): A restriction or specification in a text, e.g., the sheep is a restriction on what can be given.

The Role of Intellect in Legal Obligation
Explaining the Hanafi position on intellect as a means rather than cause of obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi view from Mu'tazila and Ash'ari on the role of intellect in taklif, and apply the Hanafi principle that intellect is a mu'arraf not a mujib.

Core Idea
Intellect defines obligation but does not cause it; revelation is the true source of legal duty.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar clarifies the Hanafi usul stance on intellect's role in taklif, contrasting with Mu'tazila and Ash'aris. The author reconciles the apparent tension that intellect alone makes one liable for faith yet those without da'wah are excused. Through rational argument and the hadith 'rafi' al-qalam', the Hanafi position is refined: intellect is a tool (mu'arraf) not a cause (mujib).

Guided Path
1
Identify school positions
Extract the three positions: Hanafi, Mu'tazili, Ash'ari. Note the key phrase: 'العقل موجب عندهم ومعرّف عندنا'.
2
Locate the Hanafi core
Focus on the statement that intellect is mu'arraf (definer) not mujib (cause). Understand that obligation requires revelation.
See the author's reconciliation: those lacking da'wah are excused if they lacked opportunity for reflection.
3
Trace practical consequence
Examine the thamarah al-khilaf: blood money for killing a person before da'wah. Hanafi: no daman; Ash'ari: daman required.
This follows from the difference in the basis of obligation.
4
Analyze the hadith
The hadith 'رفع القلم عن ثلاث' is cited to show that minors and insane are not obligated. This supports the Hanafi view that obligation is from revelation, not mere intellect.
The hadith is ahad but its meaning is clear (qati' al-dalala).

Commentary
This passage is a nuanced Hanafi usul discussion on the role of intellect ('aql) in legal obligation (taklif). The author begins by stating the apparent contradiction: if a person does not believe in faith or disbelief (i.e., remains indifferent), he is still considered from the people of hell because the obligation of faith arises from mere intellect. However, in worldly laws (shara'i'), such a person is excused until proof (hujjah) reaches him. This is the position narrated from Abu Hanifa and Abu Mansur al-Maturidi. The difference with the Mu'tazila is then clarified: they hold that intellect is a cause (mujib) for obligation, while the Hanafis hold it is merely a means of definition (mu'arraf) — the actual obligation comes from revelation. The correct Hanafi view is that one who has never received the prophetic call (da'wah) is not obligated by mere intellect alone; he is excused if he does not believe, because he lacked the opportunity for reflection (ta'ammul). However, if he is given sufficient time and experience (tajribah) to contemplate the signs of creation, he is no longer excused even without the call, because the extended time serves as an equivalent to the call in awakening his heart. The estimation of this time is left to Allah due to individual differences; the suggestion of three days (by analogy with the murtad's respite) is deemed weak. The passage then contrasts the Ash'ari position: for them, the basis of goodness and evil (husn wa qubh) is purely revelational (shar'i), not intellectual. Therefore, one who has never received the call is excused completely, and his blood-money (daman) is like that of a Muslim if killed. The Hanafis disagree: since intellect can perceive good and evil, such a person is not excused, and no blood-money is paid. Finally, the validity of a minor's faith is discussed. Ash'aris reject it because they require a revelatory basis; Hanafis accept it as valid (sahih) but note that the minor is not obligated (mukallaf) due to the hadith 'The pen is lifted from three...'. The hadith is cited with multiple chains, showing its authenticity. The analytical tool here includes a madhhab dispute map contrasting Hanafi and Ash'ari usul, and a dalil block for the hadith.

التكليف (al-taklif): Legal obligation; the imposition of duties by the Lawgiver upon a responsible person.
حسن وقبح (husn wa qubh): Goodness and badness of acts; in this text, Hanafis and Mu'tazila hold they are intellectual; Ash'aris hold they are revealed.

The Principle of Had al-Imtidad in Acts of Worship
Sets the criteria for when prolonged insanity triggers makeup of prayers, fasting, zakat, and hajj.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the general concept of had al-imtidad and identify the different limits for prayer, fasting, zakat, and hajj as derived from the Hanafi usul principle of avoiding hardship.

Core Idea
Each worship has its own duration threshold for insanity to trigger makeup, based on feasibility.

The Hanafi school recognizes that prolonged insanity can excuse makeup of missed worship, but only after a certain duration – the 'limit of prolongation'. This lesson introduces the general principle and how it applies differently to prayer, fasting, zakat, and hajj, as derived from the source text.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of had al-imtidad
Identify that the limit is the maximum duration of insanity after which makeup of a missed worship becomes obligatory. It differs per worship due to different levels of hardship.
This is a usul rule derived from the ratio legis (talil) of preventing hardship.
2
Extract the limits for each worship from the text
Prayer: more than a day and night (six prayers or 24 hours). Fasting: the entire month of Ramadan. Zakat: the whole lunar year. Hajj: analogous to zakat.
Note that for prayer, there is a dispute on counting method, but the overall threshold is one day/night.
3
Recognize the ratio legis (talil) behind the limits
The limits are set to balance obligation and hardship. For example, missing six prayers is regarded as 'repetition' (tikrar) that makes makeup burdensome, so a longer insanity exempts.
Abu Yusuf extended facilitation by considering 'most of the year' sufficient for zakat/haij.
4
Apply the principle to a hypothetical scenario
Given a case of insanity lasting for a specific duration, determine for each worship whether makeup is required by comparing with the stated limits.
This is a test of understanding the rule rather than the detailed disputes.

وحد الامتداد في الصلاة أن يزيد على يوم وليلة ولكن باعتبار الصلاة عند محمد حلبه، يعني ما لم تصر الصلاة ستّا لا يسقط عنه القضاء، وباعتبار الساعات عندهما حتى لو جُنّ قبل الزوال، ثم أفاق في اليوم الثاني بعد الزوال لا قضاء عليه عندهما؛ لأنه من حيث الساعات أكثر من يوم وليلة، الشيخين عليه القضاء ما لم يمتدّ إلى وقت العصر حتى يصير الصلاة ستّا، فيدخل في حد التكرار. محمد مه أي تكرار الصلاة وعنده وفي الصوم باستغراق الشهر حتى لو أفاق في جزء من الشهر ليلاً أو نهارا يجب عليه القضاء في ظاهر الرواية، وعن شمس الأئمة الحلواني: أنه لو كان مفيقًا في أول ليلة من رمضان، فأصبح مجنونا، ثم استوعب باقي الشهر لا يجب عليه القضاء، وهو الصحيح؛ لأن الليل لا يُصام فيه، فكان الإفاقة والجنون فيه سواء، ولو أفاق في يوم من رمضان، أي في وقت النية فلو كان قبل الزوال يلزمه القضاء، ولو كان بعده لا يلزمه في الصحيح. وفي الزكاة باستغراق الحول؛ لأنها لا تدخل في حدّ التكرار ما لم تدخل السنة الثانية. وكذا في الحج أي الزكاة وأبو يوسف له أقام أكثر الحول مقام الكل تيسيرًا ودفعا للحرج في حق المكلف.
And the limit of prolongation in prayer is that it exceeds a day and a night, but according to Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on him) with consideration of the number of prayers, meaning until the number of prayers becomes six, he does not stop requiring makeup. And according to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) with consideration of hours, so if one becomes insane before noon, then becomes sane the next day after noon, no makeup is obligatory upon him according to them, because in terms of hours it is more than a day and a night. The two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf?) say: makeup is obligatory as long as it does not extend to the time of Asr until the prayers become six, entering the limit of repetition. Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on him) says: repetition of prayer. And according to him (Muhammad?) in fasting, it is based on the entire month, so if he becomes sane in any part of the month, night or day, makeup is obligatory in the apparent narration. And from Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani: if he was sane on the first night of Ramadan, then becomes insane the next morning, covering the rest of the month, makeup is not obligatory, and this is the correct opinion, because the night is not a time for fasting, so being sane or insane are equal in it. And if he becomes sane in a day of Ramadan, i.e., during the time of intention, if before noon, makeup is required; if after noon, it is not required in the correct opinion. And in zakat, it is based on the whole year, because it does not enter the limit of repetition until the second year enters. And similarly in hajj, i.e., zakat. And Abu Yusuf (may Allah have mercy on him) considered the majority of the year as equivalent to the whole, as a facilitation and to prevent hardship on the obligated person.

Commentary
This passage specifies the 'limit of prolongation' (had al-imtidad) for different acts of worship. For prayer: temporary insanity longer than a day and night (i.e., missing six prayers) triggers makeup; the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) use actual hours (about 24 hours) while Muhammad uses the count of six fard prayers. For fasting: the relevant period is the entire month of Ramadan; if one becomes sane at any point during the day or night, makeup is required per apparent narration, but Al-Halwani opines that sanity on the first night only does not require makeup since the night is not for fasting. Also, if one becomes sane before noon on a day, that day's fast must be kept unless insanity made it impossible. For zakat: the entire year is the period; if insanity extends into the second year, then it enters the limit of repetition (i.e., becomes burdensome to recalculate). For hajj, the rule is analogous to zakat. Abu Yusuf makes a facilitation: if the insanity covers most of the year (more than half), it is treated as the whole year, so no zakat is due for that year. These rules aim to prevent undue hardship in making up missed obligations.

Key Terms
حد الامتداد (had al-imtidad): The threshold of duration of a legal excuse (insanity) beyond which makeup of worship becomes obligatory.
التكرار (al-tikrar): Repetition of missed prayers, referring to hardship when more than six prayers are missed.
