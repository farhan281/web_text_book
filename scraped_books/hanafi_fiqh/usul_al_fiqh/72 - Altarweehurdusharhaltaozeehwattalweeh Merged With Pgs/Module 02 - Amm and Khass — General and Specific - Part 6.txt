The Definitive Generality of 'Kull' and 'Jami'' in Hanafi Usul
Explaining 'kull' and 'jami'' as definitive general terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the use of 'kull' with nakira (individual generality) and with ma'rifa (total generality), and apply the concept of 'umum 'ala sabil al-infiraad to analyze phrases like 'kullu man dakhala awwala'.

Core Idea
Kull and jami' are definitive in generality, giving individual coverage with indefinites and total coverage with definites.

This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary clarifies a crucial distinction: when the general term 'kull' (all/every) enters upon an indefinite noun (nakira), it provides generality that covers each individual separately, disregarding others. But when it enters upon a definite noun (ma'rifa), it indicates the totality of the group. The author illustrates this with the example of a reward promised to 'everyone who enters the fortress first' and shows how the addition of 'kull' forces a metaphorical reading of 'first' to allow multiple recipients. Understanding this linguistic precision is essential for correctly interpreting general texts (umum) in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms and their definitive nature
Locate the statement that 'kull' and 'jami'' are muhkaman (definitive) in their generality. This means they are not susceptible to specification (takhsis) without strong evidence, unlike other general tools such as 'man' or 'ma'.
The Hanafi position holds that these two words have a higher epistemological weight than other general expressions.
2
Distinguish between nakira and ma'rifa contexts
When 'kull' enters upon a nakira (indefinite noun), it indicates 'umum al-afrad' (generality of individuals) – each individual is considered separately. When it enters upon a ma'rifa (definite noun), it indicates 'al-majmu'' (the totality) – the group as a whole.
Example: 'kullu rajulin' = every man (each individually); 'al-qawmu kulluhum' = all of the people (the entire group).
3
Understand 'umum 'ala sabil al-infiraad'
This is the principle that when 'kull' attaches to a nakira, each individual is intended while disregarding others. The usuliyyun call this 'generality on the way of individualization'.
This is the default interpretation for 'kull' + nakira.
4
Apply to the example: 'kullu man dakhala hadha al-hisna awwala'
If ten people enter the fortress together, each one is considered 'first' relative to those who have not yet entered. Because 'kull' individualizes each entrant, the word 'awwal' necessarily applies to each, requiring a metaphorical interpretation (i.e., 'preceding relative to those behind' rather than a single chronological first).
Contrast with 'man dakhala awwala' without 'kull', which is general on the basis of substitution (badal) – only the first single entrant qualifies.
5
Note the unique contribution of this commentary
The author claims to have uniquely pointed out the second difference: that 'man dakhala awwala' is 'amm 'ala sabil al-badal' (substitutive generality), and the addition of 'kull' changes the nature of the generality to require multiplicity in the description 'awwal'.
This highlights the precision of Hanafi usul in analyzing linguistic structures.

Commentary
This passage analyzes the words 'kull' (every/all) and 'jami'' (all/whole) as general terms (alfaz al-‘umum) in Hanafi usul. The text states that 'kull' and 'jami'' are 'muhkaman' (definitive) in their generality, unlike other general tools which may be subject to specification. The key distinction: if 'kull' enters upon a nakira (indefinite noun), it indicates generality of individuals (each one individually); if it enters upon a ma'rifa (definite noun), it indicates totality (the whole group). This is called 'umum 'ala sabil al-infiraad' (generality on the basis of individualization). An example: 'Kullu man dakhala hadha al-hisna awwala' (Everyone who enters this fortress first, for him is such-and-such). If ten people enter together, each one is entitled to the reward because each individual is considered 'first' relative to those outside (who did not enter yet). This differs from 'Man dakhala hadha al-hisna awwala' (Whoever enters this fortress first) which is general on the basis of substitution (ala sabil al-badal) — only one person can be first. The addition of 'kull' changes the meaning: it necessitates that 'awwal' (first) applies to multiple individuals, leading to a metaphorical interpretation where 'first' means 'preceding relative to those who lag behind' rather than a single chronological first. The author claims this distinction is uniquely pointed out by him. The Urdu commentary reiterates that 'kull' and 'jami'' are definitive in creating generality upon whatever they enter.

Key Terms
كل (kull): A general term that is definitive (muhkam) in its generality; when attached to a nakira it indicates individual universality; when attached to a ma'rifa it indicates totality.
جميع (jami'): Similar to 'kull,' a general term that is definitive in generality, indicating totality when attached to a definite noun.
عموم على سبيل الانفراد (‘umum ‘ala sabil al-infiraad): Generality that considers each individual separately, disregarding others; applicable when 'kull' enters upon a nakira.
محكم (muhkam): Definitive, unambiguous; a term that is fixed in its meaning and not subject to specification except by strong evidence.
عموم على سبيل البدل (‘umum ‘ala sabil al-badal): Generality that applies to only one instance at a time, as a substitute; e.g., 'man dakhala awwala' applies to only the first entrant.

Rule of Kull on Nakirah vs Ma'rifah
Explaining the rule of kull when entering on nakirah vs ma'rifah and its implications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of 'kull' to distinguish between individual and collective generality in general texts, and analyze objections using the concept of muhkam.

Core Idea
When 'kull' enters a nakirah, it means each individually; when it enters a ma'rifah, it means all together.

This lesson unpacks a key distinction in Hanafi usul: the behavior of the word 'kull' (كل) depending on whether it modifies an indefinite (nakirah) or definite (ma'rifah) noun. This distinction affects how we understand general commands in the Quran and Sunnah, and determines the scope of rulings. The passage also clarifies the special status of 'kull' and 'jami'' as muhkam words, which are resistant to further specification by qiyas after textual specification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases
Recognize when 'kull' enters a nakirah (indefinite) vs a ma'rifah (definite). Nakirah examples: كل رجل (every man), كل من دخل (everyone who enters). Ma'rifah examples: كل الرجال (all the men).
The definiteness marker is the definite article 'ال' or a proper noun.
2
State the rule for each
On nakirah: the ruling applies to each individual separately (عموم افراد). On ma'rifah: the ruling applies to the collective whole (عموم مجموع).
This is a distinctively Hanafi position; other schools do not make this explicit distinction for 'kull'.
3
Apply the rule to the 'first' example
In 'كل من دخل هذا الحصن أولا', since 'من دخل' is nakirah, each entrant is considered 'first' in a metaphorical sense (relative to later entrants). Without 'kull', 'first' would be literal and singular.
The metaphorical interpretation is necessary because 'kull' demands multiplicity.
4
Understand the muhkam property
'Kull' (and 'jami'') are muhkam words, meaning they are strong in indicating generality and cannot be further specified by qiyas after a textual specification. They can still be specified by independent textual evidence (takhsis), but not by qiyas once specified.
This distinguishes them from weaker general words like أي (any) or alladhi (those who).
5
Derive practical rulings
Use the rule to interpret general statements. For example, if a ruler says 'كل جندي يحصل على جائزة' (every soldier gets a reward), each individual soldier is entitled. If he says 'كل الجنود يحملون هذا السلاح' (all the soldiers carry this weapon), it means they jointly carry it, not each individually.
Context and the predicate help confirm the intended meaning.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed explanation of the word 'kull' (كل) as a term of generality ('amm) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author begins with the rule: when 'kull' enters upon a nakirah (indefinite noun), it gives generality for individuals (عموم افراد), meaning each individual is considered separately. When it enters upon a ma'rifah (definite noun), it gives generality for the collective (عموم مجموع), meaning all individuals together form the subject of the ruling. The Arabic text provides a linguistic analysis using the example of 'من دخل هذا الحصن أولا' (whoever enters this fortress first). Without 'kull', 'first' (أول) is taken literally as a single individual. But with 'kull', since 'kull' demands multiplicity, 'first' must be interpreted metaphorically (مجاز) as 'the one preceding relative to those behind', allowing multiple individuals to each be 'first' relative to those who entered after them. The Urdu commentary elaborates: if ten enter together, each is entitled to a reward because each is 'first' relative to the others? Actually, the example is about a reward for the first entrant, and if ten enter simultaneously, each is considered first only if 'kull' is used. The passage then discusses a potential objection: verses like 'خلق كل شيء' (He created everything) and 'أوتيت كل شيء' (she was given everything) seem to contradict the idea that 'kull' is muhkam (strong) in generality, since they exclude certain things. The author provides three answers: first, 'muhkam' here means it cannot be reduced to a single individual; second, in the first verse, 'شيء' means 'thing of existence', excluding Allah and attributes; in the second, rational specification occurs; third, 'muhkam' means it cannot be further specified by qiyas after specification by a text. Finally, the passage reiterates the rule with practical examples: 'كل رجل' (every man) means each individually, while 'كل الرجال' (all men) means collectively. This is a key distinction in Hanafi usul for understanding the scope of general commands in the Quran and Sunnah.

Key Terms
كل (kull): A word of generality that upon entering a nakirah indicates individual generality (عموم افراد), and upon entering a ma'rifah indicates collective generality (عموم مجموع).
عموم (umum): Generality; the inclusion of all individuals covered by the term without exception by virtue of the word itself.
نكرة (nakirah): An indefinite noun; when kull enters upon it, the ruling applies to each individual separately.
معرفة (ma'rifah): A definite noun; when kull enters upon it, the ruling applies to the collective whole.
محكم (muhkam): In this context, a word of generality that is strong in indicating generality and cannot be further specified by qiyas after textual specification; it does not become restricted to a single individual.

Distinguishing 'Man' and 'Kull' in Conditional Reward Allocation: A Hanafi Usul Analysis
Analyzing the usul distinction between 'man' and 'kull' in reward allocation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'umum 'ala sabīl al-infirād and 'umum 'ala sabīl al-badalīyah, and apply these distinctions to determine reward entitlement in conditional statements involving 'awwal'.

Core Idea
The word 'awwal' shifts from real to metaphorical meaning when 'kull' replaces 'man', changing the reward from a single individual to a group sharing one reward.

This passage examines a classic usul problem: how the same reward conditional yields different rulings based on the choice of 'man' vs 'kull'. The analysis centers on the nature of generality ('umum) and the dual meaning of 'awwal'. You'll see how Hanafi usul resolves a potential contradiction between literal and metaphorical interpretation through context (qarīna).

Guided Path
1
Identify the reward statements
Focus on the two key phrases: 'man dakhala awwalan' (whoever enters first) and 'kull man dakhala awwalan' or 'jami' man dakhala awwalan' (all/everyone who enters first). Note the different rulings for simultaneous entry.
The 'man' version gives no reward to simultaneous entrants; the 'kull' version gives one collective reward.
2
Understand the two types of generality
Distinguish between 'umum 'ala sabīl al-infirād (generality affecting each individual separately) and 'umum 'ala sabīl al-badalīyah (generality on a substitutive basis, where only one can fulfill the condition). The former applies to 'kull', the latter to 'man'.
These are Hanafi usul concepts critical for interpreting conditional statements.
3
Recognize the two meanings of 'awwal'
'Awwal' has a real (haqiqi) meaning: an individual preceding all others, unique and not multiple. It also has a metaphorical (majazi) meaning: preceding relative to those behind, which admits multiplicity. The choice of meaning depends on the word governing 'awwal'.
In isolation, 'awwal' defaults to its real meaning. Context can shift it to metaphorical.
4
Apply the appropriate meaning
In 'man dakhala awwalan', use the real meaning + badalīyah: only one person can be 'first' relative to all others, so simultaneous entrants get no reward. In 'kull man dakhala awwalan', use the metaphorical meaning + infirād: each entrant is 'first' relative to those behind, so each qualifies but they share one collective reward.
The shift occurs because 'kull' introduces a new generality that overrides the substitutive one.
5
Analyze the objection and resolution
Fakhr al-Islam said 'jami'' is a metaphor for 'kull', but an objector argued this forces combining haqiqa and majaz in the same utterance. The commentator refines: only the first aspect (entitlement of the first, whether one or many) is intended, not that each individual gets a full reward. Context of incitement supports this.
This resolution avoids the usul impossibility of combining literal and metaphorical meanings.

Here, each of the ten men is first in relation to those who lag behind, disregarding others, so each deserves full reward. In the case of ten entering together, the meaning of precedence is not found in any of them, so no one deserves reward. There is another difference between the two mentioned cases. The second meaning is 'preceding with respect to others' – that is, preceding in relation to different ones – this is a metaphorical meaning, and multiplicity can occur: it can be one individual, or there can be several individuals who are preceding in relation to those lagging behind. But when 'kull' is added to it, there remains this same generality as in the case without 'kull' – that 'first' can apply to each one – and another generality emerges: that 'first' can be multiple persons. So in this case, the substitutive meaning is eliminated, because if that meaning remained, 'kull' would be of no benefit. So the conclusion is that in the case of 'kull', the metaphorical meaning of 'awwal' is intended: the person who is preceding in relation to those who lag behind deserves the reward. Therefore, if ten people enter together, each of them possesses this meaning, so each deserves the full reward. And if ten men enter separately, only the one who entered first deserves the full reward. (And) 'jami'' has its generality on the basis of combination. So if he says: 'All who enter this fortress first, for them is such-and-such', and ten enter, they have one reward. If they enter separately, the first deserves [the reward]. Thus 'jami'' becomes a metaphor for 'kull'. This is what Fakhr al-Islam (may Allah have mercy on him) mentioned in his Usul. It is not possible to say that if entry occurs collectively it is taken in the real meaning, and if individually it is taken in the metaphorical, because at the time of utterance one of them must be specifically intended, and intending each one specifically contradicts intending the other; thus combining real and metaphorical is necessary. Here the first matter is intended, so that the first deserves the reward whether one or more. The real meaning and the second matter are not intended, so that if a group enters, the whole group deserves one reward. Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul analysis of the difference between the words 'man' (whoever) and 'kull' (every/all) in the context of a reward for being the first to enter a fortress. The author, commenting on a matn from Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi, explains that when one says 'man dakhala awwalan' (whoever enters first), the word 'awwal' (first) is taken in its real (haqiqi) meaning: an individual who precedes all others. The generality here is on a substitutive basis (ala sabīl al-badalīyah), meaning only one person can be first. If multiple enter simultaneously, no one qualifies because the real meaning of 'awwal' is not found in any. However, when 'kull' is added to form 'kull man dakhala awwalan' or 'jami' man dakhala awwalan', a different ruling applies. 'Kull' introduces a second kind of generality that allows 'awwal' to be taken metaphorically (majazi) as 'preceding relative to others behind'. So if ten enter together, each one is first in relation to those who lag behind, and each gets the full reward. This is because the metaphorical meaning admits multiplicity. The author also addresses an objection: Fakhr al-Islam said that in the phrase 'jami' man dakhala awwalan', the word 'jami'' is a metaphor (musta'ar) for 'kull' because its generality is on the basis of combination, leading to one reward for a group entering separately? The objector says this forces combining real and metaphorical use. The commentator resolves it by explaining that the intended meaning is only the first aspect: that the first (whether one or many) deserves reward, without specifying that each of a group gets full reward. The context of incitement supports this.

Key Terms
عموم علی سبیل الانفراد ('umūm 'alā sabīl al-infirād): Generality where the ruling applies to each individual separately, without requiring simultaneity.
عموم علی سبیل البدلیت ('umūm 'alā sabīl al-badalīyah): Generality where the ruling applies to individuals on a substitutive basis, i.e., only one can fulfill the condition at a time.
اول (awwal): Two meanings: (1) real: an individual preceding all others without multiplicity; (2) metaphorical: preceding relative to those behind, admitting multiplicity.
جمع بین الحقیقة والمجاز (jam' bayn al-ḥaqīqah wa al-majāz): Combining the real and metaphorical meanings in a single usage, which is generally deemed impossible in Hanafi usul.

Interpretation of جَمِيع in Conditional Reward Statements
Linguistic analysis of 'جميع' in usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that 'al-jam‘ bayna al-ḥaqīqah wa al-majāz' is impermissible to evaluate interpretations of 'جميع' in conditional reward statements and determine the correct entitlement for the first entrant.

Core Idea
The word 'جميع' is a metaphor for the first entrant, not each individual, to avoid combining literal and metaphorical intentions.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi usul problem: how to interpret the word 'جميع' when used in a conditional reward statement like 'جميع من دخل هذا الحصن أولا فله كذا'. The challenge is to determine who is entitled to the reward when multiple people enter together or individually. The discussion hinges on the distinction between haqiqah (literal) and majaz (metaphorical) and the principle that one cannot intend both in a single utterance.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Scenario and the Linguistic Issue
Identify the key elements: the reward statement, the condition of entering first, and the ambiguity of 'جميع' between collectivity and individuality.
2
Analyze Fakhral-Islam's Position
He interprets 'جميع' as a metaphor for 'each' (every individual). Note the objection: this leads to combining literal and metaphorical intentions.
3
Trace the Author's Refinement
The author reinterprets 'جميع' as a metaphor for 'the first entrant' (السابق), avoiding the combination problem. Understand the reasoning based on context of encouragement and the absence of evidence for individual rewards in group entry.
4
Derive the Consequence for Group Entry
Apply the refined interpretation: if a group enters together, they all share one reward, because the metaphor applies to the first entrant, and collectively they are all first.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed linguistic and usuli analysis of the word 'جميع' (all) when used in a conditional reward statement. The scenario is: a leader says 'All who enter this fortress first will receive a reward.' The question arises: if ten people enter together, do they all get a full reward, or only one reward? Fakhral-Islam (the Hanafi usuli) held that 'جميع' is a metaphor for 'each' (كل), so that each individual entrant gets a reward. The author here defends that view against an objection that it would then combine literal and metaphorical meaning in one utterance, which is impermissible. The author argues that the word is used metaphorically to mean 'the first entrant' whether one or many, and that the context of encouragement shows that collectivity is not a condition. Thus, if a group enters together, they get only one reward, because the metaphor applies to the first entrant, and if they entered together, they are all equally first? Actually the author says 'the first' (السابق) is entitled; so if they enter together, they share the reward? The text concludes that if a group enters, they have one reward, not each a full reward. This is a nuanced argument about how to avoid combining literal and metaphorical intention. The key point is that the word is used metaphorically to indicate the entitlement of the first entrant, regardless of number, and the reward is not multiplied for each individual in a group entry.

Key Terms
جَمِيع (jamī‘): Here, originally denotes collectivity but is used metaphorically to indicate the first entrant.
حَقِيقَة (ḥaqīqah): Literal meaning; the word's original sense of collectivity.
مَجَاز (majāz): Metaphorical meaning; here, the word is used metaphorically to mean the first entrant.
الجَمْع بَيْنَ الحَقِيقَة والمَجَاز (al-jam‘ bayna al-ḥaqīqah wa al-majāz): Combining literal and metaphorical intentions in one utterance, which is considered impermissible.

Ruling on the Word 'Jami'' and Its Generality
Explaining the ruling of 'jami' and responding to objections about haqiqah and majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of 'umum 'ala sabil al-ijtima' to the word 'jami'' and evaluate the resolution of the objection regarding combining haqiqah and majaz.

Core Idea
The word 'jami'' conveys collective generality; its metaphorical interpretation does not combine haqiqah and majaz but reflects a broader metonymy.

In Hanafi usul, the word 'jami'' (all) is not a straightforward universal quantifier. Its generality operates on the basis of assembly, not distribution. Moreover, when context demands, it can be interpreted metaphorically without violating the prohibition against combining literal and figurative meanings. This passage from Sharh al-Talwih unpacks this rule and defends it against a classic objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Ruling
State the principle: the word 'jami'' carries 'umum 'ala sabil al-ijtima' – the ruling applies to all individuals collectively, not severally.
This is the foundational premise of the analysis.
2
Understand the Example and Its Scenarios
Examine the example 'All who enter this fortress first will get such-and-such reward'. Distinguish the two scenarios: collective entry (ten enter together) and individual entry (each enters singly). Determine the outcome in each according to Hanafi interpretation.
Collective entry yields one reward for the group; individual entry gives the reward only to the first entrant.
3
Recognize the Objection
The objection claims that this interpretation combines literal meaning (collective reward) and metaphorical meaning (individual reward) in one word, which is invalid in usul.
The objection is based on the principle that a single word cannot simultaneously carry two different intended meanings.
4
Analyze the Weak Response and Its Refutation
Review the weak response that the intended meaning is determined by external circumstances. Then understand the refutation: meaning is fixed at the time of speech, not by later events. Hence the weak response fails.
This step underscores the importance of the moment of utterance (zaman al-takallum).
5
Appreciate the Author's Resolution via 'Umum al-Majaz
Study the author's own response: 'jami'' is borrowed (musta'ar) for 'kull' in a broad sense that covers both collective and individual precedence. This is not combining haqiqah and majaz but a single metaphorical interpretation that includes both scenarios. The context of encouraging precedence supports this reading.
The resolution demonstrates the Hanafi method of reconciling textual interpretation with legal purpose (maqsad).

Commentary
This passage is a detailed textual analysis of the word 'جميع' (jami') and its ruling regarding 'umum (generality). The author begins by stating the core rule: the generality in 'جميع' is 'عموم على سبيل الاجتماع' (generality on the basis of assembly), meaning that when the word 'جميع' is used, the ruling applies to all individuals collectively as a single group, not to each individual separately. He then illustrates with the example: 'جميع من دخل هذا الحصن أولاً فله كذا' (All who enter this fortress first will have such-and-such). If ten people enter together, they receive one reward collectively; if they enter singly, only the first entrant gets the reward. In the latter case, 'جميع' is carried metaphorically to mean 'كُلّ' (every) with the sense of individual entitlement for the first entrant alone.

Then an objection is raised: This approach seems to combine literal (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz) meanings of 'جميع' in different scenarios, which is generally deemed invalid in usul. The author first records a weak response that attempts to avoid the problem by saying the intended meaning varies with the external situation. He refutes this by pointing out that the intended meaning is determined at the time of speech, not by later external events; thus a single speech act cannot have two different intentions. He then provides his own correct response: 'جميع' is not used literally here; rather, it is borrowed (musta'ar) for the word 'كُلّ' in a specific sense that encompasses both collective and individual precedence. This is a case of 'عموم المجاز' (generality of metaphor), where the metaphor covers both scenarios without combining contradictory intentions. The author explains that taking 'جميع' literally would yield an undesirable result (denying the sole entrant reward), so it must be interpreted metaphorically to preserve the purpose of encouraging precedence. This demonstrates the Hanafi usul principle of interpreting words in light of context and purpose, and shows how objections about combining haqiqah and majaz are resolved by showing that the metaphorical meaning is broader and includes the relevant scenarios.

Does Hikayat al-Fi'l Indicate Generality? Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Analyzing whether narration of Prophet's action indicates generality, with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on hikayat al-fi'l, identify the underlying usul principle, and apply the Hanafi method of extending rulings via qiyas when the action is ambiguous.

Core Idea
A narrated action of the Prophet is not automatically general; Hanafis treat it like mushtarak and use qiyas to extend the ruling.

The question of whether a Companion's report of the Prophet's action (hikayat al-fi'l) establishes a general rule has been a point of contention between Hanafis and Shafi'is. This passage clarifies the Hanafi methodology: the action itself is context-specific, but its underlying rationale may be extended by analogy. The example of the Prophet praying inside the Kaaba illustrates how Hanafis permit fard prayer there by qiyas, while Shafi'is restrict it to nafl.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
What is the dispute about hikayat al-fi'l and generality? Determine the core question being debated.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafis hold that hikayat al-fi'l does not indicate generality; it is like mushtarak (shared among possible meanings). One must seek evidence to prefer a meaning, and if none, the ruling applies to all possibilities by qiyas.
Mushtarak means the action is ambiguous in scope.
3
State the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the action is specific to its particular context and cannot be extended to other cases without separate evidence.
4
Trace the Hanafi reasoning with the Kaaba example
The Prophet prayed inside the Kaaba (nafl). Hanafis argue that the underlying illa (facing qibla) is common to both nafl and fard; thus by qiyas, fard is also permissible inside the Kaaba.
Shafi'is say the action is specific to nafl because turning one's back to part of Kaaba is only tolerated in nafl.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafis allow fard prayer inside Kaaba; Shafi'is do not. This shows how usul principles affect fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute-based discussion on the scope of 'hikayat al-fi'l' (narration of the Prophet's action) and whether it denotes generality ('umum). The author first addresses an objection regarding the hadith 'qada bil-shuf'a lil-jar' (He judged with pre-emption for the neighbor). The opponent argues that if hikayat al-fi'l is not general, then this narration should be specific to the particular neighbor who was a partner, not general to all neighbors. The author responds that this narration is not hikayat al-fi'l but rather transmission of the Prophet's statement by meaning (riwaya bi al-ma'na), so it is like a general verbal statement. Even if considered hikayat al-fi'l, the word 'al-jar' (the neighbor) with the lam istighraq indicates generality. Then the author delves into the disagreement of the imams on the ruling of hikayat al-fi'l. Some argue that a just, linguistically knowledgeable Companion's narration of an action as general proves generality. The majority of usuliyyun (including both Shafi'is and Hanafis) hold that hikayat al-fi'l does not indicate generality because the action occurred in a specific context. However, within this majority there is a nuance: Imam al-Shafi'i says the action remains specific to its particular circumstance, while the Hanafis say the action becomes like a mushtarak (shared meaning) among various possibilities; one must seek evidence to prefer one meaning, and if no preference is found, the ruling applies to all by analogy (qiyas). The author illustrates with the example of the Prophet praying inside the Kaaba. Shafi'i argues this permits only nafl (supererogatory) prayer because the narrated action was nafl, and fard requires facing the qibla without turning one's back to any part of the Kaaba. Hanafis counter that the underlying illa (reason) is the same for both fard and nafl – facing the qibla is obligatory in both, and if some deviation is allowed in nafl by the Prophet's action, then by qiyas it is allowed in fard as well. This demonstrates the Hanafi method of extending rulings through qiyas when the action's context does not provide a specific distinction. Finally, the author returns to the shuf'a example, explaining that the Hanafis accept the generality of the hadith because it is a verbal statement transmitted by meaning, not a mere action narration. The Shafi'i objection that hikayat al-fi'l lacks generality is answered by distinguishing the nature of the transmitted report.

Key Terms
حکایت فعل (hikayat al-fi'l): Narration of the Prophet's action (fi'l) by a Companion, as opposed to narration of his saying.
عموم (umum): Generality; a word covering all individuals or instances it can include.
شفعة (shuf'a): Pre-emption right of a co-owner or neighbor to buy the sold share of property.
روایت بالمعنی (riwaya bi al-ma'na): Transmitting the meaning of a hadith rather than its exact wording.

Distinguishing Hikayat al-Fi'l from Riwaya bi al-Ma'na: The Shuf'a Example
Analyzing whether narration of Prophet's action indicates generality, with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between narration of an action (hikayat al-fi'l) and narration of a saying by meaning (riwaya bi al-ma'na), and explain why the Hanafis treat the shuf'a hadith as general.

Core Idea
A narration of the Prophet's statement transmitted by meaning is treated like a general verbal command, not a context-specific action.

The hadith 'qada bil-shuf'a lil-jar' is a key proof for the Hanafi ruling that the right of pre-emption extends to neighboring non-partners. The Shafi'is object that this is hikayat al-fi'l and thus specific to the original partner-neighbor. The Hanafis respond that this narration is not of an action but of a saying transmitted by meaning, and the word 'al-jar' includes all neighbors due to the lam istighraq. This lesson clarifies the critical distinction between types of narrations and its impact on generality.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on shuf'a
According to Hanafis, shuf'a is established for three categories: partner in the property, partner in rights (like irrigation), and adjacent neighbor (jar mulasiq). This is broader than the Shafi'i position.
The Hanafi position relies on the hadith 'qada bil-shuf'a lil-jar'.
2
State the Shafi'i objection
Shafi'is argue that the narration is hikayat al-fi'l (a report of the Prophet's action) and thus specific to the particular neighbor involved (who was a partner). Hence, it does not extend to non-partner neighbors.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response: distinguish the type of narration
The Hanafis respond that the narration is not hikayat al-fi'l but riwaya bi al-ma'na of the Prophet's saying: 'al-shuf'a li al-jar'. It is a verbal statement transmitted by meaning, so it is treated as a general verbal command.
Riwaya bi al-ma'na is acceptable when the transmitter is knowledgeable of the language.
4
Examine the linguistic argument
Even if considered hikayat al-fi'l, the word 'al-jar' includes the lam istighraq, which indicates generality (all neighbors). The lam is for istighraq al-jins because there is no specific referent (ma'hud).
Lam istighraq is a grammatical indicator of generality.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafis grant shuf'a to non-partner neighbors; Shafi'is do not. This shows how the classification of a narration affects fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute-based discussion on the scope of 'hikayat al-fi'l' (narration of the Prophet's action) and whether it denotes generality ('umum). The author first addresses an objection regarding the hadith 'qada bil-shuf'a lil-jar' (He judged with pre-emption for the neighbor). The opponent argues that if hikayat al-fi'l is not general, then this narration should be specific to the particular neighbor who was a partner, not general to all neighbors. The author responds that this narration is not hikayat al-fi'l but rather transmission of the Prophet's statement by meaning (riwaya bi al-ma'na), so it is like a general verbal statement. Even if considered hikayat al-fi'l, the word 'al-jar' (the neighbor) with the lam istighraq indicates generality. Then the author delves into the disagreement of the imams on the ruling of hikayat al-fi'l. Some argue that a just, linguistically knowledgeable Companion's narration of an action as general proves generality. The majority of usuliyyun (including both Shafi'is and Hanafis) hold that hikayat al-fi'l does not indicate generality because the action occurred in a specific context. However, within this majority there is a nuance: Imam al-Shafi'i says the action remains specific to its particular circumstance, while the Hanafis say the action becomes like a mushtarak (shared meaning) among various possibilities; one must seek evidence to prefer one meaning, and if no preference is found, the ruling applies to all by analogy (qiyas). The author illustrates with the example of the Prophet praying inside the Kaaba. Shafi'i argues this permits only nafl (supererogatory) prayer because the narrated action was nafl, and fard requires facing the qibla without turning one's back to any part of the Kaaba. Hanafis counter that the underlying illa (reason) is the same for both fard and nafl – facing the qibla is obligatory in both, and if some deviation is allowed in nafl by the Prophet's action, then by qiyas it is allowed in fard as well. This demonstrates the Hanafi method of extending rulings through qiyas when the action's context does not provide a specific distinction. Finally, the author returns to the shuf'a example, explaining that the Hanafis accept the generality of the hadith because it is a verbal statement transmitted by meaning, not a mere action narration. The Shafi'i objection that hikayat al-fi'l lacks generality is answered by distinguishing the nature of the transmitted report.

Shuf'a for the Neighbor: General vs. Specific Cause
Discussion on whether a non-sharik neighbor has right of pre-emption.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on shuf'a for a non-sharik neighbor, identify the two Hanafi responses to the Shafi'i objection, and evaluate the usul principle regarding 'al' for istighraq.

Core Idea
The definite article 'al' can indicate generality even in reported actions, supporting the Hanafi extension of shuf'a to all neighbors.

The right of pre-emption (shuf'a) is a key property right. The Hanafi school extends it to non-sharik adjacent neighbors, while the Shafi'i school restricts it to co-owners. This dispute hinges on usul al-fiqh principles: how to interpret a narrated action of the Prophet and the scope of the definite article. Understanding these principles will sharpen your ability to evaluate hadith-based arguments.

1

Clearly state the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on shuf'a for a non-sharik neighbor. Note the practical difference: who can claim pre-emption.
Hanafi: yes; Shafi'i: no. The practical consequence is the thamarah al-khilaf.
2

Locate the core hadith cited: 'al-shuf'a thabita li al-jar' and the Companion's report 'qada bi al-shuf'a li al-jar'. Understand that these are the proofs.
The dalil is a hadith ahad, so thubut is zanni.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i objection
The Shafi'i objection argues that the reported action is specific (khass) to the sharik neighbor because it is a hikayat al-fi'l (narration of an action), which according to usul has no 'umum (generality).
Hikayat al-fi'l is generally considered specific to the observed circumstances in Hanafi usul too.
4
Trace the first Hanafi response
The first response denies that the narration is a hikayat al-fi'l; instead, it is riwaya bi al-ma'na (narration by meaning) of the Prophet's statement. Thus the objection is misdirected.
This shifts the discussion to the wording of the original hadith.

Assuming it were hikayat al-fi'l, the Hanafis argue that the word 'al-jar' contains the definite article for istighraq (comprehensive generality). Since 'al' is not for a specific covenant, it covers every neighbor, sharik or not.
This is a classic usul argument about the function of 'al'.

Conclude the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi fiqh, a non-sharik neighbor can claim shuf'a; under Shafi'i, he cannot. This shows how usul principles directly affect fiqh rulings.
The tarjih favors the Hanafi position.

Classifying Statements After a Question or Incident
Usul rule classifying post-question statements into four types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a statement that appears after a question or incident into one of four Hanafi categories and derive the appropriate interpretation (jawab or ibtida').

Core Idea
A statement after a question is not always an answer; an addition (ziyada) indicates a new beginning.

In Hanafi usul, the context of a question or incident does not automatically turn every following statement into an answer. The school has developed a precise classification based on independence and presence of an addition. This lesson unpacks the four categories and the principle that the general wording is considered over the specific cause, highlighting a key difference with Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Determine independence of the statement
First, check if the statement is mustaqil (independent) or not. Non-independent statements like 'bala' or 'na'am' are clear answers.
Non-independent statements always class as answers.
2
Classify among the four categories
If independent, further classify: clearly an answer (category 2), apparently an answer (category 3), or apparently a beginning (category 4). The text gives examples: 'He forgot and performed sajdah' for category 2, 'If I have lunch... without addition' for category 3, and 'If I have lunch today... with addition' for category 4.
Look for the presence/absence of an addition (ziyada) beyond what is needed for a direct answer.
3
Apply default rule for first three categories
Categories 1-3 (non-independent, clearly answer, apparently answer) are all treated as jawab (answer). The statement is interpreted in light of the preceding context.
Hanafis and Shafi'is agree on these three.
4
Handle the fourth category according to Hanafis
If the statement is independent and includes an addition (ziyada) beyond the scope of a simple answer, it is considered ibtida' (beginning of a new statement). The addition is presumed to convey new information.
This is the Hanafi position; Shafi'is treat it as an answer.
5
Understand the underlying principle and dispute
The Hanafi approach is based on the principle al-'ibra li 'umum al-lafz la li khusus al-sabab (consideration is for the general wording, not the specific cause). The addition triggers a new intent. The Shafi'i school gives more weight to the context and treats all independent statements after a question as answers.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) affects rulings on oaths, divorces, and testimonies.

Classification of Speech After a Question: Four Categories
Explains four categories of speech after a question and Hanafi ruling on each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify speech after a question into the four Hanafi categories and apply the rule that extra words indicate an independent statement.

Core Idea
Extra words in a response change it from jawab to ibtida' — new benefit creates new ruling.

This passage presents a foundational tool in Hanafi usul: how to determine whether speech following a question or event is a response (jawab) or an independent statement (ibtida'). The classification hinges on two features: independence of meaning and the presence of extra words. Understanding this classification is crucial for correctly interpreting legal texts, especially when general rulings are derived from specific incidents.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish independent vs. non-independent speech
First, determine if the speech makes sense on its own (mustaqil) or requires the preceding context (ghayr mustaqil).
Non-independent examples: 'na'am' (yes).
2
Among independent, identify three subtypes
Decide if the independent speech is definitely a response, apparently a response, or apparently a beginning (with possibility of response).
Examples from the passage: 'He forgot and prostrated' (definite answer), 'If I have lunch...' (apparent answer without extra), 'If I have lunch today...' (apparent beginning with extra).
3
Apply the Hanafi rule for the fourth category
When extra words are present beyond the minimal answer, the speech is treated as an independent statement (ibtida') because the extra words must be given effect.
The principle: al-'ibratu li 'umumi al-lafz — consider generality of wording.
4
Note the Shafi'i disagreement and the thamarah
Al-Shafi'i holds all speech after a question is a response. The practical consequence: in legal contexts, the extra words may create a new ruling under Hanafi but not under Shafi'i.
The author supports the Hanafi position by citing the Companions' practice.

Commentary
This passage lays out a key usul rule: how to interpret speech that occurs after a question or incident. The author provides a four-part classification. First, speech may be non-independent (ghayr mustaqil) – it has no meaning without the preceding context, like the simple replies 'bala' (yes) or 'na'am' (yes) to a question. Second, speech may be independent and clearly a response (jawab qat'an), like the hadith 'He forgot and prostrated' and 'Ma'iz committed adultery and was stoned' – these are complete sentences that only make sense as responses to a question about what happened. Third, speech may be independent and apparently a response, but with the possibility of being a new statement (ibtida'), like inviting someone to lunch and the reply 'If I have lunch, then such-and-such' without any extra words – the structure suggests a conditional response. Fourth, speech may be independent and apparently a new statement, with the possibility of being a response, like the same reply but with extra words (e.g., 'If I have lunch today' – adding 'today' shows extra detail). According to the Hanafi school, the first three categories are always treated as responses, because the context clearly indicates that. The fourth category is treated as an independent statement (ibtida') because the extra words signal a new benefit (fa'ida jadida). However, if the speaker claims he intended it as a response, he is believed in his internal intention (diyanatan) but the ruling (qada'an) follows the apparent meaning. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, holding that all speech after a question is treated as a response regardless of extra words. The author then ties this to the famous usul principle: 'Consideration is for the generality of the wording, not for the specificity of the cause' (al-'ibratu li 'umumi al-lafz la li khususi al-sabab). This means that when a general statement comes in response to a specific incident, its ruling is general, applying to all similar cases, not limited to that incident. The Companions themselves followed this principle, citing general texts that were revealed for specific events. The passage thus demonstrates how Hanafi usul uses context and extra wording to determine the legal weight of a statement.

مُسْتَقِل (mustaqil): Speech that is independently meaningful, not requiring the preceding context to be understood.
غَيْر مُسْتَقِل (ghayr mustaqil): Speech that is not independently meaningful; it relies on the preceding question or incident for its meaning.
جَوَاب (jawab): A response that directly answers a preceding question or addresses a preceding incident.

Classification of Independent Speech and the Principle of General Wording
Analysis of four speech types and a usul dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify independent speech (kalam mustaqil) into its four types and apply the Hanafi principle that rulings are based on general wording (umum al-lafz) to determine whether a statement is a response or an initiation.

Core Idea
General wording governs, not the specific cause — added words must not be futile.

Understanding how to classify independent speech is crucial for deriving rulings from texts. This lesson introduces the four types of kalam mustaqil based on their relationship to preceding speech, then applies them to a conditional oath scenario. You will see how the Hanafi usul principle that consideration is given to the generality of the wording (i'tibar umum al-lafz) leads to a distinct ruling compared to the Shafi'i school, and why the added word 'today' cannot be ignored.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed textual analysis from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary. It classifies independent speech (kalam mustaqil) into four types based on its relationship to preceding speech, then applies this classification to a conditional oath scenario to demonstrate the Hanafi position and contrast it with the Shafi'i view. The analysis is driven by the fundamental usul principle that consideration is given to the generality of the wording rather than the specificity of the cause (al-ibratu bi-umumi al-lafzi la bi-khusus al-sabab).

The author first describes four types of independent speech:
1. (First type not explicitly given in this excerpt but implied) — possibly speech that is purely independent with no connection.
2. The second type: speech where it is predetermined that it is a response to what preceded, with no possibility of being an independent statement. Example given: a statement that is clearly connected and can only be understood as an answer.
3. The third type: speech that appears to be a response but also could be an independent initiation. Example: 'Come, eat with me.' He said, 'If you eat, my slave is free.' The response is likely conditional but could also be a new statement.
4. The fourth type: speech that appears to be an initiation but could possibly be a response. Example: adding a temporal qualifier like 'today' to the conditional response, which suggests new information.

The Hanafi ruling: In types two and three (and presumably the first), the words are interpreted as a response to the prior statement. In the fourth type, however, the addition of 'today' makes the statement appear as an independent initiation, so it is not interpreted as a response. This interpretation preserves the benefit of the added word ('today') as providing new meaning; if it were taken as a mere response, the addition would be superfluous. Consequently, if a person says 'If you eat today, my slave is free' in response to an invitation, and the invitee eats any food on that day (the invited food or other, with the inviter or alone), the oath is broken and the slave is freed, because the condition is not restricted to the invited meal. If the speaker claims he intended it as a response (i.e., meaning only if you eat the invited food with me), his claim is accepted in religious terms (diyaanatan) but not in judicial terms (qadaan) due to the apparent meaning and his personal interest.

Imam al-Shafi'i differs: he treats the fourth type as a response, accepting the speaker's intention both diyaanatan and qadaan.

The passage then explains that the Hanafi view is a practical application of the principle of considering the general wording over the specific cause. The reason is that inference (istidlal) is based on what is apparent (zahir), and the wording is apparent, whereas the specific cause or context (sabab) is internal and not a valid basis for rulings. This principle prevents confining Quranic verses to their occasions of revelation. The passage also distinguishes between inference from ambiguous expressions (which is invalid because it is internal) and from solitary reports (khabar wahid), which is valid because it is external even though it yields only probability.

Key Terms
کلام مستقل (kalam mustaqil): Speech that is syntactically and semantically complete by itself, but its interpretation as a response or initiation depends on context and apparent meaning.
جواب (jawab): A statement that serves as an answer to a preceding statement or question, often conditional.
استیناف (isti'naf): An independent statement that is not a response to what precedes; a fresh start.
اعتبار عموم اللفظ (i'tibar umum al-lafz): The principle that legal rulings are derived from the general wording of a text, not from the specific occasion or cause of revelation (sabab).
خصوص سبب (khusus al-sabab): The specific cause or occasion that prompted a revelation or utterance; not a basis for restricting a general ruling.
