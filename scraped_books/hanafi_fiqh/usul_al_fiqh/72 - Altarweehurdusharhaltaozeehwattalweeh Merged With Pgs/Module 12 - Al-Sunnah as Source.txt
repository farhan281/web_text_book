Justifying the Addition of Mishkāt to Sunnah
Resolving an apparent contradiction in a textual attribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul method of tawjih to resolve apparent inconsistencies in textual attributions by reinterpreting ambiguous terms or focusing on core meanings.

Core Idea
Hanafi tawjih resolves textual contradictions by reinterpreting terms or prioritizing core meanings.

This passage presents a typical Hanafi usul exercise: an objection to the phrase 'Mishkāt al-Sunnah' is raised, followed by two reconciliatory responses. The objection hinges on interpreting 'Mishkāt' as the Prophet's mouth, which only produces sayings, while 'Sunnah' includes actions and approvals. The two responses demonstrate key usul techniques: takhṣīṣ (specification) and ta’wīl (interpretive extension). This lesson will guide you through the objection and responses, illustrating how Hanafi scholars safeguard textual coherence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
What is the apparent inconsistency? The objector assumes 'Mishkāt' means the Prophet's mouth, which only emits sayings; but 'Sunnah' includes actions, sayings, and approvals. Thus the addition seems invalid.
The objection relies on a literal reading of 'Mishkāt' as mouth and a comprehensive definition of 'Sunnah'.
2
Analyze the first response
The first response limits 'Sunnah' to its most important components (a‘ẓam afrād), which are sayings. By specifying 'Sunnah' in this way, the addition becomes correct because sayings do issue from the mouth.
This is takhṣīṣ (specification) by focusing on the most prominent part of a general term.
3
Analyze the second response
The second response reinterprets 'Mishkāt' as the Prophet’s heart or essence, from which all types of Sunnah (actions, sayings, approvals) originate. This removes the inconsistency without restricting 'Sunnah'.
This is ta’wīl (interpretive extension) – giving the term a more inclusive meaning based on context.
4
Compare the two methods
Both responses are valid Hanafi methods of tawjih. The first uses takhṣīṣ (specification) of the predicate; the second uses ta’wīl (extensive interpretation) of the subject. Each preserves the attribution while addressing the objection differently.
In later usul works, such multiple responses are often provided to cover different interpretive possibilities.

Attribution of 'Auqada' to Allah
Explaining why the commentator attributed 'auqada' to Allah instead of the Prophet

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi view that the Sunnah originates from Allah, using the Quranic proof from Surah An-Najm.

Core Idea
The Prophet is a conduit; the real source of Sunnah is Allah.

This lesson addresses a subtle but important point in Hanafi usul: why do commentators attribute the 'illumination' of the Sunnah directly to Allah, even though the Prophet (ﷺ) is the one who conveyed it? The answer lies in the divine nature of prophetic speech, as established by the Quran. Understanding this reinforces the binding authority of the Sunnah as revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
The question assumes that since the Prophet (ﷺ) actively explained the Sunnah, attribution should go to him. Recognize why this assumption might seem natural.
2
Locate the Quranic Proof
The commentator cites Quran 53:3-4. Read the verse and note its direct statement that the Prophet does not speak from personal desire but from revelation.
The verse is Surah An-Najm, Ayahs 3-4.
3
Analyze the Inference
From the verse, derive that every utterance of the Prophet in matters of religion is divinely inspired. Therefore, the ultimate source is Allah, making the Prophet an intermediary (wāsita).
Hanafi usul holds that the Sunnah is wahy ghayr matluww (unrecited revelation).
4
Apply to the Attribution
Conclude that attributing 'auqada' to Allah is correct because the action originates from Him, even though it manifests through the Prophet.
This principle applies to all Prophetic statements of religious import.

Reconciling Conflicting Hadiths: Basmalah and Hamdalah
Analyzing the author's use of hal to combine two hadiths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of combining conflicting texts (ta'arud) by identifying the interpretive technique used and deriving a reconciled ruling.

Core Idea
Two apparently contradictory hadiths can be reconciled by making one actual and the other relative — thus both are observed.

In this passage from al-Talwih, the commentator Taftazani explains why the author Sadr al-Shariah chose to write 'hamidan lillah' after the basmalah. This seemingly grammatical choice solves a deeper usul problem: two hadiths demand beginning important matters with basmalah and hamdalah respectively, yet one cannot begin with both simultaneously. Understanding this reconciliation teaches you a fundamental Hanafi method for handling textual ta'arud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two hadiths and their apparent conflict
The passage quotes two hadiths: one requires basmalah at the start of any important matter, the other requires hamdalah. Literally, beginning with one precludes beginning with the other, creating an apparent contradiction.
The hadith texts use the words 'abtar' (severed) and 'ajzam' (defective) to emphasize the necessity.
2
Spot the interpretive tool used by the author
The author uses 'hamidan' as a hal (circumstantial accusative), which describes the state of the subject during the beginning. This makes the hamdalah a condition or accompaniment, not the actual beginning itself.
The hal construction is a grammatical device that integrates the praise into the action without making it the initial act.
3
Understand the reconciliation mechanism
By making basmalah the actual beginning and hamdalah relative (as a hal), both hadiths are fulfilled: the beginning is with basmalah in reality, and with hamdalah in a relational sense.
This is an application of the usul principle 'al-jam' awla min al-tarjih' (reconciliation is preferable to preferring one over the other).
4
Apply the principle to other cases
This method of combining texts through grammatical or contextual interpretation is widely used in Hanafi usul. Whenever two texts appear to conflict, the Hanafi school prioritizes jam' (reconciliation) by adjusting the scope or modality of one text.
Example: combining hadiths on raising hands in prayer is another instance where Hanafis use jam'.

He preferred the circumstantial construction over the usual practice of a nominal or verbal sentence like 'al-hamdu lillah' or 'ahmadu Allah' to create equality between praise and naming (basmalah) and to maintain harmony between them. For it is reported in the hadith: 'Every matter of importance that does not begin with Bismillah is severed (abtar),' and 'Every matter of importance that does not begin with praise of Allah is defective (ajzam).' He attempted to make praise a condition for the beginning, coming as a hal from it, just as the basmalah occurs as a hal. It was possible to combine them by presenting one before the other, so that the beginning is actually with that one and relatively with the second. Commentary
This passage from al-Talwih (Taftazani's super-commentary) analyzes the opening phrase of al-Tawdih. The author, Sadr al-Shariah, wrote 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim hamidan lillah'. The word 'hamidan' (praising) is a hal (circumstantial accusative) referring to the implicit subject of the basmalah. The commentator explains that the author chose this construction instead of the usual nominal or verbal praise to give equal weight to both the basmalah and the hamdalah, and to harmonize them. Two hadiths are cited: one says that any important matter not begun with basmalah is 'abtar' (incomplete or cut off from blessing), and another says that any important matter not begun with praise of Allah is 'ajzam' (lacking or defective). These two hadiths appear contradictory if only one act of beginning can be performed. The solution is to begin with one (the basmalah) actually, and the other (hamdalah) relatively, by making the praise a circumstantial clause that accompanies the beginning. Thus both hadiths are observed. The author followed the hadith that gives precedence to basmalah (the one reported with 'abtar') by placing basmalah first. This shows the usul principle of combining contradictory texts when possible.

أبتر (abtar): Severed, cut off from blessing or completion.
أجزَم (ajzam): Defective, lacking in completeness or blessing.

Reconciling Conflicting Hadiths: The Hal Construction
Analyzing the grammatical and hadith basis for the author's opening

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul method of jam' (reconciliation) to conflicting hadiths by analyzing the grammatical hal construction and deriving its legal effect.

Core Idea
When two hadiths command different beginnings, the Hanafi method reconciles by making one the real start and the other a circumstantial hal.

The author opens his book with 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim, hamidan lillah' — combining basmalah and hamdalah in a single phrase. This is not accidental. Two hadiths appear to command beginning with one or the other. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi usul technique of reconciling such conflicting texts through grammatical construction, a powerful tool in istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent conflict
Both hadiths use imperative language (amr) implying obligation to begin with that specific phrase. Since beginning with one precludes the other, they seem contradictory.
2
Apply the principle of jam'
The Hanafi method prioritizes reconciling texts over abandoning them. Here, one text can be made primary and the other secondary via a grammatical hal phrase.
3
Determine which text is given precedence
The author gives precedence to basmalah based on Quranic practice and ijma'. Hamdalah is attached as a hal, making it a secondary beginning.
4
Analyze the grammatical mechanism
'Hamidan' is a hal (circumstantial adverbial) from the implied subject of the basmalah, meaning 'I begin praising Allah'. This fulfills both hadiths without contradiction.
5
Evaluate the effect of omitting the conjunction
The author omits 'wa' between basmalah and hamdalah to avoid implying subordination, thus treating both as equal in importance.

His saying 'In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful, praising Allah' is a hal (circumstantial adverbial) from the implied pronoun in the prepositional phrase related to the ba' — that is, 'I begin the book, praising Allah.' He preferred the method of hal over what is customary among them of nominal or verbal sentences like 'Praise be to Allah' and 'I praise Allah' to equalize praise and naming and to observe correspondence between them. For it has come in the hadith: 'Every matter of importance that is not begun with Bismillah is severed (abtar), and every matter of importance that is not begun with Alhamdulillah is maimed (ajzam).' So he intended to make praise a restriction for the beginning, as a hal from it, just as naming (basmalah) occurred likewise. However, he gave precedence to naming because the two texts are apparently conflicting, since beginning with one of the two things precludes beginning with the other. And reconciliation is possible by giving precedence to one over the other, so that beginning with it occurs in reality, and with the other occurs in relation to what is other than it. So he acted upon the text that came with precedence of naming and the consensus established upon it. And he omitted the conjunction so that it would not indicate subordination, thus making them equal. And it is not permissible that 'praising' be a hal from the doer of 'saying' because his saying 'and after' — according to the established copy with the author — prevents that. Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis from the sharh, explaining the author's grammatical choice in the opening line: 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim, hamidan lillah.' The commentator argues that 'hamidan' is a hal (adverbial accusative of state) from the implied subject of the basmalah, meaning 'I begin the book, praising Allah.' The author chose this construction over the conventional nominal or verbal forms (e.g., 'Alhamdulillah') in order to give equal weight to both praise (hamd) and naming (basmalah). This is because two hadiths, one requiring beginning with basmalah and the other with hamdalah, apparently conflict. The author resolves the conflict by beginning with basmalah as the real beginning and attaching praise as a hal, making praise a secondary beginning. Matn Hadith Precedes Ijma Absolutely
Question and answer about absolute precedence of hadith matn over ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to DISTINGUISH between matn and thubut of a hadith and EXPLAIN why the Hanafi school holds that Sunnah (matn) absolutely precedes Ijma.

Core Idea
The matn of a hadith is always clear and definitive; its thubut may be uncertain, so the absolute precedence of Sunnah over Ijma refers to the matn.

At first glance, the claim that 'Sunnah absolutely precedes Ijma' seems to contradict the Shari'ah principle that only definitively established (qat'i al-thubut) Sunnah overrides consensus. This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary resolves the tension by distinguishing between the matn (textual content) and thubut (chain transmission) of a hadith. The matn, being the actual words of the Prophet, is always clear and carries its own authority, while thubut can be subject to doubt due to chain issues. The Companions themselves demonstrated this by accepting the hadith 'Prophets do not inherit' solely based on its clear matn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent contradiction
The author states that Sunnah absolutely precedes Ijma, but the Shari'ah only gives precedence to Sunnah that is qat'i al-thubut (definitively established). This seems inconsistent.
The opponent's objection is based on the assumption that 'Sunnah' in the author's statement includes both matn and thubut.
2
Understand the opponent's objection
The opponent argues that only Sunnah with qat'i al-thubut (e.g., mutawatir) can override Ijma. Since most Sunnah is zanni al-thubut, it cannot absolutely precede Ijma.
This objection is common among those who do not distinguish matn from thubut.
3
Apply the Hanafi response: matn vs thubut
The Hanafi response distinguishes between matn (text) and thubut (chain). The matn of a hadith is always clear and definitive in meaning, regardless of chain authenticity. Thubut, on the other hand, concerns the reliability of transmission.
The matn is the actual prophetic utterance; its meaning is paramount.
4
Examine the evidence from the Companions
The passage cites the example of Abu Bakr (RA) using the hadith 'Prophets do not inherit' (clear matn) to argue about inheritance. All Companions accepted it without questioning its thubut, showing that matn clarity alone is sufficient.
This historical precedent supports the Hanafi view.
5
Conclude with the resolution
Thus, when the author says 'Sunnah absolutely precedes Ijma', he means the matn of the Sunnah, not the entire hadith including its chain. This resolution clears the apparent contradiction.
In practice, a hadith with a clear matn but weak chain still takes precedence over Ijma according to this view.

Definition of Sunnah and the Metaphor of Manassa for Texts
Expansion on the nature of Sunnah and role of mujtahids

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two types of Sunnah (qawl and fi'l) and explain the metaphor of manassa in the context of mujtahids' derivation of rulings.

Core Idea
Sunnah is speech and action; speech explains laws, but rulings are divine while causes are discovered by mujtahids.

This lesson breaks down a key passage from a Hanafi commentary on the definition of Sunnah. The author divides Sunnah into verbal and practical categories, emphasizing that speech is the primary vehicle for conveying Sharia rulings. A rich metaphor comparing texts to a bride's display platform (manassa) is then introduced and defended against a potential misunderstanding. Through this, you will learn a foundational distinction in Hanafi usul: the rulings themselves are divinely revealed, but the underlying causes ('ilal) are extracted by human intellectual effort.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of Sunnah
The passage begins by stating 'al-sunnah darbani qawlun wa fi'lun' — Sunnah is of two types: speech (qawl) and action (fi'l). Note that speech is highlighted as the primary means for explaining the laws.
In Hanafi usul, Sunnah also includes taqrir (silent approval), but the passage focuses on qawl and fi'l.
2
Analyze the metaphor of manassa
The commentator explains the word 'manassa' (platform) and how it is used metaphorically: texts are like platforms on which brides (meanings) are displayed for the mujtahids who reflect upon them.
The term 'arūs' (bride/groom) applies to both genders, indicating that the meanings displayed can be either apparent or hidden.
3
Address the potential flaw in the metaphor
A question is raised: if texts are platforms for thinkers' thoughts, does that imply derived meanings are human inventions? The answer distinguishes between two types of meanings: apparent (zahirah) which are the rulings themselves (divine), and hidden (batinah) which are the causes ('ilal) discovered by mujtahids.
This answers the criticism and preserves the divine origin of rulings while acknowledging human reasoning in finding causes.
4
Understand the role of 'fasl al-khiṭāb'
The passage ends with reference to 'fasl al-khiṭāb' (discerning speech), which is an attribute of the Prophet's speech that distinguishes truth from falsehood. It further emphasizes the high rank of the Sunnah's speech.
This phrase underscores the clarity and authority of the prophetic speech.

Finally, 'fasl al-khiṭāb' (discerning speech) is explained as a distinguishing characteristic of the Sunnah's speech. Throughout, the commentary emphasizes the divine origin of rulings while acknowledging the role of human intellectual effort in extraction.

Superiority of Verbal Sunnah over Practical Sunnah
Explaining the benefit of 'atf al-khass 'ala al-amm' and the superiority of verbal Sunnah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that verbal Sunnah holds greater authority than practical Sunnah and identify the three reasons for its superiority as stated in the text.

Core Idea
Verbal Sunnah is superior: most rulings derive from it, it is the primary source of explanation, and its authority is unanimously agreed upon.

In this passage, the commentator defends why 'fasl al-khitab' (decisive speech) is mentioned separately after 'Sunnah'—it seems redundant at first. The key is that this construction, known as 'atf al-khass 'ala al-amm' (attaching the particular to the general), serves a critical benefit: to establish the superiority of the Prophet's words over his actions. Let us unpack the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic phenomenon
Notice that 'wa fasla khitabihi' (and his decisive speech) follows 'bi-sunan al-nabi al-mustafa' (by the Sunnah of the Prophet). Since Sunnah includes both words and actions, this mention appears to be a repetition of what is already included.
The construction is an example of 'atf al-khass 'ala al-amm'.
2
Recognize the implied objection
The objector argues that such a repetition is pointless unless there is a specific benefit (fā'idah). Without benefit, the construction would be invalid.
The principle: 'atf al-khass 'ala al-amm' is only valid when it conveys additional meaning.
3
Understand the response
The commentator affirms that there is indeed a benefit: to give preference to verbal Sunnah over practical and tacit Sunnah.
This preference is a key Hanafi usul position.
4
Extract the three reasons
The text lists three reasons: (1) Most legal rulings are based on the Prophet's words, not his actions. (2) The primary source for explaining rulings is words. (3) The authority (hujjiyyah) of verbal Sunnah is unanimously agreed upon, while the authority of actions is disputed.
Memorize these three reasons—they are frequently cited in Hanafi usul.
5
Apply to usul reasoning
When deriving rulings, give precedence to clear verbal evidence. If a hadith (verbal) conflicts with an action-based inference, the verbal evidence prevails.
This does not mean actions are disregarded; they are given weight but are considered less definitive.

Commentary
This passage addresses two distinct but related issues. The first is a defense of using the term nazm for the Quran, answering an objection that nazm is associated with poetry, which is criticized in the Quran. The response distinguishes between the literal meaning (stringing pearls) and the metaphorical application to poetry, asserting that the term nazm is used in its literal sense for the Quran, so no disrespect. The second and main issue is Imam Abu Hanifa's famous ruling permitting recitation of the Quran in Persian (or any non-Arabic language) during prayer, even without an excuse. The text provides two dalils: first, the allowance of seven modes of recitation (ahruf) indicates that the words of the Quran are not rigidly fixed in one form, showing flexibility. Second, the objective of prayer is devotion and understanding; words are a means to convey meaning. Focusing on miraculous Arabic words might distract from the spiritual state, so meaning is prioritized. The text adds that this concession (rukhsat) is one of dropping an obligation (isqat), so it applies even without necessity. However, it also mentions conditions: the reciter must be a Sunni (not an innovator), and the Persian translation must be unambiguous. This ruling is a distinctive Hanafi position; the Shafi'i school, for example, generally requires Arabic recitation even if one does not understand it. The passage thus exemplifies Hanafi usul's emphasis on maqasid (purposes) and istihsan (juristic preference).

Definition of Sifat and Refutation of a Commentator's Objection
Commentary on the definition of 'sifat' and response to a commentator's critique

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of sifat, distinguish it from ism mushabbih sifat, and evaluate the commentator's objection and the Hanafi responses.

Core Idea
Sifat: meaning intended per se, essence subordinate; weight must be from the genus of derived words.

This passage tackles the precise linguistic definition of 'sifat' (adjective/attribute) in Hanafi usul. A commentator raised an objection that the definition was too narrow, excluding common adjectives like 'atshan and abyad. The Hanafi commentary provides two responses, clarifying the intended scope of the derived weight condition and the deeper philosophy of word coinage (wad'). Understanding this definition is crucial for correctly classifying words in textual analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of sifat
Find the matan's formula: 'ainu ma wudi'a lahu min al-mushtaqi minhu ma'a wazni al-mushtaq'. Break it down: the exact verbal meaning from the derived root along with the weight of the derived word. Note that the verbal meaning is primary.
The matan author places verbal meaning first to indicate it is intended per se.
2
Understand the condition 'with the weight of the derived word'
Recognize that this condition means the weight must be from the genus of derived words (e.g., fa'il, maf'ul, fa'lan, mufa''al, mustaf'al), not just fa'il/maf'ul.
Instrument and place nouns (muf'al, mif'al) are not from this genus.
3
Analyze the commentator's objection
The commentator assumed 'derived weight' meant only fa'il/maf'ul, thus excluding words like 'atshan (fa'lan), mudahraj (mufa''al), and abyad (af'al). He argued the definition is not comprehensive.
The objection is based on a narrow view of 'mushtaq'.
4
Examine the first response (genus of derived words)
The first response expands the scope: 'with the weight of the derived word' means any weight belonging to the genus of derived words. So fa'lan and mufa''al are included; af'al and muf'al are not.
This retains the exclusion of instrument nouns while including many adjectives.
5
Examine the second response (intention of coinage)
The second response delves deeper: in true sifat, the meaning is intended per se and the essence is subordinate. In resembling isms like 'ahmar', the meaning is external and serves only as a cause for naming, or is part of the object of coinage (instrument nouns).
The commentator failed to consider the intention per se, focusing only on weight.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the definition of 'sifat' (adjective/attribute) in Arabic morphology, specifically addressing an objection raised by the commentator (sharih) against the matan author's definition. The passage opens by explaining the initial rule: in common usage, the weight (wazn) of a derived word (mushtaq) is considered only for ism al-fa'il (active participle) and ism al-maf'ul (passive participle), not for instrument nouns (ism al-ala) like miftah or place/time nouns (ism al-zarf) like maqtal. 

The commentator then objects that the matan author's definition of sifat as 'the exact meaning of the derived-from root combined with the derived weight' is not comprehensive because it would exclude well-known sifat like 'atshan (thirsty), mudahraj (rolled), and abyad (white) which do not have the weight of fa'il or maf'ul. If one tries to include them by broadening the expression, then instrument and place nouns would also be included, which are not sifat.

Two responses are given. The first response clarifies that 'with the derived weight' means 'with the weight of the genus of the derived word'. Weights like fa'lan (for 'atshan) and mufa''al (for mudahraj) are part of that genus, so they qualify as sifat. But weights like af'al (for abyad) and muf'al (for maqtal) are not from the genus of derived words, so they are not sifat.

The second response delves deeper into the philosophy of word coinage (wad'). It distinguishes three types: (1) words coined for an unspecified essence with a specific intended meaning — these are 'sifat'; the meaning is external to the object of coinage but intended per se, and the essence is subordinate. (2) words coined for a specific essence without any meaning — 'ism'. (3) words coined for a specific essence but with a meaning taken into account that has a connection: either the meaning is external but serves as a cause for naming (like 'ahmar' for a person with redness) or the meaning is part of the object of coinage (like instrument and place nouns). These latter two are called 'ism' but resemble sifat. The matan author's definition distinguishes true sifat from these resemblants: in sifat the meaning is intended per se; in the resemblants, the meaning is either not intended per se or is part of the object. The commentator mistakenly focused only on the derived weight and took 'mushtaq' narrowly as fa'il/maf'ul, leading to the objection.

Key Terms
صفت (sifat): A word coined for an unspecified essence with a specific intended meaning that subsists in that essence; the meaning is intended per se and the essence is subordinate.
وزن مشتق (wazn al-mushtaq): The morphological pattern of a derived word, specifically from the genus of derived forms (e.g., fa'il, maf'ul, fa'lan, mufa''al, mustaf'al).
مشتق (mushtaq): A word derived from a root, having a meaning associated with that root, as opposed to a simple noun.
اسم مشابہ صفت (ism mushabbih sifat): A word that resembles sifat but differs because the meaning is either not intended per se (e.g., احمر) or is part of the object of coinage (e.g., مفتاح).

Hikayat al-Fi'l: Why Narrated Actions Lack Generality
Explaining the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on hikayat al-fi'l

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a narrated action (hikayat al-fi'l) does not impart generality and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument regarding prayer inside the Ka'bah.

Core Idea
A narrated action is like a mushtarak: it applies directly only if a meaning is preferred; otherwise, the ruling extends by qiyas.

This passage delves into a foundational usul al-fiqh dispute between the Hanafis and Imam al-Shafi'i. The core question: does a reported action of the Prophet automatically apply generally? The text unpacks the Hanafi position that such actions are specific to their context and require careful analysis before extension.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi principle
Identify the rule: hikayat al-fi'l does not impart generality because the action occurred with specific attributes. It is like a mushtarak (homonym).
2
Examine the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that obligatory prayer inside the Ka'bah is invalid because it involves turning one's back to parts of it; he interprets the Prophet's action as voluntary (nafl).
3
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafis respond: since the permissibility of the specific action is established by the Prophet's act, and there is no difference between fard and nafl regarding qibla during ikhtiyar, permissibility extends to fard via qiyas.
4
Distinguish the shuf'a example
The hadith 'He judged with pre-emption for the neighbor' is not a narrated action but a transmission of a saying; moreover, 'al-jar' is general due to the lam al-istighraq, so it does not contradict the principle.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute (ikhtilaf usuli) between the Hanafis and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the generality of narrated actions (hikayat al-fi'l). The text opens by stating the rule: a narrated action of the Prophet does not carry generality because the action occurred with a specific attribute. It then draws an analogy with a mushtarak (homonym) term: if one meaning can be preferred by evidence, it is taken; if all meanings are equally likely, the ruling applies to some cases directly from the action and to others via qiyas. Imam al-Shafi'i argues that obligatory prayer inside the Ka'bah is invalid because it necessitates turning one's back to part of it, and thus he interprets the Prophet's action as voluntary (nafl). The Hanafis respond by noting that the permissibility of the specific action is established by the Prophet's deed, and since there is no difference between fard and nafl regarding the direction of the qibla in a state of choice, the permissibility extends to other cases (including fard) through qiyas. The text then addresses a potential counterexample: the hadith 'The Prophet judged with pre-emption for the neighbor' appears to be a narrated action that is general. The author resolves this by explaining that this is not a narration of an action but a transmission of a saying (qawl) reformulated; the word 'al-jar' is general due to the lam al-istighraq. Thus this case does not undermine the principle. The Urdu commentary further elaborates the disagreement among scholars and the reasons for each position.

Key Terms
حكاية الفعل (hikayat al-fi'l): Narration of a specific action performed by the Prophet on a particular occasion with specific attributes.
عموم (‘umum): Generality; the quality of applying to all individuals of a category.
مشترك (mushtarak): A term with multiple possible meanings; the narrated action is considered like a homonym.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical deduction used to extend a ruling to new cases based on a common illah.
