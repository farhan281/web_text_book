Muhkam and Mutashabih: Degrees of Textual Clarity
Analogy between rulings and a house with clear and hidden parts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between muhkam and mutashabih texts, classify texts on the clarity spectrum (nass, zahir, mufassar, muhkam, khafi, mujmal, mutashabih), and explain the Hanafi position that mutashabih has rulings known only to Allah.

Core Idea
Texts have degrees of clarity: muhkam is fully clear, mutashabih is completely hidden, known only to Allah.

This passage uses a striking analogy: just as a house has visible rooms and hidden chambers accessible only to the owner, so too do the rulings derived from Quranic texts vary in clarity. Some texts are completely clear (nass, zahir, mufassar, muhkam), while others are hidden (khafi, mujmal) or utterly inaccessible (mutashabih). The commentator defends this analogy against two objections and clarifies that even mutashabih contains rulings, though they remain unknown to us. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi classification of textual clarity and its implications for legal derivation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Analogy
Recognize that the commentator compares the locus of rulings (the Quran) to a house with both visible and concealed parts. The owner (Allah) alone has access to the most hidden parts, just as He alone knows the true interpretation of mutashabih.
The analogy is between two relationships: house parts vs. rulings derived from text categories.
2
Identify the Text Categories
From the passage, list the categories mentioned: nass, zahir, mufassar, muhkam (clear side) and khafi, mujmal, mutashabih (hidden side). Note that muhkam is the clearest, mutashabih the most hidden.
Hanafi usul orders texts by clarity: nass (plain), zahir (apparent), mufassar (explained), muhkam (firm). Then less clear: khafi (hidden), mujmal (ambiguous), mutashabih (unfathomable).
3
Analyze the Objections and Answers
Two objections are raised: (1) These text categories belong to the Quran, not to rulings, so the analogy fails. (2) Mutashabih has no ruling, so it cannot be part of the 'locus of rulings'. The commentator answers: The rulings are derived from these texts, so the analogy holds; and mutashabih does have a ruling, but it is unknown to us.
This underscores the epistemological humility of the Hanafi school regarding mutashabih.
4
Apply the Hanafi Definition of Mutashabih
The passage states that mutashabih is 'confined' (maqsurat) and its meaning is completely hidden. The benefit of its revelation is to test firmly grounded scholars (al-rasikhun fi al-ilm) by preventing them from reaching its secrets. This aligns with the salaf view.
In Hanafi usul, mutashabih is a category of text whose meaning is known only to Allah; it is not subject to ta'wil (interpretation) by human reason.

The Arabic portion elaborates on mutashabih, explaining that its meaning is confined and inaccessible, and the wisdom of its revelation is to test scholars by preventing them from reaching its secrets. This is a standard Ash'ari/Hanafi view that the mutashabihāt are known only to Allah.

Key Terms
محكم (muḥkam): A clear ruling whose meaning is evident and not subject to abrogation. Highest level of clarity.
متشابه (mutashābih): A verse whose meaning is ambiguous and known only to Allah; used to test scholars.
نص (nass): A text that is plain and unambiguous, indicating its meaning clearly.
ظاهر (zāhir): A text whose apparent meaning is clear but may be open to interpretation.
مفسر (mufassar): A text that is explained, detailing something previously ambiguous.
خفي (khafī): A text that is hidden but can be uncovered by research.
مجمل (mujmal): A text that is ambiguous in meaning, requiring clarification from another source.

Can a Single Science Have Multiple Subjects? The Condition of Relational Attributes
Analysis of the rule for subject multiplicity in sciences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the condition under which a single science can have multiple subjects and apply it to classify sciences like uṣūl al-fiqh, logic, medicine, and fiqh.

Core Idea
A science can have multiple subjects only if its investigated attributes arise from both sides of a relation.

This passage delves into a foundational issue in the classification of sciences: can one science study more than one subject? The author critiques the absolute claim and provides a nuanced rule based on the nature of the attributes investigated. By examining examples from medicine, uṣūl al-fiqh, logic, and fiqh, you will learn how to determine whether a science's subject is singular or multiple.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and counter-claim
The majority (jumhūr) hold that multiplicity is absolutely possible, citing medicine as an example. The commentator (Ṣāḥib al-Tawḍīḥ) rejects this absolute statement and offers a detailed condition.
Understand that the disagreement is about the necessity of a condition, not about the possibility itself.
2
Understand the key concepts: 'investigated attributes' (ʿawāriḍ dhātiyya) and 'relation' (iḍāfa)
The subject of a science is defined by the essential attributes (ʿawāriḍ dhātiyya) that are investigated. If these attributes involve a relation (iḍāfa) between two things, then multiplicity may be possible.
Relation means one thing is considered in its connection to another, e.g., in uṣūl al-fiqh, we investigate the relation between evidences and rulings.
3
Distinguish the three cases based on the origin of attributes
Case 1: Attributes arise from both sides of a relation → subject is multiple (e.g., uṣūl al-fiqh). Case 2: Attributes arise from only one side → subject is singular (e.g., logic). Case 3: There is no relation at all → subject is singular (e.g., fiqh).
In medicine, the attributes of medicines are only considered in relation to the human body, so they arise from one side; thus the true subject is the human body alone.
4
Apply the rule to given examples
For each science mentioned (uṣūl al-fiqh, logic, medicine, fiqh), determine whether the subject is singular or multiple by analyzing the origin of the investigated attributes.
Use the three-case framework: if the attributes arise from both sides of a relation, the subject is multiple; otherwise, it is singular.

From the discussions (one discussion) is that they mentioned that sometimes a single science has more than one subject, like the science of medicine, for example, in which we discuss the states of the human body and the states of medicines and the states of their likes. The investigation in this matter is that if the investigated (thing) in a science is the relation (iḍāfa) of one thing to another – as in uṣūl al-fiqh we discuss establishing rulings from evidences, and in logic we discuss the conveyance of a conception or judgment (known) to a conception or judgment (unknown) – and sometimes some of the attributes that have bearing on the investigated issues arise from one of the two related things, and some attributes from the other; then the subject of that science will be both related things. So the difference of the subject would prove the difference of the science. And if by 'one science' one means that it is conventionally called one science without considering a meaning that proves unity, then that has no weight. Conditions for Multiplicity of a Science's Subject
Usul rule defining when a science can have multiple subjects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a science can have multiple subjects and apply the Hanafi criterion of idafa and awarid to determine whether a given science's subject is single or multiple.

Core Idea
A science can have two subjects only when its investigated matter is a relation and relevant attributes arise from both sides.

In this passage, the author of al-Tawdih examines a foundational usul question: can a science have more than one subject? While some may assume any multiplicity is possible, the Hanafi school restricts it to a specific relational condition. The key lies in analyzing the investigated matter (mab?ūth 'anhu) and the attributes (ʿawāriḍ) that have a bearing on it. By studying the examples of usul al-fiqh, fiqh, and logic, you will learn to apply this criterion precisely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the investigated matter (al-mab?uth 'anhu)
For any science, first determine what entity or relation it investigates. This is the core subject that the science studies.
The investigated matter may be a single thing (like actions of a mukallaf in fiqh) or a relation between two things (like evidences and rulings in usul).
2
Check if the investigated matter is a relation (idafa)
Ask: does the science study a relation between two distinct things? If yes, proceed to step 3. If no, the subject is single.
For example, fiqh studies actions of mukallaf directly—no relation between two separate entities.
3
Examine the relevant attributes (awarid)
If the matter is a relation, determine whether the attributes that have a bearing on the investigation arise from both sides of the relation or only one.
Attributes arising from both sides means each side contributes something necessary to the science's inquiry.
4
Apply the rule for multiplicity
If both sides contribute relevant attributes, the subject is both things together (dual). If only one side matters, or if the matter is not a relation, the subject is single.
In usul al-fiqh: attributes from evidence (general, common, etc.) and from ruling (worship, punishment) both matter, so subject is dual.
5
Confirm with counterexamples
Verify the rule by applying it to other sciences: fiqh (non-relational → single), logic (relation but only one side's attributes matter → single).
This step reinforces that the rule is not arbitrary but follows from the nature of the investigated matter.

The Fourfold Division of Lafz in Hanafi Usul
Presenting the four classifications of lafz and disputes on their order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four divisions of lafz (wad', isti'mal, dhuhur/khafa', kayfiyya dalala) and explain the dispute between Sadr al-Shariah and Fakhr al-Islam regarding their order.

Core Idea
The word's meaning is analyzed through four lenses: coinage, usage, clarity, and mode of indication — and their order follows a logical sequence.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, a word (lafz) is not a monolithic entity; it can be classified from multiple perspectives. This lesson presents the four fundamental divisions of lafz as established in the tradition, and highlights a key scholarly dispute about the order of these divisions. Understanding these classifications is essential for correctly interpreting legal texts and deriving rulings.

Guided Path
1
Enumerate the four divisions
Recall the four aspects by which a word is divided: by its coinage (wad'), by its usage (isti'mal), by the clarity or hiddenness of its meaning (dhuhur/khafa'), and by its mode of indication (kayfiyya dalala).
These correspond to the first, second, third, and fourth divisions respectively.
2
Understand the logical sequence
According to the Talwih commentary, the divisions follow a natural order: first coinage, then usage, then indication. Clarity/hiddenness is a subset of indication, so it comes third, and the fourth division covers the remaining aspects of indication.
This sequence underpins Sadr al-Shariah's arrangement.
3
Compare the two scholars' orders
Sadr al-Shariah (author of al-Tawdih) places division by usage (isti'mal) second and division by clarity (dhuhur/khafa') third. Fakhr al-Islam (al-Pazdawi) reverses this order, placing clarity second and usage third.
The difference is in the priority of usage vs. clarity.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The text supports Sadr al-Shariah by arguing that a word must be used in a meaning before its clarity can be assessed. Therefore, usage precedes clarity.
This reasoning is considered logically sound.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The dispute affects the order in which usul topics are taught and the analytical priority when interpreting a text. Following Sadr al-Shariah, one would first determine whether a word is used literally or metaphorically before evaluating its clarity.
The Talwih endorses Sadr al-Shariah's ordering, making it the preferred view.

By consideration of its coinage (wad'): Here begins a summary of the four divisions. The first division is by consideration of coinage, and according to the majority school it has four types: khass, 'amm, mushtarak, mu'awwal. And according to the author of al-Tawdih: khass, 'amm, mushtarak, and jam' munakkar. And 'Allama Fakhr al-Islam titled the first division as: al-Taqsim al-Awwal fi Wujuh A'zam Safha [؟] i.e. the first division is about the ways of the nazm, by consideration of form and language. Then by consideration of its usage (isti'mal): i.e. the second division of the word is by consideration of using the word for a meaning, i.e. the word will be divided into whether it is used for its original coinage-meaning or for a meaning other than its coinage-meaning. Then by consideration of the clarity (dhuhur) of the meaning from it: a division by consideration of clarity and hiddenness of meaning. It has eight types: four by clarity: zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam; and four by hiddenness: khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih. This is the division that the author of al-Tawdih makes the third division, while Fakhr al-Islam makes it the second division. And the division by consideration of usage, which the author of al-Tawdih makes the second division, Fakhr al-Islam makes it the third division. The matn (author) also opposed Fakhr al-Islam in titling, because the matn expressed it as 'by consideration of the clarity and hiddenness of the meaning' while Fakhr al-Islam expressed it as 'al-Thani fi Wujuh al-Bayan bi Dhalik al-Nazm kaifa [؟]'. Question: When Fakhr al-Islam made the division by usage the third division, why did the matn make it the second division? Answer: Because first a word is used in a meaning, then it is seen whether the meaning has clarity or hiddenness; i.e. the rank of clarity/hiddenness is posterior to usage. Hence it is appropriate that the division of the word by consideration of usage be prioritized over the division by consideration of clarity/hiddenness. Then in terms of how it indicates: The fourth division is by consideration of the quality of indication (kayfiyya dalala). That is, what is the quality of the word's indication of its meaning? According to this consideration, both the author of al-Tawdih and Fakhr al-Islam have four types, although there is difference in titling. Fakhr al-Islam titled the fourth division as: al-Rabi' fi Wujuh al-Wuquf 'ala Ahkam A'zam, i.e. the fourth division is about the ways of pausing upon the rulings of the nazm, while the author of al-Tawdih expressed it as 'ham fi Kayfiyyat Dalalatihi 'Alayhi kaifa' [؟].
Al-Talwih: His statement: Since the Quran intends that the word indicating meaning by coinage necessarily requires coinage for the meaning, usage in it, and indication upon it. Therefore the division of the word in relation to its meaning: if by consideration of its coinage for it, then it is the first; if by consideration of its usage in it, then it is the second; if by consideration of its indication upon it, then if clarity and hiddenness are considered, it is the third, otherwise it is the fourth. And Fakhr al-Islam made these divisions divisions of the nazm and meaning, and he made the divisions emerging from the first three classifications to be attributes of the word, while the divisions emerging from the fourth classification he considered sometimes as istidlal bi al-'ibara, bi al-ishara, and bi al-dalala.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourfold classification of lafz (word) in Hanafi usul. The divisions are: (1) by wad' (coinage), producing types like khass, 'amm, etc.; (2) by isti'mal (usage), whether in the original meaning or metaphorical; (3) by dhuhur/khafa' (clarity/obscurity), producing the eight categories from zahir to mutashabih; (4) by kayfiyya dalala (mode of indication), which includes indication by ibara, ishara, and dalala (though here only mentioned briefly). The passage highlights a dispute between Sadr al-Shariah (author of al-Tawdih) and Fakhr al-Islam (al-Pazdawi) regarding the ordering of the second and third divisions. Sadr al-Shariah places usage before clarity, arguing logically that a word must be used before its clarity can be assessed. Fakhr al-Islam reverses the order. The Talwih commentary (by Taftazani) supports Sadr al-Shariah's ordering by explaining the logical sequence: coinage, then usage, then indication (with clarity as a subset of indication). This classification is fundamental in Hanafi usul for analyzing legal texts.

Key Terms
وضع (wad'): The original coinage or assignment of a word for a specific meaning.
استعمال (isti'mal): The actual usage of a word for a meaning, whether the original (haqiqa) or metaphorical (majaz).
ظہور (dhuhur): Clarity of the meaning from the word, as in zahir, nass, etc.
دلالہ (dalala): Indication; the way a word points to its meaning.

The Third Division: Clarity Levels of Words
Defining the third division of words by clarity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any word into one of the eight clarity levels (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam, khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih) based on its clarity and acceptance of abrogation or interpretation.

Core Idea
A word's legal force depends on its clarity level — from the clear and unchangeable muhkam to the hopelessly ambiguous mutashabih.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, words are not all equal in clarity. Some are immediately understood, others require reflection, and some remain ambiguous. This lesson unpacks the third major division of words: those that are clear (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) and those that are hidden (khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih). Understanding this hierarchy is essential for determining whether a text can be interpreted or is fixed.

Guided Path
1
Determine if the meaning is clear or hidden
First, check if the word's meaning is apparent or obscure. If clear, proceed to the next step; if hidden, go to step 4.
Clarity is relative to the listener's linguistic competence.
2
Subdivide clear words: does it admit interpretation (ta'wil)?
If the meaning is clear but can be interpreted (e.g., it has a possible alternative meaning), it is either zahir (clarity comes from wording) or nass (clarity comes from context). If it does not admit ta'wil, it is either mufassar (accepts abrogation) or muhkam (does not accept abrogation).
Zahir and nass differ in the source of clarity.
3
Subdivide hidden words: identify the cause of hiddenness
If the meaning is hidden, determine the cause. If due to something outside the wording (e.g., a technical term), it is khafi. If due to the wording itself, see if reflection can resolve it (mushkil). If not, check if clarification is hoped for (mujmal) or impossible (mutashabih).
Mujmal requires external bayan; mutashabih has no clarification.
4
Apply classification to a sample word from the Qur'an
Take the word 'yad' (hand) in 'yad Allah fawqa aydihim' (48:10). Here 'yad' is clear (zahir) for power, not a literal hand. But if taken literally, it would be mutashabih. This shows the importance of context.
Example from Hanafi tafsir.
5
Note the overlapping perspectives
Remember that these categories are not mutually exclusive across divisions. A word can be clear in one sense and hidden in another, e.g., 'ayn can be both 'amm (general) and mushtarak (shared) from different angles.
The author explicitly addresses this.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on al-Tawdih, explaining the classification of words (alfaz) in Hanafi usul. The author presents two divisions: the third division based on clarity/obscurity, and the fourth division based on mode of indication. In the third division: if a word's meaning is clear and it does not accept abrogation, it is muhkam (e.g., the clear verses of the Qur'an that are not abrogated). If the meaning is hidden due to something outside the wording itself, it is khafi (e.g., a word with an external ambiguity). If hidden due to the wording but resolvable through reflection, it is mushkil. If clarification is hoped for but not yet known, it is mujmal (ambiguous). If no clarification is possible, it is mutashabih (unclear). In the fourth division: words indicate meaning in four ways: ibarah (direct meaning, e.g., the apparent meaning of 'salah' for prayer), isharah (implied meaning, e.g., 'do not say 'uff' implies prohibition of hitting), dalalah (connoted meaning via linguistic understanding, e.g., 'do not say 'uff' implies respect for parents), and iqtida' (necessitated meaning, e.g., 'wash your faces' implies washing with water). The author notes that these categories are not mutually exclusive because they are from different perspectives. For example, the word 'ayn can be both 'amm (general) and mushtarak (shared) depending on the consideration. This is important for understanding how Hanafi usul analyzes texts.

Key Terms
المحكم (al-muhkam): A word whose meaning is clear and does not accept abrogation.
الخفي (al-khafi): A word whose meaning is hidden due to something outside the wording itself.
المشكل (al-mushkil): A word whose meaning is hidden due to the wording itself but can be understood through reflection.
المجمل (al-mujmal): A word whose meaning is ambiguous and requires clarification from an external source.
المتشابه (al-mutashabih): A word whose meaning is unclear and for which no clarification is expected.

The Fourth Division: Modes of Indication
Defining the fourth division: modes of indication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which mode of indication (ibarah, isharah, dalalah, iqtida') a given textual indication falls under according to Hanafi usul, and explain the practical consequence for deriving rulings.

Core Idea
How a word indicates its meaning — directly, by implication, by linguistic convention, or by necessity — determines the strength of the legal evidence.

A text can convey meaning in four distinct ways: directly through the wording (ibarah), by implication (isharah), through linguistic understanding (dalalah), or by necessity for the statement to make sense (iqtida'). This lesson unpacks the fourth division of words in Hanafi usul, which classifies the mode of indication. Mastering this allows you to extract rulings from texts that go beyond the literal wording.

Guided Path
1
Determine if the meaning is conveyed by the wording itself (nazm)
First, ask: is the meaning directly expressed in the text's arrangement? If yes, proceed. If no, skip to step 4.
Nazm refers to the structured wording of the text.
2
If by wording: is the wording intended for that meaning?
If the text is specifically phrased to convey that meaning, it is ibarah (direct). If the meaning is understood from the wording but not the primary intended meaning, it is isharah (implied).
Example: 'Do not say 'uff' to them' indicates ibarah for prohibition of 'uff', and isharah for prohibition of hitting.
3
If not by wording: is the meaning understood linguistically?
If the meaning is derived from the language's conventions (e.g., mafhum al-muwafaqah), it is dalalah (connoted). If it is not understood by language but is necessary for the validity of the statement, it is iqtida' (necessitated).
Dalalah is based on linguistic usage; iqtida' is based on logical necessity.
4
Identify the mode for a sample text
Apply to 'wash your faces and hands' (5:6). The ibarah is washing the literal face and hands. The iqtida' is the necessity of water to perform the washing; water is not mentioned but required.
Hanafis use iqtida' to establish obligations that are essential for an act.
5
Understand the strength hierarchy
Ibarah is strongest, followed by isharah, then dalalah, then iqtida'. But all are valid sources of rulings in Hanafi usul.
Some schools may deny iqtida' as a valid dalil.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on al-Tawdih, explaining the classification of words (alfaz) in Hanafi usul. The author presents two divisions: the third division based on clarity/obscurity, and the fourth division based on mode of indication. In the third division: if a word's meaning is clear and it does not accept abrogation, it is muhkam (e.g., the clear verses of the Qur'an that are not abrogated). If the meaning is hidden due to something outside the wording itself, it is khafi (e.g., a word with an external ambiguity). If hidden due to the wording but resolvable through reflection, it is mushkil. If clarification is hoped for but not yet known, it is mujmal (ambiguous). If no clarification is possible, it is mutashabih (unclear). In the fourth division: words indicate meaning in four ways: ibarah (direct meaning, e.g., the apparent meaning of 'salah' for prayer), isharah (implied meaning, e.g., 'do not say 'uff' implies prohibition of hitting), dalalah (connoted meaning via linguistic understanding, e.g., 'do not say 'uff' implies respect for parents), and iqtida' (necessitated meaning, e.g., 'wash your faces' implies washing with water). The author notes that these categories are not mutually exclusive because they are from different perspectives. For example, the word 'ayn can be both 'amm (general) and mushtarak (shared) depending on the consideration. This is important for understanding how Hanafi usul analyzes texts.

Key Terms
العبارة (al-ibarah): Direct indication: the word is explicitly intended for that meaning.
الإشارة (al-isharah): Implied indication: the word implies a meaning beyond the direct wording.
الدلالة (al-dalalah): Connoted indication: the meaning is understood through linguistic convention.
الاقتضاء (al-iqtida'): Necessitated indication: the meaning is necessary for the validity of the statement.

The Four Types of Textual Indication (Dalalat al-Nass)
Four-part classification of textual indication with exhaustive rationale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four types of dalalat al-nass ('ibaratu al-nass, isharatu al-nass, dalalatu al-'illah, iqtida' al-nass) and apply the Hanafi classification to identify which type a given textual indication belongs to.

Core Idea
A text indicates meaning either through its wording or through conceptual implication, producing four exhaustive types.

In Hanafi usul, understanding how a legal text conveys its meaning is crucial for correct istinbat. This lesson introduces a foundational classification: the four types of textual indication (dalalat al-nass). Through this classification, you will learn to systematically analyze a text's explicit and implicit dimensions, a skill essential for deriving rulings with precision.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two primary perspectives
A text's indication of a meaning arises either from its composition (nazm) or from its concept (mafhum). Distinguish which perspective applies to the meaning you are seeking.
Nazm refers to the wording and its direct construction; mafhum refers to the implied meaning derived from the text.
2
Analyze composition-based indications
If the perspective is composition, ask: is the word coined for this meaning directly? If yes, it is 'ibaratu al-nass. If the meaning is not directly coined but implied by the composition, it is isharatu al-nass.
Example of 'ibaratu al-nass: "O you who have believed, establish prayer" directly commands prayer. Example of isharatu al-nass: "And from them you eat tender meat" (Quran 16:14) implies the permissibility of eating fish, though not coined for that.
3
Analyze concept-based indications
If the perspective is concept, determine whether the derived meaning comes from a linguistic (lughawi) concept or a necessary requirement. If linguistic, it is dalalatu al-'illah (indication by cause). If it is a necessary requirement for the statement to be valid or complete, it is iqtida' al-nass.
Dalalatu al-'illah example: "Do not say to them 'uff'" (Quran 17:23) implies prohibition of hitting via the cause (lesser harm implies greater harm). Iqtida' al-nass example: "Unless you are travelers" (Quran 4:43) requires the meaning of "unless you are traveling and unable to find water" for the statement to be valid (i.e., tayammum permit is implied).
4
Verify exhaustive enumeration
Confirm that these four are exhaustive. Any meaning from a text must fall into one of these categories because the division is based on mutually exclusive criteria: composition vs. concept, and within each, two subcategories.
Hanafi scholars assert that no fifth type exists; any textual indication can be classified as one of these four.
5
Apply to a leading Hanafi example
Take a Quranic verse or hadith and determine for a specific meaning which of the four types applies. Walk through the reasoning step by step.
Example: The verse "And complete the Hajj and 'umrah for Allah" (Quran 2:196). The explicit obligation is 'ibaratu al-nass. The implication that one may not combine them without ihram is isharatu al-nass. The necessity of following the Prophet's actions is iqtida' al-nass.

Commentary
This passage from al-Tawdih presents a classification of the ways a text indicates its meaning. The commentator explains that there are four types: 1) 'ibarat al-nass (explicit statement), 2) isharat al-nass (implicit indication), 3) dalalat al-'illah (indication by underlying cause), and 4) iqtida' al-nass (necessary implication). This classification is fundamental in Hanafi usul for understanding the scope of textual implications.

Key Terms
عبارة النص ('ibaratu al-nass): The explicit indication of the text, where the word was coined for the exact meaning.
اشارة النص (isharatu al-nass): The implicit indication of the text, where the word implies a meaning not directly coined for it.
دلالة العلة (dalalatu al-'illah): Indication by the underlying cause (ratio legis) of the ruling.
اقتضاء النص (iqtida' al-nass): The necessary requirement of the text, something implied for the statement to be correct.

Mushtarak and Mu'awwal: Definitions and Objections
Defines mushtarak and mu'awwal as categories of nazm, with objections and answers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify mushtarak and mu'awwal as categories of nazm, and explain the distinction between mu'awwal and tafsir based on the type of proof (definitive vs. predominant opinion).

Core Idea
A mushtarak term resolved by predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y) is mu'awwal; by definitive proof it becomes tafsir.

The previous lesson introduced the four divisions of nazm (khass, 'amm, mushtarak, mu'awwal). This passage by Fakhr al-Islam provides a precise definition of mu'awwal and addresses two objections. Understanding this will clarify how Hanafis handle ambiguous terms in legal texts and why mu'awwal remains a division of nazm by form and language.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four divisions of nazm
From the passage, list the four categories: khass, 'amm, mushtarak, mu'awwal. Note that mu'awwal is defined only for mushtarak.
2
Understand the definition of mu'awwal
Mu'awwal is a mushtarak term where one meaning is given preponderance (tarajjaha) by predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y). Recognize that ghalib al-ra'y includes zann ghalib from reflection, khabar al-wahid, or qiyas.
3
Recognize the two objections
Objection 1: Mu'awwal may apply to non-mushtarak terms (like mujmal, mushkil). Objection 2: Preponderance may be by definitive proof, not only ghalib al-ra'y.
The passage cites al-Mizan as the source of the objection.
4
Analyze the answers
Answer 1: The definition is limited to mu'awwal from mushtarak as a division of nazm by form and language. Answer 2: Ghalib al-ra'y includes any predominant conjecture, whether from textual indicators or reflection; definitive proof produces tafsir, not ta'wil.
5
Differentiate mu'awwal from tafsir
If a mushtarak term is clarified by a definitive proof (qat'i), it becomes tafsir and exits the divisions of nazm by form/language. If by a speculative proof (zanni), it remains mu'awwal within those divisions.

His statement: 'Then the mushtarak.' Fakhr al-Islam and others mentioned that the divisions of nazm (linguistic arrangement) by form and language are four: al-khass, al-'amm, al-mushtarak, and al-mu'awwal. He interpreted al-mu'awwal as what has been preponderated (tarajjaha) from mushtarak, some of its meanings, by predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y). An objection was raised: mu'awwal may not be from mushtarak, and its preponderance may not be by predominant opinion, as mentioned in al-Mizan: that mujmal, mushkil, khafi, and mushtarak when explanation (bayan) reaches them by a definitive proof are called mufassar; and when their obscurity is removed by a proof containing some doubt, like khabar al-wahid or qiyas, they are called mu'awwal. The answer to the first is that the intended is not a definition of mu'awwal absolutely, but mu'awwal from mushtarak, because it is the one that is among the divisions of nazm by form and language. As for the second, ghalib al-ra'y means predominant conjecture (zann ghalib), whether obtained from khabar al-wahid, qiyas, or reflection on the expression itself, as in three quru'. And the meaning of it being among the divisions of nazm by form and language is that the ruling after ta'wil is attributed to the expression. It is said: the meaning of ghalib al-ra'y is reflection (ta'mul) and ijtihad in the very expression. He restricted it to mushtarak and preponderance by ijtihad and reflection in the expression itself to verify its being among the divisions of nazm by form and language. For mushtarak is placed for multiple meanings, each of which is possible in alternation. When it is taken to one of them by consideration of the expression (i.e., the word placed), it does not exit from the divisions of nazm by form and language, i.e., by placement. Unlike when it is taken to it by a definitive proof, then it is tafsir, not ta'wil; or by qiyas or khabar al-wahid, then it is not by this consideration among the divisions of nazm by form and language. Similarly if it is not mushtarak but khafi, mujmal, or mushkil, and its obscurity is removed by definitive or conjectural proof.

Commentary
This passage discusses the categorization of linguistic expressions (nazm) in usul al-fiqh. Fakhr al-Islam divides them into four: khass (specific), 'amm (general), mushtarak (homonym/ambiguous), and mu'awwal (interpreted). He defines mu'awwal as a mushtarak term where one of its meanings is given preponderance (tarajjih) by predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y). Two objections are raised: (1) mu'awwal can apply to non-mushtarak terms like mujmal, mushkil, khafi; (2) preponderance may be by definitive proof or other means, not necessarily ghalib al-ra'y. The answers clarify that the definition is limited to mu'awwal as a division of nazm by form and language, which requires that the term itself (mushtarak) is the locus of interpretation, and that the preponderance is via predominant conjecture (zann ghalib) from reflection, ijtihad, or textual indicators like khabar al-wahid or qiyas. The key distinction: if the mu'awwal is determined by a definitive proof (qat'i), it becomes tafsir (exegesis) not ta'wil; if by a speculative proof (zanni), it remains within the divisions of nazm. Additionally, the discussion touches on the textual clarity hierarchy: mujmal, mushkil, khafi when clarified by definitive proof become mufassar; by speculative proof become mu'awwal.

Key Terms
مشترك (mushtarak): A word that has multiple meanings, each possible in alternation (e.g., 'quru' means both purity and menstruation).
مؤول (mu'awwal): A mushtarak term where one meaning is preponderated by predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y) through ijtihad and reflection on the expression.
غالب الرأي (ghalib al-ra'y): Predominant opinion or strong conjecture (zann ghalib) that arises from evidence like khabar al-wahid, qiyas, or reflection on the expression itself.
تأويل (ta'wil): Interpretation that removes obscurity by a proof containing some doubt (shubha), resulting in a mu'awwal.

Definition and Conditions of Ta'wil in Hanafi Usul
Defining ta'wil as a linguistic classification after reflection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi definition of ta'wil to classify whether an interpretation is ta'wil or tafsir based on the strength of the proof.

Core Idea
Ta'wil is probabilistic interpretation of ambiguous words; tafsir is definitive exegesis.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, not every interpretation is equal. The scholars distinguish between tafsir, which is definitive and binding, and ta'wil, which is probabilistic and requires ijtihad. This lesson unpacks the conditions under which an interpretation is considered ta'wil, using the classic example of the word 'quru'' (menstrual periods) from Quran 2:228.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object of ta'wil
Ta'wil applies to words that are mushtarak (homonym), khafi (obscure), mujmal (ambiguous), or mutashabih (unclear). These words have multiple possible meanings.
The word must be capable of more than one interpretation.
2
Distinguish ta'wil from tafsir
If the meaning is established by a definitive proof (dalil qat'i), it is tafsir. If the proof is probabilistic (zanni), it is ta'wil.
Definitive proof includes mutawatir texts or clear linguistic rules.
3
Understand the role of ijtihad
Ta'wil requires reflection (ta'ammul) and ijtihad based on dominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y). It is not arbitrary guesswork.
The interpreter must consider the form and language of the word.
4
Recognize what does NOT produce ta'wil
When a meaning is carried by qiyas (analogy) or khabar wahid (solitary report), it does not enter the same linguistic classification as ta'wil.
These are separate mechanisms of legal reasoning.
5
Apply the classification to an example
The word 'quru'' in Quran 2:228 means both 'menstruation' and 'purity'. Without a definitive proof, choosing one meaning is ta'wil.
This illustrates the probabilistic nature of ta'wil.

Definition of al-Ism al-Zahir and Conditions for Sifat
Defining al-ism al-zahir and refining the definition of al-sifah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-ism al-zahir and al-sifah in the Hanafi usul sense, and identify which derived patterns qualify as sifat according to the technical definition.

Core Idea
Only fa'il and maf'ul patterns are sifat in Hanafi usul; other derived forms are not.

This passage from al-Talwih refines the Hanafi classification of nouns. It distinguishes al-ism al-zahir from pronouns and demonstratives, and then carefully defines al-sifah as a derived noun limited to active and passive participle patterns. This precision avoids conflating adjectives with other derived nouns like time, place, and instrument. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying 'umum and khusus in legal texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scope of al-ism al-zahir
Determine what is excluded: pronouns and demonstrative nouns. The term 'zahir' means explicit noun not falling into those categories.
2
Extract the definition of al-sifah
Find the phrase: 'a derived noun whose meaning is exactly the meaning of its root along with the pattern of the derived word.' This is the core definition.
3
Identify the restrictive condition
The pattern must be fa'il or maf'ul. The author explicitly excludes patterns like af'al, fa'lan, fi'l, mustaf'il, mufa'lal, and nouns of time, place, instrument.
This is the critical refinement.
4
Analyze the rebuttal to potential objections
A hypothetical objector argues that the definition could be extended to other patterns. The commentator shows that such extension leads to equally improbable analyses, thus defending the restriction.
5
Apply the definition to examples
Test patterns: 'darib' (fa'il) qualifies; 'abyad' (af'al) does not; 'maqtal' (maf'al) is a noun of place, not sifat.

Commentary
This passage from al-Talwih, the super-commentary on al-Tawdih, addresses two interrelated issues in Hanafi usul al-fiqh regarding the classification of nouns. First, it resolves a question about a word being both 'amm (general) and mushtarak (homonymous): the aspect (haythiyyah) that generates this dual characterization is merely relational (i'tibari), not essential, so there is no real contradiction—essential contrast (tabayun dhati) is avoided. This is a typical Hanafi approach to resolve apparent conflicts in linguistic categories. Second, and more substantially, the passage defines al-ism al-zahir (the explicit noun) as excluding pronouns and demonstratives, and then defines al-sifah (adjective) as a specific type of derived noun (ism mushtaq) whose meaning is exactly the meaning of its root combined with a grammatical pattern. Crucially, the text argues that only the patterns of fa'il (active participle) and maf'ul (passive participle) qualify for this definition. Other common patterns like af'al (e.g., abyad, afdal), fa'lan (e.g., 'atshan), fi'l (e.g., khayr), mustaf'il (e.g., mustakhraj), and mufa'lal (e.g., mudahraj) are excluded because their meanings cannot be reduced to 'root meaning + pattern' in the same way. The commentator refutes an objection that this restriction is arbitrary by showing that the alternative—accepting all derived patterns as adjectives—leads to equally improbable analyses. This is a refined linguistic argument ensuring that the usul terminology remains precise: 'sifah' in the technical sense is narrower than common usage. The Urdu translation and explanation reinforce that the restriction is to avoid including nouns of time, place, and instrument (asma' al-zaman, al-makan, al-alah) which, though formally derived, do not function as adjectives.

Key Terms
الاسم الظاهر (al-ism al-zahir): A noun that is not a pronoun or demonstrative; it is an explicit noun that includes common nouns and adjectives.
الصفة (al-sifah): A derived noun (ism mushtaq) whose meaning is exactly the meaning of its root combined with the pattern of fa'il (agent) or maf'ul (patient), indicating a specific meaning subsisting in an indeterminate essence.
المشتق (al-mushtaq): A word derived from a root (mushtaq minhu) that shares the root's basic meaning with an additional grammatical pattern.

Inference from Apparent Wording in Hanafi Usul
Concluding analysis on inference from apparent wording

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school relies on apparent wording (zahir) over hidden context (amr batin) for legal inference, and apply this principle to distinguish between valid and invalid bases for istidlal.

Core Idea
The apparent wording is the foundation of legal inference; hidden intentions or contexts cannot override it.

This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary concludes a discussion on the relationship between general wording and specific causes. It affirms a key principle: legal rulings are derived from the apparent meaning of texts, not from hidden matters such as the specific occasion of revelation or the intention of the speaker. Understanding this principle is critical for proper istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Recognize 'lafz zahir' (apparent wording) and 'amr batin' (hidden matter) as central concepts. The passage uses them to distinguish valid and invalid bases for inference.
These terms are foundational in Hanafi usul.
2
Understand the reasoning
The passage explains why lafz zahir can serve as a basis for rulings: it is clear and manifest. In contrast, amr batin (like hidden intentions or contextual restrictions) lacks the capacity to bear a ruling because it is not apparent.
The key is the epistemological status: zahir is knowable, batin is not.
3
Apply the distinction
Analyze examples from the passage: inference from ambiguity (ibham) is invalid because it is a hidden matter; inference from a solitary report (khabar wahid) is valid because it is an apparent matter, even though it is only conjectural (zanni).
This shows that zann does not negate zahir; rather, the criterion is appearance, not certainty.
4
Note the nuance on khabar wahid
The passage explicitly states that khabar wahid is valid for istidlal because it is zahir, despite containing zann. This distinguishes it from ibham, which is not even apparent.
This is a distinctively Hanafi position; other schools may have different criteria.

Commentary
This passage is a concluding analysis from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary, likely from Al-Tarwih fi Hall al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih. It affirms a core principle of Hanafi legal theory: legal inference (istidlal) is based on the apparent (zahir) wording of texts, not on hidden intentions or contextual restrictions that are not manifest. The passage states that a specific cause (sabab khas) does not negate the general wording ('umum al-lafz); both are considered. The reason given is that wording is apparent and can serve as a basis for rulings, whereas the hidden context (amr batin) such as intentions or specific circumstances that are not linguistically expressed lack the capacity to bear a ruling. Therefore, inference from ambiguity (ibham) is invalid because it is hidden. However, even a solitary report (khabar wahid) is accepted for inference because it is apparent, even though it is only conjectural (zanni). This contrasts the Shafi'i view which may give more weight to specific context over general wording in some cases.
