The Dual Meaning of Hukm and the Principle of Umum Majaz
Defending the dual meaning of hukm against an objection of combining literal and figurative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi concept of 'umum majaz' to defend the use of a term in both literal and figurative senses within a single argument, and distinguish it from the fallacy of jam' bayn al-haqiqah wa al-majaz.

Core Idea
Hukm means both Allah's address and its effect; combining literal and figurative is allowed via umum majaz, not fallacious.

Hanafi usul recognizes that the term 'hukm' can refer to two realities: God's eternal address (khitab Allah) and the resultant legal classification (athar). When proving rulings from proofs, the term 'thubut' (establishment) is used for both certain proofs (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) and speculative proof (qiyas). This might seem to mix literal and figurative meanings, but the Hanafi school resolves it through the concept of 'umum majaz' — a general sense that encompasses both literal and figurative instances. In this lesson, we unpack the objection and the Hanafi response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings of hukm
Recognize that 'hukm' can mean either Allah's address (khitab Allah) or its effect/result (athar) on actions. Both are valid in Hanafi usul.
The passage opens with this dual meaning.
2
Understand the objection about jam' bayn al-haqiqah wa al-majaz
The objector claims that using 'thubut' for both certain proofs and speculative proof combines literal (certain) and figurative (speculative) meanings, which is invalid.
The objection arises from a strict view of linguistic usage.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response: umum majaz
The Hanafi answer is that 'thubut' is used in a general sense (umum) that includes both the literal and figurative instances, making them individuals of that general concept.
This is not a combination but a single general usage.
4
Apply the concept to similar cases
Think of other terms like 'ghalabat al-zann' which applies to both strong (ra'ij) and weak (marjuh) predominant opinion. This illustrates how umum majaz operates in usul al-fiqh.
This reinforces the validity of the principle.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis responding to an objection about the definition of 'hukm' in Usul al-Fiqh. The author first explains that the term 'hukm' can refer to either Allah's address (khitab Allah) or its effect (athar). A hypothetical objection is raised: if hukm means Allah's address, it cannot be proven by proofs because the address is eternal, not originated; if it means the effect, then the effect is proven by the three proofs (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) but not by qiyas, since qiyas only reveals rulings, does not establish them. The author's answer clarifies that by 'proof from proofs' they mean proof in our knowledge, not the address itself—our knowledge is originated, so no problem. Then a second objection is made: the word 'thubut' (proof/establishment) is used in both a certain sense (for the three proofs) and a speculative sense (for qiyas), which combines literal and figurative meanings (jam' bayn al-haqiqah wa al-majaz), which is invalid. The response invokes the principle of 'umum majaz' (general figurative usage): the term 'thubut' here is used in a general sense that encompasses both certain and speculative meanings, just as a word can have a general sense that includes both literal and figurative instances. This is similar to how 'al-ghalb' (predominance) can apply to both strong and weak predominant opinion. Thus, the combination is permissible.

اثر (athar): The effect or result of the address, i.e., the legal classification (e.g., wajib, haram).
عموم مجاز (umum majaz): A word used in a sense that includes both literal and figurative meanings, making them individuals of that general sense.

Defining Dalil: Proof as Epistemic Indicator
Refuting a misconception about the meaning of dalil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dalil as 'what yields knowledge of the existence or non-existence of a thing' and distinguish it from an illa (cause), applying this definition to both qat'i and zanni proofs.

Core Idea
A dalil is an epistemic indicator, not a causal producer — it yields knowledge, not existence.

This passage confronts a misconception that legal proofs are not 'real' proofs or that their definition fails for temporal rulings. The commentator clarifies that a dalil, regardless of its nature, is simply something that yields knowledge — certainty or preponderant opinion — about a matter. This broad definition fits all types of shar'i proofs and remains valid whether the ruling is eternal or temporal. Understanding this distinction between proof (dalil) and cause (illa) is foundational for Hanafi istidlal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the misconception
The commentator begins by dismissing a claim: that if 'ruling' is taken in a certain way, the definition of dalil fails. He calls it 'a statement with no substance.' Recognize that some scholars may have argued that dalil must produce existence itself, not just knowledge.
The misconception likely stems from confusing dalil with illa (cause).
2
Extract the correct definition of dalil
The passage states: 'the meaning of proof is nothing other than what yields knowledge of the existence of a thing or its non-existence.' This is the classical Hanafi definition. Write it down and memorize it.
Key phrase: 'ma yufidu al-ilm bi thubut al-shay' aw intifa'ihi'.
3
Understand the scope of 'ilm
The term 'ilm is taken broadly to include both definitive apprehension (al-idrak al-jazim, i.e., certainty) and preponderant opinion (al-rajih, i.e., probability). Therefore, both qat'i and zanni proofs are valid dalils.
This is a distinctive Hanafi position; some other schools restrict 'ilm to certainty.
4
Distinguish dalil from illa
The commentator explicitly rejects that a dalil 'yields existence itself' as external causes do. A dalil indicates or points to a ruling; it does not bring it into being. This distinction is crucial for correct istidlal.
Compare: fire (illa) causes heat; a hadith (dalil) indicates obligation.
5
Apply to an example
Consider the Quranic verse 'Aqimu al-salat' (establish prayer). This is a dalil: it yields knowledge of the obligation of prayer (either certain if mutawatir, or preponderant if ahad). It does not cause the obligation; Allah's command does.
The dalil's role is epistemic, not ontological.

Adillah as Amarat or Ilal Haqiqiyyah: The Nature of Evidences in Hanafi Usul
Dispute on whether adillah are real causes or indicators

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafi usul classifies adillah as amarat (indicators) rather than ilal haqiqiyyah (real causes), and identify the epistemological consequences for qiyas.

Core Idea
Adillah are indicators of rulings, not efficient causes; they give knowledge, not existence.

This passage tackles a foundational question in usul al-fiqh: are the shar'i evidences (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) real causes that bring about rulings, or are they merely indicators that point to pre-existing rulings? The Hanafi position, defended here, is that they are amarat—they provide knowledge (either certainty or preponderant opinion) of the ruling, but do not create the ruling itself. This has direct implications for how qiyas is understood: it is a 'manifestor' (muzhir) of a ruling, not an establisher (muthbit). Understanding this distinction is key to grasping the Hanafi theory of evidence and legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central dispute
Recognize the two opposing views: (1) adillah are ilal haqiqiyyah (real causes) that establish rulings, (2) adillah are amarat (indicators) that only provide knowledge of rulings. The passage seeks to defend the second view.
The objector cites the matn's earlier statement that the first three usul are 'muthbit li-l-ahkam'.
2
Analyze the definition of 'dalil'
The text quotes the standard definition: 'dalil is that which gives knowledge of the existence or non-existence of a thing.' Note that it does NOT define dalil as that which brings the thing into existence—that is the role of external causes (ilal kharijiyyah).
This definition is crucial: it shifts the epistemic role of evidence from ontological creation to epistemic indication.
3
Examine the two meanings of 'hukm'
Sadr al-Shariah distinguishes: hukm as the divine address (khitab) vs. hukm as the effect of the address (e.g., obligation). The commentator argues that whether we take one or the other, the role of adillah remains the same: they provide knowledge of the hukm, not its existence.
If hukm is the eternal khitab, 'thubut' must mean knowledge of it; if hukm is the effect, the three primary proofs establish it definitively, but qiyas only manifests.
4
Resolve the apparent contradiction
The objector's point is valid: the matn itself said the first three principles are 'muthbit'. The response is that 'thubut' in that context refers to knowledge, not ontological creation. For qiyas, it is never 'muthbit' but only 'muzhir'.
This preserves the consistency of Hanafi usul: none of the adillah are ilal; they are all amarat.
5
Conclude the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: qiyas-derived rulings are not considered directly established by divine address; they are only made manifest by qiyas. This affects how muftis rank evidences and claim authority.
This is a key distinction from other madhahib that may treat qiyas as equally productive.

A dalil is that from which knowledge of the existence or non-existence of a thing is obtained. At most, knowledge includes certainty and probability; it is either definitive or preponderant. Definitive from the three proofs, preponderant from qiyas. Commentary
This passage is an usul dispute over the nature of shar'i evidences (adillah). The issue arises from Sadr al-Shariah's (author of al-Tawdih) distinction between two meanings of hukm: as divine address (khitab) and as the object established by that address. The commentator (sharh) argues that all adillah are mere indicators (amarat), not efficient causes (ilal haqiqiyyah) that actually bring about rulings. Even if we grant they are causes, the definition of dalil is about providing knowledge of a thing's existence, not establishing existence itself—that is the function of external causes. The author then presents a defense: the objector's point is valid because the matn himself stated that the first three principles (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) are absolute principles that establish rulings. The resolution is that when hukm means the eternal divine address, 'thubut' must be interpreted as our knowledge of it; when hukm means the resulting obligation/prohibition, only the three primary proofs establish it definitively, while qiyas only manifests (yuzhir) the ruling. This preserves the consistency of the Hanafi usul position.

Key Terms
دليل (dalil): That which gives knowledge of the existence or non-existence of a thing.
علة حقيقية (illah haqiqiyyah): A real cause that actively brings about a ruling.
امارة (amarah): An indicator that leads to preponderant opinion about a ruling.

The Unity of Subject Matter in a Science
Explaining the rule that a single science cannot have multiple subjects unless proportionate.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition under which a single science can have multiple subjects and apply the criterion of proportionality (tanasub) to determine whether a set of subjects can be unified.

Core Idea
Multiple subjects can form one science only if they share an essential or accidental property (tanasub).

This passage from a commentary on al-Tawdih delves into a foundational methodological principle: a science must have a unified subject matter. It explains that while ordinarily a single science cannot have multiple subjects, an exception is allowed when the subjects are proportionate. Understanding this principle is crucial for defining the scope of each Islamic science and avoiding confusion between disciplines.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the principle that a single science (ilm) must have a unified subject matter (mawdu'). It is from a commentary (sharh) on al-Tawdih. The text first presents the view that the subject of a science can be multiple if it is a relational (idafi) investigation where different aspects arise from different relational terms, as in usul al-fiqh where evidences and rulings both serve as subjects. However, it then argues that in most cases the subject must be one. The commentator explains that if the subject matter is not relational (e.g., fiqh investigates the actions of the obligated person regarding obligation/prohibition — these are not relations but attributes), or if it is relational but the relevant accidents arise from only one term (e.g., logic investigates conveyance but focuses on the conveyor), then the subject is singular. The reasoning is that multiple subjects would lead to multiple sciences, which contradicts the assumption of a single science. The passage then presents a critical investigation (wa fihi nazar): mere multiplicity of problems does not require multiple sciences, as one science can have many problems. The real criterion is proportionality (tanasub) of the subjects — they must share a common property, either essential (like quantity in geometry) or accidental (like the body and its parts in medicine). This is a classic usul al-fiqh discussion about the definition and scope of a science.

This passage is a detailed discussion on whether the Quran can be given a true definition (hadd). The author argues that it cannot, because it is a unique entity (shakhsi). Two answers are given: First, the Quran is not truly shakhsi but like a shakhsi in terms of defintion. Second, in the author's terminology, a 'person' (shakhsi) is defined as specific words in a specific order, even if the locus is multiple; the essence is one, only the locus varies, analogously to a famous poem that is considered a single work even though recited by many. If it is meant only as an identificatory definition, then no problem.

The Hanafi Doctrine of ‘Ismat and Its Application to Theft
Hanafi justification for no guarantee based on ‘ismat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi distinction between ‘ismat al-‘abd and ‘ismat Allah leads to the ruling that a thief is not liable for the stolen property after amputation.

Core Idea
Theft violates Allah's protection, not the owner's; thus hadd suffices, no daman.

In this passage, Imam Abu Hanifa justifies why a thief who destroys the stolen property does not have to pay compensation: the property's protection shifts to Allah at the moment of theft. We will explore the concept of ‘ismat and its legal implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the dispute
What is the exact fiqh question? A thief steals and the property is destroyed. Is the thief liable for compensation (daman) in addition to the fixed punishment (hadd) of amputation?
The Hanafi position: only amputation, no compensation. Shafi'i: both.
2
Define ‘ismat and its two types
‘Ismat means legal inviolability or protection. Hanafis distinguish between ‘ismat al-‘abd (protection belonging to the human owner) and ‘ismat Allah (protection belonging to Allah). Violation of the former incurs a right of the servant (e.g., daman); violation of the latter incurs a right of Allah (e.g., hadd).
3
Apply the concept to theft
At the moment of theft, the stolen property leaves the owner's protection and enters Allah's protection. Therefore, the crime of theft is an aggression against Allah's protection, not the owner's. Consequently, the thief owes no compensation to the owner.
The property is considered to have lost its ‘ismat al-‘abd.
4
Conclude the ruling
Since the crime is against Allah, the appropriate consequence is the hadd punishment (amputation). The owner's right to compensation is extinguished because the property was no longer under his protection when destroyed.
This is a key Hanafi usul argument: the shift in protection changes the legal consequence.

The Verse 'Faqta'u Aydiyahuma' as a Specific Command: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Dispute over scope of amputation verse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate how the classification of the Quranic command 'faqta'u' as khass (specific) versus 'amm (general) leads to different rulings on the thief's liability.

Core Idea
How you read 'faqta'u' determines whether guarantee is added.

This lesson focuses on the usul dispute about the Quranic verse. The Hanafis argue it is a specific command (khass) for cutting only; Shafi'is may interpret it differently. We will examine the two positions and the underlying principles.

Guided Path
1
State the verse and identify the key command
The Quran says 'faqta'u aydiyahuma' (so cut off their hands). The imperative verb 'faqta'u' is the central legal term. We need to determine its scope.
2
Classify the command according to Hanafi usul
Hanafi usul holds that 'faqta'u' is a specific (khass) command. A khass text is definitive (qat'i) and limited to its explicit meaning. It cannot be extended to include additional obligations unless another text supports it.
The principle: al-khass yufidu al-qat' wa la yata'addahu.
3
Apply the principle to the ruling
Since the verse only commands cutting, the thief's liability is exhausted by that punishment. Including compensation would be adding to the text without evidence. Therefore, the Hanafi ruling is that only amputation applies.
4
Present the Shafi'i counter-view
The Shafi'i school may classify 'faqta'u' as 'amm (general) or apply other principles (e.g., general liability for property destruction) to argue that the thief must also compensate. They may also use qiyas to link the non-mentioned guarantee to the theft context.
The Shafi'i objection is that the verse does not explicitly negate guarantee; thus, the general rule of returning stolen property applies.
5
Determine the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafi judges only order amputation; Shafi'i judges order amputation plus restitution of the property or its value.
This difference has real implications in Islamic criminal law.

Balancing Shabah: Sale with Khiyar on One of Two Items
Weighing shabah analogies for sale with khiyar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul analysis of shabah (analogical resemblance) to determine the validity of a sale when a conditional option (khiyar) applies to only one of two items, and evaluate which shabah predominates based on knowledge of subject and price.

Core Idea
When both subject and price of the khiyar item are known, shabah naskh validates the sale; when unknown, shabah istithna invalidates it.

This passage demonstrates how Hanafi usul uses the concept of shabah (resemblance) to weigh competing analogies. In a sale of two items with a condition of khiyar on one, the khiyar item is included in the offer but not fully in the ruling. This creates two potential analogies: resemblance to naskh (abrogation, where the condition modifies the contract) and resemblance to istithna (exception, where the item is temporarily excluded). The Hanafi position balances these based on whether the subject and price of the khiyar item are known, with knowledge tipping the scale toward validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Four Possible Cases
The passage outlines four scenarios regarding knowledge of the subject (mahall) and price (thaman) of the khiyar item: (1) both known, (2) subject known but price unknown, (3) price known but subject unknown, (4) both unknown. Understand each case as a distinct starting point.
These cases arise when a seller says, 'I sell you this and that for 2000, this for 1000 and that for 1000, with the condition that you have the option concerning that (one of the items).'
2
Understand the Two Analogies: Shabah Naskh and Shabah Istithna
The condition of khiyar creates two possible analogies. Shabah naskh: the condition modifies the contract like abrogation modifies a prior ruling, so the khiyar item is part of the contract but subject to change. Shabah istithna: the condition excludes the khiyar item from the binding effect, like an exception. Recognize that in Hanafi usul, shabah is a weaker form of qiyas used to weigh competing interpretations.
The passage uses 'shabah al-naskh' and 'shabah al-istithna' throughout.
3
Apply the Balancing Rule
The Hanafi position balances: when both subject and price are known, knowledge removes gharar (uncertainty), so shabah naskh predominates and the sale is valid. When either is unknown, ignorance triggers gharar, so shabah istithna predominates and the sale is invalid. Verify this in each of the four cases.
The phrase 'رَعَيْنَا الشَّبَهَيْنِ' indicates the balancing.
4
Contrast with the Free+Slave Case
The author contrasts with a sale of a free person and a slave together, where the free person is not a valid object of sale. Here, only shabah istithna applies (since the free person is completely excluded), so the slave's sale is invalid according to Abu Hanifa. This highlights why knowledge alone is insufficient when the khiyar item is inherently invalid.
The contrasting case shows the limit of shabah naskh: it cannot override an invalid subject.

So when it is known that the resemblance of abrogation requires validity in all, and the resemblance of exception requires invalidity in all, we considered both resemblances and said: When the subject of the option or its price is unknown, the sale is not valid due to consideration of the resemblance of exception; and when each of them is known, the sale is valid due to consideration of the resemblance of abrogation. And the resemblance of exception is not considered here such that it would be invalid due to an invalid condition, which is that accepting what is not a sold item becomes a condition for accepting the sold item. Unlike when he sold a free person and a slave for one thousand in one transaction, and stated the price of each, where the sale of the slave is invalid according to Abu Hanifa, may Allah have mercy on him, because the free person is not included in the sale at all, so it becomes like an exception without the resemblance of abrogation, so what is not a sold item becomes a condition for accepting the sold item.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul analysis of a specific mas'ala from Hanafi fiqh: a sale involving two items in one contract with a condition of khiyar (option) for one of them. The author, in the sharh tradition of al-Tawdih, explains how two competing legal analogies (shabah) are weighed. The khiyar item is included in the offer (ijab) but not in the ruling (hukm) — it is treated as part of the transaction contractually but not as a definitive sale. This creates a resemblance (shabah) to naskh (abrogation) in that the condition modifies the contract like a substitution, and to istithna (exception) in that the option item is temporarily excluded. The Hanafi position balances both: if the subject and price of the khiyar item are known, the resemblance to naskh predominates and the sale is valid; if unknown, the resemblance to istithna (and the resulting gharar/ignorance) invalidates the sale. The author then contrasts this with the case of selling a free person and a slave together, where the free person is utterly outside the contract, so only the resemblance to istithna applies, making the slave's sale invalid (according to Abu Hanifa). The passage illustrates how usul tools like shabah (analogical resemblance) are used to derive fiqh rulings with nuance.

Key Terms
شبه (shabah): Resemblance or similarity that forms the basis of an analogical extension (qiyas) in Hanafi usul; here, the resemblance of the conditional option to naskh (abrogation) and istithna (exception).
النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation; here used as an analogy because the condition (khiyar) modifies the original contract, similar to how naskh modifies a prior ruling.
الاستثناء (al-istithna): Exception; here analogized because the khiyar item is temporarily excepted from the binding effect of the sale.
الخيار (al-khiyar): Option; a contractual condition allowing the buyer or seller to cancel the sale within a specified period.
