Two Objections to the Definition of I'jaz and Their Answers
Critiquing and defending a definition of i'jaz in speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the two objections to the definition of i'jaz in speech and explain the Hanafi responses using the distinctions between realized/potential ways and generic/individual unity.

Core Idea
I'jaz means the Quran surpasses all human ways, actual and potential; God's power does not negate its miraculous nature.

In this lesson, we examine two logical objections that were raised against the standard definition of i'jaz (miraculous inimitability) of speech. The objections challenge the scope of 'all other ways' and the oneness of i'jaz. The commentator provides precise answers rooted in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, distinguishing between human and divine capacity and between generic and specific unity. Understanding these objections sharpens your ability to defend foundational concepts of Quranic miraculousness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two objections
Read the passage carefully and isolate the two distinct objections against the definition of i'jaz fi al-kalam. Note the exact wording of each objection as presented by the commentator.
The first objection concerns ambiguity in 'all other ways'; the second concerns the possibility of multiple levels of i'jaz.
2
Analyze the first objection and its answer
Break down the first objection: if 'all other ways' means only actual past ways, future ways could rival; if both actual and potential ways, then God's ability to bring a like would contradict i'jaz. Then study the answer: 'all other ways' refers to human ways only – both actual and potential – because i'jaz is defined relative to human incapacity. God's power is irrelevant.
Notice the key distinction between turuq muhaqqaqa (realized) and turuq muqaddara (potential), and how the answer confines the scope to kalam bashari.
3
Analyze the second objection and its answer
The second objection argues that if multiple levels of eloquence are beyond human ability, then i'jaz would have multiple 'ways'. The answer introduces the concept of wahid nau'i (generic one) vs wahid shakhsi (individual one). I'jaz is one in genus – the single limit of human inability – encompassing all instances.
This distinction is crucial in usul al-fiqh for understanding general categories.
4
Relate the premise about prophets' miracles
The commentator includes a premise: each prophet’s miracle matches the field at its peak in his time. This supports the idea that the Quran’s i'jaz is tied to the zenith of eloquence in the Prophet’s era, reinforcing that only human ways are considered.
This is a thematic argument linking the definition to historical context.

Objections on the Definition of I'jaz in Speech. And here are two investigations: This is the last part of the commentary's phrase; the commentator intends to mention two questions and answer them. Question (1): The formulation of the question is that you have defined i'jaz in speech as: 'that it conveys the meaning in a way that is more eloquent than all other ways.' Here, which ways are meant? If you say that it means only the realized ways (already existing), i.e. those that have come into existence in past time, then this definition of i'jaz would not be correct, because for i'jaz it is necessary that the speech cannot be opposed, whereas in this case opposition would be possible, because among the potential ways—those whose possibility exists in the future—there might be a way that could rival the speech. And if you say that by 'ways' are meant both the realized ways (already existing) and the potential ways (existing in potency), then in that case bringing a like of this speech would be impossible. But in a miraculous speech this condition is not laid down, because even though the Noble Quran is miraculous, Allah is able to bring a like of it. That is, among the potential ways there can be a way that equals the Quran in eloquence and rhetoric. Then what is the meaning of the author's statement 'more eloquent than all other ways'? Question (2): You have said that i'jaz has only one way, whereas in the book named Miftah and the book named Nihayat al-I'jaz it is written that the highest limit of eloquence and what is close to the highest of the high ranks, but for humans bringing their like is impossible, so both of these are miraculous. Then the ways of i'jaz in speech become multiple. Answer to the first question: It is that we take the second alternative: that the way is more eloquent than all others, generally, whether it is more eloquent than the realized ways already existing or more eloquent than the potential ways. But those ways are specific to human speech. The context is that i'jaz in speech refers to the speech of Allah. The objective is that i'jaz in Allah's speech is that it is more eloquent than all the ways that can be expressed in the speech of other than Allah, whether realized or potential, such that it is impossible for other than Allah to bring its like. That Allah is able to bring its like does not contradict the i'jaz of Allah's speech. Every prophet was given a miracle in accordance with the excellence prevalent in his time. Second answer: Before understanding this, it is necessary to know a premise by way of introduction. The premise: Every miracle given to a prophet was given in opposition to that thing which had reached its zenith in that time. For example, in the time of Jesus (peace be upon him), medicine had reached its zenith, so he was given the miracle of reviving the dead. In the time of Moses (peace be upon him), magic and sorcery had reached their zenith, so he was given the miracle of the staff. Similarly, in the time of the Holy Prophet (peace be upon him), eloquence and rhetoric had reached their zenith, as seven odes were hung at the threshold of the Kaaba and people were unable to compete, so he was given the miracle of the Quran, that despite being eloquent and rhetorical, people could not oppose it. The summary of the answer: Now the gist of the answer is that by 'other ways' are meant the realized ways (already existing), because the concept of being more eloquent than any potential way can only be imagined if the realized ways at that time had not reached the zenith of eloquence, while this is impossible. And if it were so, then it would be against the sunna of Allah. In summary, the commentators' statement that for i'jaz in speech it is necessary to be more eloquent than potential ways is mere affectation. Answer to the second question: 'One' has two types: (1) generic one, like 'human'; (2) individual one, like 'Zayd'. Under the generic one there can be many individuals. So here 'one' means generic one, i.e., i'jaz has one limit that encompasses all instances of i'jaz: that humans are incapable of opposing it and bringing its like, unlike distorted speech which has no limit. Commentary
This passage presents two logical objections to the definition of i'jaz fi al-kalam and their refutations. The first objection concerns the scope of 'all other ways' (jami' ma'ada min al-turuq): if only actual past ways are meant, future ways could rival the speech; if both actual and potential ways are meant, then God's ability to bring a like would contradict i'jaz. The answer clarifies that 'all other ways' refers to human ways, both actual and potential, but not God's ways. God's power to create a like does not negate i'jaz because i'jaz is defined relative to human incapacity. The second objection is that if multiple levels of eloquence are beyond human ability, i'jaz would have multiple ways. The answer distinguishes between wahid nau'i (generic one) and wahid shakhsi (individual one). I'jaz has one generic limit: human inability to produce anything like it, regardless of the specific level of eloquence. The commentator also provides a premise: each prophet's miracle aligns with the field that was most advanced in his time, which supports the idea that i'jaz in speech (Quran) is a miracle because eloquence was at its peak in the Prophet's era.

Key Terms
طرق محققہ (turuq muhaqqaqa): Ways of expression that have actually occurred and are known.
طرق مقدرہ (turuq muqaddara): Ways of expression that are potential and could occur in the future.
واحد نوعی (wahid nau'i): A generic unity, a single category that includes multiple individuals.
واحد شخصی (wahid shakhsi): A specific individual, e.g., a particular person.

Redundancy in Definition: The Case of 'Bil-Istidlal'
Debating the necessity of 'bil-istidlal' in fiqh definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi argument that 'bil-istidlal' is redundant in the definition of fiqh by applying the principle of implied meaning (dalalah iltizamiyyah) to assess definitional elements.

Core Idea
A phrase that necessarily implies meaning renders its explicit mention redundant in a precise definition.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, definitions are crafted with precision. The classical definition of fiqh as 'al-ʿilm bi al-aḥkām al-sharʿiyyah al-ʿamaliyyah min adillatihā' sparked debate: does the qualifier 'bil-istidlāl' add anything? This lesson unpacks the hashya critique and the commentator's defense, sharpening your ability to assess redundancy in legal definitions.

Key Terms

بالاستدلال (bil-istidlal): through reasoning or inference, explicitly requiring mental deduction from proofs.
التزاما (iltizaman): by necessity or implication; a meaning that necessarily follows from a phrase without being explicitly stated.
تصريح بما علم التزاما (tasrih bima 'ulima al-iltizam): explicit mention of what is already known by implication.

Proof for Multiple Essential Attributes in a Simple Essence
Rational proof (dalil aqli) for multiple essential attributes in a simple essence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rational proof (dalil aqli) of elimination to demonstrate that multiple essential attributes (a'radh dhatiyya) can coexist in a simple essence (wahid haqiqi), and identify why infinite regress (tasalsul) and dependence on a separate thing are invalid.

Core Idea
A simple essence can have many essential attributes without composition because each attaches directly, not through parts or separate things.

This passage presents a rational argument proving that the Divine Essence, though simple, possesses multiple essential attributes. The argument systematically eliminates impossible modes of attachment—through a part, a separate thing, or an infinite chain—leaving direct essential attachment as the only coherent possibility. Understanding this proof strengthens the conceptual foundation for using composite 'illal (effective causes) in qiyas, a key Hanafi usul principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the question
The passage asks: Can a simple essence (like Allah) have multiple essential attributes without becoming composite?
The term 'essential attribute' (arad dhati) means an attribute that inheres in the essence directly, not accidentally.
2
List possible modes of attachment
Each attribute must attach to the essence via one of four ways: (1) through a part of the essence, (2) through a separate thing, (3) through another attribute, or (4) directly to the essence itself.
These are exhaustive logical possibilities.
3
Eliminate impossible options
- Via a part: impossible because the essence is simple (no parts). - Via a separate thing: implies the essence needs something else, indicating deficiency, which is impossible for the Divine. - Via another attribute: leads to infinite regress (tasalsul) if circular, or stops at an attribute that must then attach directly.
The argument uses the principle of non-contradiction and the impossibility of tasalsul.
4
Conclude direct attachment
At least one attribute must attach directly to the essence, proving the existence of an essential attribute. This attribute then becomes a locus for other attributes to attach through it, but ultimately they too must attach directly to avoid regress.
This establishes that multiple essential attributes can co-exist in a single simple essence.
5
Apply the reasoning
The proof shows that a simple essence can support a plurality of attributes without composition. This is crucial in Hanafi usul for validating qiyas where an 'illah (effective cause) may be composed of multiple attributes.
The passage also addresses an objection: that the chain could stop at the first essential attribute, but the response shows that would still necessitate multiplicity.

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof (dalil aqli) for the possibility of multiple essential attributes (a'radh dhatiyya) in a single simple essence (wahid haqiqi), specifically in the context of divine attributes. The commentator expands on the matn's argument. The proof works by elimination: each attribute of Allah must attach either through a part, a separate thing, another attribute, or directly to the essence. Since Allah is simple (has no parts), and cannot depend on a separate thing (would imply deficiency), the only way to avoid infinite regress (tasalsul) is that some attributes attach directly (bi dhati) to the essence, proving an essential attribute. Then other attributes must likewise attach directly, proving multiple essential attributes. The commentator addresses an objection: if one attribute attaches via another essential attribute, that still results in multiplicity. Thus, the argument establishes that multiple essential attributes are rationally necessary for a simple essence without implying composition or deficiency.

Key Terms
عرض ذاتي (arad dhati): An accident or attribute that is essential to the essence, not accidental, and whose attachment is by itself without need of an intermediary.
واحد حقيقي (wahid haqiqi): A being that is simple in essence, having no parts or multiplicity in its substance.
تسلسل (tasalsul): Infinite regress, an impossibility in causal chains.

Applying Istithna' to Sales: Illustrative Examples
Applying istithna' analogy to sales cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of istithna' to sales contracts by identifying whether an exception creates uncertainty (gharar) or a void condition (shart fasid) and derive the ruling that the sale is void.

Core Idea
An exception in a sale that allocates a share of the price to the excepted item creates uncertainty, invalidating the entire contract.

In this passage, the commentator illustrates how the usul principle of istithna' (exception) is applied to two specific sales scenarios. The first involves selling a free person alongside a slave; the second involves selling two slaves with an exception of one at his share of the price. Both cases lead to the conclusion that the sale is void, but the reasoning differs slightly: one is due to a void condition (shart fasid), the other due to uncertainty (gharar). This lesson will walk through the analogical reasoning and help you apply the same method to other cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base principle of istithna'
Istithna' (exception) in speech prevents the excepted item from entering the ruling of the general statement. In sales law, this principle governs whether an exception creates a valid or invalid contract.
This principle is foundational to understanding the examples.
2
Analyze the two sales examples
Case 1: Selling a free person and a slave together – the free person is not legally eligible for sale, so the contract effectively 'excepts' him, creating a void condition (accepting a non-sellable item as part of the deal). Case 2: Selling two slaves with an explicit exception of one at his share of the price – the excepted slave is legally eligible but explicitly removed, creating uncertainty about the price of the remaining slave and adding a void condition.
Note that in Case 1, istithna' is not real but analogous, while in Case 2, it is real.
3
Apply the principle to derive the ruling
In both cases, the sale is void. For Case 1, the void condition invalidates the contract. For Case 2, the uncertainty (gharar) in the price of the remaining slave and the void condition invalidate the sale. The passage emphasizes that the sale 'with a share' (bihissa) ab initio is void, though it could be valid as a remainder.
The reasoning shows how usul principles translate into concrete fiqh rulings.
4
Distinguish between real and analogous istithna'
The passage explicitly notes that in Case 1, istithna' is not present in reality, but the effect is analogous because the free person does not enter the contract. In Case 2, istithna' is real. Despite the difference, both lead to the same ruling of invalidity, demonstrating the breadth of the principle.
This distinction helps in applying the principle to novel cases.

The analog of istithna' is when one sells a free person and a slave for a price, or sells two slaves except this one with its share of the thousand, the sale becomes void because one of them did not enter into the sale, so the sale became with a portion ab initio, and because that which is not sold becomes a condition for accepting the sold item, so it becomes void due to the void condition. In the second case, which is when he sold two slaves except this one, the reality of istithna' is present. When one of them does not enter the sale, the sale of the other is not valid for two reasons: First, the sale of the other becomes with its share of the price corresponding to both, and a sale with a portion ab initio is void due to uncertainty. We said 'ab initio' because a sale with a portion as a remainder is valid, as will come in the case which is analogous to naskh. Second, the sale of the other is a sale with a condition contrary to the requirement of the contract, and that is that accepting that which is not sold—the free person or the excepted slave—becomes a condition for accepting the sold item.

Commentary
This passage is from a Hanafi usul commentary explaining the application of istithna' (exception) to sales contracts through two illustrative examples. The author, building on the discussion of takhsis and naskh, shows how the principle of istithna' operates in furu' (subsidiary matters). In the first example, a free person and a slave are sold together for one price. Since a free person cannot be the subject of sale, he does not enter the contract, making the sale analogous to istithna'—the free person is effectively 'excepted'. The ruling is that the entire sale is void. In the second example, two slaves are sold with a specific exception of one slave at his share of the thousand. Here, the istithna' is real, and the sale of the remaining slave is invalid for two reasons: (1) the price corresponding to both becomes uncertain when allocated to the unsold slave (gharar), and (2) accepting the unsold item as a condition violates the requirement that the sold item be free from conditions not beneficial to the contract. The passage emphasizes that the analogy holds despite the difference in the nature of the exception. The Urdu 'تشریح' clarifies that in the first case there is no actual istithna', but the effect is similar. This exercise demonstrates Hanafi methodology of applying usul principles to concrete fiqh cases, particularly the rule that an exception in a sale creates uncertainty or introduces a void condition, rendering the contract invalid.

Key Terms
استثناء (istithna'): An exception that prevents the excepted item from entering the ruling of the preceding general statement.
بیع بالحصہ (bay' bi al-hissa): A sale where the price is allocated to a portion of the item; considered void ab initio due to uncertainty but can be valid as a remainder.
شرط فاسد (shart fasid): A condition that is void and invalidates the contract when it is contrary to the requirement of the contract.

Shabah (Resemblance) in Determining Sale Validity: The Case of Free Person and Slave
Ruling on sale validity when free and slave sold together

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of shabah (resemblance) to determine the validity of a sale involving an ineligible item, and distinguish between istithna and naskh analogies.

Core Idea
A sale is invalid if an ineligible item is included from the start, as it resembles exclusion (istithna) not abrogation (naskh).

In this passage, the author tackles a subtle issue: selling a free person (who cannot be sold) together with a slave, each with a specified price. One might assume the sale is valid since the price is known and the slave is fine. However, Imam Abu Hanifa rules it invalid based on the concept of shabah (resemblance). The reasoning hinges on whether the free person enters the offer from the beginning. This lesson will guide you through the two competing analogies—istithna (exclusion) and naskh (abrogation)—and show you how the Hanafi method of istihsan (juristic preference) chooses the correct analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two analogies
Understand the two types of resemblance: istithna (exclusion) makes the sale invalid; naskh (abrogation) makes it valid.
In the passage, istithna refers to a corrupt sale where a condition excludes part of the price; naskh refers to a valid conditional sale.
2
Determine inclusion in the offer
Check whether the free person is included in the initial offer (ijab). Since a free person cannot be sold, it is impossible for it to enter the offer.
This is the key distinction from the valid case where the item is capable of being sold.
3
Apply the principle of shabah
Since the free person is never included, the transaction resembles istithna: it is as if the seller is excluding the free person from the sale, but the exclusion is built-in from the start. Therefore, the sale of the slave is also invalid.
Resemblance to naskh would only occur if the free person were replaceable with a valid item subject to a condition.
4
Contrast with the option condition case
If the free person were replaced by an item that can be sold but is subject to a condition (like an option), the condition is separate and the sale is valid (naskh). Here, the ineligibility of the free person prevents any valid sale.
This contrast clarifies when naskh applies: only when the item itself is capable of being sold.

Commentary
This passage is an extended ruling on the validity of a sale when a free person and a slave are sold together with separate prices. The author first distinguishes between two scenarios in a sale with an option condition: (1) If the object of option or price is unknown, the sale resembles an exclusion (istithna) and is invalid; (2) If both are known, it resembles abrogation (naskh) and is valid. Then a specific case is examined: selling a free person (who cannot be sold) and a slave together, each with a specified price. One might think this case resembles the first valid scenario (since both price and object are known), leading to the sale being valid. However, Imam Abu Hanifa rules the sale invalid. The author explains: the free person is incapable of being sold, so it never enters the offer from the start; thus, the transaction is analogous to selling a slave with a condition excluding part of the price (istithna), not to a valid conditional sale (naskh). Hence the slave's sale fails. This is contrasted with a proper option condition where the slave is fully capable of being sold. The reasoning uses the concept of shabah (resemblance) to determine the ruling, a key Hanafi tool.

Key Terms
مشابہت (mushābaha): Resemblance or analogy used to determine the legal nature of a transaction.
استثناء (istithnā'): Exclusion; here it refers to a transaction where a conditional exclusion renders it analogous to a corrupt sale.
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation; here used metonymically to mean a valid conditional transaction that overrides general rules.

A) Because the price is ignored due to the free person's inclusion.

Key Takeaway
The validity of a sale depends not only on price certainty but also on the legal capacity of the items to be sold; items that cannot be sold never enter the offer, making the transaction resemble istithna and invalid.
