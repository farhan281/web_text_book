The Precise Attachment of 'Min Adillatihā': Why a Muqallid's Knowledge Is Not Fiqh
Clarifying grammatical attachment in the definition of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the phrase 'min adillatihā' modifies knowledge and not rulings, and state the consequence for the definition of fiqh.

Core Idea
Fiqh requires direct examination of detailed evidences; second-hand knowledge is not fiqh.

This passage resolves a subtle ambiguity in the definition of fiqh. The phrase 'min adillatihā' (from its evidences) could be misread as modifying 'rulings', which would inadvertently include the muqallid's knowledge. The commentator carefully shows that it modifies 'knowledge', and that fiqh is only the knowledge gained by personally examining the evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the grammatical point
The phrase 'min adillatihā' is attached either to 'al-ahkām' (rulings) or to 'al-ʿilm' (knowledge). The false assumption is that it attaches to 'rulings'. The correct view is that it attaches to 'knowledge'.
2
Understand the implication of the false assumption
If it modified rulings, then knowing rulings that are based on detailed evidences might suffice, and the muqallid would be included in the definition of fiqh.
3
Grasp the rebuttal
The commentator refutes this: (a) from evidence we obtain knowledge of the thing, not the thing itself; (b) if 'ruling' means the eternal address of Allah, it cannot be obtained; (c) the muqallid's knowledge is based on the mujtahid's statement, not on examining evidence.
Thus the muqallid's knowledge is not directly obtained from the evidence.
4
Note the restriction to 'detailed evidences'
The restriction to detailed evidences excludes knowledge derived from general causes or indicators (e.g., knowing obligation from the existence of a cause without examining the specific textual evidence).
Such general knowledge is also not fiqh.
5
Conclude the status of the muqallid
Therefore, the muqallid's knowledge does not qualify as fiqh because it is not obtained through direct examination (naẓar) of detailed evidences.
Fiqh is only for the mujtahid who personally examines the evidences.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of the matn's phrase 'min adillatihā' (from its evidences). There is a potential misunderstanding: one might think that 'min adillatihā' modifies 'al-ahkām' (rulings), meaning that the rulings come from detailed evidences. If so, the muqallid (follower) who knows the rulings from the mujtahid would also have knowledge from evidences indirectly, and thus his knowledge might be considered fiqh. The commentator refutes this by clarifying that 'min adillatihā' actually modifies 'al-ʿilm' (knowledge) not 'al-ahkām'. Thus, fiqh is knowledge that is directly obtained by examining detailed evidences. The muqallid's knowledge is not obtained through such examination; it is only based on the mujtahid's statement. Additionally, the commentator notes that if 'ruling' is taken as the eternal address of Allah, it cannot be 'obtained' from anything. The restriction to 'detailed evidences' further excludes knowledge derived from general principles, such as knowing that something is obligatory merely because its cause exists (without examining the specific evidence). Such knowledge is not considered fiqh. Thus, the passage refines the definition of fiqh and excludes the muqallid's knowledge from it.

Key Terms
مقلد (muqallid): One who follows a mujtahid without knowing the detailed evidence.
ادلة تفصيلية (adillah tafṣīliyyah): Detailed evidences from the Qur'an and Sunnah that are directly examined to derive a ruling.

Definition of Fiqh as a Faculty: The Four Objections Resolved
Dispute over defining fiqh as a faculty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of fiqh as a faculty (malaka), explain the four objections raised against it, and evaluate the responses of the commentators.

Core Idea
Fiqh is a faculty enabling deduction, not knowledge of all rulings.

How do we define fiqh? Is it knowing every single ruling, or having the capacity to derive them? This passage dives into a classic usul dispute: Ibn al-Hajib objects to defining fiqh as a faculty (malaka), but the Hanafi commentators defend it with subtle reasoning about obstacles to ijtihad. By working through their arguments, you will sharpen your own understanding of what it means to be a mujtahid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contested Definition
The passage revolves around defining fiqh. The matn (Ibn al-Hajib) objects to defining fiqh as a 'faculty' (malaka). First, grasp what the proponents claim: fiqh is a stable faculty that enables a mujtahid to derive rulings from detailed evidence.
2
Understand the Four Objections
Ibn al-Hajib raises four objections: (1) some mujtahids never know some rulings, (2) some make mistakes, (3) some issues have no room for ijtihad, (4) the word 'ilm (knowledge) is not used for a faculty. List these and see how they attack the definition.
The objections are based on the assumption that a faculty must guarantee correct knowledge of everything.
3
Follow the Commentators' Responses
The commentators (sharih) respond: (1) conflict of evidences, (2) presence of obstacles, (3) conflict between wahm (imagination) and reason, (4) similarity of truth and falsehood. Note how each response targets a different type of obstacle, showing that the faculty remains intact even if rulings are not always produced.
The responses aim to show that inability to answer does not negate the faculty; external factors cause the failure.
4
Evaluate the Strength of Arguments
The text then provides further critique: some say the first response (conflict of evidences) is weak because suspension (tawaqquf) itself is a ruling; others say the second (obstacles) is weak because if conditions are met, obstacles should not exist. Reflect on which responses are stronger and why.
This step develops your critical thinking: not all arguments are equally sound.

The Independence of a Later Mujtahid from Earlier Qiyasi Rulings
Rule in usul al-fiqh on the independence of later mujtahids from earlier qiyasi rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a later mujtahid is not obligated to know the qiyasi rulings of earlier mujtahids and apply the two arguments from the Hanafi usul perspective.

Core Idea
A later mujtahid must follow his own ijtihad; earlier qiyasi rulings are not binding because revelation through ijtihad is indirect.

This lesson explores a key principle in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the relationship between the ijtihad of earlier and later mujtahids. The passage addresses a hypothetical objection: if fiqh is knowledge of rulings that appear from revelation, then qiyasi rulings (derived via analogy) also appear from revelation indirectly, so shouldn't a later mujtahid be required to know them? The author provides two clear answers, both affirming the independence of later mujtahids. By the end, you will be able to articulate these arguments and understand the epistemological status of qiyasi rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central question
The passage presents a second objection: if fiqh is defined as knowledge of rulings whose appearance is from revelation, then qiyasi issues should be included because qiyas manifests revelation. So why does the author exclude them from the definition?
This objection hinges on the meaning of 'appearance from revelation'.
2
Examine the first answer
Answer (1) distinguishes between the earlier mujtahid's perspective and the later one. For the earlier mujtahid, his qiyasi ruling may or may not correspond to the actual divine ruling, since mujtahids can err. For the later mujtahid, taqlid is forbidden; he must rely on his own ijtihad. Hence, knowing the earlier ruling is not necessary.
Key principle: Al-mujtahid yukhti' wa yusib (the mujtahid errs and is correct).
3
Examine the second answer
Answer (2) refines the definition: 'appearance from revelation' means directly, without intermediary. Qiyasi rulings involve the intermediary of the mujtahid's reasoning, so they are not fiqh in the direct sense. Thus, later mujtahids do not need to know them.
This upholds the strict definition of fiqh as directly revealed knowledge.
4
Understand the practical implication
The passage affirms that every mujtahid is independent. A later mujtahid cannot be bound by the qiyasi conclusions of an earlier one, because ijtihad is personal and taqlid is impermissible for a qualified mujtahid.
This protects the dynamism of ijtihad across generations.

Conditions for Ijtihad and Exclusion of Taqlid from Usul
Discussing ijtihad's condition re: ijma and taqlid's exclusion from usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition that a later mujtahid cannot contradict compound ijma, and explain why taqlid is excluded from usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Compound ijma binds later mujtahids; taqlid is not usul because it does not derive from the four evidences.

This passage from a commentary on usul al-fiqh clarifies two important points: first, the condition that ijtihad must avoid contradicting the compound consensus of earlier mujtahids; second, that discussions of taqlid in fiqh books are merely comparative and do not make taqlid a subject of usul. Understanding these distinctions sharpens your grasp of the scope and method of Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for ijtihad regarding prior opinions
Find the statement that says 'it is required in matters where opinions of ijtihad have preceded, to avoid contradicting ijma.' This establishes a restriction on later ijtihad.
2
Understand compound ijma (ijma murakkab)
When multiple mujtahids of one era have expressed opinions on a matter, their consensus forms compound ijma. A later mujtahid cannot give a new opinion opposing that consensus; he may only prefer among the existing views.
This is unique to Hanafi usul; other schools may define ijma differently.
3
Analyze the exclusion of taqlid from usul
The commentator argues that taqlid is not part of usul al-fiqh because the muqallid does not derive rulings from the four evidences. The discussion of taqlid in books is only for contrast with ijtihad.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi reasoning for excluding taqlid
The muqallid attains fiqh issues through the rules of his madhhab, not through the four evidences. Therefore, his method does not qualify as usul, which is the study of the evidences themselves.
This clarifies the boundary of usul al-fiqh as a discipline.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary addresses two key issues. First, it establishes a condition for ijtihad: if multiple mujtahids of a given era have already expressed opinions on a matter, a later mujtahid cannot contradict them, lest he violate compound ijma (ijma murakkab). However, if no ijtihad or only a single mujtahid's opinion exists, a new mujtahid may differ or give preference. Second, it discusses whether taqlid (following) belongs to usul al-fiqh. The commentator argues it does not, because the muqallid's knowledge of rulings is not derived from the four evidences (Quran, Sunnah, ijma, qiyas) but from following the rules of a madhhab. Thus taqlid is merely the opposite of ijtihad and is discussed in fiqh books only for comparison, not as a core subject of usul. The passage ends with a critique of the matn author's claim that tawassul occurs through taqlid, arguing it is far-fetched.

Key Terms
اجتہاد (ijtihad): The process of deriving fiqh rulings from the four evidences using one's own reasoning.
اجماع (ijma): Consensus of mujtahids of an era on a ruling; violating it is prohibited.
تقلید (taqlid): Following a mujtahid's ruling without knowing the evidence from the four sources.

Key Takeaway
Compound ijma is binding; later mujtahids may only prefer, not contradict. Taqlid is not part of usul al-fiqh because it does not derive from the four evidences.
