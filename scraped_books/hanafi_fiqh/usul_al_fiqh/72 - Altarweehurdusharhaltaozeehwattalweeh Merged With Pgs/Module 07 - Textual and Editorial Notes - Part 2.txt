Understanding Badal al-Ishtimal: Why 'Based on Four Pillars' is Not a Direct Substitute
Grammatical analysis of substitution type

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between badal al-ishtimal and badal istilahi, and apply the rule of intendedness to identify substitution types in Hanafi textual commentary.

Core Idea
Badal al-ishtimal completes a deficient expression without replacing it, preserving the intendedness of both.

In Hanafi usul commentaries, careful grammatical analysis reveals precise relationships between phrases. This passage addresses a specific instance: why the commentator treats 'based on four pillars' as a substitution of inclusion (badal al-ishtimal) rather than a direct grammatical substitute (badal istilahi). The key lies in whether the first phrase is intended in its own right or merely a preface.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two phrases
Locate the first sentence ('an ja'ala usul al-shar'iyya') and the second ('buniya 'ala arba'at arkan') in the commentary. Note that the first is meant to extol usul al-fiqh but is deficient; the second fully expresses its grandeur.
2
Apply the rule for badal al-ishtimal
Recall: if a first expression is deficient in conveying the intended meaning and a second expression supplies that deficiency, then the second is a badal al-ishtimal from the first. Here, the first phrase only mentions 'made the foundations', while the second adds the key point of 'based on four pillars' to complete the praise.
Badal al-ishtimal is a grammatical substitution where the substitute includes the intended meaning but does not replace the original; both remain meaningful.
3
Test the condition for badal istilahi
For a direct substitute (badal istilahi), the substitute (badal) is the intended meaning and the substituted (mubdal minhu) is not intended—it is only a preface. In this case, both phrases are intended: the first sets the context, the second completes it. Therefore, direct substitution fails.
The commentator explicitly states: 'the first sentence is also intended, not unintended.'
4
Evaluate the alternative condition of syntactic position
Some scholars argued that a sentence must be in a syntactic position (mahall i'rab) to function as mubdal minhu. The commentator refutes this as only a predominant rule, not universal, citing that apposition with a letter (عطف بحرف) commonly occurs with sentences not in a syntactic position. Thus the intendedness criterion is decisive.
This step reinforces that the core reason is intendedness, not syntactic constraints.

Linguistic Analysis of 'Auda'a' and the Usul Concept of Tadmeen
Analysing the verb 'auda'a' with preposition 'fi' using tadmeen.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the concept of tadmeen to explain unusual prepositional usage in Arabic legal texts and distinguish it from tasamuh.

Core Idea
Tadmeen: a verb implies another meaning to justify a different preposition.

In this lesson, we explore a classic usul example: the verb 'auda'a' normally takes a direct object, but in Quranic phrasing it appears with 'fi'. The commentator presents two linguistic explanations: tasamuh (leniency) and tadmeen (implication). We focus on tadmeen as the preferred method for precision, and we see how this linguistic tool is vital for interpreting legal texts correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the verb and its normal transitivity.
Look at the verb 'auda'a' (to deposit). It is normally transitive with two objects, e.g., 'auda'tuhu malan' (I entrusted him wealth). It does not take a preposition like 'fi'.
2
Observe the unusual construction in the text.
The text says 'wa 'auda'ha fiha' (and He deposited them in them). The verb is followed by 'fi', which is not its typical preposition.
This is the linguistic puzzle.
3
Consider the first explanation: tasamuh.
One answer is that the language allows leniency (tasamuh) – sometimes a verb takes an unusual preposition loosely. This is a simple but imprecise explanation.
Tasamuh is accepted but not preferred for legal precision.
4
Apply tadmeen as the precise solution.
Tadmeen means the verb implies the meaning of another verb. Here 'auda' implies 'idraj' (insertion), which naturally takes 'fi'. So the underlying phrase is 'auda'ha mudrajan fiha' (He deposited them inserted in them).
This is the preferred Hanafi approach.
5
Confirm the resulting interpretation.
With tadmeen, the construction is grammatically sound: the verb takes 'fi' because of the implied meaning. This precision is crucial for deriving correct legal rulings from the text.
No objection (la i'tirad).

1
Identify the proposed grammatical category
The commentator states that 'fasl khitab' is an idafa of the type 'idafat al-sifa ila al-mawsuf' – an adjective-noun attribution. This means 'fasl' should be an adjective describing 'khitab'.
2
Recognize the objection
The objection is that both 'fasl' and 'khitab' are infinitives (masdars). For a masdar to be predicated on another masdar, their meanings must be identical. But 'fasl' means 'separation' and 'khitab' means 'speech' – different meanings, so the construction would be invalid.

Linguistic Research on Key Terms: Hadab, Urwah, and Sihr
Lexical definitions and metaphorical explanation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the lexical terms hadab and urwah, explain their metaphorical usage in the phrase ‘muttaqina ahadab al-sihr mutamassikan bi’urwat al-i’jaz’, and cite the support from al-Sihah for the definition of sihr.

Core Idea
The commentator uses lexical precision and metaphor to show that eloquent human speech can approach but never equal the Quran's i'jaz.

After establishing the theoretical framework, the commentator now delves into lexical research. This passage clarifies three key terms—hadab, urwah, and sihr—and explains how the matn uses them metaphorically. By citing the authoritative lexicon al-Sihah, the commentator strengthens the definition of sihr as subtle speech, and then resolves the matn's hyperbolic claim that human speech can be 'like i'jaz' — emphasizing that true i'jaz belongs solely to divine speech.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three key terms and their literal meanings
From the text, extract the definitions of hadab (loops on a garment), urwah (handle of a pitcher), and sihr (taking/captivating, subtle speech). Note that ahadab is the plural of hadab.
2
Understand the metaphorical contrast
The matn uses 'ahdab al-sihr' (loops of sihr) and 'urwat al-i'jaz' (handle of i'jaz). The commentator explains this as hyperbole: clinging to the loops of sihr and holding the handle of i'jaz means the speech approaches sihr and i'jaz in subtlety and eloquence, but not in reality.
The urwah (handle) is stronger than the loops, suggesting i'jaz is the more secure grasp.
3
Recognize the role of al-Sihah citation
The commentator cites the famous lexicon al-Sihah to support the definition of sihr as 'al-akhdh' (taking) and anything subtle and delicate. This answers an implied question about the basis for the matn's use of sihr.
This shows how Hanafi usul relies on authoritative linguistic sources.
4
Extract the commentator's conclusion
The commentator explicitly states that the matn's phrase is mubalaghah (hyperbole). In reality, i'jaz is found only in the speech of Allah (Quran). This prevents misinterpretation that human speech can be miraculous.

Resolves apparent contradiction in pronoun usage in matn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the grammatical rule governing pronoun reference to 'usul al-fiqh' in the matn of Al-Tawdih and explain why the author's usage is consistent.

Core Idea
Non-rational plurals take feminine singular pronouns; titular definitions treat the phrase as singular masculine.

This passage addresses a seemingly contradictory pronoun usage in the matn of Al-Tawdih. The author uses a feminine pronoun when referring to usul al-fiqh in the additive definition, but a masculine pronoun in the titular definition. The commentator resolves this by invoking a grammatical rule about non-rational plurals and the shift in reference when the term becomes a proper title.

Identify the Pronoun Shift
Locate the two occurrences: feminine 'ha' in 'fa'rifha' (additive definition) and masculine 'hu' in 'al'an na'rifuhu' (titular definition).
The same phrase takes two different pronouns.

Analyze the Grammatical Basis
Understand that 'usul' is a plural of non-rational 'asl' (jam' ghayr 'aqil). Arabic grammar requires feminine singular pronouns for such plurals.
This rule is consistent across classical Arabic.
3
Recognize the Definition Type
Additive definition (idafi) retains the plurality of 'usul'; titular definition (laqabi) treats the phrase as a singular proper name (mu'akkad).
The meaning shifts from a compound to a title.
4
Apply the Rule
Conclude that no contradiction exists because the pronoun refers to different grammatical identities: plural non-rational in one context, singular title in the other.
The commentator's explanation resolves the apparent inconsistency.

Analyzing the Redundancy Objection in the Hanafi Definition of Fiqh
Response to criticism that the definition of fiqh contains redundancy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the redundancy objection to the Hanafi definition of fiqh and apply the usul principle that a qayd may serve to remove a delusion (daf' wahm).

Core Idea
'Istidlal' in the definition of fiqh is not redundant; it removes the delusion that fiqh knowledge is intuitive.

You may have seen the classic Hanafi definition of fiqh: 'al-ilm bi al-ahkam al-shar'iyyah al-amaliyyah min adillatiha al-tafsiliyyah'. Some scholars objected that the word 'istidlal' (inference) appears in some versions as redundant. This passage from the commentary on Al-Tawdih wa Al-Talwih untangles the logic behind this objection, revealing the meticulous care Hanafi usul scholars take in every word of a definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Redundancy Objection
First, understand the core charge: if 'min adillatiha al-tafsiliyyah' (from detailed evidence) already implies inference, then adding 'istidlal' is redundant. If it does not imply inference, then 'min adillatiha' is itself insufficient. The objector argues that both answers from the commentator fail.
This objection targets the logical consistency of the definition.
2
Analyze the Two States of 'min adillatiha'
The passage breaks down the relationship: either 'min adillatiha' indicates istidlal explicitly (صراحتاً) or by implication (التزاماً). In the first case, istidlal could still be needed to dispel a delusion (دفع وهم) that the knowledge is intuitive. In the second case, istidlal might be required for explicit articulation if implicit meanings are not accepted in definitions.
This distinction is key to understanding the Hanafi approach.
3
Understand the Purpose of 'istidlal'
Work through the two scenarios: (a) if explicit indication, then 'istidlal' is a qayd ihtirazi to exclude the possibility of intuitive knowledge; (b) if implicit indication, then 'istidlal' serves either as a specification of what is known implicitly (if implicit meanings are not allowed in definitions) or as mere clarification (if implicit meanings are allowed).
In each case, 'istidlal' has a distinct function beyond redundancy.
4
Evaluate the Replies to the Replies
The passage then considers two counter-replies: (1) The matn's statement that redundancy exists is not negated by the above benefits; redundancy remains but is acceptable. (2) The matn's 'wa la shak annahu' means the restriction is not necessary, not that it is impermissible. Analyse these to see how the author resolves the tension.
The final position acknowledges some redundancy but argues it serves a valid purpose.

The Circularity (Dawr) in Defining Quran and Mushaf

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A valid definition cannot make two terms mutually dependent—that is dawr, and it is invalid.

One of the foundational challenges in Hanafi usul is defining key terms without falling into logical circularity. In this passage, the commentator (Sharh) examines the problem of defining 'Quran' and 'Mushaf' in terms of each other. We will first understand why this circularity arises, then analyze the commentator's rejection of an attempted solution that distinguishes between 'Book' (Kitab) and 'Quran'.

Guided Path
1

Read the opening lines: Quran is defined as 'what is between the covers of the Mushaf' and Mushaf as 'what contains the Quran'. Recognize that each term references the other directly.

2
Understand the condition for real definition
The commentator notes that in a real (haqiqi) definition, all parts must be known. If we ask 'What is Mushaf?' the answer 'what contains the Quran' already requires knowing Quran. This creates a cycle.
Circularity (dawr) occurs when a thing depends on itself through an intermediary.
3
Examine the first attempted escape
Some scholars propose that the definition of 'what has been transmitted to us' applies to the Book (Kitab), not Quran. Then Mushaf depends on Quran, and Book depends on Mushaf, changing the direction of dependency (jihah al-tawaqquf).
They claim this breaks the cycle because Quran and Book are treated as distinct.
4
Apply the commentator's refutation
The commentator asserts that Quran and Book are identical in essence (mahiyya). If Mushaf depends on Quran, it also depends on Book, so the dependency remains unchanged. Hence circularity persists.
Identity of essence is the key principle: if two terms share the same reality, any dependency on one is dependency on the other.

It will be understood from the indication or by relying on custom (urf) that Mushaf is well-known in custom; everyone recognizes what Mushaf is. So understanding Quran depends on Mushaf, but understanding Mushaf does not depend on Quran. However, if the definition were real (haqiqi), then circularity (dawr) would be necessary because in a real definition it is necessary to know the definitions of all parts, so inevitably Mushaf would be defined as 'what contains the Quran.' Then knowledge of Quran would depend on Mushaf, and knowledge of Mushaf would depend on Quran, and this is stopping a thing upon itself, which is called circularity and is invalid. To avoid circularity, some scholars offered interpretations and the commentator refuted them. The second part of the commentary's text: The commentator refutes some scholars who said that circularity does not arise. So understanding Mushaf depends on Quran, but understanding Quran does not depend on Mushaf; rather understanding the Book depends on Mushaf. So direction of dependency changes, and when direction changes, circularity does not occur. The commentator's refutation: The commentator said that there is no difference between Quran and revealed recitation; both have the same essence and reality. Thus Mushaf became general, and its knowledge does not depend on Quran, so circularity does not occur. However, another objection arises: if Mushaf is taken in a general sense, then abrogated recitations and irregular (shadhdh) readings would be included in Quran because Mushafs contain abrogated and irregular readings. They answered that although due to the generality of pages these readings would enter, they are excluded by the condition 'what has been transmitted to us' because abrogated verses and irregular readings are not transmitted by tawatur. The commentator's refutation: The commentator (Sharh) refuted this answer: The customary meaning of Mushaf, 'what contains the Quran,' has become a reality, and the lexical meaning has become metaphorical (majaz) in custom. So if Mushaf is taken in its lexical meaning, there is an adoption of a metaphorical meaning from a real meaning, and in definitions one does not adopt metaphorical from real. So we take the real meaning, 'what contains the Quran,' and hence circularity occurs. 'If it is said': This is the sixth part. Here the commentator mentions another group's response and refutes it. The usuli scholars said that from 'Quran' is intended the universal meaning (mafhum kulli) – i.e., Quran is a universal meaning that applies to the whole and to each individual verse. Therefore knowledge of the universal meaning depends on Mushaf, and in the definition of Mushaf 'what is written in it of the Quran,' by Quran is meant the individual totality (majmu' shakhsi). So the direction of dependency has changed: the universal meaning is dependent, and the individual totality is the thing on which it depends. Hence circularity does not occur, because circularity would only occur if the universal meaning depended on the universal meaning.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion from the commentary (Sharh) on the definition of Quran and Mushaf, specifically addressing the logical problem of circularity (dawr). The commentator presents and refutes several attempts to avoid circularity. The core issue: To define Quran as 'what is between the covers of the Mushaf' and Mushaf as 'what contains the Quran' creates a vicious circle. The commentator examines six responses: (1) Distinguishing between 'Book' and 'Quran' – rejected because they are identical in essence. (2) Distinguishing Quran from 'revealed recitation' – rejected because they are the same. (3) Using the author's (Musannif) statement that the definition is general – shows that Quran and Book are the same. (4) Taking 'Mushaf' in its lexical meaning (general) – rejected because customary meaning has become real, and definitions do not use metaphorical meanings. (5) Distinguishing between the universal meaning of Quran and the individual totality – rejected? The text ends before the refutation, but the commentator's position is clear: circularity remains. The passage highlights a fundamental logical requirement in definitions: a defined term cannot be part of its own definition in a way that creates circular dependency. All attempted solutions fail because Quran and Mushaf refer to the same reality.

Key Terms
دور (dawr): A logical fallacy where a thing is defined by another thing that itself depends on the first thing.
ماہیت (mahiyya): Essence or quiddity of a thing; what makes it what it is.
جہت توقف (jihah al-tawaqquf): The direction or aspect of dependency in a definition.

Can a Specific Person (Shakhsi) Be Defined? A Usul Dispute
Dispute over definition of a specific person in usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the arguments for and against defining a specific person in usul al-fiqh and apply the Hanafi resolution to analogous definitional problems.

Core Idea
A specific person can be defined by his ultimate existence, but not by a purely mental definition that fixes him in the mind.

In usul al-fiqh, definitions (ta'rif) are crucial for legal reasoning. This passage presents a dispute: can a specific individual, like Zayd, be logically defined? An objector argues yes, using a composite definition of essence and accidents. The Hanafi commentator refutes two attempted answers and settles on a nuanced resolution. Understanding this debate sharpens your ability to handle definitions in legal discourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
The passage asks whether a specific person (shakhsi) can be defined by a had (definition). The objector claims yes because a person is a composite of essence and individuation. Your task is to isolate the question and the opposing views.
The dispute is over the possibility of a real definition (ta'rif haqiqi) for a person.
2
Analyze the objector’s argument
The objector says a person like Zayd is a composite by consideration (murakkab i'tibari) of essence and individuation. Therefore, a definition including both (e.g., rational animal + specifics) is valid and provides distinction. Identify the components of this argument.
The objector’s example: Zayd = rational animal, black, short stature.
3
Examine the first answer and its refutation
The first answer says such a definition is merely verbal (ta'rif lafzi), not real (haqiqi), so it does not solve the problem. The commentator rejects this because it would reduce the definition of the Quran to verbal as well, which is untenable. Understand why the commentator finds this answer insufficient.
The commentator applies consistency: if we accept this, many standard definitions become verbal.
4
Examine the second answer and its refutation
The second answer argues that definitions including accidents (e.g., youth, handsomeness) are not always true because accidents change. The commentator refutes that when accidents change, the defined itself ceases to be that same person; thus the definition need not apply. Recognize the logical move.
The commentator shifts focus: the definiendum is not permanent when accidents vanish.

Extract the final resolution (al-Haqq)
The commentator concludes that a person can be defined by a definition that distinguishes him by his ultimate existence (wujud ke intiha), but not by a purely mental definition. This means a definition can refer to his unique existence, not to changeable attributes. State the resolution in your own words.
The key is that definition by existence is stable and non-mental.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute about whether a person (الشخصی), such as Zayd, can be given a definition (حد) in the logical sense. The objector argues that a person is a composite (مرکب اعتباری) of essence and individuation, so a definition including both is possible. The final resolution (الحق) is that a person can be defined by a definition that distinguishes him by his ultimate existence (وجود کے انتہاء), but not by a purely mental distinction. Thus, it is correct to say that a person has a definition. This discussion is rooted in Hanafi usul al-fiqh's engagement with logic and definition.

Usul dispute on the nature of the Quran

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the dialectical objection against meaning-only Quran and explain the Hanafi response using the concept of khalifah.

Core Idea
The Quran is both meaning and nazm, but meaning can stand alone when a substitute fulfills the role of nazm.

This passage presents a classic usul dispute: is the Quran only the meaning, or does it necessarily include the Arabic wording? The objection raises two problems if meaning alone is considered Quran. The Hanafi answer addresses the first problem by arguing that the reality of nazm is preserved through its substitute (khalifah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of the objection
The objector presents a dilemma: either only meaning is Quran, or meaning is not Quran. Each horn leads to problems. You need to recognize this dialectical structure.
This is a standard usul method of objection (i'tirad).
2
Understand the first horn's implications
If only meaning is Quran, the objector claims two consequences: (1) nazm (Arabic wording) loses all reality and importance, which is impossible because nazm is either essence or part of Quran; (2) the accepted definition of Quran (al-maktub fi al-masahif) would not apply since that definition describes words.
These are logical consequences, not textual proofs.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to the first horn
The answerer accepts the first horn (meaning is Quran) but rejects the two problems. He argues that nazm retains its reality until it has a substitute (khalifah). Thus, when Persian recitation occurs, the substitute preserves the role of nazm.
The concept of khalifah is key: it allows meaning to stand in for the original wording when necessary.
4
Apply the response to practical scenario
Consider a person reciting a Persian translation of the Quran in prayer. According to the Hanafi view, this is valid because the meaning (the essence of Quran) is present, and the Persian text acts as khalifah for the Arabic nazm.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf: the practical difference in ruling.
5
Note the incomplete nature of the passage
The passage cuts off mid-sentence; the full answer may continue on the next page. The second horn (meaning is not Quran) and its response are not yet addressed.
This suggests a larger argument that may be completed later.

Shari'ah Types vs. Logical Types
Explaining how shari'ah defines types based on legal specialization

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a logical type (naw' mantiqi) and a shari'ah type (naw' shar'i) and explain the Hanafi basis for the latter.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, a 'type' is defined by its legal effects, not by logical genus-species.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi usul concept: the nature of 'type' (naw') in the context of shari'ah. The passage contrasts the logical classification (e.g., 'horse' as a species under animal) with a shari'ah-based classification (e.g., 'man' and 'woman' as distinct types due to unique rulings). Understanding this distinction is vital for correctly applying analogical reasoning and identifying the scope of legal texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of classification
Note that the passage contrasts 'logical type' (naw' mantiqi), like 'horse' (faras), with 'shari'ah type' (naw' shar'i), like 'man' (rajul). The key difference is the basis: logical type relies on genus-species hierarchy; shari'ah type relies on shared legal rulings.
The example 'horse' is a natural species, while 'man' in shari'ah is a legal category.
2
Extract the rulings exclusive to men
The passage lists prophethood, imamate, and testimony in hudud and qisas as exclusive to men. These rulings form the basis for considering men as a distinct type.
These are not all exclusive rulings; just examples.
3
Understand why man is not a logical type
Logically, 'man' and 'woman' belong to the same species (human). But shari'ah separates them because of differing legal capacities. Thus, the shari'ah type does not align with the logical type.
This shows that fiqh categories may override natural classifications.
4
Apply the concept to other categories
Consider other fiqh categories like 'free' and 'slave'. Although biologically the same species, they are treated as distinct types due to different rulings (e.g., eligibility for qisas).
This reinforces the Hanafi principle: legal effect determines type.

Commentary
This passage explains the concept of 'type' (naw') in usul al-fiqh as used in the Hanafi school. The author comments on a statement that divides words by consideration of type (i.e., genus and species). He clarifies that shari'ah does not always follow the logical definition of type. For example, 'horse' (faras) is a logical type because it is a species under animal. But 'man' (rajul) in shari'ah is not a logical type; rather, shari'ah considers man and woman as two distinct types due to the specific rulings that apply exclusively to men, such as prophethood, imamate, and testimony in hadd and qisas cases. Thus, the shari'ah definition of type is based on legal effects, not purely on genus-species hierarchy.
