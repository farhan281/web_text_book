Mutlaq Refers to an Individual, Not the Essence
Critical refinement of mutlaq's definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi definition of mutlaq (as an individual of the concept, not the essence) and apply it to interpret a Quranic mutlaq command.

Core Idea
Mutlaq commands an individual of the concept, not the abstract essence – freeing 'a believing slave' means one actual slave, not the idea of freedom.

A common initial understanding of mutlaq is that it refers to the named essence (musamma) itself, free from restrictions. But the Hanafi school refines this: mutlaq actually denotes an individual instance of that essence. This distinction is crucial for correctly interpreting divine commands, such as the Quranic injunction to free a believing slave. Misunderstanding it could lead to abstract, impractical obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested statement
Read the matn's claim: 'If what is intended from it is the named (musamma) without any restriction, then it is mutlaq.' This suggests mutlaq points to the essence itself.
The commentator flags that this implication is wrong.
2
Understand the wrong implication
If mutlaq referred to the essence, a command like 'free a believing slave' would mean freeing the abstract concept of 'believing slave', which is impossible. The obligation must be executable.
3
Examine the Quranic counter-example
The verse (al-Mujadilah 58:3) commands freeing a 'raqabatin mu'minatin'. The Hanafi reading: this requires an actual individual slave who is believing, not the conceptual essence.
The word 'raqabah' is a mutlaq noun indicating any single individual of that category.
4
Derive the correct definition
Thus, mutlaq in Hanafi usul refers to an individual (fard) of the concept, unrestricted by accidental attributes. It does not refer to the essence (musamma) per se.
Essence is the abstract category; individuals are its concrete instances.
5
Apply the definition
When encountering any mutlaq command, interpret it as requiring one unspecified individual of the named category, without additional conditions.
This principle affects many fiqh rulings derived from mutlaq Quranic or hadith texts.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement of a statement from the matn that defines mutlaq as referring to the named essence (musamma) without restriction. The commentator argues that this suggests the mutlaq refers to the essence itself, not to an individual instance. He corrects this by pointing to the Quranic example 'freeing a believing slave' (al-Mujadilah 58:3). In Hanafi usul, the mutlaq here does not command freeing the abstract concept of 'believing slave', but rather freeing a specific individual instance of that concept, without any additional restrictions. Thus, mutlaq denotes an individual (fard) of the concept, not the conceptual essence itself. This is a standard Hanafi position on the meaning of mutlaq.

Key Terms
مطلق (muṭlaq): A word indicating an individual instance of a concept without restriction by accidental attributes.
مسّمى (musammā): The named thing, the essence itself.
فرد (fard): An individual instance of a concept.

Indication of the Absolute (Mutlaq): Meaning or Individuals?
Debate on whether mutlaq indicates meaning or individuals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that mutlaq primarily indicates meaning (mafhum) and explain how external qarinah specifies it to individuals.

Core Idea
Mutlaq indicates the meaning; the individual is shown by context, not the word itself.

This passage examines a central question in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: does an absolute (mutlaq) word indicate its meaning or an individual instance? The objector argues from the Quranic verse 'tahriru raqabatin' that mutlaq must indicate individuals because only individuals are freed. The commentator defends the Hanafi principle that mutlaq primarily indicates the meaning (the genus), and any indication of a specific individual is due to an external contextual indicator (qarinah kharijiyyah). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly deriving rulings from revealed texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
The objector claims mutlaq indicates both meaning and individual equally. The Hanafi position (defended by the commentator) holds that mutlaq primarily indicates the meaning (mafhum).
2
Examine the objector's dalil
The objector cites the Quranic phrase 'tahriru raqabatin' (freeing a slave) as evidence that mutlaq indicates an individual, because only an individual can be freed.
The word 'raqabah' is absolute (mutlaq).
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The commentator argues that the individual reference comes from the verb 'tahrir' (freeing), an external qarinah, not from the word 'raqabah' itself. The verb necessitates a concrete object, but the absolute word itself only indicates the meaning (the genus 'slave').
External qarinah: a contextual indicator outside the word.
4
Understand the principle
In Hanafi usul, mutlaq indicates the meaning (mafhum) primarily; specification to individuals (afrad) occurs only via external contextual indicators (qarinah kharijiyyah).
This is a foundational Hanafi rule.
5
Apply the principle
When encountering a mutlaq text, first understand its general meaning. Any restriction to a specific individual or subset must be justified by an external qarinah.
Example: The verse 'wa anfusakum' (Q. 2:184) is mutlaq; it means 'your souls' generally, but contextual qarinah specifies it to those who are present.

His saying 'idha uriyda': From here the commentator is making a question upon the author. Question: The author had said that ism jins and sifat are not free from two states: either its meaning is intended or its individual. From this it is understood that the indication of the absolute (mutlaq) is only upon the meaning, not upon the individual. This is incorrect. Because the indication of the absolute is upon the meaning as it is upon the individual. The proof is the saying of Allah Almighty: 'tahriru raqabatin' (freeing a slave). In this, 'raqabah' is absolute. Now the meaning of 'raqabah' is not freed; rather the individual is freed. Answer: The original indication of the absolute is upon the meaning, and in the noble verse, the indication upon the individual is due to an external contextual indicator (qarinah kharijiyyah), and that indicator is 'tahrir' (freeing), because the meaning cannot be freed; only an individual can be freed.

Commentary
This passage presents a usul debate about the indication of absolute (mutlaq). The objector claims that mutlaq indicates both meaning (mafhum) and individual (fard) equally, citing the Quranic verse 'tahriru raqabatin' (Q. 4:92, 5:89, 58:3) where the absolute 'raqabah' apparently refers to an individual slave. The Hanafi position, defended by the commentator, is that mutlaq primarily indicates the meaning (the genus or essence), not any particular individual. The indication of an individual in the verse comes from an external contextual indicator (qarinah kharijiyyah), specifically the verb 'tahrir' (freeing), because only a concrete individual can be freed, not a mere meaning. This preserves the principle that absolute (mutlaq) remains general in its essential indication, and specification to individuals requires an external factor. This is a key Hanafi usul principle distinguishing the essential dalaalah of mutlaq from its contextual application.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A word that indicates its meaning without any restriction (qayd).
قرينہ خارجيہ (qarinah kharijiyyah): An external contextual indicator that specifies the mutlaq to a particular individual.
