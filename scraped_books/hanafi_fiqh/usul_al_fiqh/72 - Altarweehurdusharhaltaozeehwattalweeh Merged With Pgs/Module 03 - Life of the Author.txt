The Importance of Usul al-Fiqh Commentaries: Al-Tarwih as a Case Study
Two scholars praise a new Urdu commentary on a difficult Usul text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why classical Usul al-Fiqh texts like Al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih require commentaries, and identify the key features of a beneficial commentary as exemplified by Al-Tarwih.

Core Idea
A good commentary unlocks the latent meaning and application of a dense classical text, making it accessible without oversimplifying.

This lesson introduces you to the scholarly context behind a new Urdu commentary on a foundational Usul al-Fiqh work. You will understand why even experts need commentaries, and what makes a commentary truly helpful for students of the Hanafi tradition. The passage comes from two taqariz (praise notes) written by scholars endorsing Maulana Muhammad Luqman's 'Al-Tarwih li Hall al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core texts
Recognise that Al-Tawdih (by Sadr al-Shariah) and Al-Talwih (by Taftazani) are central but extremely difficult works of Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh. They have been part of the Dars Nizami curriculum for centuries.
2
Understand the difficulty
The passage states the texts are 'extremely intricate and abstruse' (ادق و اغمض) and require an expert teacher. Note that Taftazani's commentary itself includes critique (جرح و تعدیل), adding another layer of complexity.
3
Recognise the gap
Despite previous Urdu commentaries, the need for a new, detailed, and accessible explanation existed. The author's work fills that gap by including diacritical marks, lexical research, and clarification of the commentator's intent.
The principle 'Knowledge is not with one but with all' (ليس العلم عند احد بل العلم عند الجمیع) justifies the addition of yet another commentary.
4
Identify the commentary's features
Al-Tarwih is described as a 'beautiful bouquet' containing benefits, points, investigations, verifications, and so on. It aims to make the text easy for students while preserving scholarly depth.

This passage consists of two taqariz (commendatory notes) written by respected scholars, Hazrat Maulana Irshad Ahmad and Hazrat Maulana Abdul Sattar, praising the new Urdu commentary 'Al-Tarwih li Hall al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih' by Maulana Muhammad Luqman. Both scholars highlight the importance and difficulty of the classical Usul al-Fiqh text Al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih (by Sadr al-Shariah and Allamah Taftazani), which has been a standard part of the Dars Nizami curriculum but is notoriously intricate. They explain that Maulana Luqman's commentary fills a critical gap by providing a detailed, accessible Urdu explanation that includes linguistic analysis, diacritical marks, and clarification of the commentator's intentions. The first note emphasizes the need for an expert teacher to understand the text and praises the author for making it comprehensible. The second note elaborates on the significance of Usul al-Fiqh and the long-standing difficulty students face, asserting that this new commentary is a beautiful and invaluable resource for scholars and students alike. Both conclude with prayers for the acceptance and completion of the work.

Biographical details of a Hanafi usul authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the key biographical details of al-Bazdawi and explain the significance of his memory feat in the context of Hanafi usul scholarship.

Core Idea
Al-Bazdawi's memory feat proves the superiority of memorizing legal details over mere Quran memorization in scholarly debate.

Al-Bazdawi (d. 482 AH) is the author of Kashf, a foundational text in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. His biography reveals not only his scholarly lineage but also his extraordinary memory, which he used to defend the Hanafi method against a Shafiʻi opponent. Understanding his life helps us appreciate the intellectual environment that produced this key work.

In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Al-Tarwih li Hall al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih. His title: Burhan al-Din. Allama Suyuti mentioned his name as Mas'ud and his father's name as 'Umar, and this is well-known. Hafiz Ibn Hajar said his name is Mahmud, and Mulla 'Ali Qari said his name is 'Umar and his father's name is Mas'ud. Birth: He was born in the month of Safar 722 AH in Taftazan, which is a city in the province of Khurasan. Nawab Siddiq Hasan Khan attributed him to Nasa', and also reported that someone asked him, 'Are you from Nasa'?' He replied, 'Men are from women.' It is written about Nasa' that it had 12,000 flowing springs, and near the Khanqah of Abu 'Ali al-Daqqaq there are four thousand saints buried, so Nasa' is called Sham-i Khurd. Early state: Some have narrated that he was very dull-witted initially, indeed in the study circle of 'Adud al-Din there was no one more unintelligent than him, but he was foremost in effort, striving, and studying books. One night he saw in a dream an unfamiliar person telling him, 'Come, let us go for recreation.' I said, 'I was not created for recreation; despite extensive study, I cannot understand books. If I go for recreation, what will become of me?' He left upon hearing this, and after a while came again. Similarly, after the third coming and going, he said, 'The Prophet (peace be upon him) is remembering you.' I got up in panic and walked barefoot. Outside the city there was a place with some trees. When I reached there, the Prophet (peace be upon him) was sitting with a group of his Companions. Seeing me, He said in a tone of reproach, 'We called you repeatedly, and you did not come.' I said, 'O Messenger, I did not know that you were remembering me.' Then I complained of my difficulty in worship. He said, 'Open your mouth.' I opened my mouth, and He placed his blessed saliva into my mouth, and after supplication said, 'Go.' After waking up, when he attended the session of 'Adud al-Din and the lesson began, during the lesson he raised several objections which his companions thought were meaningless, but the teacher was amazed and said, 'Sa'd, today you are not the same as before.' Acquisition of knowledge: He benefited from various virtuous teachers and shuyukh, including 'Adud al-Din and Qutb al-Din Razi, etc. After completing his education, while still in the prime of youth, he began to be counted among the senior scholars. Allama Kafawi said, 'The eyes have not seen a scholar like him.' His students: Renowned students who benefited from him include: 'Abd al-Wasi' ibn Khidr, Shaykh Shams al-Din Muhammad ibn Ahmad Abu al-Hasan Burman al-Din Haydar. The personality of Allama Taftazani in the view of scholars: Ibn Nujaym al-Misri said: 'The leadership of the Hanafi school ended with him in his time.' Scholars have written that knowledge in the East ended with him. Allama Kafawi said: 'He was among the beauties of the time; the eyes have not seen his like among the prominent figures.' Allama Taftazani (may Allah have mercy on him) was most fortunate; his equal is not found among the great scholars. His ability and breadth of knowledge can be gauged from the fact that even his opponent Mir Sayyid Sharif al-Jurjani used to benefit from his books and take advantage of his ability. Thus, Allama Maraghi in his history of outstanding figures in eloquence said about Sayyid Sharif: 'At the beginning of composition and during authorship, he would dive into the oceans of his (i.e., Taftazani's) investigations and pick pearls from his specifications, and acknowledge the loftiness of his station, the grandeur of his rank, and the height of his status.' However, later due to a debate between the two, animosity arose, so each would strive to refute and falsify the other's statements. Allama Ibn Khaldun (may Allah have mercy on him) said in his Muqaddimah: 'I saw in Egypt the books of Allama Taftazani, which bear witness that he had a firm mastery in the science of kalam, usul al-fiqh, and rhetoric, and in them are investigations that indicate he had knowledge of the philosophical sciences and a high standing in all arts.' Thus he said: 'I came across in Egypt multiple compositions of a man well-known as Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani that bear witness that he has a firm mastery in the science of kalam, usul al-fiqh, and rhetoric, and in them is what indicates he has knowledge of the philosophical sciences and high standing in all arts.' Greatness of rank: One day, Amir Timur sent his messenger on an errand and gave him general permission to mount any horse he needed when necessary. The messenger needed a mount at a certain place. Incidentally, Allama Taftazani was encamped there, and his horses were tied in front of the tent. The messenger went there and without hesitation untied a horse. The praised Allama was inside his tent at that time. When he learned of the incident, he became very angry and had the royal messenger beaten and expelled. When the messenger returned to court, he complained about the Allama. One can easily imagine the state of Amir Timur upon hearing this story—great agitation.

Al-Tarwih li-Hall al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih. Ahmad Abu al-Hasan Burhan al-Din Haydar. The personality of Allamah Taftazani in the view of scholars. Ibn Nujaym al-Misri says: The leadership of the Hanafi school ended with him; in his time the leadership of the Hanafi school concluded with him. Scholars have written that knowledge ended with him in [؟] the east and west. Allamah Kafawi says: He was among the beauties of the time; eyes have not seen his like among the notable and leading figures. Allamah Taftazani (may mercy be upon him) was distinguished of his age; his like is not found among great scholars. His ability and breadth of knowledge can be estimated from the fact that even a rival like Mir Sayyid Sharif Jurjani used to benefit from his books and take advantage of his ability. Thus Allamah Maraghi, in the history of 'Ilm al-Balaghah, says about Sayyid Sharif: 'He used to, at the beginning of authorship and during composition, dive into the seas of his (i.e., Taftazani's) investigations and pick pearls from his refinements, and acknowledge the loftiness of his status, the grandeur of his rank, and the elevation of his position.' But later, due to a debate between the two, enmity developed between them, causing each to engage in refuting and disparaging the other's statements. Allamah Ibn Khaldun (may mercy be upon him) says in the Muqaddimah: 'I saw in Egypt several writings of a man known as Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani, which testify that he has a firmly-rooted proficiency in the sciences of Kalam, Usul al-Fiqh, and Bayan. In their midst are indications that he has knowledge of the philosophical sciences and a high standing in all arts.' He says: 'I came across in Egypt numerous writings of a man known as Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani, testifying that he has a firmly-rooted proficiency in the science of Kalam, Usul al-Fiqh, and Bayan. In their midst are indications that he has knowledge of the philosophical sciences and a high standing in all other arts.' Glory of status: One day, Amir Timur sent his messenger on some errand and gave him general permission that, when needed, he may mount anyone's horse. The messenger needed a mount at one place; coincidentally, Allamah Taftazani was pitched there and his horses were tied at the front of the tent. The messenger went there and immediately untied a horse without hesitation. The praised Allamah was in his tent; when informed of this incident, he became very angry and had the royal messenger beaten and thrown out. When the messenger returned to court, he complained about the Allamah. One can easily imagine how angry Amir Timur must have become at this incident. Due to rage, he remained silent for a while, then said: 'If King Shah Rukh had done this, he would certainly have been punished; but I cannot do anything to such a priest whose pen has conquered every city and land before my sword.' Death: A debate took place between Allamah Jurjani and Allamah Taftazani in Timur's court. A Mu'tazili named Nu'man was appointed as judge. Mir Sayyid Sharif was eloquent of tongue, and Taftazani had a slight stammer. Also, Allamah Nu'man al-Mu'tazili was, for some reason, displeased with Allamah Taftazani (may mercy be upon him). Therefore, the decision was given in favor of Mir Sayyid Sharif. This incident greatly distressed Allamah Taftazani, first because the respected Allamah was quite renowned among the common and elite, and it was popularly known that he was the greatest scholar in academic taste; second, because Mir Sahib's access to Timur's court was only due to Allamah Taftazani. In any case, his distress increased and he became bedridden. Treatment was sought but it was utterly ineffective, until on Muharram 22, 792 AH, on Monday, he passed away in Samarkand and was buried there. Later, on Jumada al-Ula 9 (Wednesday), he was transferred to the area of Sarakhs. Works: His famous works are: 1. Sharh Tasrif Zanjani; 2. Mutawwal (Sharh Talkhis); 3. Mukhtasar al-Ma'ani; 4. Sa'diyya (Sharh Hammiyya); 5. Sharh Aqa'id al-Nasafi; 6. Hashiya Sharh Mukhtasar al-Usul; 7. al-Irshad; 8. Tahdhib al-Mantiq wa al-Kalam; 9. Talwih – this rare book was written in the land of Turkestan in Dhu al-Qa'dah 758 AH; 10. Miftah al-Fiqh [؟] 11. Sharh Talkhis al-Jami' al-Kabir, completed in 786 AH in Sarakhs; 12. Began writing al-Fatawa al-Hanafiyya on Sunday, 9 Dhu al-Qa'dah 769 AH. Madhhab: There is disagreement about his madhhab. Mulla 'Ali al-Qari (may mercy be upon him) mentioned him in Tabaqat al-Hanafiyya. The author of Bahr al-Ra'iq, Allamah Ibn Nujaym al-Misri, and Sayyid Ahmad al-Hadi (may mercy be upon him) also wrote him as a Hanafi. For example, Allamah Ibn Nujaym says: 'The leadership of the Hanafi school ended with him in his time, to the extent that he was appointed judge of [؟] al-'Ilmiyya.' This is a strong proof of his being Hanafi. His being Hanafi is also supported by the fact that he took interest in writing works on Hanafi fiqh. Thus, Allamah Saruji (may mercy be upon him) wrote a commentary on Hidaya; Allamah Taftazani completed it. Also, he wrote a commentary on Talkhis al-Jami' al-Kabir and authored a book on Hanafi fatawa. But a group says he is Shafi'i; thus, the author of Kashf al-Zunun, Haji Khalifa Chalpi, Imam Suyuti (may mercy be upon him), and Allamah Kafawi considered him a Shafi'i.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical account of Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani, a prominent scholar whose works are central to the field of Usul al-Fiqh, particularly his 'Talwih' which is a supercommentary on Sadr al-Shari'ah's 'Tawdih'. The author begins by citing scholars to establish Taftazani's immense stature in the Hanafi school. Ibn Nujaym states that leadership of the Hanafi school ended with him, signifying his final authority. Allamah Kafawi praises him as unique among his contemporaries. Interestingly, even his opponent Mir Sayyid Sharif Jurjani initially benefited from Taftazani's works, indicating his scholarly dominance. However, a later debate caused rivalry. Ibn Khaldun's testimony confirms Taftazani's mastery in Kalam, Usul al-Fiqh, and Bayan, as well as philosophy. The anecdote involving Amir Timur illustrates Taftazani's boldness and Timur's respect for him: Timur refused to punish Taftazani for assaulting his messenger, declaring that Taftazani's pen had conquered territories before his sword. The death narrative recounts a debate in Timur's court where Taftazani lost due to bias (the judge was a Mu'tazili and Taftazani had a stammer), leading to his fatal depression. His works list includes 'Talwih', which he wrote in 758 AH, and later works on Hanafi fiqh. The madhhab dispute is critical: while many Hanafi authorities (Mulla Ali Qari, Ibn Nujaym) count him as Hanafi, others (Haji Khalifa, Suyuti) consider him Shafi'i. The text provides evidence for both, such as his appointment as 'qadi al-'ilmiyya' and his Hanafi writings. This disagreement matters because Hanafi usul relies heavily on Taftazani's 'Talwih'. If he were Shafi'i, his usul methodology (e.g., on the definition of 'amm') might not be authoritative for Hanafis. However, the Hanafi tradition has overwhelmingly adopted his usul works as part of its canon, effectively treating him as a Hanafi.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of Allama Mas'ud ibn Umar al-Taftazani (d. 792/1390), a major Hanafi theologian and linguist, against two accusations: (1) that he held Shi'a views on imamate, and (2) that he showed disrespect to the Companion Mu'awiya ibn Abi Sufyan. Thus, Taftazani's orthodoxy is clear.

(2) Some scholars believe the example was inserted (mudraj) into the text by a Rafidi to discredit Taftazani, as it is absent from his longer work al-Mutawwal. The author acknowledges that even if unintentional, the example was inappropriate and beneath Taftazani's stature.

Knowledge of Tawhid: Definition and Scope
Clarifying what 'knowledge of tawhid and attributes' means

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the nuanced definition of 'knowledge of tawhid' in Hanafi usul and distinguish between iman and 'ilm al-kalam.

Core Idea
Knowledge of tawhid is not faith itself but the theological science that produces firm rooted belief.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies a phrase from the matn: 'knowledge of tawhid and attributes'. At first glance, it might seem to mean simply having faith. But a logical analysis shows that cannot be correct. Instead, the commentator defines it as 'ilm al-kalam, the speculative theology that fortifies belief. This distinction is crucial for understanding the role of kalam in the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term under analysis
Locate the phrase 'knowledge of tawhid and attributes' (علم التوحید والصفات) in the matn. This is the term being defined.
The term appears in the context of the foundations of fiqh.
2
State the two possible meanings presented
The commentator raises two possibilities: either it means faith (iman) or the science of kalam. Write them down.
These are the literal interpretations before deeper analysis.
3
Explain why each literal meaning is problematic
If it means iman, then the foundation and that which is founded upon become identical. If it means kalam, then belief is already included in kalam, again causing circularity. Understand the logical issues.
The objection is that the term cannot simply be synonymous with iman or kalam without further refinement.
4
Understand the commentator’s resolution
The commentator resolves by distinguishing between absolute belief and firm belief (rusūkh). Absolute belief is included in kalam, but firmness (rusūkh) is not; rather, rusūkh depends on studying kalam. Thus, 'knowledge of tawhid' means the study that produces firm belief.
This is the key move: kalam provides the argumentation that makes belief firm.
5
Recognize the Hanafi position and its significance
The Hanafi school holds that kalam is necessary for achieving rusūkh, and therefore 'knowledge of tawhid' refers to 'ilm al-kalam. This justifies the study of theology within usul al-fiqh.
Contrast with schools that reject kalam.

Ta'rid allows respectful critique; early scholars are superior to later ones in originality.

This passage from a commentary on al-Tawdih reveals the author's subtle criticism of Fakhr al-Islam's earlier usul work. The commentator clarifies the author's intention, defends the early scholars' rank, and explains linguistic techniques such as hazf wa al-iyal. 'Zahir al-Burhan' means overwhelming proof and superior argument. 'Markuz' means buried, derived from 'rakaztu al-rumh' (I planted the spear in the ground). 'Kunuz' means buried treasures. 'Manar' means large rocks. Art of hazf wa al-iyal (ellipsis and connection). 'Shabih bihi 'ibaratu' is an intervening sentence; the commentator praises the author for comparing the difficult phrases of Fakhr al-Islam's Usul to large rocks. The similarity is that just as extracting pearls and jewels from rocks is difficult, so is understanding meanings from the phrases of usul al-fiqh. 'Marmuz' is derived from 'ramz', meaning to signal with lips or eyebrows. The commentator says that 'ramz' requires the preposition 'ila', but sometimes the preposition is dropped and the governed noun becomes the agent of the gerund. In this sentence, the commentator explains that the author made a ta'rid (indirect criticism) against Fakhr al-Islam. Ta'rid means to hint at a fault. That is, in al-Tanqih he hinted that Fakhr al-Islam's Usul had some redundancies that should have been removed; and in 'tanzim' he pointed out that the contents of Fakhr al-Islam's book were scattered and needed arrangement, so he arranged them; and 'haultu ta'yin muradahu' he indicated that the book was difficult and he made it easy; and 'ala qawa'id al-ma'qul' he indicated that usul al-fiqh was not based on logical rules, such that definitions and proofs observe the conditions mentioned in the science of logic, due to which one definition does not apply to another category. Early scholars are superior to later ones: 'Mimma lam yaltafit ilayhi al-masha'ikh' – In this sentence the commentator removes a doubt: that 'Allamah Fakhr al-Islam is among the shaykhs, and the shaykhs are concerned with meanings, not with verbal embellishment. Therefore one should not imagine that the later scholars' rank is higher than the early ones. Rather, the early scholars have greater merit because they extracted jewels from the ocean of Quran and Hadith; the later scholars' job is only to arrange them. His statement 'murad fihi': In this sentence only one thing is explained: that the pronoun 'fīhi' refers to al-Tanqih, and all pronouns after it also refer to al-Tanqih. This explanation was needed because all previous pronouns referred to the book of usul al-fiqh, so one might mistakenly think that 'fīhi' also refers to usul al-fiqh. That doubt is removed, and it is clarified that the referent is not usul al-fiqh but rather al-Tanqih.

Commentary
This passage is from a commentary on al-Tawdih, where the commentator (Sharih) explains the author's (Matan) praise of his own work al-Tanqih and his indirect criticism (ta'rid) of Fakhr al-Islam's earlier work on usul al-fiqh. The commentator begins with lexical analysis of key terms: 'nazar' (deep reflection), 'lamh' (cursory glance), 'tanqih' (refinement, like pruning branches), and 'tanzim' (stringing pearls). He then explains that the author's speech is intentionally critical: Fakhr al-Islam's book contained redundancies (which should have been removed), disorganized material (needing rearrangement), and lacked logical rigor in definitions and proofs. The commentator defends early scholars (mutaqaddimun) like Fakhr al-Islam, asserting their superiority over later scholars (muta'akhkhirun) because they derived original meanings from Quran and Hadith. The later scholars merely organize and refine. The commentator also clarifies a grammatical point about the pronoun 'fīhi' (in it), stating it refers to al-Tanqih, not the subject of usul al-fiqh, to avoid confusion. This passage is biographical in that it discusses the scholarly hierarchy and the commentator's view of his own work relative to earlier masters.

Key Terms
تعریض (ta'rid): Indirect criticism or hint at a fault; the author uses this to point out shortcomings in Fakhr al-Islam's work without explicit blame.
حذف و الایصال (hazf wa al-iyal): A linguistic technique where a preposition is dropped and its object becomes the direct agent of a verb or gerund.
تنقیح (tanqih): Refinement or pruning; specifically, cutting off excess branches from a tree. Used metaphorically for improving a scholarly work by removing redundancies.
نظر (nazar): Deep contemplation or reflection, as opposed to a fleeting glance (lamh).
لمح (lamh): A quick, sidelong glance; superficial looking.
