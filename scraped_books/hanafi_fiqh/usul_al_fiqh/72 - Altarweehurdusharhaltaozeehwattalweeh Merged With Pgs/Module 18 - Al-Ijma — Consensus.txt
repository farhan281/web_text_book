Compound Consensus (Ijma' Murakkab) and Its Restriction on Later Ijtihad
Explaining that a second mujtahid must know prior rulings to avoid violating compound ijma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule of compound consensus (ijma' murakkab) and explain why it restricts a later mujtahid from issuing a new opinion outside the implicitly agreed range.

Core Idea
When mujtahids agree on a set of opinions, later ijtihad is bound within that set — compound consensus closes the door to new possibilities.

This lesson unpacks a key Hanafi usul principle: compound consensus (ijma' murakkab). The passage explains that when multiple mujtahids in the same era have issued various opinions on a single issue, their collective action creates an implicit agreement that only those opinions are valid. A later mujtahid must be aware of this range and cannot introduce a completely new ruling, as doing so would violate the consensus. Understanding this rule helps you see how the Hanafi school preserves stability while accommodating legitimate ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key actors
The passage mentions two groups: the earlier mujtahids who expressed opinions on an issue, and the later (second) mujtahid who wishes to give a new ruling. Identify their roles in the rule.
2
Understand how compound ijma forms
Multiple mujtahids in one time period perform ijtihad on the same issue and arrive at a set of different opinions. Their collective action, though not unanimous on a single ruling, constitutes a practical agreement that only these opinions are possible.
This is not ijma' on one opinion but ijma' on a range.
3
State the rule for the later mujtahid
The later mujtahid cannot give a new opinion that falls outside the range of opinions already expressed. Doing so would break the compound consensus.
The passage says: 'if he gives a new opinion, it would be against their compound consensus.'
4
Explain the rationale
The earlier mujtahids have implicitly agreed that the issue admits only those opinions. Any new opinion would imply that the earlier mujtahids missed a possibility, which is contrary to the integrity of their ijtihad.
This principle prevents fragmentation and maintains the authority of early scholarship.

Distinguishing Tashkik from Shuhrah in Legal Definitions
Objection on including ijma' in fiqh definition for Prophet's era

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tashkik and shuhrah in the context of legal definitions and apply this distinction to evaluate the validity of a definition's conditions.

Core Idea
A condition in a definition can be based on common knowledge (shuhrah), not doubt (tashkik), if its absence in certain contexts is well-known.

In this passage, we encounter a critical objection to the Hanafi definition of fiqh. The objector argues that because ijma' did not occur during the Prophet's time, the definition fails to cover the Companions' fiqh. The response clarifies that this is not a case of introducing doubt (tashkik) into the definition, but rather a recognition of a well-known fact (shuhrah). Understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating the soundness of legal definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector claims that the condition of 'occurrence of ijma'' in the definition of fiqh makes it inapplicable to the Prophet's era, thus excluding the Companions' fiqh from the definition.
2
Understand the nature of the objection
The objector treats this as tashkik (doubt/uncertainty) — an ambiguous condition that would make the definition invalid.
3
Apply the Hanafi response
The response reclassifies the condition as shuhrah (common knowledge): it is a well-known fact that ijma' did not occur during the Prophet's lifetime, so the condition is understood contextually.
Shuhrah does not introduce doubt; it acknowledges an obvious limitation.
4
Evaluate the outcome
The definition remains valid. The Companions' fiqh is still covered because the condition applies only after the Prophet's era, which is a known exception.
This shows that definitions can include context-dependent conditions without becoming ambiguous.

Musawwiba vs Ghair Musawwiba: Zann Ghalib and Certainty in Qiyas
Explaining the two schools on ijtihad and how zann ghalib leads to certainty in qiyasi issues

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the musawwiba and ghair musawwiba positions on ijtihad and evaluate how zann ghalib renders qiyasi rulings qat'i according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, zann ghalib (preponderant conjecture) is elevated to certainty in practical rulings, making qiyasi issues qat'i after ijtihad.

This passage explores a foundational dispute in usul al-fiqh: whether every mujtahid is correct (musawwiba) or only one (ghair musawwiba). The commentator argues that regardless of the position, both sides agree that zann ghalib leads to certainty, thus qiyasi issues become qat'i. This has direct implications for the nature of fiqh itself—whether it is a body of certain knowledge ('ilm) or merely conjecture.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two schools on ijtihad
Recognize that scholars divide into musawwiba (every mujtahid is correct) and ghair musawwiba (only one mujtahid is correct).
These are the two major positions in usul regarding the correctness of ijtihad.
2
Understand the role of zann ghalib
Note that ijma and ahad narrations (whose meaning is tawatur) establish that zann ghalib leads to certainty in practical matters.
Zann ghalib is not mere speculation but a strong preponderant belief endorsed by shari'a.
3
Follow the musawwiba argument
Trace how the musawwiba conclude that qiyasi issues, after a mujtahid attains zann ghalib, are established in Allah's knowledge and thus qat'i.
This is based on the principle that shari'a treats zann ghalib as equivalent to certainty.
4
Examine the ghair musawwiba alternative
See that the ghair musawwiba also accept zann ghalib as binding (obligatory to act upon) and likewise conclude that qiyasi issues become qat'i.
Both schools converge on the final ruling despite different reasoning.
5
Analyze the objection and response
Understand the objection that zanni cannot be qat'i and the response distinguishing the time before and after ijtihad/istinbat.
The syllogism (shakl awwal) formalizes the argument that qiyasi issues become qat'i after deduction.

Defending Qiyas: The Taqrir Objection
Responding to objections against qiyas and ijma as legal proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the objection that qiyas requires taqrir (confirmation) in the branch and explain the Hanafi response distinguishing between taqrir in re and taqrir in knowledge.

Core Idea
Qiyas does not fail because the mujtahid may err; only taqrir in knowledge is needed.

This passage addresses the fourth objection against qiyas: that it transfers a ruling from specific to general and requires taqrir (establishment) in the branch. The Hanafi response dissects the meaning of taqrir to show the objection is not fatal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The opponent claims that qiyas transfers a ruling from a specific to a general application, and such a transfer requires confirmation (taqrir) in the branch case; without it, the transfer is invalid.
The objection is based on the idea that any transfer needs the new case to be settled.
2
Understand the two meanings of taqrir
The Hanafi response asks: what is meant by taqrir? Two possibilities: taqrir in reality (fi nafs al-amr) or taqrir in our knowledge (fi ilmna).
This distinction is crucial for the rebuttal.
3
Analyze the consequence of each meaning
If taqrir in reality is meant, it is not necessary because the mujtahid may err; the ruling might not be actually established yet the qiyas can still be attempted. If taqrir in our knowledge is meant, it does not make qiyas an absolute source (asl mutlaq), because an absolute source would establish the ruling in reality.
The argument does not require qiyas to be infallible.
4
Conclude the defense
In either interpretation, the objection does not invalidate qiyas. The Hanafi position stands: qiyas is a valid tool of legal reasoning even if it does not produce absolute certainty.
The focus is on the validity of the method, not the certainty of outcome.

Ijma as a Source: Certainty and Independence
Responding to objections against qiyas and ijma as legal proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three Hanafi arguments for why ijma is a valid proof even without an explicit textual basis, and contrast ijma's certainty with qiyas's speculative nature.

Core Idea
Ijma can derive rulings without a textual proof and transforms speculative rulings into certain ones.

This passage defends ijma against the objection that it needs a textual proof (sanad). Three responses are given: first, ijma may not need a proof at all; second, even if it does, the need is only for its own existence, not for being a source for other rulings; third, ijma increases certainty (from speculative to definite), while qiyas does the opposite. This distinguishes ijma from qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection against ijma
The opponent claims that ijma needs a proof (sanad) to be valid, presumably because it is used as a source for rulings. The text responds to this claim.
The objection questions the authority of consensus independent of textual evidence.
2
First response: ijma may not need a proof at all
The text suggests that Allah may directly inspire a ruling in the minds of the scholars, leading to consensus without any prior textual evidence from Quran, Sunnah, or a Companion's saying. Such ijma would be valid without a sanad.
This is a strong position asserting the possible independence of ijma.
3
Second response (concessive): even if a proof is needed, it is only for ijma's own existence
If we concede that ijma requires a proof, the need is of two types: (a) for its own realization (existence), and (b) for its role as a source for other rulings. In ijma, only the first type applies; ijma itself does not need a proof when it is used to derive other rulings. This contrasts with qiyas, which always requires the three principles (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma).
This shows that ijma is a self-sufficient source for deriving further rulings.
4
Third response: ijma increases certainty, qiyas decreases it
Before ijma, a ruling may be based on a single Companion's saying, which is speculative (zanni). After ijma, it becomes certain (qat'i). In contrast, qiyas takes a certain ruling from a text and makes it speculative when extended to new cases. Thus, ijma and qiyas have opposite effects on certainty, making them incommensurable.
This highlights the unique authority of ijma as a source that can elevate rulings to certainty.
5
Conclude: analogy between ijma and qiyas is invalid
Because of the differing effects on certainty, analogizing (qiyas) the need for a proof from ijma to qiyas is fallacious (qiyas ma'a al-fariq). The text emphasizes that ijma is a perfect source (asl kamil) while qiyas is not.
The final point reinforces that objections to one do not transfer to the other.

Ijma' Murakkab: Binding Nature of Earlier Scholarly Range
Rule on the scope of late ijtihad under composite consensus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of ijma' murakkab to determine when a later mujtahid may or may not introduce a new opinion.

Core Idea
Multiple prior opinions on an issue create a binding range; a later mujtahid may not exit it.

The source passage explains a crucial rule in Hanafi usul: when several mujtahids from an earlier era have expressed opinions on an issue, their collective positions form a composite consensus (ijma' murakkab). A later mujtahid must stay within that range, only being able to prefer an existing view. However, if no earlier ijtihad exists, or only one, then a new opinion is permitted. This prevents contradicting a tacit consensus while allowing flexibility in unexplored areas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario for ijma' murakkab
The source specifies: multiple mujtahids of one era have given opinions on an issue. This creates a range of valid opinions.
The range includes all the opinions expressed, not just one uniform ruling.
2
Apply the restriction
A later mujtahid cannot add a new opinion outside that range. He can only choose one of the existing opinions.
Choosing an existing opinion is called tarjih (preference).
3
Identify exceptions
If no ijtihad preceded, or only one mujtahid opined, free ijtihad is allowed, even opposing the single earlier view.
The exception prevents stagnation in areas with no established scholarly tradition.
4
Consider the rationale
The restriction avoids violation of consensus (ijma'). The exception allows for growth where there is no established consensus.
Ijma' murakkab is considered a weak consensus, but still binding to maintain coherence.

Subject Matter of Usul al-Fiqh: Proofs and Rulings
Dispute on subject matter of usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on the subject matter of usul al-fiqh and evaluate al-Amidi's counter-argument.

Core Idea
Usul al-fiqh studies both the proofs and the rulings, not just the proofs.

What exactly does usul al-fiqh study? Is it only the sources of law (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) or also the legal rulings (wajib, mandub, etc.) themselves? This passage presents a key dispute between the Hanafi majority and the Shafi'i scholar al-Amidi. Understanding this debate clarifies the scope and purpose of usul al-fiqh as a discipline.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
Recognize that the dispute is about the mawdu' (subject matter) of usul al-fiqh: does it include only the adilla (proofs) or also the ahkam (rulings)?
2
State al-Amidi's position
Al-Amidi (a Shafi'i) argued that usul al-fiqh deals only with the four proofs. Rulings need only be conceived (tasawwur), not discussed in detail.
He believed detailed study of rulings belongs to fiqh, not usul.
3
State the Hanafi majority position
The majority (jumhur), including Hanafis, hold that both proofs and rulings are integral subjects. Rulings are five kinds with detailed conditions that must be studied.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on comprehensive coverage.
4
Analyze the Hanafi argument
The Hanafi response: some conditions of rulings refer to proofs, others to rulings themselves. Dividing them arbitrarily into 'main' and 'subsidiary' is unjustified. Proofs are more frequent, but rulings are not mere appendages.
This logical argument supports including ahkam.
5
Determine the practical consequence
In Hanafi usul works, chapters on ahkam (like al-wujub, al-nadb) are fully developed. In Shafi'i works by al-Amidi, they are often shorter or omitted.
This thamarah al-khilaf shows the curricular impact.
