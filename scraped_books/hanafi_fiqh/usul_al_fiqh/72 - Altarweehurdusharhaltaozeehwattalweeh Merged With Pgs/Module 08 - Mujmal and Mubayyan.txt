Three Generalities and One Specificity in 'Al-Hamdulillah'
Parsing 'al-hamdulillah' for usul principles of umum and khusus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of generality (umum) in the phrase 'al-hamdulillah' and apply the Hanafi usul principle that nominal sentences indicate permanence.

Core Idea
A single phrase can reveal multiple layers of generality through its grammatical structure.

The phrase 'al-hamdulillah' is not merely a statement of praise. Hanafi usul scholars have analyzed it to uncover three distinct generalities: the all-encompassing nature of the definite article, the timelessness of a nominal sentence, and the lack of specification of the praiser. Additionally, one specificity arises from the lam in 'Allah'. This lesson will guide you through this parsing exercise, sharpening your ability to extract usul principles from everyday expressions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first generality (umum)
Look for the alif-lam (definite article) in 'al-hamd'. According to the passage, it is for istighraq (all-inclusiveness). This indicates that every individual instance of praise is for Allah.
Hanafi usul: 'al' for istighraq makes the word 'amm covering all members.
2
Identify the second generality
Note that the phrase is a nominal sentence (jumlah ismiyyah). A nominal sentence is not restricted to any specific time, implying that praise is for Allah at all times.
Hanafi principle: nominal sentences signify permanence (thubut), verbal sentences signify renewal (tajaddud).
3
Identify the third generality
Observe that the author (the phrase itself) does not mention the subject (fa'il) of praise. The omission means the praiser is not specified, so every praiser's praise is for Allah.
This is a generality of the agent.
4
Identify the specificity (khusus)
Look at the lam in 'Allah'. It is understood as lam al-ikhtisas (exclusive attribution), indicating that praise belongs exclusively to Allah.
This specificity restricts the attribution to Allah alone.

Definition of Kitab: Words and Meanings, Not Just Writings
Clarifying that 'kitab' denotes words and meanings, not just physical writings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the literal and figurative meanings of 'kitab' and explain the relationship between indicator (dal) and indicated (madlul) in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Kitab denotes words and meanings, not just physical ink; writing is indicator, meaning is indicated.

We often think of 'kitab' as a physical book or text. But in legal usage, especially for the Qur'an as al-Kitab, the term refers primarily to the words and their meanings. This lesson unpacks a subtle usul argument: how physical writings (nuqush) can stand figuratively for the content they carry, using the relationship of dal (indicator) and madlul (indicated).

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Read the objector's claim: from the commentator's phrase, it seems 'kitab' means physical writings (nuqush), but that contradicts the true meaning of words and meanings. What exactly is the objection?
The objector takes the literal meaning as the only possible meaning.
2
Locate the answer's key move
The answer invokes 'majaz' (figurative usage). It says that by 'nuquush' the words and meanings are intended figuratively. Why is this permissible?
See the phrase: 'مجاز نقوش سے الفاظ و معانی مراد ہیں'.
3
Analyse the linguistic mechanism
The reason given is that writings are dal (indicators) and words are madlul (indicated). Also words are dal for meanings. Using the indicator to mean the indicated (or vice versa) is common in Arabic speech. This is the relationship (qarinah) that legitimizes the majaz.
This follows the Hanafi usul principle that figurative usage is valid when there is a clear relationship.
4
Connect to usul al-fiqh
Why is this distinction important? In legal texts, especially the Qur'an, the term 'kitab' appears frequently. Understanding it as words and meanings (not paper) affects how we derive rulings. For example, the Qur'an is called 'al-Kitab' even though it is primarily oral revelation.
This is a key point in understanding the nature of the Qur'an as divine speech.
5
Apply to similar terms
Can this reasoning extend to other terms like 'qawl' (speech) or 'kalam'? Consider how 'qawl' can refer to the content of speech, not just the utterance. Try to identify similar dal-madlul relationships.
This trains the student to recognize figurative usage in other legal terms.

Why Hamd is Chosen as Bayan Over Shukr
Rationale for bayan selection via munasaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the criterion of munasaba (appropriateness) between a mujmal and its mubayyin, and apply it to evaluate alternative bayan choices.

Core Idea
Hamd is chosen as bayan for kalim because both are tongue-specific; shukr fails the munasaba test.

In this passage, the commentator addresses an implicit question: why did the author of the matn select 'hamd' as the bayan (clarifier) for 'al-kalim al-tayyib' rather than 'shukr'? The answer lies in the principle of munasaba — the necessity of precise correspondence between the mujmal and its mubayyin. By analyzing the defining property of each term, we uncover a core usuli principle: the bayan must share the same modality as the mujmal. This is a classic example of rational (aqli) derivation in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mujmal and the proposed mubayyin
Recognize that al-kalim al-tayyib is the mujmal (ambiguous/unclear) term, and the author has chosen hamd as its mubayyin. Note the implicit alternative: shukr.
The passage assumes the reader knows the earlier context where hamd is declared the bayan.
2
Isolate the defining property of the mujmal
The commentator clarifies that kalim is specifically 'what the human utters with the tongue.' Its scope is limited to verbal expression.
This definition excludes actions of the heart and limbs.
3
Check the property of each candidate bayan
Hamd is also restricted to speech (tongue). Shukr, however, can be performed by heart, tongue, and limbs. Thus, shukr has a broader scope than kalim.
Munasaba requires matching specificity.
4
Apply the principle of munasaba
Because hamd shares the exact same modality (tongue) as kalim, it has munasaba. Shukr lacks this because it extends beyond speech.
The bayan must be appropriate to the mujmal in terms of scope and quality.
5
Conclude the author's choice
Therefore, hamd is the correct bayan; any other choice (like shukr) would violate the requirement of munasaba and cause ambiguity.
This demonstrates a careful, rational selection based on linguistic precision.

Arrangement of the Four Sources: The Precedence of Sunnah Over Ijma
Ordering sources and addressing a dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school places the text of the Sunnah (matn al-sunnah) before Ijma and identify the condition (zann) that may delay it.

Core Idea
The text of the Sunnah absolutely precedes Ijma; only uncertainty in transmission delays it.

In this passage, the commentator addresses a key question in usul al-fiqh: the order of the four sources of Shariah. While the sequence is Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas, some might object that a zanni (uncertain) hadith should not outrank the consensus of the community. The commentator clarifies that the discussion is specifically about the text of the Sunnah (matn al-sunnah), which is qat'i in transmission. The delay of a zanni narration is due to an external obstacle, not a deficiency in the text itself. This lesson breaks down the reasoning and the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four sources mentioned
Recognize the sequence: Kitab, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas. The commentator states they are the pillars of rulings.
The order is not arbitrary but reflects the epistemological priority.
2
Locate the objection
Find the anticipated question: why is Sunnah placed before Ijma if it is zanni?
The objection is implicit in the commentator's clarification.
3
Understand the clarification
The commentator restricts 'Sunnah' to 'matn al-sunnah' (the certain text). Only when there is 'arid al-zann' (obstacle of uncertainty) is the Sunnah delayed.
Arid al-zann refers to weakness in the chain of transmission.
4
Apply the rule
In practice, a Hanafi jurist prioritizes an authentic hadith over an existing ijma, unless the hadith is weak in transmission.
This has real impact on fatwa and ijtihad.

Real definitions can be perfect or imperfect, essential or accidental—only hadd tamm captures the full essence.

In Hanafi usul, defining terms accurately is crucial for legal reasoning. The Role of Haythiyya in Defining the Subject of a Science
Debating whether haythiyya is part of subject or a qayd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the role of haythiyya in defining a science's subject and distinguish when it functions as a part (juz') versus a qualification (qayd).

Core Idea
Haythiyya is the universal aspect under which a subject's accidents are investigated, not an accident itself.

In Islamic epistemology, every science is defined by its subject-matter and the aspects (haythiyya) under which it investigates that subject's essential accidents. This lesson unpacks the nuanced debate over whether haythiyya is part of the subject or a mere qualification, and why the correct understanding avoids logical problems like tautology and subject-sharing.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion on the role of 'haythiyya' (aspect or consideration) in defining the subject-matter of a science, a key topic in Islamic epistemology and logic as applied to usul al-fiqh. The author (presumably the writer of the matn) examines two scenarios: when haythiyya acts as a mediator (wasita) and when it does not. In the first case, when haythiyya is a mediator, it becomes part of the subject, so the science does not discuss the hayth itself but the accidents that arise through it. In the second case, the science discusses the hayth itself. An example is given: in theology (ilm al-ilahi), the hayth of existence is part of the subject, so discussing it leads to tautology; but in physics, motion and rest themselves are discussed. The commentator (sharh) then explains that the matn author distinguishes between two types: if hayth is not an essential accident, it is a part; if it is, it is a species of essential accidents. An objector (mu'tarid) proposes making hayth a qayd (qualification) instead of a part, which avoids two problems: (1) the need to discuss the subject's parts when hayth itself is discussed, and (2) the issue of two sciences sharing the same subject. For the second problem, if hayth is a qayd, then each science has a distinct qualified subject. The commentator then questions the real difference between part and qayd, suggesting it is merely terminological. Then the famous objection (al-ishkal al-mashhur) is presented: haythiyya cannot be among the investigated accidents because it would cause circularity (thing preceding itself). The standard answer is that the hayth refers to the possibility or capacity (imkan/isti'dad), not the actual accident. The verification (tahqiq) concludes that the subject is qualified by haythiyya as a universal consideration, not meaning that every accident attaches through that specific hayth. This is a subtle logical analysis relevant to the foundations of fiqh sciences where the subject matters (like the acts of worship or transactions) are defined under specific aspects.

The Quran Cannot Be Defined by a Formal Limit (Hadd)
Argues the Quran is a unique entity defying definition by limit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that the Quran cannot be defined by a formal limit (hadd) and distinguish between definition of essence (ta'rif al-mahiyyah) and identification (tashkhis).

Core Idea
The Quran's uniqueness as a singular, non-repeatable entity (shakhsi) makes it impossible to define by composition of parts.

In this passage, the author addresses a fundamental question: can the Quran be defined like anything else? The answer has profound implications for how we understand the term 'Quran' and its parts. Let's follow the reasoning to see why a formal definition leads to circularity and how the Hanafis resolve it by distinguishing between defining the essence and simply identifying the object.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central claim
The author asserts that the Quran, being a unique individual (shakhsi), cannot be defined by a limit (hadd) because a limit requires enumerating parts, but a unique entity has no common parts.
Note that 'shakhsi' means an individual that is not a member of a genus.
2
Understand the circularity problem
If you define 'surah' as a part of the Quran, and then define the Quran as consisting of surahs, you have a circular definition (dawr). This arises when trying to define the essence (mahiyyah).
Circularity makes the definition invalid.
3
Distinguish two approaches
The passage contrasts ta'rif al-mahiyyah (definition of the essence) with tashkhis (identification). Tashkhis avoids circularity by pointing to a conventional instance, like using 'mushaf' to refer to the Quran without defining its essence.
Identification uses a conventional label, not a logical definition.
4
Apply the Hanafi resolution
The author suggests using 'surah' as a customary identifier (like 'mushaf'), not as part of an essential definition. This allows us to refer to surahs without circularity.
This is the key to avoiding the problem.
5
Conclude
The Quran can only be identified, not defined by a formal limit. This conclusion defines the Hanafi position and limits the role of definition in usul al-fiqh.
This conclusion has implications for how we approach other unique terms in Islamic law.

Distinguishing Nazm and Lafz in Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh
Clarifying whether nazm and lafz are synonymous and defining related terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define lafz and nazm, explain their differences using the conditions of mutarrabat al-ma'ani and mutanasikat al-dalalat, and apply this distinction to the ruling on reciting Quranic meaning in prayer.

Core Idea
Nazm is ordered, context-aware composition; lafz is any utterance — they are not synonymous.

This passage from a commentary on Al-Tawdih wa Al-Talwih addresses a common confusion: are the terms 'lafz' and 'nazm' synonyms? By dissecting their linguistic and terminological meanings, the author clarifies that nazm specifically involves a deliberate arrangement of words that respects both the order of meanings and the mode of indication required by the context. This distinction is not merely academic; it has direct implications for the validity of reciting the Quran's meaning in prayer, as permitted under certain conditions.

Guided Path
1
Define lafz and nazm linguistically and terminologically
Start by extracting the definitions from the passage: lafz literally means 'throwing' and is any utterance; nazm literally means 'stringing pearls' and refers to ordered speech. Terminologically, lafz is any spoken word, while nazm has two definitions: the act of arranging words with specific properties, or the arranged words themselves.
Note the distinction: lafz includes any utterance, even disordered; nazm requires order and contextual suitability.
2
Identify the two conditions for nazm: mutarrabat al-ma'ani and mutanasikat al-dalalat
The passage specifies that for speech to be nazm, the words must be 'mutarrabat al-ma'ani' (ordered according to the mind's requirement of what should come first/last) and 'mutanasikat al-dalalat' (the mode of indication matches the context: explicit or implied). These conditions ensure the arrangement is meaningful and rhetorically appropriate.
These conditions apply to the Quran and classical poetry; mere lafz lacks this structure.
3
Analyze the example 'qafā bi-ka min dhikrā ḥabīb wa manzil' vs its altered order
The passage uses a line of poetry. When the order is correct, it is nazm. When altered (e.g., 'bik qafā min ḥabīb dhikrā wa manzilī'), the words are still lafz but not nazm because they lose the proper ordering and contextual suitability. This clarifies the boundary between the two terms.
The example demonstrates that mere utterance (lafz) can become nazm only when arranged correctly.
4
Apply the distinction to the ruling on reciting Quranic meaning in Persian in prayer
The passage discusses a practical usul issue: can one recite the meaning of the Quran in Persian during prayer? The evidence is the verse 'faqra'ū mā tayassara min al-Qur'ān' (recite what is easy of the Quran). The Hanafi position allows it under conditions (e.g., the person is not an innovator, speaks one or two words that are unambiguous), based on the principle that the meaning is what matters, not the exact nazm. This shows that the definition of nazm is not absolute; it can be dropped when needed, similar to the rukhsa (permission) in prayer.
This ruling highlights the practical weight of the nazm/lafz distinction: the nazm of the Quran is normally required, but in special circumstances the meaning suffices.
5
Synthesize the Hanafi position and its usul foundation
Summarize that lafz and nazm are not synonyms. Nazm is a specialized subset of lafz with ordering and contextual conditions. The Hanafi use of nazm in the matn to refer to lafz is metaphorical, to show respect. The definition of nazm has direct implications for interpreting Quranic recitation requirements and for understanding the structure of revealed speech.
This distinction underpins many usul discussions about the Quran's inimitability (i'jaz) and the permissibility of translation in ibadat.

Key Terms
لفظ (lafẓ): That which is uttered by the human being, whether it conforms to the context or not, and whether it is a single word or multiple.
نظم (nazm): Either (1) the arrangement of words that are mutarrabat al-ma'ani and mutanasikat al-dalalat according to contextual requirements, or (2) the arranged words themselves that meet these criteria.
الفاظ مترتبة المعانی (alfāẓ mutarrabat al-ma'ānī): Words whose order corresponds to the order of meanings as required by reason: the word indicating a meaning that should be first comes first, and that which should be last comes last.
الفاظ متناسقة الدلالات (alfāẓ mutanasikat al-dalālāt): Words whose modes of indication (dalāla) match the contextual requirements: if the context demands explicit (muṭābaqī) indication, the word uses that; if it demands implied (iltizāmī), the word uses that.

Ordering of the Four Divisions of Lafz in Hanafi Usul
Explains the order and rationale of the four divisions of lafz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four divisions of lafz in the correct logical sequence according to the Hanafi usul and explain the rationale behind the alternative ordering proposed by Fakhr al-Islam.

Core Idea
The four divisions of lafz follow a logical order: positing, usage, clarity, and indication—but Fakhr al-Islam prioritizes clarity over usage.

In Hanafi usul, the word (lafz) is classified under four aspects: its initial positing (wad'), its usage in that meaning (isti'mal), the clarity or obscurity of its meaning (zuhur wa khafa'), and its mode of indication (dalalah). The sequence of these divisions is not arbitrary; it reflects a logical progression. The Matn presents one order, while Fakhr al-Islam offers a different sequence based on whether one prioritizes disposition in the word or in the meaning. This lesson unpacks these two orders and their justifications.

Guided Path
1
Name the four divisions
Recall the four categories: wad' (positing), isti'mal (usage), zuhur wa khafa' (clarity and obscurity), and dalalah (indication).
These are standard in Hanafi usul.
2
Identify the Matn's order
State the order as wad' → isti'mal → zuhur/khafa' → dalalah. Understand the rationale: usage follows positing, then clarity/obscurity, then the manner of indication.
The word 'thumma' in the Matn indicates sequential order.
3
Contrast with Fakhr al-Islam's order
State his order: wad' → zuhur/khafa' → isti'mal → dalalah. Explain his premise: disposition in the word (related to wad' and zuhur) precedes disposition in meaning (isti'mal).
Fakhr al-Islam prioritizes clarity of the word before its usage.
4
Understand the premise
Distinguish between tasarruf fi al-lafz (disposition in the word) and tasarruf fi al-ma'na (disposition in meaning). Recognize that wad' is the foundation, and zuhur/khafa' is tied to tasarruf fi al-lafz.
This premise is key to Fakhr al-Islam's reasoning.
5
Evaluate the difference
Note that both orders agree wad' is first and dalalah is last; the disagreement is in the middle two. Consider practical implications for analyzing texts.
The Matn's order may be more intuitive; Fakhr al-Islam's emphasizes understanding word clarity before usage.

The Principle of Tabayun in Classifications
Objection and two-part resolution on classification overlap

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of mutual exclusivity (tabayun) within a single division and distinguish between different divisions to resolve apparent overlaps in classification.

Core Idea
Types within one division must be mutually exclusive; different divisions can coexist.

When studying classifications (taqsimat) of Islamic legal terms, you might encounter categories that seem to overlap. For example, a word can be both 'specific' (khass) and 'explicit' (sarih). This raises the question: does the rule of mutual exclusivity (tabayun) require that types be completely non-overlapping? The commentator addresses this objection by clarifying that tabayun is required only within a single division, and even within a division, different aspects (haythiyyat) can resolve apparent conflicts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Recognize the scenario: a word is classified as khass and also as sarih; these two types seem to combine, violating the assumption of mutual exclusivity.
The objection assumes all divisions are one.
2
Understand the rule of tabayun
Tabayun means that no single instance can fall under two types of the same division. This is a standard usul principle.
The rule applies per division, not across divisions.
3
Apply the first answer
Show that khass and sarih belong to different divisions (e.g., division of delal vs division of wuduh). Therefore, they can overlap without violating tabayun.
Analogous to a word being mu'rab and ma'rifa from different grammatical divisions.
4
Apply the second answer
Even if considered one division, the overlap is resolved by considering different aspects (haythiyya). A word is 'aam in one aspect and mushtarak in another, like 'ayn being general for sight and homonymous for multiple meanings.
Aspects prevent real contradiction.
5
Conclude the correct application
The rule of tabayun remains valid; apparent conflicts arise from mixing divisions or ignoring aspects.
Always check whether types belong to the same division.

If it is said: This is the sixth part of the commentary. The purpose is to mention a question and answer it. The question: Is it necessary for the types of every division to be mutually exclusive (not applying to each other), whereas here the types from the division can be combined together? For example, a word is specific (khass) and also explicit (sarih); these two types are combined. So mutual exclusivity is not realized? Answer (1): The rule that mutual exclusivity is necessary applies to a single division. Here, within each individual division, the types do not overlap. For example, in the first division, what is specific is not general, what is general is not shared (mushtarak) or reinterpreted (mu'awwal). However, types from different divisions can overlap. This is like a word being mu'rab and mabni according to one division, and ma'rifa and nakira according to another; so mu'rab can be ma'rifa or nakira, and ma'rifa or nakira can be mu'rab or mabni. But types of the same division and the same basis cannot overlap, e.g., a single word cannot be both mu'rab and mabni, or both ma'rifa and nakira. Answer (2): If we accept that this rule applies to all divisions, then a qualification (qayd) of aspect (haythiyya) is considered: a word is general from the aspect that its application is for one meaning shared by many individuals, and it is shared (mushtarak) from the aspect that it is coined for multiple meanings. For example, the word 'ayn is general from the aspect that it applies to all instances of the faculty of sight, and shared from the aspect that it applies to multiple lexically designated objects.

Commentary
The commentator presents an objection: In the earlier classification, types like 'specific' and 'explicit' seem to overlap. The first answer clarifies that mutual exclusivity is required only within a single division (taqsīm wāhid). The four divisions given earlier are separate; thus within the first division (e.g., khass vs 'aam), they are mutually exclusive. But a word can be both khass (from one division) and sarih (from another division). This is analogous to the grammatical divisions: a word can be mu'rab and ma'rifa simultaneously because they belong to different classifications. The second answer even if we consider all divisions as one, the overlap is resolved by considering different aspects (haythiyyat): a word is 'aam in one respect and mushtarak in another, as explained with the example of 'ayn.

Key Terms
تباین (tabayun): The condition that types within a single division must be mutually exclusive, i.e., no single instance can belong to two types simultaneously.
حیثیت (haythiyya): The aspect or perspective under which a thing is considered, allowing a word to fall under different categories in different respects.
تقسیم (taqsim): A division or classification of a category into mutually exclusive types under a single principle.

Definition of Mu'awwal in Hanafi Usul
Defining mu'awwal as a division of nazm and addressing objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi definition of mu'awwal, explain the role of ghalib al-ra'y, and distinguish between mu'awwal and mufassar.

Core Idea
Mu'awwal is a mushtarak word given precedence by predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y), not by definitive proof.

In Hanafi usul, texts are classified by clarity. One category is mu'awwal (interpreted), which arises when a mushtarak (homonymous) word is given one meaning based on predominant opinion. This passage defines mu'awwal, addresses objections that it may apply more broadly, and clarifies that the definition is specific to mushtarak as a division of nazm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four divisions of nazm
The passage states that Fakhr al-Islam and others divided nazm (textual expression) into four: khas, amm, mushtarak, and mu'awwal.
2
Understand the definition of mu'awwal
Mu'awwal is defined as a mushtarak word whose meaning is given precedence by ghalib al-ra'y (predominant opinion).
The interpretation must be based on strong assumption, not certainty.
3
Recognize the two objections
(1) Mu'awwal may not always be from mushtarak; (2) The precedence may not always be by ghalib al-ra'y.
These objections are raised in the passage.
4
Analyze the responses
The author responds that the definition is specific to mu'awwal from mushtarak, and ghalib al-ra'y includes any predominant zann, whether from khabar al-wahid, qiyas, or reflection on wording.
Example: three quru'.
5
Distinguish mu'awwal from mufassar
If ambiguity is removed by a definitive proof (qat'i), it becomes mufassar; if by speculative proof, it is mu'awwal.
This clarifies the boundary.

His statement 'Then the mushtarak': Fakhr al-Islam and others mentioned that the divisions of nazm in terms of wording and language are four: the specific, the general, the homonym, and the interpreted. And he interpreted the interpreted as that from the homonym, some of its aspects are given precedence by predominant opinion. And it was objected that the interpreted may not be from the homonym, and its precedence may not be by predominant opinion, as mentioned in al-Mizan: that the mujmal, mushkil, khafi, and mushtarak when they are clarified by a decisive proof are called mufassar, and when their ambiguity is removed by a proof that contains doubt, like khabar al-wahid or qiyas, they are called mu'awwal. And he answered the first that the intended meaning is not the definition of absolute mu'awwal, but the mu'awwal from the mushtarak, because it is among the divisions of nazm in wording and language. And about the second: that predominant opinion means the predominant assumption, whether it results from khabar al-wahid, qiyas, or reflection on the wording, as in the case of three menstrual periods (quru'). And the meaning of it being among the divisions of nazm in wording and language is that the ruling after interpretation is attributed to the wording. And it was said: the intended by predominant opinion is reflection and ijtihad on the wording itself. And he restricted it to homonym and the precedence by ijtihad and reflection on the wording itself to verify that it is among the divisions of nazm in wording and language. For the mushtarak is placed for multiple meanings, each of which is possible alternatively. So when it is taken to one of them by looking at the wording, i.e., the placed word, it does not exit from the divisions of nazm in wording and language, i.e., placement. Unlike when it is taken to it by a decisive proof, for then it is interpretation (tafsir) not ta'wil; or by qiyas or khabar al-wahid, for then it is not, in this consideration, among the divisions of nazm in wording and language. Likewise if it is not mushtarak but khafi, mujmal, or mushkil, and its ambiguity is removed by decisive or speculative proof.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion on the definition of 'mu'awwal' (interpreted) as one of the four divisions of textual expression (nazm) in Hanafi usul: khas, amm, mushtarak, and mu'awwal. The author, Fakhr al-Islam and others, define mu'awwal as a mushtarak (homonym) that is given precedence to one of its possible meanings by ghalib al-ra'y (predominant opinion). Two objections are raised: (1) mu'awwal may not always be from mushtarak, and (2) the predominance may not be by ghalib al-ra'y as defined. The author responds: (1) The definition is specific to mu'awwal from mushtarak, because only that is a division of nazm in terms of wording and language. (2) Ghalib al-ra'y means predominant zann (opinion), whether from khabar al-wahid, qiyas, or reflection on the wording itself (like three quru'). He also explains that if the meaning is determined by a definitive proof (qat'i), it is called tafsir (exegesis), not ta'wil. If by a speculative proof (zanni), it is ta'wil. The passage clarifies the Hanafi position that mu'awwal is a distinct category that involves interpretation based on predominant opinion, not definitive evidence.

Key Terms
مشترك (mushtarak): A word that is placed for multiple meanings, each possible alternatively.
مؤول (mu'awwal): That which is given precedence to one of its possible meanings from mushtarak by predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y).
غالب الرأي (ghalib al-ra'y): Predominant assumption or strong guess, whether from khabar al-wahid, qiyas, or reflection on the wording.
مفسر (mufassar): A word whose ambiguity is removed by a decisive proof (qat'i).
نظم (nazm): Textual expression or composition.
