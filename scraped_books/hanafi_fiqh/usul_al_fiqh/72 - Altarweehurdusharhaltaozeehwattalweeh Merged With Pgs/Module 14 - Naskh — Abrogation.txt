Pronoun Before Antecedent in Hanafi Usul
Explaining the permissibility of pronoun before antecedent using Quranic evidence and mental presence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that permits pronoun before antecedent when the referent is mentally present (madhkūr fī al-dhihn) and justify its use in textual analysis.

Core Idea
Pronoun before antecedent is allowed when the referent is mentally present — a key Hanafi usul principle.

In the opening of Al-Tawdih, the author uses the pronoun 'إليه' before explicitly mentioning Allah. This violates the normal Arabic grammatical rule that the antecedent should come first. The commentator defends this by invoking a Hanafi usul exception: when the referent is already present in the mind, the pronoun is permissible. This lesson unpacks that justification and shows how it operates in legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the usul issue
The issue is 'al-iḍmār qabl al-dhikr' (pronoun before antecedent). Normally, this is impermissible. The commentator must show why it is allowed here.
The pronoun is the suffix 'hi' in 'ilayhi'.
2
Recognize the general rule and exception
The general rule is that the antecedent must be mentioned before the pronoun. The exception is when the referent is mentally present (madhkūr fī al-dhihn). Understand the basis for this exception in Hanafi usul.
This exception is not arbitrary; it is derived from the Quranic verse itself.
3
Analyze the three justifications
The commentator gives three reasons why Allah is mentally present: (1) His remembrance is in every believer's heart, especially when starting a book; (2) The verse itself praises Allah, so He is already in focus; (3) The student of Islamic sciences intends only Allah. Each supports the exception.
All three converge on the same idea: the referent is mentally present.
4
Apply the principle to the text
Now apply the exception to the phrase 'إليه يصعد الكلم الطيب'. Show that since Allah is mentally present, the pronoun is valid. This explains the author's stylistic choice.
This is a practical application of the usul principle.
5
Evaluate the linguistic argument about 'kalim'
The commentator also discusses that 'kalim' is singular but used collectively, allowing masculine adjective. This shows the thoroughness of the defense.
This is secondary but reinforces the overall argument.

Commentary
This passage is an extended commentary on the opening of the matn 'Al-Tawdīḥ' by Ṣadr al-Sharīʿa, specifically on the phrase 'إليه يصعد الكلم الطيب' (to Him ascends good speech). The commentator (Sharīḥ) addresses a technical usul al-fiqh issue: the permissibility of using a pronoun (الضمير) before its antecedent (المرجع) is explicitly mentioned in the text. This is known as 'الإضمار قبل الذكر'. In Arabic grammar, the general rule is that the antecedent must be mentioned before the pronoun, but there are exceptions when the referent is mentally present.

The passage first explains why the author chose this opening: it indicates that Allah's remembrance is ever-present in a believer's heart, especially when beginning an Islamic work. Thus, there is no need to mention 'Allah' explicitly before the pronoun. The author also provides three justifications for this usage:
1) The remembrance of Allah is present in the heart of every believer, especially at the start of a book, so the pronoun refers to the mentally present (مذكور في الذهن).
2) The phrase itself is praise of Allah, and Allah's essence is determined as the object of all praise, so the mind is already focused on Him.
3) The student of Islamic sciences has his aim only towards Allah, so Allah's presence is in his mind and the pronoun naturally refers to Him.

The commentator also discusses the linguistic status of the word 'كلم' (speech), arguing that it is singular in form but often used to denote the collective (plural meaning). He cites Qur'anic verses to show that the word can take masculine or feminine adjectives. This is to support that 'الكلم الطيب' takes a masculine adjective 'الطيب' because it is treated as a collective singular.

Finally, the passage identifies the 'iqtibās' (Qur'anic borrowing) in the author's phrase: the author skillfully weaves the Qur'anic verse 'إليه يصعد الكلم الطيب' into his own text, making it appear as his original words while referencing the Qur'an subtly. This is considered a 'lafīf' (subtle) iqtibas because it carries deeper meanings about the prophet Muhammad's (ﷺ) intercession? No, that is not mentioned. The commentator notes that this opening is unique among books.

The entire passage is a defense of the author's stylistic and legal choice, demonstrating the depth of usul al-fiqh reasoning applied even to textual composition.

Literal vs. Metaphorical Interpretation of 'Su'ud' in Quran
Explaining the metaphorical meaning of su'ud in the Quran

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish literal (haqiqi) from metaphorical (majazi) usage in Quranic interpretation using a rational (aqli) argument, and apply this to avoid attributing spatial location to Allah.

Core Idea
Literal ascent implies direction; metaphorical ascent averts anthropomorphism.

When the Quran says 'to Him ascends good speech' (Quran 35:10), does this mean words physically travel upward? In this lesson, we examine how a Hanafi commentator (sharih) resolves the apparent implication of spatial movement by invoking majaz (metaphor). This is a classic example of how usul al-fiqh protects core theological principles (like Allah's transcendence) when interpreting texts that might otherwise be misunderstood literally.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning of su'ud
The word 'su'ud' lexically means physical movement from a lower place to a higher place. Recognize that this is the haqiqah (literal sense).
2
Recognize the theological problem if taken literally
If su'ud is taken literally, good words would be moving towards Allah's place/direction, which implies Allah has a spatial location. This contradicts the Hanafi creed that Allah is beyond space and direction.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) – the consequence is impossible.
3
Locate the commentator's solution: majaz
The commentator states that su'ud here is not haqiqah but majaz, meaning 'turning towards' (tawajjuh). This removes the anthropomorphic implication while preserving the verse's meaning.
The shift from haqiqi to majazi is a common interpretive tool in Hanafi usul.
4
Understand the rational basis for the interpretation
The argument is rational: if literal meaning leads to an impossibility (ta'til or tashbih), then figurative meaning must be adopted. This exemplifies the Hanafi principle of interpreting ambiguous (mutashabih) texts in line with definitive theological truths.

Takhsis vs Naskh: Why Qiyas Specifies but Does Not Abrogate
Usul principle: legitimacy of ta'lil in takhsis vs naskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that ta'lil is valid for takhsis but not naskh, and determine whether a later specific text is a specifier or abrogator.

Core Idea
Qiyas can specify but not abrogate: specification is clarification, abrogation is opposition requiring equal proof.

This lesson delves into a foundational distinction in Hanafi usul: the difference between takhsis (specification) and naskh (abrogation) regarding the use of qiyas. The author explains that while qiyas can specify a general ruling, it cannot abrogate a text because abrogation requires the opposing proof to be of equal authority, while specification is merely explanatory. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly deriving rulings when later evidence appears to conflict with earlier general texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two operations: takhsis and naskh
Takhsis restricts a general ruling to some individuals; naskh removes a ruling entirely. Note that both can result from a specific text, but the legal effect differs.
2
Understand the nature of each: bayan vs mu'arada
Takhsis is clarification (bayan) of what was always intended; naskh is opposition (mu'arada) between two contradictory texts. This difference underlies the ruling on qiyas.
3
Apply the condition of equality
Mu'arada requires the two proofs to be equal in authority. Qiyas is weaker than text, so it cannot oppose a text. Bayan does not require equality.
4
Determine the ruling for a delayed specific text
If a specific text (khass) comes after a general text (amm) and contradicts it, it is treated as naskh if delayed, not as takhsis.
Timing is key: delayed khass abrogates; contemporaneous khass specifies.
5
Contrast with the opposing view
Some scholars (e.g., Mutakallimin) might argue that qiyas can abrogate. The Hanafi response is that equality is necessary, and qiyas lacks it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rulings on istithna' in sales and identify when an exception invalidates a contract due to ignorance or contradictory conditions.

Core Idea
An exception in a sale that creates ignorance or invalidates a condition initially makes the contract void, but the ruling may become valid subsequently.

The author now moves from the abstract usul principle to concrete subsidiary issues (furu') from sales law. Two examples illustrate how istithna' (exception) operates similarly to takhsis and naskh. In the first, a free man is included with a slave; in the second, an exception is made for one of two slaves. Both sales are initially void, but for different reasons. These examples solidify the usul concepts by showing their practical application.

Guided Path
1
Analyze the first example: selling a free man and a slave for a single price
The free man cannot be sold. The contract includes him, so the sale of the slave becomes a sale by share initially, which is invalid due to ignorance of the price for the slave alone.
This is analogous to istithna' because the free man is excepted from the ruling of sale, but the exception is not real since he was never a valid commodity.
2
Analyze the second example: selling two slaves except one for a share of the price
The exception is real (one slave is excluded). The sale of the remaining slave is initially by share, invalid due to ignorance. However, after the exception, the sale may become valid subsequently, analogous to naskh.
The subsequent validity is akin to naskh: the initial ruling (invalidity) is removed for the remaining sale.
3
Relate the examples to usul: istithna' as analogue of takhsis/naskh
Istithna' prevents the excepted item from entering the ruling of the general statement, similar to takhsis. The subsequent validity after exception mirrors naskh: the removal of the initial invalidity.
4
Identify the conditions for validity of the remaining sale
The sale of the remaining item must not involve a non-commodity (like a free man) and must not create ignorance of price. If the exception removes one item, the price for the other must be known.

تشریح:۔ یہاں سے نسخ کی نظیر بیان کی ہے اور وہ یہ ہے کہ ایک آدمی نے دو غلاموں کو ایک ہزار کے بدلے میں فروخت کیا اور ایک غلام قبل از تسلیم بائع کے پاس مرگیا تو دوسرے زندہ غلام میں بیع بالحصہ منعقد ہو جائے گی یہ مسئلہ نسخ کے مناسب ہے کیونکہ شیخ کا معنی ہے ایک حکم کو ثابت ہونے کے بعد تبدیل کر دینا اور یہاں بھی ایسے ہی ہے۔ بوقت عقد یہ غلام ایجاب کے تحت داخل تھا اور بیچ میں داخل تھا لیکن جب سپرد کرنے سے پہلے بائع کے ہاتھ میں ہی مر گیا تو اس میں بیچ فسخ ہوگئی تو گویا یہ پہلے والے حکم کی تبدیلی ہے اس لیے نسخ کی طرح ہو گیا ۔ تو اب دوسرے غلام میں بیع فاسد نہیں ہوگی بلکہ اس میں بیع منعقد ہو جائے گی باقی یہاں بھی بیع بالحصہ لازم آرہا ہے لیکن یہ بیع بالحصہ چونکہ بقاء ہے اور بقاء بیع بالحصہ جائز ہوتی ہے۔ ہاں ابتداء درست نہیں ہوتی کیونکہ ابتداء کی صورت میں بیچ کے منعقد کرنے کے وقت ثمن میں جہالت لازم آتی ہے جو بیع کے منافی ہے اور یہاں اگر چہ شمن میں جہالت لازم آتی ہے لیکن بوقت عقد نہیں بلکہ بعد میں طاری ہونے والی ہے اور بعد میں طاری ہونے والی جہالت بیع کیلئے مفسد نہیں ہے۔
Commentary: Here the analogy of naskh is mentioned, and it is that a man sold two slaves for one thousand, and one slave died before delivery while in possession of the seller, then the sale on the remaining living slave will be executed on a per-share basis. This matter is suitable for naskh because the meaning of naskh is to change a ruling after it becomes established, and here it is similar. At the time of contract, this slave was included in the offer and included in the sale, but when it died before delivery while in the seller's hand, the sale for it was rescinded. So it is a change of the previous ruling, therefore it is like naskh. So now the sale on the other slave will not be void but will be executed. Here the per-share sale is necessary, but this per-share sale is in continuation (baqa'), and continuation of a per-share sale is permissible. However, initial per-share sale is not valid because at the time of contract, ignorance of the price (jahalah) arises, which contradicts sale. Here, although ignorance of the price arises, it is not at the time of contract but later, and ignorance arising later is not detrimental for the sale.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary explaining the analogy of naskh (abrogation) in fiqh. The author gives a concrete example from sales law: a person sells two slaves for a single price of one thousand. Before delivery, one slave dies in the seller's possession. The contract is affected by a kind of per-share sale (bay' bi'l-hissa). The ruling is that the sale on the living slave is valid. The reason is that at the time of contract, the sale included both slaves. When one dies before delivery, it is as if the original ruling for that slave is abrogated. This is likened to naskh because naskh means changing a ruling after its establishment. The key point is that per-share sale is valid in continuation (baqa') but not initially, because initial per-share sale creates ignorance of the price. Here the ignorance arises after the contract, so it does not invalidate. This illustrates how usul concepts (naskh) have practical fiqh applications.

Key Terms
نَسْخ (naskh): Abrogation of a previous ruling by a later one
بيع بالحصّة (bay' bi'l-hissa): Sale of a share in a divisible object
جهالة (jahalah): Ignorance of the price or subject matter that invalidates a contract if present at contract time
