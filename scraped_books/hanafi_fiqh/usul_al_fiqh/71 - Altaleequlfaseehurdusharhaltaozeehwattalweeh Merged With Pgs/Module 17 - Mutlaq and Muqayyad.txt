Proper and Common Nouns: Derived vs. Non-Derived in Hanafi Usul
Classifies proper nouns and common nouns into derived or non-derived

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given Arabic noun as derived (mushtaqq) or non-derived (jamid) when it is a proper noun or common noun, using the Hanafi usul framework.

Core Idea
A noun's derivation matters in usul: derived nouns carry a verbal relationship; jamid nouns do not.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, words are classified not only by their literal meaning but also by their linguistic structure. A key distinction is between mushtaqq (derived) and jamid (non-derived) nouns. This lesson examines how this applies to proper nouns (aʿlām) and common nouns (asmāʾ al-ajnas), helping you identify when a word's derivation affects its legal interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word type
Determine if the word is a proper noun (ʿalam) or common noun (ism jins).
Proper nouns like Zaid refer to specific entities; common nouns like rajul refer to a generic type.
2
Check for a verbal root
See if the word can be traced back to a verb with a meaningful connection (e.g., maqtal from qatala).
If no clear verbal root exists, the noun is likely jamid.
3
Exclude attributes (ṣifah)
If the word is a ṣifah (e.g., Ḍārib), it is not considered under ism jins mushtaqq even if derived; the author separates ṣifah as a counterpart.
This is a specific Hanafi usul rule.
4
Classify as mushtaqq or jamid
Apply: derived from verb → mushtaqq; otherwise jamid. Incorporate the ṣifah exception.
Example: Hatim = mushtaqq; Zaid = jamid.

Mutlaq Refers to an Unspecified Individual, Not the Essence
Arguing that mutlaq implies an unspecified individual.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that mutlaq indicates an unspecified individual (fard lā ʿalā al-taʿyīn) and distinguish this from the view that it indicates the essence.

Core Idea
Mutlaq in commands indicates an unspecified individual, not the abstract concept.

This passage tackles a foundational dispute in uṣūl: does the absolute term (muṭlaq) refer to the essence or to an unspecified individual? The Qur’anic example 'taḥrīru raqabah' provides the key evidence. Understanding this distinction is crucial for deriving rulings from primary texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
State the opposing views: some say mutlaq indicates the essence (musammā) without restriction; the Hanafi school argues it indicates an unspecified individual.
The passage begins by summarizing the author's view that mutlaq may appear to mean essence, but then corrects it.
2
Examine the Hanafi proof
Find the Qur'anic command 'taḥrīru raqabah' (free a slave). The act of freeing applies to an actual individual slave, not the concept, proving that mutlaq refers to an unspecified individual.
The proof is based on the verb 'taḥrīr' which necessitates an individual action.
3
Analyze the two answers
First answer: In the absence of qarīna (context), mutlaq could indicate essence, but the qarīna of the verb shifts it to an individual. Second answer: According to the theory that ism jins is placed for the scattered individual (fard muntashir), musammā itself is the individual.
Both answers support the Hanafi position, with the second providing a direct link between the term and individual.
4
Distinguish the opposing view
The opposing school holds that mutlaq indicates the essence. The Hanafi response shows that either through qarīna or through the nature of ism jins, mutlaq actually indicates an individual.
This step helps you see the differences in usul methodology between schools.

Mutlaq: Essence or Individual?
Definition of mutlaq with objections and responses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain whether mutlaq denotes the essence or an unspecified individual and apply contextual clues to determine the intended meaning.

Core Idea
Mutlaq primarily denotes the essence; contextual clues (qara'in) specify an individual.

This passage tackles a foundational question in Hanafi usul: when the Lawgiver uses an absolute noun like 'raqabah' (slave), does it refer to the abstract concept of slavery or to an actual individual slave? The answer shapes how we derive rulings from texts. The commentator defends the view that mutlaq primarily denotes the essence, but acknowledges that contextual clues can shift the meaning to an individual.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Matn Claim
The author of the matn states that mutlaq denotes the named essence (misma) without restriction. Understand this as the default position.
2
Recognize the Objection
The commentator notes that the Quranic phrase 'tahrir raqabah' seems to demand an individual slave, not the concept. This suggests mutlaq may denote an individual.
The objection is based on a strong textual proof (dalil naqli).
3
Analyze the First Answer
The commentator replies that mutlaq's primary meaning is the essence, but contextual clues (qara'in) like 'tahrir' (freeing) require an individual. Similar to 'udkhul al-suq' (enter the market).
Without clues, the essence remains intended.
4
Analyze the Second Answer
Some scholars hold that ism jins is coined for the distributed individual. On this view, the mutlaq's essence is itself the distributed individual, so intending the individual is the same as intending the essence.
This answer reconciles the objection without needing qara'in.
5
Apply to Examples
Practice with Quranic and everyday examples to determine when mutlaq denotes essence vs. individual.
E.g., 'raqabah', 'suq'.

Rule of Mutlaq and Muqayyad: When Restriction Applies
Stating rule for mutlaq and muqayyad and exception via istilzam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a mutlaq text becomes restricted by a muqayyad text according to the Hanafi usul rule and apply the principle of istilzam to justify restriction.

Core Idea
A mutlaq is not automatically restricted by a different-ruling muqayyad; restriction occurs only when one ruling logically implies the other.

This lesson clarifies the Hanafi rule regarding mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (restricted) terms that appear together in a text. The default rule is that each remains on its own scope if their rulings differ. However, an exception arises when one ruling logically entails a ruling that requires the other to be restricted—a principle called istilzam. You will learn to distinguish this exception from the general rule and apply it through a concrete example.

Guided Path
1
State the default rule for mutlaq and muqayyad
According to the text, the mutlaq runs on its absoluteness and the muqayyad runs on its restriction. This is the baseline when they appear together but have different rulings.
The default applies unless an exception is triggered.
2
Identify when the exception is triggered
The exception occurs when the two rulings are different but one of them logically implies a ruling that is not explicitly mentioned. This logical implication is called istilzam.
Istilzam is key: the implied ruling must be necessary for the explicit ruling.
3
Analyze the example: freeing a slave
The text gives: 'Free a slave (mutlaq) on my behalf' and 'Do not make me the owner of an unbelieving slave (muqayyad).' The two rulings are different (obligation vs. prohibition), but the prohibition of ownership implies prohibition of freeing an unbelieving slave because freeing requires ownership.
The implication is via istilzam: nafy tamlik entails nafy itiq.
4
Apply istilzam to restrict the mutlaq
Since the negation of ownership entails negation of freeing, the mutlaq 'slave' in the first command becomes restricted to 'believing slave' (i.e., not an unbelieving slave). Thus, the mutlaq is carried on the muqayyad despite different rulings.
The restriction is wajib (obligatory) in this case, per the text.
5
Generalize the principle
This principle applies to any similar case where one ruling logically requires another, even if not explicitly stated. The Hanafi school uses istilzam to justify restriction when other schools would not.
Always check for logical entailment before restricting the mutlaq.

Commentary
This passage states a fundamental usul rule regarding mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (restricted) texts. The default rule is that an absolute term remains on its generality, and a restricted term remains on its restriction. When both appear together in a text, if the rulings (ahkam) are different (e.g., one is obligation, another is prohibition), then the absolute is not restricted by the muqayyad; each applies independently. The exception arises when the two rulings are different but one of them logically implies a ruling not explicitly stated. In that case, the absolute becomes restricted by the muqayyad. The example given: 'Free a slave (absolute) on my behalf' and 'Do not make me the owner of an unbelieving slave (muqayyad).' Here, the absolute command to free a slave does not specify the religion. The prohibition of owning an unbelieving slave implies that freeing an unbelieving slave is also prohibited, because freeing necessarily involves ownership (you cannot free what you do not own). Thus, the absolute 'slave' is restricted to believing slave (i.e., not an unbelieving slave). The key point is the logical implication (istilzam): the negation of tamlik (ownership) entails negation of itiq (freeing) because itiq requires ownership. Hence, the absolute is restricted. The passage emphasizes that this is not a general rule for all cases of mutlaq and muqayyad, but only when such implication exists.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A term that is unrestricted, applies to all instances of its kind without qualification.
مقید (muqayyad): A term qualified by a description that limits its application.
استلزام (istilzam): Logical implication where one ruling necessarily entails another ruling not explicitly mentioned.

Commentary
This passage is from a commentary (sharh) on Al-Tawdih wa Al-Talwih, explaining the usul principle of how mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (restricted) interact. The teacher begins by stating the default rule: mutlaq remains on its absolute scope, and muqayyad remains on its restriction; one is not automatically carried onto the other. However, an exception exists when the two rulings are different but one entails a ruling not explicitly stated. The example given is: someone says 'Free a slave for me' (absolute) and 'Do not make me own a disbelieving slave' (restricted). Since freeing entails ownership, negating ownership of a disbelieving slave implies negating the freeing of that disbelieving slave, so only a believing slave can be freed. This is a case of 'luzum' (entailment) where the mutlaq is necessarily restricted.

Next, when the ruling (hukm) is the same but the incidents (ahdath) differ – e.g., kaffarat al-yamin (oath expiation) and kaffarat al-qatl (killing expiation) – the Hanafi position (Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions) is that mutlaq is not carried to muqayyad. Thus in oath expiation, any slave suffices; in killing expiation, only a believing slave. Imam al-Shafi'i, however, carries the absolute onto the restricted in both cases, making a believing slave required even in oath expiation. Some Shafi'is restrict this to cases where analogy demands.

Finally, when both the ruling and the incident are the same, as in sadaqat al-fitr (zakat al-fitr), the determining factor is whether the mutlaq and muqayyad both enter upon the cause (sabab). The cause for zakat al-fitr is the human head, and two texts exist: one absolute ('every free and slave') and one restricted ('every free and slave from the Muslims'). The passage cuts off here, but the principle is that when both texts relate to the same cause, the muqayyad is interpreted as clarifying the mutlaq, so the restriction applies. This is consistent with the Hanafi principle that if the cause is one, the absolute is carried upon the restricted (a position shared with Shafi'i in this scenario).

الحكم (al-hukm): The legal ruling itself (e.g., obligation of freeing a slave)
السبب (al-sabab): The cause or occasion for the ruling (e.g., the human head for zakat al-fitr)

Haml al-Mutlaq 'ala al-Muqayyad: Conditions and the Shafi'i Dispute
Explaining haml al-mutlaq with conditions and Shafi'i dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when the Hanafi school requires carrying an absolute text to a qualified text and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the criteria of conflict on the same incident and affirmative ruling.

Core Idea
The absolute is only carried to the qualified when they conflict on the same incident and the ruling is affirmative.

This lesson dives into a pivotal usul al-fiqh rule: haml al-mutlaq 'ala al-muqayyad. The Hanafi school does not apply this rule universally; it only applies when there is genuine conflict (ta'arud) between two texts on the same legal incident with an affirmative ruling. Through examples like sadaqat al-fitr and the expiatory fast, you will see how the school distinguishes between different causes and affirmative vs. negative rulings, and how this leads to a practical difference with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of entry
Determine whether the absolute and qualified texts enter upon the cause (sabab) or upon the ruling (hukm) of the same incident.
Entry upon the cause means each text establishes a separate reason for the ruling; entry upon the ruling means both texts govern the same incident.
2
Check the nature of the ruling
Verify whether the ruling is affirmative (muthbit) or negative (manfi). The carrying rule applies only to affirmative rulings.
Negative rulings (e.g., prohibitions) allow scope for avoiding both texts by inaction, so carrying is not needed.
3
Apply to the sadaqat al-fitr example
Consider the two hadiths: one says 'from every freeman' (mutlaq), the other says 'from every freeman and Muslim slave' (muqayyad with 'Muslim'). Since the descriptions differ, they are different causes; thus no conflict and no carrying.
Hanafi: both causes are acted upon independently, so sadaqat al-fitr is due for both Muslim and non-Muslim slaves.

The absolute is carried to the qualified because acting on both is impossible (one permits non-consecutive, the other requires consecutive).

Commentary
This passage discusses the Hanafi usul principle of haml al-mutlaq 'ala al-muqayyad (carrying the absolute to the qualified). The author explains that the rule depends on whether the absolute and qualified enter upon the cause (sabab) or upon the ruling (hukm), and whether the ruling is affirmative or negative. In the example of sadaqat al-fitr, two hadiths: one says 'from every freeman' (absolute), the other says 'from every freeman and Muslim slave' (qualified with 'Muslim'). Since these are different causes (the absolute refers to freemen, the qualified adds the description of Islam for slaves), there is no conflict, so both must be acted upon: charity is given for both Muslim and non-Muslim slaves. Imam Shafi'i disagrees, carrying the absolute to the qualified, thus limiting charity to Muslim slaves. Next, when both texts enter upon the same ruling and the same incident (e.g., fasting three days as expiation, with Ibn Mas'ud's qira'a adding 'consecutive'), there is conflict, and the absolute is carried to the qualified by consensus. Then the author discusses negative rulings: if the ruling prohibits an action, like 'do not free a slave' (absolute) and 'do not free a disbelieving slave' (qualified), the absolute is not carried because one can avoid both by freeing no slave. The Shafi'i argument is that the qualified is 'speaking' and the absolute is 'silent', so the speaking takes precedence. The Hanafi response: that only applies when there is genuine conflict, which is not present here. Finally, the Hanafi proof from Quran and Ibn Abbas: adding qualifications causes hardship and contradicts the Quranic command not to ask about things and to leave ambiguous what Allah left ambiguous.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): An unqualified word that applies to all individuals without restriction.
مقید (muqayyad): A word qualified by a description or condition that restricts its application.
حمل المطلق على المقید (haml al-mutlaq 'ala al-muqayyad): Interpreting the absolute text in light of the qualified text, restricting the absolute to the qualified meaning.
تعارض (ta'arud): Conflict between two textual evidences that cannot be reconciled.
سبب (sabab): Cause or reason that triggers a legal ruling.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Restriction by text is valid; restriction by qiyas is not – the Hanafi dividing line.

In this passage, the author defends the Hanafi usul principle on mutlaq and muqayyad against two objections. The first objection concerns zakat on camels, where Hanafis restrict the absolute 'camels' to 'grazing camels' despite both texts sharing the same cause. The author responds by clarifying that the restrictions are based on explicit texts (nusus), not on qiyas, and thus are fully consistent with the Hanafi position.

1

When mutlaq and muqayyad enter upon the same cause, the mutlaq is not carried on the muqayyad. When they enter upon different incidents, carrying is allowed but must be based on textual proof, not qiyas.
This rule is key to understanding the objections.

The opponent cites the hadith 'In five camels, zakat' (absolute) and 'In five grazing camels, zakat' (restricted). Both share the same cause (nisab), so according to the Hanafi rule, the mutlaq should not be carried on the muqayyad. Yet Hanafis restrict.

The opponent implies inconsistency.

Haqiqi and Majazi Usage of Mushtaq After Zawaal
Explanation of haqiqi and majazi usage of mushtaq after source is removed

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between haqiqi and majazi usage of mushtaq after zawaal of the source, and identify when haqiqah urfiyyah applies.

Core Idea
After the source ceases, a mushtaq is majaz unless customary usage elevates it to haqiqah urfiyyah.

Words change their legal force when the condition they describe disappears. A 'derived noun' (mushtaq) like 'owner' (malik) or 'buyer' (mushtari) is normally used literally only while the source meaning (ownership, buying) is present. Once that source ceases, the word becomes metaphorical—unless everyday custom has turned it into a new reality. This lesson unpacks this usul rule and its surprising effects on legal oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Key Terms
Define mushtaq, mushtaq minhu, haqiqah, majaz lughawi, and haqiqah urfiyyah as they appear in the text.
These terms are the foundation of the discussion.
2
Understand the Default Rule
When the mushtaq minhu (source) is present, the mushtaq is haqiqi. After zawaal (removal) of the source, the usage becomes majaz lughawi.
This is the general principle.
3
Recognize the Exception: Haqiqah Urfiyyah
If customary usage makes the post-zawaal application widespread, the word becomes haqiqah urfiyyah—a type of haqiqah.
Example: 'mushtari' after purchase is completed.
4
Apply to the Slave Example
Compare 'malik' (owner) and 'mushtari' (buyer) when a person sells half a slave then buys the second half. For 'malik', after selling the first half ownership ceases, so calling him 'malik' after buying back is majaz. For 'mushtari', the act of buying triggers customary usage, so it becomes haqiqah urfiyyah.
The legal consequence: emancipation depends on which word is used in the oath.
5
Extract the Legal Consequence
If the oath uses 'in milktu' (if I own), the second half is not freed because 'malik' is majaz. If it uses 'ishtaraytu' (if I buy), the second half is freed because 'mushtari' is haqiqah urfiyyah.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement from a super-commentary on Al-Tawdih wa Al-Talwih, analyzing the validity of intending multiple divorces in various divorce utterances according to Hanafi usul. The author begins by distinguishing two positions (jawaab): the first answer (jawaab awwal) and the second answer (jawaab thaani). Under the second answer, the intended divorce is considered an attribute of the woman, so intending three is invalid because the attribute does not originally admit multiplicity. However, in phrases like 'anti taliq talaqan' or 'anti al-talaq', the intention of three is valid, but this seems contradictory. The resolution is that in 'anti taliq talaqan', the word 'talaq' is interpreted as a verbal noun (masdar) meaning the act of divorcing, so the phrase implies a hidden verb: 'I divorce you three divorces.' Thus the intention specifies the number. In 'anti al-talaq', the word 'al-talaq' is a generic noun (ism al-jins) indicating a single entity, but a conceptual single (wahid i'tibari) like 'the whole as a whole' can represent three divorces. The passage then addresses an objection regarding 'anti ba'in' (you are irrevocably separated). Since baynuna is established by implication (iqtida'), it should also not allow intention of three. The response is that baynuna is a genus with two types: khafifa (revocable) and ghaliza (irrevocable, which is three). Since the implication cannot specify a specific number (due to lack of generality), but it must specify one of the two types because they cannot coexist. Therefore, intending the severe type (which is three) is valid. Finally, the author contrasts baynuna with talaq: baynuna has qualitative types, while talaq has only quantitative differences (one, two, three). Hence the analogy fails. The passage emphasizes that in Hanafi usul, a generic noun (ism jins) indicates a single individual by default, and intending a conceptual whole (like three as a unit) is only valid if the word can bear that meaning, as in 'al-talaq' where the definite article can imply the whole. But in implied contexts (iqtida), only the necessary minimum is established unless there is a type distinction that forces specification.

Key Terms
الطلاق (al-talaq): In this passage, it is analyzed as either an attribute of the woman (sifa) or a verbal noun (masdar) indicating the act of divorcing; also a generic noun (ism jins) indicating a single individual by default.
البينونة (al-baynuna): A state of separation; in this passage, it is treated as a genus with two types: khafifa (revocable) and ghaliza (irrevocable, corresponding to three divorces).
المقتضى (al-muqtada): That which is implied or necessitated by the utterance; its establishment is by necessity (darura), so it has no generality (umum).

The Baynuna Analogy and Implication (Iqtida') in Hanafi Usul
Resolving the baynuna objection through genus/type distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why intending the severe form of baynuna (which is three divorces) is valid despite the establishment of baynuna by implication (iqtida'), and distinguish this from talaq.

Core Idea
Baynuna is a genus with two types; intention of one type is valid in implied contexts, but not a specific number.

This lesson addresses an objection: if baynuna is established by implication (iqtida'), and implication has no generality, then how can intending three divorces (which is a specific number) be valid? The Hanafi response is that baynuna is a genus with two types (khafifa and ghaliza), and the speaker must intend one type, so intending the severe type (which is three) is valid. However, talaq differs because it has only quantitative differences, not qualitative types.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the objection
The objector claims: since baynuna is established by iqtida', and iqtida' has no generality, intending a specific number (three) should be invalid.
This objection parallels the earlier discussion on 'anti taliq'.
2
Understand the Hanafi response
The Hanafi scholars respond by analyzing baynuna as a shared term (mushtarak) or a genus with two types: khafifa (revocable) and ghaliza (irrevocable).
Khafifa corresponds to one or two divorces; ghaliza corresponds to three.
3
Apply the rule of iqtida' to types
In iqtida', only the minimum necessary is implied. But when two types are mutually exclusive, the speaker must intend one of them. Therefore, intending the ghaliza type (which is three) is valid.
This is different from specifying a number because the type inherently corresponds to a number.
4
Distinguish talaq from baynuna
Talaq has only quantitative differences (one, two, three), not qualitative types. Hence, the same reasoning does not apply to talaq in the context of iqtida'.
This distinction is crucial for the overall theory.
5
Conclude on the validity of intending three in baynuna
Therefore, if a husband says 'anti ba'in' intending three divorces, it is valid because he intends the severe type of baynuna, which is three divorces.
The intention is on the type, not the number directly.

When the attribute in the woman is not originally multiple, then intending three is not valid. And the ta'liq (suspension) which is an attribute of the husband, its establishment is by implication (iqtida'), so intending three is also not valid because there is no generality in that which is implied. This is the reason mentioned in al-Hidayah. The first answer includes both 'anti taliq' and 'tallaqtuki', and the second answer is specific to 'anti taliq'. And when he says 'anti taliq talaqan' or 'anti al-talaq', in both intending three is valid. But its explanation according to this second answer is difficult because the second answer is that the divorce which is an attribute of the woman does not admit intention of three, and in 'anti taliq talaqan' there is no doubt that it is the same divorce which is an attribute of the woman, so intending three should not be valid. So we answer that when he intends three, it becomes specified that here 'talaq' means 'tatliq' which is the act of the husband, and it becomes a masdar (verbal noun) with an implied verb, so the phrase becomes: 'You are divorced because I have divorced you three divorces.' And his saying 'anti al-talaq' when he intends three, its meaning becomes: 'You are the one upon whom three divorces have occurred.' But according to the first answer, this objection does not arise because it does not say that the divorce which is an attribute of the woman does not admit intention of three; rather it permits that, and intention of three is valid as long as the word 'talaq' is uttered, even if it is an attribute of the woman. And his saying 'like other generic nouns' means when the word 'talaq' is like the uttered word, but because it is a generic noun and a generic noun is a singular noun that does not indicate number but indicates an individual, whether that individual is real like a real single, or an individual and a conceptual single, like all generic nouns. The rule for all generic nouns is that when they are uttered, they do not indicate number but indicate one, whether that one is real or conceptual, as will come in the chapter. For example, the word 'al-talaq' is an individual noun and it includes a real single, and a conceptual single can also be intended, and the whole as a whole is a conceptual single, and the whole as a whole in divorce is three, so intending it is valid. His saying 'If it is said: The establishment of baynuna etc.' This is an objection to what was said that in 'anti taliq' the masdar which is established from the speaker by way of creation (insha') is a legal matter, not a linguistic one, so its establishment is by implication, therefore intending three is not valid. Similarly, the establishment of baynuna also, so in 'anti ba'in' the establishment of baynuna from the speaker is a legal matter, so intending three should not be valid. We say: Yes, but baynuna... the answer is that we admit that baynuna is established by way of implication, but baynuna is either light (khafifa) or severe (ghaliza) because baynuna is a shared term between light and severe. Light is that which can be revoked, and severe is that which cannot be revoked, and that is three. Or say as a genus: baynuna is a genus, and light and severe are two types under that genus. If baynuna is shared, then intending one of the two possible meanings is valid in the implied. Similarly, intending one of the two types is valid. Because it is necessary that one of the two types is established, and the two types cannot coexist, so the speaker must intend one of them. However, intending a specific number in the implied is not valid because there is no generality in the implied, and the establishment of the implied is by necessity, and a specific number is not necessary. So only the minimum amount is established which is certain. But in the case of two types, there is no certain minimum because types are mutually exclusive, so no minimum or maximum can be certain, unlike 'little' and 'much' where there is no type opposition, so a certain minimum is possible. Therefore, in the case of two types, the speaker's intention of one type is valid; if he intends severe baynuna, then three divorces are intended. And also, intending metaphor (majaz) in the implied is not valid, so in 'anti taliq talaqan' as we mentioned, intending three divorces as a conceptual single is a metaphorical single, and it cannot be intended in the implied. His saying 'and not so is talaq' means we said that the word 'talaq' is not like the word 'baynuna' because the individuals of talaq differ not by type but only by number—one, two, or three—so this is a difference by number, not by type. And it cannot be said that talaq is divided into types. This is a question; the summary is that just as baynuna has two types—one revocable and one irrevocable—similarly talaq also has two types.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement from a super-commentary on Al-Tawdih wa Al-Talwih, analyzing the validity of intending multiple divorces in various divorce utterances according to Hanafi usul. The author begins by distinguishing two positions (jawaab): the first answer (jawaab awwal) and the second answer (jawaab thaani). Under the second answer, the intended divorce is considered an attribute of the woman, so intending three is invalid because the attribute does not originally admit multiplicity. However, in phrases like 'anti taliq talaqan' or 'anti al-talaq', the intention of three is valid, but this seems contradictory. The resolution is that in 'anti taliq talaqan', the word 'talaq' is interpreted as a verbal noun (masdar) meaning the act of divorcing, so the phrase implies a hidden verb: 'I divorce you three divorces.' Thus the intention specifies the number. In 'anti al-talaq', the word 'al-talaq' is a generic noun (ism al-jins) indicating a single entity, but a conceptual single (wahid i'tibari) like 'the whole as a whole' can represent three divorces. The passage then addresses an objection regarding 'anti ba'in' (you are irrevocably separated). Since baynuna is established by implication (iqtida'), it should also not allow intention of three. The response is that baynuna is a genus with two types: khafifa (revocable) and ghaliza (irrevocable, which is three). Since the implication cannot specify a specific number (due to lack of generality), but it must specify one of the two types because they cannot coexist. Therefore, intending the severe type (which is three) is valid. Finally, the author contrasts baynuna with talaq: baynuna has qualitative types, while talaq has only quantitative differences (one, two, three). Hence the analogy fails. The passage emphasizes that in Hanafi usul, a generic noun (ism jins) indicates a single individual by default, and intending a conceptual whole (like three as a unit) is only valid if the word can bear that meaning, as in 'al-talaq' where the definite article can imply the whole. But in implied contexts (iqtida), only the necessary minimum is established unless there is a type distinction that forces specification.

Key Terms
الطلاق (al-talaq): In this passage, it is analyzed as either an attribute of the woman (sifa) or a verbal noun (masdar) indicating the act of divorcing; also a generic noun (ism jins) indicating a single individual by default.
البينونة (al-baynuna): A state of separation; in this passage, it is treated as a genus with two types: khafifa (revocable) and ghaliza (irrevocable, corresponding to three divorces).
المقتضى (al-muqtada): That which is implied or necessitated by the utterance; its establishment is by necessity (darura), so it has no generality (umum).

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi usul, when a term established by iqtida' is a genus with mutually exclusive types, intending one type is valid, even if that type corresponds to a specific number.
