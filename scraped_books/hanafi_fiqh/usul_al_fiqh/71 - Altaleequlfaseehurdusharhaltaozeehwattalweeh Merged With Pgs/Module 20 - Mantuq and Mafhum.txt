The Invalidity of Combining Literal and Metaphorical Meanings from One Word
Explaining the rule that one word cannot simultaneously convey both literal and metaphorical meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a single word cannot intend both its literal (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz) meanings simultaneously, and identify the prevailing meaning in scriptural and juristic texts.

Core Idea
The literal meaning has precedence; one word, one intended meaning.

Words often have both a literal and a metaphorical usage. A key question in usul al-fiqh is whether a single utterance can convey both at once. The Hanafi school firmly holds it cannot. This lesson unpacks the reasoning — tarjih al-haqiqah — and applies it to three concrete fiqh examples, sharpening your ability to interpret legal texts precisely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule and Its Justification
Read the opening statement: 'Cannot intend both literal and metaphorical meanings together.' Note the reason: the literal (haqiqah) is the original (asl) and followed (matbu'), while the metaphorical (majaz) is subordinate (tabi'). The followed takes precedence over the subordinate.
This is a core Hanafi linguistic principle.
2
Analyze the First Branch: Will for Mawali
The word 'mawla' literally means the manumitter (lower mawla) and metaphorically the manumitter's manumitter. Since the literal meaning is intended, the metaphorical is excluded. Hence only the manumitter receives the bequest.
The rule prevents extending the word to cover both.
3
Analyze the Second Branch: Hadd for Khamr
The hadith uses 'khamr' literally for fermented grape wine. Other intoxicants are metaphorical applications. The literal meaning is taken, so the hadith directly applies only to grape wine. (Other intoxicants require separate evidence for hadd.)
This shows how the rule restricts the scope of a textual command.
4
Analyze the Third Branch: Lamastum
The Quranic 'lamastum' is interpreted metaphorically as intercourse, not literally as touch. If both meanings were possible together, the text would be ambiguous. The rule forces one meaning; the context (and other evidence) indicates the metaphorical.
Focus on the impossibility of combining both meanings.
5
Generalize the Principle
The rule applies whenever a word has both literal and metaphorical meanings in a single utterance. Only one can be intended, determined by context and the priority of the literal. This guides interpretation of all legal texts.
Memorize the maxim: al-haqiqah muqaddamah 'ala al-majaz.

Commentary
This passage states a usul rule: a single word cannot simultaneously convey both its literal (haqiqi) and metaphorical (majazi) meanings. The reasoning is that the literal is the original and has precedence; therefore, if one meaning is intended, the other is excluded. Three fiqh examples illustrate this. First, in a will for 'mawali' (clients), the word is literal for the freed slave's manumitter (mawla asfal), not for the manumitter of the manumitter (mawla a'la), which is metaphorical. Since literal is intended, the metaphorical is excluded. Second, in the hadith 'Whoever drinks khamr, flog him,' the word khamr literally means fermented grape wine, so other intoxicants are not included in that ruling via the same word, though they have similar punishments by other evidence. Third, the Quranic phrase 'lamastum al-nisa' says Allah, 'lamastum' is interpreted metaphorically as intercourse, not literally as touch with the hand, because both cannot be intended simultaneously. These are applications of the rule that literal and metaphorical meanings cannot be combined from one word.

Key Terms
حقیقت (haqiqah): The literal, original meaning of a word.
مجاز (majaz): The metaphorical meaning derived from the literal by some connection.

The Letter Fa for Immediate Sequence (Ta'qib)
States the rule of fa for immediate sequence with a Hanafi legal example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that fa indicates immediate sequence to interpret conditional legal statements and determine whether the legal result follows.

Core Idea
Fa signals the result follows immediately - a delay nullifies the condition.

In this lesson, you will learn a key linguistic principle in Hanafi usul: the letter fa does not just indicate sequence, but immediate sequence. This has real consequences for how we understand conditional statements in divorce, contracts, and other legal acts. The passage we examine states the rule and applies it to a divorce case: a man says, 'If you enter this house and this house, then you are divorced.' The ruling depends on whether the second entry happens without delay.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic rule
What does 'al-fa' li al-ta'qib' mean? The passage defines it: 'the governed noun occurs immediately after its predecessor without delay.'
2
Understand the application to conditional sentences
Why does fa enter the result (jaza')? Because the result occurs right after the condition is realized.
The condition must be fully completed before the result takes effect.
3
Analyze the divorce example
In the example, the condition for divorce is entering two houses sequentially. The second entry must happen immediately; any delay breaks the condition.
The phrase 'بلا تراخی' (without delay) is key.
4
Derive the legal ruling
If both entries are immediate, divorce occurs. If there is a gap, the condition is not met and divorce does not occur.
5
Consider broader applications
This rule applies to any conditional statement using fa in legal contexts, such as sales, vows, and divorces.

Commentary
This passage explains the grammatical and legal principle of the particle fa (ف) in conditional sentences. The author states that fa indicates ta'qib (immediate sequence), meaning that the element introduced by fa follows the preceding element without any temporal gap. This is why fa is used to introduce the result (jaza') of a condition: because the occurrence of the result necessarily follows immediately upon the realization of the condition. The commentator (Khan) provides an example: 'If you enter this house and (then) this house, then you are divorced' – the condition for divorce is entering both houses in sequence, and the second entry must be immediate (without delay) for the divorce to take effect. This illustrates that fa imposes immediacy; if there were a delay, the condition would not be fulfilled. This rule is important in Hanafi fiqh for understanding conditional statements in contracts, divorces, and other legal acts where sequence matters.

Application of Ghayat to Ajl, Yamin, and Implicit 'Fi'
Applying ghayat rules to ajl, yamin, and distinguishing the particle fi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of ghayat al-isqat to fixed terms (ajal) and oaths (yamin), and distinguish the legal effect between explicit and implicit use of the particle 'fi'.

Core Idea
When 'until' is used for omission, the limit itself is not omitted but included.

In this passage, the Hanafi usul expands the rule of ghayat (limit) from the context of wudu elbows to terms and oaths. The key is that the limit is a 'limit for omission' (ghayat al-isqat), meaning the period beyond the limit is omitted, but the limit itself remains covered by the original ruling. Additionally, the particle 'fi' (in) as a container can be explicit or implicit, affecting whether the action encompasses the entire container or merely a part.

Guided Path
1
Recall the base rule from the elbows
The passage references a prior ruling about the limit for wiping the elbows in wudu. That rule established that a limit placed for omission does not omit the limit itself.
This is the foundation for the analogy.
2
Apply to 'ajl' (fixed term)
Consider the debtor's statement: 'I will not demand the price until Ramadan.' The original statement covers all time, but adding 'until Ramadan' omits the period after Ramadan. Thus, Ramadan itself is not omitted and falls under the prohibition.
The limit (Ramadan) is included in the term.
3
Apply to 'yamin' (oath)
Similarly, in an oath 'I will not speak to Zayd until Ramadan', the oath covers all time, but 'until Ramadan' omits after. Therefore, speaking is prohibited during Ramadan.
Same principle: the limit is included.
4
Analyze the particle 'fi' as container
The passage explains that 'fi' indicates containment, but there is a difference between mentioning it explicitly and suppressing it. When suppressed (e.g., 'I fasted this year'), it implies encompassment (isti'ab). When explicit (e.g., 'I fasted in this year'), it merely indicates some fasting within.
Implicit 'fi' demands full coverage of the container.
5
Apply the 'fi' distinction to divorce
For divorce: 'You are divorced (in) tomorrow' (without 'fi') means divorce occurs at the beginning and covers the whole day. 'You are divorced during tomorrow' (with 'fi') allows the husband to intend the end of the day.
The explicit 'fi' permits partial coverage.

Commentary
This passage extends the principle of ghayat al-isqat to two new contexts: ajl (fixed term) and yamin (oath). The Four Types of Dalalah in Hanafi Usul
Defining the four types of word-meaning indication: 'ibarah, isharah, iqtida', dalalat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four types of textual indication (dalalah) as defined by Hanafi usul and apply them to derive rulings from texts.

Core Idea
Words indicate meaning in four ways: explicit (ibarah), implied part (isharah), necessary assumption (iqtida'), and logical consequence (dalalat).

This passage introduces a foundational classification in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: how a word indicates its meaning. Understanding these four types—'ibarah, isharah, iqtida', and dalalat—is essential for deriving rulings from the Qur'an and Sunnah. Each type has a distinct relationship to the original meaning, affecting the strength and scope of the derivation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three bases for indication
The passage explains that a word's indication can relate to the original coined meaning (ma'na wad' lah), a part of it, or a necessary concomitant (lazim) of it. These three bases underlie 'ibarah, isharah, and dalalat respectively.
Iqtida' is a special case involving a laziman that must be assumed for the statement to be valid.
2
Distinguish 'ibarah, isharah, and dalalat
'Ibarah is the direct, explicit meaning intended by the speaker. Isharah is an implied part of that meaning (a necessary component). Dalalat is a necessary consequence that is separate from the original meaning and occurs later in realization.
Example: 'Do not say uff' – 'ibarah prohibits saying uff; isharah implies prohibition of any similar expression of annoyance; dalalat implies prohibition of beating (since beating is a greater harm).
3
Understand iqtida' al-nass
Iqtida' is a meaning that must be assumed for the text to be true, often involving an omitted word or condition. It is based on a lazim that is antecedent (mutaqaddim) to the original meaning.
Example: 'I have made lawful for you the cattle' – iqtida' requires the assumption of proper slaughter for the statement to be applicable.
4
Apply each type to a concrete example
Given a text, identify which type(s) of dalalah are present. For instance, the command 'Aqimu al-salah' directly indicates obligation (ibarah). The phrase 'and wipe your faces and hands' implies that the water must be pure (isharah, as purity is part of the act). Commentary
This passage explains the well-known Hanafi classification of textual indication (dalalah) into four types. The first three ('ibarah, isharah, dalalat) are based on three types of meaning: the original meaning (ma'na wad' lah), a part of that meaning (juz' wad' lah), and a necessary consequence (lazim) that is subsequent to the original (mutakhkhir fi al-tahaqquq). The fourth type (iqtida') is special: it considers a necessary concomitant (lazim) that is antecedent (mutaqaddim) and upon which the validity of the statement depends. In other words, 'ibarah al-nass is the direct, explicit meaning for which the text was spoken; isharah al-nass is an implied meaning that is part of the explicit meaning (e.g., the permissibility of combining dry ablution from the verse 'فامسحوا بوجوهكم وأيديكم' implies that it is done with clean earth); dalalat al-nass is a meaning that is not part of the explicit but is necessarily connected (like the prohibition of saying 'uff' from the verse prohibiting hitting parents). Iqtida' al-nass is a meaning that must be assumed for the statement to be true (e.g., 'أحلت لكم بهيمة الأنعام' requires the assumption of proper slaughter). This classification is fundamental in Hanafi usul for deriving rulings from texts.

Key Terms
عبارة النص (ibarah al-nass): The explicit, direct indication of the text to its primary, coined meaning, intended by the speaker.
اشارة النص (isharah al-nass): An implied meaning that is necessarily part of the explicit meaning, understood from the text without being the primary intent.
اقتضاء النص (iqtida' al-nass): A meaning not explicitly stated but necessarily required for the validity of the text, often involving omission (hadhf) or assumption.
دلالة النص (dalalat al-nass): An inferred meaning that is a necessary consequence (lazim) of the explicit meaning, subsequent in realization.

'Ibarat al-Nass and Isharat al-Nass: Direct Expression vs Implied Indication
Defining 'ibarah and isharah with divorce example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'ibarat al-nass and isharat al-nass using the context (siyaq) of a given text and apply this classification to the divorce example.

Core Idea
The context determines if a meaning is 'ibarah or isharah: what the speech is for is 'ibarah; what it is not for is isharah.

In Hanafi usul, understanding the context (siyaq) is essential for proper textual interpretation. This lesson introduces two foundational types: 'ibarat al-nass (direct expression) and isharat al-nass (implied indication). Using the example of a husband's divorce statement, we will see how the same utterance can contain both types depending on the intended meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the utterance and the speaker's goal
Read the divorce scenario: a husband says 'Every woman of mine is divorced' to please his wife by divorcing another. Determine his immediate goal: divorcing the other woman, not all wives.
The context reveals the mukallaf's intention.
2
Distinguish the 'meaning placed' from the 'context'
The literal meaning (al-ma'na al-mawdu' lahu) is divorce of all women. The context (siyaq al-kalam) is only for divorcing the other woman. Separate the two.
The meaning placed may be broader than intended.
3
Label each meaning according to siyaq
Any meaning that the context is FOR is 'ibarat al-nass (direct). Any meaning that the context is NOT for but is still understood is isharat al-nass (implied). Here, the part about the other woman is 'ibarah; the rest (all wives, mahr, 'iddah) are isharah.
4
Apply the same analysis to the Quranic verse

For example, sunrise is a cause for the existence of light. When sunrise is known, knowledge of light is necessarily established, but not vice versa: from the existence of light, the existence of sunrise is not necessary because it could be light from fire. So since the first implication is constant, it is stronger than the second which is not constant. Second proof: A text that establishes the cause necessarily establishes the effect by way of following, because the effect is subordinate and the cause is primary; when the primary is established, the subordinate is necessarily established. But a text that establishes the effect cannot be considered as establishing the cause, which is primary relative to the effect. In summary, one who establishes the subordinate is not considered to establish the superior. Thus it is considered good (yuhsin) to say that the effect is established by 'ibarat al-nass that establishes the cause, and it is not considered good to say that the cause is established by 'ibarat al-nass that establishes the effect. From this, the definitions of 'ibarat al-nass, isharat al-nass, and iqtida' al-nass become clear. So such implication is called dalalat al-nass. For example, 'Do not say to them 'uff'' (17:23) — this text indicates the prohibition of beating them by dalalat al-nass, because beating parents is a thing that contains the meaning of annoyance (adha), and annoyance is a meaning that every linguist understands that the ruling in the spoken (prohibition of saying 'uff') is due to that meaning. So the implication of the prohibition of beating is dalalat al-nass. The reason for confining these four types is: the meaning intended from the speech, if it is the very meaning placed (ayn al-mawdu' lahu) or a part of it, or a necessary consequence that is not antecedent, then the word is 'ibarat al-nass for it if the context (siyaq al-kalam) is for that meaning. And it is isharat al-nass if the context is not for that meaning.

'Ibarat al-nass is what the context is for; isharat al-nass is everything else understood from the text.

Explains when a meaning qualifies as dalalat al-nass and provides examples.

Core Idea
A meaning qualifies as dalalat al-nass only if any linguist understands it as the cause of the ruling.

This passage from 'Al-Talwiḥ wa al-Tawḍīḥ' explains a critical distinction in Hanafi usul: the difference between mere textual implication and genuine indication by the text (dalalat al-nass). The author emphasizes that for a meaning to be considered dalalat al-nass, it must be immediately understood by any competent speaker of Arabic, not just a trained mujtahid. This condition separates dalalat al-nass from qiyas. Through several Quranic examples, the passage shows how the same text can yield multiple levels of indication.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition on the types of textual indication (dalalat al-nass) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author (musannif) establishes a critical condition for a meaning to qualify as dalalat al-nass: the meaning (which is the 'illa/cause of the ruling) must be understood by any linguist ('arif lughah), not just a mujtahid. This distinguishes dalalat al-nass from qiyas, whose 'illa is only discerned by a mujtahid. The text then provides several Quranic examples to illustrate the application of 'ibarat al-nass (explicit text), isharat al-nass (implicit reference), and dalalat al-nass (indication through cause). For the verse on the poor emigrants, the direct ruling that they deserve a share is 'ibarat al-nass, while the implication that they lost ownership of abandoned property is isharat al-nass. For the verse on the father's obligation to provide for the nursing mother, the direct ruling is 'ibarat al-nass, and derived issues (lineage to father, father's authority over child's property, exclusivity of spending, no need to fix nursing wage for the mother) are isharat al-nass. When the same cause (non-fixation of wage exists for a non-mother nurse, it becomes dalalat al-nass. The verse on inheritance and the verse on kaffarah (feeding ten poor) are also analyzed to show how the derived rulings are isharat al-nass or dalalat al-nass. The author's contribution is praised as groundbreaking. This classification is crucial for Hanafi jurists to determine which textual indications carry evidentiary weight for establishing legal rulings.

Key Terms
دلالة النص (dalalat al-nass): The indication of a text to a meaning that is the 'illa (cause) of the ruling found in the spoken words (manṭūq), understood by any linguist without specialized ijtihad.
عبارة النص (ibarat al-nass): The explicit meaning of the text according to its original context (siyaq nizm), i.e., the direct ruling intended by the arrangement.
اشارة النص (isharat al-nass): A meaning indicated by the text but not directly intended by the context; it is derived from a part of the literal meaning or an implication not primary.
اقتضاء النص (iqtida' al-nass): The necessity of the text requiring a prior necessary condition for its correctness; the indication of a prior necessary consequent (lazim mutaqaddim).

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dalaalat al-nass, distinguish it from mantuq, and apply it to derive rulings from the underlying cause of a text.

The ruling extends beyond the literal words to all cases sharing the same cause.

This is dalaalat al-nass in action.

Kaffarah for intercourse applies by dalaalah to women, and eating/drinking also incur kaffarah because they share the cause of violating the fast.
These examples show the scope of dalaalat al-nass in Hanafi fiqh.

Commentary
This passage defines the usul term dalaalat al-nass (implicit indication of the text). It is distinguished from mantuq (explicit wording). Dalaalat al-nass derives a ruling from the underlying cause (illah) of the explicit text, not from its literal words. The author gives three examples: (1) The verse prohibiting saying 'uff' to parents implies the prohibition of hitting them by greater reason (qiyas awla) because hitting is more harmful. (2) The explicit text (hadith) on kaffarah for intercourse applies by nass to the man, but by dalaalah also to the woman because the cause (violation of fast) is common. (3) Similarly, eating and drinking also necessitate kaffarah by dalaalah, because the cause (violation of fast through nullifiers) is shared with intercourse. This demonstrates how Hanafi usul extends rulings beyond explicit texts using the underlying rationale.

Refutation of Limiting Causes for Specification (Takhsis)
Refuting limitation of specification causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi refutation of the claim that causes of specification are limited to four conditions, and apply the counterexamples to critique mantiq al-mukhalafah.

Core Idea
Causes of specification are not limited—no single example can establish a universal rule of mantiq al-mukhalafah.

This passage from a commentary on Al-Tawdih addresses a key usul dispute: whether a description always implies negation of the ruling from what is not described (mantiq al-mukhalafah). The Shafi'i school argues that when four specific conditions are absent, the description must serve to restrict the ruling. The Hanafi school refutes this, asserting that the causes of specification are infinite and that descriptions can serve other purposes like definition, praise, or emphasis. Understanding this argument is essential for correctly deriving rulings from textual descriptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposing claim
The Shafi'i position is that mantiq al-mukhalafah is valid when four conditions are met. If those conditions are absent, the description implies that the ruling applies only to the described entity and is negated from others.
The four conditions: (1) description not the usual way of exit, (2) not in response to a question, (3) not incidental, (4) speaker knows listener is ignorant.
2
Locate the Hanafi counterclaims
The author provides several examples where descriptions are present without those four conditions, yet opposition is not intended. These include the definition of 'body', praise/blame, emphasis, and the Quranic verse 'And there is no creature on earth'.
These examples show that the benefits of description are diverse and not limited to specification.
3
Analyze the example of 'body long, wide, deep'
If a body were described as 'long, wide, deep' for opposition, it would falsely imply that anything without these attributes is not a body. But that is impossible because all bodies have those dimensions. Instead, the description is a definition of the entity.
The description is for disclosure of reality (تعريف), not restriction.
4
Analyze the Quranic example
In 'There is no creature on earth' (Al-An'am 6:38), the description 'on earth' is not meant to exclude creatures elsewhere, because all creatures are on earth. The description is generic and for emphasis, not opposition.
The proponent would have to affirm mantiq al-mukhalafah here, but they do not because it is clearly not intended.
5
Evaluate the rebuttal of the 'tall man' argument
The opponents cite 'a tall man does not fly' as an example where the description 'tall' appears useless unless it implies that non-tall men fly. The Hanafi response is that a single example cannot form a universal rule; there may be hidden benefits unknown to us.
The author stresses that the Quran and Sunnah contain profound wisdom beyond human comprehension.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute in usul al-fiqh regarding the validity of mantiq al-mukhalafah (concept of opposition). The Hanafi position is that the causes of specification (takhsis) are not limited to the four conditions laid out by those who affirm mantiq al-mukhalafah (primarily the Shafi'i school). The author refutes the claim that whenever these four conditions are absent, one must assume that the description implies negation of ruling from other than the described. Mafhum al-mukhalafah fails because we cannot be certain that all causes of specification are absent from a mentioned attribute.

The Hanafi Approach to Mafhum Mukhalafah: Testimony with Place Specification
Presents dispute between Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn on testimony with place specification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle regarding mafhum mukhalafah to evaluate the acceptance of testimony when a place is specified.

Core Idea
Mafhum mukhalafah creates only a weak doubt (shubha) in Hanafi usul, which does not override a strong probable interpretation.

This lesson examines a classic usul dispute: when witnesses say 'we know no heir in this land,' does the specification imply knowledge of an heir elsewhere? The Sahibayn say yes, creating doubt and rejection. Abu Hanifa says no—the specification is either unnecessary or a precaution against exaggeration. Understanding this dispute reveals the Hanafi principle on mafhum mukhalafah: it is considered a weak indication, not a definitive source.

Guided Path
1
Identify the operative phrase
Look at the witnesses' statement: 'لا نعلم له وارثاً في أرض كذا' (We know no heir for him in such-and-such land). The phrase 'في أرض كذا' is a specification (takhsis) of the place.
2
Recognize the Sahibayn's application of mafhum mukhalafah
The Sahibayn argue that specifying the place implies that the witnesses know of an heir elsewhere (mafhum mukhalafah), which creates doubt. Therefore, the testimony is rejected.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's response
Abu Hanifa offers two arguments: (1) The silence about other lands is not in a place of need because knowledge of other lands is not required; (2) The specification may be to avoid exaggeration (ijtizaf), i.e., they only investigated this land and are not claiming knowledge of other lands.
These arguments show that the specification does not necessarily indicate an heir elsewhere.
4
Compare the strength of the two possibilities
The Sahibayn's inference is based on a weak shubha (doubt) from mafhum mukhalafah. Abu Hanifa's interpretation is a strong probability (ihtimal qawi). In Hanafi usul, a strong probability outweighs a weak doubt.
5
Derive the Hanafi usul rule
The Hanafi position is that mafhum mukhalafah is not a definitive proof; it only creates a weak doubt that cannot overrule a clear statement or a strong probability. Thus, the testimony is accepted.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute about the acceptance of testimony when witnesses specify a place in their statement 'we know no heir in such-and-such land'. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) reject the testimony because they apply mafhum mukhalafah: the specification implies that there might be an heir elsewhere, creating doubt. Abu Hanifa accepts the testimony, arguing that the mention of the specific land is either unnecessary (since knowledge of other lands is not required) or is meant to avoid exaggeration. The weak shubha of mafhum mukhalafah does not outweigh the strong probability of caution. Thus, the testimony is accepted. This reflects the Hanafi principle that mere possibility (shubha) does not invalidate a clear statement when there is a valid interpretation.

شبہ (shubha): Doubt or ambiguity that affects the legal ruling.
