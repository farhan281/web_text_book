The Indwelling Relationship (Ḥulūl) in Hanafi Usul
Definition and scope of ḥulūl with a dispute against philosophers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the indwelling relationship (ḥulūl), distinguish the Hanafi acceptance of ḥulūl from the philosophical restriction, and identify the water-in-jug example as a valid ḥulūl.

Core Idea
Ḥulūl is a valid basis for metaphor when the occupant needs the place; Hanafis accept it broadly.

In this lesson, we examine the concept of ḥulūl (indwelling) as one of the nine relationships that allow metaphorical usage in language. The passage explains that the place (maḥall) is considered original relative to the occupant (ḥāl), and vice versa when the occupant is the main intention. The water-in-jug example clarifies this relationship, and the author contrasts the Hanafi view with that of the philosophers, who restrict ḥulūl to accidents in substances.

Guided Path
1
Define ḥulūl and its elements
Identify the occupant (ḥāl) and the place (maḥall) in the relationship. Understand that the place is considered original because the occupant needs it.
Use the water (occupant) and jug (place) as the core example.
2
Analyze the water-in-jug example
Explain why applying the name of the place to the occupant is valid (e.g., calling water 'the jug') and vice versa (calling the jug 'the water') when the occupant is the main intention.
The intended sense is water from the jug, not the jug itself.
3
Distinguish Hanafi and philosophical ḥulūl
Note that Hanafi usul scholars accept the indwelling of water in the jug as ḥulūl, while philosophers define ḥulūl only as the inherence of accidents in substances, rejecting water (a substance) as an occupant.
This difference leads to broader metaphorical possibilities in Hanafi usul.
4
Identify the legal significance
Recognize that this relationship is one of the nine recognized 'alāqāt (connections) for metaphor (majāz). Understanding ḥulūl is essential for grasping how shar‘ī terms can be extended metaphorically.
The lesson sets the foundation for the next lesson on applying this to legal terminology.

Metaphor in Shar‘ī Terms: The Role of Shared Mashrū‘iyya
Extension of metaphor to shar‘ī terms based on shared legislative purpose.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that metaphorical usage between shar‘ī terms is valid when they share a common legislative purpose (mashrū‘iyya), and explain the example of bequest (waṣiyya) and inheritance (irth).

Core Idea
Shared legislative purpose (mashrū‘iyya) allows shar‘ī terms to be used metaphorically for each other.

Building on the previous lesson's exploration of indwelling, this lesson examines how the same relationships (especially similarity) allow metaphor to operate within legal terminology. The passage asserts that when two legislated concepts share a common 'manner of legislation' (kayf shar‘), one term can be used metaphorically for the other. The concrete example given is bequest (waṣiyya) and inheritance (irth), both involving succession after death. This principle is foundational for Hanafi istiḥsān and fatwā reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the analogy with linguistic metaphor
Just as 'asad' (lion) is metaphorically applied to a courageous man due to shared bravery, shar‘ī terms can be metaphorically applied when they share a legislative meaning.
The linguistic example establishes the pattern: relationship of similarity (mushābaha).
2
Understand the concept of 'kayf shar‘' (manner of legislation)
Each legislated act has a specific purpose and manner in which it is legislated. When two acts share this 'kayf shar‘', metaphor becomes possible.
It is not just any similarity, but a similarity in the legislative purpose and structure.
3
Analyze the example of bequest (waṣiyya) and inheritance (irth)
Both involve succession after death (istikhlāf ba‘d al-mawt). After covering the deceased's essential needs, property is transferred to heirs (irth) or to beneficiaries (waṣiyya). This shared characteristic allows one to be used metaphorically for the other.
The passage states that both share the 'meaning and manner of legislation' (ma‘nā wa kayfiyya mashrū‘iyya).
4
Identify the condition of necessity (luzūm)
Just as linguistic metaphor requires a necessary connection (luzūm), shar‘ī metaphor also requires that the metaphorical usage be necessary for clarity or conciseness.
The text concludes: 'so in legal matters, metaphor requires a necessary connection.'
5
Differentiate the Hanafi position from opponents
The Hanafi school permits this metaphorical extension, while some mutakallimūn and Shafi‘is may restrict metaphorical usage in legal terms to avoid confusion.
The Hanafi response is that the connection based on mashrū‘iyya is a valid basis for extension, as seen in the wasiyya/irth example.

Majaz in Shar'i Terms: Conditions of Validity
Explaining the conditions for valid majaz in shar'i terminology using examples from fiqh transactions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In legal language, words are often used metaphorically based on causal relationships. However, the Hanafi school distinguishes between a cause that is 'mere' (sabab mahd) and one that is specifically intended for its effect. This lesson explains the concept and its implications for interpreting expressions like 'you are free' in the context of divorce and emancipation.

Guided Path
1
Define sabab mahd
Understand that a sabab mahd is a cause that leads to an effect but is not legislated for that effect; it has other independent applications. For example, milk al-raqaba (ownership of a slave's body) can exist without milk al-mut'ah (ownership of usufruct), as with a foster sister.
This is a key distinction in Hanafi usul.
2
Identify the direction of metaphor
Recognize that for a sabab mahd, you may use the cause's name to refer to the effect (e.g., 'emancipation' for 'divorce'), but you cannot use the effect's name to refer to the cause (e.g., 'divorce' for 'emancipation').
The metaphor is unidirectional.
3
Apply to the given example
Take the case of a husband saying 'you are free' (anti mu'taqah) to his wife. Here, emancipation (cause) is used for divorce (effect), so divorce occurs. Conversely, if he says 'you are divorced' (anti taliq) to his female slave, emancipation is not effected because the reverse metaphor is invalid.
This is the Hanafi ruling, contrary to al-Shafi'i.
4
Understand the disagreement
The Shafi'i school allows bidirectional metaphor based on the general connection between cause and effect. The Hanafi response limits it due to the nature of sabab mahd.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is whether the slave becomes free by the word 'talaq'.

Why Khiyar al-Shart is Contrary to Qiyas
Explains the rationale behind khiyar al-shart as an exception.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school considers khiyar al-shart to be 'ala khilaf al-qiyas' and state the role of khatar (risk) in this ruling.

Core Idea
Khiyar al-shart is upheld despite being contrary to qiyas because necessity overrides the prohibition of uncertainty in sale.

This passage delves into a classic Hanafi issue: the option of condition (khiyar al-shart) in a sale contract. By its nature, a sale requires certainty; any condition that introduces risk (khatar) is normally invalid. Yet the law allows this option as an exception, justified by necessity and careful placement on the ruling, not the cause. Let's unpack the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core conflict
Sale is a contract that does not tolerate risk (khatar). A condition suspending the sale's finality creates uncertainty between occurrence and non-occurrence.
Khatar is the key concept – uncertainty that renders a contract like gambling.
2
Understand the qiyas-based rule
Normally, qiyas would prohibit any condition that introduces risk into sale because it undermines the certainty required for valid ownership transfer.
Analogy would reject khiyar al-shart.
3
Recognize the exception
The Hanafi school permits khiyar al-shart on the basis of necessity (darurah) to prevent harm (ghubn). This overriding consideration makes the ruling 'ala khilaf al-qiyas.
Necessity justifies the deviation.
4
Determine how the condition operates
The condition is designed to affect only the ruling (hukm) – the binding effect of the sale – not the cause (sabab) – the contract itself. This limits the scope of uncertainty.
By operating on hukm only, the sale's existence is secure.
5
Compare with divorce and manumission
In divorce and manumission, conditions are allowed on the cause because these are acts of removal (isqat) which tolerate risk. This contrast clarifies the uniqueness of sale.
The nature of the contract matters.

The Operation of Conditions: Hukm vs Sabab
Distinguishes between conditions in sale and in divorce/manumission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between how conditions operate on hukm (ruling) versus sabab (cause) in Hanafi usul, using sale and divorce as examples.

Core Idea
Conditions in sale affect only the hukm; conditions in divorce affect the sabab because the nature of the act differs.

This passage clarifies a critical doctrinal point: why conditions in some contracts focus on the ruling while in others they penetrate the cause. The answer lies in whether the contract is about acquisition (tamlik) or removal (isqat). Understanding this distinction is key to applying Hanafi usul correctly.

Guided Path
1
Recall the nature of sale
Sale is an act of acquisition (tamlik) that transfers ownership. It requires complete certainty; thus any condition that introduces uncertainty is generally prohibited.
Sale must be free of gharar (excessive uncertainty).
2
Understand the exception in khiyar al-shart
To allow the option but avoid invalidating the contract, the condition is applied to the ruling (hukm) – i.e., the binding effect – not to the cause (sabab) – the contract itself.
This limits the scope of uncertainty.
3
Compare with divorce and manumission
Divorce and manumission are acts of removal (isqat). They do not create ownership; they terminate a relationship. Removal can tolerate risk because it is not a gain.
Thus a conditional divorce is valid by suspending the cause itself.
4
Identify the reason for the difference
Isqat can admit conditions on the sabab because the outcome is not a financial transaction; uncertainty is less harmful. In contrast, tamlik requires certainty to avoid gambling.
The category of the act determines the rule.
5
Apply to other contracts
Consider other contracts (e.g., gift, loan) – are they tamlik or isqat? This helps predict whether a condition would affect the sabab or hukm.
This is a usul tool for new cases.

Istihsan in Udhiya: From Charity to Blood
Explains istihsan in udhiya: from charity to blood sacrifice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school applies istihsan to prefer blood sacrifice over charity in udhiya and derive the ruling for after the sacrifice period.

Core Idea
A text and purification wisdom transfer the ruling from charity to blood sacrifice in udhiya, but after the period, the original rule returns as precaution.

This lesson examines a key application of istihsan in the Hanafi school: the case of udhiya. While financial worship generally requires giving the very object as charity (tasaddaq bi 'l-'ayn), a specific text and the wisdom of purification lead to blood sacrifice (iraqa) being wajib during the days of sacrifice. We will explore the reasoning, the role of conjecture, and what happens if the sacrifice is missed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original rule and its exception.
Recognize that the general principle in financial worship is to give the specific item as charity (tasaddaq bi 'l-'ayn). Then note that for udhiya, this is set aside in favor of blood sacrifice (iraqa).
2
Understand the cause (ta'lil) and its conjectural nature.
The passage gives a wisdom: blood sacrifice purifies the meat, just as charity purifies wealth. But this cause is conjectural (maznun) and not decisive by itself.
3
See how istihsan operates through text (nass) over qiyas.
The text about blood sacrifice overrides the analogy (qiyas) that would require charity. Istihsan is applied by favoring the text-based ruling over the general rule.
Identify the textual category of the nass (amm? khass?) to see how it restricts the general principle.
4
Apply the temporal dimension: after the time, the original rule applies again.
If the animal was not sacrificed during the prescribed days, the original rule of charity is revived as a precaution, not by qiyas. This shows the limits of the nass's application.

It is a financial worship, so originally it could be that charity of the sheep itself should be given. But it has been transferred to blood sacrifice, so that in the days of sacrifice this charity of the sheep itself is transferred to blood sacrifice, i.e., slaughter this sheep and let its blood flow, in order to purify the food, so that the food of hospitality from Allah becomes pure for the servants. They understand that charity wealth is the filth of sins; by its removal, dirt is removed, and impurity exits from wealth, purifying the remaining wealth. Similarly, in the days of sacrifice, with blood sacrifice, the meat of hospitality will become pure, and impurity will be removed, and the guests will receive pure and wholesome food from Allah. Therefore, blood sacrifice is obligatory in the days of sacrifice. But we did not act on this conjectural cause (ta'lil maznun) meaning that the original in financial worship is charity of the eye; we did not act on this cause at the time of sacrifice, so we do not permit charity of the eye at the time of sacrifice. And because the text (nass) about blood sacrifice occurs in the days of sacrifice, we did not act on the original of charity in the presence of the text; charity of the sheep itself is not permissible in those days. And we acted on it after the time: when the time of sacrifice ended and due to some excuse the sheep of sacrifice could not be slaughtered, then we acted on that original, i.e., charity of the sheep, as a precaution, not by analogy, so this objection does not arise that it necessitates analogy in something not known by analogy. And therefore, since after the time we acted on this original of charity, when the next year came and that sheep remains, the ruling does not transfer to sacrifice and blood sacrifice. Because it had probability, i.e., because charity of the eye had the probability of being the original, and a ruling was already given with it, so it is recorded with doubt; due to this doubt that possibly blood sacrifice was the original and its time has passed, the first ruling is not invalidated.

Commentary
This passage discusses a ruling about udhiya (sacrificial animal) and the principle of istihsan. The author explains that financial worship (ibada maliyya) originally requires giving the very object as charity (tasadduq bi 'ayn al-sha). However, for udhiya, the ruling is transferred to blood sacrifice (iraqa) due to a specific text. The reason given is that blood sacrifice purifies the meat, just as charity purifies wealth. But this cause is conjectural (maznun), so in the days of sacrifice, the original rule of charity is set aside, and blood sacrifice is obligatory. After the time of sacrifice, if the animal could not be slaughtered, the original rule (charity) is applied as a precaution, not by qiyas. The author also explains that even if a doubt arises about whether the original rule might have been blood sacrifice, the first ruling (based on charity) stands because it was established with probability.

Key Terms
تصدق بالعين (tasaddaq bi 'l-'ayn): Giving the very object as charity (as opposed to its value).
إراقة الدم (iraqat al-dam): Blood sacrifice (slaughtering and letting blood flow) in udhiya.
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Preferring a stronger evidence (text, necessity) over a weaker analogy.
