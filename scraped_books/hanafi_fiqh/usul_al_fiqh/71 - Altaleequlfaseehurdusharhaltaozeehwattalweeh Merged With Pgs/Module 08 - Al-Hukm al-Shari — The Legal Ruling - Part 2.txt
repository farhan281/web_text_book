The Hanafi Definition of Fiqh and Its Three Conditions
Defense of the Hanafi definition of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of fiqh and explain the three conditions: (1) knowledge of all rulings revealed with clarity, (2) the faculty of deduction (malakat al-istinbat), and (3) that qiyasi issues are not part of the definition.

Core Idea
Fiqh is not mere knowledge of rulings; it requires the faculty of deriving new rulings from texts.

In this passage, the commentator defends the Hanafi definition of fiqh against objections. The definition aims to be comprehensive and precise: fiqh is knowledge of all practical rulings that have been revealed with clarity, accompanied by the faculty of istinbat (deduction). This distinguishes a true faqih from a mere narrator of rulings. The passage also explains why qiyasi issues (masail qiyasiyya) are deliberately excluded from the definition to avoid circularity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components of the definition
Read the passage and note that fiqh is defined as (a) knowledge of all rulings, (b) that have been revealed with clarity (zuhur), (c) along with the faculty of istinbat. Notice that these are the three essential conditions.
The plural 'ahkam' is intended to include all revealed rulings, not just a subset.
2
Understand the objection about the plural 'ahkam'
The commentator objects that a plural implies at least three, so knowing only one or two rulings would not be fiqh. The author responds that the faculty is not acquired from one or two rulings; it requires repeated exposure. The objection is based on a hypothetical that cannot occur in reality.
The Hanafi position is that the definition is based on actual, not hypothetical, circumstances.
3
Analyze the condition of 'zuhur' (appearance of revelation)
The definition specifies that revelation must have 'appeared' to the mujtahid, meaning it has reached him. If a ruling has been revealed but not yet reached the mujtahid, its knowledge is not required for him to be called a faqih. He may perform ijtihad on it until revelation arrives.
This condition protects against requiring knowledge of rulings that are not accessible.
4
Examine the condition of malakat al-istinbat
The faculty of deduction is essential. It means the ability to derive qiyasi branches from textual rulings by generalizing causes. Mere knowledge from listening is not enough; one must be able to reason and deduce.
The two possible meanings of 'istinbat': deriving branches from rulings (preferred) or deriving rulings from evidences.
5
Understand why qiyasi issues are excluded
Qiyasi issues are the result of ijtihad, not a prerequisite. Including them would cause circularity (dawr): if fiqh depends on knowledge of qiyasi issues, and those issues come from fiqh, the definition would be circular. This holds for both the first and later mujtahids because taqlid of earlier mujtahids is not allowed.
Later mujtahids must perform their own ijtihad; they cannot simply accept earlier results.

The commentator said in Al-Talwih: His statement 'Rather it is knowledge' is an invented definition, i.e., the author invented this definition himself. 'Controlling (the subject matter)': Since the author objected to the definition of the Shafi'is that it does not specify the intended meaning by which its subject matter can be controlled, so the author adopted a definition by which the subject matter is controlled: he intended all rulings with which revelation appeared with clarity and those on which consensus has been concluded, but he made the faculty of deduction a condition. By intending all rulings, he excluded some, saying that knowledge of some is not called fiqh. 'Rather it indicates': This definition indicates that when revelation appears with clarity with one or two rulings, knowledge of them along with the faculty is not called fiqh, even if at that time those are all the rulings. When one accepts three rulings, then at that time his name will be faqih. The reason for the objection is that the word 'ahkam' is plural, and the least individuals of a plural are three. The commentator's objection is based on a mere hypothetical that is impossible by habit, because the faculty is acquired through repeated perception of the particulars of rulings, not from one or two rulings. The author's definition is with respect to actual and established individuals, or possible individuals. The commentator's objection is only with respect to hypothetical impossible individuals; how could it be possible that the faculty is acquired from knowledge of one or two rulings? 'The condition of revelation with appearance': He added the condition of appearance with revelation, by which he wants to avoid the case where revelation has occurred regarding a ruling but has not reached the mujtahid. For his faqahat, it is not necessary to know such a ruling; rather at that time the mujtahid can perform ijtihad regarding this ruling. Then when the revelation of that ruling reaches him, if his ijtihad agreed with it, then that is good; otherwise, he must revert to the text. 'With the faculty of deduction along with knowledge of what was mentioned': For faqahat, along with knowledge of the mentioned rulings, having the faculty of deduction is a condition. Since the word 'istinbat' has 'al' as a substitute for the possessive phrase, there are two probabilities for the possessive phrase: first, it is the qiyasi furu', then the pronoun 'minha' refers to 'ahkam', so the meaning is that he can derive qiyasi branches from textual rulings. The second probability is that the possessive phrase is the rulings, then the pronoun 'minha' refers to 'adillah', so the meaning is that he can derive rulings from the evidences. Knowledge of a ruling from hearing the text solely based on the listener's knowledge of language without having ability of consideration and inference is not counted as fiqh. The first is more appropriate because proficiency in fiqh is acquired only by deriving qiyasi branches from textual rulings through generalizing the causes. Since with 'la al-masail al-qiyasiyah' he declared qiyasi issues unnecessary and even corrupt for the realization of fiqh and excluded them, instead he added the condition of the faculty of deduction. So the intended meaning is derivation of qiyasi branches from rulings, not derivation of rulings from evidences. 'Not the qiyasi issues': It is not a condition in the definition of fiqh to know the qiyasi issues because they are the result of the faqih's fiqh and ijtihad. Since qiyasi issues are branches that are deduced through ijtihad, their knowledge depends on a person being a faqih. If fiqh were to depend on knowledge of these issues, circularity would occur. Therefore, knowledge of qiyasi issues is not conditional in the definition of fiqh. Before this, there was an objection: The circularity (dawr) occurs for the first qa'is (one who performs qiyas) because qiyasi issues are the result of his fiqh; if his fiqh is made dependent on knowledge of these issues, circularity would occur. But for later mujtahids, if knowledge of the qiyasi issues that the earlier mujtahid deduced is made a condition, no circularity occurs. We say: That is not permissible. The summary of the answer is that taqlid is not permissible for a mujtahid. Therefore, a later mujtahid cannot verify the qiyasi issues of an earlier mujtahid; rather he must himself perform ijtihad in these qiyasi issues and act accordingly. Now, if knowledge of these qiyasi issues is made a condition for the later mujtahid, circularity will occur because in that case these issues become the result of the later mujtahid's fiqh since they are obtained through his ijtihad. 'Taswish qat al-ra': It is necessary for a later mujtahid to obtain knowledge of the qiyasi issues of earlier mujtahids as a conceptual knowledge ('ilm tasawwuri) so that he does not accidentally oppose consensus. 'For qiyasi issues': The basis of this objection is that qiyas manifests rulings, it does not establish rulings; rather the establishing of rulings is by the texts that are the maqis 'alayh. Qiyas only shows that the texts include these qiyasi issues. In that case, the qiyasi issues become included in the manifestation of revelation's descent because for the mujtahid it is zuhur (manifest).

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the definition of fiqh, defending the author's (al-Tawdih's) invented definition against objections raised by the commentator (Taftazani in al-Talwih). The passage type is an expansion and defense of a definition. The definition under discussion is: knowledge of all practical rulings of Shariah that have been revealed with clarity and on which consensus exists, accompanied by the faculty of deduction (malakat al-istinbat). The commentator objects that the plural 'ahkam' necessitates at least three rulings, so if a person knows only one or two rulings, his knowledge would not be called fiqh. The author's response is that the faculty of deduction is not acquired from one or two rulings; it develops through repeated perception of many particulars, so the hypothetical case is impossible in practice. The passage then explains the condition that revelation must have 'appeared' (zuhur) to the mujtahid; rulings not yet reached do not need to be known for fiqh. The key condition of 'malakat al-istinbat' is then discussed: it means the ability to derive qiyasi branches from textual rulings, not merely hearing from texts. The author then argues that qiyasi issues (masail qiyasiyya) themselves are not part of the definition because they are the result of fiqh, not a condition; including them would cause circularity (dawr). The passage further refutes an objection that circularity only applies to the first mujtahid, not later ones, by stating that later mujtahids cannot rely on taqlid of earlier ones; they must perform their own ijtihad. Thus, for them too, making knowledge of qiyasi issues a condition would cause circularity. Finally, it is noted that later mujtahids need conceptual knowledge of earlier mujtahids' results to avoid contradicting consensus, but that is not part of the definition. This passage illustrates the Hanafi emphasis on the faculty of ijtihad as central to fiqh, and the rejection of mere memorization of rulings as sufficient.

Key Terms
ملكة الاستنباط (malakat al-istinbat): The faculty of deriving qiyasi branches (furu' qiyasiyya) from textual rulings by generalizing causes (ta'mim al-'ilal).
المسائل القياسية (al-masail al-qiyasiyya): Issues derived through qiyas (analogy) from textual rulings. They are the result of ijtihad and not part of the definition of fiqh to avoid circularity.
نزول الوحي بالظهور (nuzul al-wahi bi al-zuhur): Revelation that has reached the mujtahid with clear and apparent textual evidence. Rulings not yet reached are not required for the title of faqih.

Definition of Fiqh and the Role of Qiyasi Issues
Commentator critiques author's definition of fiqh regarding qiyasi issues

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key elements of the Hanafi definition of fiqh and explain why knowledge of qiyasi issues is not a condition for fiqh according to the commentator.

Core Idea
Fiqh is the ability to derive rulings from evidence, not knowledge of derived issues; including qiyasi issues would cause circularity.

This lesson examines a critical refinement of the definition of fiqh in Hanafi usul. The commentator argues that the author's definition inadvertently makes fiqh an ever-changing set of rulings rather than a fixed science. By analyzing the circularity argument and the role of ijtihad, you will understand why the faculty of derivation (istinbat) is central to the Hanafi concept of fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its technical usage
Recognize that fiqh as a technical term among Hanafi scholars refers to a specific science, not a universal concept. Not the lexical meaning 'understanding' but the ability to derive rulings from detailed evidence.
The commentator insists that fiqh must be a determined science, like other Islamic sciences.
2
Analyze the author's definition and the exclusion of qiyasi issues
The author's definition apparently requires derivation of rulings but excludes knowledge of qiyasi issues. Understand why: if fiqh is defined as knowledge of all derived rulings, then a mujtahid must know the qiyasi issues derived by earlier mujtahids, which leads to a problem.
The source says 'la al-masa'il al-qiyasiyya' – qiyasi issues are not part of the definition.
3
Understand the circularity objection
If knowledge of qiyasi issues were a condition for being a faqih, then the first mujtahid's faqahat would depend on knowing issues that only his own ijtihad produces – a vicious circle. The commentator highlights this to justify the exclusion.
For the first qa'is (mujtahid), the circle is direct. For later mujtahids, an indirect circle arises because they cannot rely on earlier ijtihad.
4
Evaluate the response regarding the later mujtahid
A later mujtahid cannot blindly follow earlier qiyasi issues (taqlid). He must perform his own ijtihad. Therefore, making his knowledge of those issues a condition would also lead to a circle, as they become the result of his own ijtihad.
The commentator adds that even conceptual knowledge (tasawwuri) of earlier issues is not required for faqahat, though it may be useful to avoid violating consensus.
5
Recognize the final definition: fiqh as malakat al-istinbat
The commentator concludes that fiqh is the faculty of deriving subsidiary rulings (furu' qiyasiyya) from the prescribed rulings through generalization of causes. This puts the emphasis on the process, not the product.
The definition becomes: 'Istinbat al-furu' al-qiyasiyya min al-ahkam' – derivation of qiyasi branches from rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a critical discussion on the definition of fiqh. The commentator (sharh) engages in a detailed refutation of the author's (musannif) definition. The author's definition apparently required derivation of rulings from evidences but excluded knowledge of qiyasi (analogical) issues. The commentator argues that fiqh is the ability to derive rulings from texts, not just knowledge of derived issues. A key point is that qiyasi issues are the result of ijtihad, so making them a condition for the definition would cause a circularity. The passage also addresses the question of whether a later mujtahid must know the qiyasi issues derived by earlier mujtahids. The commentator argues that since taqlid is not permissible for a mujtahid, and he must rely on his own ijtihad, there is no circle. Additionally, the commentator raises an objection that the author's definition makes fiqh a changing entity, not a fixed science, which contradicts the technical definition of fiqh as a specific science among the scholars (al-qawm). The text thus reveals the Hanafi usul emphasis on the process of derivation (istinbat) as the core of fiqh, rather than mere memorization of rulings.

Why Fiqh is Called 'Ilm Despite Being Zanni
Explaining why the term 'ilm is applied to fiqh despite its speculative nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the three Hanafi arguments for applying the term 'ilm to fiqh and evaluate the impact of the taswib/takhti' debate on the certainty of fiqh rulings.

Core Idea
Fiqh is called 'ilm because it is either certain by definition, or 'ilm covers speculative knowledge, or its establishment becomes certain through the Lawgiver's acceptance of dominant opinion.

You have learned that fiqh often relies on ijtihad and qiyas, which produce speculative (zanni) outcomes. Yet scholars call it 'ilm — knowledge. This lesson unpacks the apparent contradiction by presenting three responses from Hanafi usul. These answers not only justify the terminology but also reveal how speculative rulings acquire a certain status in shari' law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
The core tension: why call fiqh 'ilm when it is based on zann? 'ilm normally implies certainty.
2
First Answer: Definitional Certainty
The definition of fiqh includes only certain rulings (from revelation and ijma), so the term 'ilm applies to that subset.
This answer limits fiqh to certain rulings.
3
Second Answer: Broad Usage of 'Ilm
'Ilm is used broadly for any organized science, even speculative like medicine.
Similar to how medicine is called 'ilm.
4
Third Answer: Lawgiver's Consideration of Dominant Opinion
The Shari' has decreed that when a mujtahid has dominant opinion (ghalabat al-zann), the ruling is established (yuthbut al-hukm). This makes the establishment certain, though the path is speculative.
The key principle: 'kullama ghalaba zann al-mujtahid bi al-hukm yuthbut al-hukm'.
5
Refinement: Taswib vs Takhti'
The third answer is fully correct under taswib (every mujtahid correct). Under takhti' (only one correct), the 'establishment' means it is obligatory to act or established relative to evidence, still giving certainty for the mujtahid's obligation.
Both views uphold that fiqh is 'ilm.

Commentary
This passage addresses a key question: how can fiqh, being speculative (zanni), be called 'ilm (knowledge) which typically denotes certainty? The author presents three answers. First, that the definition of fiqh includes only those rulings that are certain: those from revealed texts and ijma. Second, that 'ilm is a broad term applied both to certain and speculative knowledge, as in medicine. Third, a deeper argument: Sharia considers a mujtahid's dominant opinion (ghalabat al-zann) as establishing the ruling in a way that makes it certain in terms of obligation, even if the process is speculative. This third answer is further refined based on whether one holds that every mujtahid is correct (taswib) or that only one is correct (takhti'). In the taswib view, the ruling itself becomes certain; in the takhti' view, the ruling is certain in the sense of being obligatory to follow or established relative to the evidence. Thus fiqh qualifies as 'ilm in all interpretations.

Key Terms
علم ('ilm): Here used in a broad sense covering both certain and speculative knowledge, as in the sciences like medicine.
ظن (zann): Speculative or probable knowledge, as opposed to certainty (qat').
غلبہ ظن (ghalabat al-zann): The preponderance of opinion that leads to the establishment of a ruling according to the Lawgiver.
تصويب (taswib): The doctrine that every mujtahid is correct in his ijtihad.

The Use of 'Ilm for Fiqh: Objections and Responses
Addresses objection that fiqh is zanni yet called 'ilm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and explain the three Hanafi answers to the objection that fiqh is speculative yet called 'ilm, and apply the third answer using ghalabat al-zann to justify the certainty of obligation in a derived ruling.

Core Idea
Fiqh is called 'ilm because its rulings are either certain in themselves or certain in obligation through the Lawgiver's validation of ghalabat al-zann.

You have learned that fiqh is defined as knowledge of practical rulings from detailed evidence. But an important objection arises: since fiqh relies on ijtihad and qiyas, it is speculative (zanni), so how can it be called knowledge ('ilm), which implies certainty? This passage presents three powerful responses from the Hanafi tradition, defending the application of 'ilm to fiqh. Understanding these responses is essential for grasping the epistemological basis of Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection states: fiqh is based on ijtihad and qiyas, so it is speculative; 'ilm is only for certitude, so why call fiqh 'ilm?
2
Analyze the first answer: fiqh is certain
The first answer argues that according to the author's definition, fiqh includes only certain rulings from revelation and consensus, so it is certain. But this seems to exclude speculative rulings from fiqh.
3
Analyze the second answer: 'ilm is broad
The second answer points out that 'ilm is used broadly, covering both certain and speculative domains (like medicine), so fiqh being speculative does not prevent calling it 'ilm.
4
Analyze the third answer: ghalabat al-zann gives certainty of obligation
The third answer is the most profound: the Lawgiver has validated ghalabat al-zann as a basis for action. Thus, when a mujtahid attains ghalabat al-zann, the ruling is established as certain in terms of obligation to act, even if the path to it is speculative. This answer is further refined for those who hold that only one mujtahid is correct.
5
Evaluate the clarifications on text and consensus
The commentary clarifies that texts (including ahad hadith) and consensus provide certainty in themselves, barring impediments. This ensures that rulings from these sources remain within the purview of 'ilm.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on Al-Tawdih, addressing a fundamental epistemological issue in Hanafi usul: whether rulings derived through zann (predominant opinion) can be called 'ilm (knowledge). The author presents three responses to an objection that such rulings are merely zanni and thus outside the definition of fiqh. The first response notes that qiyas based on a text (nass) yields qat'i rulings, but pure qiyas (qiyas without a direct text) is excluded. However, some texts (like khabar wahid) are zanni in themselves, so this answer is insufficient. The second response argues that nass and ijma' are qat'i in themselves when considered without obstacles; thus rulings from them are qat'i. The third and most elaborate response distinguishes between the ruling itself and the path to it. The mujtahid arrives at the ruling via a zanni path, but once arrived, the ruling becomes qat'i for him. This is supported by ijma' that acting on predominant opinion is obligatory, effectively making the ruling certain in divine knowledge (on the taswib view that every correct mujtahid is correct). On the view that only one mujtahid is correct (mukhti'a), the ruling is not certain in itself, but the obligation to act upon it is certain due to qat'i evidence (ijma' and tawatur in meaning of khabar wahid). This obligation to act is itself a ruling (hukm), so knowing it constitutes fiqh. The commentator clarifies that 'obligation to act' means the mujtahid must be certain about the ruling's classification (wajib, haram, etc.), which is indeed knowledge of the rulings. Thus, fiqh rulings based on zann are properly called 'ilm.

علم (‘ilm): Knowledge that is qat'i (certain) and excludes the possibility of error.
مظنون (maznun): The ruling or matter about which one has presumption (zann) – i.e., not certain in itself.

Interpreting 'Thubut al-Hukm': Epistemological Establishment of the Ruling
Critical refinement of thubut al-hukm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school interprets 'thubut al-hukm' as knowledge of the ruling's existence (thubut al-ilm bi al-hukm) and apply this interpretation to resolve the objection that eternal rulings cannot be proven by temporal proofs.

Core Idea
Proofs produce knowledge of the rule, not the rule itself – the eternal cannot be temporally caused.

A commentator objects that interpreting 'thubut al-hukm' as knowledge is useless, because proofs are mere indicators. But this misses the deep theological point: the ruling as divine address is eternal, so temporal proofs cannot create it; they can only give knowledge of it. This lesson unpacks that argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Read the commentator's criticism: he claims that interpreting 'thubut al-hukm' as 'thubut al-ilm bi al-hukm' is useless because proofs are mere indicators (amarat) that give knowledge, so stating the obvious is redundant.
The commentator thinks the author is saying something trivial.
2
Understand the problem of the eternal ruling
If 'hukm' means the divine speech itself (the eternal attribute of speech), then saying 'it is proven by proofs' is problematic because what is eternal cannot be brought into existence by something temporal. Proofs cannot cause the ruling to exist.
This is the ontological problem: the ruling pre-exists, so proofs cannot 'prove' it.
3
See the solution: epistemological reading
By interpreting 'thubut' as knowledge, the problem dissolves. The proof does not cause the ruling to exist; it gives the knower knowledge of the ruling's existence. The author's interpretation is necessary to avoid theological inconsistency.
This reading also accommodates both certain and conjectural knowledge (qat'i and zanni).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A proof gives knowledge of a thing; a cause produces the thing itself.

Translation in prayer is valid only if the person is not an innovator, the meaning is conclusive, and the order is preserved.

This lesson examines the practical ruling and its conditions for reciting the Quran's meaning in a language other than Arabic during prayer. Commentary
This passage is from a commentary (sharh) on a Hanafi usul text, likely al-Hidayah or a similar work. It discusses the definition of the Quran and the permissibility of reciting its translation (e.g., Persian) in prayer. The author explains that Imam Abu Hanifa considered the meaning (ma'na) to be the primary objective of the Quran, while the wording (nazm) is secondary. Therefore, in prayer, if one recites the meaning in Persian, it can fulfill the obligation under certain conditions: the person must not be an innovator, the translation must not involve ta'wil (interpretive meanings), and the order should not be disturbed. The passage then addresses objections: if the Quran is only meaning, why is nazm part of its definition? And if nazm is essential, why does the Imam allow dropping it? The answer is that the Imam does not drop nazm absolutely; rather, Persian nazm serves as a substitute. The verse 'faqra'u ma tayassara min al-Qur'an' (recite what is easy of the Quran) is interpreted to include both compositional parts (with nazm) and simple parts (meaning only), supporting the permissibility. This highlights the Hanafi approach of prioritizing ma'na (meaning) while still valuing nazm (form) as a means. The discussion shows how usul principles (definition of Quran, partitive min, etc.) are applied to derive a fiqh ruling.

Key Terms
نظم (nazm): The wording or composition of the Quran, specifically the Arabic words in their order.
معنی (ma'na): The meaning or message of the Quranic words.

Definition of Quran and Persian Recitation in Prayer
Debate on Quran's definition and its implications for recitation in prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that the Quran is the revealed meaning (al-ma'na al-mujarrad) and apply the istinbat from Quran 73:20 to justify Persian recitation in prayer, while addressing the objection of combining literal and metaphorical use.

Core Idea
The Quran is the meaning; Arabic wording is necessary but can be substituted by Persian in prayer due to istihsan.

This passage tackles a core Hanafi usul question: what exactly is the Quran? Is it the Arabic wording (nazm) plus meaning, or just the meaning? The answer directly affects whether a prayer recited in Persian translation is valid. Imam Abu Hanifa's position, based on a creative interpretation of 'faqra'u ma tayassara min al-quran', allows Persian recitation, but this raises objections about disregarding the Arabic nazm and combining literal and metaphorical usage of the word 'quran'. We will walk through the arguments and see how Hanafi usul resolves them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions on Quran definition
First, recognize the two views: (1) Quran is mere meaning, (2) Quran is meaning + Arabic nazm. The first view leads to the objection that if only meaning matters, then the transmitted Arabic nazm becomes irrelevant. The second view implies that Persian cannot be Quran, so prayer in Persian would not fulfill the obligation.
The Hanafi position is that Quran is the meaning, but the nazm (Arabic wording) is still essential as the original expression.
2
Understand Abu Hanifa's solution: substitution (qaim maqam)
Abu Hanifa treats Persian nazm as a substitute for Arabic nazm. This preserves the requirement of nazm (so the definition 'nazm containing meaning' holds) while allowing non-Arabic recitation. Thus, the objections about disregarding nazm are repelled.
Substitution (qaim maqam) is a key Hanafi concept in rulings of necessity.
3
Analyze the istinbat from Quran 73:20
The verse 'faqra'u ma tayassara min al-quran' is interpreted by Abu Hanifa to mean 'recite what is easy of the Quran's meaning'. The partitive 'min' includes both compositional parts (nazm+meaning) and simple parts (meaning alone). The word 'tayassara' (ease) indicates that simple meaning qualifies.
This interpretation is based on the Hanafi principle that 'amm is generally binding and partitive 'min' does not specify quantity.
4
Address the objection of jama' bayna al-haqiqah wa al-majaz
Objectors claim that using 'quran' for both Arabic and Persian combines literal and metaphorical meanings, which is not allowed. The Hanafi response: the literal meaning is intended by ibarat al-nass (direct text) and the metaphorical by dalalat al-nass (implication), so they are from different perspectives and no combination occurs.
Alternatively, the word 'quran' is used in a general metaphorical sense for any nazm indicating meaning, avoiding literal-metaphor combination.
5
Examine the scope and later scholarly caution
The permissibility is limited to Persian according to some (because it is close to Arabic or the language of Paradise). Later scholars, as a precaution, require sajdah tilawah for Persian recitation and prohibit touching a Persian Quran without wudu, showing that they consider meaning still carries some Quranic sanctity.
This shows a development: early scholars allowed Persian, later scholars added restrictions out of respect.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion on the definition of Quran and its implications for recitation in prayer, specifically regarding Persian translation. The author presents two positions: (1) Quran is mere meaning, and (2) Quran consists of meaning and Arabic wording (nazm). The first position leads to objections about disregarding nazm, but the Imam (Abu Hanifa) resolves this by treating Persian nazm as a substitute (qaim maqam) for Arabic nazm, preserving the requirement of nazm while allowing non-Arabic recitation in prayer. The second position holds nazm essential, but the Imam interprets the verse 'faqra'u ma tayassara min al-quran' (recite what is easy from the Quran) as referring to meaning alone, allowing Persian recitation. The passage then addresses the objection of combining literal and metaphorical usage (jama' bayna al-haqiqah wa al-majaz) when using 'quran' for both Arabic and Persian. The answer clarifies that the two meanings are intended from different perspectives (literal by textual expression, metaphorical by implication), so no combination occurs. The discussion extends to non-Arabic languages: some limit the permissibility to Persian due to its eloquence or as the language of Paradise. Finally, regarding junub and ha'id, it is impermissible to habitually recite in Persian as it disrespects the Quran. Later scholars, as a precaution, apply rules like sajdah tilawah to Persian recitation, but early scholars did not address this. The author concludes that nazm as a pillar was not required by early scholars for the permissibility of prayer.

Invalid Sales Due to Price Uncertainty and Invalid Conditions
Explains two invalid sales and their causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two reasons a sale becomes fasid when a free person or an exception is involved, and apply the Hanafi principles of bay' bi al-hissah and shart fasid to evaluate similar contracts.

Core Idea
Including a non-saleable item or excepting an item from a lump-sum sale creates price uncertainty and an invalid condition, voiding the entire contract.

This passage from Al-Talwih commentary examines two sales that look valid but are legally corrupt (fasid) in the Hanafi school. The first involves selling a free person together with a slave; the second involves selling two slaves while excepting one. Both fail because they introduce uncertainty in the price and a condition that contradicts the nature of the contract. As you read the Arabic, focus on how the author uses the concepts of 'bay' bi al-hissah' (sale by share) and 'shart fasid' (invalid condition) to invalidate these transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Read the passage and note the two cases: (a) selling a free person with a slave for one price, (b) selling two slaves and excepting one from the sale (istithna').
Both are discussed together to highlight parallel invalidities.
2
Extract the two legal flaws (illal)
For each case, the text gives two reasons: (i) it becomes a bay' bi al-hissah initially, causing ignorance of the price (jahl al-thaman); (ii) it includes a condition (shart) that is contrary to the contract's requirements (shart fasid) by making acceptance of what is not sold a condition for acceptance of the sold item.
These are the operative causes of corruption.
3
Distinguish real vs. analogous exception
The author notes that the first case (free person) is not a true istithna' but resembles it, whereas the second case (slave exception) is a true istithna'. Understand why: in the first, the free person is inherently unsaleable, so the 'exception' is implied; in the second, there is an explicit exclusion.
This distinction affects the reasoning but not the ruling both are fasid.
4
Apply the ruling on bay' bi al-hissah
Recall that in Hanafi school, a sale by share (allocating price to each item) is invalid at inception (ibtida'an) but becomes valid if it arises subsequently (ba'd al-'aqd). Here it occurs at the start, causing invalidity.
The text says: 'bay' bi al-hissah ibtida'an batil, wa in kan al-baqa' sahih'.
5
Conclude the overall hukm
Both sales are ruled fasid (corrupt) in the Hanafi school. The two reasons apply to both cases, despite the difference in the nature of the exception.
Fasid here means the contract is not void (batil) but cannot be enforced as is.

Commentary
This passage discusses two related issues in Hanafi sales law. The first issue is selling a free person together with a slave in one contract for a single price. The second issue is selling two slaves for a lump sum while excepting one of them. In both cases, the sale is corrupt (fasid) for two reasons. First, the price becomes uncertain because it must be allocated proportionally to each item, but since one item is not actually a valid subject of sale (the free person in the first case, the excepted slave in the second), the price division leads to ignorance of the price at the time of the contract. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three positions on the default meaning of amr and apply the Hanafi dalil from the Quran to argue that amr indicates wujub.

Core Idea
The command (amr) by default demands obedience; the Quran threatens those who disobey, proving obligation, not mere permission.

In this lesson, we explore a foundational usul dispute: what does a command (amr) imply by default? The Hanafi school, relying on clear Quranic texts, argues for obligation (wujub). We will examine the proofs from Surah al-Nur and al-Ahzab, and contrast them with the opposing views that hold for permission (ibahah) or recommendation (nadb). Understanding this ikhtilaf is key to deriving rulings correctly from primary texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Central Issue
Recognize that the disputed point is the default legal implication (muqtada) of the command (amr) when no external evidence (qarinah) is present. The majority agrees amr has one meaning, but they differ on what it is.
The source states: 'امر کا موجب و مقتضاء ایک ہے' (The command has one required meaning).
2
State the Three Positions
List the three views: some Malikis hold ibahah (permission), the Mu'tazilah and one Shafi'i view hold nadb (recommendation), and the majority (including Hanafis) hold wujub (obligation). Understand the reasoning behind each.
3
Analyze the Hanafi Proof: First Verse
Examine Surah al-Nur 24:63: 'So let those beware who oppose his command, lest a trial strike them or a painful punishment.' The threat of fitna or 'adhab alim for opposing the command shows it is obligatory, because such a threat is only warranted for abandoning an obligatory act.
The passage argues: 'فلیحذر...' implies fear of punishment, which would be meaningless if the command were not wajib.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Proof: Second Verse
Examine Surah al-Ahzab 33:36: 'It is not for a believing man or woman, when Allah and His Messenger have decreed a matter, to have any choice in their matter.' The negation of choice (khayar) indicates compulsion, which is the essence of wujub. The passage also clarifies that 'qada' here means a verbal command, not a creative act, because it is attributed to both Allah and the Messenger.
The phrase 'اذا قضى الله ورسوله امرا' shows the decree is legislative and binding.
5
Understand the Opposing Arguments and Hanafi Response
The ibahah view argues that the lowest certain level of request is permission; the nadb view argues that any request implies at least preference. The Hanafi response is that the threat of punishment and denial of choice in these verses raise the level to obligation, overriding the minimal certainties.
The thamarah al-khilaf: commands without qarinah are wajib in Hanafi, but might be mandub or mubah in other schools.

Commentary
This passage examines the dispute regarding the default legal implication (muqtada) of the command (amr) in usul al-fiqh. The majority of scholars agree that amr has a single meaning, but they differ on what it is: some Malikis hold that it indicates ibahah (permission) because the lowest certain level of request is permission; the Mu'tazilah and one opinion of Imam al-Shafi'i hold that it indicates nadb (recommendation) because any request implies at least a preference for action over abandonment; but the Hanafi majority, and most scholars, hold that amr indicates wujub (obligation) by default. The Hanafi proof is based on two Quranic verses: first, Surah al-Nur 24:63, which threatens those who oppose the Prophet's command with fitna or painful punishment—such a threat is only appropriate for an obligation; second, Surah al-Ahzab 33:36, which denies believers any choice when Allah and His Messenger decree a matter, implying compulsion. The passage further analyzes the word 'qada' in the second verse, arguing that it means a verbal command, not a creative act, because it is attributed to both Allah and the Messenger. This supports the interpretation that the verse refers to legislative commands, which carry binding force.

Key Terms
امر (amr): A command; a request for an action from a superior to an inferior.
موجب (mūjib): The required or implied meaning of a word, here of the command.
اباحہ (ibāḥah): Permissibility; the act is allowed with no preference.
ندب (nadb): Recommendation; the act is encouraged but not obligatory.
وجوب (wujūb): Obligation; the act must be performed, and abandonment is punishable.
قضاء (qaḍā'): Decree or judgment; in the verse, it refers to a verbal command (qawl), not a creative act.

Permanence of Obligation Despite Exit of Time: The Hanafi Usul of Qada and Extension by Qiyas
Deriving a core usul principle from Quran, hadith, and qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the Hanafi principle that obligations due to a cause are not dropped by exit of time, apply it to qada, and extend it to vowed acts and i'tikaf using qiyas.

Core Idea
An obligation triggered by a cause remains; only the similar act is required in qada; loss of time virtue has no compensatory duty.

This lesson explores a foundational Hanafi usul principle: when an obligation becomes due because of a cause (sabab), it is not dropped merely because the specified time has passed. Instead, the obligated person must perform a similar act (mithl) as qada. This principle is derived from the Quran and hadith, and extended by qiyas to vows and i'tikaf. We will walk through the proofs and the analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the principle and its exceptions
Identify the core rule: an obligation due to a cause does not drop with exit of time. Note the exceptions: Friday and Eid prayers cannot be made up after their time.
The principle is general for all wajibat except those with specific conditions that cannot be replicated.
2
Examine the textual proofs
Analyze the Quranic verse (2:184) and the hadith on missed prayer. Understand how they show that only the similar act is required, not compensation for lost virtue.
The verse 'fa'iddatun min ayyamin ukhar' is key; the hadith uses 'falyusalliha idha dhakara'.
3
Address the potential objection
Consider the critique that these texts create a new obligation. The author responds that they affirm the continuation of the original obligation, not a new one.
This clarifies that qada is not a separate duty but the same duty carried over.
4
Extend the principle by qiyas
Apply analogical reasoning to vowed acts (nazr) and i'tikaf. Since the rule holds for fasting and prayer, it holds for other acts triggered by a cause.
Qiyas is used to manifest the inclusion of these cases in the same textual proof.
5
Apply to the specific case of i'tikaf in Ramadan
Resolve the scenario where a person vows i'tikaf in Ramadan but misses it. Understand why the specific fast drops and then returns, and why qada is immediate without waiting for the next Ramadan.
The virtue of Ramadan overrides the specific fasting requirement, but after loss of that virtue, the original vow reasserts.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Ta'liq al-Fasih explains a core usul principle: obligations that become due by a cause do not lapse merely with the passage of time. The author begins by stating the rule: when an obligation (wajib) is necessitated by a cause (sabab), it is not dropped when its time exits. The obligated person must fulfill it by performing a similar act (mithl) of what was missed. The exception is obligations like Friday prayer and Eid prayers, which cannot be made up at all after their time. The 'virtue of time' (sharaf al-waqt) is lost without compensation (daman), meaning there is no requirement to compensate for missing the preferred time; only sin accrues if the delay was deliberate. The author adduces the Quranic verse 'fa'iddatun min ayyamin ukhar' (al-Baqarah 2:184) and the hadith 'Whoever forgets a prayer, let him pray it when he remembers' as proofs that only the similar act is required in qada, not compensation for lost virtue. A potential objection is addressed: if the Quran and hadith command qada, doesn't that create a new obligation for qada? The author clarifies that these texts do not create a new obligation; they merely affirm the continuation of the original obligation that was not dropped. Next, the author extends this principle to vowed acts (nazr) and i'tikaf via qiyas. A specific case is considered: a person vows i'tikaf in Ramadan. He performs the Ramadan fasts but not the i'tikaf. May he make up the i'tikaf in another Ramadan? The answer is complex. The vow of i'tikaf in Ramadan requires fasting specific to i'tikaf. However, in the first Ramadan, that specific fasting is dropped because the Ramadan fast (fard) is superior and fulfills the requirement. But once that Ramadan has passed, the virtue of that time is gone, and the return to the original vow is possible only after a long period (from Shawwal to next Ramadan). Since the specific fast can be performed in any month, the vow returns to its original rule, necessitating the specific fast for qada. The author concludes that obliging qada without waiting for the next Ramadan (i.e., without regard for lost virtue) is more cautious (ahwat) than obliging it with the virtue of time. This demonstrates how usul principles like baqa' al-hukm (continuation of ruling) and qiyas are applied to derive detailed fiqh rulings.

Key Terms
قضاء (qada'): Making up a missed obligatory act after its time has passed.
سبب (sabab): The cause that makes an obligation due, such as the onset of Ramadan for fasting or the vow (nazr) for i'tikaf.
مثل (mithl): The similar act that is required in place of the missed obligation, such as praying two rak'ahs for a missed prayer.
شرف الوقت (sharaf al-waqt): The virtue or merit of performing an act within its appointed time.
ضمان (daman): Compensation or guarantee; here specifically the legal responsibility to make up for lost virtue of time.
نذر (nazr): A vow to perform an act of worship, making it obligatory upon oneself.
اعتكاف (i'tikaf): Seclusion in the mosque for worship, which according to Hanafi school requires fasting.

Types of Performance in Hanafi Usul: Ada Mahd and Shabih bil-Qada
Classifying performance into pure performance (complete and deficient) and performance resembling qada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ada mahd kamil, ada mahd qasir, and shabih bil-qada, and apply these categories to the prayer of the masbuq and lahiq.

Core Idea
Performance in time can be pure (complete or deficient) or resemble qada when the group obligation is lost.

In Hanafi usul, how we perform a commanded act affects its legal classification. This lesson explores the finer categories of performance (ada) beyond just 'on time' vs 'make-up.' You will learn that even when performed within its time, an act can be classified as complete, deficient, or resembling qada depending on whether its prescribed attributes are fulfilled. This distinction is crucial for understanding rulings on congregational prayer and how changes during prayer affect obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the commanded act and its prescribed attribute
First, determine the act (e.g., obligatory prayer) and how it was legislated (e.g., with congregation). The attribute is part of the commanded act.
Hanafi usul: the manner of performance (وصف) is integral to the command.
2
Classify as ada or qada
If the act is performed within its designated time, it is ada. If after the time, it is qada. This lesson focuses on ada.
Time is the primary factor for ada.
3
Check if the attribute matches legislation
If performed exactly as commanded (e.g., prayer in congregation), it is ada kamil (complete pure performance). If not (e.g., praying alone), it is ada qasir (deficient pure performance).
The masbuq's leftover portion prayed alone is also ada qasir because the attribute of congregation is missing for some rak'ahs.
4
Identify when ada resembles qada
If the act is performed within its time (hence ada) but the original commitment to perform with the group has been lost (e.g., the lahiq, who misses the end of congregation), it is shabih bil-qada. It is ada in time but qada in terms of the group attribute.
The lahiq completes the remainder individually but with the intention of completing the same prayer; the lost group attribute makes it resemble qada.
5
Apply to the traveler scenario
A traveler praying qasr (2 rak'ahs) behind a traveler imam becomes resident mid-prayer. If the imam finishes before the follower returns, the follower completes as traveler (2 rak'ahs) because qada is not changed by new circumstances. If the imam hasn't finished, the follower completes as resident (4 rak'ahs) because the ongoing act is still ada, and residence lifts the concession.
This shows how the category (ada/qada) affects the number of rak'ahs.

Niyyat al-Iqamah: Distinguishing Masbuq and Lahiq in Hanafi Usul
Explains effect of niyyat al-iqamah on masbuq and lahiq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of 'iltizam' (undertaking) to determine whether a missed portion of prayer is adaa or qada, and to distinguish the rulings for masbuq and lahiq regarding niyyat al-iqamah.

Core Idea
What was not undertaken cannot be qada; what was undertaken cannot be changed by niyyat al-iqamah.

In this lesson, we examine a subtle but crucial Hanafi usul distinction: why a masbuq (latecomer) can transform his missed rak'ahs into adaa by making niyyat al-iqamah, while a lahiq (one who returns after a valid excuse) cannot. The key lies in the concept of 'iltizam'—the legal undertaking to perform the prayer with the imam. By tracing this principle, you will understand how the school derives rulings based on the nature of one's connection to the congregation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories: masbuq and lahiq
In the passage, two distinct types of followers are described: the masbuq (one who joins late) and the lahiq (one who had an excuse and later continues). Determine the defining feature of each—namely, whether they had undertaken (iltizam) to perform the entire prayer with the imam.
Masbuq has no prior undertaking for the missed portion; lahiq had undertaken the full prayer.
2
Locate the usul principle: effect of iltizam on adaa vs qada
The passage states that for the masbuq, the missed portion is adaa because 'he had not undertaken to perform with the imam in that amount.' For the lahiq, the missed portion after the event is qada because he had undertaken. Identify this as the operative usul principle.
The ruling hinges on whether the follower had committed to performing that part with the imam.
3
Apply the principle to niyyat al-iqamah
Niyyat al-iqamah is an intention to complete the prayer individually after the imam finishes. For the masbuq, this intention intervenes upon the un-undertaken portion, making it adaa. For the lahiq, the undertaking prevents this transformation; his missed portion remains qada.
The intention cannot override a pre-existing legal obligation (iltizam).
4
Consider the exception: lahiq's speech and new intention
The passage adds that if a lahiq speaks and then makes niyyat al-iqamah, he becomes a musta'nif (one who starts anew). In that case, his entire prayer becomes adaa. This shows that breaking the original undertaking by speech allows the intention to operate.
This exception reinforces the rule: once the undertaking is nullified, the intention can work.

The Rule of Qada: Rational Like vs. Irrational Like
States and proves the usul qa'ida that qada is only with like if rationally conceivable, else needs text.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to APPLY the Hanafi rule that qada of a missed act of worship is required only if the likeness is rationally conceivable, and identify cases where qada is imposed by textual command despite an irrational like.

Core Idea
If a missed worship has a rationally conceivable like, it is made up in that like; otherwise, qada is not required without a textual command.

This passage introduces a foundational principle of Hanafi usul al-fiqh regarding qada, the making up of missed acts of worship. The core question is: must the missed act be performed in an exactly similar manner (like), or are there cases where a different compensation suffices? The rule distinguishes between acts where the intellect can grasp the similarity (like for like) and acts where it cannot. In the latter, qada is only obligatory if the Lawgiver explicitly commands it via a text (nass). We will unpack this rule, examine its evidence from the Qur'an, and see how it applies to real cases like ta'dil al-arkan in prayer and quality attributes in zakat.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two types of 'like'
Identify that the passage divides qada into two categories: qada with a rationally conceivable like (mithl ma'qul) and qada with a rationally inconceivable like (mithl ghayr ma'qul). The first is accepted by intellect, e.g., praying a missed prayer with another prayer. The second is not, e.g., paying fidya for a missed fast.
The intellect's inability to grasp similarity does not mean the act is invalid; it means the default rule is no qada unless specified.
2
The default rule: no qada without nass for irrational like
The passage states: 'كل ما لا يعقل له مثل قربة لا يقضى إلا بنص' – every act of worship for which a rational like is not conceivable is not made up except by a text. That is the qa'ida.
Recognize this as an exception to the general principle that missed acts must be compensated.
3
Find the textual proof (nass) for exceptional cases
The author lists examples like standing at Arafat, throwing pebbles, sacrifice, and Takbirat al-Tashriq. Though their like is irrational, qada is required because of textual command. Analyze the specific proof for Takbirat al-Tashriq: the Qur'anic verses (7:205 and 7:55) show that the original rule for dhikr is quietness, so the loud Takbirat al-Tashriq is a departure from the norm, making it time-specific and qada-able only by text.
This demonstrates how a verse can establish a default ruling that is later modified for a specific case.
4
Apply the rule to dependent attributes (wasf)
The passage applies the rule to ta'dil al-arkan (proper posture) in prayer. If prayer was performed without proper posture, one cannot make up just the posture because it is an attribute (wasf) that does not stand alone. Making it up with the original prayer would void the original (since you cannot repeat an attribute without the act). Therefore, no qada for ta'dil alone.
Similarly, if one paid defective dirhams for zakat, the attribute of quality is not made up alone.

The author provides examples: Standing at Arafat, throwing pebbles, sacrifice, and Takbirat al-Tashriq are acts whose qada is not rationally conceivable but are required due to nass. Takbirat al-Tashriq specifically are acts of worship prescribed in a particular time; the original rule for dhikr is softness, as per Quranic verses (Q. 7:205, Q. 7:55). Then the passage applies the rule to ta'dil al-arkan (proper posture in prayer): if one prays without ta'dil, one cannot make up just the ta'dil because ta'dil is an attribute (wasf) that does not stand alone; making it up with the original prayer would negate the original, which is invalid. Similarly, if one gave defective dirhams for zakat, the attribute of good quality is not made up alone because it is a dependent attribute.

Key Terms
مثل معقول (mithl ma'qul): A like that is rationally conceivable, i.e., the intellect can comprehend the similarity between the original and the replacement.
مثل غير معقول (mithl ghayr ma'qul): A like that is not rationally conceivable; the intellect cannot understand the similarity.
قربة (qurba): An act of worship intended to draw near to Allah, often time-specific.
وصف (wasf): An attribute or quality that does not stand alone as an independent entity.

Qada Yushbih al-Ada: The Third Type of Substitute Performance
Defining the third type of qada with an example from Eid prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qada yushbih al-ada, identify its conditions, and apply it to a scenario from Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
When an act has no exact substitute, a resembling performance suffices to fulfill the original demand.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, qada (substitute performance) is divided into three categories. The first two are qada bi al-mithl al-ma'qul (rationally understood equivalent) and qada bi al-mithl ghayr al-ma'qul (non-rational equivalent, textually prescribed). This lesson introduces the third category: qada yushbih al-ada, where the substitute resembles the original act in some aspect but is not an exact match. This concept is essential for understanding how certain acts can be performed when their original conditions are missed.

Commentary
This passage defines a third category of qada (substitute performance) in Hanafi fiqh: qada yushbih al-ada (qada that resembles the original performance). The author first reminds of the two earlier categories: qada bi al-mithl al-ma'qul (substituting with a rationally equivalent act, e.g., substituting prayer with prayer) and qada bi al-mithl ghayr al-ma'qul (substituting with an act that is not rationally equivalent, e.g., substituting fasting with fidyah, which is textually prescribed). The third category applies when the original act itself cannot be substituted, but there is a similar act that can be performed at the missed time. The example given is the extra takbirs in the Eid prayer. If a person joins the prayer late and finds the imam in ruku, he should say the takbirs while bowing because ruku resembles qiyam (standing) in certain aspects, allowing the takbirs to be performed in a modified way. This is not a full qada (since takbirs have no independent qada), but it is a performance that resembles the original (ada).

Key Terms
قضاء (qada): Substitute performance of an act of worship after its time has passed or when the original form cannot be completed.
قضاء يشبه الأداء (qada yushbih al-ada): The third type of qada where the substitute act resembles the original performance in form or intent, used when the exact substitute is not available.
