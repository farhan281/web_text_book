Conditions for Valid Proof and the Place of Taqlid in Usul al-Fiqh
Lists conditions for proof validity and argues that usul al-fiqh includes muqallids.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a dalil to establish a ruling and explain why some Hanafi usulis include taqlid within usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
A proof is valid only if it is not abrogated, has no stronger opponent, and does not contradict earlier ijmā'; usul also applies to muqallids.

This passage from the commentary on al-Tawdih and al-Talwih lays out the prerequisites for a Sharʿī proof to yield a ruling. It then addresses a key question: Is usul al-fiqh only for mujtahids, or can it also serve muqallids? The discussion reveals a broader understanding of 'tawassul' that encompasses both.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions for a valid dalil
Find the explicit conditions: the proof must not be abrogated (mansukh) and there must be no equal or stronger opposing proof (mu'arid).
These are foundational in Hanafi usul.
2
Understand the condition regarding qiyas
If the proof is a qiyas, it must not contradict the composite ijmā' of earlier mujtahids. However, if the issue was never subject to ijtihad or only one opinion exists, opposition is allowed.
Composite ijmā' arises when multiple opinions exist, restricting later mujtahids.
3
Recognize the universal rule of usul
The definition of usul al-fiqh includes a major premise (kubra) or concomitant, which must satisfy the above conditions. Knowledge of these conditions constitutes knowledge of the universal rule itself.
The universal rule is the core of usul.
4
Examine the debate on tawassul
The term 'tawassul' (attaining fiqh) in the definition of usul may seem limited to mujtahids, as they use the four proofs. The muqallid's proof is the statement of the imam, not those four.
This is the strict view.
5
Evaluate the expansion to include taqlid
Some scholars argue that tawassul applies to muqallids as well because they also use a universal premise (e.g., 'Everything Abu Hanifa says is correct'). Thus, taqlid discussions logically belong in usul.
This expansive view is supported by examples from Hanafi works.

The author said in al-Tawdih: 'He said: The proof is that every ruling from the Shar'i proofs...' (meaning) a ruling is established by each proof only when it fulfills the conditions that are mentioned in their proper places. Those conditions are: that the proof is not abrogated, and that there is no opponent equal or stronger to it, and the qiyas (analogy) should be such that no mujtahid among the earlier mujtahids reached that qiyas. If it opposes the consensus of all mujtahids, it becomes invalid. For example, if there is a difference of opinion among mujtahids on an issue, and several opinions appear, then that becomes a composite ijma (that only these opinions are possible). Now a later mujtahid performing ijtihad on that issue cannot go beyond those earlier opinions; if he opposes all of them, his statement is not correct. But this is when the issue has already been subject to ijtihad and opinions; if the issue was never subject to ijtihad before, or only one mujtahid's opinion exists, then the later mujtahid may oppose it without doubt. This is what the commentator (sharh) said in al-Talwih. So the aforementioned universal rule of usul al-fiqh, whether used as a major premise in a proof or as a universal concomitant (mulazama), can be true in its universal form only when it includes these mentioned restrictions. Hence, the knowledge of the discussions related to these restrictions is in reality the knowledge of this universal rule itself, which is one of the premises (the major or the concomitant) of the proof of fiqhi issues. Therefore, all these discussions are part of the issues of usul al-fiqh. Our saying 'tawassul biha' (attaining fiqh through usul) in the definition of usul al-fiqh apparently shows that this attaining is specific to the mujtahid. Because the topics investigated in this science are the rules by which the mujtahid attains fiqh. And the one who attains fiqh is only the mujtahid, because fiqh is the knowledge of rulings derived from proofs, and the proof of the muqallid is not counted among the four proofs. For this reason, discussions of taqlid are not mentioned in our books of Hanafi usul al-fiqh. But it is not far-fetched to say that 'tawassul ila al-fiqh' is general for both the mujtahid and the muqallid, meaning that the muqallid can also attain fiqh. However, through the four proofs, only the mujtahid attains it. As for the muqallid, his proof is the statement of the mujtahid. For example, the muqallid says: 'This ruling is established in my view because it is the opinion of Abu Hanifa, and everything that is the opinion of Abu Hanifa is established in my view.' So the second universal proposition (everything that is the opinion of Abu Hanifa is established) would be considered part of usul al-fiqh. This is why some scholars have mentioned the discussions of taqlid and istifta in usul books. Therefore, in this case, when the attaining (tawassul) also occurs for the muqallid, and the discussions of taqlid and istifta are included in usul books, then the definition of usul al-fiqh would be: 'the knowledge of...' (incomplete).

Commentary
This passage is from a commentary on al-Tawdih by Sadr al-Shariah, with expansions from al-Talwih by al-Taftazani. It begins by stating conditions for a proof (dalil) to establish a ruling: it must not be abrogated, not have an equal or stronger opponent, and the qiyas (analogical reasoning) must not contradict the ijmā' of earlier mujtahids. If previous mujtahids have expressed opinions on an issue, a later mujtahid cannot go beyond those opinions (composite ijmā'). However, if the issue was never subject to ijtihad or only one opinion exists, opposition is allowed. Then the text discusses the definition of usul al-fiqh: the term 'tawassul' (attaining) in the definition could be specific to mujtahids, as only they use the four proofs (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas). But the author argues that it can also include muqallids, because a muqallid uses the statement of the imam as a proof, and the universal rule 'Everything that Abu Hanifa says is right' serves as a major premise. Thus, some scholars include discussions of taqlid and istifta in usul works. The passage concludes that if this view is accepted, the definition of usul al-fiqh would encompass both mujtahid and muqallid.

Key Terms
دليل (dalil): A proof from the Shar'i sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas) that establishes a ruling.
منسوخ (mansukh): A proof that has been abrogated by a later revelation; such a proof cannot establish a ruling.
معارض (mu'arid): An opposing proof that is either equal in strength (musawi) or stronger (rajih). A ruling cannot be established if there is an effective mu'arid.
تقليد (taqlid): Following the opinion of a mujtahid without knowing the evidence; the muqallid's proof is the mujtahid's statement.

Tasdiq Diyanatan wa Qadaan: Accepting Claims of Opposite Intention
Explains the ruling on accepting claims of opposite intention in conditional manumission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of tasdiq diyanatan wa qadaan to determine whether a claim of intending the opposite meaning is accepted religiously, judicially, or both.

Core Idea
A speaker's claim to intend the opposite is accepted in court only if it makes his outcome stricter; if it reduces his burden, only religiously accepted.

In Hanafi usul, not all claims are treated equally by the judge. When a person makes a conditional statement and later claims he meant the opposite, the court must decide whether to believe him. This lesson unpacks the rule: if the claimed meaning is stricter for the speaker, he is believed both diyanatan (before Allah) and qadaan (in court). If it reduces his burden, he is believed only diyanatan due to suspicion of lying.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original statement and the claimed opposite meaning
For example, the speaker said 'in milkatu 'abdi fahuwa hurr' and later claims he meant 'in ishtaraytu' (a stricter condition because it frees the slave). Or he said 'in ishtaraytu' and claims he meant 'in milkatu' (a lighter condition).
The literal meaning is the default; the claimed meaning is the intended opposite.
2
Determine whether the claimed meaning makes the speaker's outcome stricter or lighter
Compare the legal effect under the literal vs claimed meaning. If the claimed meaning leads to a greater obligation or loss for the speaker (e.g., freeing the slave when it would not have been freed), it is stricter. If it reduces his obligation or loss, it is lighter.
Stricter = the speaker gains no advantage; lighter = he avoids a burden.
3
Apply the rule: stricter leads to tasdiq diyanatan wa qadaan; lighter leads to tasdiq diyanatan only
If the claimed meaning is stricter, the speaker is believed both religiously and judicially because he is not suspected of lying. If it is lighter, he is believed only religiously (diyanatan) because the court suspects he is lying to avoid the consequence.
Judicial acceptance requires lack of suspicion (tuhma).
4
Understand the linguistic justification: isti'arah (metaphor) from cause to effect or vice versa
The claim is based on a valid metaphor: applying the name of the cause (ishra) to the effect (milk) or vice versa. This metaphor is linguistically sound regardless of legal acceptance, but the legal consequence depends on the benefit to the speaker.
The validity of the metaphor does not guarantee judicial acceptance.

Commentary
This passage is the second part of the discussion, focusing on the principle of tasdiq (acceptance of a claim) in legal contexts when a speaker claims to have intended the opposite of the literal meaning. The author clarifies that the slave manumission case was a preface; the real objective is to explain when such claims are accepted. The rule: If the speaker's claimed intention leads to a stricter outcome for himself (like freeing the slave when it would otherwise not be free), he is believed both diyanatan (before Allah and in conscience) and qadaan (in court) because he is not suspected of lying. But if his claimed intention leads to a reduction in his own burden (like not freeing the slave when the literal would have freed), he is believed only diyanatan, not qadaan, due to suspicion of lying. The author then ties this back to the earlier discussion of isti'arah: even in the reduction case where the claim is not accepted judicially, the linguistic metaphor (applying cause to effect or vice versa) remains valid in itself; its validity is unaffected by legal consequences. The final part connects to the earlier principle that the application of a derived term is valid only when the derived-from meaning is present, suggesting that the metaphorical transfer must be based on a genuine cause-effect relationship established in language and law.

Key Terms
تصديق ديانة (tasdiq diyanatan): Accepting a person's claim as true in religious and moral terms, regardless of legal evidence.
تصديق قضاء (tasdiq qadaan): Accepting a person's claim in court based on evidence and lack of suspicion of lying.

Distinguishing Isti'ara from Itlaq al-Sabab 'ala al-Musabbab
Verification: examples are isti'ara, not itlaq al-sabab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish isti'ara from itlaq ism al-sabab 'ala al-musabbab in usul al-fiqh and evaluate the condition of sama' for majaz according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Not every causal relationship yields itlaq al-sabab; these examples are isti'ara via necessary concomitance, and sama' of types suffices for creative metaphor.

This passage resolves a common confusion in Hanafi usul: are legal metaphors like using 'mahr' for marriage instances of applying the cause to the effect, or are they metaphorical transfers based on necessary concomitance? The author clarifies they are isti'ara and then addresses a critical debate about whether specific metaphorical usages must be transmitted from the Arabs or only the type of relationship.

Guided Path

The passage contrasts two ways of interpreting the examples: (a) itlaq ism al-sabab 'ala al-musabbab (applying the name of the cause to the effect) and (b) isti'ara (metaphor based on a shared attribute). Your task is to note the author's claim that these are isti'ara, not the former.
The key difference: itlaq al-sabab requires a direct causal link; isti'ara only needs a necessary concomitant (lazim).

Understand the reasoning for rejecting itlaq al-sabab
The author argues that mahr is not a cause for marriage's rights (like divorce powers), and bay' is not a cause for the full ownership rights in marriage. Therefore, the relationship is not causal but one of necessary concomitance, which justifies isti'ara.
Test each example: does the alleged cause actually produce the effect uniquely? If not, it's isti'ara.

Recognize the one-way nature of isti'ara
Isti'ara is unidirectional: using 'asad' for 'shuja'' is valid, but not vice versa. The author uses this to show that if these were itlaq al-sabab, they should be reversible, but they are not (except the genuine causal pair bay' and milk).
Reversibility is a test: if it works both ways, it's likely a true causal relationship; if only one way, it's isti'ara.

Commentary
This final passage provides a verification (tahqiq) that the examples discussed earlier—marriage with the word mahr, divorce with the word 'ishq, and ijarah with the word bay'—are all instances of isti'ara (metaphor), not of itlaq ism al-sabab 'ala al-musabbab (using the name of the cause for the effect). The reason is that mahr is not the cause of the rights that come with marriage, such as the power to divorce. Similarly, bay' is not the cause of the full ownership rights that come with marriage. The shared attribute is only a necessary concomitant (lazim), which justifies isti'ara. The passage reiterates that isti'ara is one-sided, like using 'asad' for 'shuja' but not vice versa. However, the example of bay' and milk (ownership) is valid both ways because bay' is truly the cause and milk is the effect. The passage then discusses the condition of sam' (hearing from language authorities) for majaz: some hold that only the types of relationships need to be heard, not specific instances, allowing for creative metaphor; others require hearing of specific instances. The text ends with an incomplete example about 'nasl' (offspring).

Key Terms
اطلاق اسم المسبب على السبب (itlaq ism al-musabbab 'ala al-sabab): Using the name of the effect for the cause; a type of majaz where the relationship is causal.
سماع (sama'): Linguistic transmission; the condition that a usage must have been heard from the people of language to be considered valid.
استعارة (isti'ara): Metaphor; a word used for a meaning different from its original due to a shared attribute, when that attribute is a necessary concomitant (lazim).
