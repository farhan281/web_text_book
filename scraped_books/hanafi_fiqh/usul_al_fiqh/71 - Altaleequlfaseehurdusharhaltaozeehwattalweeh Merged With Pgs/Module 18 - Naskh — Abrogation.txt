Huddām: Does a Second Marriage Nullify Previous Divorces?
Explains Hanafi vs Shafi'i on huddām for less than three talags

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi and Shafi'i interpretations of the Quranic ghaya in verse 2:230 to determine whether a second marriage after less than three talags creates a new halal status.

Core Idea
The meaning of 'hatta' determines whether a second marriage after divorce resets the count of talags.

The concept of huddām arises when a divorced woman remarries another husband and later returns to her first husband. While both schools agree that after three talags a second marriage creates a new halal, they differ when the divorce was less than three. This dispute hinges on the interpretation of the Quranic phrase 'hatta tankiha' (until she marries another husband). In this lesson, we will analyze the usul principle that generates this difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
What is the specific scenario that causes disagreement? The passage describes a woman divorced with less than three talags (one or two), who marries a second husband, divorces, then returns to the first husband.
The agreed scenario (three talags) is not disputed.
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
Hanafis hold that any valid second marriage creates a new halal, nullifying all previous divorce power. They cite the Quranic 'hatta' as establishing a new permissibility.
They view the ghaya as not only ending prohibition but also initiating a new legal status.
3
State the Shafi'i position and its proof
Shafi'is restrict huddām to three talags only. They interpret 'hatta' strictly as a limit that ends an existing prohibition; if no prohibition exists (less than three), the limit has no effect.
The Shafi'i argue that the verse does not create a new halal; it only removes an existing one.
4
Analyze the usul principle: the scope of the ghaya
Both schools treat 'hatta' as a ghaya. The difference lies in whether a ghaya can establish a new ruling beyond ending the previous one. Hanafis see the verse as implying a new halal; Shafi'is do not.
This reflects a broader usul disagreement about the function of ghaya in legislative texts.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: Under Hanafi, after one divorce and remarriage, the first husband gets full three talags again. Under Shafi'i, he retains the remaining two talags.
This affects the finality of divorce and the possibility of irrevocable separation.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute about the concept of huddām (demolition) in divorce law. Hanafis hold that any valid marriage with a second husband after divorce (whether three talags or less) creates a new halal status, thus nullifying any remaining divorce power the first husband had. Shafi'is restrict this to only the case of three talags, because the Quranic phrase 'hatta tankiha zawjan ghayrahu' (until she marries another husband) is a ghaya (limit) that only ends an existing prohibition. In less than three talags, there is no complete prohibition, so no huddām occurs. The author notes that this issue is controversial, and he omits further details due to length.

Key Terms
ہدم (huddām): Demolition of previous divorce rights by a new valid marriage, giving full three talags again
حل جدید (hall jadīd): New halal status (permissibility) created by marriage to a second husband, which overrides previous divorce limitations
مادون الثلث (mā dūna al-thalāth): Divorce of one or two talags (less than three)

Naskh Between Two General Verses: The Pregnant Widow's Iddah
Analysis of conflicting verses and companion methodologies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of naskh to resolve a conflict between two general Quranic verses, and distinguish it from tarjih.

Core Idea
When two general verses conflict, the later abrogates the earlier for overlapping cases.

In this passage, we encounter a classic usul problem: two general Quranic verses that both apply to a single case—the pregnant widow. Surah Baqarah (2:234) mandates a four-month ten-day iddah for widows; Surah Talaq (65:4) mandates delivery for pregnant women. How do we reconcile them? The Companion scholars Ibn Mas'ud and Ali offer two different methodologies: naskh (abrogation) and tarjih (preference by taking the longer period). This lesson will guide you through the reasoning and the Hanafi reliance on naskh when revelation order is known.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two General Texts
Locate the two verses: Baqarah 2:234 (general for all widows) and Talaq 65:4 (general for all pregnant women). Recognize that both are 'amm and include the pregnant widow.
General texts (amm) cover all individuals within their scope unless specified otherwise.
2
Recognize the Conflict (Ta'arud)
For the pregnant widow, both verses give different rulings: one says 4 months 10 days, the other says delivery. Since both are general and include her, they conflict on this specific case.
Conflict (ta'arud) occurs when two texts with equal authority give contradictory rulings on the same subject.
3
Apply the Principle of Naskh (Ibn Mas'ud's View)
Determine the revelation order. Ibn Mas'ud asserts that Surah Talaq (the 'short Surah of Nisa') was revealed after Surah Baqarah. Therefore, the later verse (Talaq 65:4) abrogates the earlier (Baqarah 2:234) for the overlapping case. The ruling is delivery.
In Hanafi usul, naskh is valid when the chronology is certain and the texts are of equal authority (both Quran).
4
Consider Ali's Alternative: Tarjih by Longer Period
Ali did not accept naskh here. Instead, he reconciled by giving the pregnant widow the longer of the two periods (ab'adal-ajalayn). If 4 months 10 days is longer, she observes that; if the pregnancy lasts longer, she observes delivery.
Tarjih is a different conflict resolution method, preferring the stronger or more precautionary ruling.
5
Identify the Hanafi Preferred View
The Hanafi school follows Ibn Mas'ud's method: naskh is applied because the revelation order is known and both verses are decisive (qat'i). Thus, for a pregnant widow, the iddah ends with delivery.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Analogy Between Naskh and Sale of Two Slaves
Demonstrates analogy between abrogation and proportional division in sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the analogy of naskh to identify when a sale by share is valid after the loss of part of the subject matter.

Core Idea
When part of a contract subject is lost, the ruling on the rest remains, just as abrogation removes part of a ruling without affecting the remainder.

This passage uses the concept of naskh (abrogation) to explain a fiqh ruling about a sale where one of two items is destroyed before delivery. The Hanafi school distinguishes between an initial sale by share (invalid) and a subsequent one (valid). The analogy deepens your understanding of how legal rulings can be partially removed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base analogy
The author compares the sale case to naskh. Understand what naskh is: removal of an established ruling.
2
Break down the sale case
Two slaves sold for a lump sum; one dies before delivery. What happens to the contract? The contract is rescinded for the deceased slave.
3
Apply the analogy
Just as naskh removes the ruling on one part while leaving the rest, the sale contract is rescinded for the deceased slave but remains valid for the living one proportionally.
The remaining sale is a 'bay' bi al-hissah baqā'an' (subsequent sale by share).
4
Note the subtle point
The remaining sale is not an initial sale by share (invalid) but a subsequent one (valid). The incidental ignorance of price is tolerated.
Incidental jahālah does not invalidate the contract.

Commentary
This passage presents an analogy (qiyas) to naskh (abrogation). The case is: a person sells two slaves for a lump sum of one thousand, but one slave dies in the seller's possession before delivery. In Hanafi law, the sale of the deceased slave is rescinded (faskh), but the sale of the surviving slave remains valid on a proportional basis (bay' bi al-hissah). This is analogous to naskh because initially the sale contract covered both slaves, but after the death of one, the ruling on that one is removed, similar to how a legal ruling is abrogated after being established. Importantly, the remaining sale is not considered a sale by share from the outset (which would be invalid), but rather a subsequent division that arises after the contract's validity, which is permissible. The incidental ignorance of the price (since the price must now be allocated proportionally) does not corrupt the contract because it occurred after the sale was valid.

Key Terms
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a previously established ruling by a later revelation.
بیع بالحصہ بقاءً (bay‘ bi al-ḥiṣṣah baqā’an): A sale by share that arises after the contract's inception, which is valid in Hanafi law.

Khiyar in a Combined Sale: Analogy to Naskh and Istithna'
Analogical derivation of khiyar ruling in combined sale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the analogies of naskh and istithna' to derive the ruling of a khiyar condition in a combined sale and evaluate the four scenarios.

Core Idea
When khiyar is stipulated on one of two items sold together, the ruling depends on whether the subject is included in the offer (naskh side) or excluded from the ruling (istithna' side).

This passage presents a sophisticated usul analysis of khiyar (option) in a combined sale of two items. The Hanafi jurists analogize the situation to two mechanisms: naskh (abrogation) because the option condition effectively removes an item from the contract; and istithna' (exception) because the item is not included in the sale's legal effect. The author then derives rulings for four scenarios based on knowledge of the subject and price, and shows that the perspective taken leads to different conclusions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core legal problem
Understand the scenario: two items sold in a single contract with a condition giving an option to rescind regarding one of them. The key question is whether this condition invalidates the contract.
This is a classic usul problem of conflicting analogies.
2
Understand the two analogies
The condition can be seen as either naskh (abrogating part of the offer) or istithna' (excluding the subject from the legal effect). Recognize that the subject of khiyar is included in the offer but not in the ruling.
Naskh operates on the wording; istithna' operates on the ruling.
3
Apply the analogies to the four scenarios
The passage enumerates four scenarios based on whether the subject of khiyar and its price are known. From the naskh perspective, the sale is valid in all four; from the istithna' perspective, it is invalid. The Hanafi mufta bihi considers both shabahs and rules valid only when both are known.
The first scenario (both known) is the only valid case.
4
Determine the operative ruling
The author concludes that when both subject and price are known, the sale is sahih. This is the mufta bihi. In other scenarios, the sale is fasid due to ignorance or invalid condition.
This ruling reflects the balance between the two analogies.

It was said: When the subject of khiyar is excluded from the sale, accepting the offer regarding what is not sold becomes an invalid condition. Since it is one contract, the buyer accepts with a single acceptance, so making acceptance of the non-sold item a condition corrupts the contract. So this doubt (shubhah) is considered invalid compared to naskh. Unlike the case of a free person and a slave sold together: even if the share of each in the price is stated, according to Abu Hanifah it is invalid because the free person cannot be included in the sale at all, so it is a complete example of istithna'; hence the sale is not valid in any case. And the explanation of its suitability through takhsis is as follows: just as takhsis in terms of the wording resembles naskh, and in terms of ruling resembles istithna', so is this mentioned case. Because the masjid [subject] which has khiyar is included in the offer but not included in the ruling. From the aspect that it is included in the offer, returning it with the khiyar condition is altering the previous contract, so it is like naskh, just as it alters a general ruling. And from the aspect that it was not included in the ruling, returning it with the khiyar condition is an expression that it was not included, so in this regard it is like istithna', just as istithna' expresses the non-inclusion of the excepted item. And when it has two resemblances, this case becomes like takhsis, which has a resemblance to naskh and a resemblance to istithna'. Considering both resemblances, we said: If the subject of khiyar and its price are both known, the sale is valid, otherwise not. And this issue has four aspects: First: the subject of khiyar and its price are both known, like when one sells 'this' and 'that', i.e., two slaves, for two thousand, this one for one thousand and that one for one thousand, in one contract with one offer, on condition that either the seller or the buyer has khiyar regarding 'that' (but not this). Second: the subject of khiyar is known (i.e., 'that') but the price is not known (how much for each). Third: the opposite: the price is known (this one for one thousand and that for one thousand) but the subject of khiyar is not known (whether khiyar is in 'that' or 'this'). Fourth: neither the subject of khiyar nor the price is known, e.g., selling both for two thousand on condition that he has khiyar in one of them. So if we consider that the subject of khiyar is included in the offer, giving the resemblance of naskh, then in all four cases the sale is valid. At most, it becomes a sale by portion (bay' bi al-hissah), but this sale by portion is not from the beginning; rather, it arises later because both slaves were included in the offer and the sale is a single sale, so initially there is no sale by portion. But when one is returned due to the khiyar condition, it becomes a sale by portion, and that does not make the sale invalid. If we consider that it is not included in the ruling of sale, then in all four cases the sale is invalid. As for when each one [is considered]: the reasons for invalidity: in the first case where both subject and price are known, the reason for invalidity (if we adopt the second perspective) is that when the subject of khiyar is not included in the ruling, it is as if it is not sold, so acceptance of the sold item is conditioned on acceptance of the non-sold item. Since the offer is single, the buyer cannot accept only one; he accepts both. So when acceptance of the non-sold item is a condition for acceptance of the sold item, this is an invalid condition. And in the second and third cases where one of them is unknown, e.g., in the second only price is unknown, and in the third only the subject of khiyar is unknown, for this reason the invalid condition (acceptance of non-sold) plus ignorance exist. In the fourth, both price and subject are unknown, and the invalid condition is also present. Commentary
This passage is an Istinbat (derivation) that uses analogical reasoning (qiyas) to determine the ruling of a sale contract where one of two items sold together is subject to a condition of option (khiyar). The author first explains why this case resembles both naskh (abrogation) and istithna' (exception). Resemblance to naskh: because including the subject of khiyar in the offer (ijab) and then removing it by condition is like abrogating a general ruling. Resemblance to istithna': because the subject of khiyar is not included in the ruling (hukm) of the sale, similar to an excepted item. Since the case has two resemblances, it is treated like takhsis (specification), which also has both aspects. The key ruling: when both the subject of khiyar and its price are known, the sale is valid; otherwise it is invalid. The author then enumerates four possible scenarios: (1) both subject and price known, (2) subject known but price unknown, (3) price known but subject unknown, (4) both unknown. From the perspective of resemblance to naskh (subject included in offer), all four sales are valid, but may become sales by portion (bay' bi al-hissah) later. From the perspective of resemblance to istithna' (subject not included in ruling), all are invalid due to the condition of accepting a non-sold item. The Hanafi position is that considering both aspects, the sale is valid only in the first scenario (both known) because the invalid condition is balanced by the clarity. The commentary follows the author's sequence, explaining each scenario and the underlying reasoning.

Key Terms
خیار (khiyar): An option or right to rescind a contract, stipulated at the time of sale.
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a ruling by a later revelation; used here analogically for removal of a contract term.
استثناء (istithna'): Exception, excluding part of a statement; here analogical to excluding the khiyar item from the sale ruling.
ایجاب (ijab): Offer in a contract; the wording that creates the obligation.
بیع بالحصہ (bay' bi al-hissah): Sale by portion where the price is allocated to each item; if arises later, it may be valid.

The Legal Effect of 'Illa an Adhana' in Oaths
Contrasts two oath formulations and their legal effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of metaphorical interpretation (majāz) to derive the ruling for oaths containing the phrase 'illā an ādhana' and explain the role of doubt (shakk) in preventing hanth.

Core Idea
When 'illa an adhana' follows an oath, it acts as a limit, not an exception—once permission is given, the oath is fulfilled.

This passage examines a subtle distinction between two similar oath formulations: 'lā takhruj illā bi-idhnī' and 'lā takhruj illā an ādhana.' While the first clearly requires permission for each act, the second is interpreted metaphorically as 'until I permit' (ilā an ādhana). The Hanafi analysis hinges on a linguistic argument: since 'permission' is not of the same genus as 'exit,' the literal exception reading fails. This leads to a preferred interpretation that lifts the prohibition entirely after one permission, but an alternative reading exists. In the presence of two valid interpretations, doubt prevents the imposition of sin (hanth).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two oath formulations
Notice the textual difference: 'illa bi-idhni' vs 'illa an adhana'. The first is clear; the second requires analysis.
The key is the use of 'an' + present tense to form a masdar.
2
Recognize the linguistic issue
Since 'idhn' (permission) is not of the same genus (jins) as 'khuruj' (exit), the literal exception reading of 'illa' is impossible.
Exception requires the excepted item to be of the same type as the excepted-from.
3
Apply the Hanafi metaphor rule
When literal meaning is impossible, 'illa' shifts to metaphorical meaning of ghāyah (limit), interpreted as 'ilā an adhana' (until I permit).
This lifts the prohibition once permission is given at any time.
4
Consider the alternative interpretation
Alternatively, 'an adhana' can be treated as a time adverbial: 'except at the time of my permission,' requiring permission for each act.
This would make each exit without permission a hanth.
5
Apply the doubt principle
Since two valid interpretations (metaphor and time) conflict, doubt (shakk) arises. In oaths, doubt prevents hanth.
This reflects the Hanafi maxim: oaths are interpreted leniently to avoid sin.

Two Interpretations of 'إلا أن أذن' in Oaths
Analyzing two interpretations of 'الا ان اذن' in oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two Hanafi interpretations of the oath phrase 'إلا أن أذن' and apply the principle that doubt wards off hanth.

Core Idea
When an oath phrase permits two conflicting interpretations, doubt prevents hanth.

This passage delves into the linguistic and legal analysis of a common oath phrase: 'لا تخرج إلا أن أذن' (Do not exit unless I permit). At first glance, the phrase seems straightforward, but the Hanafi school identifies two plausible interpretations. The first treats 'إلا أن أذن' as a metaphorical limit (ghaya) meaning 'until permission', while the second treats it as a real exception from a general time. These two readings lead to different rulings on whether the oath is violated (hanth) by repeated exits after one permission. The author concludes that because both interpretations are valid and contradictory, doubt arises, and the principle 'penalties are warded off by doubts' applies, so no hanth occurs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath phrase and its possible meanings
Begin by isolating the critical phrase 'الا ان اذن' in the oath 'لا تخرج'. Note that it can be read as either a limit ('until permission') or an exception ('except with permission'). Understand that this ambiguity drives the entire analysis.
The phrase 'ان اذن' contains 'ان' which can be masdariyya (making 'idhn' a verbal noun) or can imply a time adverb.
2
Analyze the first interpretation: ghaya (limit)
Follow the reasoning that 'ان' is masdariyya, so 'اذن' is a masdar. Since permission is not of the same genus as exit, 'الا' cannot be a real exception. Hence 'الا' becomes metaphorical for 'غاية' (limit), meaning 'until permission'. Once permission is given, the prohibition ends permanently.
This interpretation does not require permission for each subsequent exit.
3
Analyze the second interpretation: istithna (exception) of time
Consider the alternative: 'ان مع الفعل المضارع' can indicate a time adverb (e.g., 'وقت'). Then 'الا ان اذن' means 'except at the time of my permission'. The phrase becomes 'لا تخرج وقتاً إلا وقت إذني' (do not exit at any time except at my permission). Under negation, the general word 'وقتاً' grants generality, so every exit requires explicit permission.
This interpretation demands permission for each individual exit; any unauthorized exit causes hanth.
4
Compare the legal consequences
Under the ghaya interpretation, one permission suffices for all future exits; subsequent exits without permission do not break the oath. Under the time-exception interpretation, each exit without permission (after the first) breaks the oath. Thus the same act yields opposite rulings depending on which interpretation is adopted.
The two interpretations are mutually contradictory but linguistically plausible.
5
Apply the principle of doubt
Since both interpretations are valid and conflict, doubt (شك) arises about whether hanth actually occurs when someone exits after a single permission. The Hanafi legal maxim 'الحدود تدرأ بالشبهات' (penalties are warded off by doubts) is then invoked. Therefore, no hanth is incurred, and no expiation is required.
This outcome reflects the Hanafi preference for leniency in oath matters.

Metaphorical 'Fi' and 'Ma'a': Linguistic Keys to Conditional Divorce
Explains the metaphorical use of 'fi' for concomitance leading to condition, and the use of 'ma'a' for simultaneity in divorce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of metaphorical usage to distinguish between conditional and declarative statements in divorce, and identify the legal effect of 'fi' with 'will of Allah' versus 'knowledge of Allah'.

Core Idea
Metaphorical 'fi' turns a divorce into a conditional suspension; but 'fi 'ilm Allah' is a declaration of certainty, not a condition.

Sometimes, a simple preposition becomes a legal pivot. In this passage, the Hanafi jurists explain how the preposition 'fi' (in) can be used metaphorically to indicate concomitance (muqaranah), which then functions as a condition. This transforms a divorce statement into a suspended one. However, when the same 'fi' is used with 'knowledge of Allah', the meaning flips: it becomes a declaration of a known fact, triggering immediate divorce. Understanding this linguistic subtlety is crucial for correctly ruling on divorce utterances.

Guided Path
1
Identify literal vs. metaphorical usage
First, recognise when 'fi' cannot denote physical containment. If it cannot, it is used metaphorically for muqaranah (concomitance). This shifts its meaning from containment to condition.
Example: 'fi al-dar' (in the house) is literal; 'fi mashiyat Allah' is metaphorical.
2
Understand muqaranah as a bridge
Muqaranah between container and contained is extended to condition and consequence. Because a condition and its result are concomitant, 'fi' acquires a conditional meaning.
The same reasoning applies to 'ma'a' for simultaneity.
3
Apply to 'fi mashiyat Allah'
In 'you are divorced by the will of Allah', 'fi' is metaphorical for condition. Since Allah's will is not certain for this specific event, the divorce is suspended (ta'liq) and does not occur.
Equivalent to 'if Allah wills' (in sha'a Allah).
4
Contrast with 'fi 'ilm Allah'
Allah's knowledge encompasses all things with certainty. Therefore 'fi 'ilm Allah' cannot be conditional; it affirms that the divorce is known to Allah, making it a declarative statement. The divorce occurs immediately.
The focal point is certainty: uncertain → condition; certain → declaration.
5
Analyse 'ma'a' for simultaneity
The preposition 'ma'a' indicates simultaneity without separation. In divorce, saying 'you are divorced once with once' causes both talags to occur at the same moment.
This affects the count of divorces and possibility of reconciliation.

This passage continues the analysis of the preposition 'fi' by discussing its metaphorical use. When 'fi' cannot denote literal containment (like in 'fi mashiyat Allah'), it is used metaphorically to indicate concomitance between two things, and this concomitance gives it the meaning of a condition. This is because the relationship between a condition and its consequence is one of concurrence. Therefore, 'fi' in such contexts functions like 'if' or 'when'. Abrogation of Obligation: Does Permissibility Remain?
Usul dispute on naskh of wujub and survival of ibaha

Learning Objective

Core Idea
After naskh of wujub, Hanafi holds nothing remains; Shafi'i holds ibaha or nadb persists.

When a command that originally required an act as obligatory is later abrogated, does the act revert to being merely permissible or recommended? This question touches on the nature of legal commands and the scope of abrogation. The Hanafi and Shafi'i schools diverge based on how they interpret the original command: was it exclusively for obligation, or did it also encompass permissibility? This lesson unpacks that dispute using examples from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
Determine what exactly is being disputed: after naskh of wujub, does the act become mubah or mandub, or does it fall into a void of no ruling?
The dispute is not about whether abrogation removes the obligation, but about what residue, if any, remains.
2
State the Shafi'i position and its basis
The Shafi'i school argues that the original command included two aspects: the prohibition of leaving (which is abrogated) and the permissibility of doing (which remains). Hence, after naskh, the act is still mubah or mandub.
This relies on the idea that the command's meaning (dalalah) covers both parts, and abrogation only removes the obligation part.
3
State the Hanafi position and its basis
The Hanafi school holds that the command was used exclusively for wujub; it never indicated ibaha. Therefore, when wujub is abrogated, nothing remains. The act becomes neither mubah nor mandub.
The examples of cutting nails and facing Bayt al-Maqdis illustrate that after their obligations were abrogated, the acts were not considered recommended or permissible.
4
Analyze the linguistic argument about majaz
The Shafi'i view argues that the remaining ibaha is not majaz because it is an indication of the whole upon a part (dalalah al-kull 'ala al-juz'), which is not considered figurative speech. The Hanafi view implicitly rejects that the command ever indicated ibaha.
Understanding the distinction between dalalah al-kull 'ala al-juz and isti'mal fi ghayr ma wudi'a lahu is key to grasping the disagreement.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Identify a concrete scenario where the difference matters, e.g., cutting nails during Hajj after the obligation was abrogated. According to Hanafi, it is neither recommended nor permissible; according to Shafi'i, it may be permissible.
The text does not give the full ruling for those examples, but the principle is clear.

usul dispute on naskh and majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Shafi'i and Hanafi positions on the survival of permissibility after abrogation, and explain why the Shafi'i view does not consider the remaining meaning as majaz using the concept of dalalat al-kull ala al-juz.

Core Idea
The Shafi'i school holds that permissibility remains after naskh, and it is not majaz because it was part of the original command's meaning.

This passage examines a subtle usul question: when an obligatory command is abrogated, does the permissibility (ibaha) or recommendation (nadb) that was implied within it survive? Imam al-Shafi'i argues that it does, and that this surviving meaning is not a metaphorical usage (majaz) because it was part of the original whole. The Hanafi school disagrees, asserting that both the obligation and the permissibility vanish. To understand this, we must delve into the definition of majaz and the concept of dalalat al-kull ala al-juz.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
The issue is whether after the abrogation (naskh) of an obligatory command (wujub), the act remains permissible (mubah) or recommended (mandub). The Shafi'i school says yes, the Hanafi school says no.
The discussion is about the imperative (amr) and its effects.
2
Understand the Shafi'i position and its linguistic basis
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the original command indicated both the obligation and the permissibility of the act. Abrogation removes only the obligation, leaving the permissibility intact. This is not a new usage of the word, so it is not majaz. It is like using 'insan' to mean 'hayawan natiq' – the word indicates both parts, so using it for one part is not majaz.
The concept used is 'dalalat al-kull ala al-juz' – the whole's indication of its part.
3

Majaz is using a word for other than what it was originally coined for. If the intended meaning is part of the original meaning (e.g., using 'insan' for just 'natiq'), it is not majaz because the part is included in the whole. Thus, the Shafi'i claim is that the remaining permissibility is not majaz.
Contrast with using 'insan' for only 'hayawan' – that would be majaz because 'hayawan' is not part of the original meaning in the same way.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school holds that the permissibility was not an independent part but was contingent on the obligation. When the obligation is removed, the permissibility also disappears. The act becomes legally neutral in a different sense – neither recommended nor permissible, but simply not prohibited? Actually, the Hanafi view is that it is neither nadb nor ibaha.
The example given: cutting nails was obligatory by a command; after abrogation, it is neither recommended nor permissible.
5

The practical consequence: In Hanafi fiqh, an abrogated obligatory act (like facing Bayt al-Maqdis) is neither recommended nor permissible to do; in Shafi'i, it remains recommended or permissible. This affects daily practice and legal rulings.
This difference matters for understanding the scope of naskh and the nature of commands.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute regarding the effect of abrogation on the imperative. The author first presents the Shafi'i position: when an obligatory command is abrogated, the permissibility (ibaha) or recommendation (nadb) that was implied within the obligation remains. The question is whether calling this remaining meaning 'majaz' (metaphorical) is correct. The answer given is that it is not majaz because the original command was used for wujub, and the permissibility was part of that meaning (dalalat al-kull ala al-juz). Just as using 'insan' for 'hayawan natiq' is not majaz because the whole indicates both parts; similarly, the command indicated both the act's permissibility and the prohibition of leaving, so after abrogation the remaining part is not a new usage but a leftover from the original. The author then contrasts the Hanafi position: after abrogation, neither nadb nor ibaha remains; the act becomes legally neutral (mubah) if it is not prohibited, but here it is neither recommended nor permissible due to the nature of the abrogation. Examples given are cutting nails (which was obligatory by command but after abrogation is neither recommended nor permissible) and facing Bayt al-Maqdis in prayer (similarly, after abrogation, it is not a recommended or permissible direction).

Key Terms
مجاز (majaz): Using a word for other than what it was originally placed for, without the intended meaning being part of the original whole.
دلالة الكل على الجزء (dalalat al-kull ala al-juz): The whole's indication of its part, which is not considered majaz because the part is included in the whole.
استعمال اللفظ في غير ما وضع له (isti'mal al-lafz fi ghayr ma wudi'a lahu): Using a word in a sense other than its original coinage, which is the essence of majaz.

Hanafi vs Shafi'i: What Remains After Naskh?
usul dispute with practical examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on abrogated commands to practical cases, contrasting it with the Shafi'i ruling, and identify the legal consequences for acts like facing Bayt al-Maqdis and cutting nails.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, after abrogation of an obligation, the act is neither recommended nor permissible.

Building on the theoretical dispute, this lesson focuses on the practical outcome of the two schools' positions. The Hanafi view that nothing remains after naskh has concrete implications for acts that were once obligatory. The passage provides two clear examples: cutting nails and facing Bayt al-Maqdis. By seeing these examples, you will internalize the Hanafi ruling and understand its difference from the Shafi'i approach.

Guided Path
1
Recall the Hanafi position
The Hanafi madhhab holds that after abrogation of wujub, neither permissibility (ibaha) nor recommendation (nadb) remains. The act becomes legally neutral but not in the sense of being mubah; it simply has no ruling attached to it.
This is the key difference from Shafi'i.
2
Study the first example: cutting nails
The passage says that cutting nails was obligatory (wajib) by a command, and after its abrogation, it is neither recommended nor permissible. Understand that this means the act is not preferred (mustahabb) but also not prohibited; it is simply a permissible act in the sense of being legally allowed? Wait, the text says 'neither recommended nor permissible' – so it is not even mubah? This requires careful interpretation: the Hanafi view is that the act is not even mubah in the sense of being a neutral act with no reward or punishment; rather, it falls outside the scope of the ruling entirely? Actually, the phrase 'نہ مستحب ہے نہ مباح' means it is neither mustahabb nor mubah. So it is not even mubah. This implies that after naskh, the act is not considered a lawful act at all? But logically, if it's not haram, it must be mubah. The Hanafi distinction is that the original permissibility was part of the obligation, so when obligation falls, that permissibility also falls, and the act becomes like any other act not addressed by shari'ah – it is simply neither mandated nor recommended nor prohibited. This is a nuanced point.
Some later Hanafi scholars clarify that it becomes mubah in the sense of being legally indifferent, but the text here says 'neither recommended nor permissible', so likely they mean it is not even mubah in the sense of having any positive or neutral ruling from the command; it simply reverts to the default of ibaha? This is a point of discussion. We'll follow the text.
3
Study the second example: facing Bayt al-Maqdis in prayer
Facing Bayt al-Maqdis was once obligatory, then abrogated. After abrogation, according to Hanafi, it is neither recommended nor permissible. This means one should not face it intentionally in prayer; it would not be considered a valid direction. In Shafi'i, it would be permissible or even recommended.
This has practical implications for the prayer of those who might face Bayt al-Maqdis inadvertently.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i outcome
In Shafi'i usul, the permissibility remains. So facing Bayt al-Maqdis after its abrogation would still be a valid and perhaps recommended direction for prayer. The difference is clear: in Hanafi, it is not allowed; in Shafi'i, it is.
5
Understand the underlying usul principle
The Hanafi view is based on the idea that the permissibility was not an independent part of the command but a necessary concomitant of the obligation. When the obligation is removed, the concomitant also disappears. The Shafi'i view treats the permissibility as an independent part (juz) that survives naskh.
This ties back to the concept of dalalat al-kull ala al-juz.
