Critique of a Usul Definition: Tard and Mani'
Critique of the definition of 'asl' for lacking tard and mani'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate a proposed definition in Hanafi usul by testing its comprehensiveness (tard) and exclusivity (mani') using the five-fold classification of causes.

Core Idea
A definition must be coextensive with its term; a definition that includes non-instances fails tard and is invalid.

This passage critiques a proposed definition of the fundamental Hanafi usul term 'asl' (foundation) as 'that which something needs' (ma yahtaju ilayhi). Using the five-fold classification of causes, the passage demonstrates that this definition applies to many things that are not called 'asl', thus violating the essential conditions of a proper definition: tard (comprehensiveness) and mani' (exclusivity). Understanding these criteria is crucial for precise definition in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition under critique
The definition is 'asl' as 'ma yahtaju ilayhi' (that which something needs). Note that the author is testing this definition for validity.
The definition comes from 'sahib al-Mahsul' (Fakhr al-Din al-Razi).
2
Recall the conditions for a valid definition in Hanafi usul
A valid definition must be tard (comprehensive: covers all instances of the defined) and mani' (exclusive: excludes all non-instances). Without these, the definition is faulty.
These conditions are universally accepted in logic and usul.
3
Examine the five categories of 'that which is needed'
The passage lists matter (wood), form (bed-ness), agent (carpenter), purpose (sitting), and condition (tools) as all being needed by a bed. Identify each category and note which one is called 'asl'.
This is based on Aristotelian causality adapted in Islamic thought.
4
Apply the test of tard and mani'
The definition 'ma yahtaju ilayhi' applies to all five categories, but the word 'asl' is used only for matter. Therefore, the definition is not tard (it covers too many) and not mani' (it does not exclude the other four).
This shows the definition fails both conditions.
5
State the conclusion
The definition is invalid because it lacks tard and mani'. Hence, the definition of 'asl' as 'ma yahtaju ilayhi' is incorrect.
The commentator notes that the author's remark about nominal definition is unnecessary; the failure of tard and mani' is sufficient.

Commentary
This passage critiques the definition of the usul term 'asl' (root/foundation) as 'that which something needs' (ma yahtaju ilayhi). The author notes that a valid definition must be both 'tard' (comprehensive, covering all instances of the defined term) and 'mani'' (exclusive, preventing entry of non-instances). The definition under discussion fails on the first count: it applies to many things that are needed (matter, form, agent, purpose, condition) but only matter is called 'asl' in linguistic usage. The commentary then elaborates a five-fold classification of causes (Aristotelian) to demonstrate that only the material cause qualifies as 'asl'. The conclusion is that the definition is invalid because it is not coextensive with the term.

Key Terms
اصل (asl): The thing upon which another is built; specifically the material cause (مادة) in the context of this critique
طرد (tard): Comprehensiveness: the definition applies to all individuals of the defined term
مانع (mani'): Exclusivity: the definition excludes all non-instances of the defined term
مادة (maddah): Material cause; that out of which a thing arises (e.g., wood for a bed)

Definition of Shar'iyyah and Inclusion of Qiyasi Rulings
Defining shar'iyyah and defending inclusion of qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of shar'iyyah and explain why qiyasi rulings are included in the definition of fiqh.

Core Idea
Shar'iyyah means rulings unknowable without divine address; qiyasi rulings are included because they depend on the original text.

In this passage, Sadr al-Shari'ah in al-Tawdih clarifies the crucial qualifier 'shar'iyyah' in the definition of fiqh. This term ensures that fiqh concerns only rulings that require divine revelation. The author also addresses whether rulings derived by qiyas (analogy) qualify—an important point for the Hanafi school, which extensively uses qiyas as a source of law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the lexical meaning of shar'iyyah
Shar'iyyah is defined as that which cannot be known without the address of the Lawgiver (khitab al-shari'). Understand that this dependency is what makes a ruling 'shar'i' in the definition of fiqh.
This distinguishes shar'iyyah from 'hukm' itself, which is the divine address.
2
Apply shar'iyyah to the definition of fiqh
In the definition 'knowledge of shar'i rulings', the qualifier 'shar'i' ensures that only rulings dependent on revelation are included. Without it, the term would be redundant with 'hukm'.
3
Extend the concept to qiyasi rulings
Qiyasi rulings are also shar'i because they depend on an original revealed case (maqis 'alayh). Although no direct text addresses the analogized case, the ruling relies on the original text and the shared effective cause.
This is crucial for Hanafi methodology, which relies heavily on qiyas.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'shar‘iyyah' in the definition of fiqh. The author (Sadr al-Sharī‘ah) in al-Tawdīḥ explains that 'shar‘iyyah' means rulings whose knowledge is impossible without divine address. This distinguishes it from 'hukm' (ruling) itself, which is the divine address; otherwise the two words would be redundant. The definition covers both direct textual rulings and qiyāsī rulings, because even though the divine address is not in the qiyāsī ruling itself, it is in the original case from which the ruling is derived. The passage then addresses an objection: if fiqh is simply knowledge of shar‘ī rulings, then for those who deny rational good and evil (i.e., the Ash‘arīs), even matters like the goodness of humility would be considered shar‘ī and thus enter fiqh. The Hanafī-Māturīdī position (and Mu‘tazila) is that some actions have rational good/evil (like humility is good), and these are not part of fiqh; they belong to ethics (akhlāq). Only actions whose good/evil is known solely from revelation are fiqh. Therefore, the definition is correct for Hanafīs. But for Ash‘arīs, since they believe all moral values are known only through revelation, the definition would include rational moral judgments, which contradicts the conventional scope of fiqh. Hence, the definition is not valid for Ash‘arīs. The passage also warns against adding more qualifiers like 'which does not exceed being from the religion necessarily' to the definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi-Maturidi position on rational good/evil and evaluate why it excludes certain actions from fiqh.

Core Idea

Core Idea
The minimum mahr is set by Shari'ah at ten dirhams, derived by qiyas on organ compensation.

In this lesson, we examine a classic usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over the minimum mahr (adna al-mahr). The Hanafi school argues that the Shari'ah itself fixes a floor of ten dirhams, while the Shafi'is leave it entirely to the spouses' agreement. We will trace the Hanafi proof from the Quranic word 'faradna' to the qiyas on theft penalty, and see how the thamarah al-khilaf plays out in practice.

Identify the situation where a woman delegates mahr determination to her guardian or marries without specifying mahr. This sets the stage for the ikhtilaf.
The dispute only arises when one spouse dies before consummation; both schools agree mahr is due after consummation.

The Hanafis argue that mahr is iṣāq (attached) to the contract, so a valid contract necessarily entails mahr, even without consummation.
This is based on the specific word 'iṣāq' in the contract text, which they consider nass.

The verse 'قد علمنا ما فرضنا عليهم' uses 'faradna' meaning taqdir (determination). The attribution to Allah shows that mahr quantification is His exclusive right.
Hanafis classify this as nass (specific), while Shafi'is consider it amm (general).

Metaphorical Use of 'جمیع' and Combining Literal and Metaphorical
Resolving the issue of using جمیع metaphorically for كل

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyse the metaphorical usage of 'جمیع' in Hanafi usul and apply the principle that intention at the time of speech prevents combining literal and metaphorical meanings.

Core Idea
A word can be metaphorically used for a broader meaning, but the speaker's intention at the moment of speech determines only one meaning.

This passage tackles a subtle problem in Hanafi legal interpretation: how a word like 'جمیع' (literally 'all together') can be used metaphorically to mean something broader (the first entrant, whether alone or in a group). The commentator defends the metaphor by clarifying the scope of the metaphorical meaning and rejecting the idea that both literal and metaphorical meanings can be intended simultaneously.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word and its literal meaning
Recognize that 'جمیع' literally implies assembly of individuals. The literal meaning requires a group entering together.
2
Understand the metaphorical claim
The author states that 'جمیع' is a metaphor for 'كل' (the whole). However, 'كل' has two possible meanings: (a) the first entrant (whether one or many) gets reward, or (b) each individual in a group gets full reward. Determine which meaning is intended.
The commentator explains that the metaphor is for the first meaning (first entrant), not the second.
3
Analyse the contextual clue (قرينة)
The statement is for encouragement (تحریض) to enter the fort first. This context excludes the literal meaning (which requires assembly) and supports the metaphorical meaning focusing on priority of entry.
4
Resolve the issue of combining literal and metaphorical
The commentator refutes the objection that both literal and metaphorical meanings could be intended based on external occurrence. He argues that at the moment of speech, the speaker can only intend one specific meaning; external occurrence does not change the intention.
5
Apply the ruling
The final interpretation: the metaphorical meaning of 'جمیع' covers the first entrant, whether alone or in a group. However, when a group enters together, they collectively deserve one reward, not each individual a full reward.

The commentator begins by addressing a previous objection: that using a word simultaneously in its literal and metaphorical senses (جمع بین الحقیقة والمجاز) is impossible because a speaker intends one specific meaning at a time. The objector claimed that since the ruling applies to both individual and group entrants, the word 'جمیع' must be literal (requiring assembly) or metaphorical (meaning each individual), but not both. The commentator refutes this by explaining that the metaphor here is for the first meaning of 'كل', which includes both individual and group entrants without requiring assembly. The literal meaning (assembly) is not intended, and the second meaning (each individual full reward) is also not intended because when a group enters together, they collectively deserve one supererogatory reward, as indicated by the context of encouragement (تحریض). The final conclusion is that the statement is metaphorical with a general metaphorical meaning that encompasses both the individual and the group. This is a fine point of Usul al-Fiqh concerning the interpretation of metaphors in legal texts.

Key Terms
جمیع (Jamī‘): Literally 'all' or 'entire', used metaphorically here to mean each individual in a group or the single entrant
کل (Kull): Every individual, here with two possible meanings: first meaning is the first entrant (whether one or many), second meaning is each individual in a group getting full reward
استعارہ (Isti‘ārah): Metaphorical usage where a word is used for something other than its original meaning due to a resemblance

Temporal Types of 'Alaqah and the Necessity of Mental Connection
Classification of temporal relations in metaphor

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between majaz bi-i'tibar ma kan and majaz bi-i'tibar ma ya'ul, and explain why a mental necessary connection (luzum dhihni) is required when the literal meaning is absent.

Core Idea
A valid majaz requires a recognized 'alaqah; if the literal meaning is absent entirely, a mental necessary connection must exist.

This passage from a Hanafi usul work introduces the concept of 'alaqah in majaz. It first categorizes relationships based on temporal considerations (what was vs. what becomes) and then addresses the fundamental requirement of a mental connection when no literal meaning is present.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two temporal categories
Recognize majaz bi-i'tibar ma kan (relation by what it was) and majaz bi-i'tibar ma ya'ul (relation by what it becomes). Note the examples: the first from a ruling about a slain person (spoils belong to the one who killed), the second from the word 'yatim'.
2
Understand the time condition
For a valid majaz, the time of acquisition of the literal meaning must be different from the time of coinage. If both times coincide, the usage is literal (haqiqah), not majaz.
3
Recognize the case of potential meaning (bi'l-quwwah)
When the literal meaning is not actual but potential, the 'alaqah is called isti'dad (potential). Example: calling spilled wine 'muskir' (intoxicant) because it had the potential to intoxicate.
4
See the necessity of mental connection when meaning is absent
If the literal meaning is not obtained at all (neither actual nor potential), then a mental necessary connection (luzum dhihni) between the literal and metaphorical meanings is required; otherwise, there is no 'alaqah and majaz is invalid.
5
Grasp the nature of mental transfer (intiqal)
The transfer from literal to metaphorical meaning is in generality (fi al-jumlah); it does not require strict inseparability or that the metaphorical meaning necessarily comes to mind every time.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of the 'alaqat (relations) that validate majaz (metaphorical usage) in Hanafi usul. The author begins by distinguishing majaz based on temporal considerations: majaz bi-i'tibar ma kan (relation by what it was) and majaz bi-i'tibar ma ya'ul (relation by what it becomes). The key point is that for metaphorical usage, the time of acquisition of the literal meaning must differ from the time of coinage; otherwise, the word remains in its literal sense. Then he discusses cases where the literal meaning is only potential (bi'l-quwwah), like calling spilled wine 'muskir' due to its intoxicating potential. When the literal meaning is absent entirely, a necessary mental connection (luzum dhihni) is required to enable the mind to transfer from the literal to the metaphorical meaning. This necessary connection can be purely mental (e.g., opposite relations), combined with customary external connections (e.g., 'gha'it' used for relieving oneself due to the custom of using low places), or combined with natural external connections (e.g., based on creation). Further subdivisions include part-whole relations, inherence relations (place-occupant), causal relations (cause-effect and effect-cause), and conditional relations (condition-conditioned). Each type is illustrated with examples from legal terminology and Quranic verses. This classification is essential for understanding how Hanafi jurists extend linguistic meanings to derive rulings, balancing between literal interpretation and contextual usage.

Key Terms
علاقہ ('alaqah): The relation or connection between the literal meaning (ma'na wad'i) and the metaphorical meaning (ma'na majazi) that justifies the figurative usage.
مجاز (majaz): The linguistic usage of a word in a meaning other than its original (wad'i) meaning due to a relation ('alaqah) and a contextual clue (qarinah).
لزوم ذهنی (luzum dhihni): A mental necessary connection that allows the mind to transfer from the literal meaning to the metaphorical meaning.
ماکان (ma kan): What it was; refers to a past state that serves as the basis for metaphorical usage.
مایوول (ma ya'ul): What it becomes; refers to a future or resulting state that serves as the basis for metaphorical usage.

Possibility of Literal Meaning Not Required for Metaphor

Core Idea

The Two Types of Hukm and the Term Being Shared
Explaining the two types of hukm and the dispute over whether 'hukm' is shared or metaphorical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two types of hukm (reward/punishment and validity/invalidity) and explain why the Hanafi school considers the term 'hukm' to be shared (mushtarak) rather than purely metaphorical, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf of this dispute.

Core Idea
The term 'hukm' is shared between reward and validity; establishing one does not imply the other.

This passage from al-Talwiḥ explains a fundamental distinction in Hanafi usul: the word 'hukm' carries two independent meanings. The first relates to reward and punishment based on intention, the second to validity and invalidity based on external conditions. The Hanafi school holds that after metaphorical usage, the term becomes shared (mushtarak) between these two types, while the Shafi'i school maintains it remains purely metaphorical. This difference has practical implications for whether a ruling on reward implies a ruling on validity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of hukm
The passage states hukm is of two types: (1) reward and punishment, and (2) permissibility (jawaz) and invalidity (fasad). Identify each type clearly.
2
Analyze the basis of each type
The first type is based on sincerity (sidq al-‘azima) and pure intention. The second type is based on external pillars and conditions.
3
Examine the example of wudu with impure water
The passage gives an example: a person unknowingly performs wudu with najis water and prays sincerely. The prayer is invalid due to missing condition of purity, but they still receive reward for their intention. This illustrates the independence of the two types.
This example shows how both types apply separately.
4
Trace the Hanafi argument for shared term
According to Hanafis, after extensive metaphorical usage, the term 'hukm' has become truly shared (mushtarak) between the two types. Therefore, it does not imply generality (umum). When one type is established (e.g., reward), the other (validity) is not automatically established.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i metaphorical view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that 'hukm' is purely metaphorical (majaz). Metaphors also do not convey generality. Both schools agree no generality occurs, but differ on the reason. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that in many cases, reward and validity are independent.

The Hanafi Rule on 'أو': Choice in Performatives, Doubt in Informatives
Usul rule with dispute and fiqh applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi rule for the word 'أو' (or) in performative vs. informative statements and apply it to cases of manumission, agency, and highway robbery.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, 'أو' indicates choice in performatives (إنشاء) and only secondarily doubt in informatives.

The word 'أو' is deceptively simple, yet its legal interpretation can change a ruling entirely. This passage from al-Talweeh explains why 'أو' is not primarily for doubt, as some claim, but rather for choice in performative utterances like freeing a slave. We'll see how this distinction affects real fiqh cases, from manumission to agency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested claim
Some scholars claim that the primary meaning of 'أو' is doubt (شك). Locate this claim in the text and note the author's rebuttal.
This is a usul dispute about the original disposition (وضع) of the particle.
2
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal
The author argues that the purpose of speech is communication (إفهام و تفہیم), not doubt. Doubt is secondary and arises only from context, specifically in informative statements.
Performatives (إنشاء) are never contexts for doubt because they create legal effects.
3
Distinguish between إنشاء and إخبار
Identify which contexts are performative (e.g., manumission utterances) and which are informative (e.g., reports). In إنشاء, 'أو' gives choice; in إخبار, it indicates doubt or ambiguity.
This distinction is key to the applications that follow.
4
Apply the rule to three fiqh cases
Work through the cases: (a) Manumission: 'أحد هذين حر' – speaker has choice but must specify; (b) Agency: 'وكلت هذا أو هذا' – invalid by qiyas but valid by istihsan; (c) Highway robbery: ruler has choice among penalties.
Note the interplay between linguistic origin (وضع) and Shari'ah objectives.
5
Note the role of istihsan in the agency case
Although the analogy (qiyas) would invalidate ambiguous agency, juristic preference (istihsan) upholds it because agency is based on flexibility and the ambiguity does not lead to dispute.
Istihsan overrides qiyas when there is a stronger Shari'ah consideration.

Ghayat al-Isqat: The Limit of Omission in the Wudu Verse
Explaining ghayat al-isqat through the wudu verse example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi concept of ghayat al-isqat to interpret Quranic phrases like 'ila al-marafiq' and derive the ruling that the elbows are included in wudu washing.

Core Idea
The phrase 'to the elbows' omits the part beyond the elbows, so the elbows themselves are washed.

In the wudu verse, Allah commands washing the hands to the elbows. But what does 'to the elbows' mean? A plain reading might suggest that the elbows are excluded from washing. However, Hanafi usul scholars propose a sophisticated interpretation: 'ila' here denotes a limit of omission, not a limit of washing. By understanding the full linguistic scope of 'yad', we see that the command to wash the entire hand would include the upper arm; the phrase 'to the elbows' cuts off that extension, indicating that only the upper arm is omitted, while the elbows remain within the washing obligation. Let's unpack this usul principle step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the textual problem
The word 'yad' in Arabic includes the whole arm from fingertips to armpit. The verse limits washing to the elbows. This creates a tension: does 'ila' exclude the elbows or include them?
Recognize that the default meaning of 'yad' is broader than the common understanding.
2
Distinguish between ghayat al-ghusl and ghayat al-isqat
Understand the two possible functions of 'ila' as a particle of ghayah. If it is a limit of washing, it excludes its object; if it is a limit of omission, it indicates what is dropped from a broader mandate.
Hanafi usul relies on the context to determine which type of ghayah is intended.
3
Apply ghayat al-isqat to the wudu verse
Since 'yad' covers the entire arm, 'ila al-marafiq' cannot be a limit of washing (as that would require washing beyond the elbows). Therefore it must be a limit of omission, meaning the part from the elbow to the armpit is omitted, so the elbows themselves are washed.
The conclusion: the elbows remain under the ruling of washing (ghusl).

Commentary
This passage defines the two ways of fulfilling a command: adaa (exact performance of the act as commanded) and qadaa (performing a similar act after the time). The author clarifies why the definition of adaa uses 'thabit bil-amr' (established by command) instead of 'wajib' – because adaa includes supererogatory acts which are established by command but not obligatory. Qadaa only applies to obligatory acts. The author then explains that each term can be used metaphorically for the other due to the shared element of delivering something to its rightful owner, giving examples from Quran and usage. Following this, a key usul dispute is presented: what is the cause (sabab) for qadaa? Some say a new cause is needed because the virtue of time is lost; but the majority Hanafi position (jama'at as-habina) holds that qadaa is wajib by the same cause that made adaa wajib – the obligation does not fall with time, and the missed act can be made up with a similar one. Exceptions like Friday and Eid prayers are noted. The passage then provides textual evidence from Quran (2:184) and hadith ('whoever sleeps through a prayer...') to show that only the missed wajib's like is required, not compensation for time's virtue. Finally, it extends this to vowed acts and i'tikaf via qiyas, addressing an objection that the texts appear to create a new obligation by responding that they merely confirm the continuation of the original obligation.

Key Terms
ادا (adaa): Bringing the very act that is established by the command, exactly as commanded; includes wajib and nawafil.
قضا (qadaa): Bringing an example similar to what was wajib by the command, after its performance time has passed.
سبب (sabab): The textual command (amr) that makes an act wajib; the cause of obligation.

The Cause of Qadaa: Same as Adaa or New?
Defining adaa and qadaa and the dispute over their cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that qadaa is wajib by the same cause as adaa, contrast it with the opposing view, and apply the evidence from Quran, hadith, and analogy.

Core Idea
The obligation of qadaa is not a new command but the continuation of the original obligation that does not drop with time.

After defining adaa and qadaa, a crucial usul question arises: what makes qadaa obligatory? Is it the same divine command that made the original act wajib, or does a missed act require a new command? The Hanafi school takes the former view, and this lesson examines the evidence and reasoning.

Guided Path
1
State the issue and the two positions
Identify the dispute: whether qadaa is wajib by the same cause (sabab) as adaa, or by a new cause. The passage presents the minority view that a new cause is needed, and the Hanafi majority view that the same cause applies.
The opposing view is attributed to 'al-ba'd' (some scholars).
2
Analyze the opposing view's proof
The opposing argument: acts of worship are only valid in their appointed time; when the honor (sharaf) of time is lost, the exact like cannot be known without a new text. Therefore, a new textual command is necessary.
This view emphasizes the time-bound nature of ibadaat.
3
Analyze the Hanafi view and its evidence
The Hanafi majority says qadaa is wajib by the same text that made adaa wajib, because the obligation does not drop with the exit of time. They cite Quran (2:184) and hadith (sleeping through prayer) to show that only the similar act is required, not compensation for the time's virtue.
The texts are interpreted as affirming continuation, not new obligation.
4
Examine the Hanafi response to the objection
The passage addresses an objection: the texts about qadaa of fasting and prayer appear to be new commands. The Hanafi response: these texts only inform that the previous obligation remains; they do not create a fresh obligation.
This is a key usul point: how to distinguish between enacting and affirming.
5
Note exceptions and analogical extension
Friday prayer and Eid prayers are exceptions because their like cannot be offered after time. The Hanafi extend the same principle to nadhr and i'tikaf via qiyas, where only the example is required and no compensation for time's honor.
The qiyas here is used for clarification (izhar) not for proof (ithbat), per the passage.

Commentary
This passage defines the two ways of fulfilling a command: adaa (exact performance of the act as commanded) and qadaa (performing a similar act after the time). The author clarifies why the definition of adaa uses 'thabit bil-amr' (established by command) instead of 'wajib' – because adaa includes supererogatory acts which are established by command but not obligatory. Qadaa only applies to obligatory acts. The author then explains that each term can be used metaphorically for the other due to the shared element of delivering something to its rightful owner, giving examples from Quran and usage. Following this, a key usul dispute is presented: what is the cause (sabab) for qadaa? Some say a new cause is needed because the virtue of time is lost; but the majority Hanafi position (jama'at as-habina) holds that qadaa is wajib by the same cause that made adaa wajib – the obligation does not fall with time, and the missed act can be made up with a similar one. Exceptions like Friday and Eid prayers are noted. The passage then provides textual evidence from Quran (2:184) and hadith ('whoever sleeps through a prayer...') to show that only the missed wajib's like is required, not compensation for time's virtue. Finally, it extends this to vowed acts and i'tikaf via qiyas, addressing an objection that the texts appear to create a new obligation by responding that they merely confirm the continuation of the original obligation.

Key Terms
سبب (sabab): The cause of obligation; specifically, the textual command (amr) that makes an act wajib.
مثل (mithl): An example or substitute that resembles the original wajib act, used in qadaa.
شرف الوقت (sharaf al-waqt): The special honor or virtue associated with performing an act within its prescribed time.

Change of Ownership and Its Effect on Rulings
Explains the usul rule that change of ownership causes change of ruling when the ruling is based on ownership attribute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the principle of tabaddul al-milk, apply it to scenarios involving property rulings, and distinguish rulings based on ownership from those based on essence.

Core Idea
Change of ownership changes rulings tied to property but not rulings tied to essence.

This lesson explores the Hanafi usul principle that change of ownership (tabaddul al-milk) can alter legal rulings attached to property. Through the hadith of Barirah, we see how the Prophet (PBUH) treated the same meat differently once ownership transferred from the giver to Barirah and then to him as a gift. We will also examine the key distinction between rulings based on ownership attribute and those based on inherent essence, and apply the principle to a real fiqh scenario involving dower.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
Articulate the rule that change of ownership changes rulings attached to property by virtue of ownership.
2
Identify the proof
Locate the hadith of Barirah and note how it illustrates the principle.
The hadith is a primary naqli evidence.
3
Analyze the hadith evidence
Examine the specifics: the meat was sadaqah (forbidden for the Prophet) when owned by Barirah, but became a gift (permissible) after she gave it to him. The change was due to ownership transfer.
The essence of the meat did not change; only ownership did.
4
Distinguish rulings
Differentiate between rulings based on ownership attribute (e.g., permissibility of sadaqah) and rulings based on essence (e.g., impurity of swine). The former change with ownership; the latter do not.
This distinction is crucial for correct application.
5
Apply to a concrete case
Consider the dower example: if a slave is given as mahr and the husband frees or sells him before delivery, the wife's right changes from the slave to its price because ownership changed.
This shows the principle in action.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of the usul rule that change of ownership (tabaddul al-milk) causes a change in the legal ruling attached to a property. The commentator begins with an example: if the specific item designated as dower (mahr) changes, the contract is considered altered and the performance is like a judicial decree (qada). He then cites the well-known hadith of Barirah: she received meat from sadaqah, and the Prophet (PBUH) indicated that when ownership changed (from the giver to Barirah, and then from Barirah to the Prophet as a gift), the ruling changed—the meat was no longer sadaqah for them but a gift, so he could eat it. This proves that rulings based on property (like permissibility) change with ownership, while rulings based on essence (like impurity of swine) do not. The commentator further clarifies the concept of 'ayn (essence) in this context: it means the composite of essence and attribute. He then applies this to a fiqh issue: if a slave is given as dower, he is not freed until delivered to the wife, because before delivery the husband's ownership is not fully transferred. If the husband frees or sells the slave before delivery, it is valid because he still owns him. If the judge decrees payment of price instead and later the husband reacquires the slave, the wife's right does not revert to the slave; she only gets the price. This illustrates how change of ownership alters rights and obligations.

Key Terms
تبدل الملک (tabaddul al-milk): Change of ownership from one person to another.
تبدل العین (tabaddul al-'ayn): Change of the specific item legally, i.e., the composite of essence and attribute.
