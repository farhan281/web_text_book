Priority of the Sunnah Text over Ijmāʿ
Question and answer about absolute priority of Sunnah over Ijmāʿ

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi position on the absolute priority of the matn of Sunnah over Ijmāʿ and distinguish between qatʿī and ẓannī in this context.

Core Idea
The matn of Sunnah always precedes Ijmāʿ; apparent exceptions arise when the Sunnah's proof becomes ẓannī.

In Hanafi usul, the four sources (Kitāb, Sunnah, Ijmāʿ, Qiyās) are ordered, but the relationship between Sunnah and Ijmāʿ requires careful nuance. This passage addresses an objection: is Sunnah only prioritized when it is qatʿī? The commentator clarifies that the matn (text) of Sunnah is absolutely preferred over Ijmāʿ. When a Sunnah appears to be postponed, it is because its thubūt (proof) has become ẓannī, casting doubt on its status as Sunnah—not because the text itself is subordinate.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
The objector claims that Sunnah is only preferred over Ijmāʿ when it is qatʿī; if it is ẓannī, Ijmāʿ takes precedence.
This challenges the absolute nature of the four pillars’ order.
2
Identify the Hanafi response
The commentator responds that the statement about priority refers to the matn (text) of Sunnah, which is absolutely preferred over Ijmāʿ.
The matn is the core textual content of a hadith.
3
Distinguish matn from thubūt
The matn is considered qatʿī in dalālah (clear meaning), while thubūt (proof/transmission) can be ẓannī for ahad hadith.
Hanafi usul: the clear text of a hadith is qatʿī in meaning, even if its chain is ẓannī.
4
Apply the distinction
If a Sunnah is 'postponed' after Ijmāʿ, it is because its thubūt has become ẓannī, making its identification as Sunnah doubtful—not because the matn loses priority.
Thus, the matn of a true Sunnah always takes precedence over Ijmāʿ.
5
Note the practical consequence
In fiqh, a clear ahad hadith (e.g., 'No prayer without al-Fatiha') takes precedence over an Ijmāʿ that might conflict, according to the Hanafi school.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf of the dispute.

Commentary
This passage presents a usul dispute about the order of the four sources (Kitāb, Sunnah, Ijmāʿ, Qiyās). The objector argues that the absolute order is not valid because Sunnah is only prioritized over Ijmāʿ when it is qatʿī (definitive); if it is ẓannī (speculative), then Ijmāʿ takes precedence. When a Sunnah appears to be postponed, it is because its thubūt (proof) has become ẓannī, casting doubt on whether it truly qualifies as Sunnah. This aligns with Hanafi usul: the matn of a hadith is considered qatʿī in its dalālah if it is clear, regardless of its chain, whereas Ijmāʿ is also a strong proof but yields to a clear text.

Key Terms

اجماع (ijmāʿ): Consensus of the mujtahidūn of an era.
قطعی (qatʿī): Definitive, certain in transmission or meaning.
ظنی (ẓannī): Speculative, probable.
متن (matn): The textual content of a hadith, as opposed to its chain.
ثبوت (thubūt): Proof, establishment, especially the authenticity of a hadith.

Fasl al-Khitab: Definition and Linguistic Emphasis
Defining Fasl al-Khitab and its grammatical role

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'Fasl al-Khitab' in Hanafi usul and explain its grammatical relation to 'Sunnah' as a conjunction of specific to general.

Core Idea
Fasl al-Khitab is the Prophet's decisive speech, highlighted by being conjoined to Sunnah as a specific to general.

This passage defines 'Fasl al-Khitab' as the decisive and clear speech of the Prophet (peace be upon him). The author explains that this term is grammatically conjoined to 'Sunnah' as a specific to a general, drawing attention to the special status of prophetic sayings within the broader Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two linguistic meanings
Fasl al-Khitab can mean 'decisive speech' (separating truth from falsehood) or 'clear speech' (unambiguous). Understand both as properties of prophetic sayings.
2
Recognize the grammatical construction
The phrase 'Fasl al-Khitab' is conjoined to 'Sunnah' using the conjunction 'and'. This is an example of 'atf al-khass 'ala al-amm' (conjoining specific to general), emphasizing the specific item.
This grammatical structure is used to highlight the importance of sayings.
3
Contrast sayings and actions
Sunnah includes both sayings (qawl) and actions (fi'l). By conjoining 'Fasl al-Khitab' (which refers only to sayings) to 'Sunnah', the author stresses that sayings are the focus of this discussion.
Actions are not included in 'Fasl al-Khitab'.

In both cases, it is a construct of the adjective from the described. In that case, both types of explainers of the mujmalat of the Book are mentioned, because the explanation of the mujmalat of the Book occurs through the noble hadith as well as through the Quranic verses themselves.

Commentary
This passage defines 'Fasl al-Khitab' as a characteristic of prophetic sayings (qawl) that make them decisive and clear in distinguishing truth from falsehood. It is a specific type of Sunnah (which includes both sayings and actions of the Prophet). Three Reasons for the Superiority of Prophetic Sayings
Enumerating reasons for superiority of qawl over fi'l

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three reasons for the superiority of prophetic sayings over actions and apply them to evaluate a dispute.

Core Idea
Three reasons – intended for explanation, basis of most rulings, unanimous authority – establish sayings as the primary source.

Building on the definition of Fasl al-Khitab, this passage provides three reasons why prophetic sayings (qawl) are superior to actions (fi'l) as legal proofs. These reasons are: sayings are specifically placed for explaining laws, most rulings are based on sayings, and there is unanimous agreement on the authority of sayings. The passage also discusses a nuance regarding the pronoun reference, which affects the scope of explainers of the Quran's ambiguous verses.

Guided Path

2
Understand each reason
For each reason, explain why it gives superiority. Reason 1: actions are not specifically intended for explanation. Reason 2: actions are fewer and serve to clarify sayings. Reason 3: there is disagreement on the authority of actions.
The third reason points to ikhtilaf over hujjiyyah of fi'l.
3
Consider the alternative pronoun reference
Note that if the pronoun in 'khitabuhu' refers to Allah, then both Quran and Hadith explain mujmalat; this does not affect the superiority of sayings over actions directly but is a related nuance.
This shows that the explainers of the Quran's ambiguous verses include both the Quran itself and the Sunnah.

Superiority of Prophetic Sayings: Three Proofs
Explanation of 'fasl al-khitab' and three proofs for the primacy of prophetic sayings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three reasons for the superiority of the Prophet's sayings (qawl) over his actions (fi'l) and apply this hierarchy when evaluating evidence.

Core Idea
Prophetic sayings are more authoritative than actions: they exclusively clarify law, form the basis of most rulings, and command universal consensus.

This lesson examines a key usul rule: the sayings of the Prophet (saws) hold greater legal authority than his actions. The passage from Al-Tawdih explains the meaning of 'fasl al-khitab' and presents three compelling reasons why sayings are prior. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for correct istinbat.

Guided Path

Narration of Action Does Not Imply Generality
Stating the usul rule that narrating an action does not imply generality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that Hikāyat al-fi‘l does not imply generality and apply it to a narrated action to determine whether it yields a general ruling.

Core Idea
A narrated action of the Prophet is specific to its circumstances and does not produce a general rule.

When a Companion reports an action of the Prophet, it may be tempting to treat it as a general Sunnah applicable in all similar contexts. This passage establishes a key principle of Hanafi usul: a narration of an action (Hikāyat al-fi‘l) does not imply generality. The action occurred in a specific manner, with particular details that limit its scope. Only through careful reflection (ra’y wa fikr) can a jurist determine whether the action has broader implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
Read the opening phrase: 'Hikāyat al-fi‘l la ya‘um' — the narration of an action does not imply generality. This is a categorical principle in Hanafi usul.
2
Understand the reasoning
The text explains that an action occurs in a specific manner (sifa mu‘ayyana). Therefore, the narration only conveys that specific occurrence, not a general rule.
3
Examine the example
The Prophet prayed in the Ka'bah. Details like between the two Yemeni pillars, door opposite his back, distance from wall, and it being a supererogatory prayer show the action's specificity.
4
Note the textual status
The narration is muhkam (established in its occurrence) but mushtarak (shared in meaning — open to interpretation). The jurist must reflect (ra’y wa fikr) to prefer one meaning.
5
Apply the rule
When encountering any narrated action, avoid assuming generality. Analyze context, specifics, and possibilities before deriving a ruling.

Commentary
This passage introduces a fundamental principle in Usul al-Fiqh: the narration of an action (حكاية الفعل) from the Prophet (PBUH) does not establish generality (عموم). When a Companion reports an action of the Prophet, it is understood that the action occurred in a specific manner (صفة معينة) and is not necessarily indicative of a general ruling. The example given is the Prophet's prayer inside the Ka'bah. The specific details—such as the location between the two Yemeni pillars, the orientation of the door, and the distance from the wall—show that the action was performed in a particular way. Because the action is reported without explicit generalizing intent, it cannot be taken as a general obligation or recommendation. Instead, the narration is considered 'محکم' (definitive in its occurrence) but 'مشترک' (shared in meaning), meaning it can be interpreted in multiple ways. The jurist must then use reflection (رأي وفكر) to determine which meaning is preferable. This principle is crucial for avoiding the assumption that every prophetic action is a general Sunnah. Hanafi usul treats such narrations as specific to their context, unlike some other schools that may give them broader application.

The Rule: Narration of an Action Does Not Imply Generality
Stating the usul rule and applying it to prayer inside Kaaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that narrations of actions do not imply generality to derive the permissibility of obligatory prayer inside the Kaaba via analogy.

Core Idea
Actions of the Prophet are specific to their context; they do not generate general rulings without analogy.

How do we derive legal rulings from the Prophet's actions? The Hanafi school holds that a narrated action is specific to its circumstances and cannot be generalized. In this lesson, we study the rule through the example of prayer inside the Kaaba, where the Prophet's supererogatory prayer is used by analogy to permit obligatory prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the usul rule
Locate the statement 'حكاية الفعل لا يعم' in the passage. Understand that this is the core principle: narrations of actions do not carry general applicability.
2
Analyze the example of prayer inside the Kaaba
The Prophet prayed inside the Kaaba in a specific manner (between two pillars, door behind him, etc.). This action is narrated and thus limited to that specific form of prayer (supererogatory).
The passage emphasizes that the action is ambiguous (مشترك) and requires reflection.
3
Note the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that because the action is specific, it only proves supererogatory prayer; obligatory prayer is not valid inside the Kaaba because complete facing of the Qibla is not possible.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf: the practical difference.
4
Apply the Hanafi response using qiyas
The Hanafis respond that facing the Qibla is equally required for both obligatory and supererogatory prayers. Therefore, the permissibility of supererogatory prayer (proven by the Prophet's action) extends to obligatory prayer by analogy (qiyas).
This shows how a specific action can be extended when the illa (effective cause) is shared.

Distinguishing Action Narration from Statement Narration: The Preemption Case
Applying the usul rule to the preemption hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a narration of an action and a narration of a statement by meaning, and apply this distinction to determine the generality of the preemption hadith.

Core Idea
A narration that appears to be an action may actually be a statement by meaning, and thus carry generality.

The hadith 'قضى بالشفعة للجار' (the Prophet judged with preemption for the neighbor) is used by Hanafis to establish preemption for all adjacent neighbors. The Shafi'is object that this is a narration of an action and thus not general. In this lesson, we analyze the Hanafi response that this narration is actually a statement by meaning, not an action, and therefore general.

Guided Path
1
State the preemption hadith
The hadith is: 'قضى النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم بالشفعة للجار' (The Prophet judged with preemption for the neighbor). Identify it in the passage.
2
Identify the Shafi'i characterization
The Shafi'is consider this a narration of an action (حكاية فعل), which does not imply generality. Thus, it only applies to the specific neighbor who was a partner.
3
Explain the Hanafi response
The Hanafis argue that this is not a narration of an action but a narration of a statement by meaning. The original statement was 'الشفعة ثابت للجار' (preemption is established for the neighbor), which is general. The narrator expressed it by meaning as 'قضى بالشفعة للجار'.
The key is to recognize that the core is a general statement, not a specific action.
4
Apply the distinction
Since it is a statement, the ruling is general and applies to all adjacent neighbors, not just partners. This supports the Hanafi position.

One-Sided Isti'ara: Ijarah with Bay' and Limits on Adding to Benefits
Applying one-sided isti'ara to ijarah and answering an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of one-sided isti'ara (cause-effect metaphor) to contracts and identify when a valid metaphor cannot be extended due to legal restrictions on non-existent subject matter.

Core Idea
Applying cause to effect is valid, but the reverse is not—even within a valid metaphor.

In this lesson, we explore a subtle application of the one-sided isti'ara (metaphorical usage) principle in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. We examine why the word 'bay'' (sale) can be used to conclude an ijarah (hire) contract, but the reverse—using 'ijarah' for sale—does not work. Furthermore, we tackle a sophisticated objection: if 'bay'' serves as a valid metaphor for ijarah, why can't we add 'bay'' directly to benefits (manafi')? The answer reveals a crucial distinction: the validity of a metaphor does not override the legal requirement that the subject matter of a contract must exist at the time of contracting.

Guided Path
1
Identify the one-sided isti'ara principle
The principle states that when something is a pure cause (sabab mahd) for an effect, it is permissible to apply the name of the cause to the effect (cause → effect), but not vice versa. This is derived from examples like 'asad' (lion) used for 'shuja'' (brave man).
The cause-effect relation is not symmetric in metaphorical usage; only the cause can stand for the effect.
2
Apply the analogy from talaq
The text analogizes from talaq: just as pronouncing talaq with the word 'itq' (emancipation) is valid because 'itq' is a cause for talaq, similarly ijarah can be contracted using the word 'bay'' because 'bay'' is a cause for ownership of benefits.
The qiyas is based on the shared illa: both involve a cause-effect relationship.
3
Check the reverse: using ijarah for bay'
The principle prohibits the reverse: using the word 'ijarah' to mean a sale is invalid, because ijarah is not a pure cause for sale. The one-sided nature is strict.
This preserves the integrity of each contract type as distinct causes.
4
Analyze the objection: adding bay' to benefits
The objector argues: if 'bay'' can be used metaphorically for ijarah, then surely one can say 'baca'tu manafi'a hadhihi al-dar' (I sold the benefits of this house) to conclude an ijarah. When a traveler follows another traveler in congregation, events like hadath (ritual impurity) or a change in residency status can complicate the prayer. The Hanafi school makes a crucial distinction between two types of followers: the laḥiq (one who separates from the imam due to hadath) and the masbūq (one who joins late). This lesson unpacks that distinction, showing how the categorization of the follower's remaining prayer as qada or adaa determines whether niyat al-iqama alters the number of rak'ahs. By working through the passage's talil (reasoning), you will gain a deeper understanding of how Hanafi usul principles govern derivative rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of followers
Distinguish the laḥiq (who starts with the imam but leaves due to hadath) from the masbūq (who joins after the imam has begun). Note the stage at which they separate: laḥiq misses the end, masbūq misses the beginning.
The laḥiq's separation occurs during the prayer, the masbūq's occurs before joining.
2
Classify their remaining prayer
Recognize that for the laḥiq, the part prayed alone after the imam finishes is considered qada (makeup), while for the masbūq, the part he completes after the imam finishes is adaa (present performance). This classification is based on whether the follower had undertaken to pray that part with the imam.
The laḥiq undertook the entire prayer, so his solo part is a substitute (mithl) — qada. The masbūq never undertook the missed part, so it remains adaa.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle on iqama
Apply the rule: qada does not change with the performer's residency status (safar/hadhar), while adaa does. Therefore, if a laḥiq makes niyat al-iqama, his remaining two rak'ahs stay two; if a masbūq makes iqama, his missed rak'ahs become four.
This principle is derived from the nature of qada as a compensation for a missed act, tied to the original time/place.
4
Trace the talil (rationale)
Examine the reasoning: the laḥiq is performing qada because he is completing a prayer that he had started with the imam but cannot complete with him due to hadath. Since he is a 'qadi' (performer of qada), iqama cannot alter the makeup. The masbūq, however, never committed to the missed part with the imam, so his completion is a fresh adaa, and thus iqama is effective.
The key is the undertaking (iltizam) of prayer with the imam.
5
Derive the practical rulings
From the passage, extract the specific cases: (a) laḥiq, imam finished, then iqama → 2 rak'ahs; (b) laḥiq, imam not finished, iqama during imam's prayer → 4 rak'ahs (since still adaa); (c) masbūq, imam finished, then iqama → 4 rak'ahs; (d) laḥiq who speaks and restarts, then iqama → 4 rak'ahs (restart makes it adaa).
Note the exception: if the laḥiq's imam has not finished, the laḥiq remains in adaa with the imam, so iqama raises the count.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on rulings related to travelers following other travelers in congregational prayer, focusing on the effect of hadath (ritual impurity) and niyat al-iqama (intention to reside) on the completion of the prayer. The author first explains a division: prayers prescribed for congregation may be performed fully in congregation (complete performance), partially individually (if the missed part is at the beginning—masbuq, latecomer), or partially individually at the end (lahiq, one who separates due to hadath). The lahiq's remaining part is considered qada (makeup) because he is completing the prayer that the imam already performed, but with a similar form (mithl) not identical (ayn). Therefore, the ruling of qada applies, which does not change based on the performer's residency status. Several scenarios are then detailed: If a traveler follows another traveler, then has hadath and becomes muqim (resident) either by entering his hometown or intending to reside, and if the imam has already finished the prayer, the lahiq completes only two rak'ahs (the traveler's prayer) because qada is unaffected by iqama. If the imam has not finished, and the follower becomes muqim while the imam is still praying, then he must complete four rak'ahs because he is still in adaa (performance) with the imam, and iqama changes the obligation to four rak'ahs. The masbuq (one who joins late) is distinguished: if he catches only part of the prayer and then intends to reside after the imam finishes, he completes four rak'ahs because the missed part he makes up is adaa (since he never undertook to follow the imam for that part) and therefore iqama affects it. The lahiq, having undertaken the entire prayer with the imam, treats the missed part as qada, which is not altered by iqama. The passage concludes by noting that the lahiq, when performing the remaining part alone, is considered ruling-wise behind the imam (hukman), so he does not recite Quran in those rak'ahs and does not perform sajda sahw if forgetfulness occurs, unlike the masbuq who is considered alone. Finally, a general principle about qada: it must be performed as a similar act (mithl ma'mur bihi), which can be rationally analogous (like prayer for prayer) or non-rational (like fidyah for fasting).

Key Terms
مسبوق (masbuq): A person who joins the congregation after the imam has already performed one or more rak'ahs, and then completes the missed rak'ahs after the imam finishes.
لاحق (lahiq): A person who started the prayer with the imam but then experienced hadath (ritual impurity) and left to purify, then returns after the imam has finished or is still praying. His remaining part is considered qada (makeup).
نية الإقامة (niyat al-iqama): The intention to reside in a place (not one's hometown) for more than a specific period (usually 15 days in Hanafi fiqh), which transforms a traveler into a resident, changing prayer obligations from 2 rak'ahs to 4.
