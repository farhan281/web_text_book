The Hanafi Rejection of Mafhum al-Mukhalafa: Why Absence of Description Does Not Entail Opposite Ruling
Defending Hanafi position that absence of description does not entail opposite ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a textual description, determine whether its absence yields a ruling per mafhum al-mukhalafa, and apply the Hanafi distinction between ʿadam aṣlī and ʿadam sharʿī.

Core Idea
Absence of a description yields only original absence (ʿadam aṣlī), not a positive legal ruling.

One of the most fundamental disagreements between the Hanafi and Shafiʿi schools concerns the legal force of descriptions in scriptural texts. When the Prophet ﷺ said 'There is no zakāt on stabled animals,' does that mean zakāt is wajib on grazing animals? The Shafiʿis say yes, through the principle of mafhum al-mukhalafa. The Hanafis say no: the absence of a ruling is merely the original absence of obligation, not a new ruling. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi argument, its proofs, and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed principle
Mafhum al-mukhalafa is the converse meaning: when a ruling is attached to a description, the opposite ruling applies when the description is absent. You must first recognize that this is the issue at stake.
The Hanafis do not reject mafhum al-mukhalafa entirely; they limit it to cases where the description is the effective cause (ʿillah). Here the description is not necessarily the cause.
2
State the Hanafi position and its basis
The Hanafi position: absence of the description does not produce the opposite ruling; the absence of the ruling is original absence (ʿadam aṣlī), not a positive legal ruling. Basis: a ruling exists only when its cause exists; absence of cause leaves the matter in its original state.
This is a methodological principle: qiyas only applies to positive legal rulings, not to absences.
3
Examine the primary proofs
The text cites two proofs: (1) Hadith: 'Laysa fī al-ʿulūfati zakāt' – no zakāt on stabled animals. (2) Quran 4:92: 'Fa-taḥrīru raqabatin muʾminatin' – freeing a believing slave. For each, identify how the Hanafis and Shafiʿis read the description.
The Hanafis argue that the absence of zakāt or the permissibility of freeing a non-believing slave is not derived from the description but from other evidence or original absence.
4
Understand the Shafiʿi counterargument
The Shafiʿis argue that when a ruling is attached to a description, the description is the cause of the ruling; thus its absence entails the opposite. They apply this to both the hadith and the verse.
The Shafiʿi position is more straightforward: the text's explicit link between description and ruling implies a causal link.
5
Analyze the counterexample (slave woman case) and the Hanafi response
A potential counterexample: a master claims the eldest child from his slave woman, which seems to negate lineage of others via mafhum al-mukhalafa. The Hanafis explain this through other principles: silence in a context of need is a statement, and lineage requires a claim.
The Hanafis show that even where they appear to use mafhum al-mukhalafa, they rely on different legal causes.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed defense of the Hanafi position on mafhum al-mukhalafa (the converse meaning of a conditional or descriptive statement). The text argues that when a legal ruling is attached to a specific description (e.g., 'in animals fed at home, there is no zakat'), the absence of that description does not necessarily produce the opposite ruling (e.g., zakat being obligatory). According to the Hanafis, the absence of a ruling in such cases is merely the original absence (ʿadam aṣlī) — it is not a positive legal ruling created by the text. Therefore, it cannot be extended by analogy (qiyas) to other cases. The Shafiʿis, by contrast, hold that the attachment of a ruling to a description does imply the opposite ruling when the description is absent (mafhum al-mukhalafa). The author then provides illustrative examples: the hadith on zakat for stabled animals and the Quranic verse on freeing a believing slave for expiation. The discussion then addresses a potential counterexample from Hanafi fiqh: the case of a master claiming the eldest child from his slave woman, which seems to rely on mafhum al-mukhalafa (negating lineage of the younger children). The text explains that this is not due to mafhum al-mukhalafa but rather because of other legal principles: the master's silence when speech is required serves as a statement, and proof of lineage requires a claim. The condition 'in different wombs' is explained to avoid confusion with cases where multiple children are born in one pregnancy. This passage is a typical Hanafi usul exercise in distinguishing between apparent implications and actual legal causes.

Key Terms
عدمی (ʿadamī): A ruling that is negative in nature, i.e., the absence of a legal obligation or prohibition.
مفهوم مخالفة (mafhūm al-mukhālafah): The converse meaning: that when a ruling is attached to a description, the opposite ruling applies when the description is absent.
خروج مخرج العادة (kharāj makhraj al-ʿādah): When a description in a text is mentioned merely because it is customary (i.e., the usual situation), not to specify a legal condition.

Da'wa: Necessity for Proving Lineage of Later-Born Children
Explains when later children require a claim for lineage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a da'wa (lineage claim) is required in Hanafi law and apply the rule to determine the status of children born to a slave concubine.

Core Idea
The timing of the elder's claim determines if later children need a separate lineage claim.

This passage clarifies an important nuance in Hanafi family law regarding the automatic establishment of lineage for children of a slave concubine (umm walad). While the first child's lineage is proven without a formal claim, subsequent children may require a claim depending on when the father acknowledges the elder child. This lesson unpacks the rule and its application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
Recognize the factual situation: a master has a slave concubine who bears two sets of children: an elder child and later children. The elder child claims lineage.
The key variable is the timing of the elder's claim relative to the birth of later children.
2
Determine the timing of the elder's claim
Is the elder's claim made before or after the birth of the later children? This timing is decisive.
If the claim is before, later children are automatically considered children of the umm walad. If after, they are not.
3
Apply the rule
If the elder's claim comes after the later births, the later children are not children of the umm walad but of a regular slave woman (amah). Their lineage requires a formal da'wa from the master.
Without the da'wa, their lineage is not proven.
4
Define da'wa
The word da'wa (with a kasra on the dal) is exclusively used for claims regarding lineage (nasab), not other types of claims.
This legal definition dissects the term's specific usage.

The Hanafi–Shafi'i Dispute on Conditional Clauses
Explains the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on conditional clauses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on the role of the condition (shart) in conditional statements and apply the Hanafi principle that the condition and conditioned form one complete speech.

Core Idea
The condition and conditioned together form one complete speech; the ruling applies only upon fulfillment of the condition.

This passage presents a fundamental usul al-fiqh dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the interpretation of conditional statements. Understanding this dispute is crucial for correctly applying rulings in conditional divorce, manumission, and other ta'liq-based contracts.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the core question: does the conditioned (mashrut) alone carry the ruling, or does the condition (shart) form part of the complete speech that bears the ruling?
The condition is the 'if' clause; the conditioned is the result clause.
2
Identify the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the conditioned alone is the complete speech and bearer of the ruling. The condition is merely a restrictive qualifier, like a container that specifies a circumstance.
Thus, the conditioned applies to all circumstances except when the condition is negated.
3
Identify the Hanafi position
Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the condition and conditioned together form one complete speech. Neither part alone is a complete utterance. The ruling is only invoked upon fulfillment of the condition.
When the condition is absent, the statement is silent (sakit) about the ruling.
4
Analyze the proofs
Examine the linguistic argument: a conditional sentence like 'If you enter the house, you are divorced' is a single utterance. Removing the condition leaves an incomplete part, analogous to the word 'you' in 'you are divorced'.
This supports the Hanafi view that the condition is integral.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Apply the dispute to a concrete case: if a man says 'If you enter the house, you are divorced' and the wife never enters, the Hanafi school holds no divorce occurs (the condition is not fulfilled). The Shafi'i school would consider the divorce possibly negated only by the condition's absence, but the underlying generality of the conditioned might still apply.
The practical difference arises in cases where the condition is negated or when multiple circumstances exist.

Hanafi vs Shafi'i on the Causality of an Oath for Expiation
Defending Hanafi view that oath is not a cause for expiation until breach occurs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a mu'allaq cause does not become a sabab until the condition exists, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument distinguishing financial and bodily obligations.

Core Idea
An oath is only a condition, not a cause — only breach (hinth) triggers expiation.

This passage defends the Hanafi view that an oath itself is not the cause (sabab) of expiation (kaffarah) but merely a condition (shart). The true cause is the breach (hinth) of the oath. Against the Shafi'i position that the oath is the cause of obligation and the breach only the cause of performance, the Hanafis argue that a suspended cause (mu'allaq) cannot be effective before its condition. This lesson unpacks the dispute, including the ruling on paying expiation before breaking an oath and the underlying usul principles.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi claim: the oath is not a cause for expiation; the breach is the cause. The oath is only a condition.
The phrase 'عندنا لا ينعقد سببا الاعند وجود الشرط' is key.
2
Identify the Shafi'i position and its dalil
Present Imam al-Shafi'i's argument: the oath is the cause of obligation (wujub al-kaffarah) and the breach is the cause of performance (wujub al-ada). He supports this by distinguishing financial obligations (where separation between obligation and performance is possible) from bodily ones.
Look for 'يمين سبب ہے وجوب کفارہ کا اور حنت سبب ہے وجوب ادا کا'.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to the financial/bodily distinction
The Hanafis reject the distinction, arguing that even financial rights of Allah ultimately aim at a bodily action (al-ada'), so separation is not valid. They also note that the oath's purpose is to be fulfilled, not broken, so expiation is only triggered by breach.
The text says 'مال مقصود بالذات نہیں ہوتا بلکہ مقصود حقیقی ادارہ ہے'.
4
Examine the Hanafi critique of the Shafi'i analogy between condition and term/option
The Shafi'is analogize a suspensive condition (shart) to a term (ajal) or option (khiyar) in sale, which defer the ruling but do not prevent the cause from being contracted. The Hanafis differentiate: a term/option enters upon the ruling, not upon the cause, while a condition affects the very existence of the cause.
The text states 'الاجل وشرط الخيار داخلان علی الحکم'.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Shafi'is permit paying financial expiation before breach; Hanafis do not permit any expiation before breach. Also, fulfilling a vow before its condition is invalid according to Hanafis.
This difference applies to all suspended obligations, not just oaths.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi commentary defending the school's position on the causality (sababiyyah) of an oath for expiation (kaffarah) against the Shafi'i view. The text begins by presenting Imam al-Shafi'i's argument: if the expiation is financial (e.g., feeding or clothing), it can be performed before breaking the oath because the oath itself is the cause of obligation; the breach only triggers the obligation to pay. The Hanafi commentator then refutes this by distinguishing between financial and bodily obligations. In financial obligations, the essence of obligation (nafs al-wujub) and the obligation to perform (wujub al-ada) can be separated, as in a sale where the price becomes owed immediately but payment is deferred. Thus, a financial expiation paid before breach is valid even in the Hanafi view? Actually, the text later clarifies that according to Hanafis, a conditionally suspended cause does not become a cause until the condition exists. Therefore, the oath itself is not a cause; it is only a condition (shart). The actual cause of expiation is the breach (hinth). The commentator then extends this principle to other areas: suspension of divorce and manumission upon ownership is valid because ownership? is present from the start, but fulfilling a vow or expiation before the condition is not valid because the cause has not come into being. The passage concludes by rejecting Imam al-Shafi'i's distinction between financial and bodily acts for expiation, arguing that even financial acts in worship ultimately aim at a bodily action (the act of giving), so separation between obligation and performance is not valid. The author also refutes the analogy between a condition and a term/option in sale: the term and option affect the ruling, not the cause, while a condition in suspensive clauses affects the very existence of the cause. This is a dense usul dispute that showcases Hanafi reliance on the principle that a cause must be complete with all its parts, including the condition, to be effective.

Refuting the Shafi'i Distinction Between Financial and Bodily Acts of Worship
Refuting Imam Shafi'i's distinction between financial (mali) and bodily (badani) acts of worship.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi critique of Imam al-Shafi'i's distinction between financial and bodily acts of worship regarding the separation of obligation and performance, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) between the two schools.

Core Idea
In the rights of Allah, wealth is only a means; the real objective is performance, which is always bodily — hence no separation between obligation and performance exists.

Imam al-Shafi'i famously distinguished between financial acts of worship (like zakat) and bodily acts (like fasting), arguing that in financial acts the obligation itself can be separated from the obligation of performance. The Hanafi school vigorously rejects this distinction on the grounds that wealth is never an end in itself in the rights of Allah; rather, the ultimate goal is the act of performance, which is inherently bodily. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi critique and the practical consequences of the disagreement.

The Verb 'Amr' Is Not Literally Applied to Action
Hanafi response to Shafi'i claim that action indicates obligation like verbal command

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that only verbal command indicates obligation and analyze the Shafi'i counter-argument using the principles of ishtirak and taraduf.

Core Idea
The word 'amr' applies literally only to verbal command; applying it to action would create dispreferred homonymy.

In usul al-fiqh, a key dispute exists: does the Prophet's action (fi'l) alone indicate obligation, like his verbal command (amr)? The Hanafi school argues it does not. This lesson examines the linguistic and logical arguments from the Hanafi side, including the dangers of homonymy (ishtirak) and synonymity (taraduf), and the burden of proof in inter-madhhab debate.

Guided Path
1
State the central issue
Identify the dispute: whether the Prophet's action indicates obligation (wujub) in the same way his verbal command does.
This is a core usul question about the sources of obligation.
2
Analyze the Hanafi linguistic argument
Understand why applying 'amr' literally to action leads to homonymy (ishtirak), which is dispreferred in language. Also note the test: if action were command, negating command after action would be impossible.
The key is that 'amr' is primarily a verbal concept.
3
Examine the Shafi'i proof and the Hanafi response
The Shafi'i use the verse 'And the command of Pharaoh was not right' (11:97) and the Prophet's action of ordering qada prayers. The Hanafi response treats the verse as metaphorical (majaz) or referring to speech, and denies that the qada argument proves action indicates obligation.
Note the Hanafi use of majaz and the burden of proof.
4
Evaluate the burden of proof and the principle of taraduf
The Hanafi argue that the Shafi'i are claimants and must provide evidence; the Hanafi simply deny (man'). Also, if action indicated obligation, it would create synonymity (taraduf) with verbal command, which is against the linguistic norm.
These principles strengthen the Hanafi position.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul dispute with the Shafi'i school concerning whether the action (fi'l) of the Prophet (peace be upon him) can be considered a command (amr) that indicates obligation (ijab) just as his verbal command does. The context is a discussion on the nature of 'amr' in Islamic legal theory.

The opponent (Shafi'i) had argued that the Prophet's action of performing qada (making up missed prayers) in a specific order shows that sequence is obligatory, inferring that action indicates obligation. The Hanafi response is multi-layered.

First, the Hanafi argues that the word 'amr' is not literally applied to action. If it were, then 'amr' would be a homonym (mushtarak) between speech and action. Homonymy is discouraged (khilaf al-asl) because the default is that each word has one meaning. Moreover, if someone performs an action without uttering 'if'al' (do), it is correct to say 'he did not command.' If action were literally 'amr', that negation would be impossible. This shows that 'amr' is not literal for action.

Second, the opponent cited the Quranic verse 'And the command of Pharaoh was not right' (11:97) to prove that 'amr' can be applied to action. The Hanafi responds that this is a metaphorical usage (majaz): the command (cause) is applied to the action (effect) because the action becomes obligatory only through a verbal command. Alternatively, 'amr' in the verse refers to Pharaoh's speech, not his action; the description 'not right' is metaphorically applied to the speech because of the speaker's unrighteousness.

The Hanafi then concedes for argument's sake that 'amr' could be literal in the action, but insists that the evidences (such as the verse 'Let those who oppose his command beware' 24:63) show that only verbal command indicates obligation. In that verse, 'his command' by consensus refers to the speech of Allah or the Prophet, not action. Since the word 'amr' is shared between speech and action, and one meaning (speech) is agreed upon, the other cannot be intended in the same usage because a homonym cannot convey multiple meanings simultaneously.

Finally, the Hanafi invokes the burden of proof: the opponent is the claimant that action indicates obligation, so they must bring evidence. The Hanafi simply denies (man') that the cited proofs apply to action. Additionally, accepting action as an indicator of obligation would lead to synonymity (taraduf) between speech and action for the same meaning (obligation), which is against the linguistic principle that multiple words for the same meaning are not the default.

The passage ends before completing the response to the qada argument, but the overall position is clear: only verbal command (amr qawli) indicates obligation; action does not.

Key Terms
أمر (amr): A word that primarily means a verbal command (qawl) to do something. It is not literally applied to action (fi'l).
فعل (fi'l): An action or deed performed by a person, distinct from speech.
مجاز (majaz): Metaphorical usage; applying a word to a meaning other than its original due to a relationship (e.g., cause-effect).
ترادف (taraduf): Synonymity: multiple words having the same meaning. It is considered against the default (khilaf al-asl) in language.
اشتراك (ishtirak): Homonymy: a single word having multiple meanings. It is also dispreferred compared to having a single meaning.

Refuting the Shafi'i Claim: Does the Prophet's Action Indicate Obligation?
Hanafi rebuttal of Shafi'i claim that Prophet's action obligates

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that only verbal commands (amr qawli) indicate obligation, and evaluate the linguistic and textual arguments supporting this position.

Core Idea
Only verbal commands indicate obligation; action alone cannot obligate.

One of the foundational disputes between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools is whether the Prophet's actions (af'al) can establish obligation (wujub) independently. The Hanafi position is clear: only his verbal commands (amr) carry that force. In this passage, the Hanafis mount a multi-pronged refutation, drawing on Quranic interpretation, linguistic principles, and Prophetic practice. Let us walk through their arguments step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
The Shafi'is argue that the Prophet's action can indicate obligation. The Hanafis reject this: only verbal commands do. Note the thamarah al-khilaf: if a layperson sees the Prophet perform an action, must they follow it as wajib?
This is a fundamental usul disagreement.
2
Analyze the Quranic verse (24:63)
The Hanafis cite 'فَلْيَحْذَرِ الَّذِينَ يُخَالِفُونَ عَنْ أَمْرِهِ' (Quran 24:63). They argue that 'amr' here refers to speech, not action. If the pronoun refers to Allah, it must be speech; if to the Prophet, by consensus it is his verbal command. Thus, opposing action without verbal command is not sinful.
This establishes that the threat of punishment attaches only to disobeying verbal commands.
3
Examine the synonymy (taraduf) argument
If both speech and action are taken to indicate the same meaning (obligation), that would be synonymy (taraduf). The Hanafis hold that synonymy is contrary to linguistic principle (al-asl) because one meaning should have one expression.
This is a linguistic efficiency argument.
4
Address the Shafi'i counter-argument from prayer ordering
The Shafi'is claimed that the Prophet's action of ordering the making up of prayers shows that his actions indicate obligation. The Hanafis respond that the obligation of order stems from his words 'صلوا كما رأيتموني أصلي' (Pray as you see me praying), not from the action itself.
Thus the action is not the source of obligation.
5
Consider the Prophet's disapproval of imitation
The text gives two examples: continuous fasting (sawm al-wisal) and removing sandals in prayer. When the Companions imitated these actions, the Prophet disapproved. If actions obligated, he would not have disapproved.
This is a practical proof that actions do not carry obligatory force.

Amr after Nahy: Default Obligation in Hanafi Usul
Explains the rule for commands after prohibitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for amr after nahy, apply it to identify when a qarina is present, and evaluate opposing arguments using Quranic examples.

Core Idea
A command after a prohibition defaults to obligation unless a contextual clue proves otherwise.

This passage resolves a key usul dispute: when Allah commands something previously prohibited, does the command indicate obligation, recommendation, or permissibility? The Hanafi school holds firmly to the original meaning of 'amr' (obligation) regardless of timing, while other scholars see a shift due to prohibition. The author defends the Hanafi view by showing that the opposing proofs contain contextual clues that explain the deviation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
State the Hanafi position: a command that occurs after a prohibition indicates obligation (wujub) unless there is a contextual clue (qarina) that diverts it to another meaning.
This is derived from the general principle that 'amr is for wujub'.
2
Examine the Hanafi dalil
The proof is the default usage of amr in the Arabic language and Sharia, which is obligation. The fact that it comes after a prohibition does not change this default.
The author says: 'the mentioned proofs do not differentiate whether the command occurs after a prohibition or not'.
3
Analyze opposing views
Some scholars say such a command indicates recommendation (nadb), citing Quran 62:10 'wa-ibtaghū min faḍli llāh' after the prohibition of trade during Friday prayer. Others say it indicates permissibility (ibāḥa), citing Quran 5:2 'faṣṭādū' after the prohibition of hunting in ihram.
These are the strongest counterarguments.
4
Understand the Hanafi response
Those verses contain qarain (contextual clues) that indicate nadb or ibāḥa. For example, making the command obligatory would cause hardship (ḥaraj) for people's livelihoods, or the purpose is merely to lift the prohibition. Without such a qarina, the command remains for wujub.
The qarina is the key: obligation would turn benefit into harm.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practically, if a command after a prohibition appears with no qarina, Hanafis rule it as wājib; others may rule it as mustaḥabb or mubāḥ. This affects daily acts and ibādāt.
Example: the command to 'seek provision' after Friday prayer is interpreted as recommended, not obligatory, because of the qarina of hardship.

Amr for Ibaha: Majaz or Haqiqa Qasira?
Analyzing whether the usage of amr for ibaha is majaz or haqiqa qasira

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school classifies the use of amr for ibaha as haqiqa qasira rather than majaz, and evaluate the opposing argument.

Core Idea
When amr indicates ibaha, it indicates only the permissibility of the act—a part of the original meaning—making it haqiqa qasira, not majaz.

This lesson examines a nuanced usul dispute: whether the command (amr) used for permission (ibaha) is figurative (majaz) or a limited real usage (haqiqa qasira). The Hanafi position, championed by Fakhral-Islam, argues that only part of the meaning is indicated, preserving real indication. Understanding this distinction is critical for correctly deriving rulings from commands in the Quran and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State the two views: sometimes amr for ibaha is considered majaz because ibaha is opposite to wujub; other times (Hanafi) it is haqiqa qasira because only part of the meaning is intended.
The opposite relationship (mughayara) is the key point of contention.
2
Understand the Hanafi dalil
The Hanafi argument is that amr inherently indicates wujub, but when used for ibaha it only indicates jawaz al-fi'l (permissibility to act), which is a part of the meaning of wujub. It does not indicate jawaz al-tark (permissibility to leave), which is the second part of ibaha.
Jawaz al-tark is established by the absence of a prohibition, not by the command itself.
3
Examine the opposing argument
The opposing view claims that ibaha is opposite to wujub, and applying a word to its opposite is a clear case of majaz, requiring a relationship (alaqa) like the one between 'asad' and a brave man.
The relationship for majaz is typically based on shared attribute (shabah).
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that the command does not indicate the full opposite (ibaha) but only one part (jawaz al-fi'l) which is actually a part of the original meaning of wujub. Thus, it is indication of the whole upon a part (dhalalah al-kull 'ala al-juz'), which is a type of real indication, not figurative.
This is called haqiqa qasira because the usage is real but limited to a part.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence is how we interpret commands: if it is majaz, we need a separate indicator (qarina) to determine the intended meaning; if it is haqiqa qasira, the usage is still real and the part meaning is directly indicated without needing a qarina for figurative transfer.
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi position (haqiqa qasira) as chosen by Fakhral-Islam.

Commentary
This passage discusses the nature of the command (amr) when used to indicate permissibility (ibaha) or recommendation (nadb). The author explains the disagreement: some claim it is majaz (figurative) because ibaha is opposite to wujub, and applying a word to its opposite is typically majaz. However, the Hanafi position, following Fakhral-Islam, is that it is haqiqa qasira (limited real usage) because the command only indicates one part of ibaha—the permissibility of the act (jawaz al-fi'l)—not the permissibility of leaving (jawaz al-tark). The latter is established by the absence of a prohibition, not by the command itself. Thus, the indication is of the whole (the full literal meaning of amr) upon a part (jawaz al-fi'l), which is a type of real indication, not figurative. The text uses the example of 'asad' (lion) applied to a brave man, which is majaz because the common attribute (bravery) allows metaphorical transfer. But here, the common part (jawaz al-fi'l) is actually part of the original meaning of wujub, so it is haqiqa qasira, not isti'ara.

Key Terms
حقیقہ قاصرہ (haqiqa qasira): A real (haqiqi) usage that indicates only a part of the original meaning, not the whole.
مجاز (majaz): Figurative usage where a word is used for something other than its original meaning.
دلالة الكل على الجزء (dhalalah al-kull 'ala al-juz'): Indication of the whole upon the part; a type of real indication.

Preference for Immediate Qada of I'tikaf Despite Loss of Sharf al-Waqt
Explaining why immediate qada of missed i'tikaf is more cautious than waiting for next Ramadan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the rational proof (dalil aqli) used in the Hanafi preference for immediate qada of missed i'tikaf, and explain why immediate performance is considered ahwat (more cautious).

Core Idea
Immediate qada of missed i'tikaf is more cautious than waiting for next Ramadan because the risk of death outweighs the benefit of temporal virtue.

When a vowed i'tikaf in Ramadan is missed, one must make it up. A key question arises: should the qada be performed immediately (in the current non-Ramadan time) or delayed until the next Ramadan to benefit from its special virtue? This passage explores the Hanafi reasoning, employing the principle of ahwat (precaution) and a rational proof based on the possibility of death. Understanding this analysis sharpens your ability to weigh competing considerations in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting options
The passage presents two possible ways to make up a missed i'tikaf in Ramadan: (1) perform qada immediately in the current non-Ramadan time, thereby losing the special virtue (sharf al-waqt) of Ramadan, or (2) wait for the next Ramadan to perform qada with full virtue. Label these Option A and Option B.
The author calls Option A 'وجوب القضاء مع سقوط شرف الوقت' and Option B 'وجوب القضاء مع شرف الوقت'.
2
Define the key concepts: sharf al-waqt and ahwat
Sharf al-waqt is the extra merit associated with performing worship in a specific time, like Ramadan. Ahwat means the more cautious ruling, prioritizing the obligation over optional virtue. This passage argues that Option A is ahwat.
The passage states: 'لحاظ الأولوية أن سقوط النقصان أولى من سقوط الزيادة' – dropping the deficiency (missing the worship) is more important than dropping the increase (temporal virtue).
3
Trace the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The proof is based on the possibility of death before the next Ramadan (امکان الموت). Because death is possible at any time, waiting for the next Ramadan risks losing the worship entirely. Therefore, immediate performance is safer.
The text says: 'لما ذكرنا من امکان الموت' – 'for what we mentioned of the possibility of death.' This is a rational, not scriptural, proof.
4
Explain the priority (اولویت) reasoning
The passage uses a priority argument: the increase (virtue of time) is lost when the original Ramadan passes, but the deficiency (non-performance of the intended fast) remains. Since establishing worship (صوم مقصود) is more important than preserving temporal virtue, it is better to remove the deficiency promptly. The loss of the increase is certain, but the worship can only be secured by immediate action.
The text compares 'سقوط النقصان' (dropping deficiency) and 'سقوط الزيادة' (dropping increase), concluding that dropping the deficiency is 'أولى' (more important).
5
Summarize the conclusion and its basis in Fakhr al-Islam
The author endorses Fakhr al-Islam's statement that immediate qada is the more cautious of the two aspects. The reasoning relies on rational precaution, not on a direct textual command. This shows how usul al-fiqh uses rational arguments to resolve apparent conflicts between obligation and virtue.
The key principle applied is that when optional virtue conflicts with obligatory fulfillment, the obligation takes precedence due to the risk of loss.

Manafi' as Non-Mutaqawwim: Hanafi Principle and Shafi'i Objections
States the Hanafi rule that usufructs are not valuable property and responds to Shafi'i objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that usufructs (manafi') are not mutaqawwim and evaluate the Shafi'i objections using the concepts of 'ayn substitution and contractual valuation.

Core Idea
Usufructs are not valuable property because they lack permanence; their value in contracts is accidental, not inherent.

In this passage, the Hanafi school establishes a fundamental principle: usufructs (manafi') are not considered valuable property (mutaqawwim) and therefore cannot be guaranteed when lost. The reasoning is based on the nature of usufructs as accidents (a'rad) that have no permanence. The text then presents the Shafi'i objections based on the validity of ijarah and nikah contracts, and offers a careful response distinguishing between inherent value and contractual substitution.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafis hold that usufructs (manafi') are not mutaqawwim and not subject to daman (guarantee) when lost.
This is a core principle in Hanafi Fiqh of transactions.
2
Examine the reasoning
Why? Because usufructs lack permanence (baqa') and cannot be preserved (ihraz), making them non-appraisable. They are classified as a'rad (accidents).
Preservation (ihraz) is key to being mutaqawwim.
3

Commentary
This passage establishes the Hanafi principle that usufructs (manafi') are not considered valuable property (mutaqawwim) and therefore cannot be the subject of a guarantee (daman) when lost. The reasoning is that usufructs are accidents (a'rad) with no permanence (baqa'), so they cannot be preserved (ihraz) and are not appraisable. The text then presents the Shafi'i objection: how can ijarah (lease) contracts be valid if usufructs are non-valuable? The Hanafi response is that in ijarah, the 'ayn (corporeal property) stands in place of the usufruct and is the actual subject of guarantee; the usufruct is merely incidental. A further objection is raised from nikah (marriage), where the usufruct of the wife is considered valuable as mahr. The response distinguishes that in marriage, the usufruct becomes valuable due to the contract (the mahr is given in exchange for the right of enjoyment), but this does not mean the usufruct is inherently valuable. Evidence is given: in khul' (divorce by compensation), when the usufruct of the private part is separated from the marriage contract, it is no longer valuable, showing that its value is contingent on the contract, not inherent. The passage thus defends the Hanafi position that manafi' are not inherently mutaqawwim, and the Shafi'i view is a valid ikhtilaf. The author follows a logical order: statement of the rule, examples, objections, and responses.

اجارہ (ijarah): A contract of lease where the 'ayn (corporeal property) is the actual subject, and the usufruct is incidental.
