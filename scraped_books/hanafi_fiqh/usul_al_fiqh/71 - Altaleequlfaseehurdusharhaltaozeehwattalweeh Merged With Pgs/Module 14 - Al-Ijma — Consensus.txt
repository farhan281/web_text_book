The Four Sources and Their Exhaustiveness
Classification and defense of the four sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four sources of Islamic law (Kitab, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) and explain how other claimed sources reduce to these four within Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Every legal proof reduces to one of the four: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, or Qiyas.

This passage from al-Talwih provides a systematic classification of the four sources of Islamic law and defends their exclusive sufficiency. The commentator presents two complementary ways to identify the four sources and then addresses the objection that other sources—like the laws of previous prophets, common practice, companion statements, and rational inference—are independent. By analyzing each, the text demonstrates that all such sources refer back to one of the four, thereby confirming the confinement.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two methods of limitation
The passage gives two ways to confine proofs to four: (a) revelation vs non-revelation, and within revelation: recited (Quran) vs unrecited (Sunnah); non-revelation: consensus (Ijma) vs analogical reasoning (Qiyas). (b) source: from Prophet or not; if from Prophet: miraculous (Quran) vs non-miraculous (Sunnah); if not from Prophet: infallible source (Ijma) vs fallible (Qiyas).
The second method emphasizes the condition of infallibility for Ijma.
2
Identify the objection
The objection states that other proofs like shara'i min qablana, ta'amul, qawl sahabi, tahari, akhdh bil-ahwat, akhdh bil-zahir, and al-ma'qul appear to be independent sources, so the confinement to four seems invalid.
3
Trace the reduction of each alternative source
For each claimed source, the answer states it refers back to one or more of the four. For example: shara'i min qablana are valid only if mentioned in Quran or Sunnah (so fall under Kitab or Sunnah); ta'amul from virtuous generations is a form of Ijma; qawl sahabi not based on qiyas reflects Sunnah, otherwise it is Qiyas; tahari relies on hadith (Sunnah); ahwat and zahir are methods of interpreting the four sources; ma'qul reduces to understanding the text (Qiyas or textual interpretation).
This shows the exhaustiveness of the four sources.
4
Confirm the confinement
By showing that every claimed source reduces to one of the four, the confinement stands. This reinforces the Hanafi principle that the four are exhaustive.
The commentator's rebuttal is a rational (aqlī) argument supporting the enumeration.

The commentator said in al-Talwih: 'His statement, “And the usul of fiqh has preceded,” was an explanation of the concept of usul al-fiqh.' That is, what preceded was an explanation of the meaning of usul al-fiqh, and from here begins the explanation of its instances (masadiq), i.e., the explanation of the referents, which, by way of induction, are confined to the four. The reason for confining them to these four is the following proof of limitation: The legal proof (dalil shar'i) is either revelation (wahy) or non-revelation. On the first branch, if revelation is recited (mutlu), then it is the Book of Allah; otherwise it is the Sunnah. The second branch, i.e., non-revelation, if it is the statement of the entire ummah in a particular age, then it is ijma; otherwise it is qiyas. Or, the second proof of limitation: The proof reaches us either from the Messenger (peace be upon him) or not. On the first branch, if its wording and composition have a quality of inimitability (i'jaz), i.e., its composition is miraculous such that all humans are incapable of producing its like, then it is the Book of Allah; if its composition is not miraculous, then it is the Sunnah. The second branch, i.e., the proof that does not reach us from the Messenger but issues from other persons, then we see: if infallibility from error (isma) is a condition for those persons, then it is ijma (indicated by the hadith 'My ummah will not agree upon error'); if infallibility is not a condition, then it is qiyas (as the mujtahid may err or be correct). And as for the laws of those before us (shara'i min qablana) and so on, they have been confined to the four. An objection used to arise: besides these four proofs, the laws of those before us—i.e., the law of the Torah and the Gospel that is not distorted—are also proofs for rulings. Similarly, the practice (ta'amul) among the common people, the statement of a Companion (qawl sahabi), and similar things, e.g., tahari (ijtihad seeking certainty), akhdh bil-zahir (taking by the apparent meaning), akhdh bil-ahwat (taking the precautionary)—these are also proofs for rulings. Thus the confinement to the four would not be correct. So the answer was given: They refer back (raji') to the four, i.e., these things refer back to those four proofs; they are not outside them. Because the laws of those before us are proofs only when they are mentioned in the Quran or the hadith of the Messenger without disapproval; if mentioned in the Quran, then the inference is by the Book; if mentioned in the hadith and Sunnah, then inference is by the Sunnah. And practice (ta'amul) refers back to ijma, provided that the practice is among the virtuous generations (qurun fadilah) without disapproval; no consideration is given to practice that occurs thereafter. And the statement of a Companion that is not attainable by qiyas refers back to the Sunnah; otherwise it falls under qiyas. And tahari and other things also refer back to them, because tahari involves the testimony of the heart, so it is action based on the hadith 'ask for a fatwa from your heart'; and akhdh bil-ahwat involves acting with both proofs (when they conflict); similarly, akhdh bil-zahir involves taking by the apparent meaning of the proof. And al-ma'qul (the rational) is a type of inference (istidlal) which some have considered a fifth type of proof and named 'istidlal', but in reality it refers back to clinging to the rational meaning of the text (ma'qul al-nass) or to the rational (bima'qul).

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed classification of the four sources of Islamic law and defends the confinement of valid proofs to these four against objections. The commentator explains two ways to categorize the four: first, by whether the proof is revelation or not, and if revelation, whether it is recited (Quran) or not (Sunnah); non-revelation is either ijma (consensus of the ummah) or qiyas. Second, by whether the proof comes from the Prophet or not; if from the Prophet, it is either miraculous in composition (Quran) or not (Sunnah); if not from the Prophet, the source's infallibility is considered: if the source is infallible (the entire ummah), it is ijma; if fallible (individual mujtahid), it is qiyas. The passage then addresses the objection that other proofs like the laws of previous prophets (shara'i min qablana), common practice (ta'amul), companion statements (qawl sahabi), tahari (ijtihad based on inner conviction), akhdh bil-ahwat (precautionary choice), akhdh bil-zahir (literal interpretation), and al-ma'qul (rational inference) are separate sources. The rebuttal is that all these refer back to the four: shara'i min qablana are only valid if mentioned in Quran or Sunnah, hence fall under Kitab or Sunnah; ta'amul is a form of ijma if from the virtuous generations; companion statements either reflect Sunnah (if not based on qiyas) or are qiyas; tahari is based on hadith; akhdh bil-ahwat and akhdh bil-zahir are methods of applying the two proofs; and ma'qul/istidlal is subsumed under understanding the rationale of the text, which is part of qiyas or textual interpretation. Thus the confinement to four sources stands.

Key Terms
شرائع من قبلنا (shara'i min qablana): The laws of previous prophets; they are proofs only when mentioned in Quran or Sunnah without disapproval, thus subsumed under Kitab or Sunnah.
تعامل (ta'amul): The prevailing practice among people; it refers back to ijma if it is from the virtuous generations (qurun fadilah) and not disapproved.
قول صحابی (qawl sahabi): Statement of a Companion; if not based on qiyas, it refers back to Sunnah; otherwise it falls under qiyas.
المعقول (al-ma'qul): Rational inference; it is subsumed under understanding the rationale of the text (ma'qul al-nass) and thus refers back to qiyas or textual interpretation.

The Consensus Proof for Generality of Definite Plural
Providing ijma proof for generality of definite plural

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the tacit consensus (ijma suktuti) proof that the definite plural indicates generality, and apply it to validate the Hanafi rule that amm is qat'i.

Core Idea
The Companions' silence on 'al-a'immatu min quraysh' proves the definite plural is general by tacit consensus.

In this passage, the author presents a powerful linguistic proof from consensus (ijma). At the Saqifa after the Prophet's death, Abu Bakr invoked the hadith 'al-a'immatu min quraysh' to argue that all leaders must be from Quraysh. The Ansar, native speakers of Arabic, did not object to the use of the definite plural to mean 'all leaders.' Their silence is taken as tacit consensus that the definite plural indicates generality, not just some. Let's examine this proof step by step.

Guided Path
1
Contextualize the Saqifa incident
Understand the historical dispute between Muhajirun and Ansar over leadership. The Ansar proposed two leaders, one from each group. Abu Bakr countered with the hadith 'al-a'immatu min quraysh'.
The Ansar had strong political motives to object if the hadith didn't apply to all leaders.
2
Identify the proof
The proof is the Companions' acceptance of the hadith without objection. Their silence is considered tacit consensus (ijma suktuti) that the definite plural al-a'immatu includes every individual leader.
Hanafis accept ijma suktuti as a valid proof.
3
Analyze the linguistic inference
The Ansar, being native Arabic speakers, would have objected if the plural could mean 'some leaders.' Their silence shows they understood the plural as general. Thus, the definite plural (jam' muḥallā bi al-lām) indicates generality.
This inference relies on the principle that native speakers' tacit agreement on linguistic usage is authoritative.
4
Apply the principle
This proof is used to establish that any definite plural in the Quran and Sunnah is general ('āmm) unless specified otherwise. It strengthens the Hanafi position that amm is qat'i in its indication.
Other proofs (e.g., exception) also support this.

And their holding fast: This is a second proof for the generality of the definite plural, which is based on consensus. Its summary is that after the death of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him), there was disagreement among the Companions about the caliphate. The Ansar said: 'An emir from us and an emir from you' — meaning one leader of the Muslims from us, the Ansar, and one from you, the Muhajirun. Abu Bakr adhered to the hadith, presenting the saying of the Messenger of Allah: 'The leaders (al-a'immatu) are from Quraysh.' No one denied it. So they accepted that the meaning is that the leader is from Quraysh, not from others. So it became known that the word al-a'immatu, which is a definite plural, is for istighraq, including every individual leader. If it were not general, the Ansar, who were people of the language, would have objected that it is not for generality, so some would be from you and some from us. So it became known that the people of the language are in consensus that the definite plural is for generality. And the validity of exception: That is, making an exception from the definite plural is valid. This is proof that the definite plural, which is the thing from which exception is made (mustathna minhu), includes all individuals, so that the excepted item is certainly included in it, and its exclusion by the particle of exception is valid from multiple individuals. If the mustathna minhu intended not all individuals but some, then it is possible that the mustathna minhu includes those some, and the excepted item is not included in it; then how could the exception be valid? So the exception from the definite plural is proof that this plural is inclusive of all individuals. For example: 'The angels prostrated except Iblis.' He said: 'From our understanding, this is the assembly.' The mashayikh said: The definite plural is used metaphorically in the meaning of the genus, and at that time its plurality becomes invalid, and it begins to apply to one and more. For example, if a person swears, 'I will not marry women (al-nisa),' here al-nisa is plural, but in this context its meaning is the genus of woman, which applies to one and many. So he becomes an oath-breaker by marrying one woman. Similarly, in the saying of Allah, 'Indeed the charities are for the poor (al-fuqara),' the word al-fuqara is in the meaning of the genus of poor person, so the plural intends one poor person. On the same branch: If a person makes a will of his property saying, 'Give this property to Zayd and to the poor (al-fuqara),' then half of the property goes to Zayd and half to the poor, whether one or many, because here the word al-fuqara has lost its plurality; only the genus of poor is intended. If it had retained its plurality, at least three individuals of the plural would be taken, and Zayd would be the fourth, so Zayd in that case... (text cut off).

Commentary
This passage presents two additional proofs that the definite plural indicates generality. The first is a consensus (ijma) proof: at the Saqifa incident after the Prophet's death, the Ansar wanted leaders from both groups, but Abu Bakr used the hadith 'al-a'immatu min quraysh' (the leaders are from Quraysh). The Ansar did not object, showing they understood the definite plural as including all individuals (i.e., every leader must be from Quraysh). Their silence is considered a tacit consensus (ijma suktuti) of the people of language that the definite plural is general. The second proof is from the validity of exception (istithna): one can validly say 'the angels prostrated except Iblis'; if the definite plural 'the angels' did not include all angels, the exception would not be valid because the excepted item may not have been included. Thus the definite plural must be inclusive of all individuals. The passage then discusses a nuance: sometimes the definite plural is used metaphorically to mean the genus (jins), losing its plurality. Examples: in an oath not to marry women (la atazawwaj al-nisa), breaking the oath occurs even by marrying one woman because 'al-nisa' here means the genus, not all women. The Exception Proof and the Genus Metaphor
Providing exception proof and genus metaphor for definite plural

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to use the validity of exception to prove the generality of the definite plural, and distinguish between its literal general and metaphorical genus usages in oaths, Quranic verses, and bequests.

Core Idea
Valid exception from a definite plural proves it includes all individuals; its metaphorical use as genus applies to one or more without requiring plurality.

This passage introduces a second proof: the validity of exception (istithna). If you can validly say 'the angels prostrated except Iblis,' the word 'the angels' must include every angel, otherwise the exception would not be meaningful. The passage also discusses a key nuance: sometimes the definite plural is used metaphorically to indicate the genus (jins), losing its plural meaning. For example, in an oath 'I will not marry women,' marrying one woman breaks the oath because 'women' here means the genus. This nuance affects how we interpret many legal texts.

Guided Path
1
Understand the exception proof
The proof says: If you say 'the men came except Zayd,' the word 'men' must include Zayd for the exception to work. Similarly, 'the angels prostrated except Iblis' shows that 'angels' includes Iblis (who was a jinn among them). Thus the definite plural is general.
This is a linguistic rational proof, not a textual one.
2
Apply exception to Quranic examples
The passage cites the Quranic example 'سجد الملائكة إلا إبليس' to illustrate the point. The validity of the exception proves that 'الملائكة' (the angels) includes all angels.
This supports the Hanafi view that the definite plural is for generality.
3
Identify the metaphorical shift to genus
The mashayikh (scholars) noted that sometimes the definite plural is used metaphorically for the genus. In such usage, it loses its plural meaning and applies to one or more individuals. The example: an oath 'la atazawwaj al-nisa' – 'al-nisa' means the genus of women, so marrying one woman breaks the oath.
This is a shift from the literal general meaning to a metaphorical one.
4
Analyze the practical implications
In the Quranic verse 'innama al-sadaqat li al-fuqara', the word 'al-fuqara' means the genus of poor, so one poor person qualifies. Similarly, in a will giving to 'al-fuqara', half goes to the poor as a genus, whether one or many.
If the plural retained its full generality, it would require at least three individuals in some interpretations.
5
Compare with opposing view
Some other schools might hold that the definite plural always retains its plural sense and cannot indicate a single individual. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is in the interpretation of wills: Hanafis allow one poor person to receive the share, while others might require three or more.
This shows how a seemingly linguistic dispute affects real-world fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage presents two additional proofs that the definite plural indicates generality. The first is a consensus (ijma) proof: at the Saqifa incident after the Prophet's death, the Ansar wanted leaders from both groups, but Abu Bakr used the hadith 'al-a'immatu min quraysh' (the leaders are from Quraysh). The Ansar did not object, showing they understood the definite plural as including all individuals (i.e., every leader must be from Quraysh). Their silence is considered a tacit consensus (ijma suktuti) of the people of language that the definite plural is general. The second proof is from the validity of exception (istithna): one can validly say 'the angels prostrated except Iblis'; if the definite plural 'the angels' did not include all angels, the exception would not be valid because the excepted item may not have been included. Thus the definite plural must be inclusive of all individuals. The passage then discusses a nuance: sometimes the definite plural is used metaphorically to mean the genus (jins), losing its plurality. Examples: in an oath not to marry women (la atazawwaj al-nisa), breaking the oath occurs even by marrying one woman because 'al-nisa' here means the genus, not all women. In the verse 'innama al-sadaqat li al-fuqara', the word 'al-fuqara' means the genus, so one poor person qualifies. Similarly, in a will giving to 'al-fuqara', half goes to the poor as a genus, whether one or many. The passage ends mid-sentence discussing the scenario if the plural retained its full generality, requiring at least three individuals.
