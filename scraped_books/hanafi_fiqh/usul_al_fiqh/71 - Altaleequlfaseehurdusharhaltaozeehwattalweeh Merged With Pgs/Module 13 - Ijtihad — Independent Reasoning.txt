The Shafi'i Condition of Acquisition in Defining Fiqh: Terminology, Not Necessity
A critical refinement of a Hanafi objection to the Shafi'i definition of fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Shafi'i requirement that fiqh must be acquired through reasoning and evaluate the Hanafi critique that the condition is logically unnecessary.

Core Idea
Fiqh is defined by Shafi'is as knowledge acquired through ijtihad; necessary divine knowledge is excluded — but this is a terminological choice, not a logical flaw.

Definitions in usul al-fiqh can vary across madhahib, and understanding these differences is essential for scholarly discourse. This passage addresses a Shafi'i condition in their definition of fiqh: that knowledge must be obtained through reasoning. A Hanafi author objected that this condition is redundant, but the commentator defends the Shafi'i position, arguing that terminology is arbitrary and each school may define terms as they see fit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Shafi'i condition
Locate where the passage states that fiqh, according to the Shafi'is, is knowledge of rulings acquired through consideration and reasoning (ijtihad/istidlal). Note that necessary matters (e.g., obligation of prayer) are excluded.
The condition is stated as 'iktisab' or 'istidlal'.
2
Understand the author's objection
The author (a Hanafi) argues that the Shafi'i condition is unnecessary because fiqh is a comprehensive term; knowing only one or two issues does not make one a faqih anyway, so excluding necessary knowledge is redundant.
The objection relies on the logical point that partial knowledge does not qualify as fiqh.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i response
The commentator explains that the condition is not about logical necessity but about clarifying the boundaries of their own terminology. Since the Shafi'is define fiqh as acquired knowledge, they must explicitly exclude what is not acquired (necessary knowledge).
Imam Razi's addition of 'not known from religion by necessity' in al-Mahsul is cited.
4
Recognize the resolution
The passage concludes with the principle 'la mushaḥaḥa fi al-istiṣlaḥ' — there is no dispute over terminology. Each school may define its terms, and the condition is valid within the Shafi'i framework.
This principle is crucial for cross-madhhab debates: arguments based on differing definitions are often resolved by acknowledging terminological autonomy.
5
Extract the lesson for usul
Definitions in usul al-fiqh are tools; understanding the underlying logic of each school's definition is key to avoiding pseudo-disputes. The Hanafi objection misunderstands the Shafi'i condition as a logical necessity when it is a terminological clarification.
This lesson highlights the importance of distinguishing between substantive legal disagreement and terminological differences.

His statement: 'And it is not added to it' – the terminology of the Shafi'is is that knowledge of rulings is only called fiqh when that knowledge is obtained through consideration and deduction. So much so that knowledge of the obligation of prayer and fasting, etc., which are well-known and self-evident matters of religion that both a religious person and a non-religious person know — their knowledge, according to the Shafi'is, is not counted as terminological fiqh. For this reason, these scholars mention the condition of acquisition or deduction in the definition of fiqh. Imam Razi in al-Mahsul added the condition 'that it is not known to be from religion by necessity' and said: This is to avoid knowledge of the obligation of prayer and fasting, etc., because that knowledge is not called fiqh, meaning it does not enter into the naming of fiqh, and it is not counted as fiqh, as he clarified in the condition of 'practical'. Just as in the condition of 'practical', Razi clarified that it avoids knowledge that consensus, analogy, and solitary report are proofs, despite being legal rulings, but that knowledge is not called fiqh. So avoidance is made with the condition of 'practical'. Similarly, knowledge of self-evident matters is also not called fiqh terminologically, so the condition 'not known from religion by necessity' is used to avoid them. This does not mean that if this condition were not used, it would necessarily imply that whoever knows only the obligation of prayer and fasting would be a faqih, as the author thought and objected to this condition. That necessity does not arise because 'faqih' is one who has fiqh, and fiqh is not called knowledge of some rulings, even if few; even someone who knows three rare deductive issues cannot be called a faqih, so how can someone who knows one or two issues be called a faqih? So when the Shafi'is' terminology is that knowledge of necessary matters of religion is not counted as fiqh, then excluding them from the definition of fiqh is necessary for them. Therefore, this exclusionary condition will not be wasted, and calling such issues fiqh is not correct according to them, and their terminologies cannot be objected to. Hence, there is no dispute over terminology.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement explaining the Shafi'i position that knowledge of religious rulings is only called fiqh if obtained through reasoning (ijtihad/istidlal). Imam Razi added a condition to exclude matters known necessarily from religion, like the obligation of prayer. The author had objected that this condition is unnecessary because knowledge of a few rulings does not make one a faqih. The commentator responds that the condition is still needed to clearly exclude such knowledge from the definition, as per Shafi'i terminology. Since terminology is arbitrary, there is no harm in their usage.

Key Terms
اکتساب (iktisāb): Acquisition of knowledge through reasoning and deduction, as opposed to necessary knowledge.
ضروریات دین (ḍarūriyyāt al-dīn): Matters of religion known by necessity, such as the obligation of prayer.
مسمی الفقہ (musammā al-fiqh): The naming/definition of fiqh as a technical term.

Tahawwur Qarib: External Obstacles Do Not Negate the Faculty
Refuting objections to the definition of tahawwur qarib

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that external obstacles like conflicting evidences do not negate the mujtahid's capacity (tahawwur qarib) and identify the correct response to such obstacles.

Core Idea
A mujtahid's temporary failure to know a ruling does not prove lack of tahawwur qarib; external obstacles are resolved through preference, qiyas, or suspension.

In this lesson, we examine a key debate in Hanafi usul: whether external obstacles like conflicting evidences or doubts can negate the condition of tahawwur qarib (near-mastery) for a mujtahid. The author (Sadr al-Shariah) refutes the commentator's objections, arguing that a fully qualified mujtahid can still derive rulings despite such obstacles. This clarifies that occasional ignorance does not undermine the mujtahid's capacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four objections raised by the commentator
List the four factors the commentator claims negate tahawwur qarib: ta'arud (conflicting evidences), mani' (preventer), wahm (doubt), and mushakala (semblance).
These are presented as causes for lack of knowledge.
2
Analyze the refutation for conflicting evidences
Understand that conflicting evidences do not force ignorance: if one evidence is preferred, a ruling is known; if not, qiyas or tawaqquf (suspension) provides a ruling.
Tawaqquf itself is a valid ruling in Hanafi usul.
3
Analyze the refutation for preventer (mani')
Recognize that if a preventer exists, it means conditions are incomplete; such a person would not be called a faqih. Yet even one who occasionally errs is still called a faqih, showing the objection fails.
The example of one who did not know a ruling during Abu Hanifa's lifetime is used.
4
Analyze the refutation for doubt and semblance
A fully qualified mujtahid with firm malaka cannot be persistently overcome by doubt or permanently confused by semblance; such obstacles are temporary.
Permanent inability would contradict having all conditions.
5
Conclude the Hanafi position on tahawwur qarib
Summarize that tahawwur qarib is not negated by these external factors; they are resolved through usul methods or do not persist in a fully qualified mujtahid.
The mufta bihi is the author's position as it aligns with comprehensive conditions.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of objections raised by the commentator (Taftazani) against the author's (Sadr al-Shariah's) definition of 'tahawwur qarib' (near-mastery) as a condition for ijtihad. The objection was that a mujtahid might fail to know a ruling due to conflicting evidences, existence of a preventer, doubt overcoming intellect, or resemblance of truth to falsehood. The author responds that these external factors do not negate the mujtahid's capacity (tahawwur). For conflicting evidences, if one is preferred, a ruling is known; if not, other methods like qiyas or suspension are used, so ignorance is not necessary. For a preventer, if it is removed, the conditions are complete; if not, the person lacks full conditions and should not be called a faqih, yet such a person is still called a faqih, showing the argument fails. As for doubt, a qualified mujtahid with firm malaka cannot be persistently overwhelmed by doubt. Similarly, if one has mastery of necessary sciences, truth will not permanently resemble falsehood for him. Thus, these objections are invalid.

Key Terms
التهور القريب (al-tahawwur al-qarib): Near-mastery: a firm faculty (malaka) enabling the mujtahid to derive most rulings correctly, even if occasionally missing some.
تعارض (ta'arud): Contradiction or conflict between two evidences of equal strength.
مانع (mani'): A preventer that blocks knowledge of the ruling.
وهم (wahm): Doubt or conjecture that opposes the intellect.
مشاكلة (mushakala): Semblance of truth to falsehood.

No Ruling Excludes Ijtihad
Refuting claim that some rulings have no scope for ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the hadith of Mu'adh to argue that every ruling without text or consensus is open to ijtihad and identify the opposing argument.

Core Idea
Every ruling without a text or consensus is open to ijtihad—no exception.

This passage addresses a fundamental claim in usul: that some rulings might have no scope for ijtihad. The author refutes this using the well-known hadith of Mu'adh, where the Prophet approved his use of ijtihad without restricting it to cases where 'scope' exists. The lesson unpacks the argument, the counterargument, and the Hanafi tarjih.

Guided Path
1
State the claim
Identify the claim being refuted: that some rulings have no scope for ijtihad.
This is the opposing position.
2
Present the Hanafi evidence
Recall the hadith of Mu'adh: when asked how he would judge, Mu'adh said he would use the Quran, Sunnah, then ijtihad. The Prophet approved.
Hadith is narrated by Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi.
3
Trace the istinbat (derivation)
Explain how the Prophet's silence on 'in case there is no scope' implies that ijtihad is permissible in all cases without text/consensus.
The principle is that the Prophet would not remain silent if there was an exception.
4
Examine the objection
The opposing school argues that the Prophet's silence may have been because he assumed Mu'adh would refer back in unclear matters.
This is the counterargument mentioned in the passage.
5
Analyze the tarjih (preference)
The author prefers the Hanafi interpretation because the apparent generality of the approval is stronger than the speculative alternative.
The tarjih is based on the default rule that silence indicates permissibility.

The Certainty of Speculative Rulings in Ijtihad
Analyzing whether speculative rulings can be considered certain knowledge for the mujtahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi school resolves the tension between speculative rulings (ẓann) and certain knowledge ('ilm) by distinguishing between certainty in reality and certainty in obligation, and apply the syllogistic reasoning from the passage to a new scenario.

Core Idea
A speculative ruling becomes certain in terms of obligation through definitive proofs, even if its reality remains uncertain.

This passage tackles a foundational epistemological question in Hanafi usul: can a ruling that is only speculative (maẓnūn) for the mujtahid be called knowledge ('ilm)? The author presents two major positions on ijtihad — taṣwīb kull mujtahid (every mujtahid is correct) and al-muṣīb wāḥid (only one is correct) — and shows how each supports the claim that speculative rulings attain a form of certainty. We will walk through the arguments, objections, and responses, culminating in a syllogism from Sharḥ al-Minhāj that definitively closes the gap between ẓann and 'ilm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core problem
The problem: ẓann includes possibility of opposite; 'ilm excludes it. How can a speculative ruling be called 'ilm? Recognize the apparent contradiction.
This frames the entire discussion.
2
Distinguish the two ijtihad positions
The author treats two bases: (1) taṣwīb kull mujtahid — every mujtahid's ruling is true in reality; (2) al-muṣīb wāḥid — only one is correct, but the ruling still becomes certain in obligation.
These lead to different arguments for the same conclusion.
3
Analyze the syllogism under taṣwīb
Under taṣwīb, a syllogism converts speculation into certainty: the ruling is known to be speculative for the mujtahid; everything speculative for the mujtahid is definitively established in reality; therefore, the ruling is known as definitively established.
This assumes the Lawgiver's definitive text that whatever the mujtahid determines is true.
4
Analyze the argument under al-muṣīb wāḥid
Under al-muṣīb wāḥid, ijma' and mutawātir ma'nawī reports establish that every ruling supported by predominant speculation is wājib al-'amal. The obligation itself becomes certain, even if reality is not known.
This shifts certainty from reality to obligation.
5
Examine the objections and responses
Two objections are raised: (a) that fiqh becomes knowledge of obligation rather than rulings; (b) that calling a speculative ruling 'definitive' is problematic. Responses clarify that 'ilm here means 'certain conviction' (jazm) and that the evidence includes a consensus-based premise that yields certainty.
The second response likens this to khabar wāḥid with qarā'in.
6
Extract the final syllogism from Sharḥ al-Minhāj
The ultimate effort: (Minor) Every ruling definitively obligatory to act upon is known with certainty to be Allah's ruling. (Major) Everything known with certainty to be Allah's ruling is definitively known. (Conclusion) Therefore, every ruling definitively obligatory to act upon is definitively known.
This seals the argument that speculative rulings become certain in knowledge for the mujtahid.

And the utmost possible effort in this regard – the ultimate attempt to treat speculative matters as certain – is that which some scholars mentioned in Sharḥ al-Minhāj: that the speculative ruling for the mujtahid is definitively obligatory to act upon. This is the minor premise, and its major follows, and they together produce the desired conclusion. The definitive evidence (al-dalīl al-qāṭi') – and by this definitive evidence is meant ijma' and aḥād reports that are mutawātir in meaning – proves the minor premise. Then: 'every ruling that is definitively obligatory to act upon is definitively known to be the ruling of Allah' – this is the second minor premise. And 'a ruling not obligatory to act upon' is its proof. The summary is that until one is certain that a ruling is Divine, acting upon it is not obligatory. Hence, every ruling that is obligatory to act upon is known with certainty to be a Divine ruling. And 'everything definitively known to be Allah's ruling is definitively known' – this is the major of the second minor premise. Its conclusion is that a ruling regarding which there is definitive knowledge that it is Allah's ruling cannot remain speculative but reaches the level of definitive knowledge. So everything that is definitively obligatory to act upon is known definitively.

Commentary
This passage addresses a core epistemological issue in Hanafi usul: can a ruling that is only speculative (ẓannī) for the mujtahid be called knowledge ('ilm)? The author proceeds in two stages, corresponding to two positions on ijtihad: (1) taṣwīb kull mujtahid – every mujtahid is correct; (2) al-muṣīb wāḥid – only one mujtahid is correct (the dominant Hanafi view). Under the first, the author argues that the mujtahid's speculative ruling becomes certain in itself because the Lawgiver's definitive text (the principle that whatever the mujtahid determines is the truth) converts it into a certainty. A syllogism is given: the knowledge that the ruling was not speculative for the mujtahid, together with the premise that everything speculative for the mujtahid is definitively established in reality, yields the conclusion that the ruling is known to be definitively established. Under the second position (al-muṣīb wāḥid), the author relies on definitive proofs (ijma' and mutawātir ma'nawī aḥādīth) that establish that any ruling supported by predominant speculation is wājib al-'amal (obligatory to act upon). The obligation itself becomes certain, even if the ruling's actual correspondence to divine law remains unknown. An objection is raised: if fiqh is knowledge of rulings, then on the first position fiqh would be knowledge of obligation to act, not of rulings. The author responds by clarifying that 'obligation to act' here means that the mujtahid must attain certainty (jazm) about the ruling's deontic status (obligatory, prohibited, etc.) – this certainty is precisely the knowledge of rulings. A second objection targets the second position: calling something 'definitive' when it is based on speculative evidence is problematic. Two responses are given. First, 'ilm in this context means 'certain conviction' (jazm) as opposed to mere speculation (ẓann), regardless of correspondence to reality. Second, the evidence is not merely the speculative evidence alone but conjoined with the consensus-based premise (al-muqaddimah al-ijmā'iyyah) that every ruling supported by predominant speculation is wājib al-amal – this combination yields definitive knowledge, analogous to how a single report (khabar wāḥid) accompanied by circumstantial indicators (qarā'in) becomes definitive. Finally, the author presents the ultimate effort (ghāyat mā amkana) from some scholars in Sharḥ al-Minhāj, structured as a syllogism: (Major premise) Everything known with certainty to be Allah's ruling is definitively known. (Minor premise) Every ruling that is definitively obligatory to act upon is known with certainty to be Allah's ruling (proved by the definitive evidence of ijmā' and mutawātir meaning aḥādīth). (Conclusion) Therefore, every ruling that is definitively obligatory to act upon is definitively known. This closes the gap between ẓann and 'ilm, showing that speculative rulings become certain in terms of their epistemic status for the mujtahid.

Key Terms
مظنون (maẓnūn): A ruling that is based on predominant speculation (ghālib al-ẓann) and not certain.
علم ('ilm): Certain knowledge; in this passage, it includes both certainty in reality and certain conviction (jazm) regarding obligation.
تصويب كل مجتهد (taṣwīb kull mujtahid): The position that every mujtahid is correct in his ijtihad, so his ruling is the truth in reality.
المصيب واحد (al-muṣīb wāḥid): The position that only one mujtahid's ruling corresponds to the actual divine ruling; others are wrong but excused.
مقدمة اجماعية (al-muqaddimah al-ijmā'iyyah): The consensus-based premise that every ruling supported by predominant speculation is wājib al-amal (obligatory to act upon).
وجوب العمل (wujūb al-'amal): The obligation to act upon a ruling. In this passage, it becomes certain even if the ruling's reality is uncertain.

Checkpoint
According to the passage, under the position that only one mujtahid is correct (al-muṣīb wāḥid), how does a speculative ruling become considered knowledge?
A) By direct revelation from Allah
B) By definitive proofs (ijma' and mutawātir ma'nawī) establishing that the ruling is wājib al-'amal, and this obligation is certain
C) By abandoning ijtihad and following taqlīd
D) By the mujtahid's personal whim

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi usul, speculative rulings can be called 'ilm because the obligation to act upon them is certain, either through the principle that every mujtahid is correct or through definitive proofs like ijma' that establish the certainty of obligation.
