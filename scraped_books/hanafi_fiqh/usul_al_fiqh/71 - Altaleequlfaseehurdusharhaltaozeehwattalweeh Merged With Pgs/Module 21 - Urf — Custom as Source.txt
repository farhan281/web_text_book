The Grain Oath: Resolving the Tension Between Literal and Metaphorical Meanings Through Custom
Analyzing the resolution of combining haqiqah and majaz using custom.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state how Hanafi usul uses custom ('urf) to unify literal and metaphorical meanings in oaths, and apply this principle to derive the ruling for a similar case.

Core Idea
Custom determines the intended meaning — the inner benefit of grain governs the oath, not the literal act of chewing.

In this passage, we encounter a classic Hanafi usul problem: a person swears not to eat a specific grain. According to the Sahibayn, both chewing the raw grain and eating a product derived from it break the oath. How can the same oath cover both the literal act and a metaphorical extension? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi solution: custom ('urf) determines that the inner purpose (hifz al-qut) is the intended meaning, thereby avoiding the forbidden combination of literal and metaphorical.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its apparent scope
The oath is 'I will not eat any of this wheat.' The literal act is chewing grains; the extended act is consuming products like bread or soup.
Note that the literal meaning is chewing the grain itself.
2
Recognize the problem of combining haqiqah and majaz
Using one utterance to intend both literal and metaphorical is generally disallowed in usul. Here, 'eating' literally means chewing, while the product consumption is metaphorical.
This is a classic problem: jami' bayn al-haqiqah wa al-majaz.
3
Note the Hanafi solution: custom determines the intended meaning
The passage states that by custom ('urf), the inner meaning (batin) of the oath is intended: the benefit or nourishment from the grain, not the physical act of chewing.
Custom makes the metaphorical meaning primary, so only one meaning is intended.
4
Understand how this avoids combination
Since custom indicates that the metaphorical meaning (consuming grain benefit) is the actual intent, there is no combination—the oath is understood as a single metaphorical usage.
This is an application of istihsan via 'urf.
5
Apply the principle to similar cases
Generalize: when custom consistently uses a term for its extended meaning, that meaning governs the oath. The literal meaning is not simultaneously intended.
This principle applies to any oath where the term has a customary extension.

Commentary
This passage presents a case from Hanafi fiqh where a person swears not to eat a specific type of grain. According to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), the oath is broken whether he chews the raw grain or eats a product made from it. The apparent issue is that 'eating grain' literally means chewing the grain itself, while consuming its product is a metaphorical extension. Combining literal and metaphorical meanings in one utterance is generally problematic in usul. The answer is that by custom, the inner meaning (the purpose of the oath) is what is intended. This custom-based interpretation avoids combining literal and metaphorical meanings because the custom makes the metaphorical meaning the primary intended meaning. This reflects the Hanafi use of 'urf (custom) and istihsan (juristic preference) to resolve apparent conflicts.

Key Terms
حنثة (hanthah): Wheat or grain; the object of the oath.
حنث (hanth): Breaking an oath; the legal consequence of violating a sworn statement.
عرف (urf): Customary usage; the basis for interpreting the oath's meaning.

The Meaning of 'Ala in Hanafi Usul: Obligation and Condition
Definition and expansion of the preposition 'على'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the literal meaning of 'على' according to Hanafi jurists, explain how it signifies obligation, and distinguish its usage for condition.

Core Idea
Hanafi jurists treat the obligation meaning of 'على' as literal (haqiqa) due to customary usage, not metaphor.

This lesson explores a foundational linguistic tool in Hanafi usul: the preposition 'على'. While its root meaning is physical superiority (isti'la), jurists derive from this a technical sense of obligation (wujub) through an analogy with debt. We will also see its secondary use to indicate condition. Understanding this helps you interpret legal texts accurately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning
Read the first part of the passage: 'على is for isti'la (superiority).' Recognize that the core linguistic meaning is being above or upon.
This is the starting point for the legal derivation.
2
Trace the analogy to obligation
Study the example of debt: 'alayhi daynun' (debt is upon him). The debtor is like a rider on a mount; the debt rides on him. This analogy shows why 'upon' implies obligation: what is upon someone must be carried or fulfilled.
The analogy connects physical superiority to legal obligation.
3
Distinguish the two usages
Note the two uses: primary for obligation, secondary for condition. The condition usage is illustrated by 'yubayi'uka 'ala an la yushrika' – the 'upon' introduces a condition.
Context determines which meaning applies.
4
Understand the haqiqa status
The passage states that for jurists (fuqaha), the obligation meaning is haqiqa (literal), not majaz. This is based on prevailing usage ('urf) among legal scholars.
This contrasts with lexicographers who may consider isti'la as the only literal meaning.

'Ala: for isti'la (superiority), i.e., the word 'ala is for isti'la, and according to the jurists, from it is intended obligation (wujub), like in 'alayhi daynun' [debt is upon him], the debt is established upon him, so it is as if the debt is above him and riding on him, just like a rider upon the riding beast, so the debt is also upon the debtor. Due to this suitability, according to the jurists, the word 'ala is for obligation, and this meaning is literal (haqiqa) in their view. And it is used for condition (shart): i.e., the word 'ala is also used for condition, meaning that what follows it becomes a condition for what precedes it, like 'yubayi'uka 'ala an la yushrika billahi shay'an' (he pledges allegiance to you on condition that he does not associate anything with Allah), i.e., he pledges to you subject to the condition of no shirk.

Commentary
This passage provides a linguistic and legal analysis of the preposition 'على' (upon). It begins by stating that its primary meaning is isti'la (superiority or being above). The Hanafi jurists then derive from this literal sense a technical legal meaning of obligation (wujub). They argue by analogy: just as a thing that is 'upon' someone (like a debt 'علیه دین') is above him and must be fulfilled, so the word 'على' in legal contexts signals obligation. This meaning is considered haqiqa (literal) in the terminology of the fuqaha, not metaphorical. The passage also notes that 'على' is used to indicate a condition (shart), where the dependent clause becomes a condition for the main clause, as in the pledge of allegiance to the Prophet on condition of avoiding shirk. This is a secondary usage. This analysis is typical of Hanafi usul, where linguistic analysis and customary usage ('urf) play a key role in determining legal implications.

Key Terms
على (ala): A preposition meaning 'upon', used in Hanafi fiqh primarily for obligation (wujub) and secondarily for condition (shart).
استعلاء (isti'la): Superiority or being above; the literal meaning of 'على'.
وجوب (wujub): Obligation; the legal effect inferred from the word 'على' in juristic contexts.
