The Imperative (Amr) Indicates Obligation: Quranic Proofs and Hanafi Usul
Defending Hanafi usul principle using Quranic evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi principle that amr indicates wujub, apply it to Quranic commands, and evaluate the opposing argument using the dalil from Surah an-Nur and Surah al-Ahzab.

Core Idea
Amr is haqiqah for wujub; threatening those who oppose it proves obligation.

In Hanafi usul, the default rule is that the imperative form (amr) from the Lawgiver indicates obligation (wujub) unless there is a clear contextual indicator (qarina) to the contrary. This lesson examines two powerful Quranic proofs that support this position: the threat of punishment for opposing Allah's command (24:63) and the negation of choice after Allah and His Messenger decree a matter (33:36). By analyzing these texts, you will understand why the Hanafi school holds that the burden of proof falls on those who claim a non-obligatory meaning.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi claim and the opposing view
Identify the core disagreement: Hanafis hold that amr is haqiqah for wujub, while others (e.g., Shafi'is, Mu'tazila) consider it ambiguous between wujub, nadb, and ibaha without a qarina.
The Hanafi position is based on the asl (default) and the threat verses.
2
Analyze the first proof: Surah an-Nur (24:63)
Examine the verse 'So let those who oppose his command beware lest a trial strike them or a painful punishment.' The threat of punishment only makes sense if the command is obligatory; abandoning a non-obligatory act does not incur punishment.
This is a qat'i dalala proof in Hanafi usul.
3
Analyze the second proof: Surah al-Ahzab (33:36)
Examine the verse 'It is not for a believing man or a believing woman, when Allah and His Messenger have decreed a matter, to have any choice in their affair.' The commentator clarifies that 'qada' here means verbal command (hukm qawli), and the negation of choice is absolute, which only holds if the command is obligatory.
The analysis of 'qada' as verbal command is crucial to refute the possibility that it refers to creative action.
4
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider the practical consequence: in Hanafi fiqh, every unqualified command in the Quran and Sunnah is immediately binding as wajib. For example, the command 'Establish prayer' (2:43) is obligatory without further evidence.
Other schools may seek external qarina to determine the nature of the command.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul argument proving that the imperative form (amr) indicates obligation (wujub). The commentator expands on the matn's claim by citing two major Quranic proofs. First, the verse 'So let those who oppose his command beware' (24:63) implies that opposing the command brings fear of punishment, which only makes sense if the command is obligatory, because abandoning a non-obligatory act does not incur punishment. Second, the verse 'It is not for a believing man or a believing woman, when Allah and His Messenger have decreed a matter, to have any choice in their affair' (33:36) negates choice for the believer after Allah and His Messenger decide. The commentator carefully analyzes the word 'qada' (decree) to show it means verbal command (qawl) not creative action (fi'l), because the Messenger is mentioned alongside Allah. If it meant creative action exclusive to Allah, the conjunction with the Messenger would be invalid. Additionally, if 'qada' meant the action of the one under decree, then negation of choice would not be absolute because the command could be permissive or recommendatory. Only if the command is obligatory does choice vanish, which supports the Hanafi position. The passage also cites the verse about creation 'Be and it is' and discusses the disagreement between Abu Mansur al-Maturidi and Fakhr al-Islam (al-Pazdawi) on whether this is literal or metaphorical. Maturidi holds it is metaphorical for the speed of creation, while Fakhr al-Islam holds it is literal. This discussion reinforces that amr is a real command implying obligation. The Hanafi school maintains that the default rule (asl) is that amr indicates obligation unless there is a qarina (contextual indicator) to the contrary. This is a foundational usul principle with many furu' (branches) such as the obligation of wudu from the verse 'O you who believe, when you stand for prayer, wash your faces' (5:6).

Key Terms
أمر (amr): The linguistic imperative form (e.g., 'if'al') used by the Lawgiver to demand action.
وجوب (wujub): The legal classification that an act must be performed, and omission is blameworthy.
قضاء (qada'): Decree or judgment; here specified to mean verbal command (hukm qawli) not creative action (fi'l).

Why the Command Form Implies Obligation (Wujub) Not Existence (Wujud)
Explains why legislative commands imply obligation not existence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the command form (amr) implies wujub and identify the Quranic and rational proofs that support this principle.

Core Idea
A legislative command intends obligation, not direct existence, to preserve human choice.

In Hanafi usul, the command form (if'al) is a fundamental tool for deriving rulings. But what exactly does it demand? Does it require the immediate existence of the commanded act, or something else? This passage argues that for legislative (taklifi) commands, the intent must be obligation (wujub), because requiring direct existence would negate human choice and make the servant like an inanimate object. The Shari'ah therefore transferred the necessity of existence to the necessity of obligation, which ultimately leads to existence through the servant's free choice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key problem
Recognize that if every command—both creative (takwini) and legislative (taklifi)—demanded immediate existence, then the servant would have no choice and would be compelled like inanimate objects. This would render the principle of taklif (moral obligation) invalid.
2
Trace the Hanafi solution
See that the Shari'ah in legislative commands replaced the demand for existence with the demand for obligation. Obligation (wujub) is a legal demand that the servant must fulfill with his choice; it leads to existence eventually but preserves the element of free will.
The passage uses the phrase 'obligation leads to existence' (wujub mufdi ila al-wujud).
3
Analyze the first proof: rational reasoning
Understand the rational argument: In a creative command (e.g., 'Be!' and it is), the act comes into existence immediately without human effort. Applying that to legislative commands would eliminate the servant's ability to choose to obey or disobey, thus destroying the very basis of moral responsibility.
This argument is an example of istihsan or rational preference, rooted in preserving the purpose of the law.
4
Analyze the second proof: Quranic texts
Look at the verses cited: 'Afa'asaytu amri' (Did you disobey my command?) and 'When it was said to them, bow down, they do not bow down.' The first verse implies that failing to carry out a command is disobedience, which deserves punishment—a characteristic of obligation, not mere existence. The second blames those who do not bow when commanded, again showing that the command imposes a binding duty.
In Hanafi usul, the blame and threat of punishment are qara'in (indications) that the command is for wujub.
5
Analyze the third proof: custom (urf)
Note that in common usage, when someone wants to demand a binding action that cannot be left, they use the imperative form (if'al). This shared understanding shows that the command inherently implies obligation (wujub).
Custom (urf) is a recognized source of legal interpretation in Hanafi usul.

Command After Prohibition: Hanafi Position on Wujub
Discusses whether a command after prohibition indicates obligation or not

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi position on whether a command after prohibition (amr ba'd al-hazr) indicates obligation, and apply the role of qarina in shifting the ruling.

Core Idea
A command after prohibition still indicates obligation unless a qarina diverts it to recommendation or permission.

When the Lawgiver first prohibits an act and then commands it, does the command still carry the same force of obligation? Some scholars argue that the prohibition weakens the command. The Hanafi school firmly holds that the general rule that a command indicates obligation remains intact, and only a clear external indication (qarina) can change it. This lesson explores the evidence and the counterarguments.

Guided Path
1
State the General Rule
Begin with the Hanafi principle: al-amr yufidu al-wujub – every command indicates obligation unless a qarina indicates otherwise.
2
Identify the Exception Claim
Some scholars argue that commands after prohibition indicate nadb (recommendation) or ibaha (permission). They cite Quranic verses as evidence.
The verse Fa ightagh min fadli Allah (62:10) and Idha halaltum fustadu (5:2).
3
Examine the Opposing Evidence
Analyze the verses offered by the opposing view. Note that these verses come after a prohibition and are presented as examples of amr ba'd al-hazr not indicating obligation.
4
Apply the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response argues that those verses contain qarinas (e.g., benefit for the people) that shift the meaning from obligation to recommendation or permission. Without such qarinas, the general principle holds.
The Hanafi discussion is specifically about commands devoid of qarina.
5
Conclude with the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: In the Hanafi school, any command after prohibition without a qarina is obligatory. Other schools may view it as recommended or permissible, affecting how the Mufti issues rulings.

Delivery Resembling Exchange: The Slave Dowry Case
Illustrating delivery resembling exchange through a marriage dowry case.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the scenario of delivery resembling exchange and apply the Hanafi principle that change of ownership transforms a payment into a new transaction.

Core Idea
When the same item changes ownership, its delivery can serve both as payment and as a new exchange.

This lesson unpacks a complex marriage dowry case: a woman receives her own father (a slave) as mahr. When a third party reclaims the slave and the husband later reacquires him, the delivery to the woman becomes both a fulfillment of her right (adā') and a new exchange (qadā') due to the change in ownership. This illustrates a key Hanafi usul principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial transaction
The woman marries; the dowry is her father (a slave). She owns him and he becomes free.
This is the starting point: ownership transfers from the husband to the woman.
2
Recognize the disruption
A third party claims the slave; the judge rules in their favor. The woman's ownership and emancipation are voided.
The woman loses ownership, and the husband owes her the mahr value.
3
Trace the reacquisition
The husband later reacquires the same slave. He must now deliver him to the woman.
The husband becomes owner again through a new acquisition.
4
Analyze the dual aspect
The delivery is adā' (payment) because it fulfills her original right to the slave. It is also shabīh bil-qaḍā' (resembling exchange) because ownership changed from the woman to the husband and back.
The same physical item is transferred, but its legal status has changed due to ownership history.
5
Note the condition before delivery
Before delivery, the husband can freely sell or free the slave because he owns him. After delivery, the woman's ownership is final.
This highlights that until delivery, the husband's ownership is unencumbered.

The Principle of Change of Ownership: The Hadith of Barira
Deriving the principle of change of ownership from the hadith of Barira.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the Hanafi principle that change of ownership changes a ruling from the hadith of Barira and distinguish between rulings attached to essence vs. consideration.

Core Idea
Change of ownership transforms an item's legal status unless the ruling is attached to the item's essence.

The hadith of Barira serves as the primary textual evidence for the Hanafi principle that change of ownership (tabaddul al-milk) causes a change in the item's legal status (tabaddul al-‘ayn hukman). This lesson traces the derivation, explains the distinction between rulings attached to essence and those attached to ownership, and notes the limits of the principle.

Guided Path
1
Read the hadith of Barira
The Prophet asked Barira for meat from charity. She said, 'It is charity for you.' He replied, 'It is charity for them, for us it is a gift.'
Key phrase: 'صدقة علينا، ولنا هدية'
2
Identify the initial ruling
The meat was charity, prohibited for the Prophet (since he does not eat charity).
The ruling of prohibition was due to the item being charity.
3
Note the change after ownership transfer
Once Barira received it as charity, she owned it. When she gifted it to the Prophet, ownership changed from Barira to the Prophet.
The meat is the same in essence, but its ownership changed.
4
Extract the principle
The Prophet declared it permissible (gift) after the ownership change. This shows that change of ownership changes the ruling attached to ownership.
The principle: tabaddul al-milk yūjibu tabaddul al-‘ayn ḥukman.
5
Apply the distinction
Rulings attached to the essence (dhāt) do not change with ownership (e.g., pork is haram). Rulings attached to a consideration (i‘tibār) like ownership do change.
This clarifies the scope of the principle.

Cutting Then Killing: One Crime in Hanafi Law
Explaining that killing nullifies prior cutting so only qisas for killing applies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that killing subsumes a prior cut, and identify the proof from Quran 5:3.

Core Idea
A killing erases the effect of a prior cut, making it a single crime – only qisas for killing applies.

In Hanafi criminal law, when a person cuts off a limb and then kills the victim, the cutting is not treated as a separate crime. The killing nullifies the prior harm, so only qisas for the killing is due. This lesson examines the legal reasoning behind this rule, including the Quranic evidence and the disagreement with the two companions (Sahibayn).

How does the verse prove the ruling? Slaughter cancels the prohibition of the predator's attack, just as killing cancels the effect of the cut.

It is given that cutting off a limb and then killing becomes like cutting and subsequent killing, so in that case it is a single crime. Thus, it is as if he killed him with multiple blows: in that case there is no qisas for the blows, only qisas for the soul, i.e., killing. Similarly here, there will be no qisas for the cut, only qisas for the soul, i.e., killing. This is from the perspective of meaning. That is, it is said that the killing completes the effect of the cut, so it is one crime; therefore their cause and requirement should be one. This is a unity from the perspective of meaning, not from the perspective of form. But from the perspective of form regarding the recompense of the act, there is no unity. The acts of cutting and killing are separate and multiple acts. The recompense of the act is qisas, so each act should have its own qisas. However, overlapping occurs in the recompense of the locus (damān of the part enters into the damān of the whole) where there is a recompense of the locus, not in multiple acts. An example of overlapping in the recompense of the locus is [the rule that] the compensation (arsh) for a head wound that exposes bone (mūḍiḥa) enters into the blood money for the hair (diyat al-shaʿr). The scenario is: someone inflicted a head wound (mūḍiḥa) causing the hair to be lost with no hope of regrowth; since the locus is one, the diya for the hair includes the compensation for the wound; only the diya for hair is required, not the arsh for the wound. Because diya for hair is the recompense for the locus, so the arsh for the wound is absorbed into it. And killing may eliminate the effect of the cut. This is the response to what the two companions (Ṣāḥibayn) said: that killing completes the effect of the cut, meaning the cut extends to the point of death, so the killing after the cut brings that effect to completion. The summary of the response is that killing sometimes, instead of completing the effect of the cut, eliminates and erases its effect, because with killing, the locus itself is lost. With the loss of the locus, the completion and extension of the cut are no longer possible, so how can completion be imagined? Allah says: 'And what a beast of prey has eaten, except what you slaughter' (Qur'an 5:3). This is an example that killing removes the effect of the cut. For example, a sheep was wounded by a predator and its flesh was cut and eaten; the law established prohibition. If that same wound and cutting of flesh led to death, then the exception of slaughter shows that if the sheep is still somewhat alive and is slaughtered according to the prescribed method, then the sheep is halal. Observe that this killing, which was in the form of lawful slaughter, removed the ruling of the cut and wound. If it had died from that wound, it would have been haram; the killing by slaughter eliminated that effect and made it halal. And qisas is not obligatory; this is a response to his statement: 'Just as if he killed him with multiple blows, then the answer is that in the case of blows, there is no qisas unless there is a wound or killing. When there is a wound, only the compensation (arsh) for the wound is due; if it leads to death, then qisas for the soul is due, nothing more. In any case, qisas for mere blows is never done.

Commentary
This passage is a legal ruling and dispute. It explains that if a person cuts off another's limb and then kills him, the cutting is subsumed under the killing, so only qisas for the killing is required, not for the cut. The text analogizes this to multiple blows: if one strikes another multiple times without causing visible injury, there is no qisas; only when a wound or death occurs does liability arise. Similarly, the cut is like a blow that is absorbed into the final killing. The text then addresses the objection of the Ṣāḥibayn (Abū Yūsuf and Muḥammad al-Shaybānī) who argued that the killing completes the effect of the cut, thus they are two separate crimes. The Hanafī response is that killing can instead wipe out the effect of the cut, because the entire body (locus) is destroyed. The evidence from the Qur'an (5:3) is cited: a wounded animal that would be haram becomes halal if properly slaughtered; the lawful killing overrides the earlier harm. The final paragraph clarifies that in the case of multiple blows, no qisas is due until a wound or death occurs, reinforcing the principle.
