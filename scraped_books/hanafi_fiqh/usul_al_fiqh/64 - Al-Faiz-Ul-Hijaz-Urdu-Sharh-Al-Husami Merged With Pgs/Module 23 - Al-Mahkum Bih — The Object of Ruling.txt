The Mixed Nature of Kaffarah: Worship and Punishment
Explanation of kaffarah's dual aspects and dominance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why kaffarah is predominantly worship in the Hanafi school, except for zihar, and derive the ruling on the obligation of kaffarah from its dual nature.

Core Idea
Kaffarah is worship dominant except zihar where punishment dominates because it is 'objectionable speech.'

This passage begins the classification of acts with mixed legal natures. Kaffarah (expiation) is presented as a prime example of an act that combines worship (ibadah) and punishment (uqubah). Understanding which aspect is dominant determines how the ruling is applied—especially the difference from zihar kaffarah, where punishment prevails. The Hanafi position is that worship is dominant in most expiations, making them akin to other acts of worship that are not initially obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two aspects of kaffarah
Locate in the text where kaffarah is said to have both worship and punishment aspects. Note the evidence for each: worship because it expiates sins (conceals), punishment because it becomes obligatory upon committing a prohibited act.
The term 'kaffarah' literally means 'to conceal'—this is the worship angle.
2
Determine the dominant aspect according to the Hanafi school
The text states 'our view, the worship aspect is dominant.' Understand how the school weighs the two aspects. The default is worship dominance.
This is a qa'ida: when an act has multiple aspects, the dominant one governs the ruling.
3
Identify the exception: zihar kaffarah
The text explicitly excepts kaffarah of zihar, where punishment dominates. Find the reason: 'because it is objectionable speech and falsehood.' This changes the ruling: zihar kaffarah is more punitive, requiring a different analysis.
Zihar is a husband saying to his wife 'you are like my mother's back'—a severe sin.
4
Apply the dominant aspect to derive a ruling
Since worship dominates, kaffarah is not initially obligatory; it only becomes wajib upon committing a sin. This contrasts with pure acts of worship that are initially obligatory.
This is a usul principle: initial obligation vs. contingent obligation.

Ushr is essentially an additional burden of responsibility upon the person for agricultural land, which is a type of mu'nat. If the person does not pay it, the government will take the land from him. But it also has the meaning of worship in that ushr is obligatory only on Muslims, just as other acts of worship are obligatory on Muslims. Therefore, ushr is not initially obligatory on a non-believer because a non-believer is not qualified for worship. However, according to Imam Muhammad, it remains obligatory on a non-believer. That is, if a dhimmi non-believer buys productive land from a Muslim, then ushr will remain due on him considering mu'nat, because a non-believer is qualified for mu'nat, even though not qualified for worship. In summary, considering the meaning of worship, ushr is not initially due on a non-believer, but considering the meaning of mu'nat, it remains due. Also, the expenditure categories of ushr are the same as those of zakat—this is evidence of ushr being worship. (7) An act of mu'nat that includes the meaning of punishment, like kharaj. Kharaj is essentially a land tax on land that is cultivated; if not paid, the government will take the land. But it also has the meaning of punishment. For this reason, kharaj is not initially applicable to a Muslim, because a Muslim is not eligible for punishment and humiliation initially. But since mu'nat is the primary meaning in kharaj, it will be binding on a Muslim in remaining terms, because a Muslim is eligible for mu'nat. 'Remaining' means that if a Muslim buys taxed land from a non-believer, or a non-believer becomes Muslim while possessing taxed land, then kharaj will be collected from the Muslim. In summary, considering the meaning of punishment, kharaj is not initially due on a Muslim, but considering mu'nat, it remains due. (8) A right that is established by itself, having no connection to the liability of the servant, like the fifth share of spoils and minerals. Because jihad is a right of Allah, all wealth obtained from jihad should belong to Allah, but Allah, by His favor and grace, gave four shares to the mujahideen and kept one fifth for Himself. So this khums is a right that is not obligatory upon us as an act of obedience; rather, Allah has kept it for Himself and then made His deputy, the ruler, responsible for its distribution. Similarly, minerals like treasures of gold and silver created by Allah should all belong to Allah, but Allah, by His favor, divided them into five parts, giving four parts to the owner of the land and keeping one fifth for Himself, and here too made the ruler responsible. So when the wealth obtained from jihad and minerals is a right of Allah that is established by itself and not obligatory upon servants as worship, then it is permissible to spend this khums on those who are entitled to the four-fifths, i.e., if those who are entitled to the four shares are needy, the khums which is Allah's right can be given to them, and also to their children and parents, because it is a right established by itself. If this khums were not a right established by itself but rather obligatory upon servants as an act of obedience like zakat, then giving it to those entitled to the four-fifths would not be permissible, just as the zakat of the payer, even if needy, cannot be spent on himself or his parents and children. Therefore, since it is not impurity, khums can be given to Banu Hashim, whereas zakat is not permissible for Banu Hashim because it is impurity. Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of acts that have mixed legal natures between worship (ibadah), punishment (uqubah), and maintenance burden (mu'nat). The author, commenting on the matn of Al-Husami, explains each category with examples from Hanafi fiqh.

First, kaffarah (expiation) is discussed. It has both worship and punishment aspects, but the worship aspect is dominant except for zihar kaffarah where punishment dominates because zihar involves objectionable speech.

Second, sadaqah fitr (alms for breaking fast) is categorized as worship with mu'nat. Its worship aspect purifies the fast, while its mu'nat aspect is shown by its cause (sabab) being riyas (dependency of those one supports). The worship aspect is dominant.

Third, ushr (tithe on agricultural land) is mu'nat with worship. The mu'nat is the tax burden; the worship is that it is obligatory only on Muslims. This leads to a difference: initially, ushr is not on a non-believer (worship aspect), but according to Imam Muhammad (a Hanafi imam), it remains on a non-believer who buys such land (mu'nat aspect).

Fourth, kharaj (land tax on non-Muslim land) is mu'nat with punishment. The mu'nat is the tax; the punishment is that it is not initially on Muslims because they are not subject to degradation. But it remains on a Muslim who acquires such land (mu'nat aspect).

Fifth, khums (fifth share of spoils and minerals) is a right established by itself (haqq qa'im bi dhatihi), not a duty on servants. It is not like zakat which is a purification of wealth. Therefore, khums may be given to those entitled to the four-fifths and even to Banu Hashim (who cannot receive zakat).

The passage concludes by listing examples of pure rights of servants (huquq al-ibad) such as guarantees for damaged property, diyah, usurped items, and rights of ownership and divorce.

This classification helps understand why some obligations apply differently to Muslims and non-believers, and how rights are distributed.

Key Terms
کفارہ (kaffarah): An expiatory act that conceals sins, obligatory upon committing a prohibited act, having both worship and punishment aspects.

Sadaqah Fitr: Worship with the Meaning of Maintenance
Classification of sadaqah fitr as worship with mu'nat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why sadaqah fitr is classified as worship with mu'nat (maintenance burden) and explain how the cause 'riyas' affects its obligation.

Core Idea
Sadaqah fitr is worship dominant but its cause is dependency (riyas), making it unlike pure worship.

The second category in the passage is 'worship that includes the meaning of mu'nat' with sadaqah fitr as the example. Mu'nat (maintenance burden) refers to the financial responsibility for dependents. The passage explains that sadaqah fitr purifies the fast (worship aspect) but its cause is the headship over those one supports (mu'nat aspect). This dual nature explains why it is obligatory on behalf of dependents, even those not themselves mukallaf. The Hanafi school treats worship as dominant, but the mu'nat element relaxes conditions like full legal capacity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two aspects of sadaqah fitr: worship and mu'nat
The text says sadaqah fitr purifies the fast from idle talk (worship) and its cause is riyas (headship) which is a maintenance burden (mu'nat).
The purification hadith: 'Sadaqah fitr is purification for the fasting person from vain talk and obscenity.'
2
Analyze the worship aspect: purification
The worship aspect is evidenced by the purpose of purification (tahara) from the fast's imperfections. This is similar to other expiatory acts that are worship.
Purification is a common theme in worship (e.g., wudu, repentance).
3
Analyze the mu'nat aspect: riyas as cause
The cause (sabab) of sadaqah fitr is riyas—the fact that a person supports dependents. This is why it is obligatory on behalf of children and slaves, even though they are not required to perform other acts of worship (like prayer).
Riyas means headship/guardianship over a household.
4
Determine the dominant aspect
The text says 'the worship aspect is dominant' in sadaqah fitr. This is because the primary purpose is religious purification, and the mu'nat is a secondary characteristic.
Dominance affects rulings like eligibility for recipients (similar to zakat) and whether intention is required.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of acts that have mixed legal natures between worship (ibadah), punishment (uqubah), and maintenance burden (mu'nat). The author, commenting on the matn of Al-Husami, explains each category with examples from Hanafi fiqh.

First, kaffarah (expiation) is discussed. It has both worship and punishment aspects, but the worship aspect is dominant except for zihar kaffarah where punishment dominates because zihar involves objectionable speech.

Second, sadaqah fitr (alms for breaking fast) is categorized as worship with mu'nat. Its worship aspect purifies the fast, while its mu'nat aspect is shown by its cause (sabab) being riyas (dependency of those one supports). The worship aspect is dominant.

Third, ushr (tithe on agricultural land) is mu'nat with worship. The mu'nat is the tax burden; the worship is that it is obligatory only on Muslims. This leads to a difference: initially, ushr is not on a non-believer (worship aspect), but according to Imam Muhammad (a Hanafi imam), it remains on a non-believer who buys such land (mu'nat aspect).

Fourth, kharaj (land tax on non-Muslim land) is mu'nat with punishment. The mu'nat is the tax; the punishment is that it is not initially on Muslims because they are not subject to degradation. But it remains on a Muslim who acquires such land (mu'nat aspect).

Fifth, khums (fifth share of spoils and minerals) is a right established by itself (haqq qa'im bi dhatihi), not a duty on servants. It is not like zakat which is a purification of wealth. Therefore, khums may be given to those entitled to the four-fifths and even to Banu Hashim (who cannot receive zakat).

The passage concludes by listing examples of pure rights of servants (huquq al-ibad) such as guarantees for damaged property, diyah, usurped items, and rights of ownership and divorce.

This classification helps understand why some obligations apply differently to Muslims and non-believers, and how rights are distributed.

Ushr: Maintenance with the Meaning of Worship
Classification of ushr as mu'nat with worship, with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why ushr (tithe) is classified as maintenance (mu'nat) with worship, and state the Hanafi ruling on its obligation for non-Muslims initially and by continuation.

Core Idea
Ushr is a land tax (mu'nat) but also worship because it is only on Muslims initially; non-Muslims pay it only if they acquire Muslim land.

The third category is 'mu'nat containing the meaning of worship,' exemplified by ushr (tithe on agricultural land). The passage describes ushr as a financial burden on the landowner (mu'nat) that also has a worship aspect, evidenced by its being obligatory only on Muslims initially. This leads to a notable intra-Hanafi difference: while ushr is not initially required from non-Muslims (due to worship aspect), Im?m Muhammad holds that if a non-Muslim buys land from a Muslim, ushr continues to apply because of the mu'nat aspect. The worship aspect also connects ushr's recipients to those of zakat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mu'nat aspect of ushr
Ushr is a tax on agricultural produce, a financial burden on the farmer. The passage says it is an 'additional burden of responsibility' and if unpaid, the government takes the land.
Mu'nat is the primary meaning; it is a levy on land.
2
Identify the worship aspect of ushr
Ushr is obligatory only on Muslims, similar to other acts of worship. Its proceeds are distributed to the same categories as zakat (the eight asnaf). This indicates a religious dimension.
The shared recipients (masarif) with zakat is given as evidence.
3
Understand the initial versus continuation distinction
Initially, ushr is not wajib on a non-Muslim because worship requires faith. But if a non-Muslim acquires land that was subject to ushr (by buying from a Muslim), the obligation continues because the mu'nat aspect (the tax) is independent of the person's faith.
This is an application of the rule that a right established on land continues despite change of owner.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi difference: Im?m Muhammad's view
Imam Muhammad holds that ushr remains on a dhimmi who buys land from a Muslim. This is based on the mu'nat aspect. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf may have a different view? The passage only mentions Imam Muhammad.
Imam Muhammad's view is reported as 'al-baqa' (continuation).

Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of acts that have mixed legal natures between worship (ibadah), punishment (uqubah), and maintenance burden (mu'nat). The author, commenting on the matn of Al-Husami, explains each category with examples from Hanafi fiqh.

First, kaffarah (expiation) is discussed. It has both worship and punishment aspects, but the worship aspect is dominant except for zihar kaffarah where punishment dominates because zihar involves objectionable speech.

Second, sadaqah fitr (alms for breaking fast) is categorized as worship with mu'nat. Its worship aspect purifies the fast, while its mu'nat aspect is shown by its cause (sabab) being riyas (dependency of those one supports). The worship aspect is dominant.

Third, ushr (tithe on agricultural land) is mu'nat with worship. The mu'nat is the tax burden; the worship is that it is obligatory only on Muslims. This leads to a difference: initially, ushr is not on a non-believer (worship aspect), but according to Imam Muhammad (a Hanafi imam), it remains on a non-believer who buys such land (mu'nat aspect).

Fourth, kharaj (land tax on non-Muslim land) is mu'nat with punishment. The mu'nat is the tax; the punishment is that it is not initially on Muslims because they are not subject to degradation. But it remains on a Muslim who acquires such land (mu'nat aspect).

Fifth, khums (fifth share of spoils and minerals) is a right established by itself (haqq qa'im bi dhatihi), not a duty on servants. It is not like zakat which is a purification of wealth. Therefore, khums may be given to those entitled to the four-fifths and even to Banu Hashim (who cannot receive zakat).

The passage concludes by listing examples of pure rights of servants (huquq al-ibad) such as guarantees for damaged property, diyah, usurped items, and rights of ownership and divorce.

This classification helps understand why some obligations apply differently to Muslims and non-believers, and how rights are distributed.

Kharaj: Maintenance with the Meaning of Punishment
Classification of kharaj as mu'nat with punishment, with initial exemption for Muslims and continuation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why kharaj is classified as maintenance (mu'nat) with punishment (uqubah), and identify when it becomes applicable to Muslims.

Core Idea
Kharaj is a land tax (mu'nat) with a punishment aspect; initially not on Muslims, but continues if they acquire such land.

The fourth category is 'mu'nat containing the meaning of punishment,' with kharaj as the prime example. Kharaj is a land tax imposed on non-Muslim lands, primarily a financial burden (mu'nat). However, it also has a punishment aspect because it is a form of humiliation for non-believers, and therefore it is not initially imposed on Muslims (who are not subject to degradation). But the mu'nat aspect is so strong that if a Muslim acquires land that was subject to kharaj (by purchase or conversion), the tax continues to apply, because the Muslim can bear the financial burden even if the punitive aspect is lifted. This mirrors the ushr case but with reversed roles.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mu'nat aspect of kharaj
Kharaj is a land tax, a financial burden on the cultivator. The text says 'if not paid, the government takes the land.' This is identical to ushr in being a mu'nat.
Both ushr and kharaj are land taxes, but their religious character differs.
2
Identify the punishment aspect of kharaj
Kharaj is associated with humiliation (dhillah) for non-believers. The text says 'Muslim is not eligible for punishment and humiliation initially.' This shows the uqubah aspect.
The punishment aspect is not physical but social/ financial degradation.
3
Understand the initial exemption for Muslims
Because of the punishment aspect, kharaj is not initially imposed on a Muslim. The Muslim is not subject to the humiliating tax from the start.
Initial exemption does not mean the land is free; it means the personal obligation is lifted.
4
Understand the continuation for Muslims who acquire kharaj land
If a Muslim buys kharaj land or a non-believer becomes Muslim while possessing such land, kharaj continues to be collected from the Muslim. This is because the mu'nat aspect (the land's tax liability) persists.
Continuation (baqa) is based on the land, not the person.
5
Compare with ushr
Notice the symmetry: ushr has worship aspect (non-Muslim exempt initially), kharaj has punishment aspect (Muslim exempt initially). Both have continuation based on mu'nat.
This symmetrical treatment shows the consistency of Hanafi usul in handling mixed aspects.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of acts that have mixed legal natures between worship (ibadah), punishment (uqubah), and maintenance burden (mu'nat). The author, commenting on the matn of Al-Husami, explains each category with examples from Hanafi fiqh.

First, kaffarah (expiation) is discussed. It has both worship and punishment aspects, but the worship aspect is dominant except for zihar kaffarah where punishment dominates because zihar involves objectionable speech.

Second, sadaqah fitr (alms for breaking fast) is categorized as worship with mu'nat. Its worship aspect purifies the fast, while its mu'nat aspect is shown by its cause (sabab) being riyas (dependency of those one supports). The worship aspect is dominant.

Third, ushr (tithe on agricultural land) is mu'nat with worship. The mu'nat is the tax burden; the worship is that it is obligatory only on Muslims. This leads to a difference: initially, ushr is not on a non-believer (worship aspect), but according to Imam Muhammad (a Hanafi imam), it remains on a non-believer who buys such land (mu'nat aspect).

Fourth, kharaj (land tax on non-Muslim land) is mu'nat with punishment. The mu'nat is the tax; the punishment is that it is not initially on Muslims because they are not subject to degradation. But it remains on a Muslim who acquires such land (mu'nat aspect).

Fifth, khums (fifth share of spoils and minerals) is a right established by itself (haqq qa'im bi dhatihi), not a duty on servants. It is not like zakat which is a purification of wealth. Therefore, khums may be given to those entitled to the four-fifths and even to Banu Hashim (who cannot receive zakat).

The passage concludes by listing examples of pure rights of servants (huquq al-ibad) such as guarantees for damaged property, diyah, usurped items, and rights of ownership and divorce.

This classification helps understand why some obligations apply differently to Muslims and non-believers, and how rights are distributed.

Khums: A Right Established by Itself (Not a Servant's Duty)
Classification of khums as a self-established right, distinct from servant duties

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish khums from zakat as a right established by itself (haqq qa'im bi dhatihi) and explain why it can be given to Banu Hashim and to those entitled to the four-fifths.

Core Idea
Khums is Allah's right, not a servant's duty; hence it can be given to Banu Hashim and even to the deserving four-fifths.

The final category in this passage is 'a right that is established by itself, having no connection to the liability of the servant.' The example is khums (the fifth share of spoils and minerals). The passage contrasts khums with zakat: zakat is a duty on the servant (implying purification of wealth), while khums is a right of Allah that He has kept for Himself, not imposed on servants as a tax. This essential difference explains two key rulings: (1) khums may be given to Banu Hashim (the Prophet's clan), whereas zakat cannot (zakat is 'impurity' of wealth); (2) khums may be given to those who are entitled to the four-fifths if they are needy, because it is not a purification of their wealth. The passage also clarifies that pure rights of servants (like guarantees for damage) are countless and unrelated to this category.

Guided Path
1
Understand the nature of khums as a right established by itself
Khums is a right of Allah that is not obligatory upon the servant as an act of obedience. Rather, Allah has designated a share of certain wealth for Himself and entrusted its distribution to the ruler.
This is different from zakat, which is a duty on the servant and a purification of wealth.
2
Contrast khums with zakat
Zakat is wajib on the servant as an act of worship; it purifies wealth. Khums is not a duty on the servant; it is a right that Allah has established on certain types of wealth (spoils, minerals).
The passage says 'if khums were like zakat, it would not be permissible to give it to those entitled to the four-fifths.'
3
Identify the consequence for Banu Hashim
Because khums is not 'impurity' (like zakat), it can be given to Banu Hashim. Zakat is impure and thus forbidden for Banu Hashim.
The term 'impurity' (midd/ kichil) refers to zakat being a cleansing from sins.
4
Identify the consequence for the four-fifths recipients
The four-fifths of spoils go to the mujahideen. If they are needy, they can also receive from the khums share because khums is not a purification of their own wealth.
This is in contrast to zakat, where the payer cannot receive zakat back.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of acts that have mixed legal natures between worship (ibadah), punishment (uqubah), and maintenance burden (mu'nat). The author, commenting on the matn of Al-Husami, explains each category with examples from Hanafi fiqh.

First, kaffarah (expiation) is discussed. It has both worship and punishment aspects, but the worship aspect is dominant except for zihar kaffarah where punishment dominates because zihar involves objectionable speech.

Second, sadaqah fitr (alms for breaking fast) is categorized as worship with mu'nat. Its worship aspect purifies the fast, while its mu'nat aspect is shown by its cause (sabab) being riyas (dependency of those one supports). The worship aspect is dominant.

Third, ushr (tithe on agricultural land) is mu'nat with worship. The mu'nat is the tax burden; the worship is that it is obligatory only on Muslims. This leads to a difference: initially, ushr is not on a non-believer (worship aspect), but according to Imam Muhammad (a Hanafi imam), it remains on a non-believer who buys such land (mu'nat aspect).

Fourth, kharaj (land tax on non-Muslim land) is mu'nat with punishment. The mu'nat is the tax; the punishment is that it is not initially on Muslims because they are not subject to degradation. But it remains on a Muslim who acquires such land (mu'nat aspect).

Fifth, khums (fifth share of spoils and minerals) is a right established by itself (haqq qa'im bi dhatihi), not a duty on servants. It is not like zakat which is a purification of wealth. Therefore, khums may be given to those entitled to the four-fifths and even to Banu Hashim (who cannot receive zakat).

The passage concludes by listing examples of pure rights of servants (huquq al-ibad) such as guarantees for damaged property, diyah, usurped items, and rights of ownership and divorce.

This classification helps understand why some obligations apply differently to Muslims and non-believers, and how rights are distributed.

Khums as a Self-Subsisting Right (Haqq Qa'im bi Dhatihi)
Defining khums as a self-subsisting right

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'haqq qa'im bi dhatihi', distinguish it from personal obligations like zakat, and derive the Hanafi ruling on the distribution of khums to Banu Hashim and relatives.

Core Idea
Khums is Allah's self-subsisting right, not personal filth; thus its distribution is flexible.

The passage explains a key usul distinction: some rights are self-subsisting (haqq qa'im bi dhatihi) – established by themselves without being a duty on individuals. Khums from spoils and minerals is of this type, unlike zakat which is a personal obligation. This distinction yields practical rulings on who may receive khums, including Banu Hashim and even the recipients of the four-fifths and their families.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central concept
Locate the term 'haqq qa'im bi dhatihi' and understand that it means a right that exists independently, not as a duty on a servant.
This is the key legal characterization.
2
Contrast with personal obligations
Note how the author contrasts khums with zakat: zakat is a worship duty (tā'ah) owed by individuals, while khums is not.
This contrast drives the different rulings.
3
Trace the reasoning (talil)
Follow the argument: because jihad is Allah's right, all spoils belong to Him; He graciously gave four-fifths to fighters and kept one-fifth for Himself. Thus khums is not a personal duty but a self-subsisting right.
The reasoning is based on the source of the wealth.
4
Derive the distributional consequences
From this nature, the author concludes that khums can be given to those who normally receive the four-fifths (if needy), to their parents and children, and to Banu Hashim – unlike zakat.
The flexibility stems from the absence of 'filth' (najāsah) attached to khums.
5
Reflect on the usul principle
Understand that the rule 'haqq qa'im bi dhatihi' is a legal category that affects how rights are governed: lack of personal liability allows wider discretion.
This is a general usul principle applicable beyond khums.

Commentary
This passage defines the legal nature of khums (the one-fifth share) as a 'self-subsisting right' (haqq qa'im bi dhatihi) belonging to Allah, distinct from obligations like zakat that are duties on individuals. The author, explaining the Urdu commentary on al-Husami, argues that khums from spoils of war and minerals is not a personal worship-based duty; rather, Allah reserved it for Himself and delegated distribution to the ruler. This distinction has practical consequences: since khums is not 'filth' (najasah) or impure wealth like zakat, it can be given to Banu Hashim (the Prophet's clan) and also to the very recipients of the four-fifths if they are needy, along with their parents and children. In contrast, zakat cannot be given to Banu Hashim or to one's own ascendants/descendants. The reasoning is based on the nature of the right—khums is not owed by individuals but is a share Allah kept for Himself, making it more flexible in distribution.
