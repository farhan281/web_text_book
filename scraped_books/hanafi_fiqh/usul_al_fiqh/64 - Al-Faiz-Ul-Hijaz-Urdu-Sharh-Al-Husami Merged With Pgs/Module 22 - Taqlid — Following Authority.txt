Definition and Framework of Taqlid of Sahaba
Definition and framework of taqlid of Sahaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define taqlid linguistically and technically, and identify the two conditions required for the dispute on taqlid of Sahaba.

Core Idea
Taqlid of a Companion is not always binding; conditions and context matter.

Before diving into the positions on taqlid of the Companions, we need to understand what taqlid means and under what conditions scholars even consider it obligatory. This lesson establishes the definition and the framework of the debate.

Guided Path
1
Define Taqlid
Identify the linguistic meaning (placing a necklace) and the technical meaning (accepting someone's statement without demanding proof).
Also note that taqlid is only for non-mujtahids.
2
Identify the Parties
The muqallid (follower) and muqallad (the one followed, here a Companion). A mujtahid cannot do taqlid.
3
List the Two Conditions
The passage states the dispute applies when: (1) the Companion's statement has no known dissent from another Companion, and (2) it is not proven that other Companions remained silent in acceptance (ijma sukuti).
If either condition is not met, the ruling changes.
4
Understand the Implications
If there is known dissent, taqlid is not obligatory; if there is silent acceptance, taqlid becomes obligatory by ijma.
The second condition creates a form of consensus.

The proof is that the Companion likely heard from the Prophet and gained knowledge; he lived in the Prophet's time. Even if it is proven that the Companion did not hear a hadith but spoke from his opinion, the Companion's correctness in opinion is greater than others because they witnessed revelation and know the causes of revelation. So the Companion's opinion is a proof. (2) The second opinion is from Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi: Taqlid of Companions is only obligatory in matters that cannot be perceived by qiyas, because in that case it is determined that the Companion's statement is based on hearing from the Prophet, not his opinion and qiyas, since the ruling is not attainable by qiyas, and lying is inconceivable from Companions. Commentary
This passage deals with the usul al-fiqh dispute regarding the authority of a Companion's statement (qawl al-sahabi) for later mujtahids. The author presents three positions: (1) Abu Sa'id al-Bardha'i (and a group of Hanafis, a narration from Malik, and al-Shafi'i's old view) holds that taqlid of a Companion is obligatory and qiyas is abandoned in favor of the Companion's statement. This is because the Companion either heard directly from the Prophet or, even if using ijtihad, his opinion is more reliable due to his proximity to revelation. (2) Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi (a prominent Hanafi) restricts obligatory taqlid to matters not reachable by qiyas (ghayr madrak bil-qiyas), such as those based on sensory observation or tawqif; in qiyasi matters, the Companion's statement is not binding. (3) Imam al-Shafi'i (and most Mutakallimun) denies any binding authority of a Companion's statement. The passage then clarifies two conditions for the dispute: the Companion's statement must be without known dissent from others, and without it being known that other Companions were silent (which would constitute ijma sukuti—a form of consensus). If Companions disagree, the truth is confined to their opinions, and later mujtahids must choose among them. The author also addresses a potential doubt: contradictory Companion sayings do not cancel each other out, because they indicate the absence of a textual proof (hadith), so each opinion stands as a valid basis for ijtihad. The discussion also touches on taqlid of a Tabi'i when conflicting with Companions, with some Hanafi mashayikh allowing it.

Key Terms
تقلید (taqlid): Accepting someone's saying or action as true without proof; technically, following a mujtahid without demanding evidence.
صحابی (sahabi): A Companion of the Prophet who met him, believed in him, and died as a Muslim.
اجماع سکوتی (ijma sukuti): A form of consensus where some mujtahids give an opinion and others remain silent, indicating acceptance.

The Three Positions on Taqlid of Sahaba
Three positions on taqlid of Sahaba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Sa'id al-Bardha'i, Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi, and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding taqlid of a Companion, and explain the evidence for each.

Core Idea
Bardha'i says follow the Companion always; Karkhi says only where qiyas is impossible; Shafi'i says never.

The passage presents three distinct views on whether later mujtahids must follow a Companion's statement. Each view is supported by different reasoning. This lesson unpacks each position and its proof.

Guided Path
1
State Bardha'i's Position
Bardha'i holds that taqlid of a Companion is always obligatory on the Tabi'i and later mujtahids, and qiyas is abandoned in favor of the Companion's statement.
He also includes groups like some Hanafis and the old view of al-Shafi'i.
2
Analyze Bardha'i's Evidence
His proof: the Companion either heard from the Prophet directly, or his opinion is superior due to witnessing revelation and knowing the causes. Even if his opinion, it is more correct.
Key: the Companion's proximity to revelation makes his view more reliable.
3
State Karkhi's Position
Karkhi restricts obligatory taqlid to matters not reachable by qiyas (ghayr madrak bil-qiyas). In qiyasi matters, the Companion's statement is not binding.
This is a middle position between Bardha'i and Shafi'i.
4
Analyze Karkhi's Evidence
In non-qiyasi matters, the Companion must have heard the ruling from the Prophet (since qiyas cannot reach it). In qiyasi matters, the Companion may have used his own ijtihad, which can err.
Key distinction: whether the ruling can be known through reason alone.
5
State Shafi'i's Position
Imam al-Shafi'i, along with most Mu'tazila and Ash'aris, holds that taqlid of any Companion is never obligatory, whether the matter is qiyasi or not.
They argue Companions are not infallible and later ijtihad may be better.

(۲) تقلید صحابہ کے بارے میں دوسرا قول امام ابوالحسن کرخی کا ہے، وہ کہتے ہیں کہ صحابہ کی تقلید صرف ان مسائل میں واجب ہے جن کا قیاس سے ادراک نہیں ہو سکتا ہے، کیونکہ اس میں یہ بات طے ہوگی کہ صحابی کا قول حضور سے سماع پر محمول ہے، وہ اپنی رائے اور قیاس سے نہیں کہہ رہا ہے، اس لئے کہ حکم غیر مدرک بالقیاس ہے، اور صحابہ سے کذب کا بھی تصور نہیں ہے، لہذا یہ متعین ہو جائے گا کہ صحابی حضور سے سن کر ہی بیان کر رہا ہے، اور اگر مسائل مدرک بالقیاس ہیں تو ان میں صحابی کے قول کی تقلید نہیں کی جائے گی ، اس میں یہ احتمال ہے کہ صحابی نے رائے اور قیاس سے یہ حکم بیان کیا ہو، اور رائے میں خطا کا امکان ہے اس لیے کہ صحابہ غیر معصوم ہیں ۔ وقال الشافعی : - تیسرا قول امام شافعی رضی اللہ عنہ کا ہے، کہ کسی صحابی کی تقلید نہیں کیجائے گی چاہے حکم مدرک بالقیاس ہو یا غیر مدرک بالقیاس ہو، اکثر معتزلہ اور اشاعرہ کا بھی یہی مذہب ہے۔
وهذا الخلاف فى كل ما ثبت تقلید صحابی میں یہ مذکورہ اختلاف اس وقت ہے جب یہ دو شرطیں پائی جائیں۔ (۱) کسی صحابی نے کوئی بات کہی اور دوسرے صحابی سے اس میں کوئی اختلاف ثابت نہ ہو، کیونکہ اگر دوسرے صحابی کی طرف سے اختلاف ثابت ہو گیا تو پھر بالاتفاق تقلید واجب نہ ہوگی بلکہ مجتہد کو حق ہوگا کہ ان دونوں میں سے جس پر چاہے عمل کرے۔ (۲) دوسری شرط یہ ہے کہ یہ بات بھی ثابت نہ ہو سکی ہو کہ یہ قول صحابی دوسرے صحابہ تک پہنچا اور اس پر ان سب حضرات نے سکوت کیا، کیونکہ اگر ایسی صورت ہوئی کہ وہ قول صحابہ کے پاس پہنچا اور انہوں نے سکوت اختیار کیا تو بالاتفاق تقلید واجب ہوگی کیونکہ یہ اجماع سکوتی کہلائے گا۔ دوسرا مسئلہ یہاں یہ سمجھیں کہ اگر کسی مسئلہ میں صحابہ نے اختلاف کیا ہو اور چند اقوال ہو گئے ہوں، تو حق ان اقوال سے متجاوز نہ ہوگا، بلکہ حق انہی اقوال میں منحصر ہوگا، لہذا بعد کے مجتہد کے لئے جائز نہ ہوگا کہ ان اقوال کے علاوہ اور کوئی قول اختیار کرے بلکہ اس کے لئے ضروری ہے کہ وہ انہی اقوال میں سے کسی قول کو دلائل کی روشنی میں ترجیح دے۔

'Their sayings: and one part does not fall due to another by contradiction, because the face of opinion is determined when argumentation among them by hadith did not occur, so it takes the place of qiyas. As for the Tabi'i, if he conflicts with them in fatwa, his taqlid is permissible according to some of our mashayikh, contrary to others.' Translation: The chapter that concludes the discussion on Sunnah is the chapter on following the Companions of the Messenger of Allah. Abu Sa'id al-Bardha'i said: Taqlid of a Companion is obligatory, qiyas is abandoned due to the Companion's statement because of the probability that he heard and acquired knowledge from the Prophet, and because of the superiority of the Companions' correctness in opinion due to their witnessing the circumstances of revelation and knowledge of its causes. Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi said: Taqlid of a Companion is not permissible except in matters that cannot be reached by qiyas. Imam al-Shafi'i said: No Companion's taqlid is to be made. This disagreement applies to everything proven from a Companion without internal disagreement, and without it being proven that the ruling reached another Companion who remained silent in acceptance. If the Companions disagreed on a ruling, the truth does not go beyond their sayings, and some sayings are not dropped due to contradiction because the face of opinion becomes determined when they did not argue by hadith, so the Companion's saying takes the place of qiyas. If a Tabi'i conflicts with them in fatwa, some of our mashayikh allow his taqlid, others not. Commentary: The author, after finishing the explanation of Sunnah, begins to discuss taqlid of Companions. Linguistically, taqlid means putting a necklace around one's neck. Technically, it means accepting someone's saying or action as true without proof. There is disagreement among scholars regarding taqlid of Companions. (1) Abu Sa'id al-Bardha'i says taqlid of a Companion is obligatory on the Tabi'i and later mujtahids, but not on another Companion. A group of our companions, one narration from Malik, and the qawl qadim of al-Shafi'i also hold this. Qiyas is abandoned due to the Companion's statement. The proof is that the Companion likely heard from the Prophet and gained knowledge; he lived in the Prophet's time. Even if it is proven that the Companion did not hear a hadith but spoke from his opinion, the Companion's correctness in opinion is greater than others because they witnessed revelation and know the causes of revelation. So the Companion's opinion is a proof. (2) The second opinion is from Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi: Taqlid of Companions is only obligatory in matters that cannot be perceived by qiyas, because in that case it is determined that the Companion's statement is based on hearing from the Prophet, not his opinion and qiyas, since the ruling is not attainable by qiyas, and lying is inconceivable from Companions. So it is certain that the Companion speaks from hearing. If the matters are attainable by qiyas, then taqlid of the Companion is not done because there is a probability that he expressed the ruling by opinion and qiyas, and opinion may err since Companions are not infallible. (3) The third opinion from Imam al-Shafi'i: Taqlid of any Companion is not to be done, whether the ruling is attainable by qiyas or not. Most Mu'tazila and Ash'aris also hold this. This mentioned disagreement applies when two conditions are met: (1) A Companion said something and no disagreement from another Companion is proven, because if disagreement is proven, then by consensus taqlid is not obligatory, and the mujtahid may act on whichever he wishes. (2) It is not proven that this statement reached other Companions and they remained silent, because if it reached them and they remained silent, then by consensus taqlid is obligatory, and this is called ijma sukuti. Second issue: If Companions disagreed on a matter and there are multiple sayings, the truth does not go beyond their sayings but is confined to them. Therefore, later mujtahids may not adopt a saying outside them; they must prefer one of their sayings based on evidence. The answer is that despite disagreement, no Companion presented a hadith, indicating there is no hadith in this regard, otherwise they would have presented it. Commentary
This passage deals with the usul al-fiqh dispute regarding the authority of a Companion's statement (qawl al-sahabi) for later mujtahids. The author presents three positions: (1) Abu Sa'id al-Bardha'i (and a group of Hanafis, a narration from Malik, and al-Shafi'i's old view) holds that taqlid of a Companion is obligatory and qiyas is abandoned in favor of the Companion's statement. This is because the Companion either heard directly from the Prophet or, even if using ijtihad, his opinion is more reliable due to his proximity to revelation. (2) Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi (a prominent Hanafi) restricts obligatory taqlid to matters not reachable by qiyas (ghayr madrak bil-qiyas), such as those based on sensory observation or tawqif; in qiyasi matters, the Companion's statement is not binding. (3) Imam al-Shafi'i (and most Mutakallimun) denies any binding authority of a Companion's statement. The passage then clarifies two conditions for the dispute: the Companion's statement must be without known dissent from others, and without it being known that other Companions were silent (which would constitute ijma sukuti—a form of consensus). If Companions disagree, the truth is confined to their opinions, and later mujtahids must choose among them. The author also addresses a potential doubt: contradictory Companion sayings do not cancel each other out, because they indicate the absence of a textual proof (hadith), so each opinion stands as a valid basis for ijtihad. The discussion also touches on taqlid of a Tabi'i when conflicting with Companions, with some Hanafi mashayikh allowing it.

Key Terms
تقلید (taqlid): Accepting someone's saying or action as true without proof; technically, following a mujtahid without demanding evidence.
صحابی (sahabi): A Companion of the Prophet who met him, believed in him, and died as a Muslim.
اجماع سکوتی (ijma sukuti): A form of consensus where some mujtahids give an opinion and others remain silent, indicating acceptance.
غیر مدرک بالقیاس (ghayr madrak bil-qiyas): A ruling that cannot be reached through analogical reasoning (qiyas); often matters based on textual stipulation or sensory observation.

Key Takeaway
The binding authority of a Companion's statement varies: always (Bardha'i), only in non-qiyasi matters (Karkhi), or never (Shafi'i), and the Hanafi school's dominant view is Karkhi's.
