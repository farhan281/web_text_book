The Four Sources of Shariah: Definition from the Matn
Opening definition of usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four sources of Shariah as defined in the Hanafi matn and explain why Qiyas is considered a derived source (mustanbat).

Core Idea
The Shariah's principles are three primary sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) and one derived source (Qiyas) extracted from them.

This passage opens the foundational Hanafi usul text, Al-Muntakhab al-Husami, by defining the four sources of Islamic law. The author establishes a clear hierarchy: the first three are independent and primary, while the fourth, Qiyas, is derived from them. This taxonomy underpins all subsequent Hanafi legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three primary sources
Find in the text: 'الْكِتَابَ وَالسُّنَّةُ وَإِجْمَاعُ الْاُمَةِ' (the Book, the Sunnah, and the consensus of the Ummah). These are the direct, independent sources of rulings.
These are universally agreed upon in Sunni Islam.
2
Note the fourth source and its key descriptor
The text says: 'وَالْأَصْلُ الرَّابِعُ الْقَيَاسُ الْمُسْتَشْبَطُ مِنْهَذِهِ الْأَصْوَل' (the fourth principle is Qiyas, which is derived from these principles). The word 'mustanbat' (derived) indicates that Qiyas is not independent but draws its authority from the primary sources.
'Mustanbat' is crucial for understanding Qiyas's subordinate role.
3
Understand the term 'usul al-shar'
The text calls these 'أصول الشرع' (principles of Shariah). This means they are the foundational sources from which all fiqh rulings are derived. This classification sets the scope of usul al-fiqh.
The singular 'asl' means source or root.
4
Recognize the introductory structure
The author begins with basmalah and hamdalah, following prophetic practice, then uses 'amma ba'd' to transition to the main subject. This reflects the classical method of beginning scholarly works.
This structure itself is derived from the Sunnah.

Commentary: The author began his book with the Tasmiyah and Tahmid, because in this there is following of the Quran and practice of the Hadith of the Messenger. The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'Every matter of importance that does not begin with Bismillah is severed.' In another hadith, in place of Bismillah, 'with praise of Allah' is mentioned: 'it is cut off and decisive.' Also, this was the practice of the predecessors and successors. 'Amma ba'd': The author, in following the hadith, began the sermon of the book with 'amma ba'd'. The Prophet (peace be upon him) used to mention this word in his sermons. The first one to utter this word was Prophet Dawud (peace be upon him). 'Ba'd' and 'qabl' are used for both time and place. For time, e.g., 'today is before tomorrow', 'tomorrow is after today'. For place, e.g., 'my house is before your house', 'my house is after your house'. The rest of the detail you have read in various places. 'Hamd': lexically means to praise; technically, it is verbal praise for a voluntary good quality, whether a blessing or not. 'Allah' is a proper name for the Essence of the Exalted. 'Nawal' means gift, donation. 'Salat': lexically means supplication; originally it was 'salawat', but due to a moving waw preceded by a silent sound letter, the movement of the waw was transferred to the preceding letter, becoming 'salat'. Then by way of metonymy (majaz mursal), 'salat' came to be used for the specific actions. When attributed to Allah, it means mercy; to angels, it means seeking forgiveness; to believers, it means seeking mercy and supplication; and 'salat al-tuyur' means glorification. 'Rasul' means sent one; it is used for a prophet who is sent with a new book and law.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the matn (Al-Muntakhab al-Husami). It begins with the basmalah and hamdalah, following prophetic practice. Then it defines the sources of Islamic law (usul al-shar'): three primary sources—the Quran, the Sunnah, and Ijma (consensus)—and a fourth derived source, Qiyas (analogical reasoning), which is derived from the three. The term 'mustanbat' (derived) indicates that qiyas is not an independent source but one that draws rulings from the primary sources. The commentary then explains each introductory word: 'Amma ba'd', 'hamd', 'nawal', 'salat', 'rasul'. It provides lexical and technical definitions, often with examples from Arabic usage. This sets the foundation for the usul methodology that follows.

Key Terms
أصول (usul): Principles or sources; here specifically the sources of Shariah from which rulings are derived.
الكتاب (al-kitab): The Quran, the revealed book of Allah.
السنة (al-sunnah): The sayings, actions, and tacit approvals of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him).
الإجماع (al-ijma'): Consensus of the entire Muslim community (or specifically the scholars) on a ruling.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning from a principle found in the primary sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) to a new case.

The Four Principles of Shariah and the Scope of Usul al-Shar
Enumeration of the four usul and justification of terminology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four principles of Shariah according to the Hanafi school and explain why the author uses 'usul al-shar' instead of 'usul al-fiqh'.

Core Idea
Usul al-shar includes fiqh and kalam; qiyas is a derived source not a primary one.

This lesson examines the foundational sources of Islamic law as defined by the Hanafi school. The passage clarifies that the primary sources are three: the Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma', with Qiyas added as a fourth derived source. The author's choice of the term 'usul al-shar' over 'usul al-fiqh' is intentional to encompass both fiqh and kalam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three primary principles
The text lists the Book of Allah, the Sunnah, and the consensus of the community as the first three principles.
2
Understand the rationale for 'usul al-shar'
The author uses 'usul al-shar' because the same principles are foundational to both fiqh and kalam; 'shar' is a general term including both.
This distinguishes it from 'usul al-fiqh' which is limited to jurisprudence.
3
Recognize qiyas as the fourth principle
After the three primary sources, qiyas (analogical deduction) is added as a fourth principle, but it is derived from the first three.
The text specifies 'derived from these principles' to indicate shari'i qiyas, not any analogy.
4
Grasp the dual nature of qiyas
Qiyas is called 'the fourth principle' to emphasize that it is an asl (source) in one respect but a far' (branch) in another, unlike the first three which are principles from every perspective.
This establishes its subordinate status.

The author says that the principles (adillah) of Shariah are three: the Book of Allah, the Sunnah, and the consensus of the community. He said 'usul al-shar' not 'usul al-fiqh', because the Book, Sunnah, and consensus are principles of fiqh as well as of kalam. The word 'shar' is general to both fiqh and kalam, so he said 'usul al-shar' to include both. After these three principles, the fourth principle is analogical deduction (qiyas) derived from these three principles and evidences. The qualification 'derived from these principles' indicates that not any qiyas is meant, but specifically shari'i qiyas. Also, by calling it 'the fourth principle', qiyas is distinguished from the first three because from one perspective it is an asl (principle), from another it is a far' (branch), unlike the first three which are principles from every perspective. Commentary
This passage is the commentary on the matn's statement regarding the principles of Shariah. The commentator clarifies why the author uses 'usul al-shar' instead of 'usul al-fiqh': the principles (Book, Sunnah, Ijma') are foundational to both fiqh and kalam (theology), so the general term 'shar' is appropriate to encompass both disciplines. Qiyas is distinguished as a fourth principle because it is derived from the first three and is an asl in one respect (as a source of law) and a far' in another (since it depends on the primary sources). The definition of the first principle, the Book, is given as the Quran: revealed to the Prophet, preserved in written form (mushafs), transmitted via tawatur (massively recurrent transmission) without any doubt, and it comprises both the wording (lafz) and the meaning (ma'na) according to the majority of scholars. This definition establishes the Quran's authority and inimitability.

Key Terms
أصول الشرع (usul al-shar'): The principles or evidences of Shariah, including the Book, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical deduction derived from the primary sources (Book, Sunnah, Ijma'), considered a fourth principle but not an independent source in the same sense.

Defining the Quran as the First Principle of Shariah
Definition of the Quran as the first principle

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the Quran according to the Hanafi school, listing its key attributes: revelation, written in mushafs, mutawatir transmission, and comprising both wording and meaning.

Core Idea
The Quran is defined as the revealed, written, mutawatir text that is both wording and meaning.

After establishing the four principles, the passage defines the first and foremost principle: the Book. This definition is crucial because it establishes the Quran's authority and inimitability. The Hanafi school emphasizes that the Quran includes both the revealed wording and the intended meaning, a point supported by consensus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition components
The Quran is defined as: revealed to the Messenger, written in the mushafs, transmitted via tawatur without doubt, and it is both wording and meaning.
2
Understand 'revealed to the Messenger'
This indicates the Quran is divine revelation specifically to Prophet Muhammad, not any other book.
Distinguishes it from previous scriptures.
3
Recognize 'written in the mushafs'
The Quran is physically recorded in written copies, which ensures preservation.
This differentiates it from oral traditions.
4
Grasp 'transmitted mutawatir'
The transmission is through multiple chains that make doubt impossible, establishing certainty.
This separates the Quran from ahadith that are not mutawatir.
5
Comprehend 'both wording and meaning'
The Quran consists of its precise wording (al-nazm) and its intended meanings (al-ma'na), both are integral.
Supported by consensus (ijma') of the majority of scholars.

Definition of the Qur'an in Hanafi Usul: Wording and Meaning
Defines the Qur'an with conditions and reconciles a tension regarding prayer.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four conditions of the Hanafi definition of the Qur'an and explain why Abu Hanifa's ruling on prayer does not contradict that the Qur'an is both wording and meaning.

Core Idea
The Qur'an is both wording and meaning, but for prayer, the meaning suffices when unable to recite Arabic.

This passage defines the Qur'an as the first source of Shari'ah, setting out the conditions that distinguish it from other texts. It also addresses a classic tension: if the Qur'an is defined by its Arabic wording, how can Abu Hanifa permit prayer with Persian translation? The answer lies in understanding the general definition versus the exceptional ruling for prayer.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed definition of the Qur'an (Kitab) as the first source of Shari'ah. The definition includes four conditions: it must be revealed to the Messenger, written in the mushaf collections, transmitted via mutawatir (massive continuous transmission) without any suspicion, and it comprises both the wording (nazm) and the meaning (ma'na) according to the majority of scholars. This exception does not negate the general definition that the Qur'an is both wording and meaning. The commentator also explains how each condition operates: 'al-munazzal' excludes non-revealed books; 'ala al-rasul' excludes earlier scriptures; 'al-maktub fi al-masahif' excludes verses whose recitation is abrogated (like the stoning verse); 'al-manqul mutawatiran' excludes irregular (shadhdh) readings. The passage ends with a crucial question about whether the Qur'an is merely the wording (from the definition) or merely the meaning (from Abu Hanifa's prayer ruling), which is a classic tension in Hanafi usul. The answer follows standard Hanafi: the Qur'an is both, but for prayer, the meaning is sufficient because the obligation is to communicate the message, not to reproduce the exact Arabic.

The Hanafi Definition of the Quran: Wording and Meaning

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Quran is both nazm and ma'na; fatwa requires Arabic recitation.

In this passage from Al-Husami's sharh, the author clarifies the definition of the Quran in Usul al-Fiqh. He addresses a key question: Is the Quran solely the Arabic wording, solely the meaning, or both? By analyzing the conditions of the definition and a Quranic proof, the author affirms the majority Hanafi position that the Quran is the combination of nazm and ma'na. He then explains Imam Abu Hanifa's initial allowance of Persian recitation in prayer as an exception based on an excuse, and notes his later retraction, establishing the operative fatwa that recitation must be in Arabic.

Recognize that some scholars argue Quran is only wording (nazm), while others (including the majority) say it is both nazm and ma'na. The passage presents the combined view as correct.

The Hanafi position initially allowed Persian under an excuse (fear of missing the essence of supplication), but later the Imam retracted, and the fatwa now prohibits it when one is able to recite Arabic.
This shows how the definition impacts fiqh rulings.

The passage states that the fatwa is against Persian recitation. Thus, the operative ruling is that prayer requires Arabic recitation for those able to do so.
The retraction brings the Hanafi position in line with the majority.

The Four Types of Textual Inference: Ibarah and Isharah
Introducing four types of textual inference and detailing ibarah and isharah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between ibarat al-nass and isharat al-nass, and apply the Hanafi principle that isharah is equal in obligation but weaker in case of conflict.

Core Idea
Ibarah is the direct intent; isharah is implied but equally binding unless they conflict.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational Hanafi classification of four ways a mujtahid derives rulings from the Quranic text. The passage introduces these four categories and then delves into the first two: explicit text (ibarah) and its indication (isharah). Understanding the difference is crucial for correct istinbat, as the Hanafi school treats both as binding but prioritizes ibarah when they conflict.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four types
From the classification, list all four types in Arabic and English: ibarat al-nass (explicit text), isharat al-nass (textual indication), dalalat al-nass (textual implication), iqtida' al-nass (textual requirement).
The passage focuses on the first two, but remember all four exist.
2
Define ibarah
Ibarah is the meaning for which the speech is directly uttered and intended. It is the strongest form of inference.
Example: the verse 'lil-fuqara' al-muhajirin' directly intends to give the emigrants a share of booty.
3
Define isharah
Isharah is a meaning established by the text but not directly intended; it is an implied indication. It is equal in obligation to ibarah but weaker in case of conflict.
Example: the same verse indicates that the emigrants lost their property, though that is not the primary intent.
4
Analyze the example
From Quran 59:8, separate the ibarah (obligation of a share for the Muhajirun) from the isharah (their loss of property to disbelievers). Both are valid rulings from the text.
The ibarah is stronger; if the isharah somehow contradicted the ibarah, the ibarah would prevail.
5
Apply the rule of conflict
When ibarah and isharah conflict, ibarah takes precedence. In the absence of conflict, both are equally binding.
This is a key principle for weighting evidence in Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage introduces the fourth division in the knowledge of how a mujtahid derives rulings from the Quranic text (nazm). 'Ibarah and Dalalah: Deriving the Ruling of Kafarah from a Hadith
Deriving ruling of kafarah from hadith using 'ibarah and dalalah an-nass

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'ibarat al-nass and dalalat al-nass and apply each to derive a ruling from a hadith.

Core Idea
'Ibarah gives specific rulings; dalalah unlocks general ones.

Understanding how Hanafi usul categorizes textual indications is key to deriving rulings correctly. In this lesson, we examine a hadith about a Bedouin who broke his fast. The explicit wording ('ibarah) proves expiation for him alone, while the implied meaning (dalalah) extends the ruling to all who intentionally break the fast. This demonstrates the power of dalalah in expanding rulings beyond their specific context.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling context
Read the hadith and note that the Prophet gave expiation options to a specific Bedouin who broke his fast intentionally.
2
Extract the ruling via 'ibarah
The explicit command to feed sixty poor or fast two months directly indicates expiation for that Bedouin. This is 'ibarat al-nass.
3
Identify the underlying cause (illah)
The common factor between the Bedouin and any other person who intentionally breaks fast is the act of deliberate breaking. This illah is the basis for generalization.
4
Derive the general ruling via dalalah
Because the text implies that the ruling applies whenever the cause exists, dalalat al-nass establishes that expiation is wajib for every intentional breaker of Ramadan fast.
5
Compare the two indications
Note that 'ibarah gives a specific application while dalalah gives a general rule. Both are valid from the same text but operate at different levels.

Imperative After Prohibition: Hanafi View of Permission
Defense of Hanafi view on amr after prohibition using qara'in

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that an imperative after prohibition indicates permission (ibaha) when accompanied by qara'in, and evaluate the Shafi'i counterargument using Quranic evidence.

Core Idea
An imperative after prohibition indicates permission due to accompanying qara'in, not automatic obligation.

This lesson delves into a key dispute in usul al-fiqh: when Allah issues a command after a prohibition, does it obligate or permit? The Hanafi school argues that contextual indications (qara'in) can shift the meaning to permission. Using the example of hunting after ihram, we'll see how the Hanafis employ both verbal and rational qara'in to derive ibaha, while the Shafi'is cite the verse on killing polytheists as evidence for obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi claim
State clearly that the Hanafi position is that an imperative (amr) after a prohibition (nahy) indicates permission (ibaha). The key example is the Quranic command to hunt (فاصطادوا) after the prohibition of hunting in ihram.
The claim relies on the presence of specific qara'in, not the imperative form alone.
2
Analyze the Shafi'i objection
The majority (including Shafi'is) argue that commands after prohibition can still indicate obligation, as in the verse فاقتلوا المشركين after the prohibition of fighting during sacred months. Understand their proof: the imperative form is the same, so it should carry the same default meaning of obligation.
The Shafi'i view is that the default for amr is wujub, regardless of preceding context.
3
Examine the Hanafi response: verbal qarina
The Hanafi response distinguishes the hunting verse by pointing to a verbal qarina: the preceding phrase 'احل لكم الطيبات' (good things are made lawful). The word 'halal' indicates permission, so the command is colored by this context.
The qarina is explicit: the same context declares the act lawful, so the command reinforces permission, not obligation.
4
Examine the Hanafi response: rational qarina
A rational qarina is also invoked: the command to hunt is framed as a favor (ذكر احسان) for the benefit of the servants (بندوں کے نفع). Benefit aligns with permission, not obligation, because obligation entails hardship. This rational indication supports ibaha.
The Hanafi method combines textual and rational evidence to determine the intended meaning.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: for any act that was previously prohibited and then commanded, the Hanafi rule says it becomes mubah unless a qarina indicates obligation; the Shafi'i rule says it becomes wajib unless a qarina indicates permission. Apply this to a new scenario, e.g., eating after fasting hours.
These differences affect hundreds of fiqh rulings, especially in ibadat and muamalat.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school holds that the concession for shortening prayer is an absolute dropping, not a mere permission with choice.

This lesson examines the nature of the rukhsah for qasr al-salah during travel. The Hanafi school argues that it is an isqat mahd (absolute dropping), while the Shafi'i school views it as takhfif (lightening) with choice. We will analyze the two Hanafi proofs and the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core legal issue
The dispute is over the nature of the rukhsah: is it an isqat (dropping of the original ruling) or a takhfif (lightening with choice)?
This determines whether the traveler must pray two rak'ahs or may choose.
2
State the Hanafi position and its practical consequence
According to the Hanafis, qasr is an absolute dropping so the traveler must pray two rak'ahs; praying four intentionally invalidates the prayer.
This is based on the hadith calling qasr a 'sadaqah' (charity) from Allah.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i position and its proof
Imam al-Shafi'i relies on Quran 4:101, which negates blame (junah) and conditions qasr on fear, indicating permissibility rather than obligation.
The Shafi'i also analogizes with fasting where a traveler has a choice.
4
Examine the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i proof
The Hanafis respond that the negation of blame was to remove Companions' doubt, and the condition of fear is coincidental (ittifaqi), not restrictive.
Thus the verse does not imply choice.
5
Evaluate the two Hanafi proofs
First proof: the hadith calls qasr a 'sadaqah' – charity of something that cannot be owned is an isqat. Second proof: true rukhsah requires ease in both options, but here ease is only in qasr, making it obligatory.
Both proofs lead to the conclusion that qasr is an absolute dropping.

That the name includes ease, not worthy of worship, unlike fasting because the text came with delay not with charity, and ease in fasting is conflicting, so giving choice in fasting is to seek ease. Explanation: The fourth example of the fourth type: just as the Lawgiver gave a concession to the traveler in prayer; for all people it is four rak'ahs, and for the traveler it is two rak'ahs. According to us, this qasr (shortening) concession is an isqat (dropping), i.e., it drops the azimah (original ruling). So for the traveler, there is no azimah; thus calling qasr a concession is metaphorical, because there is no azimah in its opposite. Therefore the traveler will pray only two rak'ahs; his zuhr and fajr will be equal. If he prays four, he will be sinful. If he prays four and sits after two, the extra two become nafl, but intending nafl along with fard is not permissible. If he does not sit, the prayer becomes invalid. However, since azimah exists for non-travelers, the application of metaphor to qasr prayer is not complete, it is deficient, because azimah is not totally absent. Imam al-Shafi'i (may Allah have mercy on him) says that the concession is takhfif (lightening), i.e., the traveler is given a concession of two rak'ahs for ease, but the azimah, i.e., four rak'ahs, is better, just as the traveler is given a concession not to fast in Ramadan, but the azimah, i.e., fasting, is better. Imam al-Shafi'i's proof is the saying of Allah the Exalted: 'And when you travel in the land, there is no blame upon you for shortening the prayer, if you fear that those who disbelieve may afflict you.' In this verse, blame (junah) is negated, that there is no harm in shortening; and negation of harm indicates permissibility (ibahah). The meaning is that qasr is permissible, not obligatory. And qasr is conditioned upon fear: if you fear the affliction of the disbelievers, then shorten. Our response is that some Companions had doubt that shortening involved harm, so the negation of harm was for their reassurance, and the condition of fear is coincidental (ittifaqi), not exclusionary (ihtirazi). 'And we made it an absolute dropping (isqatan mahdan)': The author is presenting the proof of the Hanafis: that we made qasr prayer, which is a concession, an isqat, we have argued with two proofs: one from the word 'rukhsah' (concession) and one from the meaning of rukhsah. The argument from the word 'rukhsah' is that 'Umar (?) asked the Prophet (peace be upon him): 'Should we shorten the prayer when we are in a state of security?' The Prophet (peace be upon him) perhaps understood from the condition 'if you fear' that qasr is specific to fear. So the Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'This is a charity (sadaqah) from Allah upon His servants, so accept His charity.' The Prophet (peace be upon him) made qasr prayer a charity, and the charity of something that does not involve the possibility of ownership (tamlik) is an absolute dropping; i.e., the charity of something that cannot be owned is an isqat. Therefore, the azimah is dropped from the traveler. And something that is of the type of isqat cannot be rejected (radd) by the servant; it cannot be refused whether he accepts it or not, like the forgiveness of qisas (retaliation). This is isqat, so it cannot be rejected. When the person entitled to qisas forgives, qisas is dropped, whether the killer accepts or not; even if he rejects, it is not rejected. In short, qasr prayer is an absolute dropping, so it cannot be rejected; rather, qasr is obligatory upon the traveler and azimah is dropped, because prayer was called charity and it cannot involve ownership. Yes, where ownership is possible, i.e., it can be owned, the charity of such a thing can be rejected; for example, if a creditor says to the debtor, 'I have given the debt as charity,' the debtor can reject it, because debt is of the type of ownership. The second argument to prove that qasr prayer is an absolute dropping is from the meaning of rukhsah. The author argues from the meaning of rukhsah in two ways. The first way: when a true concession (rukhsah haqiqiyyah) is established, the servant has a choice to act upon either the concession or the azimah, because just as there is ease (yusr) in the concession, so too there is ease in the azimah, either through reward and higher ranks, as in the case of compulsion to utter a word of disbelief: the azimah is not to say it even if one is killed, and the concession is to say it to save one's life. Here, both have ease: in the concession, ease is saving life; in the azimah, ease is attaining martyrdom if killed. So there is ease in both, hence choice is given. But if in a particular situation there is no ease in the azimah, only in the concession, then the servant has no choice, because ease is confined to the concession. Therefore, the concession becomes obligatory and the azimah is dropped. In the case of qasr prayer, this is the situation: ease is confined to qasr; there is no ease in the azimah, i.e., praying four rak'ahs—neither greater reward nor any other ease. So the concession becomes obligatory and the azimah is dropped.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of the nature of the concession (rukhsah) for shortening prayer during travel (qasr al-salah) from a Hanafi usul perspective. The commentator is expanding on the matn of Al-Muntakhab Al-Husami. The passage first establishes that the Hanafi school considers qasr to be an absolute dropping (isqat mahd) of the original ruling (azimah). This means that for the traveler, the original obligation of four rak'ahs is entirely removed, and the two-rak'ah prayer is the only valid obligation; praying four rak'ahs intentionally would be sinful. This is contrasted with the Shafi'i position, which views the concession as a mere lightening (takhfif) where the original ruling remains but is simply not required, so the traveler has a choice, with the four-rak'ah prayer being preferable (awla). The Shafi'i proof is the Quranic verse (4:101) which negates blame (junah) for shortening, indicating permissibility, and it ties qasr to the condition of fear. The Hanafi response clarifies that the negation of blame was to remove the Companions' doubt, and the condition of fear is incidental, not a true restriction. The commentator then presents the two Hanafi proofs. The first proof is linguistic: in the hadith, the Prophet called qasr a 'charity' (sadaqah) from Allah. According to Hanafi usul, charity of something that cannot be owned (like a legal obligation) is equivalent to dropping it (isqat). Since the obligation of four rak'ahs cannot be owned, it is simply dropped, and the traveler cannot reject it; it is obligatory. The analogy with forgiveness of qisas (retaliation) illustrates that an isqat cannot be rejected. The second proof is based on the meaning of concession itself. In a true concession, there should be ease (yusr) in both the concession and the original ruling, giving the servant a choice. However, if ease is only present in the concession and absent in the original ruling, then the concession becomes the only viable option, making it obligatory. For qasr, the ease is only in shortening; there is no ease or extra reward in praying four rak'ahs while traveling, so the concession is necessary. Therefore, the Hanafi position is that qasr is compulsory and the traveler must pray two rak'ahs.

Key Terms
رخصت (rukhsah): A concession granted by the Lawgiver that relaxes the original ruling (azimah) under specific circumstances.
عزیمت (azimah): The original, primary ruling of the Lawgiver without any concession.
اسقاط (isqat): The act of dropping or canceling a legal obligation, leaving no room for choice.

The Hanafi Hierarchy for Resolving Conflict of Evidences
Principles for resolving apparent contradictions between dala'il

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi hierarchy for resolving apparent contradictions between legal proofs (Quran, Sunnah, qiyas, and aqwal al-sahabah) and explain the principle of taqrir al-usul using the example of the water of the donkey.

Core Idea
No real conflict in divine sources; hierarchy guides us: Quran→Sunnah→qiyas→taqrir al-usul.

In this chapter, al-Husami outlines the Hanafi method for handling apparent conflicts between Islamic legal evidences. The key insight is that the sources themselves are perfectly consistent; any perceived contradiction is due to our ignorance of abrogation (naskh). When two proofs of equal rank conflict and cannot be reconciled, the Hanafi school prescribes a clear hierarchy for moving from one level of evidence to the next, and if even analogical reasoning fails, we fall back on the original legal principle (taqrir al-usul). The classic illustration is the case of water used by a donkey, which became a mashkuk (doubtful) case leading to a combined ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational principle
Start by noting the premise: Quranic and Prophetic proofs do not truly conflict in themselves. Conflict arises only due to our limited knowledge of abrogation. This is a fundamental Hanafi belief that ensures the integrity of revelation.
This premise separates Hanafi usul from approaches that allow real contradiction in sources.
2
Memorize the conflict-resolution hierarchy
The text gives a step-by-step order: (1) If two Quranic verses conflict, refer to the Sunnah. (2) If two hadiths conflict, refer to qiyas (analogy) and then to the sayings of the Companions. (3) If qiyas is not suitable, both conflicting proofs fall and we must resort to the next level of evidence. (4) If no further evidence is available, we rest on the original principle (taqrir al-usul).
The order is based on the relative strength of proof categories in Hanafi usul.
3
Understand taqrir al-usul
Taqrir al-usul means falling back on the original legal ruling that is established by the primary sources before the conflict arose. The text gives the example of water: originally water is pure, so even if conflicting evidence leaves its status doubtful, we hold it as pure in origin. But because its purifying ability is uncertain, we combine wudu with tayammum. This combined ruling is called 'mashkuk' (doubtful).
This is a distinctive Hanafi method: not simply suspending judgment, but deriving a practical ruling from the original asl.
4
Analyze the example of the donkey's water
Reflect on the case: conflicting proofs about the water used by a donkey left its taharah uncertain. Qiyas could not establish a new ruling initially because analogy cannot create a ruling from scratch. Therefore, the jurists said: the water is pure in itself (taqrir al-asl), but it does not remove hadath (ritual impurity), so one must perform both wudu and tayammum. This is a hypothetical mashkuk case showing the application of taqrir al-usul.
The name 'mashkuk' (doubtful) indicates the ruling is a compromise preserving the original certainty.
5
Distinguish mu'aradah from tanaqud
The commentary defines mu'aradah as opposition of two equal proofs where reconciliation is impossible. Tanaqud is the failure of a proof to correspond to its implication (i.e., a logical inconsistency within a single proof). Mu'aradah involves two separate proofs; tanaqud is an internal flaw. The Hanafi method addresses mu'aradah, not tanaqud.
Tanaqud is generally not considered a real issue in revealed sources.

Commentary
This chapter explains the Hanafi methodology for resolving apparent contradictions between Islamic legal proofs. The key principle is that there is no real conflict in the divine sources themselves; conflict arises from our ignorance of abrogation. The text lays out a hierarchy: if two Quranic verses conflict, we refer to the Sunnah. If two hadiths conflict, we refer to qiyas (analogy) and then to the sayings of the Companions. If both conflicting proofs are equal and cannot be reconciled, they both fall, and we move to the next level of evidence. If even that is impossible, we fall back on the original legal principle (al-asl). The classic example given is the water used by a donkey: conflicting proofs left it unclear, so we hold onto the original ruling that water is pure, but it does not remove ritual impurity, so we combine wudu with tayammum. The Urdu commentary defines mu'aradah as the opposition of two equal proofs where reconciliation is impossible, and tanaqud as the failure of the proof to correspond to its implication.

Key Terms
معارضة (mu'aradah): Conflict between two equal proofs such that reconciliation is impossible.
تقرير الأصول (taqrir al-usul): Resting on the original legal principle when no further evidence is available.
مشكوك (mashkuk): Doubtful case; a ruling that combines two contradictory principles (e.g., water is pure but does not remove hadath).

The Lam in Zakat Verse: Lam al-'Aqibah vs Lam al-Tamlik
Hanafi refutation of Shafi'i objection to zakat distribution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the lam in the zakat verse as lam al-'aqibah and apply the Hanafi principle to allow giving zakat to fewer than eight categories.

Core Idea
The lam in the zakat verse indicates destination, not ownership—zakat may be given to one category.

The distribution of zakat among the eight categories mentioned in Surah al-Tawbah (9:60) is a point of contention between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. The debate hinges on the grammatical function of the lam in 'lil-fuqara'i'. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi argument that the lam is lam al-'aqibah (lam of outcome), based on the ultimate destination of zakat after being paid to Allah, and the analogy of the Ka'bah as qiblah.

Guided Path
1
State the Shafi'i position and its basis
Identify the Shafi'i view that the lam in the verse is lam al-tamlik (ownership), obligating distribution to all eight categories simultaneously.
The Shafi'i rely on the apparent meaning of the verse: 'innama al-sadaqatu lil-fuqara'i...' as granting ownership to each category.
2
State the Hanafi counter-argument
The Hanafis argue the lam is lam al-'aqibah, meaning the zakat ultimately reaches the poor, not that they own it immediately.
This is supported by the concept that zakat is first paid to Allah (as a right), then transferred to recipients as deputies.
3
Follow the Hanafi proof
Examine the reasoning: zakat enters Allah's possession first, then the poor receive it as deputies. Thus, the lam indicates destination after payment.
The Hanafi also use the Ka'bah analogy: just as any part of the Ka'bah suffices as a qiblah, any one category suffices for zakat.
4
Understand the ta'lil (illah derivation)
The Hanafis derive the illah as 'need' (hajah), which is common to all categories and to individuals within a category.
This ta'lil overrides the apparent requirement of distributing to all eight categories.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis permit giving zakat to a single category or even a single individual; Shafi'is require distribution among all eight categories if possible.
The Hanafi position is mufta bihi in the Hanafi school.

The Lam in 'Innama al-Sadaqat' as Lam al-'Aqibah
Textual analysis of Quran 9:60 with a Hanafi rebuttal to the Shafi'i interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of lam al-'aqibah to analyze Quranic prepositions and derive the ruling that giving zakat to one category suffices.

Core Idea
The lam in Quran 9:60 indicates consequence, not ownership, so zakat need not be distributed to all eight categories.

This lesson delves into a classic Hanafi- Shafi'i dispute over the interpretation of the preposition 'lam' in the zakat verse. By understanding how the Hanafi school classifies this lam as indicating consequence rather than ownership, you will see how a seemingly minor grammatical point can lead to a significant difference in fiqh rulings. We will walk through the argument that zakat first belongs to Allah, making the poor eventual recipients, not immediate owners.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Disputed Lam
Locate the lam in 'innama al-sadaqatu lil-fuqara' and note that Shafi'is interpret it as lam al-tamlik (ownership), Hanafis as lam al-'aqibah (consequence).
The dispute centers on whether 'lil' indicates immediate transfer of ownership or eventual outcome.
2
Analyze the Hanafi First Proof
Examine the argument that zakat first enters Allah's possession (via the payer), then the poor receive as deputies. The lam thus denotes the final consequence – possession after Allah's deputation.
This refutes immediate ownership because the poor do not own before payment to Allah.
3
Analyze the Hanafi Second Proof
Note that the text says 'after it becomes charity', which happens upon payment to Allah, not upon the poor's possession. Therefore, the lam is for consequence.
The ordering 'payment first, then possession' supports lam al-'aqibah.
4

The author compares the eight categories to the Kaaba as a qibla: the whole Kaaba is qibla, and every part is qibla. Similarly, each category is a valid recipient due to need, not entitlement.
This illustrates that giving to one category fulfills zakat just as facing any part of the Kaaba fulfills prayer.
5
Derive the Ruling
From the above, conclude that giving zakat to one category or even one individual suffices, contrary to the Shafi'i requirement of distributing to all eight.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is a more flexible distribution.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of the Quranic verse 9:60, addressing the Shafi'i argument that the lam in 'lil-fuqara' indicates ownership (tamlik), thus requiring distribution to all eight categories. The author, a Hanafi jurist, argues that the lam is actually for consequence ('aqibah), not ownership. He provides two proofs: First, by explaining that zakat first enters Allah's possession (the poor receive as deputies), so the ultimate ownership is after a chain of events, making the lam denote consequence. Second, the text obligates giving after the wealth becomes charity, which occurs upon payment to Allah, not upon the poor's possession. Thus, the lam is not for immediate ownership. The author further argues that the eight categories are not entitled (mustahiqq) but rather needy (muhtaj), and need is the cause (illah) for giving. He analogizes them to the Kaaba: the Kaaba does not deserve prayer but is suitable as a direction. Similarly, each category is suitable as a recipient due to need. Therefore, giving zakat to one category or even one individual suffices, and this does not contradict the text because the text's mention is for need, not ownership.

Key Terms
لام العاقبة (lām al-ʿāqibah): The preposition indicating the final consequence or result, not immediate ownership.
لام التمليك (lām al-tamlik): The preposition indicating ownership or transfer of ownership.
مصارف (maṣārif): The categories or recipients of zakat as mentioned in the verse.

Definition of Ahliyyah: Capacity of Obligation and Performance
Definition and classification of capacity into two types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ahliyyat al-wujub and ahliyyat al-ada, identify their basis (dhimma and reason), and apply the distinction to determine who is obligated for acts of worship.

Core Idea
Legal capacity (ahliyyah) is two types: capacity for obligations (wujub) and capacity for performance (ada), each with distinct conditions and proofs.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational concept of legal capacity (ahliyyah) in Hanafi usul. The text defines two types: ahliyyat al-wujub (capacity to have rights and obligations) and ahliyyat al-ada (capacity to perform actions validly). Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining who is subject to divine commands (taklif) and how obligations apply across different stages of life and belief.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Term and its Branches
Locate the term 'ahliyyah' in the text and note that the chapter explicitly divides it into two types: ahliyyat al-wujub and ahliyyat al-ada. Write down the Arabic names.
The text begins with 'الأهلية نوعان'.
2
Understand the Basis of Ahliyyat al-Wujub
The text states that ahliyyat al-wujub is based on the establishment of dhimma (legal personality). The dhimma is present from birth for every human by the primordial covenant (al-'ahd al-madi). Identify the proof: Quran 7:172 and consensus of jurists.
Dhimma is the foundation; it is not dependent on reason or maturity.
3
Analyze the Proof: Quran and Ijma
The author cites the verse of the covenant (ميثاق) as evidence for dhimma. Explain how this verse shows that every human has a capacity for obligation from the beginning. Then note the consensus (ijma) of the fuqaha' on this point.
The dalil is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalala according to the consensus.
4
Apply to Special Cases: Fetus, Child, Disbeliever
Work through the scenarios: (1) Fetus before birth – has capacity for rights (wujub lahu) but not obligations (wujub 'alayhi) because dhimma is not absolute. (2) Child after birth – has full ahliyyat al-wujub but lacks ahliyyat al-ada until reason. (3) Disbeliever – obligated for faith but not for acts of obedience due to lack of eligibility for reward.
The text uses these examples to illustrate the two capacities.
5
Distinguish the Two Capacities
Summarize: ahliyyat al-wujub is universal from birth, based on dhimma, and covers rights and obligations. Ahliyyat al-ada is conditional on reason and maturity, and is necessary for valid performance and accountability. Note that obligation (wujub) itself is not an end; if its purpose (reward) is absent, the obligation may cease.
This distinction is central to many Hanafi rulings on prayer, zakat, etc.

Chapter on the explanation of capacity. Capacity is of two types: capacity of obligation (ahliyyat al-wujub) and capacity of performance (ahliyyat al-ada). As for capacity of obligation, it is based on the establishment of dhimma (legal personality). For a human is born with a dhimma capable of obligation for him and upon him, by consensus of the jurists, based on the previous covenant (the Primordial Covenant). Allah the Exalted said: 'And when your Lord took from the Children of Adam from their loins their descendants...' (to the end of the verse). Before separation (from the mother), he is a part of another, so he does not have an absolute dhimma, until it becomes such that a right is obligatory for him but not upon him. When he separates and an absolute dhimma appears for him, he becomes eligible for obligation for him and upon him. However, obligation is not intended for itself, so it is possible that it becomes void due to the absence of its ruling and purpose, just as it ceases to exist due to the absence of its object. For this reason, nothing of the laws that are acts of obedience is obligatory upon the disbeliever, because he is not eligible for the reward of the hereafter. But faith becomes incumbent upon him because he is eligible for its performance and for the establishment of its ruling. Faith is not obligatory upon a child before he attains reason, due to the absence of capacity of performance. When he attains reason and becomes capable of performance, we hold that the origin of faith is obligatory upon him, not its performance, such that its performance is valid without taklif, and it becomes an obligation (fard), like a traveler performing Jumu'ah.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional text explaining the types of legal capacity (ahliyyah). It begins with the matn: 'A chapter on the explanation of capacity. Capacity is of two types: capacity of obligation (ahliyyat al-wujub) and capacity of performance (ahliyyat al-ada).' The sharh then explains that ahliyyat al-wujub is based on the establishment of dhimma (legal personality). The proof is the consensus of jurists and the Quranic verse of the primordial covenant (Surah al-A'raf 7:172). Before a child separates from the mother, he is a part of her and does not have an absolute dhimma; rights can be established for him but not upon him. After birth, he has an absolute dhimma and becomes eligible for obligations for and upon him. However, obligation is not an end in itself; if its purpose (reward) cannot be achieved, the obligation may cease. Thus, a disbeliever is not obligated to perform acts of obedience (like prayer) because he lacks eligibility for the reward of the hereafter. But faith is obligatory upon him because he is eligible for its performance and for the establishment of its ruling. A child before attaining reason is not obligated to have faith because he lacks capacity of performance. When he attains reason, the origin of faith (i'tiqad) becomes obligatory, but its performance is valid without a formal command (taklif), similar to a traveler performing Jumu'ah (which becomes fard but without full conditions). The Urdu commentary elaborates these points.

Key Terms
أهلية الوجوب (ahliyyat al-wujub): Capacity to have rights and obligations established for and upon a person; present from birth based on dhimma.
أهلية الأداء (ahliyyat al-ada): Capacity to perform actions validly; develops with reason and maturity.
الذمة (al-dhimma): Legal personality or capacity that is the basis for obligation; present from birth by the primordial covenant.

Types of Ignorance (Jahl) and Their Legal Effect
Defining four types of ignorance and their rulings in usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four types of ignorance in Hanafi usul and identify the legal effect of each, applying the criteria of valid ijtihad, doubt (shubhah), and excuse (udhr).

Core Idea
Not all ignorance excuses; only ignorance in matters of ijtihad or genuine obscurity of evidence prevents punishment.

In Hanafi legal theory, ignorance (jahl) is not a monolithic concept. The scholars classified it into four categories, each with distinct legal consequences. Understanding these categories is essential for determining when a person’s failure to know the law can mitigate liability, especially in ibadat and muamalat. This passage from a Hanafi usul text defines the second, third, and fourth types, providing critical examples and addressing inter-madhhab disputes.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the types of ignorance (jahl) in usul al-fiqh, specifically addressing the second, third, and fourth types. The author begins by discussing the second type: the ignorance of a mujtahid who opposes clear textual evidence from the Quran or well-known hadith, or who gives preference to a rare (gharib) hadith over a well-known (mashhur) one. Such ignorance is considered 'rejected' (mardud) and has no legal effect. The author provides several examples: Dawud al-Isfahani's fatwa permitting the sale of umm walad (a slave-mother) is rejected because it contradicts the well-known hadith that she becomes free upon her master's death. Similarly, Imam al-Shafi'i's position on deliberately omitting basmalah when slaughtering is rejected because it contradicts the clear Quranic verse, and his analogy (qiyas) to forgetfulness is invalid. The examples of qasamah (oaths in murder cases) and the ruling on one witness plus an oath are also discussed, where Hanafis argue that the Shafi'i positions contradict well-known sunnah and Quran. The author then clarifies that such harsh criticism of the Imams is merely a narration from earlier scholars, not an expression of disrespect. The third type of ignorance is that which qualifies as a doubt (shubhah). This occurs in matters open to valid ijtihad or in ambiguous situations. For instance, if a person undergoing cupping breaks his fast mistakenly thinking that cupping invalidates the fast, no expiation (kaffarah) is required because it is a genuine error in a matter of ijtihad. Similarly, if someone commits zina with his father's slave-girl mistakenly believing her to be lawful for him, the hadd punishment is not applied because of the doubt. The fourth type is ignorance that serves as a complete excuse (udhr). This applies to a person who accepts Islam in dar al-harb (enemy territory); his previous actions are not punishable because he was not bound by the law due to the obscurity of evidence. Other examples include ignorance of an agent (wakil) or debtor (madun) regarding the scope of their authority or debt, the pre-emptor (shafi') unaware of the sale, the master unaware of his slave's crime, a virgin unaware that her marriage contract is invalid, and a slave-girl unaware of her option upon emancipation. However, ignorance of the option of puberty (khiyar al-bulugh) is not an excuse, as this is well-known. The commentary follows the Hanafi school, emphasizing that only certain types of ignorance mitigate legal consequences.

Key Terms
الجهل (al-jahl): Lack of knowledge that can affect legal rulings; categorized into types that excuse or do not excuse.
شبهة (shubhah): A doubt that arises from valid ijtihad or ambiguous evidence, sufficient to avert prescribed punishments (hudud) and expiations.
عذر (‘udhr): An excuse that removes liability due to genuine inability to know the ruling, e.g., new Muslim in dar al-harb.

Types of Intoxication and Their Effect on Legal Capacity
Definition of intoxication types and their legal effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of intoxication in Hanafi usul and apply the ruling on legal capacity for each type, including the istihsan exceptions.

Core Idea
Intoxication from a forbidden source does not remove legal capacity; intoxication from a permissible source does.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, legal capacity (ahlīyah) can be affected by temporary obstacles (awariḍ). One such obstacle is intoxication (sukr), which scholars classify into two categories based on the means by which it occurs. This classification has significant consequences for the validity of the intoxicated person's actions—such as divorce, sales, and acknowledgments. In this lesson, we examine the passage that defines these types and derives the rulings from the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of intoxication
From the passage, list the two categories: (1) intoxication from a permissible means (e.g., medicine, coerced/compelled drinking) and (2) intoxication from a forbidden means (e.g., voluntary wine consumption). Note that the first is treated like fainting (ighma').
The means determines the legal effect.
2
Determine the legal effect on legal capacity
For permissible-source intoxication, legal capacity is nullified (like fainting); transactions are invalid. For forbidden-source intoxication, legal capacity remains; transactions are valid except for two exceptions.
3
Examine the Quranic evidence
The verse (4:43) prohibits approaching prayer while intoxicated. The fact that Allah addresses the intoxicated proves they are still subject to divine command (mukallaf), hence their legal capacity is intact.
The address itself proves capacity.
4
Note the istihsan exceptions
Two actions are excepted by istihsan: (a) apostasy (riddah) is not effective from an intoxicated person, and (b) acknowledgment of hudud penalties (purely for Allah) is not accepted. The rationale: an intoxicated person cannot be consistent; his statements are treated as retractions.
Exceptions apply only where retraction can be presumed.

Foolishness (Safah) Does Not Affect Legal Capacity
Ruling that safah does not impair legal capacity or necessitate hijr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that safah does not impair legal capacity, and identify the specific exception of the squanderer's interdiction as textually established.

Core Idea
Safah does not weaken capacity; the hijr of a squanderer is a nass-based exception, not a general rule.

This usul passage clarifies that foolishness (safah) does not affect a person's legal capacity (ahliyya) for performing valid acts. Imam Abu Hanifa holds that no interdiction (hijr) is necessitated by safah alone. However, a specific prohibition on giving property to a foolish spendthrift at puberty is established by Quranic text—this is an exceptional ruling, not an analogizable one. Understanding this distinction is key to applying the Hanafi usul framework.

This passage discusses the legal effect of safah (foolishness, imprudence) on a person's legal capacity. Attribution of Murder Under Coercion
Explaining the principle of act attribution in coerced murder

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of act attribution under coercion to distinguish between moral culpability and physical liability, and derive the ruling for qisas/diya using the dalil of Quran 6:164.

Core Idea
In coerced murder, sin stays with the coerced, but the loss of life is attributed to the coercer—sin cannot be transferred, but physical effects can.

This lesson expands on a key Hanafi usul principle: when the locus (mahall) of a prohibited act changes due to coercion, the act's attribution is split between moral and physical consequences. We analyze the case of murder under coercion to see how the Hanafi school distinguishes between the sin (which remains with the coerced) and the loss of life (which is attributed to the coercer, making him liable for qisas/diya). The proof is drawn from Quran 6:164 and the rational impossibility of transferring sin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
First, extract the principle stated at the beginning: when the locus of a crime changes due to coercion, the act remains attributed to the coerced doer and does not transfer to the coercer.
The principle is about change of mahall.
2
Apply to Murder Under Coercion
Consider the scenario: someone is forced to kill. The author separates two aspects: the sin of murder and the physical loss of life (mahall). Note each aspect's attribution.
3
Examine the Proof
The proof for sin not transferring is Quran 6:164: 'No bearer of burdens bears another's burden.' Also, a rational argument: one cannot become an instrument (aala) for another's sin because sin requires intention and is personal.
The verse is general and applies to all sins.
4
Derive the Rulings
From the principle: (a) sin remains on the coerced killer; (b) loss of life is attributed to the coercer, making him liable for qisas or diya; (c) if the coercer is an heir, he is disinherited.
The coercer pays the penalty for the mahall, not the act.
5

Consider an opposing view that would place all responsibility on the coercer. The Hanafi response: sin requires intention which the coerced retains; the loss of life is a physical effect that can be caused by another (the coercer).
This distinction is unique to Hanafi usul.

Consequently, due to the loss of the locus, retaliation (qisas) and blood money (diya) are obligatory upon the coercer. If, in the matter of sin, the coerced is made an instrument of the coercer, then this would mean that the locus of the crime is the coercer, not the coerced, which would be a change of the locus, which is impossible and false.
