Nathr and Yamin: Separate by Wording and Effect
Explaining how a vow and an oath coexist without combining literal and metaphorical meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the legal effects arising from the wording (sighah) and the ruling (hukm) of a statement, and apply this distinction to explain how a single utterance can constitute both a nathr (vow) and a yamin (oath) without combining haqiqah and majaz.

Core Idea
Nathr comes from wording; yamin comes from effect: a single utterance can produce two rulings without combining haqiqah and majaz.

This passage addresses a subtle objection in Hanafi usul: can a single statement be both a vow and an oath without violating the rule against combining literal and metaphorical meanings? The author clarifies that the two rulings arise from different aspects—the wording gives the vow, while the legal effect (making a permissible act obligatory) gives the oath. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping how multiple legal consequences can stem from one expression.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector claims that the phrase 'Lillahi alayya an asuma rajaba' (I owe Allah to fast Rajab) cannot be both a nathr and a yamin because that would require combining literal (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz) meanings in one word, which is prohibited.
This is a typical critique against apparent dual rulings from a single utterance.
2
Distinguish wording (sighah) from ruling (hukm)
The author responds that nathr is derived from the wording (the literal meaning), while yamin is derived from the ruling or effect of the statement (ijab al-mubah). These are two different dimensions, not two meanings of the same word.
The key is the legal effect: making a mubah obligatory is considered a yamin by shariah.
3
Understand the legal effect (ijab al-mubah) as yamin
In Hanafi law, making a permissible act obligatory upon oneself (ijab al-mubah) constitutes a yamin, just as making a permissible act forbidden (tahrim al-mubah) does. This is supported by the Quranic verse: 'Qad farada Allahu lakum tahlilata aymanikum' (Allah has ordained for you the dissolution of your oaths).
The verse implies that such acts are oaths because they require expiation if broken.
4
Apply the analogy of purchasing a close relative
The author gives an analogy: buying a close mahram relative. By the wording of the purchase contract, one acquires ownership (tamalluk). But by the ruling of that contract (owning a mahram relative triggers emancipation), the relative is freed (tahrir). Similarly, the nathr wording gives the vow, and its ruling (ijab) gives the oath.
This analogy illustrates how a single act can have two different legal consequences from different aspects.

Commentary
This passage addresses a second objection: that the phrase 'Lillahi alayya an asuma rajaba' (I vow to fast Rajab) combines both a vow (nathr) and an oath (yamin) in one wording, which would supposedly require combining literal and metaphorical meanings. The commentator explains that this is not the case. The literal meaning of the statement, derived from its wording (sighah), is a vow (nathr). The oath (yamin) is derived not from a separate metaphorical meaning of the words but from the ruling (hukm) or effect of the statement: making a permissible act (mubah) obligatory upon oneself. In Islamic law, making a mubah obligatory (ijab al-mubah) functions as a yamin, similar to making a mubah forbidden (tahrim al-mubah), which is also considered a yamin. The verse 'Qad farada Allahu lakum tahlilata aymanikum' (Surah at-Tahrim: 2) is cited as evidence that such acts constitute oaths. Thus, nathr and yamin coexist without combining haqiqah and majaz in one word. The commentator then provides an analogy: purchasing a close mahram relative. By the wording of the contract, one acquires ownership (tamalluk), but by the ruling of that contract (because owning a mahram relative triggers emancipation), the relative is freed (tahrir). Similarly, the nathr wording gives the vow, and its ruling gives the oath.

Key Terms
نذر (nadh r): A vow by which a person obligates an act of worship upon themselves, generally a mubah act.
یمین (yamīn): An oath by which a person confirms or prohibits something, often requiring expiation if broken.
ایجاب (ījāb): Making something obligatory; in this context, the act of obligating a mubah act upon oneself is considered a yamin.
تحریم المباح (taḥrīm al-mubāḥ): Making a permissible thing forbidden; also constitutes a yamin.

Preference of Ishaarat al-Nass over Dalalat al-Nass at Ta'arud
Explaining the rule of preference when dalaalat and ishaarat conflict

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that ishaarat al-nass is preferred over dalaalat al-nass when they conflict, by analyzing the example of kaffara for intentional murder.

Core Idea
When two definitive textual indications conflict, the implication from the text (ishara) takes precedence over the inferred meaning (dalala).

In this lesson, we examine a key Hanafi usul principle: the preference of ishaarat al-nass over dalaalat al-nass at ta'arud. Through the classic case of kaffara for qatl 'amd, you will see how the Hanafi school derives its position and why it differs from the Shafi'i school. The passage provides the rule, the example, and an important clarification regarding qisas and diyah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting proofs
Locate the two Quranic verses: 4:92 (accidental killing) and 4:93 (intentional killing). Note the types of indication each provides.
4:92 gives 'ibaraat for kaffara and dalaalat for intentional killing via qiyas auli. 4:93 gives ishaarat al-nass through the word 'jaza'.
2
Determine the type of indication
Classify the indication from each verse: dalaalat al-nass from 4:92 (inferred by a fortiori reasoning) and ishaarat al-nass from 4:93 (implication of the word 'jaza').
Both are considered qati' (definitive) but of different kinds.
3
Apply the rule of preference
When dalaalat and ishaarat conflict, the Hanafi rule gives precedence to ishaarat al-nass. Apply this to the case.
The rule is specific to ta'arud between these two types.
4
Derive the ruling
Conclude that no kaffara is required for intentional murder, as the ishaarat indicates Hell is sufficient recompense.
This is the Hanafi position, contrary to the Shafi'i.
5
Address the objection
Understand that qisas and diyah are not recompense for the act (which is Hell) but for the victim's rights. They are separate and do not contradict the ishaarat.
This clarifies why Hanafis still require qisas despite Hell being the complete recompense for the act.

Commentary
This passage outlines the rule for handling contradiction (ta'arud) between two types of definitive textual indication: dalaalat al-nass and ishaarat al-nass. Both are qati (definitive) in principle, but when they conflict, ishaarat al-nass is given priority. The author illustrates with the case of intentional murder (qatl 'amd). The verse on accidental killing (4:92) establishes kaffara for that case by 'ibaraat, and by dalaalat al-nass (qiyas auli, a fortiori) it implies kaffara for intentional murder as well. The Shafi'i school adopts this view. However, another verse (4:93) states that the recompense for intentional murder is Hell, and by ishaarat al-nass (the implication of the word 'jaza' meaning sufficient recompense), no additional kaffara is needed. Since the two indications conflict, the Hanafi rule gives preference to ishaarat al-nass. The author then anticipates an objection: if Hell is the complete recompense, why do Hanafis still require qisas or diyah? The answer distinguishes between the recompense for the act (which is Hell) and the recompense for the victim's rights (which is qisas/diyah owed to the heirs). These are separate, so no contradiction.

Key Terms
اشارت النص (ishārat al-nass): Implication from the context or wording of the text that indicates a ruling, here specifically from the word 'jaza' meaning 'sufficient recompense'.
تعارض (ta‘āruḍ): Contradiction between two equally strong evidences that cannot be reconciled.
جزاء (jazā’): Recompense; the word's implication here is that it is sufficient and complete, excluding additional penalties.

The Fourth Type of Rukhsah: Complete Removal with General Legislation
Classification and examples of the fourth type of rukhsah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the fourth type of rukhsah and identify its application in the three examples provided in this passage.

Core Idea
The fourth rukhsah removes a ruling in specific cases while the original ruling remains generally legislated.

In Hanafi usul, concessions (rukhsah) are classified into types. This lesson explores a unique type: a ruling is completely removed in specific circumstances, yet the original ruling remains generally valid. Examples from sales, dietary law, and purification illustrate this concept.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contrast
Distinguish the two most complete types of metaphorical majaz concessions (where the original ruling is completely abrogated) from the fourth type. The fourth type is distinct because the original ruling remains generally legislated.
The metaphorical concessions are called 'majaz' because they are only metaphorically concessions; the fourth type is a true rukhsah.
2
Understand the defining characteristic
Grasp that the fourth type involves a ruling that is 'removed from the servants while it remains legislated in general' (masqut 'an al-ibad ma'a kawnihi mashru'an fi al-jumlah). The exemption is specific, not a general abrogation.
This is key: the general rule stays, but a specific scenario is excepted.
3
Analyze the first example: salam sale
In a regular sale, specifying the sold item is required. In salam (forward sale), this specification is dropped; if the item is specified, the contract becomes invalid. This is a concession because the general rule requires specification, but salam is an exception.
The 'ayniyyah (specificity) requirement is completely removed in salam.
4
Analyze the second example: wine and carrion
Wine and carrion are normally prohibited. For one who is coerced (mukrah) or compelled by necessity (mudtarr), their prohibition is completely removed. They may consume them and cannot be required to be patient.
The removal is absolute within the exemption; there is no partial restriction.
5
Analyze the third example: washing the foot
In ablution, washing the foot is obligatory. During the period of wiping over khuffs (leather socks), this washing obligation is completely removed and replaced by wiping. The original ruling (washing) remains generally legislated for those not wiping.
This illustrates how the fourth type works: the general obligation stands, but a specific case is exempted.

Commentary
This passage is a teacher's explanation of the fourth type of rukhsah within the Hanafi classification. The author first distinguishes two types of majaz (metaphorical) concessions: one is the complete removal of hardship (asr) and shackles (aghlal) from the community, which is only metaphorically called a concession because the original ruling is completely abrogated (naskh) and no longer remains legislated. The fourth type, by contrast, is a removal from the servants while the original ruling remains legislated in general (mashru' fil-jumla). The author provides three examples: (1) In salam sale (forward contract), the requirement for the sold item to be specific ('ayniyyah) is entirely dropped; if it is specified, the contract becomes invalid. This is a concession because the general rule of sales requires specification. (2) Wine and carrion: their prohibition is completely removed for one who is coerced (mukrah) or compelled by necessity (mudtarr), due to the exception (istithna'), such that they are not even required to be patient (they may consume). (3) The foot: washing it during ablution is removed during the period of wiping (mash 'ala al-khuffain) because impurity does not transmit to it. The passage ends abruptly but describes the fourth type as a 'removal from the servants' while the original ruling remains legislated in general, contrasting with the first type of metaphorical concession which is pure abrogation.

Key Terms
مجاز (majaz): Metaphorical concession; a ruling that is called a concession only in a metaphorical sense because the original ruling has been abrogated completely.
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation; the complete removal of a legal ruling by a later revealed text. In this passage, naskh is associated with the metaphorical concession where the original ruling is no longer legislated.
رخصة (rukhsah): A concession or dispensation; a ruling that permits something that would normally be prohibited or exempts from an obligation due to a valid excuse.

Metaphorical Concession: Complete and Fourth Types
Definition and exemplification of two types of metaphorical rukhsah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define metaphorical rukhsah and distinguish its two types (atamm and fourth) using Hanafi usul terminology.

Core Idea
Metaphorical rukhsah is abrogation or suspension, not a true concession with coexisting 'azimah.

In Hanafi usul, not every permission is a true concession (rukhsah). Some permissions are actually abrogations (naskh) or exceptions that remove the original ruling entirely or in specific contexts. This lesson unpacks the definition of metaphorical rukhsah, its two types, and their legal implications, using examples from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of rukhsah majaziyyah
From the text, a metaphorical rukhsah is a removal (isqat) of a ruling that is either completely abrogated (first type) or suspended in a specific context while remaining prescribed in general (fourth type).
The key is that there is no underlying 'azimah that remains optional.
2
Distinguish the 'atamm' (complete) type
The first type (atamm) refers to harsh laws of previous nations that have been completely abrogated for this Ummah. Examples: cutting off limbs for a mistake, killing the repentant, etc. These are called rukhsah only metaphorically because the original obligation is entirely absent; acting on them would be sinful.
This is essentially naskh (abrogation) resulting in ease.
3
Distinguish the fourth type
The fourth type involves rulings that are generally prescribed but are lifted in specific circumstances. Examples: the requirement to specify the object in a sale (removed in salam contracts), prohibition of wine and carrion (removed for coerced/compelled), and washing of feet (removed during wiping). Here, 'azimah exists in other situations, so the concession is metaphorical but not complete.
The ruling remains prescribed in other contexts.
4
Analyze the examples provided
For the first type: abrogated laws like needing to pray only in mosques. For the fourth: salam contract (specification waived), wine/carrion (permitted for the coerced), wiping over feet (washing replaced). In each, the original rule is either wholly gone or suspended only in that specific case.
The examples clarify the difference.
5
Apply the definition to new scenarios
Given a legal situation, determine if a concession is real (has a coexisting 'azimah) or metaphorical (no 'azimah). For instance, the permission to shorten prayers while traveling is a real rukhsah because one could still pray full. The abrogation of the law of stoning for adultery (if it were) would be metaphorical if replaced.
This tests understanding of the criterion.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition on the two types of metaphorical concession (rukhsah majaziyyah) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author begins by discussing the preference of 'azimah (strict adherence) over rukhsah (concession) when both are possible, using the example of fasting while traveling. However, if there is a genuine risk to life, rukhsah becomes obligatory. He then transitions to the definition of metaphorical concession, quoting the matn. The first type (atamm) refers to rulings that were completely abrogated for this Ummah, such as the harsh laws of previous nations (e.g., cutting off limbs for a mistake, killing the repentant, cutting off impurity, praying only in mosques, etc.). These are called rukhsah metaphorically because the original obligation is entirely absent; in fact, acting upon them would be sinful. Thus, it is a complete abrogation (naskh) in the guise of alleviation. The fourth type involves rulings that are generally prescribed but are suspended in specific circumstances. Examples include: the requirement of specifying the object in a sale—required generally but waived in salam contracts; the prohibition of wine and carrion—removed for the coerced and compelled; and the washing of feet—replaced by wiping during the period of wiping (masah). In these cases, the original obligation remains in other contexts, so the concession is metaphorical but not complete, because 'azimah is not entirely absent.

رخصة (rukhsah): A concession that allows deviation from the original strict ruling due to necessity or ease
عزیمت ('azimah): The original strict ruling that applies normally without concession
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a ruling; in this context, it refers to complete lifting of a ruling
رخصة مجازية (rukhsah majaziyyah): A metaphorical concession where the original ruling is either completely abrogated (first type) or suspended in specific contexts (fourth type), without a coexisting 'azimah

Argues mursal is stronger than musnad ahad but its superiority is by ijtihad, not for naskh

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Mursal is preferred over musnad ahad, but its strength is ijtihadi, so it cannot abrogate the Qur'an.

This lesson unpacks a key Hanafi usul rule: when a mursal and a musnad ahad conflict, the mursal is given preference. The reasoning lies in the narrator's confidence. However, an objection arises: if mursal is stronger, could it abrogate the Qur'an like khabar mashhūr? The Hanafi response draws a crucial distinction between strength based on ijtihad and strength based on naṣṣ, preserving the sanctity of the Qur'an.

Recognize that the passage establishes a rule: mursal is preferred over musnad ahad when they conflict. Note that 'musnad' here means musnad wāḥid (āḥād), not mashhūr or mutawātir.
The rule is specific to āḥād narrations.

Examine the proof: the mursil (narrator) drops the chain because he is fully confident that the report is a hadith; the musnad narrator preserves the chain due to lesser confidence. Thus, mursal carries more certainty in the narrator's mind.
This is a rational proof (qiyās or istiḥsān) based on observed behavior.

Ziyada upon the Quran by Khabar Mashhur: Rule and Epistemology
Rule explanation with examples and epistemological clarification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that khabar mashhur can add a condition to a Quranic ruling, and evaluate its knowledge level as tranquility, not certainty.

Core Idea
Khabar mashhur can add to the Quran, but only provides tranquility, not certainty.

This passage presents a crucial Hanafi usul principle: a well-known report (khabar mashhur) can supplement a Quranic ruling by adding a new condition or penalty, even though the report itself does not provide absolute certainty. The author illustrates this with three examples—stoning of the muhsan, wiping over socks, and consecutive fasts for oath expiation—and then addresses a common confusion: if khabar mashhur can add to the Quran, why does it not give certain knowledge like mutawatir? The answer lies in its origin as ahad, which introduces the possibility of doubt.

Guided Path
1
State the rule of ziyada
Identify the Hanafi position: khabar mashhur can add an additional ruling (ziyada) to a Quranic text, even if it does not abrogate the original. The addition becomes part of the operative law.
This is distinct from naskh; ziyada is a supplement, not an abrogation.
2
Analyze the three examples
Examine each example: (1) stoning for muhsan is added to the flogging verse via khabar mashhur; (2) wiping over socks restricts the general command to wash feet; (3) Ibn Mas'ud's reading adds 'consecutive' to the oath expiation verse. Note how the third example is a clear case of ziyada from a mashhur reading.
The author cautions that the first two examples are actually mutawatir in meaning, so citing them under khabar mashhur is debatable.
3
Identify the epistemological distinction
Understand the key difference: khabar mutawatir provides certain knowledge (ilm yaqeeni) because it is impossible to forge. Khabar mashhur, originally ahad, provides only tranquility (ilm tumaniyyat) because there remains a theoretical possibility of error.
This distinction is vital for understanding the binding force of different types of reports.
4
Address the misconception
Recognize the imagined objection: if khabar mashhur can add to the Quran, it should give certainty. The author's response: because it starts as ahad, doubt is inherent, so it does not reach yaqeen. This does not prevent its use in law, as tranquility suffices for action.
The Hanafi school relies on khabar mashhur for many rulings, but always with this epistemological caveat.

When a Narrator's Contradiction Makes a Hadith Inoperative
States the rule and the dispute between Abu Yusuf and Muhammad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a narrator's contradiction indicates naskh and evaluate the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on when the source denies the hadith.

Core Idea
When a narrator contradicts his own hadith, it is considered abrogated – but if the source denies it, the Hanafis split.

This passage from Al-Husami presents a key Hanafi principle in evaluating hadiths: a narrator's subsequent contradictory word or deed can render his own narration inoperative. The underlying reasoning is that if the hadith was clear and well-known, the contradiction can only be explained by abrogation (naskh). However, the Hanafi school itself divides on a closely related question: what if the person from whom the hadith is narrated (the source, e.g., a Companion) denies it? Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad take opposing positions, grounded in their disagreement on a parallel case involving witnesses against a judge. This lesson unpacks the rule, the dispute, and the legal analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule and Its Condition
State the core rule: action upon a hadith is dropped if the narrator contradicts it after narrating, by word or deed. Note the condition: the hadith is manifest (zahir) and cannot be hidden from such authorities.
The rule applies only when the hadith is clear and the narrator is a reliable authority (e.g., a Companion).
2
Understand the Epistemological Basis
The contradiction cannot be attributed to forgetfulness because the hadith is manifest. The only plausible explanation is that the ruling was abrogated (naskh). Thus, the hadith is considered abrogated and no longer actionable.
This reflects the Hanafi principle that narrators' actions can serve as evidence of naskh.
3
Explore the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
When the source (al-marwi 'anhu) denies the hadith, two views exist: Abu Yusuf says the hadith becomes inoperative; Muhammad says it remains accepted. The author sides with Abu Yusuf, calling it 'al-ashbah' (more appropriate).
The dispute is rooted in their different approaches to weighing conflicting evidence.
4
Analyze the Qiyas (Analogy) to the Judge Case
The dispute parallels their disagreement on two witnesses testifying against a judge who does not remember the case. Abu Yusuf rejects the testimony (like the source's denial outweighs the transmission); Muhammad accepts it (like the transmission is reliable despite the denial).
The qiyas shows that the source's denial is treated similarly to a judge's lack of memory.
5
Apply the Tarjih (Preference)
The author concludes that al-ashbah (the more similar view) is that action drops, i.e., the hadith is inoperative. This tarjih is based on the strength of the source's denial when the hadith is clear.
In practice, a Hanafi mufti would follow Abu Yusuf's view unless there is a reason to prefer Muhammad's.

Commentary
This passage states the Hanafi rule: if a narrator (or a Companion) later contradicts his own hadith by word or deed, and the hadith was clear enough that it could not have been unknown to them, then the hadith is considered abrogated (naskh) and is no longer actionable. The explanation is that the narrator's contrary action indicates the hadith was either abrogated or erroneously transmitted. The Urdu commentary explains that the narrator's contradictory action is a sign that the hadith is not reliable. The passage then mentions a dispute: if the person from whom the hadith is reported (the source, e.g., the Companion) denies it, what then? One view (attributed to Abu Yusuf) says the hadith becomes inoperative, which the author considers the stronger view (الاشبه). The other view (Imam Muhammad) says it is still accepted. This dispute is analogous (far') to their disagreement on two witnesses testifying against a judge about a case the judge does not recall: Abu Yusuf rejects their testimony (because the judge's memory is a counter-evidence), Muhammad accepts it (because the witnesses' memory is more reliable regarding the specific event). The analogy shows the principle: when the source denies, his denial may outweigh the transmission, especially if he is an imam known for integrity.

The Hanafi Rule for Resolving Ta'arud (Apparent Conflict) Between Evidences
Explains the rule for resolving apparent conflicts between scriptural evidences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi hierarchy for resolving apparent conflicts between scriptural evidences: starting with naskh, then Sunnah (for conflicting verses), then Qiyas then sayings of Companions (for conflicting hadiths), and define ta'arud and distinguish it from tanaqud.

Core Idea
When two proofs appear to conflict, they both drop; we move to the next evidence in the hierarchy.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi usul al-fiqh rule for handling apparent contradictions (ta'arud) between legal proofs. You will learn why such conflicts are only apparent, the hierarchy for resolving them, and the key distinction between ta'arud and tanaqud. The passage also provides a legal definition of ta'arud and explains the conditions for its occurrence.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the rules for resolving apparent conflicts between Islamic legal evidences. It begins by stating that conflicts between proofs do not occur in reality in the Quran and Sunnah; they only appear due to human ignorance of which text is abrogating (nasikh) and which is abrogated (mansukh). The text then lays out a hierarchy for when conflict seems to arise: if two verses conflict and we do not know which is abrogating, we turn to the Sunnah (hadith). If two hadiths conflict, we turn to Qiyas (analogy) and then to the sayings of the Companions (aqwal al-sahaba) in that order. The reason is that if two proofs truly conflict and cannot be reconciled, both are dropped, and we move to the next source in the hierarchy. An example is given: the case of water from a wild donkey (sawr al-himar) where, after conflicting proofs and no valid Qiyas, the water is considered pure in origin but its use in wudu is doubtful, so tayammum is added to wudu. The passage then defines ta'arud (conflict) as the confrontation of two equal proofs that cannot be combined, distinguished from tanaqud (contradiction) where the proof exists but its implication does not. It also notes the requirement of eight unities for conflict. The author addresses a potential objection: why do we see conflicts in revelation? The response is that it is due to ignorance of nasikh and mansukh. An example of verses on recitation in prayer is given: one verse seems to obligate recitation for both imam and follower, another commands silence. Since the dates are unknown, both drop and we use hadith to resolve that the follower should not recite. The passage ends by noting a disagreement about the order between Qiyas and sayings of Companions: the Hanafi position is Qiyas first, then sayings; some others reverse this order, while a third group distinguishes matters subject to Qiyas from those not subject.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the rules for resolving apparent conflicts between Islamic legal evidences. It begins by stating that conflicts between proofs do not occur in reality in the Quran and Sunnah; they only appear due to human ignorance of which text is abrogating (nasikh) and which is abrogated (mansukh). The text then lays out a hierarchy for when conflict seems to arise: if two verses conflict and we do not know which is abrogating, we turn to the Sunnah (hadith). If two hadiths conflict, we turn to Qiyas (analogy) and then to the sayings of the Companions (aqwal al-sahaba) in that order. The reason is that if two proofs truly conflict and cannot be reconciled, both are dropped, and we move to the next source in the hierarchy. An example is given: the case of water from a wild donkey (sawr al-himar) where, after conflicting proofs and no valid Qiyas, the water is considered pure in origin but its use in wudu is doubtful, so tayammum is added to wudu. The passage then defines ta'arud (conflict) as the confrontation of two equal proofs that cannot be combined, distinguished from tanaqud (contradiction) where the proof exists but its implication does not. It also notes the requirement of eight unities for conflict. The author addresses a potential objection: why do we see conflicts in revelation? The response is that it is due to ignorance of nasikh and mansukh. An example of verses on recitation in prayer is given: one verse seems to obligate recitation for both imam and follower, another commands silence. Since the dates are unknown, both drop and we use hadith to resolve that the follower should not recite. The passage ends by noting a disagreement about the order between Qiyas and sayings of Companions: the Hanafi position is Qiyas first, then sayings; some others reverse this order, while a third group distinguishes matters subject to Qiyas from those not subject.

The Inviolability of Revelation: Resolving Apparent Contradictions through Naskh
Discusses the impossibility of real contradiction in revelation and the role of naskh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that no real contradiction exists in the Quran and Sunnah, and explain how apparent contradictions are resolved by identifying naskh (abrogation).

Core Idea
Real contradiction cannot exist in divine revelation; apparent conflicts are due to ignorance of naskh.

This lesson establishes the foundational belief that the Quran and Sunnah are free from real contradiction. Any perceived conflict is a result of our limited knowledge of which ruling came later and abrogated the earlier one. Understanding this principle is essential before applying the hierarchy of evidences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim
The author states: real contradiction (ta'āruḍ ḥaqīqī) never occurs in the Quran or Hadith. Recognize this as a core Hanafi position.
Contradiction would imply deficiency; Allah's speech is perfect.
2
Understand the Reason
Contradiction is a sign of weakness. Allah is free from all imperfections, so His revelation cannot be contradictory.
This is a theological (kalām) basis for the usul rule.
3
Identify the Cause of Apparent Contradiction
Apparent contradictions arise from ignorance of naskh (abrogation). One verse may grant a ruling, and a later verse may change it.
The later ruling supersedes the earlier; no real contradiction exists.
4
Apply the First Step in Resolution
When two evidences seem to conflict, the first step is to check for naskh by determining the chronological order.
If the abrogating and abrogated are known, the later ruling is followed.
5
Note the Consequence
If naskh cannot be determined, do not assume a real contradiction; instead, move to the next level of the hierarchy (as covered in the next lesson).
The hierarchy begins only after failure to resolve by naskh.

Commentary
This passage from al-Fayd al-Hijazi, a commentary on al-Husami, explains the Hanafi rule for resolving apparent contradictions (ta'āruḍ) among Shar‘ī evidences. The author begins by affirming that real contradiction never exists in the Qur’an or Sunnah; perceived contradictions arise from ignorance of naskh (abrogation). When two conflicting evidences are encountered, the first step is to check for naskh: if one verse or hadith abrogates the other, the later ruling supersedes. If naskh is unknown, one moves to a different category of evidence: for conflicting verses, turn to the Sunnah; for conflicting hadiths, turn to the sayings of the Companions (aqwāl al-ṣaḥāba), then to qiyās (analogy). There is a difference of opinion regarding the order of qiyās and qawl al-ṣaḥaba: the preferred Hanafi position (as held by al-Husami and the author) places qiyās before qawl al-ṣaḥaba, while some scholars reverse the order. The passage provides two examples: (1) The apparent conflict between the verses about recitation in prayer (Q 73:20 and Q 7:204) is resolved by the hadith that the follower is not required to recite, as the imam's recitation suffices. (2) The conflicting hadiths about the number of bowings in the eclipse prayer (one bowing per rak‘ah vs. two) are resolved by qiyās, relating the eclipse prayer to regular prayers. The passage then discusses the case where even qiyās is contradictory: if all types of evidence (Qur’an, Sunnah, qawl al-ṣaḥaba, qiyās) are in conflict, then taqrīr al-uṣūl is applied—the original ruling before the contradiction is retained. The example of the leftover water of donkeys (su’r al-ḥimār) illustrates this: hadiths differ on its purity, and Companions' opinions differ, so the original principle (that leftover of a forbidden animal is impure) is upheld according to Hanafis. This passage is a clear statement of the Hanafi methodology for resolving conflicting evidences within the framework of uṣūl al-fiqh.

Key Terms
تعارض (ta'āruḍ): Apparent contradiction between two evidences that cannot be reconciled except by preferring one over the other or by abrogation
ناسخ ومنسوخ (nāsikh wa mansūkh): Abrogating and abrogated; a later ruling that supersedes an earlier ruling on the same matter
حقيقي (ḥaqīqī): Real, genuine; used by the author to assert that only apparent contradiction exists in revelation

The Procedural Hierarchy for Resolving Apparent Contradictions
Lays out the procedural hierarchy for resolving apparent contradictions after naskh is exhausted.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the step-by-step Hanafi hierarchy for resolving apparent contradictions among evidences (Quran → Sunnah → qawl al-ṣaḥāba → qiyās) and apply it to the examples of recitation in prayer and the eclipse prayer.

Core Idea
When naskh is unknown, resolve contradiction by moving to a different category of evidence in a fixed order.

This lesson covers the core procedural rule for resolving apparent contradictions when naskh cannot be determined. The Hanafi school prescribes a specific order: when two verses conflict, turn to the Sunnah; when two hadiths conflict, turn to qawl al-ṣaḥāba then qiyās (or qiyās first according to the preferred view). The passage provides two key examples: the recitation in prayer (verses resolved by hadith) and the eclipse prayer (hadiths resolved by qiyās). Understanding this hierarchy is essential for correct istinbāṭ.

Guided Path
1
Recall the Prerequisite
Before applying this hierarchy, you must have already checked for naskh and failed to find abrogation. This hierarchy only applies when naskh is unknown.
The passage explicitly states: 'اگر ناسخ و منسوخ کا علم نہ ہو'.
2
Identify the Type of Conflicting Evidence
Determine whether the conflict is between two Quranic verses, two hadiths, or other types. The hierarchy differs based on the category.
For verses: first Sunnah, then qawl al-ṣaḥāba, then qiyās. For hadiths: there is a dispute.
3
Apply the Hierarchy for Conflicting Verses
If two verses conflict and naskh is unknown, turn to the Sunnah. Use authentic hadiths that address the same issue.
Example: verses about recitation in prayer (Q 73:20 vs Q 7:204) are resolved by the hadith: 'When one recites, be silent.'
4
Apply the Hierarchy for Conflicting Hadiths (Preferred View)
According to the author and al-Husami, first turn to qiyās, then to qawl al-ṣaḥāba. Some scholars reverse the order.
Example: eclipse prayer hadiths are resolved by qiyās to regular prayers (one bow per rak'ah).
5
Note the Condition for Moving to Lower Proofs
Turning to a subsequent proof is only valid if it is possible to do so. If the next category also presents conflict or cannot be applied, then taqrīr al-uṣūl takes over (covered in next lesson).

Commentary
This passage from al-Fayd al-Hijazi, a commentary on al-Husami, explains the Hanafi rule for resolving apparent contradictions (ta'āruḍ) among Shar‘ī evidences. The author begins by affirming that real contradiction never exists in the Qur’an or Sunnah; perceived contradictions arise from ignorance of naskh (abrogation). When two conflicting evidences are encountered, the first step is to check for naskh: if one verse or hadith abrogates the other, the later ruling supersedes. If naskh is unknown, one moves to a different category of evidence: for conflicting verses, turn to the Sunnah; for conflicting hadiths, turn to the sayings of the Companions (aqwāl al-ṣaḥāba), then to qiyās (analogy). There is a difference of opinion regarding the order of qiyās and qawl al-ṣaḥaba: the preferred Hanafi position (as held by al-Husami and the author) places qiyās before qawl al-ṣaḥaba, while some scholars reverse the order. The passage provides two examples: (1) The apparent conflict between the verses about recitation in prayer (Q 73:20 and Q 7:204) is resolved by the hadith that the follower is not required to recite, as the imam's recitation suffices. (2) The conflicting hadiths about the number of bowings in the eclipse prayer (one bowing per rak‘ah vs. two) are resolved by qiyās, relating the eclipse prayer to regular prayers. The passage then discusses the case where even qiyās is contradictory: if all types of evidence (Qur’an, Sunnah, qawl al-ṣaḥaba, qiyās) are in conflict, then taqrīr al-uṣūl is applied—the original ruling before the contradiction is retained. The example of the leftover water of donkeys (su’r al-ḥimār) illustrates this: hadiths differ on its purity, and Companions' opinions differ, so the original principle (that leftover of a forbidden animal is impure) is upheld according to Hanafis. This passage is a clear statement of the Hanafi methodology for resolving conflicting evidences within the framework of uṣūl al-fiqh.

Key Terms
اقوال صحابہ (aqwāl al-ṣaḥāba): Sayings or legal opinions of the Companions of the Prophet, considered a source of evidence in some contexts
قیاس (qiyās): Analogical reasoning to extend a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a common effective cause (ʿilla)
ساقط (sāqiṭ): Fallen, invalid; said of a proof when it cannot be used due to contradiction

The Ultimate Fallback: Taqrīr al-Uṣūl (Maintaining the Original Principle)
Explains the rule of taqrīr al-uṣūl and illustrates it with the example of leftover water of donkeys.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define taqrīr al-uṣūl, state when it applies, and apply it to the example of the leftover water of donkeys (su’r al-ḥimār).

Core Idea
When all categories of evidence are in conflict, the original ruling before the dispute is maintained.

This lesson covers the final resort in resolving contradictions: taqrīr al-uṣūl (establishing the original principle). When even the hierarchy of proofs fails—because the next category also contains contradictions—the Hanafi jurist defaults to the original ruling that was known before the conflict arose. The passage illustrates this with the example of the leftover water of donkeys (su’r al-ḥimār), where hadiths and Companions' opinions differ on its purity. In such a case, the original principle (that the leftover of a forbidden animal is impure) prevails.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Failure of the Hierarchy
Taqrīr al-uṣūl is only invoked when every category of evidence (verses, hadiths, qawl al-ṣaḥāba, qiyās) contains contradictions that cannot be resolved individually.
The passage says: 'اگر دو آیتوں میں تعارض کے وقت تیسری حجت کی طرف رجوع مشکل ہو، اس طرح کہ دوسنتوں میں بھی تعارض ہو جائے، اقوال صحابہ میں بھی اور دو قیاسوں میں بھی تعارض ہو جائے تو پھر تقریر اصول واجب ہوگا'.
2
Understand the Meaning of Taqrīr al-Uṣūl
Taqrīr al-uṣūl means 'maintaining the original principle.' It refers to reverting to the ruling that was established before the conflicting evidences arose, i.e., the default ruling from primary sources.
The text explains: 'ہر چیز کو اس کی اصل پر رکھا جائے گا، یعنی تعارض سے پہلے جو حکم تھا اس کو برقرار رکھا جائے گا'.
3
Identify the Original Ruling
Determine what the ruling was before the conflict. This ruling is derived from the general principles of Shari'ah, such as original purity or prohibition.
Example: for the leftover of donkeys, the original principle is that the leftover of an animal whose meat is forbidden is impure.
4
Apply to the Example of Su’r al-Ḥimār
In the case of the leftover water of donkeys, the hadiths conflict (one says it is pure, another implies impurity), and the Companions also differ. Therefore, taqrīr al-uṣūl applies, and the original ruling (impurity) is maintained.
The passage mentions Ibn Umar (RA) stating impurity as the default.
5
Note the Condition
Taqrīr al-uṣūl becomes obligatory ('واجب') when turning to a third proof is impossible (متعذر). It is not a fallback to be used lightly.
The author emphasizes this with 'وعند تعذر المصير إليه'.

Commentary
This passage from al-Fayd al-Hijazi, a commentary on al-Husami, explains the Hanafi rule for resolving apparent contradictions (ta'āruḍ) among Shar‘ī evidences. The author begins by affirming that real contradiction never exists in the Qur’an or Sunnah; perceived contradictions arise from ignorance of naskh (abrogation). When two conflicting evidences are encountered, the first step is to check for naskh: if one verse or hadith abrogates the other, the later ruling supersedes. If naskh is unknown, one moves to a different category of evidence: for conflicting verses, turn to the Sunnah; for conflicting hadiths, turn to the sayings of the Companions (aqwāl al-ṣaḥāba), then to qiyās (analogy). There is a difference of opinion regarding the order of qiyās and qawl al-ṣaḥaba: the preferred Hanafi position (as held by al-Husami and the author) places qiyās before qawl al-ṣaḥaba, while some scholars reverse the order. The passage provides two examples: (1) The apparent conflict between the verses about recitation in prayer (Q 73:20 and Q 7:204) is resolved by the hadith that the follower is not required to recite, as the imam's recitation suffices. (2) The conflicting hadiths about the number of bowings in the eclipse prayer (one bowing per rak‘ah vs. two) are resolved by qiyās, relating the eclipse prayer to regular prayers. The passage then discusses the case where even qiyās is contradictory: if all types of evidence (Qur’an, Sunnah, qawl al-ṣaḥaba, qiyās) are in conflict, then taqrīr al-uṣūl is applied—the original ruling before the contradiction is retained. The example of the leftover water of donkeys (su’r al-ḥimār) illustrates this: hadiths differ on its purity, and Companions' opinions differ, so the original principle (that leftover of a forbidden animal is impure) is upheld according to Hanafis. This passage is a clear statement of the Hanafi methodology for resolving conflicting evidences within the framework of uṣūl al-fiqh.

Bayan al-Tabdil: The Definition of Naskh (Abrogation)
Defining naskh as bayan al-tabdil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define naskh as bayan al-tabdil and explain its dual nature from the perspectives of the Lawgiver and humans.

Core Idea
Naskh is a clarification of the pre-eternal term of a ruling, not a change in divine will.

In Hanafi usul, abrogation (naskh) is not a divine afterthought but a disclosure of what was always known to Allah. This lesson unpacks the definition of naskh as 'bayan al-tabdil' — the clarification of change — using the analogy of killing to show how the same event appears differently from Allah's perspective and ours.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Read the first sentence: naskh is defined as bayan al-tabdil (clarification of change). Commit this phrase to memory — it is the core of the Hanafi position.
The term 'bayan al-tabdil' is unique to Hanafi usul.
2
Distinguish the two perspectives
The text states naskh is 'bayan mahd' (pure explanation) from the Lawgiver's side, but 'tabdil' (change) from the human side. Understand why: Allah knew the ruling's term eternally, so revealing the abrogator is merely announcing that limit.
This preserves Allah's immutability and refutes the concept of badā' (change in divine knowledge).
3
Analyze the analogy of killing
The author compares naskh to killing: from the killer's perspective it is a change (ending life), from Allah's perspective it is the manifestation of the pre-decreed ajal. Draw the parallel: the abrogating text is the 'killer' that reveals the ruling's term.
This analogy reinforces that naskh does not imply any new divine decision.
4
Identify the locus (mahall) of naskh
A ruling must be inherently probabilistic (muhtamil li'l-wujud wa'l-'adam) — meaning it could logically be continued or removed. If a text attaches a time limit (tawqit) or indicates perpetuity (ta'bid), that ruling is not subject to naskh.
For example, the five daily prayers are never abrogated due to perpetual texts.
5
Apply the definition to a sample case
Consider the abrogation of the direction of prayer (qibla) from Jerusalem to the Kaaba. The original ruling was absolute, so it appeared permanent. The abrogating verse clarified its limited term. From Allah's perspective: pure bayan. From ours: tabdil.
This case illustrates how naskh operates in practice.

Commentary
This passage presents the definition of naskh (abrogation) as bayan al-tabdil (the clarification of change). The author explains that from the perspective of Allah (صاحب الشرع), naskh is purely a clarification (bayan) because Allah knew from eternity that the ruling had a limited duration. However, when He revealed the ruling in absolute terms without initially specifying its term, humans understood it to be permanent. Then, when the abrogating text comes, it appears to us as a change (tabdil) in the ruling. In reality, it is merely a clarification of the pre-known time limit. The analogy of killing is given: a killer takes a life, which from his perspective is a change and termination of the person's life, but from Allah's perspective, it is the manifestation of the appointed time of death (ajal) which was already decreed. The ruling that is subject to naskh must be inherently probabilistic (muhtamil li'l-wujud wa'l-'adam), i.e., it could either continue or be abrogated, and it must not have been joined by a proof indicating its perpetuity or a specific limited time. This definition is foundational in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, emphasizing that naskh does not indicate a change in Allah's will but rather a disclosure of His eternal decree.

Key Terms
النَّسْخ (al-naskh): Abrogation: the lifting of a Shari'ah ruling by a later textual evidence; from Allah's perspective, it is a clarification of the pre-known term.
بيان التَّبْدِيل (bayan al-tabdil): The clarification of change: a term for naskh indicating it appears as a change to us but is pure clarification from Allah's knowledge.

Core Idea
Naskh is Allah's declaration of a pre-known duration, not a change of His will.

Abrogation (naskh) is a key concept in usul al-fiqh that addresses how certain rulings of the Shari'ah have been superseded. The Hanafi school defines naskh uniquely as a declaration (bayan) of the duration of a ruling that was always known to Allah but was revealed without an apparent time limit. This lesson unpacks that definition, the analogy used to illustrate it, and the conditions that determine which rulings can be abrogated.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two perspectives
Recognize that from Allah's perspective, naskh is a mere declaration of the pre-ordained expiration of a ruling; from the human perspective, it appears as a change or substitution.
The analogy of killing helps cement this distinction.
2
Identify the locus of naskh
Determine that naskh only applies to rulings that inherently admit the possibility of continuation or abrogation (i.e., not eternally fixed or temporally specified).
Rulings with explicit eternal wording or indication cannot be abrogated.
3
Note the conditions for abrogability
A ruling must be free from elements contradictory to naskh, such as temporal specification (tawqit) or eternal perpetuity (ta'bid) proven by text or indication.
Examples include verses promising eternal punishment or the collective body of sharīʿah at the Prophet's death.
4
Link to the proof of permissibility
The permissibility of naskh is established by Quran 2:106, which affirms that Allah replaces verses with better or similar ones.
This verse is cited in the passage and is qat'i in transmission and meaning.

Commentary
This passage defines naskh (abrogation) as a type of bayan al-tabdil (declaration of change). It emphasizes the distinction between the perspective of the Lawgiver (Allah) and human perspective. From Allah's perspective, naskh is merely a declaration (bayan) of the pre-ordained duration of a ruling, while from human perspective it appears as a change (tabdil) because the ruling seemed permanent. The analogy with killing illustrates this: killing is a declaration of the end of life from Allah's perspective but a change from human perspective. The passage then discusses the locus (mahall) of naskh: it applies only to rulings that in themselves admit the possibility of being continued or abrogated, and are not attached to any element that contradicts abrogation, such as explicit temporal limitation (tawqit) or eternal perpetuation (ta'bid) proven by text or by indication (dalalah). Examples include Quranic verses promising eternal punishment ('Abiding therein forever') which cannot be abrogated. The passage then provides the linguistic and technical definitions of naskh, citing Quranic verses that use 'tabdil' synonymously, and outlines the various linguistic meanings before giving the technical definition. This is a foundational discussion in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, establishing the scope and limits of naskh. The author implies that naskh is permissible and known, and its permissibility is proven by the Quran (2:106).

Key Terms
النَّسْخ (al-naskh): In the technical sense, it is to declare the duration of an absolute ruling that was known to Allah but He left it absolute; from Allah's perspective it is a declaration, from human perspective a change.
التَّبْدِيل (al-tabdil): Synonym for naskh; change or replacement, used in the Quran (16:101) to refer to abrogation.
التَّوْقِيت (al-tawqit): Temporal specification; attaching a specific time limit to a ruling, which prevents naskh.
التَّأْبِيد (al-ta'bid): Eternal perpetuation; a ruling proven to be eternal by text or indication, which cannot be abrogated.

Naskh Before Action: Hanafi vs Opponent
Dispute on whether naskh can occur after belief but before action

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that naskh can occur after belief to a new case, and evaluate the opponent's objection based on the combination of good and bad.

Core Idea
Naskh is valid once the command is believed in the heart, even if action is not yet possible.

This lesson explores a classic usul dispute: can a ruling be abrogated after people believe in it but before they can act on it? The opponent argues it is impossible because it would require combining two contradictory rulings (good and bad) in one moment. The Hanafis respond with textual evidence from the Mi'raj and a hadith, asserting that belief itself is sufficient for the abrogation to be valid. Understanding this debate sharpens your ability to analyze the interplay between belief, action, and the timing of legal commands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
The dispute is specifically about naskh (abrogation) occurring after the person has believed in the command (i'tiqad qalbi) but before they have the ability to perform the action. The opponent claims this is impossible.
This is not about naskh before revelation, but after belief without action.
2
State the Hanafi position and its proofs
Hanafis hold that naskh is valid once the command is believed. They cite the hadith 'The intention of the believer is better than his action' and the Mi'raj story where fifty daily prayers were reduced to five before anyone had performed them.
The key point: belief (i'tiqad) is the foundation; action is secondary.
3
Analyze the opponent's objection
The opponent argues that naskh before action would necessitate combining good (husn) and bad (qubh) in one moment, which is logically impossible. They give an example: first forbidding prayer at noon, then commanding it before the person acts.
The first command is considered 'bad' (prohibition) and the second 'good' (command); they cannot coexist.
4
Apply the Hanafi response to the hypothetical
Hanafis respond that belief is separate from action. The first command's binding period ends with naskh; the person only held a belief, which is not contradictory to the second command. The moral quality applies to the commands themselves, not to the moment of belief.
Thus, no actual contradiction occurs because the person never acted on the first command.

Commentary
This passage presents a dispute regarding the permissibility of naskh (abrogation) before the possibility of acting upon the abrogated command, but after having believed in it. The opponent (likely some Mu'tazila or others) argues that naskh before the ability to act is impossible because it would require combining two contradictory rulings (good and bad) at the same time. For example, if someone is told not to pray at noon, and then before he can act on that prohibition, it is abrogated and he is told to pray at noon, then the prohibition was bad (forbidding prayer) and the command is good — they cannot coexist in the same moment. Therefore, naskh can only occur after one could have performed the action, not merely after belief. The Hanafis respond by distinguishing between belief (i'tiqad qalbi) and action. They argue that belief is the foundation; an action without belief is not worship, but belief without action can be worship. They cite the hadith: 'The intention of the believer is better than his action.' This indicates that belief alone carries weight. Their second proof is the event of the Isra' and Mi'raj: initially fifty daily prayers were made obligatory, but before anyone could perform them (since revelation was still being reduced), they were abrogated to five. Yet the Prophet and the Ummah had believed in the obligation. This demonstrates that abrogation can occur after belief but before action. Thus, the Hanafi position is that naskh is valid as long as the command was believed, even if action was not yet possible.

Key Terms
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a previous legal ruling by a later one.
اعتقاد قلبی (i'tiqad qalbi): Heartfelt belief in the obligation of a ruling.
حسن و فتح (husn wa futh): Good and evil; here used to contrast the moral quality of rulings.

Qiyas Cannot Abrogate Any Source
Rule that qiyas is not a valid nasikh for any of the four sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why qiyas cannot serve as a nasikh for any of the four sources and apply the rule to distinguish between authoritative and non-authoritative abrogation.

Core Idea
Qiyas cannot abrogate any source: a weaker proof cannot override a stronger one.

This lesson clarifies an essential rule in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: analogical reasoning (qiyas) cannot abrogate (naskh) any of the four primary sources—the Qur'an, Sunnah, ijma, or even another qiyas. The passage explains the reasoning behind each prohibition and provides a vivid example from the Companion Ali ibn Abi Talib. Understanding this rule prevents the misuse of qiyas to override clear textual evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
State the rule: qiyas cannot be a nasikh for any of the four sources. Note that there is consensus (jumhur) on this point.
2
Understand why qiyas cannot abrogate Qur'an and Sunnah
The Qur'an and Sunnah are qat'i (definitive) in transmission, while qiyas is zanni (speculative). A weak proof cannot abrogate a strong one. The Companions also abandoned qiyas in favor of clear textual practice.
The Urdu word 'qawmi' likely means 'qat'i' (definitive).
3
Analyze the example from Ali
Ali's statement: 'If religion were based on qiyas, the inner part of the khuff would be more deserving of wiping than the outer part. But I saw the Prophet wiping the outer part.' This shows that explicit Sunnah overrides qiyas.
The hadith is a proof that qiyas cannot abrogate a sunnah practice.
4
Apply the rule to ijma
Qiyas cannot abrogate ijma because ijma is based on the meaning of the Qur'an and Sunnah, which are stronger. Ijma itself is a strong source.
5
Apply the rule to conflict between two qiyases
When two qiyas contradict, neither falls; they remain valid but conflicting. Since abrogation requires one ruling to effectively replace another, qiyas cannot abrogate another qiyas.
This highlights the relative weakness of qiyas compared to other sources.

Imam al-Shafi'i argued that allowing naskh of the Qur'an by the Sunnah leads to objection (ta'n) against the Prophet, as people might accuse him of contradicting Allah.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: naskh is not objection but clarification; the Prophet was sent as a mubayyin (clarifier), and Allah can undertake clarification of what He revealed on the Prophet's tongue.
Hanafis also point out that Shafi'is themselves allow takhsis (specification) of Qur'an by Sunnah, which is analogous.
4
Enumerate the four types of naskh
The passage lists: (1) Qur'an by Qur'an, (2) Sunnah by Sunnah, (3) Qur'an by Sunnah, (4) Sunnah by Qur'an. For type (2), four sub-types regarding mutawatir and ahad are mentioned.
Examples: (1) 'iddah verses, (2) visiting graves hadith, (3) permissibility of many wives by hadith, (4) change of qibla.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Shafi'is hold that the Qur'an can only be abrogated by the Qur'an, not by the Sunnah, while Hanafis allow cross-abrogation. This affects rulings where only a hadith contradicts a Qur'anic verse.

Commentary
This is a Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute passage from a commentary on al-Husami. The author first clarifies that the cessation of the share of mu'allafatu al-qulub is not due to consensus but due to the cessation of the cause (dawah) — a key Hanafi principle that a ruling ends when its cause ends, not through abrogation per se. This sets the stage for the main discussion on naskh. The core rule: naskh is permissible by both the Qur'an and Sunnah, and also one can abrogate the other. Imam Shafi'i objects that this opens the door for criticism of the Prophet. The Hanafi response is that naskh is merely a statement of the duration of a ruling, not a denial of the original. The Prophet was sent as a clarifier (mubayyin), so clarifying the duration is part of his role. The text then enumerates four types of naskh: (1) Qur'an by Qur'an (e.g., 'iddah verses), (2) Sunnah by Sunnah (e.g., visiting graves), (3) Qur'an by Sunnah (e.g., permissibility of many wives by hadith), (4) Sunnah by Qur'an (e.g., change of qibla). For type (2), four sub-types are mentioned regarding mutawatir and ahad; the majority reject naskh of mutawatir by ahad. The Shafi'i objection to types (3) and (4) is refuted by arguing that naskh is not objection but clarification, and that Shafi'is themselves allow takhsis of the Qur'an by the Sunnah, which is analogous. Finally, naskh of recitation and ruling together or separately is permissible because the text has two independent rulings: the permissibility of prayer (its recitation) and the legal meaning it conveys. Examples include parts of Surat al-Ahzab and the ten-suckling verse. The passage is structurally: dispute statement, Hanafi proof, types, sub-types, refutation of Shafi'i, and additional categories of abrogated.

Key Terms
نَسْخ (naskh): Clarification of the duration of a legal ruling (بَيَانُ مُدَّةِ الْحُكْمِ), not an annulment of the original ruling.
التَّلَاوَةُ وَالْحُكْمُ (al-tilawah wa al-hukm): The recitation of a verse and the ruling it contains; each can be abrogated independently.
مُبَيِّن (mubayyin): Clarifier; one who explains. The Prophet was sent as a mubayyin to clarify the meanings and durations of rulings.

Types of Naskh of Sunnah by Sunnah: The Four Scenarios and the Majority Position
Lists four scenarios of abrogation of Sunnah by Sunnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four scenarios of naskh of Sunnah by Sunnah and apply the Hanafi rule that mutawatir cannot be abrogated by ahad.

Core Idea
Three of four types of Sunnah-by-Sunnah naskh are unanimously allowed; only mutawatir-by-ahad is rejected by the majority.

In Usul al-Fiqh, naskh (abrogation) is classified by the source of the naskh and mansukh. This lesson focuses on the second type: naskh of Sunnah by Sunnah. Understanding which scenarios are acceptable and which are disputed is crucial for correctly weighing evidences when rulings appear to conflict. The passage lists four possible combinations based on the transmission strength (mutawatir vs. ahad) and identifies the point of ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Understand the classification by transmission strength
Recognize that Sunnah is divided into mutawatir (mass-transmitted, certain) and ahad (single/limited chains, probable). These two categories can be paired in naskh.
Mutawatir gives certainty (qat'i), ahad gives probability (zanni).
2
Identify the three unanimously permissible scenarios
The passage states that naskh of (1) mutawatir by mutawatir, (2) ahad by ahad, and (3) ahad by mutawatir are all allowed by consensus (bil-ittifaq).
These involve equal or stronger evidence replacing weaker or equal evidence.
3
Recognize the disputed scenario
The fourth case—mutawatir by ahad—is rejected by the majority (jumhur) but accepted by some scholars. This is the key point of ikhtilaf.
The Hanafi school follows the majority view.
4
Apply the Hanafi principle of certainty
Hanafi usul holds that a mutawatir (qat'i al-thubut) cannot be abrogated by a khabar wahid (zanni al-thubut) because certainty cannot be overridden by probability.
This principle is derived from the epistemological weight of the evidence.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The difference of opinion leads to acceptance or rejection of certain abrogations in fiqh. For example, the abrogation of the ruling on visiting graves is accepted because the abrogating hadith is also ahad, not mutawatir.
This keeps the scope of valid naskh clear for Hanafi jurists.

The Hanafi Doctrine: Addition to the Text is Abrogation
Usul dispute: Hanafi view that ziyadah is naskh

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Adding to a text changes the original obligation; hence it abrogates it — the right of Allah is indivisible.

In this passage, al-Husami presents a foundational usul dispute: does an addition to a revealed legal text (ziyadah 'ala al-nass) count as abrogation (naskh) or as an independent ruling? The Hanafis argue it is naskh because the original prescribed matter becomes only part of the required right, and since Allah's rights are not divisible, the addition effectively changes the obligation. The Shafi'is hold that such additions are independent rulings, not abrogation. This difference has major consequences for which sources can add what.

Identify that ziyadah 'ala al-nass is considered naskh by the Hanafis. Note that the opposite view (not naskh) is attributed to al-Shafi'i.

Understand the reasoning: the original prescribed matter (al-mashru') becomes only part (ba'd) of the required right (haqq). The author uses the example of zihar to illustrate: if a man after a month of fasting becomes ill and feeds 30 poor people, it is not valid because the addition (feeding) changes the original obligation.

4

Compare the practical result: Hanafis reject many additions by khabar wahid or qiyas, whereas Shafi'is accept them as independent rulings not constituting naskh. This difference affects which sources can introduce new obligations or conditions.
For example, Hanafis do not require recitation of Fatihah in prayer based on ahad hadith, while Shafi'is do.

This passage discusses the concept of ziyadah 'ala al-nass (addition to the revealed text). According to the Hanafis, such an addition constitutes naskh (abrogation) because it changes the original prescribed matter (al-mashru') from being the whole right (haqq) to being part of it. Since the right of Allah does not admit of divisibility, a part does not have the legal effect of the whole. The reasoning is illustrated by the example of a person who performs zihar. If he becomes ill after fasting one month and then feeds thirty poor people (instead of completing two consecutive months of fasting), it is not valid. This shows that an addition (here, feeding as an alternative) changes the original obligation, and is thus naskh. The Hanafi scholars applied this principle in concrete rulings: they did not consider recitation of al-Fatihah in prayer as a pillar based on a khabar wahid, because that would be an addition to the text; they rejected adding stoning for the unmarried fornicator based on khabar wahid; they rejected adding purification as a condition for tawaf al-ziyarah; and they rejected adding faith as a condition for the slave in expiation. In all these cases, the addition would constitute naskh, and since khabar wahid cannot abrogate the Quran or mutawatir Sunnah, such additions are invalid.

Key Terms
زیادۃ على النص (ziyadah 'ala al-nass): An addition to a revealed legal text (Quran or Sunnah) that imposes an extra requirement.

Ziyadah 'ala al-Nass: Addition to the Text as Abrogation
Usul rule: ziyadah as naskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school considers an addition to a revealed text as abrogation (naskh) and identify the evidentiary standard required for such an addition.

Core Idea
Adding a condition to a divine text abrogates the original, because Allah's rights are indivisible.

In this lesson, we examine the fourth type of naskh in Hanafi usul: ziyadah 'ala al-nass (addition to the text). The Hanafi position is that when a new condition or restriction is added to a previously established ruling, it effectively abrogates the original ruling, not merely clarifies it. This is because Allah's rights (huquq Allah) are indivisible, so the original absolute ruling becomes only a part and loses legal effect. We will explore the proof, the dispute with the Shafi'i school, and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Define the fourth type of naskh
State what ziyadah 'ala al-nass means: an addition (e.g., a condition, restriction, or qualification) to a revealed text that modifies the original ruling. Understand that this addition is non-independent (ghayr mustaqill).
The addition does not stand alone but is integrated into the original ruling.
2
Understand the Hanafi proof
Grasp the argument that the addition makes the original text become 'some of the right' (ba'd al-haqq). Since Allah's rights are indivisible, the part does not have the ruling of the whole; thus the original is effectively abrogated. Study the example of zihar: a person who fasts one month of the required two and then feeds poor people does not fulfill the obligation.
3
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Recognize that Imam al-Shafi'i considers such additions as bayan (clarification) and takhsis (specification), not naskh. According to Shafi'is, additions can be established by khabar wahid or qiyas.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
See the practical difference: Hanafis require mutawatir or mashhur evidence for any addition to the Qur'an, because it is naskh. Shafi'is accept khabar wahid and qiyas for additions because they are considered clarifications. Examples: Hanafis reject making recitation of al-Fatiha a pillar in prayer based on a solitary hadith, while Shafi'is accept it.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of the fourth type of naskh (abrogation) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: ziyadah 'ala al-nass (addition to the text). The author, commenting on al-Husami, explains that this type occurs when an additional condition, restriction, or qualification is introduced to a previously established ruling, thereby altering the original text. The Hanafi position is that such an addition constitutes abrogation (naskh), not mere clarification (bayan). The proof is based on the nature of Allah's rights (huquq Allah), which are indivisible. When a ruling is initially given as absolute (mutlaq) and later a condition is added, the original absolute ruling becomes only a part of the composite whole. Since Allah's rights cannot be accepted in parts, the original absolute ruling loses its legal effect; thus the addition effectively abrogates it. The author illustrates this with the example of zihar: a person must fast two consecutive months. If after one month he falls ill and feeds poor people instead, this does not fulfill his obligation because Allah's right does not tolerate partial fulfillment. The Hanafi scholars applied this principle to reject certain additions to Islamic law based on weak evidence (khabar wahid or qiyas), such as making the recitation of al-Fatiha a pillar of prayer, adding exile for unmarried fornicators, requiring purity for the farewell circumambulation, or requiring the attribute of faith in a slave for expiation. In contrast, Imam al-Shafi'i considers such additions to be clarifications (bayan) and specifications (takhsis), which can be established by isolated reports or analogy. The passage concludes that since Hanafis view ziyadah as naskh, it requires the same evidentiary standard as abrogation—only mutawatir or mashhur reports can add to the Qur'an, not khabar wahid or qiyas.

Key Terms
النَّسْخ (al-naskh): Abrogation: the removal of a legal ruling by a later divine text.
الزِّيَادَةُ عَلَى النَّص (al-ziyadah 'ala al-nass): Addition to a revealed text, such as adding a condition or restriction that modifies the original ruling.
بَعْضُ الْحَقِّ (ba'd al-haqq): A part of the obligation owed to Allah, which does not have the legal weight of the whole due to the indivisibility of Allah's rights.
حُقُوقُ اللَّهِ (huquq Allah): Rights owed to Allah, such as acts of worship and penalties, which are not divisible and cannot be partially fulfilled.

Core Idea
Adding a condition to an absolute Quranic ruling is naskh; only mutawatir or mashhur reports can do so.

This passage tackles a core dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: does adding a restriction to a Quranic text via khabar wahid count as abrogation or clarification? The Hanafi position is that it is naskh, preserving the Quran's primacy. Understanding this distinction is vital for deriving rulings in prayer, penal law, pilgrimage, and expiation.

Commentary
This passage is a classic discussion in Hanafi uṣūl al-fiqh concerning the legal status of adding a qualifying condition or restriction (qayd) to an existing absolute (muṭlaq) text. The central issue: is such an addition (ziyādah) a form of abrogation (naskh) or merely a clarification (bayān)? The author first defines the fourth type of naskh: addition to the text where the original ruling remains but its attribute is abrogated. For example, adding a condition of purity to ṭawāf, or adding exile to the ḥadd of lashes, or adding the condition of faith to freeing a slave in certain expiations. The Hanafi position, represented by Imām Abū Ḥanīfah and his followers, is that such an addition is genuinely naskh, even if it appears to be a bayān. Imām al-Shāfiʿī holds that it is not naskh but bayān (explanation). The Hanafi proof is rational: when an absolute ruling becomes restricted, the original absolute becomes a part of the new composite ruling. The composite is a right of Allah, and parts of Allah's rights do not have the same status as the whole. Therefore, the absolute loses its independent ruling; that is naskh. The author illustrates with examples from fiqh: (1) In prayer, the Quranic verse 'faqra'ū mā tayassara min al-Qur'ān' (Sūrat al-Muzzammil 73:20) indicates that any portion of the Quran suffices, but the ḥadīth 'lā ṣalāta illā bi fātiḥati al-kitāb' requires al-Fātiḥah. Hanafis do not make al-Fātiḥah a rukn (pillar) because that would abrogate the Quranic absoluteness via khabar wāḥid. Instead, they consider it wājib. Shāfiʿīs consider it a rukn. (2) For zinā of a non-muḥṣan, the Quran says 'fajlidū kulla wāḥidin minhumā mi'ata jaldah' (Sūrat al-Nūr 24:2), which imposes only lashes. The ḥadīth 'al-bikru bil-bikri jaldu mi'atin wa taghrīb' adds exile. Hanafis do not treat exile as part of the ḥadd because that would abrogate the Quran via khabar wāḥid; exile is at the ruler's discretion. Shāfiʿīs include it as part of the ḥadd. (3) For ṭawāf, the Quran says 'walyatṭawwafū bil-bayti al-ʿatīq' (Sūrat al-Ḥajj 22:29) without purity condition. The ḥadīth 'al-ṭawāfu bil-bayti mithlu al-ṣalāh' implies purity. Hanafis do not require purity as a condition, only as wājib (obligatory) based on the ḥadīth, but not a condition of validity. Shāfiʿīs make it a condition. (4) For expiation (kaffārah) of ẓihār and yamīn, the Quran says 'taḥrīru raqabah' (Sūrat al-Mujādilah 58:3) without faith condition, while for qatl, it says 'fa taḥrīru raqabatin mu'minatin' (Sūrat al-Nisā' 4:92). Hanafis do not use qiyās to add the faith condition to the first, because that would abrogate the Quranic absolute via qiyās. The text cuts off before completing this point. The passage overall demonstrates the Hanafi commitment to preserving the primacy of the Quran's wording, allowing only mutawātir or mashhūr reports to abrogate or add to the Quran.

Key Terms
زیادتی (ziyādah): An addition to a text by way of a restrictive condition (qayd) or attribute that changes the original ruling's scope.
نسخ (naskh): Replacement or nullification of a previous Shariah ruling by a later one; here, specifically partial abrogation by adding a condition.
بیان (bayān): Clarification or explanation of what was originally intended in the text.
خبر واحد (khabar wāḥid): A report transmitted by a single chain or limited number of narrators, not reaching tawātur.
