The Particle Min for Tab'id and its Legal Effect
Explaining the partiality meaning of min with a manumission example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that 'min' indicates tab'id to derive the ruling in a manumission case and evaluate the Sahibayn's alternative interpretation.

Core Idea
The particle 'min' signals partiality, restricting the generality of 'man' — a Hanafi internal dispute arises over its interpretation as bayaniyya.

This lesson unpacks a classic Hanafi usul dispute about the preposition 'min'. By analyzing a manumission example from Imam Abu Hanifa, you will see how the literal meaning of 'min' as tab'id (partiality) interacts with the general relative pronoun 'man' to produce a nuanced ruling. The Sahibayn offer a different interpretation, treating 'min' as clarificatory, leading to a different outcome. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to identify how linguistic particles affect legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic particles and their default meanings
Recognize that 'min' is originally for tab'id and 'man' is for generality.
2
Apply the particles to the manumission wording
In the phrase 'a'tiq min 'abidi man shi'ta', determine the combined effect: 'man' gives general permission, 'min' imposes partiality, resulting in freeing all but one.
3
Contrast with the alternative wording 'man sha'a'
Note that when 'man' is followed by a general attribute (sha'a), the generality of the attribute overrides the partiality of 'min', so all slaves who wish are freed.
4
Compare the Sahibayn's position
They interpret 'min' as bayaniyya, so no partiality; thus all are freed in both cases. Understand the thamarah al-khilaf.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the particle 'min' and its use in legal language. The author, following most jurists, holds that the original meaning of 'min' is tab'id (partiality). The ruling given by Imam Abu Hanifa illustrates this: if a master tells his agent 'a'tiq min 'abidi man shi'ta 'itqahu' (free from my slaves whoever you wish to free), the agent can free all the slaves except one. This is because the relative pronoun 'man' in 'man shi'ta' indicates generality (being a relative pronoun with potential generality), but the preposition 'min' before 'abidi' indicates partiality. The combination results in a ruling that respects both: the agent has the general permission to free slaves (due to 'man'), but must leave at least one unfreed (due to 'min'). In contrast, if the master says 'a'tiq man sha'a' (free whoever wishes), using 'man' as a relative pronoun without the partial 'min' and with the attribute 'sha'a' (wishes) being general and attributed to 'man', the entire speech becomes general, and all slaves who wish are freed. The passage also contrasts the view of the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), who interpret 'min' in this context as bayaniyya (clarification), not tab'id, so they see no need to exclude any slave. This is a classic Hanafi internal dispute rooted in the linguistic analysis of the particle 'min'.

Key Terms
من (min): Preposition for tab'id (partiality) – indicates that the ruling applies to part of the object.
من (man): Relative pronoun meaning 'whoever' – indicates generality.

Interpreting 'Min' in Conditional Oaths: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and Sahibayn on interpreting 'min' in freeing slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between min tab'idiyya and min bayaniyya and apply the Hanafi principle of contextual attribution to resolve a difference in usul.

Core Idea
The meaning of 'min' shifts from partiality to explanation depending on whose attribute it is attached to.

This passage explores a nuanced usul dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). The disagreement centers on the Arabic particle 'min' in conditional oaths like 'any of my slaves you wish are free.' The Hanafi master distinguishes based on whether the will is attributed to Allah (general attribute) or to the addressee (human attribute), while the Sahibayn consistently treat 'min' as explanatory. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to assess how context and linguistic classification affect legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Disputed Phrase
Take the statement 'min 'abidi man shi'ta' (any of my slaves you wish). Note that the word 'min' is used before 'abidi' (my slaves).
2
Classify 'min' Based on Attribution
Determine to whom the will (mashiyyah) is attributed. If attributed to Allah, 'min' is classified as tab'idiyya (partial) but the generality of the attribute overrides it. If attributed to a human, 'min' retains its partiality.
This is Abu Hanifa's position.
3
Apply Abu Hanifa's Principle
If the will is a general attribute (like Allah's), the generality cancels the particularity indicated by 'min tab'idiyya', making the statement general. If the will is attributed to a human, the particularity remains, requiring at least one slave not to be freed.
4
Compare with Sahibayn's View
The Sahibayn consider 'min' in this context as bayaniyya (explanatory), not tab'idiyya, so the statement includes all slaves uniformly regardless of attribution.
Thus, in both cases all slaves are freed according to them.
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: In the human-attributed case, Abu Hanifa rules that at least one slave is not freed, while the Sahibayn rule that all are freed.
This is the legal consequence of the usul disagreement.

The Legal Effect of 'Fi' in Conditional Sentences
Textual analysis of 'fi' in conditional utterances

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule to determine whether a conditional phrase is a zarf or mi'yar based on the presence or absence of 'fi', and explain the exception for impossibility.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, 'fi' transforms a conditional phrase from a standard to a container, narrowing the scope.

The preposition 'fi' may seem like a simple grammatical element, but in legal utterances—like oaths and divorce—its presence or absence changes the ruling. Imam Abu Hanifa distinguished between mentioning 'fi' and omitting it, affecting whether the phrase is interpreted as a container or a standard. This lesson unpacks that rule with examples from divorce and oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify presence/absence of 'fi'
Locate the preposition 'fi' directly before the conditional phrase. If present, the phrase is a container; if absent, it is a standard.
Example: 'Fi al-dahr' vs 'al-dahr'.
2
Apply the Hanafi rule
With 'fi': following phrase is a zarf (container) for the action, meaning the condition is fulfilled by any part of it. Without 'fi': the phrase is a mi'yar (standard), meaning the condition requires the entire thing unless intention specifies otherwise.
This is Imam Abu Hanifa's position.
3
Determine default scope
For a zarf, the condition applies to any portion (e.g., fasting one hour). For a mi'yar, default is the whole (e.g., fasting entire lifetime) but intention can shift it.
Intention can modify the standard but not the container? The passage shows intention affects the standard.
4
Handle impossibility
If literal meaning of 'fi' is impossible (e.g., 'fi dukhulik al-dar' where entering cannot be a container), interpret 'fi' metaphorically for muqarana (concomitance).
Then the condition is concurrent with the event.
