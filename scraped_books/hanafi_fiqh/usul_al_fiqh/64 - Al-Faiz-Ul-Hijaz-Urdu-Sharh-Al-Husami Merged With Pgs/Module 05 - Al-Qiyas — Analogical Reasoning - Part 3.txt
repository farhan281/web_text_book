The Principle: A Substitute Can Differ from Its Original When States Differ
Explanation of a usul principle with two illustrative cases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi usul principle regarding the substitute (khalifah) and apply it to examples such as diyah for qisas and tayammum for wudu.

Core Idea
A substitute can depart from its original when the underlying states differ.

This passage introduces a crucial usul principle: although a substitute normally follows the original, it can have its own ruling if the circumstances change. The text illustrates this with two cases: diyah (blood money) substitutes for qisas (retaliation) but is allocated to the deceased's estate rather than the heirs, and tayammum substitutes for wudu but requires intention whereas wudu does not. By understanding why the states differ, you will see how the Hanafi school maintains consistency while allowing necessary exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The opponents claim that since qisas is for the heirs, its substitute diyah should also belong to the heirs.
2
Locate the Hanafi response
The original (qisas) cannot satisfy the deceased's need because the deceased is dead; diyah (property) can. Therefore their states differ.
The state difference is the key: the deceased cannot benefit from qisas but can benefit from diyah through debt payment.
3
Understand the general rule
A substitute departs from the original only when their states differ.
This is the core principle being taught.
4
Apply to the second example
Wudu (water) is inherently pure, no intention needed; tayammum (earth) is impure, a concession, so intention is required.
The state difference between pure water and impure earth necessitates a different ruling.
5
Conclude the practical effect
In qisas/diyah, diyah goes to the deceased's estate; in wudu/tayammum, intention is wajib in tayammum but not in wudu.

Commentary
This passage expounds a usul principle: normally a substitute (khalifah) follows the ruling of the original (asl), but if the state or nature of the two differs, the substitute can have its own ruling. The objection was: since qisas is for the heirs, diyah (its substitute) should also be for the heirs. The answer: qisas cannot serve the deceased's need (since dead have no capacity), but diyah can (it's property that can pay debts). Therefore, diyah goes to the deceased's estate, not to the heirs initially. The text gives another example: wudu (ablution with water) does not require intention, but tayammum (dry ablution) does require intention. Water is naturally pure, tayammum uses earth which is impure and is a concession, so intention is needed to distinguish it from normal impurity removal. This illustrates the principle well.

Key Terms
اصل (asl): The original ruling or act (e.g., qisas, wudu) from which a substitute is derived.
خلیفہ (khalifah): The substitute or successor that takes the place of the original (e.g., diyah for qisas, tayammum for wudu).

The Legal Definition of Hazl and Its Effect on Rescindable Contracts
Defines hazl and its effect on rescindable contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of hazl and explain its analogy to khiyar, applying it to contracts like sale and lease.

Core Idea
Hazl makes a contract voidable, not void, and is like an option in sale.

This passage opens by defining hazl as play—intending the opposite of a term's proper meaning. The author explains that hazl does not negate the outward act (so apostasy is still apostasy) but it contradicts the serious consent needed for legal rulings. Consequently, hazl operates like a contractual option (khiyar), making rescindable contracts voidable until ratified. This lesson unpacks the definition and its immediate consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of hazl
Hazl is defined as 'play' or intending by a word something other than its conventional meaning. It is the opposite of seriousness (jidd).
Distinguish from majaz: hazl is disapproved.
2
Analyze the effect on consent
Hazl does not contradict consent to the direct act (e.g., uttering words of apostasy) but it contradicts the choice and consent required for the legal ruling. Thus the act is still liable to worldly and Hereafter consequences.
Example: apostasy while jesting still incurs disbelief.
3
Understand the analogy to khiyar
The author analogizes hazl to a condition of option (khiyar) in sale. Both create a situation where the contract is concluded but not immediately binding.
The illa: both allow for rescission.
4
Apply to sale and lease
If parties agree on a sale in jest, the sale is fasid (voidable) and ownership does not transfer. However, if they later authorize (ratify) it with seriousness, it becomes valid. Abu Hanifa adds that ratification must occur within three days.
This mirrors the khiyar rule.

Commentary
This passage defines 'hazl' (jest) as a legal impediment. The author explains that hazl means play—intending something other than the actual meaning of a statement. It does not negate the outward act itself (so one can still be liable for apostasy even when joking), but it contradicts the serious choice and consent required for legal rulings. Therefore, hazl acts like a conditional option (khiyar) in contracts: it affects contracts that are rescindable, like sale and lease. If parties agree to a sale in jest, the contract is fasid (voidable) and does not transfer ownership, but if they later authorize it (confirm with seriousness), it becomes valid. Abu Hanifa adds that the authorization period must be limited to three days, analogous to the option period. The author then presents a dispute: if parties agree on a sale for a mentioned price (e.g., 2 dirhams) but secretly intend a different price (1000 dirhams), Abu Hanifa says the jest is void and the stated amount is the actual price, making the sale potentially invalid due to the price condition. The two companions (Sahibayn) argue that if the jest only concerns the price mutuality, not the original contract, then the contract is valid with the intended price because seriousness in the original contract can coexist with jest in the price. The author sides with Abu Hanifa, saying that giving effect to the original seriousness is preferable over the condition. The explanation section clarifies that hazl is distinct from metaphor (majaz) because hazl is disapproved while metaphor is accepted.

جد (jidd): Seriousness; intending the literal or metaphorical meaning of an expression.
خیار (khiyar): Option or right to rescind a contract within a specified period.

Intra-Madhhab Dispute: Jest in Pricing – Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-madhhab dispute on jest in pricing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding jest in the price of a sale and identify the practical consequence.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa voids the jest condition; Sahibayn uphold the intended price.

This lesson zooms in on a specific case: the parties agree on a sale with a stated price (e.g., two dirhams) but jokingly intend a different price (e.g., a thousand dirhams). The author presents two views: Abu Hanifa treats the jest as void and the stated price as actual, resulting in a fasid sale due to the condition; the Sahibayn treat the jest as limited to the price, so the sale is valid with the intended price. Understanding this disagreement sharpens your skill in applying hazl as an impediment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed case
The case: parties agree on a sale for a stated price (e.g., 2 dirhams) but in jest intend a different price (e.g., 1000 dirhams). They are serious about the sale but joking about the price.
Two examples: (1) sale for 2 dirhams intending 1000; (2) sale for 100 dinars intending 1000 dirhams.
2
State Abu Hanifa's view
Abu Hanifa: the jest regarding the price is void, so the stated price (2 dirhams) is the actual price. However, the condition 'on condition that the price is 1000 dirhams' is a fasid condition, making the sale fasid.
Result: sale is fasid, not valid.
3
State Sahibayn's view
Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad): the jest is only in the price mutuality, not in the original contract. It is possible to be serious about the contract and jest about the price intentionally. Therefore, the intended price (1000 dirhams) should be followed, and the sale is valid.
Result: sale is valid with the intended price.

Commentary
This passage defines 'hazl' (jest) as a legal impediment. The author explains that hazl means play—intending something other than the actual meaning of a statement. Hazl in Wealth-Based Contracts: The Sahibayn's Ruling
Ruling on hazl in wealth-based contracts (khul', 'itq, sulh).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that hazl in wealth-based contracts like khul' is void, and determine when the contracted wealth becomes obligatory and divorce takes effect.

Core Idea
Jest in khul' cannot prevent divorce or payment—the legal effect follows the apparent contract, not the hidden intent.

In this lesson, we examine a key usul principle: when a contract's purpose is wealth (mal), jest (hazl) does not alter the legal consequences. Based on a passage from al-Mabsut, the Sahibayn rule that in khul' and similar contracts, the stated terms take effect, and the internal jest is disregarded. We will explore the analogy to khiyar al-shart and understand why the cause (sabab) overrides intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contracts where wealth is intended
List the contracts discussed: khul', 'itq 'ala mal, sulh for intentional homicide. Notice that in these, wealth is an essential element and must be stipulated.
2
Understand the forms of hazl in khul'
Three forms: hazl in the original contract (denying it entirely), hazl in the amount (stating more or less), hazl in the type (e.g., dirhams vs dinars).
3
State the ruling of the Sahibayn
In all scenarios (persisting, abandoning, free-minded, disputing), the hazl is void; divorce occurs and the stated wealth is due. This is from al-Mabsut, Kitab al-Ikrah.
4
Analyze the analogy to khiyar al-shart
Since khul' does not allow khiyar al-shart (option to cancel), jest, which also indicates dissatisfaction, is similarly ineffective. The cause (sabab) of divorce and wealth is present, so the effect follows. This is a clear qiyas jaliy.
5
Derive the underlying usul principle
When the cause (sabab) for a ruling is established (here, the contract of khul' with stated compensation), external factors like jest or secret agreement do not negate the effect.
This principle relates to the Hanafi doctrine of 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' but with objectified intent.

2
Assess Instrumentality
Ask: Can the coerced person be a mere instrument (āla) for the coercer in performing this act? For example, killing with a sword — the coerced acts like a sword. Eating — the coerced cannot be an instrument because one cannot eat through another.
This is a physical/natural possibility, not a legal fiction.
3
Apply the Rule for Instrumentality Possible
If instrumentality is possible, the coerced's choice (ikhtiyar fasid) is overridden by the coercer's sound choice (ikhtiyar sahih). The act is attributed to the coercer, and liability falls on him.
Example: coercing someone to kill — qisas on coercer.
4
Apply the Rule for Instrumentality Impossible
If instrumentality is impossible, the coerced's corrupt choice stands. The act is attributed to the coerced, and the ruling applies to him.
Example: coercing someone to eat while fasting — the coerced's fast is broken.
5
Handle the Locus Change Exception
Even if instrumentality is possible, attribute the act to the coerced if making him an instrument would change the locus (mahall) of the act, such as forcing a muhrim to violate his own ihram. In such cases, the coercion becomes invalid and the act goes back to the coerced.
The change in locus contradicts the coercer's command, voiding the coercion.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Husami deals with the crucial usul al-fiqh topic of ikrah (coercion) and its effect on the attribution of actions. The author explains the Hanafi position in a nuanced manner, distinguishing three scenarios. First, when the coerced person (mukrah) can be considered a mere instrument (ala) of the coercer (mukrih)—like a sword in killing—the act is attributed to the coercer because the coerced's choice is corrupt (fasid) and thus nullified by the sound choice of the coercer. This applies to acts where the coerced can physically be an instrument, such as killing by cutting. Second, when the act is such that the coerced cannot be an instrument—like eating, speaking divorce, or intercourse—the act is attributed to the coerced because he cannot be an instrument for another. Here, the corrupt choice of the coerced prevails since there is no conflict; the coercer's sound choice does not override because it is impossible for the coercer to perform the act through the coerced as an instrument. Examples include breaking one's fast under coercion, divorce, manumission, and zina (where the coerced pays the mahr). Third, when the act is potentially instrumental but the locus (mahall) would change if instrumentality were assumed—like a muhrim being forced to violate his own ihram. In this case, the act remains attributed to the coerced because making him an instrument would transfer the violation to the coercer's ihram, which is impossible since one cannot violate another's ihram. Thus, the coercion becomes invalid, and the act goes back to the coerced. The passage then summarizes key rulings in Arabic: a person coerced to kill sins because murder is a violation of religion and he cannot be an instrument in that regard; a coerced seller's delivery is limited to him because it involves his own property; and a coerced manumitter is the actual speaker, but the legal effect of 'destruction' transfers to the coercer. Finally, the text presents the Shafi'i counterview: all verbal acts under coercion are void because they lack valid intention and choice. The Hanafi position, however, distinguishes between types of acts and holds that some coerced acts (like divorce) are effective, while others (like sale in some cases) may transfer liability to the coercer.

The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute on Coerced Verbal Acts
Analyzing the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on coerced verbal acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on the validity of coerced verbal acts and evaluate the Hanafi response.

Core Idea
Hanafis: coerced verbal acts are valid; Shafi'is: all are void. The difference lies in the role of intention.

This lesson examines the debate between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the effect of coercion on verbal transactions such as divorce, sale, and manumission. The Hanafi position distinguishes between the act of utterance and the legal consequence, while the Shafi'i position insists that valid speech requires free choice. We will analyze the arguments and the practical differences.

The Shafi'i argument: speech is valid only if it expresses inner intent; coercion vitiates both intent and free choice, so the speech is meaningless.
This is a direct challenge to the Hanafi ruling on coerced divorce.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi Response
Hanafis respond by distinguishing between types of acts. In speech, the coerced is the actual speaker; his utterance is real and can be intended even under coercion. The effect may be transferred to the coercer in some cases (like usurpation), but the act itself is not void.
The Hanafi response preserves the validity of the act while adjusting liability.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Husami deals with the crucial usul al-fiqh topic of ikrah (coercion) and its effect on the attribution of actions. The author explains the Hanafi position in a nuanced manner, distinguishing three scenarios. First, when the coerced person (mukrah) can be considered a mere instrument (ala) of the coercer (mukrih)—like a sword in killing—the act is attributed to the coercer because the coerced's choice is corrupt (fasid) and thus nullified by the sound choice of the coercer. This applies to acts where the coerced can physically be an instrument, such as killing by cutting. Second, when the act is such that the coerced cannot be an instrument—like eating, speaking divorce, or intercourse—the act is attributed to the coerced because he cannot be an instrument for another. Here, the corrupt choice of the coerced prevails since there is no conflict; the coercer's sound choice does not override because it is impossible for the coercer to perform the act through the coerced as an instrument. Examples include breaking one's fast under coercion, divorce, manumission, and zina (where the coerced pays the mahr). Third, when the act is potentially instrumental but the locus (mahall) would change if instrumentality were assumed—like a muhrim being forced to violate his own ihram. In this case, the act remains attributed to the coerced because making him an instrument would transfer the violation to the coercer's ihram, which is impossible since one cannot violate another's ihram. Thus, the coercion becomes invalid, and the act goes back to the coerced. The passage then summarizes key rulings in Arabic: a person coerced to kill sins because murder is a violation of religion and he cannot be an instrument in that regard; a coerced seller's delivery is limited to him because it involves his own property; and a coerced manumitter is the actual speaker, but the legal effect of 'destruction' transfers to the coercer. Finally, the text presents the Shafi'i counterview: all verbal acts under coercion are void because they lack valid intention and choice. The Hanafi position, however, distinguishes between types of acts and holds that some coerced acts (like divorce) are effective, while others (like sale in some cases) may transfer liability to the coercer.

Key Terms
إكراه (ikrah): Compelling someone to act against their will through threat of harm that overcomes their choice
آلَة (āla): An instrument through which another acts; the act is not attributed to the instrument but to the user
اختیار فاسد (ikhtiyar fasid): A choice that is legally defective due to coercion, not completely null but inferior to sound choice

Attribution in Coerced Sale: The Principle of Taslīm
Applying the principle of attribution to coerced sale and delivery

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of attribution (nisba) to determine who is legally responsible for acts performed under coercion in a sale contract.

Core Idea
In coerced sale, delivery is attributed to the coerced seller, not the coercer, or it becomes usurpation.

This lesson explores a key usul al-fiqh rule: the attribution of acts performed under coercion. Using the example of a coerced sale, we examine why Hanafi jurists insist that the delivery of the sold item (taslīm) is attributed to the coerced seller (mukrah) rather than to the coercer (mukrih). Understanding this principle is essential for correctly assigning legal consequences in contracts affected by duress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its context
The passage discusses two acts: the sale contract itself and the delivery of the sold item. Both occur under coercion. Note that the coerced person is the seller, not the buyer.
The sale is valid but defective due to lack of consent.
2
Trace the attribution claimed by the author
The author asserts that both the sale and the delivery are attributed to the coerced seller (mukrah). The sale is valid because it issued from a qualified person (the seller) in its proper locus (his property).
Attribution under coercion is not automatically transferred to the coercer.
3
Examine the counterfactual: the instrument (āla) argument
The passage considers what would happen if the coerced were considered an instrument of the coercer. If so, the delivery would be attributed to the coercer, and the act would become usurpation (ghasb) — a different legal category.
Changing attribution changes the essence of the act (dhāt al-fi'l).
4
Apply the principle of act essence
The ruling (hukm) of an act follows its essence. If the essence changes from sale to usurpation, the legal consequences change. Since the coercer commanded a sale, not usurpation, the attribution cannot be shifted.
This is a rational proof (dalīl 'aqlī) showing the necessity of attributing delivery to the coerced.

And similarly we said regarding the coerced in sale: The second subsidiary issue that is derived from the previous principle is this: if someone is forced to sell, and due to coercion he sells, and then he is forced to hand over the sold item, and he does hand it over, then both the sale and the delivery of the sold item will be attributed to the coerced [mukrah], and the rulings of the sale will pertain to the coerced. Therefore, when the buyer takes possession of the sold item, ownership is established for the buyer, although this ownership is defective (fāsid) because the consent of the coerced is missing. The contract of the coerced takes place because the act of sale issued from someone qualified for it and in its proper locus. Therefore, the coerced cannot be made an instrument (aala) for the coercer, such that the delivery would be attributed to the coercer. Because the coerced's handing over of the sold item to the buyer is a disposition (tasarruf) in his own sale, so this delivery will be attributed only to the coerced. This is because if, in the delivery, the coerced is made an instrument for the coercer, then the locus would also change, and the essence of the act would also change. The locus would change because the act of the coerced would become the act of the coercer, since the coerced has been made an instrument, and an act is not attributed to an instrument. Then it would be as if the coercer took the coerced's property in an un-Islamic way by threatening him, and that is usurpation (ghasb). Then the coercer would be the one disposing of usurped property, whereas the coercer had commanded the coerced to dispose through sale by delivery and completion. If the coerced is made an instrument, then disposition in usurped property becomes necessary, so this constitutes a change in the essence of the act. Changing the ruling of the act necessitates a change in the essence of the act. Therefore, we say that in the act of delivery, the coerced is not an instrument for the coercer; rather, the act of delivery is attributed solely to the coerced.

Commentary
This passage applies the same principle from the previous lesson to the case of a coerced sale (bay' bil-ikrah). The ruling: both the contract of sale and the physical delivery of the sold item are attributed to the coerced seller (mukrah), not to the coercer (mukrih). Consequently, the buyer acquires ownership (though defective due to lack of consent). The reasoning: the coerced is qualified to perform a sale, and the act occurs in its proper locus (his own property). He cannot be considered an instrument of the coercer, because if he were, the delivery would become an act of the coercer, which would make the coerced's property usurped (ghasb) by the coercer, contradicting the command to complete a sale. Changing the attribution would change the essence of the act from a sale to a usurpation, which is invalid. Thus, Hanafi fiqh maintains the attribution to the coerced.

Dual Aspect of Delivery in Coerced Sale
Answering an objection about dual attribution of delivery in coerced sale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two legal aspects of delivery (taslim) in a coerced sale and explain how each aspect leads to a different attribution of liability.

Core Idea
Delivery in a coerced sale is both sale completion and usurpation—attributed to different parties.

This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary addresses a subtle objection: If delivery (taslim) in a coerced sale can be seen as usurpation (ghasb) from the coercer's perspective, why isn't it attributed to the coercer? The commentator explains that delivery has two legal aspects, each with its own attribution. This lesson unpacks that dual-aspect reasoning, a hallmark of Hanafi usul al-fiqh's ability to assign multiple legal characterizations to a single act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objector argues that if delivery can be an instrument for the coercer (as usurpation), it should be attributed to the coercer. But the text attributes it to the coerced. Spot the flaw: the objector ignores the dual nature of delivery.
The objection is based on a partial view of the act.
2
Recognize the two aspects of delivery
Delivery has two aspects: (a) completion of the sale contract (shared consent) and (b) destruction of the coercer's right (usurpation). In aspect (a), the coerced acts for himself; in aspect (b), the coerced serves as an instrument of the coercer.
The same physical act carries two legal meanings.
3
Derive the consequence for liability
Because delivery can be seen as usurpation (aspect b), if the sold item is destroyed, the coercer can claim compensation (daman) from the coerced. This shows the coercer is treated as the true perpetrator of the usurpation.
The dual aspect is not contradictory; it is a nuanced solution.

And we have attributed it: This is an answer to a question. The question is: any act in which the coerced can be an instrument for the coercer can be attributed to the coercer. And delivery, in the sense that it destroys the coercer's right of possession and is usurpation, is capable of the coerced being an instrument for the coercer. But you (the commentator) did not make the coerced an instrument in this aspect either, therefore you attributed the act of delivery only to the coerced and not to the coercer. The essence of the answer is that delivery has two aspects: first, it completes the sale — when this aspect is considered, the coerced cannot be made an instrument for the coercer; rather, the act is attributed to the coerced, and in that case delivery is not usurpation. Second, it destroys possession and is usurpation — when this aspect is considered, the coerced can be an instrument for the coercer. Therefore, in this aspect, this delivery is destruction (itlaf) and usurpation, and the destruction is attributed to the coercer. For this reason, if the sold item is destroyed in the buyer's possession, the coercer (with fatha on the ra') has the option to claim from the coerced the value of the day of delivery as compensation (daman). In short, we have attributed the delivery, in terms of usurpation, to the coercer, and therefore in case of destruction of the sold item, the coercer is liable for compensation.

Commentary
This passage addresses an objection: since delivery (taslim) of a sold item can be seen as usurpation (ghasb) of the coercer's right, why isn't it attributed to the coercer? The answer: delivery has two distinct aspects. As the completion of a sale, it is attributed to the coerced seller because he is performing his own contract. As a destruction of the coercer's right (since the coercer ordered the sale under duress), it can be seen as usurpation, and in that aspect, the coerced is an instrument of the coercer, so the usurpation is attributed to the coercer. This dual attribution means that if the sold item is later destroyed, the coercer can claim compensation from the coerced. This nuanced view shows Hanafi fiqh's sophisticated treatment of coercion: an act can have multiple legal characterizations simultaneously.
