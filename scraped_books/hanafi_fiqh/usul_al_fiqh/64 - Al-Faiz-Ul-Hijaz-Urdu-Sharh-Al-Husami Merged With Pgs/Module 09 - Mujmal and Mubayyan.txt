Definition and Ruling of Mujmal
Defining mujmal, its ruling, and examples from riba and salah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mujmal, state its ruling (tawaqquf), explain the need for bayan from the Lawgiver, and apply this concept to the example of riba.

Core Idea
Mujmal requires suspension of judgment until the Lawgiver's explanation arrives.

This lesson examines the concept of mujmal (ambiguous text) in Hanafi usul. Understanding mujmal is crucial because it determines when a text cannot be acted upon without external clarification. The passage defines mujmal, explains its ruling of tawaqquf (suspension), and illustrates with Quranic and prophetic examples, showing how mujmal leads to bayan and then to qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of mujmal
Locate the definition in the Arabic text: 'وَضِدُّ الْمُفَشَرِ الْمُجْمَلُ وَهُوَ مَا ازْدَحَمَتْ فِيهِ الْمَعَانِي فَاشْتَبَهَ الْمُرَادُ بِهِ اشْتِبَاهَا لا يدرك الْابَبَيانِ مِنْ جِهَةِ الْمُجْمِلِ'. Note that meanings are crowded and the intended meaning is confused, knowable only through bayan (explanation) from the speaker.
Mujmal is the opposite of mufassar.
2
Understand the ruling of mujmal
Extract the ruling: 'وَحُكْمُهُ التَّوَقُّفُ فِيْهِ عَلَى اعْتِقَادِ حَقِيَةِ الْمُرَادِ بِهِ إِلَى أَنْ يَأْتِيَهُ الْبَيَانُ'. This means one must suspend judgment (tawaqquf) while believing the intended meaning is true, until explanation arrives.
Tawaqquf does not mean rejection; it is a pause for clarification.
3
Analyze the example of riba
The Quranic verse 'حُرِّمَ الرِّبَا' is mujmal because 'riba' (increase) is ambiguous. The Prophet (ﷺ) explained it with six commodities (wheat, barley, dates, salt, gold, silver), specifying conditions of equality and hand-to-hand delivery. After bayan, mujtahids derived the 'illa (effective cause) via qiyas.
This shows the progression: mujmal → bayan → qiyas.
4
Differentiate mujmal from mushkil
The text implies that mujmal cannot be resolved by seeking and reflection (talab wa ta'ammul), whereas mushkil can. This distinction is critical in Hanafi usul.
Common misunderstanding: students often confuse the two.
5
Apply the concept to salat, zakat, sawm
These words have multiple linguistic meanings; in Shari'ah, they become mujmal until the Prophet (ﷺ) explained them as specific acts of worship. This reinforces the need for prophetic bayan for mujmal terms.
After bayan, these terms become mubayyan (clear).

And the opposite of mufassar is mujmal, and it is that in which meanings are crowded so that the intended meaning is confused such that it cannot be known except through explanation (bayan) from the one who uttered it, like the verse of riba. Explanation: Mujmal is that speech in which many meanings are gathered, and because of that the intended meaning becomes so confused that it cannot be known from the wording itself, nor through seeking and reflection. Rather, until an explanation comes from the Lawgiver or from the one who uttered it, its intended meaning cannot be known. The example of mujmal is like a person who changes his appearance and dress and leaves his homeland, mixing among people; without asking people, his trace cannot be found. Similarly, in mujmal, until explanation comes from the one who uttered it, its intended meaning cannot be known. An example is the verse "He has forbidden riba." Riba means increase, but which increase? Because not every increase is forbidden, otherwise the door of trade would be closed. So riba is mujmal with respect to its intended meaning. And the Prophet (peace be upon him) clarified its intended meaning through six items: wheat, barley, dates, salt, gold, silver. In these six items, if it is with the same genus, then equality and hand-to-hand delivery are required. If there is increase on either side, it is riba. So riba was mujmal, and its intended meaning became known through the explanation from the Prophet (upon him blessings and peace). Then the mujtahids reflected and pondered to derive the 'illa (effective cause) of riba. According to the Hanafis, the 'illa is quantity (qadr) together with genus (jins); according to the Shafi'is, it is food (tu'm) and currency (thamaniyya); and according to the Malikis, it is storage (iddikhar) and sustenance (qiwam). Because the ruling of riba in the six items is mu'awwal (interpretable) and beyond the six items it is mushkil, and since mushkil requires reflection, the scholars derived the above-mentioned 'illas through reflection. Another example: words like salat, zakat, sawm, etc., are used in many meanings. In the Shari'ah, what are their intended meanings? This is not known, so these words become mujmal. Through the explanation of the Prophet (upon him blessings and peace), the intended meanings of salat, zakat, sawm—i.e., specific acts of worship—became known. The ruling of mujmal is that before explanation comes from the one who uttered it, it is wajib to hold the belief that whatever the intended meaning is, it is true.

Commentary
This passage defines mujmal (ambiguous text) and explains its ruling. The text is an expansion of the matn definition. Mujmal is the opposite of mufassar; it is speech in which multiple meanings are so tightly packed that the intended meaning cannot be determined from the wording itself, nor by seeking and reflection (talab and ta'ammul). It requires explanation (bayan) from the speaker. The ruling is tawaqquf (suspension of judgment) while maintaining belief that the intended meaning is true, until the explanation arrives. The example of riba is given: the Quranic prohibition of riba (increase) is mujmal because 'increase' is ambiguous. Which increase is forbidden? The Prophet (ﷺ) clarified by specifying six commodities and conditions of equality and hand-to-hand exchange. After this explanation, the ruling became clear, and mujtahids then derived the 'illa (effective cause) to extend the ruling to other items through qiyas. Note: The text mentions that for Hanafis, the 'illa is quantity plus genus; for Shafi'is, it is food and currency; for Malikis, it is storage and sustenance. Another example is words like salat, zakat, sawm which have many linguistic meanings but in Shari'ah were explained by the Prophet (ﷺ) to mean specific acts. The key is that mujmal cannot be resolved by human reasoning alone; it must await divine or prophetic explanation.

Key Terms
مجمل (mujmal): Speech whose intended meaning cannot be known except through external explanation.
بيان (bayan): Explanation from the Lawgiver that clarifies the intended meaning of a mujmal text.
توقف (tawaqquf): Suspension of judgment; not to form a final opinion until further clarification.

Definition and Example of Mujmal
Defining mujmal and illustrating with examples from riba hadith and ibadah terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mujmal, identify its characteristics, and explain why bayan from the Lawgiver is necessary before acting upon a mujmal text.

Core Idea
Mujmal texts require external clarification from the Lawgiver before any ruling can be applied.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, textual ambiguity is not a flaw but a category with its own rules. This passage defines mujmal, contrasting it with clear texts, and illustrates through the Quranic prohibition of riba and the terms for prayer and zakat. Understanding mujmal is foundational for proper istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Define mujmal linguistically and technically
Extract the definition from the text: a word or phrase that has multiple possible meanings, making the intended meaning unclear. Note that the ambiguity cannot be resolved by simply examining the wording or through intellectual effort.
Mujmal is a distinct type of ambiguous text in Hanafi usul.
2
Identify the characteristics of mujmal
List the conditions: multiple meanings, no contextual preference, cannot be resolved by personal reasoning (talb wa ta'ammul). The key is that clarification must come from the Lawgiver (shari‘).
This distinguishes mujmal from other ambiguous categories like mushtarak.
3
Analyze the riba example as mujmal
The Quran prohibits 'riba', which literally means increase. But not all increases are prohibited, so the term is mujmal. The Prophet clarified that riba applies to six specific commodities (gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, salt) when exchanged unequally and hand-to-hand.
This shows the process: mujmal -> bayan -> ruling.
4
Understand the ruling on mujmal before bayan
The text states that before clarification, one must believe that the intended meaning is true, but cannot act upon it. Action requires the bayan.
This is a key principle: belief without action until clarification.
5
Recognize the post-bayan step: deriving the ‘illah
After the Prophet's clarification, mujtahids derived the effective cause (‘illah) to extend the riba ruling beyond the six items. The passage lists the Hanafi, Shafi‘i, and Maliki ‘illahs.
This shows the transition from mujmal to qiyas.

Commentary
This passage defines mujmal (ambiguous text) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author explains that mujmal is a word or phrase that gathers multiple possible meanings, making the intended meaning unclear. The confusion cannot be resolved by simply examining the wording or by intellectual effort (talb wa ta’ammul); rather, clarification (bayan) must come from the Lawgiver (shari‘). The second example is the words salat, zakat, sawm, which are ambiguous in the Quran but clarified by the Prophet’s actions and sayings. The passage concludes with the ruling: before clarification, one must believe that whatever the intended meaning is, it is true and binding.

Key Terms
مجمل (mujmal): A word with multiple possible meanings, requiring external clarification from the Lawgiver to determine the intended meaning.
بیان (bayan): Clarification that removes ambiguity, typically from the Prophet or the Quran itself.
علة ('illah): The effective cause that links a ruling to its case, used in qiyas to extend rulings to new cases.

Sarih and Kinaya: Explicit and Implicit Speech in Legal Declarations
Definition and ruling of sarih and kinaya in legal declarations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sarih and kinaya expressions and state the legal ruling for each in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Sarih needs no intention; kinaya requires intention to be effective.

In Hanafi legal theory, words used in contracts and legal declarations are divided into two categories: sarih (explicit) and kinaya (implicit). This lesson clarifies each category with examples and explains why sarih does not require the speaker's intention while kinaya does. Understanding this distinction is essential for determining when a contract is formed or a legal effect takes place.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Locate the definitions of sarih and kinaya in the passage. Note that sarih includes words like 'I sold', 'I bought', 'I gifted', while kinaya is any implicit expression that does not directly name the contract.
2
Extract the ruling for sarih
The passage states that for sarih, the legal effect attaches immediately to the words themselves, and there is no need for intention (azima). Understand why: because the intended meaning is apparent (zahir).
Azima here means mental resolve or intention behind the utterance.
3
Extract the ruling for kinaya
For kinaya, action upon the word is not obligatory (i.e., the legal effect does not occur) except with intention (niyya). The reason is that the intended meaning is hidden (mustatir).

The Fourth Type: Al-Muqtada (the Implied)
Defines muqtada as an implied condition needed for textual validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the muqtada in a given text and explain how it is derived as a necessary condition for textual validity.

Core Idea
Muqtada is an implied condition necessary for the text's validity, equally definitive as explicit text but yielding in conflict.

In Hanafi usul, texts often require an unstated element for their legal or rational validity. This lesson introduces the concept of muqtada — the implied addition that the text demands. Understanding muqtada is crucial for correctly deriving rulings from commands and other speech acts, as it bridges explicit wording and implied legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Deconstruct the definition
Read the definition of muqtada: it is an extra element (ziyada) that the text requires for its own validity. Identify the key phrase: 'ثَبَتَ شَرُطًا لِصِحَةِ الْمَنْصُوصِ عَلَيْهِ' (established as a condition for the validity of the textually indicated matter).
Muqtada is not explicitly stated but is understood to prevent the text from being meaningless.
2
Identify the four components
The passage lists four elements: (1) muqtadi (active participle) – the text itself that requires the addition; (2) muqtada (passive participle) – the assumed addition; (3) iqtida' – the act of requiring; (4) hukm al-muqtada – the ruling established by the addition. Note the Arabic terms: المقتضى (اسم فاعل) and المقتضى (اسم مفعول).
The vowel difference is critical: muqtadi (كسر القاف) vs muqtada (فتح القاف).
3
Apply to the example
Analyze the example: 'Free your slave on my behalf for a thousand.' Why is a sale implied? Because emancipation requires ownership. The command (muqtadi) cannot be valid without a preceding sale (muqtada). The iqtida' is the need for the sale, and the ruling is the transfer of ownership.
The hadith 'لا عتق فيما لا يملك' reinforces the necessity of prior ownership.
4
Compare muqtada with dalala al-nass
The passage states that rulings from muqtada are equally definitive (qat'i) as those from explicit textual indication (dalala al-nass). However, in case of conflict, dalala al-nass takes precedence. Understand the example of Umm Qays's hadith: the explicit command 'wash it with water' (dalala al-nass) is preferred over the implied prohibition of other liquids (muqtada).
This hierarchy is a key feature of Hanafi usul: explicit wording over implied necessity.

Using the example of someone saying 'Free your slave on my behalf for a thousand,' the commentary shows that the command to emancipate implicitly requires a preceding sale to transfer ownership, because one cannot free what he does not own. Thus the sale is assumed (muqtada) and its ruling (ownership) is attached to the command. Finally, the passage addresses the strength of rulings from muqtada: they are considered definitive like rulings from explicit textual indication (dalala al-nass), but in case of conflict, the explicit indication takes precedence. The example of Umm Qays's hadith about washing menstrual blood illustrates this: the explicit command 'Wash it with water' indicates that water purifies, but the implied muqtada that other liquids are not permissible is overridden if there is another explicit proof allowing other liquids. This passage is essential for understanding how Hanafi scholars derive rulings from implied meanings and prioritize different types of textual evidence.

The Authority of Iqtida' and Its Difference from Mahdhuf
Explaining the epistemological weight of iqtida' and distinguishing it from mahdhuf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE that iqtida' al-nass yields qat'i rulings but is subordinate to dalala al-nass, and DISTINGUISH between muqtada (shar'an) and mahdhuf (lughatan) using the given sign.

Core Idea
Iqtida' gives qat'i knowledge, but dalala takes precedence in conflict; mahdhuf is a linguistic omission, muqtada is a shari'i necessity.

While iqtida' produces definitive rulings like dalala, the two can conflict. This lesson explores the priority rule and clarifies a common confusion: the difference between the implied meaning from iqtida' (muqtada) and a simple omitted word (mahdhuf).

Guided Path
1
Understand the qat'i nature of iqtida' rulings
The author states that rulings established by iqtida' are as definitive (qat'i) as those from dalala al-nass. This is a strong epistemological claim.
Qat'i means certain, not open to ijtihad in its source.
2
Learn the conflict resolution rule
In case of conflict, dalala al-nass (the clear indication of the text's purpose) overrides iqtida' al-nass (the implied necessity).
This is a tarjih rule within Hanafi usul.
3

The hadith 'Wash it with water' (ghasluha bi'l-ma') has two implications: iqtida' restricts to water, but dalala (purpose of removing impurity) allows other liquids. Dalala wins.

4
Distinguish muqtada from mahdhuf
Muqtada is implied by shari'a for textual validity; mahdhuf is a word omitted for linguistic conciseness. The sign: if inserting the omitted word disconnects the speech, it's mahdhuf (e.g., 'Ask the town' -> 'Ask the people of the town'). If insertion is natural, it's muqtada.
Example: 'Wa is'alu al-qaryah' (And ask the town) — you ask inhabitants, not the town itself. The omitted 'ahl' is mahdhuf.

Muqtada: without it the text fails shar'an; mahdhuf: without it the text is linguistically incomplete but understood.

Commentary
This passage discusses the concept of iqtida' al-nass (textual implication) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author defines iqtida' as the requirement by a text that something additional be implied for the text to be valid. He breaks it down into four components: (1) muqtadi (the explicit text itself), (2) muqtada (the implied meaning), (3) iqtida' (the act of requiring), and (4) hukm al-muqtada (the ruling that results). Thus, the Hanafi take the broader permission. Finally, the author distinguishes between muqtada (implied by shari'a) and mahdhuf (a word omitted for linguistic conciseness). The sign is that if the omitted word is inserted, the speech becomes disconnected (as in 'Ask the town' where 'town' is not literally asked but its inhabitants). This distinction ensures precision in usul analysis.

Types of Istithna': Muttasil and Munqati'
Definition and examples of connected and disconnected exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between istithna' muttasil and munqati', identify each type in a given statement, and explain the legal implication of each type in Quranic texts.

Core Idea
An exception either removes something included (muttasil) or is a separate statement (munqati') — both affect legal interpretation.

This passage defines the two fundamental types of exception (istithna') in Arabic and Hanafi usul. Understanding the difference between connected (muttasil) and disconnected (munqati') exception is essential for correctly interpreting legal texts. The classification turns on whether the excepted item is conceptually part of the general category. Let's walk through the analytical steps to master this distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure
Locate the exceptive particle 'illa' and identify the mustathna (excepted item) and mustathna minhu (general category).
2
Check genus inclusion
Determine whether the mustathna is of the same genus as the mustathna minhu and could logically be part of it. If yes, proceed to step 3; if no, go to step 4.
3
Classify as muttasil
If included, it is muttasil: the mustathna is removed from the mustathna minhu in ruling. Example: 'The people came except Zayd' – Zayd is among people.
4
Classify as munqati'
If not included, it is munqati': the mustathna is not part of the mustathna minhu; 'illa' functions like 'but' (lakinna). Example: 'The people came except a donkey' – donkey is not part of people.
5
Apply to Quranic example
Analyze the verse 'They are enemies to me except the Lord of the worlds'. Since Allah is not among idols, it is munqati'. If the people worshipped both, it could be muttasil.

This passage defines the two types of istithna (exception) in Arabic grammar and usul al-fiqh. Expression by Silence Equivalent to Speech
Defines first type of expression by necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the first type of expression by necessity and apply the Hanafi rule that silence can be equivalent to speech when contextual clues indicate a legal meaning.

Core Idea
Silence plus context can equal speech: the Hanafi principle of bayān ḍarūrat gives legal force to unspoken meanings when necessity and context combine.

This lesson introduces a foundational concept in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: expression by necessity (bayān ḍarūrat). The author exemplifies how silence, when accompanied by sufficient context, can carry the same legal weight as explicit speech. This principle expands the sources of law beyond literal texts, allowing jurists to derive rulings from implied meanings in revealed sources.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of expression by necessity
Recognize that bayān ḍarūrat occurs when silence or context provides a legal meaning that is considered as if it were explicitly spoken. This is not bare silence but silence accompanied by contextual clues that make the meaning clear.
Hanafi usul distinguishes between silence that is merely absence of speech and silence that functions as expression due to contextual necessity.
2
Analyze the Quranic example
Examine the verse 'And his parents inherit him, so for his mother is the third' (Quran 4:11). The opening clause 'his parents inherit him' establishes that both mother and father are heirs but does not specify shares. The subsequent clause specifies the mother's share as one-third.
The father's share is not mentioned explicitly; it is deduced from the context of partnership.
3
Identify the contextual clue
The contextual clue is the opening clause stating partnership. Without this clue, specifying the mother's share alone would not imply the father's share. The partnership creates the necessity to assign the remainder to the father.
Thus, silence about the father's share is transformed into an expression through the context of the opening clause.
4
Distinguish from bare silence
Contrast this with a scenario where only the mother's share is stated without any preceding partnership clause. In that case, the father's share would remain ambiguous, and silence would not be equivalent to speech.
The difference is the presence of a contextual element that makes the meaning inevitable.
5
Apply to other scenarios
Consider another example: The Prophet's ﷺ silence on a companion's action in his presence is often taken as approval (taqrīr). This is another form of bayān ḍarūrat where silence, coupled with the context of prophetic knowledge and opportunity to object, conveys legal permissibility.
This shows how the principle extends beyond textual interpretation to prophetic practice.

The Conjunction as Clarification: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i on Necessity-Based Bayan
Explaining the fourth type of necessity-based bayan and the dispute with Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the conjunction ('atf) can serve as a clarification (bayan) for an ambiguous number under necessity, and explain the Shafi'i counterargument and the Hanafi response.

Core Idea
When speech is frequent, the omitted noun is clarified by the conjunct—Hanafis accept it; Shafi'is do not.

This lesson examines a classic usul dispute about how far necessity (darurah) and custom ('urf) can override the literal ambiguity of a number. The Hanafis argue that when someone says 'I owe one hundred and a dirham', the conjunction itself clarifies that the hundred are also dirhams, avoiding lengthy speech. Imam al-Shafi'i objects, maintaining that the hundred remains ambiguous until the speaker explains it. Understanding this disagreement reveals deeper methodological differences between the schools.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the ruling: the 'atf (conjunction) serves as bayan (clarification) for the hundred. So 'one hundred and a dirham' means 101 dirhams.
This applies only to measurable/weighable items.
2
Identify the opponent and their view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the hundred remains mujmal (ambiguous) and the conjunction does not clarify it; the speaker's own intention is authoritative.
3
Follow the opponent's proof
Al-Shafi'i analogizes to the case of 'one hundred and a garment', where the garment does not specify the hundred. The same should apply to dirhams.
4
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafis distinguish: the deletion of the noun is customary only for items generally owed in the dhimma (makil/mawzun), not for specific items like clothes. Thus the analogy is invalid.
The Hanafi rely on 'urf and darurah.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In practice, a Hanafi judge rules that 101 dirhams are owed; a Shafi'i judge would ask the speaker to clarify his intention.
This difference has direct impact on litigation.

2

Hanafis argue that the conjunct provides the explanation (bayan) for the omitted qualifier. Thus 'one hundred and a dirham' is understood as 'one hundred dirhams and a dirham,' totaling 101 dirhams. The basis is the frequent custom of omitting the tamyiz in numbers when the context clarifies it, as in transactions like 'for one hundred and ten dirhams.'

Hanafis respond that the custom of omitting the qualifier applies only to items that are commonly established as obligations in the dhimma, namely measured (makil) or weighed (mawzun) goods. Cloth, being neither, is not subject to this custom except in salam sales. Hence the analogy to cloth fails.
This response limits the scope of the rule and shows how custom operates as a source of declaration only within its recognized domain.

Extract the usul principle
The passage illustrates that bayan al-darurah via omission is valid when supported by widespread custom ('urf) and the need for brevity, but it is restricted to items that are typically owed in the dhimma (makyulat, mawzunat). This is a key example of how Hanafi usul integrates 'urf into the interpretation of speech acts.

The Cause Resembling a Cause ('Illat Mushabbah bi al-Sabab)
Explains the nature of a cause resembling a cause with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'illat mushabbah bi al-sabab and identify it in the examples of nisab, terminal illness (marad al-mawt), and purchase of a close relative (shira' al-qarib), explaining why each is not a true cause.

Core Idea
A cause resembling a cause establishes a ruling only when an additional condition is present.

In Hanafi usul, not all causes ('ilal) are immediate and independent. Some are called 'illat mushabbah bi al-sabab—they look like a cause and are treated as one in naming, but their effect depends on a later condition. This lesson dissects this concept through the examples given in the text: nisab for zakat, terminal illness for restricted transactions, and purchase of a relative for manumission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause and the ruling
For each example, state the description claimed as the cause (e.g., nisab) and the ruling it is said to cause (obligation of zakat).
The ruling is what the cause is supposed to bring about.
2
Check if the ruling is immediate or delayed
Determine if the ruling takes effect at the moment the cause exists or requires another condition. For nisab, zakat obligation is delayed until the year passes and increase exists.
If delayed, the cause is not a true cause.
3
Identify the intermediary condition
Find the additional condition that must occur for the ruling to apply. For terminal illness, the condition is connection with death. For purchase, the intermediary is ownership.
This condition distinguishes a resembling cause from a true cause.
4
Classify the cause
If the ruling is immediate and the cause is independently effective, it is a true 'illah. If the ruling is delayed and depends on an intermediary, it is an 'illat mushabbah bi al-sabab.
The text also notes degrees of resemblance; the cause may still be stronger if the intermediary arises from the cause itself.
5
Apply to new examples
Given a new scenario (e.g., shooting an arrow as cause of death), determine whether it is a true cause or resembling cause.
Use the same criteria: delay and intermediary.

Similarly for terminal illness: like nisab, terminal illness is a cause in name and meaning for changing rulings, not a cause in legal effect. The meaning is that before terminal illness a person has full discretion over his wealth, but during terminal illness only the right to donate up to one-third remains, because the heirs' right becomes attached. In any case, terminal illness is a cause in name because it is legislated to change from freedom and permissibility to restriction and prohibition; and a cause in meaning because it is effective in preventing transactions beyond one-third; and not a cause in legal effect because the ruling of terminal illness – i.e., restriction from transactions – is not established immediately, but rather is established by connection of the illness with death; the ruling is delayed until connection with death, and the description of connection is not an independent cause, so terminal illness is a cause resembling a cause. Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Husami discusses the concept of 'illat mushabbah bi al-sabab (cause resembling a cause) and the rules for substituting one thing for another in legal reasoning. The author begins by explaining that nisab (the minimum threshold for zakat) is not a true cause but resembles one, because the obligation of zakat is attached to it from the start of the year, yet actual payment requires the passage of a year (hawalan al-hawl) and the presence of increase (nama'). Similarly, terminal illness (marad al-mawt) is a cause in name and meaning for restricting transactions beyond one-third, but its ruling is delayed until the illness connects with death, so it resembles a cause more strongly than nisab. Purchase of a close relative (shira' al-qarib) is also a cause resembling a cause for manumission, because ownership of a relative triggers freedom, but the ownership itself is from the purchase, so there is an intermediary. Then the text states a general rule: when a ruling depends on two effective descriptions, the one that occurs later in existence is considered the cause (in legal effect and meaning), while the earlier one has only a semblance of causality. This is applied to riba: in riba al-nasi'ah (sale on credit), there is a semblance of excess (fadl) even if no actual excess, so prohibition is established by that semblance. Finally, travel (safar) is a cause for concessions (rukhsah) in name and legal effect but not in meaning; the actual effective element is hardship (mashaqqah), but the law substitutes travel for hardship to make things easy. This substitution (iqamah al-shay' maqam ghayrihi) has two forms: (1) substituting the motivating cause (sabab da'i) for the motivated effect (mad'u), as in travel and illness; and (2) substituting the evidence (dalil) for the evidenced (madlul), as in a report about love being treated as actual love for purposes of divorce, or purity (tahr) being treated as need (hajah) for permitting divorce. The passage is crucial for understanding how Hanafi usul treats causes that are not direct and how substitution operates in legal reasoning.

Key Terms
علت ('illah): A cause that is effective in establishing a ruling, either directly or as a cause resembling a cause.
سبب (sabab): A cause that is not intrinsically effective but is substituted for the true cause due to ease.
اقامۃ الشیء مقام غیرہ (iqamah al-shay' maqam ghayrihi): Substituting one thing for another in legal reasoning; two types: (1) motivating cause for motivated effect, (2) evidence for evidenced.

Substitution (Iqamah al-Shay' Maqam Ghayrihi) and the Rule of Two Descriptions
Explains substitution and the rule of two descriptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of substitution (iqamah al-shay' maqam ghayrihi) recognized in Hanafi usul and apply the rule that when a ruling depends on two effective descriptions, the later one in existence is the cause in legal effect.

Core Idea
The law substitutes a motivating cause for the real cause, and evidence for the evidenced, to ease application.

Hanafi usul introduces a flexible approach to causation: sometimes the Shari'ah treats a description as a cause even though the real effective element is something else. This is called iqamah al-shay' maqam ghayrihi — substitution. The text explains two types and also gives a rule for when two effective descriptions exist: the later one becomes the cause in legal effect. This lesson unpacks these mechanisms using the examples of travel, illness, riba, and divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of substitution
First type: substituting the motivating cause (sabab da'i) for the motivated effect (mad'u), e.g., travel for hardship. Second type: substituting evidence (dalil) for the evidenced (madlul), e.g., the report about love for actual love.
The text gives clear examples for each.
2
Analyze the travel example
Travel is a cause for concessions in name and legal effect, but not in meaning because the real effective element is hardship. Travel is substituted for hardship to make the ruling easy.
This is the first type of substitution.
3
Analyze the rule of two effective descriptions
When a ruling is attached to two descriptions that both have an effect, the one that occurs later in existence is the cause in legal effect and meaning. The earlier description has only a semblance of causality.
Applied to riba: in riba al-nasi'ah, the later description (excess) is the cause; the earlier (delay) has semblance.
4
Apply to the divorce example
In the hadith 'Whom I love, her is free', the report about love is substituted for actual love. This is the second type of substitution: evidence for evidenced.
The evidence (report) stands in for the actual cause (love).

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Husami discusses the concept of 'illat mushabbah bi al-sabab (cause resembling a cause) and the rules for substituting one thing for another in legal reasoning. The author begins by explaining that nisab (the minimum threshold for zakat) is not a true cause but resembles one, because the obligation of zakat is attached to it from the start of the year, yet actual payment requires the passage of a year (hawalan al-hawl) and the presence of increase (nama'). Similarly, terminal illness (marad al-mawt) is a cause in name and meaning for restricting transactions beyond one-third, but its ruling is delayed until the illness connects with death, so it resembles a cause more strongly than nisab. Purchase of a close relative (shira' al-qarib) is also a cause resembling a cause for manumission, because ownership of a relative triggers freedom, but the ownership itself is from the purchase, so there is an intermediary. Then the text states a general rule: when a ruling depends on two effective descriptions, the one that occurs later in existence is considered the cause (in legal effect and meaning), while the earlier one has only a semblance of causality. This is applied to riba: in riba al-nasi'ah (sale on credit), there is a semblance of excess (fadl) even if no actual excess, so prohibition is established by that semblance. Finally, travel (safar) is a cause for concessions (rukhsah) in name and legal effect but not in meaning; the actual effective element is hardship (mashaqqah), but the law substitutes travel for hardship to make things easy. This substitution (iqamah al-shay' maqam ghayrihi) has two forms: (1) substituting the motivating cause (sabab da'i) for the motivated effect (mad'u), as in travel and illness; and (2) substituting the evidence (dalil) for the evidenced (madlul), as in a report about love being treated as actual love for purposes of divorce, or purity (tahr) being treated as need (hajah) for permitting divorce. The passage is crucial for understanding how Hanafi usul treats causes that are not direct and how substitution operates in legal reasoning.

Key Terms
اقامۃ الشیء مقام غیرہ (iqamah al-shay' maqam ghayrihi): Substituting one thing for another in legal reasoning; two types: (1) motivating cause for motivated effect, (2) evidence for evidenced.
سبب داعی (sabab da'i): The motivating cause that brings about an effect; substituted for the real cause for ease.
مدعو (mad'u): The motivated effect; the real intended outcome that is substituted by a cause.

The Four Invalid Forms of Bequest to an Heir
Usul definition: four forms of invalid bequest.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four forms of invalid bequest to an heir, and explain the Hanafi dispute on literal bequest.

Core Idea
A bequest to an heir is prohibited; even disguised forms are invalid, and quality becomes compensable under suspicion.

The Shariah allocates fixed shares to heirs, so a sick person cannot benefit an heir through a bequest. However, one may try to circumvent this through sales, acknowledgments, explicit bequests, or uneven exchanges. This passage classifies four such forms and clarifies when they are invalid, including an intra-Hanafi dispute on sales at fair price.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
List the four forms: literal (surah), meaning (ma'ni), real (haqiqa), and doubtful (shabah). Note that the first three are clearly invalid; the fourth involves suspicion of favoritism.
This taxonomy helps in recognizing disguised transactions.
2
Analyze the literal bequest dispute
Examine the difference between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Sahibayn) regarding a sale to an heir at a fair price. Abu Hanifa considers it invalid because it is a disguise; Sahibayn allow it if the price is equivalent.
This is a classic example of applying sadd al-dharai.
3
Understand the doubtful bequest
In ribawi goods, exchanging superior for inferior of same genus is normally valid (quality not compensable). Here, due to suspicion of bequest, quality becomes compensable, mirroring rules for minors.
This illustrates how suspicion can change a ruling (sadd al-dharai).
4
Apply the principle to new scenarios
Given a transaction that benefits an heir, classify it into one of the four forms and determine the Hanafi ruling, considering the intra-madhhab difference.
Practice connecting the taxonomy to real cases.

ولما تولى الشرع :- سوال مقدر کا جواب ہے کہ جب ثلث مال میں شریعت نے اختیار دیا ہے، تو مریض ثلث مال میں اپنے وارث کے لئے وصیت کر سکتا ہے، کیونکہ ثلث اس کا اپنا حق ہے، حالانکہ وصیت وارث کے لئے جائز نہیں ہے۔ اس کا جواب دے رہے ہیں کہ وارث کے لئے جب اللہ تعالیٰ نے خود وصیت کردی ، سب کے حقوق متعین کردئے تو اس مریض کی وارث کے لئے وصیت باطل قرار دی جائے گی، وصیت کی چار صورتیں ہیں (۱) صورةُ (۲) معنی (۳) حقیقہ (۴) اور شبہہ ، وصیت کی چاروں صورتیں باطل ہیں ،سب کی مثالیں دیکھیں۔ صورۃ وصیت کی مثال: یہ ہے کہ مریض اپنے وارث کو کوئی معین چیز من مثل کے عوض یا ثمن غیر مثل کے عوض نیچے تو اگر چہ اس کی قیمت لے لی ہے مگر صورۃ وصیت ہے کہ وہ مخصوص چیز وارث کو دیدی تقسیم میراث میں وہ معین چیز اس وارث کے حصہ میں آ بھی سکتی تھی اور نہیں بھی آسکتی تھی تو امام صاحب کے نزدیک یہ بیج جائز نہیں ہے، کیونکہ صورۃ وصیت ہے، صاحبین کے نزدیک ثمن مثل سے یہ بیج جائز ہے کیونکہ اس سے ورثاء کا حق باطل نہیں ہوتا ہے۔ معنی وصیت :- یہ ہے کہ مریض وارث کے لئے قرض کا اقرار کرے کہ فلاں وارث کا میرے ذمہ اتنا قرض ہے، یا یوں کہے کہ صحت کے زمانہ مین جو میرا قرض فلاں وارث کے ذمہ تھا وہ میں نے وصول کر لیا ہے، تو یہ صورۃ تو اقرار ہے مگر معنی وصیت ہے کہ مریض اس طرح اس وارث کو مالی فائدہ پہنچانا چاہتا ہے یہ امکان ہے۔ حقیقی وصیت یہ ہے کہ مریض کسی وارث کے لئے کھلے الفاظ میں وصیت کرے۔ شبہ وصیت یہ ہے کہ مریض اموال ربویہ میں اپنا جید مال اسی کے جنس کا ردی مال لیکر کسی وارث کو نیچے، جیسے ایک کیل عمدہ گیہوں ایک کیل ردی گیہوں کے بدلہ نیچے، تو یہ نا جائز ہوگا کیونکہ شہبۂ وصیت موجود ہے، ممکن ہے کہ مریض اپنے وارث کو جود وعمدگی کا نفع پہنچانا چاہتا ہے، حالانکہ دو ایک جنس کی چیزوں میں عمدہ اور ردی کی بچ جائز ہے،ربو نہیں ہے، اس میں جودت کا اعتبار نہیں ہے لیکن یہاں رفع تہمت کے لئے جودت متقوم ہوگی ، ردی کے مقابلہ میں جودت کی الگ سے قیمت معتبر ہوگی، جیسے باپ یا وصی کو یہ اختیار نہیں ہے کہ بچہ کی عمدہ چیزا اپنی گھٹیا چیز سے بیچ کریں، کیونکہ بچوں کے حق میں بھی جودت متقوم ہے، الگ سے اس کی قیمت ہوگی ، حالانکہ عام معاملات میں جودت غیر متقوم ہے، مگر یہاں تہمت کی وجہ سے جودت متقوم ہوگی ، اس کا اعتبار ہوگا۔
And since the Shariah has given authority in one-third: this is a response to an implied question: when the Shariah has given authority in one-third, then a sick person can make a bequest for his heir from one-third, because one-third is his own right, even though a bequest for an heir is not permissible. The response is that when Allah Himself has made a bequest for the heir (i.e., fixed their shares), the sick person's bequest for the heir will be declared invalid. Bequest has four forms: (1) literal (surah), (2) meaning (ma'ni), (3) real (haqiqa), and (4) doubtful (shabah). All four forms are invalid. See examples. Example of literal bequest: the sick person sells a specific item to his heir for an equivalent price or for a non-equivalent price, even though he takes its price, but it is a literal bequest because he gave that specific item to the heir; in the division of inheritance, that specific item could or could not have come into the heir's share. According to Imam Abu Hanifa, this sale is not valid because it is a literal bequest. According to the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), this sale is valid with the equivalent price because the rights of the other heirs are not nullified. Bequest in meaning: the sick person acknowledges a debt for the heir, saying that such an heir has such a debt on me, or says that during health, the debt that the heir owed me, I have received it. So outwardly it is an acknowledgment, but in meaning it is a bequest, as the sick person intends to give financial benefit to the heir in this way. Real bequest: the sick person explicitly makes a bequest in clear words for an heir. Doubtful bequest: the sick person, in the case of ribawi goods, gives his good-quality property to an heir while taking inferior quality of the same genus, like one measure of good wheat for one measure of poor wheat. This is invalid because the suspicion of bequest exists: it is possible that the sick person wants to give the benefit of quality to his heir. Even though in general transactions of the same genus, exchange of good for bad is permissible, there is no riba, and quality is not considered, here for the sake of avoiding suspicion, quality becomes compensable; the value of quality against poor quality will be considered separately. Just as a father or guardian does not have the authority to sell a child's good property for his own inferior property, because for children also quality is compensable, its value is separate, even though in general transactions quality is not compensable. Here, due to suspicion, quality becomes compensable and will be considered.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the Hanafi rule regarding bequests (wasiyya) made by a sick person to his legal heir. Although the Shariah permits the sick person to dispose of one-third of his estate, a bequest to an heir is prohibited by the Quranic verse that sets fixed shares. The author explains that even if the sick person tries to benefit his heir indirectly, it is invalid. Four categories are enumerated: (1) Literal (surah) bequest: a sale to the heir at a fair price but of a specific item that might have been allocated to the heir anyway. Imam Abu Hanifa deems it invalid because it is a disguised bequest; the Sahibayn allow it if the price is fair because no harm to other heirs. (2) Meaning (ma'ni) bequest: an acknowledgment of debt or receipt of debt that effectively gives the heir money. (3) Real (haqiqa) bequest: explicit verbal bequest. (4) Doubtful (shabah) bequest: an exchange of similar goods where the sick person gives superior quality for inferior, which is normally valid but here considered invalid due to the suspicion of favoritism. In such cases, quality becomes compensable (mutaqawwim) to avoid this, similar to rules protecting minors' property.

Key Terms
صورۃ وصیت (surat al-wasiyya): A literal bequest: a transaction that outwardly is a sale but in substance benefits an heir at the expense of other heirs.
شبۂ وصیت (shabah al-wasiyya): A doubtful bequest: an exchange of similar goods where the sick person gives superior quality for inferior, creating suspicion of favoritism.
