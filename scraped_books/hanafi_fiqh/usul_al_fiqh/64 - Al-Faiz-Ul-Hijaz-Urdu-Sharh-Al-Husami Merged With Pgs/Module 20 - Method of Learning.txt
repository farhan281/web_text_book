Acceptance of News from a Fasiq: Worldly vs. Hadith
Distinguishing conditions for accepting a fasiq's report in worldly matters and hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi conditions for accepting a fasiq's report in worldly matters versus hadith transmission, and apply the qiyas reasoning underlying this distinction.

Core Idea
A fasiq's report is accepted with scrutiny in necessities, but rejected in hadith due to availability of just narrators.

In Hanafi usul, the acceptance of a report from a fasiq (sinner) is not uniform; it depends on the context and necessity. This passage clarifies when scrutiny (taharri) can salvage such a report—in worldly matters like food purity—and why it cannot in prophetic traditions. The reasoning is rooted in a careful balance between necessity and the availability of reliable alternatives.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contexts
The passage distinguishes between worldly matters (e.g., halal/haram of food, purity of water) and hadith transmission. First, clearly separate these two domains.
2
Understand the condition of necessity
In worldly matters, fasiqs are often the only people present in markets and wilderness, creating a necessity to rely on their reports. In hadith, just narrators are plentiful, so no necessity exists.
This necessity is not absolute; one can fall back on the original ruling (istishab).
3
Recognize the role of taharri (scrutiny)
Even when necessity exists, the fasiq's report is not blindly accepted; the listener must perform taharri to reach a dominant presumption of truth. Without taharri, the report is not credible.
Taharri is not required when the reporter is just ('adl).
4
Analyze the underlying qiyas
The reasoning uses analogy: in worldly matters, acceptance with taharri is due to necessity and absence of accusation of lying. In hadith, the illa (necessity) is absent, so the ruling differs. This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) based on the presence or absence of the effective cause.
The fasiq is not accused of lying because his report binds himself as well.
5
Apply the rule to a new scenario
Given a scenario (e.g., a fasiq reports that a well is impure, no just person available), state the ruling: accept with taharri. For a hadith narrated by a fasiq, reject even with taharri.

Commentary
This passage is a rule-based explanation of when a fasiq's (sinner's) report is accepted. The commentator clarifies a key distinction in Hanafi usul: in worldly matters like determining halal/haram food or pure/impure water, where upright witnesses are scarce, a fasiq's report is accepted provided the listener exercises scrutiny (taharri) to reach a dominant presumption. However, in hadith transmission, the fasiq's report is rejected outright even with scrutiny, because the necessity is absent—there are plenty of reliable narrators. The commentator then addresses an objection: if necessity exists, why not accept unconditionally? The reply is that the necessity is not absolute; one can fall back on the original ruling (istishab) that things are permissible and pure until proven otherwise. Hence, the fasiq's report is given partial weight but requires scrutiny. Another objection: if accepted with scrutiny in worldly matters, why not in hadith? The answer is the abundance of just narrators, so no need for reliance on fasiqs in religious matters. This shows the Hanafi method of balancing necessity with caution.

Key Terms
فاسق (fāsiq): A person who commits major sins or persists in minor sins; lacking moral reliability.
تحري (taḥarrī): Scrutiny; effort to ascertain the truth without direct knowledge, leading to a dominant presumption.
عدل (ʿadl): A just person; one who avoids major sins and does not persist in minor sins, making him reliable for testimony and narration.
ضرورة (ḍarūrah): Necessity; a situation where one cannot proceed without relying on the available source, even if imperfect.

Bayan: Definition and Its Five Types
Opening of the chapter on Bayan defining its five categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define bayan in Hanafi usul, state its five types, and distinguish Bayan al-Taqrir and Bayan al-Tafsir with examples.

Core Idea
Bayan is the mechanism by which ambiguous or general revelation receives its precise legal meaning.

This passage opens the chapter on Bayan (elucidation), a foundational concept in usul al-fiqh. After discussing the primary proofs (Book and Sunnah), the author explains how these proofs are clarified. Bayan is not limited to ambiguous texts; it also reinforces clear texts against misinterpretation. Learn the five categories that every usul student must memorize.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Statement
The author begins: all the proofs (Quran and Sunnah) admit of bayan. Understand that bayan is a necessary process for deriving rulings from revelation.
This applies to both clear and unclear texts.
2
Define Bayan Terminologically
Bayan linguistically means to make manifest or become manifest. In usul, it is the act of making the intended meaning clear to the addressee.
The Quranic verse 'He taught him elucidation' (55:4) is cited.
3
List the Five Types
Memorize the five categories: Bayan al-Taqrir, Bayan al-Tafsir, Bayan al-Taghyir, Bayan al-Tabdil, and Bayan al-Darurah. Note that the passage only elaborates on the first two.
The remaining three are discussed later in the text.
4
Analyze Bayan al-Taqrir
This type strengthens speech to remove possibilities of metaphorical or specific interpretations. Example: 'wa la tairun yatiru bi-janahayhi' – 'tair' could mean a swift thing metaphorically, but 'bi-janahayhi' restricts it to birds. Also, 'kulluhum' after 'al-malaikatu' removes specification.
This bayan is valid whether connected (mawsul) or separate (mafsul).
5
Analyze Bayan al-Tafsir
This type clarifies ambiguous (mujmal) or homonymous (mushtarak) expressions. Example: 'aqimu al-salah' is ambiguous regarding method; the Prophet's practice clarifies it. Similarly, the homonym 'quru' is clarified by the hadith specifying it means menstruation.
This bayan is essential for applying general commands.

Commentary
This passage is the formal opening of the chapter on Bayan (elucidation) in usul al-fiqh. The author first states that all the proofs (hujaj) previously discussed—namely the Quran and Sunnah in their various types—admit of bayan. He then defines bayan linguistically and terminologically: it is the act of making the intended meaning manifest to the addressee. The core of the lesson is the classification of bayan into five types: (1) Bayan al-Taqrir: confirming or strengthening speech to remove possibilities of metaphorical or specific interpretations. Example: the Quranic phrase 'wa la tairun yatiru bi-janahayhi' uses 'tair' which could metaphorically mean a swift thing, but 'bi-janahayhi' restricts it to birds. Also, the plural 'malaikah' in 'fa-sajada al-malaikatu' could indicate only some angels, but the subsequent word 'kulluhum' removes that possibility. This type is valid whether the clarifying statement is connected (mawsul) or separate (mafsul) from the original, by scholarly consensus. (2) Bayan al-Tafsir: clarifying what is ambiguous (mujmal) or homonymous (mushtarak). Example: the command 'aqimu al-salah' is ambiguous regarding the method, but the Prophet's practice (sunnah) explains it. Similarly, the word 'quru' is homonymous between purity and menstruation, but the hadith specifies it means menstruation. The passage then mentions that Bayan al-Taqrir and Bayan al-Taghyir share a common rule (that they are valid both mawsul and mafsul), though the text cuts off before completing the discussion. This classification is fundamental for understanding how the primary sources are interpreted: bayan is the mechanism by which general, ambiguous, or potentially metaphorical texts are given their precise legal meaning.

Key Terms
البيان (al-bayān): Making the intended meaning manifest to the addressee.
بيان تقرير (bayān al-taqrīr): Strengthening speech to remove possibilities of metaphorical or specific meaning.
بيان تفسير (bayān al-tafsīr): Clarifying that which is ambiguous (mujmal) or homonym (mushtarak).
