Following Prophetic Actions with Unknown Manner
Explaining the Hanafi position on ittiba' when the manner is unknown, with rebuttal of opposing views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that ittiba' is the default principle for the Prophet's actions and derive the ruling when the manner (jihah) is unknown.

Core Idea
When the Prophet's manner of action is unknown, the Hanafi school assumes permissibility (ibaha) until evidence specifies otherwise.

This passage addresses a key usul dispute: how should Muslims follow the Prophet's actions when the legal status (jihah) is unknown? The Hanafi school, following al-Jassas, offers a practical solution: we take the lowest rank of his actions, which is ibaha. This contrasts with the suspension of al-Daqqaq and al-Ghazali and the absolute following of Malik. Understanding this principle is essential for deriving correct rulings from the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the core question: what is the ruling when we know the Prophet did an action but do not know whether it was wajib, mandub, or mubah for him?
2
Identify the Hanafi position
Locate the view attributed to al-Jassas: if the manner is known, follow that manner; if unknown, act at the lowest rank (ibaha). Recognize the evidence: the Quranic command of ittiba' and the principle that ittiba' is the default.
3
Identify opposing views
Find the views of Abu Bakr al-Daqqaq and al-Ghazali (suspension until manner known) and Imam Malik (absolute following unless evidence of prohibition). Understand their reasoning.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Note the Hanafi response: suspension is impractical; absolute following ignores possible specificity. The middle ground of ibaha is safe and maintains the principle of ittiba'.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
See the practical difference: for an unknown manner, Hanafi says mubah, al-Daqqaq says suspended, Malik says wajib. This affects how a Muslim approaches such actions.

The author says: Dhillat (humiliating action) is not from the chapter of ittiba'. That is, if an action occurred from the Prophet (S.A.W.) as dhull, the ummah will not follow him in that action. Rather, it is necessary that an explanation be attached to the action of dhull indicating that it is dhull. This explanation sometimes comes from the doer himself, as when Musa (A.S.) said: 'This is from the work of shaytan.' That is, Musa (A.S.) regretted his action and sought forgiveness. And sometimes this explanation comes from Allah, as when Adam (A.S.) committed dhull by eating from the tree, Allah said: 'And Adam disobeyed his Lord and erred,' and 'And Adam forgot, and we did not find in him resolve.'
Yes, here a question may arise: The author mentioned four categories that are followed, and also mentioned the action that is not followed, such as dhull. So the author should also mention haram and makruh, because like dhull, they are also not followed. The answer is: Haram and makruh are not found in the actions of the Prophet (A.S.), because the prophets (A.S.) are protected from major sins according to the majority of Muslims, and according to us (the Maturidis) they are also protected from minor sins, contrary to the Ash'aris. And haram and makruh fall under major and minor sins. In short, their occurrence from prophets is not possible. Hence, there is no need to mention them. 'And there is disagreement concerning his other actions. The correct view is what al-Jassas (R.A.) said: What we know of the actions of the Messenger (S.A.W.) to have occurred upon a certain manner (jihah), we follow him in performing it upon that manner. And what we do not know upon which manner he did it, we may do it upon the lowest rank of his actions (S.A.W.), which is ibaha (permissibility), because following (ittiba') is the principle. So adhering to it is obligatory until evidence of its specificity to him arises.' Translation: There is disagreement concerning following all the actions of the Prophet (A.S.). The correct view is what Abu Bakr al-Jassas said: The actions of the Messenger (S.A.W.) that we know upon which manner they occurred, we will follow him in doing that action in that manner. And the action we do not know in which manner he did it, it is permissible for us to do that action upon the lowest rank of his actions, and the lowest rank is ibaha. Because following is the principle, so it is obligatory to act upon this principle until evidence arises that the action is specific to him.
Explanation: All those actions of the Messenger (S.A.W.) that occurred from him intentionally (bil-qasd), not unintentional actions like turning at the end of two rak'ats of Asr due to forgetfulness, nor naturally occurring actions like eating and drinking, nor actions specific to him like marrying more than four, the obligation of tahajjud and chasht, etc., following is not necessary in these. Apart from these, all actions that occurred from him intentionally are disputed. Abu Bakr al-Daqqaq and Imam al-Ghazali say: Until it is known in which manner he did the action—on the manner of ibaha, or nadb, or wujub—we will suspend judgment. Imam Malik says: Absolute following is obligatory until evidence of prohibition arises.
The author says: The correct view is that of Imam Abu Bakr al-Jassas: For the actions of the Messenger (S.A.W.) regarding which we come to know in which manner he did them, we will follow him in those actions in that manner. So if an action was mubah for him, it will be mubah for us; if it was mandub for him, it will be mandub for us; if it was wajib for him, it will be wajib for us. And for the action regarding which it is not known in which manner he did it, we will perform that action upon the lowest rank of his actions, and the lowest rank is ibaha. Because he cannot do haram or makruh, necessarily that action will be mubah. And considering it mubah, we will act upon it. Because following is the principle, as per His statement: 'Indeed in the Messenger of Allah you have a good example.' Hence, that action will be mubah until evidence arises that it is specific to him.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute concerning the obligation of following the Prophet's actions (ittiba') when the manner (jihah) of the action is unknown. The author first distinguishes actions of humility (dhull) that require an explanatory context to indicate they are not to be followed. He then answers a potential objection: why did the author not mention haram and makruh as not to be followed? Because prophets are infallible (isma), so such actions do not occur. The main dispute: what about the Prophet's other actions? The correct opinion (according to al-Jassas, endorsed by the author) is that if we know the manner, we follow that manner; if we do not know, we take the lowest rank among his actions, which is ibaha (permissibility). This is because ittiba' is the established principle, and we adhere to it until evidence of specificity arises. The commentary explains that actions are categorized: unintentional (sahw), natural (tab'i), specific to the Prophet (khassa), and general intentional actions. For general intentional actions without known manner, we stop at ibaha. This differs from Abu Bakr al-Daqqaq and al-Ghazali (who suspend) and Malik (who obliges absolute following). The Hanafi position is practical: follow the known, assume permissibility for the unknown.

Key Terms
اقتداء (Iqtida'): Following or imitating the Prophet's actions in religious matters
ذلت (Dhillat): An action indicating humility or a lapse, not to be followed without explanatory context
اباحت (Ibaha): Permissibility; the lowest rank of the Prophet's actions, assumed when manner is unknown

Prophetic Ijtihad and Divine Confirmation
Stating the Hanafi position on the Prophet's use of ijtihad and confirmation as decisive proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that prophetic ijtihad, when divinely confirmed, yields a qati' ruling and distinguish it from the ijtihad of other scholars.

Core Idea
Prophetic ijtihad confirmed by divine approval becomes decisive (qati'), unlike scholars' ijtihad which remains zanni.

In this passage, the author clarifies the Hanafi position on whether the Prophet Muhammad (S.A.W.) used ijtihad in deriving legal rulings. The correct view is that he did practice ijtihad when revelation ceased, but with a key safeguard: he was protected from error, and any ijtihad he was 'confirmed' upon (i.e., no divine correction) becomes a definitive proof. This lesson unpacks that rule and its implications for usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The passage addresses whether the Prophet used ijtihad (personal reasoning) in matters not covered by explicit revelation.
Some schools deny prophetic ijtihad entirely; this passage affirms it under certain conditions.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi view is that the Prophet did practice ijtihad when he ceased to receive revelation on a particular matter.
This is based on the belief that the Prophet's actions are guided and protected.
3
Identify the condition of confirmation
The passage emphasizes that the Prophet 'was not confirmed upon error' (la yuqarru 'ala al-khata). If he was confirmed (iqrar) on an ijtihad, it means divine approval.
Confirmation is indicated by the lack of correction or explicit approval from Allah.
4
Explain the consequence: decisive indication
When confirmed, the ijtihad becomes a dalalah qati'ah (decisive indication) of the ruling, equivalent in certainty to revelation.
This is unlike the ijtihad of scholars, which remains zanni (speculative).
5
Contrast with others' ijtihad
Others (non-prophets) rely on ra'y (opinion), which does not achieve qati' status. The passage compares this to ilham (inspiration) which is also a proof but not decisive.
This distinction is crucial in Hanafi usul: only prophetic ijtihad can yield qati' rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a usul rule regarding the Prophet's use of ijtihad. The author states that the sunan include an explanation of how the Prophet derived legal rulings via ijtihad. There is disagreement on this issue, but the correct view (according to the Hanafis) is that the Prophet did indeed practice ijtihad when revelation stopped concerning matters he encountered. Importantly, he was not left on error; if he was confirmed (i.e., divine confirmation or lack of correction) on a particular ijtihad, that becomes a qati' (definitive) indicator of the ruling. This is unlike the statements of others (non-prophets) that are based on ra'y (personal opinion), which are not qati'. This confirmation is compared to ilham (inspiration), which is a hujjah (proof). The implication is that prophetic ijtihad, once confirmed, carries the weight of decisive evidence, unlike the ijtihad of later scholars.

The Rule of Iqtida' bi Af'al al-Nabi
Ruling on iqtida' when manner is known or unknown

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the Prophet's actions into categories (intentional, involuntary, natural, specific) and apply the Hanafi rule that when the legal manner of an intentional action is unknown, the default is ibahah until evidence of specificity.

Core Idea
Follow the Prophet's intentional actions; when manner unknown, presume ibahah.

The Prophet's actions (af'al) serve as a primary source of Shari'ah. However, not all his actions carry the same legal weight. Some were specific to him, others were involuntary or natural. For his intentional actions, we must know their legal status (wajib, mandub, mubah) to emulate correctly. This lesson clarifies the Hanafi position on iqtida' and the default ruling when the manner is unknown.

Guided Path
1
Categorize the Prophet's actions
Distinguish between actions done intentionally for legislation, those done involuntarily (sahw), naturally (eating), and those specific to him (e.g., marrying >4). Only intentional actions fall under iqtida'.

Hanafi Position on Previous Shari'as
Determining the binding nature of previous shari'as for Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the binding nature of previous sharī‘ahs and apply the criterion of nakīr to determine whether a previous ruling is binding.

Core Idea
A previous sharī‘ah is binding on Muslims only if mentioned in the Qur'ān or Sunnah without disapproval (nakīr).

The question of whether Muslims must follow laws revealed to previous prophets is a key usul dispute. This lesson presents the Hanafi resolution: previous rulings mentioned with approval become part of our Prophet's sharī‘ah, while those mentioned with disapproval are abrogated. Understanding this principle is essential for proper istinbāṭ from the Qur'ān.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ikhtilaf
Recognize the two opposing views: one holds that previous sharī‘ahs are abrogated entirely, the other that they are binding if adopted.
The Hanafis take a middle position with a clear criterion.
2
State the Hanafi rule
The rule: any previous law mentioned in the Qur'ān or Sunnah without disapproval (nakīr) is binding as part of our sharī‘ah.
Key term: nakīr = clear censure or abrogation.
3
Examine the examples
Look at the verse of qiṣāṣ (5:45) – mentioned without nakīr → binding. Contrast with the food prohibitions on Jews (4:160, 6:146) – mentioned with nakīr → not binding.
The presence of nakīr is decisive.
4
Understand the rationale
Allah's mention without disapproval indicates the ruling is approved for all times; mention with disapproval shows it was a temporary punishment.
5
Apply the rule
When encountering a Qur'ānic verse or hadith that recounts a previous law, check for any hint of nakīr. If absent, adopt it.
This is a practical tool for istinbāṭ.

Dalalat al-Nass: The Textual Basis for Permitting Value in Zakat
Response to Shafi'i objection on giving value in zakat via textual indication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between dalalat al-nass and ta'lil in deriving rulings, and apply the Hanafi methodology to the zakat value case.

Core Idea
Dalalat al-nass permits value in zakat because the text's purpose is fulfilling diverse needs, not the specified item itself.

This passage addresses a classic dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is: can you give cash instead of a goat in zakat? The Hanafi response clarifies that the permission comes from the text's own indication (dalalat al-nass), not from independent reasoning (ta'lil). This distinguishes the Hanafi usul approach that recognizes implicit meanings in the text. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping how Hanafi jurists derive rulings while maintaining textual authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the objection
The ruling in question is the permissibility of giving value (price) instead of the specified animal (goat) in zakat. The Shafi'i objection is that this changes the explicit text of the hadith ("For every five camels, one goat") and violates the conditions of qiyas.
The Shafi'i position is based on strict adherence to the literal wording.
2
Locate the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response states that the change in ruling is not due to ta'lil (reasoning by 'illa) but due to dalalat al-nass (textual indication) or iqtida' al-nass (implication of the text).
Dalalat al-nass is a recognized source of law in Hanafi usul.
3
Analyze the textual indication
The passage argues that Allah has taken responsibility for the provision of all creatures (Quran 11:6) and has made zakat a means to fulfill the diverse needs of the poor. The specified item (goat) cannot meet all those needs, but its value can. Therefore, the text itself indicates that value is the intended payment.
The Quranic verse and the hadith's purpose together create a textual indication beyond the literal wording.
4
Distinguish the two rulings
The passage clarifies that there are two rulings: (1) the permissibility of substitution (istibdal) – this comes from dalalat al-nass; (2) the requirement that the substitute be suitable for the poor's possession and need – this comes from ta'lil. Ta'lil does not change the text but establishes the suitability condition.
This two-step reasoning preserves the role of ta'lil without altering the original text.
5
Apply the analogy
The passage ends with an analogy: just as the obligation is removal of impurity, and water is a tool suitable for removal; and glorification of Allah is the obligation, and takbir is a tool for glorification; similarly, in zakat, the obligation is to provide for the poor, and value is a suitable tool. After ta'lil, the suitability remains as it was.
The analogy illustrates that ta'lil establishes the suitability of the tool, not the obligation itself.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi response to a Shafi'i objection concerning the permissibility of paying the value (price) instead of the specified animal (e.g., a goat) in zakat. The Shafi'i argument claims that by using ta'lil (reasoning by 'illa) to allow value, one changes the explicit text (nass), thus violating the fourth condition of qiyas (that the ruling of the asl cannot be changed). The Hanafi response clarifies that the change is not due to ta'lil but to dalalat al-nass (textual indication) or iqtida' al-nass (implication of the text). The text argues that the purpose of zakat is to provide for the diverse needs of the poor; a specified item like a goat cannot fulfill these diverse needs, but its value can. Therefore, the Lawgiver's intent is the value, and the mention of the specified item is merely to estimate the amount. This change is derived from the text's own indication, not from independent reasoning. The ta'lil is then preserved to establish a second ruling: that the substitute must be suitable for the poor's possession and need. The passage ends with an analogy from purification (water as a tool for removing impurity) and prayer (takbir as a tool for glorification), illustrating that after ta'lil, the suitability of the tool remains. This demonstrates the Hanafi usul principle that ta'lil can coexist with textual indication, each serving a different purpose.

Key Terms
تعلیل (taʿlīl): The act of deriving or stating the effective cause (ʿilla) for a legal ruling; in this passage, it refers to reasoning that permits giving value based on the purpose of fulfilling need.
دلالة النص (dalālat al-naṣṣ): Textual indication; the implicit meaning derived from the text that goes beyond its literal wording but is contextually understood. In this passage, it is the source for allowing substitution of the specified item.
صلاحية (ṣalāḥiyya): Suitability or capacity; the quality that makes a substitute acceptable for fulfilling the legal purpose. Here, it refers to the substitute's ability to meet the poor's needs and be possessed by them.

Invalid Ignorance: Mujtahid Opposing Clear Texts
Usul dispute with three case studies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a mujtahid's opinion is considered invalid jahl due to contradiction with a clear text (Quran or mashhur hadith), and apply the Hanafi criterion of preferring mashhur over gharib.

Core Idea
A mujtahid's opinion opposing a clear text or preferring a rare hadith is rejected jahl.

This lesson examines the third type of jahl in Hanafi usul: a mujtahid's ignorance that manifests as contradicting an explicit text (nass) or preferring a rare (gharib) hadith over a well-known (mashhur) one. The source provides three concrete fiqh examples to illustrate this principle. By understanding these, you'll see how the Hanafis evaluate the strength of evidence and why some ikhtilaf is considered invalid.

Commentary
This passage discusses the third type of ignorance: that of a mujtahid who contradicts clear texts (nusus) or prefers rare (gharib) hadith over well-known (mashhur) ones. The text provides three specific examples to illustrate this. First, the case of Dāwūd al-Iṣfahānī (founder of the Ẓāhirī school) who allowed selling an umm walad (a slave mother of a master's child) based on a hadith of Jābir, but this contradicts the more authentic hadith of Ibn ʿAbbās that such a slave becomes free upon the master's death; thus the sale is invalid. Second, Imām al-Shāfiʿī's view that deliberately omitting the basmala when slaughtering an animal still renders the meat lawful, using a weak hadith and an analogy with forgetfulness. However, this directly contradicts the Qur'anic verse 6:121, and the analogy with forgetfulness does not hold because the underlying cause (ʿillah) is different. Third, the issue of qasāma (oaths in case of unknown murder): the Hanafīs hold that qasāma never leads to qiṣāṣ (retaliation), only to diya (blood money) being imposed on the locality if they refuse to swear; Imām al-Shāfiʿī's old opinion allowed qiṣāṣ if the heirs swear. This demonstrates that even great mujtahids can commit jahl if they rely on weak evidence against clear nusus. The Hanafī position is to follow the well-authenticated (mashhūr) hadith and the explicit Qur'an.

Key Terms
حديث مشہور (ḥadīth mashhūr): A hadith that is well-known and widely transmitted, considered stronger than a rare (gharib) hadith.
ام ولد (umm walad): A female slave who has borne a child to her master.
قسامة (qasāma): A legal procedure where fifty oaths are taken from the people of a locality when a murder victim is found and the killer is unknown.

Checkpoint
What is the Hanafi position on selling an umm walad (slave-girl who has borne her master's child)?
A) It is permissible with the consent of the master.
B) It is not permissible; she becomes free upon her master's death.
C) It is permissible only if she is not the mother of the master's child.
D) It is makruh but valid.

Key Takeaway
An opinion that contradicts a clear text (Quran or mashhur hadith) is not valid ijtihad but rejected jahl in Hanafi usul.
