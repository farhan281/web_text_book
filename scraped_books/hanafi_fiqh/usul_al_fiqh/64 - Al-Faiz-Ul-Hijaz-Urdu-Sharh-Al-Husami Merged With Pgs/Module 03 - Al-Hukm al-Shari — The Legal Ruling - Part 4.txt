Hazl Nullifies Acknowledgment and Pre-emption Surrender
Explains that hazl voids acknowledgments and surrender of pre-emption rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that hazl voids acknowledgments and surrender of pre-emption to analogous cases, and explain the qiyas linking hazl to khiyar al-shart.

Core Idea
Hazl indicates non-existence, so acknowledgments and surrenders in jest are void — akin to khiyar al-shart voiding sale.

This passage from Al-Husami extends the effect of hazl from the category of origination (insha') to reports (ikhbar). Acknowledgment, being a report of a past event, is void if made in jest because jest signals that the reported event never occurred. Similarly, the surrender of the right of pre-emption, though an act of relinquishment, is analogized to a sale and is void if done jestingly, just as delivery and payment are void during the option period. The author uses clear qiyas to establish that hazl destroys the legal significance of these acts, preserving the original rights.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal acts affected by hazl
The passage specifically mentions acknowledgment (iqrar) and surrender of pre-emption (taslim shuf'ah). Acknowledgment is a report about a past event; surrender is a relinquishment of a right.
Also mentions discharge of debt as another case.
2
Understand the rationale (illah) for voidness
Hazl indicates that the thing reported or the consent is non-existent. For acknowledgment, the reported event did not occur. For surrender, the consent is not genuine.
The illah is the lack of reality (haqiqah) behind the statement.
3
Trace the qiyas used for surrender of pre-emption
Surrender of pre-emption is analogized to a sale. When a sale is made with a conditional option (khiyar al-shart), delivery and payment are void because consent is incomplete. Similarly, hazl prevents full consent, so surrender in jest is void.
The asl is the voidness of delivery/payment during khiyar; the far' is surrender of pre-emption in jest.
4
Apply the rule to analogous cases
Similarly, discharging a debtor from his debt in jest is void, as stated in the passage. This extends the rule to any act of transfer or acknowledgment made in jest, preserving the original obligation.
The rule is general: any report (ikhbar) affected by hazl is void.

And as for acknowledgment, jest nullifies it: The author, having finished the discussion of the first type (origination), is now discussing the second type (reports). If someone makes an acknowledgment in the manner of jest and frivolity, whether it is an acknowledgment of matters that do not admit of abrogation like divorce, emancipation, etc., or of matters that admit of abrogation like sale, etc., then this acknowledgment will be void. For example, two people decide that they will make a mock acknowledgment of sale in front of people, whereas in reality there is no sale between them, then due to jest the acknowledgment will be void. Because jest indicates that the thing reported is non-existent, since they reported by way of jest, it has no reality. And acknowledgment is based on the existence of the thing reported. So by way of jest, the existence of the thing reported is not proven. Therefore the acknowledgment will be void. The author says: Just as acknowledgment becomes void due to jest, similarly after the demand of gift (talab mawahabah) and demand of witnessing (talab ishhad), the surrender of pre-emption (taslim shuf'ah) will also be void. The detail is that the demand of pre-emption is of three types: (1) Talab mawahabah: the pre-emptor demands immediately upon knowledge of sale; if he delays, the right is lost. (2) Talab taqrir wa ishhad: after the first demand, the pre-emptor bears witness before the seller, buyer, or agent that he claims his right of pre-emption. This demand establishes the right, and subsequent delay does not void it. (3) Talab khusumat: the pre-emptor files a case before the judge. After this detail, understand the issue: If the pre-emptor, after talab mawahabah and talab ishhad, surrenders the right of pre-emption in jest (i.e., renounces his right), then due to jest, the surrender is void, and the right remains. Because surrender of pre-emption is in the meaning of sale, and sale (delivery of the sold property and payment of price) is void due to khiyar al-shart (option of condition), because khiyar prevents full consent to the contract. Similarly, jest is like khiyar al-shart. So just as surrender due to khiyar is void, so too due to jest. Similarly, if a debtor is discharged from his debt by way of jest, this discharge is void and the debt remains.

Commentary
This passage covers the second category of hazl: reports (ikhbar), specifically acknowledgment (iqrar). The rule is clear: any acknowledgment made in jest is void, regardless of the subject matter, because jest indicates the reported thing does not exist. An acknowledgment is only valid when the thing being reported actually occurred. After that, the author extends the same principle to the surrender of pre-emption rights (shuf'ah). He first explains the three types of talab shuf'ah (demands) for context, then states that if after the first two demands the pre-emptor surrenders his right in jest, the surrender is void. The reasoning is that surrender of pre-emption is like a sale, and sale is voided by khiyar al-shart; hazl is analogous to khiyar. Therefore, the right of pre-emption remains. Even discharge of debt in jest is void. This shows the consistent Hanafi position that hazl nullifies any act that reports or transfers rights, with the exception of faith and certain other matters.

خیار شرط (khiyar al-shart): Option of condition; a contractual stipulation allowing cancellation within a specified period.

Core Idea
Jest does not affect insha' rulings — faith, divorce, and emancipation are effective regardless of playful intent.

In this passage, the author explains a key Hanafi usul principle: matters of faith are originated acts (insha') that cannot be undone by jest. Just as a compulsion-induced conversion is valid, a jest-induced conversion is also legally effective. This lesson unpacks the concept of insha' and its immunity to hazl.

Effect of Jest (Hazl) on Pre-emption and Debt
Analogy between hazl and khiyar al-shart

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that hazl (jest) is analogous to khiyar al-shart in voiding legal acts of sale and its derivatives, and identify the illa (prevention of final consent) that causes the voidness.

Core Idea
Hazl voids transactions of sale and their analogs due to lack of genuine consent.

This passage addresses a subtle point in Hanafi usul: the effect of jest on certain legal acts. While hazl does not nullify words of divorce or manumission, it does void transactions that are essentially sales, such as surrender of pre-emption right and cancellation of debt. The author uses qiyas to analogise hazl to the option of condition (khiyar al-shart), both of which prevent final acceptance (ridā bil-hukm). In this lesson, we will unpack the analogy and derive the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal acts
The text mentions two acts: surrender of shuf'a (pre-emption right) and discharge of debt. Both are performed in jest. The question is: do they take effect?
Both acts are in the meaning of sale – shuf'a surrender is like selling your right; debt discharge is like giving something for nothing.
2
Establish the analogy
The author analogizes hazl to khiyar al-shart. Just as khiyar al-shart voids delivery and payment in a sale (because the party retains the option to cancel), hazl also indicates that the party does not genuinely consent. The illa is the lack of ridā bil-hukm (satisfaction with the transaction).
Khiyar al-shart suspends finality; hazl indicates lack of seriousness.
3
Derive the ruling
Since the illa (lack of final consent) is present in both khiyar and hazl, the ruling from the asl applies to the far': the legal act is void, and the original right remains. Therefore, surrender of shuf'a in jest is void; the pre-emption right persists. Similarly, debt cancellation in jest is void; the debt remains.
This is an application of qiyas jaliy.
4
Apply to similar cases
This principle can be extended to any transaction in the nature of sale performed in jest. For example, jokingly waiving compensation for a service would also be void.

Ruling on a Disbeliever's Jestful Declaration of Faith
States that a disbeliever jesting with Islam becomes Muslim

Learning Objective

Travel as a Cause of Concession in Hanafi Usul
Defining safar as a cause of concession affecting qasr and fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that travel permits qasr and postponement of fasting but does not justify breaking an intended fast, and distinguish it from illness.

Core Idea
Travel is a chosen cause of concession, not a necessity; it permits easing worship but not abandoning it.

In this passage from Al-Husami, the author explores how travel (safar) functions as one of the 'awarij (legal excuses) that bring about concessions (takhfif) in worship. The key nuance is that travel is a voluntary act, not a necessity like illness. Consequently, if a person begins fasting with the intention to fast, subsequent travel does not permit breaking the fast. This lesson unpacks the rule and its application.

Core Idea
Error after ijtihad excuses in worship but not in harming others; it removes criminal intent but leaves residual liability.

The concept of error (khatta') is a critical tool in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, as it mediates between strict liability and excuse. In this passage, al-Husami defines khatta' and explains its differential treatment depending on whether the rights involved belong to Allah or to human beings. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying rulings in worship, criminal law, and transactions.

Commentary
This passage defines khatta' (error) as a legal concept and explains its effects. The author first states that error is considered a valid excuse for rights of Allah (huquq Allah) when it occurs after ijtihad (effort to determine the correct ruling). In Hanafi jurisprudence, acting mistakenly (khata') significantly impacts liability. While the doer is absolved from sin and criminal penalties like hadd or qisas, they remain responsible for compensation (diya/kaffara). Commentary
This passage from Al-Faiz-ul-Hijaz, a commentary on Al-Husami, defines ikrah (duress) as the seventh and last 'arid (impediment) affecting the mukallaf. The commentary explains that ikrah does not nullify legal capacity (ahliyyah) because the intellect remains intact; rather, it affects consent (rida) and choice (ikhtiyar) in varying degrees. The author distinguishes two types: ikrah kamil (complete duress) which corrupts choice through threats of death or limb loss, and ikrah qasir (incomplete duress) which removes consent but not choice, through threats of beating or imprisonment. A third type (threatening family) is noted as having no legal effect. The key principle is that ikrah does not render the mukrah's actions void per se; instead, it alters the ruling: some acts become permissible (rukhsah) under complete duress (e.g., uttering disbelief, breaking prayer/fast, destroying property, violating ihram), while others remain forbidden (killing, wounding, adultery). For women, enabling adultery under duress is treated differently because lineage is not cut off. The effect on contracts: transactions requiring consent (sale, lease) are invalidated by ikrah, while acknowledgments (iqrar) are also invalid because they require the existence of the thing informed. In khul', if duress is attached to accepting the payment, divorce occurs but the payment is not owed, analogous to divorce of a minor. This is contrasted with jest (hazl), which only prevents consent to the ruling, not the cause.

Key Terms
الإكراه (al-ikrāh): Compelling a person to do something they would not willingly do, via threats that overcome resistance.
الإكراه الكامل (al-ikrāh al-kāmil): Complete duress: threat of death or loss of limb; corrupts choice (ikhtiyar) and establishes coercion (ilja').
الإكراه القاصر (al-ikrāh al-qāṣir): Incomplete duress: threat of beating or imprisonment; removes consent (rida) but not choice.

Definition and Types of Ikrah (Coercion)
Defines ikrah and enumerates its types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ikrah, distinguish between its two main types (kamil and qasir), and identify the third type with no legal effect.

Core Idea
Ikrah corrupts choice (complete) or removes consent (lesser); both affect rulings but do not remove legal capacity.

Coercion (ikrah) is a key impediment in Hanafi usul that affects the validity of actions. This lesson introduces the concept and its classification into complete coercion (ikrah kamil) and lesser coercion (ikrah qasir), as well as a third type without legal effect. Understanding these distinctions is essential for analysing when a coerced person is excused or remains liable.

Guided Path
1
Define ikrah
Recall the technical definition: compelling someone to act against their will, such that they would not have acted if not coerced.
The term ikrah (إکراه) implies forcing with a credible threat.
2
Identify the two main types
List the two types: ikrah kamil (threat of death/limb) and ikrah qasir (threat of beating/imprisonment). Explain how each affects choice and consent.
Ikrah kamil corrupts ikhtiyar (choice); ikrah qasir removes rida (consent) but preserves choice.
3
Acknowledge the third type
Note the type not discussed in the text: coercion by threat to family (parents, wife, son). This type does not corrupt choice or remove consent, so it has no legal effect.
Some later Hanafi scholars give it limited effect, but the author omits it.
4
Apply the definition to examples
From the passage, understand how the type of coercion determines whether an action becomes permissible, obligatory, or remains forbidden.
The examples given later will be explored in the next lesson.

The woman's action is separate from the man's in the matter of permission, because the lineage is not severed from the woman. So the woman enabling the man to commit zina is not like murder, unlike the man. And for this reason, if a woman [is coerced?] she will establish doubt in repelling the hadd punishment, not the man. From all these details, it is established that coercion does not have the ability to invalidate anything among all sayings and actions, except by a proof that changes it to the like of the action of the one not coerced. And the effect of coercion will appear when coercion is complete in changing the attribution, and the effect of coercion will appear when coercion is lesser in causing the loss of consent. So due to coercion, that action becomes invalid which is susceptible to annulment and depends on consent, like sale and lease. And all acknowledgments [iqrar] will not be valid, because the validity of acknowledgments requires the existence of the thing acknowledged, and there is proof that the thing acknowledged does not exist. Explanation: The seventh and last impediment from the impediments of legal capacity is ikrah. Ikrah is called compelling a person to do something he does not want to do, that if he were not compelled, he would not do it. Then ikrah is of two types: (1) complete coercion (ikrah kamil) and (2) lesser coercion (ikrah qasir). Complete coercion is that he is compelled by the threat of destruction of life or limb. This type of coercion corrupts choice and necessitates compulsion, meaning the coerced person is forced to do the action. Lesser coercion is that he is coerced by the threat of beating or imprisonment. This type does not corrupt choice, i.e., choice remains, but there is no consent for the action. There is a third type that the author did not mention because rulings are not related to it, and that is being coerced by threatening to imprison parents, wife, or son. In this type, neither choice is corrupted nor consent is nullified. The [Italiyah?] say that coercion, whatever its type, neither contradicts capacity for obligation nor capacity for performance, nor does it nullify the divine address in any case, because reason remains in this state, and the coerced person is in trial and test, and trial can only happen when the servant has power to do or not do, and from trial the address is established. Do you not see that he is wavering: the coerced person is tested and trial is realized. The proof of this is that the coerced person, in the action to which he is compelled, is wavering between obligation, prohibition, permissibility, and concession. That is, four types of rulings are related to the coerced person regarding the action he is compelled to do: (1) Sometimes doing it becomes obligatory, like if coercion is for eating carrion, then its prohibition is lifted and eating it becomes obligatory to save life; if he does not eat and dies, he deserves punishment because he intentionally destroyed himself. (2) Sometimes the action is forbidden, like if coercion is for zina or murder, and he does it to save life, it is forbidden. (3) Sometimes doing it is permissible, like if coercion is for breaking fast in Ramadan, then breaking fast is permissible for him. (4) Sometimes doing it is a concession, like if coercion is for uttering words of disbelief, then with heart's affirmation, it is a concession to utter with the tongue, although the resolve is not to say it, even if life goes. The fact that these four rulings are related to the coerced person is proof that the coerced person is legally responsible and addressed, otherwise these rulings would not be related to him. In any case, the coerced person is responsible for these rulings, and sometimes he becomes sinful for doing them, and sometimes he deserves reward. So if coercion is for killing someone or for committing zina with a woman, there is absolutely no concession; if he does it, he will be sinful. And if coercion is for eating carrion, drinking wine, or eating pork with complete coercion, then the prohibition is completely lifted; eating them becomes obligatory; if he does not eat and dies, he will be sinful. Benefit: The difference between ibaha (permissibility) and rukhsah (concession) is that in rukhsah, the prohibition of the act remains, but the coerced person is allowed to do it, like the concession to utter words of disbelief. In ibaha, the prohibition of the act is lifted and the act becomes lawful, like the permissibility of eating carrion in case of coercion. Legal Effects of Ikrah: Fard, Haram, Mubah, Rukhsah
Explains the four possible rulings under coercion with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four possible rulings under coercion and distinguish between ibaha (permissibility) and rukhsah (concession).

Core Idea
Under coercion, an act can become obligatory, remain forbidden, become permissible, or be a concession — each with distinct implications.

Coercion does not simply excuse wrong actions; it changes the ruling in nuanced ways. The passage outlines four possible rulings for a coerced person: fard, haram, mubah, and rukhsah. Understanding when an act becomes obligatory (like eating carrion to save life) versus when it remains forbidden (like murder) is crucial for applying Hanafi law correctly.

Explanation: The seventh and last impediment from the impediments of legal capacity is ikrah. Ikrah is called compelling a person to do something he does not want to do, that if he were not compelled, he would not do it. Then ikrah is of two types: (1) complete coercion (ikrah kamil) and (2) lesser coercion (ikrah qasir). Complete coercion is that he is compelled by the threat of destruction of life or limb. This type of coercion corrupts choice and necessitates compulsion, meaning the coerced person is forced to do the action. Lesser coercion is that he is coerced by the threat of beating or imprisonment. This type does not corrupt choice, i.e., choice remains, but there is no consent for the action. There is a third type that the author did not mention because rulings are not related to it, and that is being coerced by threatening to imprison parents, wife, or son. In this type, neither choice is corrupted nor consent is nullified. The [Italiyah?] say that coercion, whatever its type, neither contradicts capacity for obligation nor capacity for performance, nor does it nullify the divine address in any case, because reason remains in this state, and the coerced person is in trial and test, and trial can only happen when the servant has power to do or not do, and from trial the address is established. Do you not see that he is wavering: the coerced person is tested and trial is realized. The proof of this is that the coerced person, in the action to which he is compelled, is wavering between obligation, prohibition, permissibility, and concession. That is, four types of rulings are related to the coerced person regarding the action he is compelled to do: (1) Sometimes doing it becomes obligatory, like if coercion is for eating carrion, then its prohibition is lifted and eating it becomes obligatory to save life; if he does not eat and dies, he deserves punishment because he intentionally destroyed himself. (2) Sometimes the action is forbidden, like if coercion is for zina or murder, and he does it to save life, it is forbidden. (3) Sometimes doing it is permissible, like if coercion is for breaking fast in Ramadan, then breaking fast is permissible for him. (4) Sometimes doing it is a concession, like if coercion is for uttering words of disbelief, then with heart's affirmation, it is a concession to utter with the tongue, although the resolve is not to say it, even if life goes. The fact that these four rulings are related to the coerced person is proof that the coerced person is legally responsible and addressed, otherwise these rulings would not be related to him. In any case, the coerced person is responsible for these rulings, and sometimes he becomes sinful for doing them, and sometimes he deserves reward. So if coercion is for killing someone or for committing zina with a woman, there is absolutely no concession; if he does it, he will be sinful. And if coercion is for eating carrion, drinking wine, or eating pork with complete coercion, then the prohibition is completely lifted; eating them becomes obligatory; if he does not eat and dies, he will be sinful. In ibaha, the prohibition of the act is lifted and the act becomes lawful, like the permissibility of eating carrion in case of coercion. Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive treatment of ikrah (coercion) in Hanafi law. It begins by stating specific rulings: with complete coercion, the prohibition of carrion, wine, and pork is removed; and permission is given for uttering disbelief, invalidating prayer and fasting, destroying property, violating ihram, and for a woman to enable a man to commit zina. The distinction between a man and a woman in such permission is explained: a woman's act does not sever lineage, so it is not like murder. The passage then defines ikrah and divides it into two main types: complete coercion (ikrah kamil) which corrupts choice and creates necessity, and lesser coercion (ikrah qasir) which leaves choice intact but removes consent. A third type (threat to family) is mentioned but has no legal effect. Next, the passage argues that ikrah does not affect legal capacity (ahliyyah) because the coerced person retains reason and is accountable; the proof is that the coerced person wavers between four possible rulings: fard, haram, mubah, and rukhsah. These rulings are explained with examples: eating carrion under coercion becomes fard; zina or murder remains haram; breaking fast in Ramadan becomes mubah; uttering kufr is a rukhsah. The passage concludes with a benefit (faydah) distinguishing ibaha (where prohibition is lifted) from rukhsah (where prohibition remains but permission is granted).

Key Terms
اباحت (ibaha): Permissibility where the original prohibition is lifted entirely.
رخصت (rukhsah): Concession permitting the act while the prohibition remains in principle.
عزیمت (azimah): The original ruling (resolve) without concession.

Rational Proof That the Mukrah Remains Mukallaf
Provides a rational proof for the legal capacity of the coerced person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the rational proof (dalil aqli) that a coerced person remains legally obligated (mukallaf) and apply it to determine rulings.

Core Idea
The coerced person wavers between alternatives, proving they have ability to choose and thus remain subject to divine address.

A key question in usul is whether coercion removes legal responsibility. The passage provides a rational proof: the coerced person is in a state of hesitation (taraddud) between doing and not doing the action, which shows they have both capabilities and are being tested. This establishes that they remain mukallaf, as indicated by the four possible rulings (fard, haram, mubah, rukhsah) that apply to their action.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proof's starting point
The passage says 'Do you not see that he is wavering?' This indicates the coerced person deliberates, proving he has ability to act or refrain.
The term 'تردد' (taraddud) is key.
2
Link wavering to capacity
Wavering implies the person has both power (qudra) and option (ikhtiyar). Only one with capacity can be tested (ibtilā').
Ibtilā' (trial) is the basis for divine address.
3
Connect to the four rulings
The fact that the action can be classified as fard, haram, mubah, or rukhsah shows that the coerced person is addressed by these rulings. Without submission to divine command, such classification would be meaningless.
This is a proof from the effect (athar).
4
Refute the misconception
Some might think coercion removes obligation. The proof shows that even under coercion, the person remains a responsible agent, though the ruling may change (e.g., eating carrion becomes fard).
Coercion changes the ruling but not the fact of being addressed.

The Coerced Person is Mukallaf: Four Rulings as Proof
Rational proof that a coerced person is mukallaf through classification of rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four possible rulings (fard, haram, mubah, rukhsah) that apply to a coerced person's act and explain why this classification proves that the coerced person remains legally accountable (mukallaf) in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A coerced person is still mukallaf because his act can be classified as fard, haram, mubah, or rukhsah — rulings that only apply to an accountable person.

This passage from Hanafi usul al-fiqh addresses a critical question: Does coercion negate legal accountability? The author argues no, by examining the possible rulings that can attach to a coerced act. You will learn how the very existence of these rulings — ranging from obligation to concession — demonstrates that the coerced person remains legally addressed (mukhatab) and subject to reward or punishment accordingly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim
The passage asserts that a coerced person (mukrah) is still mukallaf (legally accountable). This is the central thesis.
The claim is established through a rational proof based on the variety of rulings that apply to the coerced act.
2
Recognize the Proof Structure
The proof is a dalil aqli (rational evidence): If the coerced person were not mukallaf, no shari ruling could apply to his act. But rulings do apply, so he must be mukallaf.
The four possible rulings serve as evidence that a ruling is indeed attached to the coerced act.
3
List the Four Rulings and Their Examples
The passage enumerates fard (eating carrion to save life), haram (murder or zina under coercion), mubah (breaking a fast in Ramadan), and rukhsah (uttering kufr while heart is firm).
Each ruling corresponds to a specific scenario; note the conditions that trigger each.
4
Distinguish Ibahah from Rukhsah
The passage clarifies that ibahah removes the prohibition entirely, while rukhsah leaves the prohibition intact but permits the act as a concession.
This distinction is crucial: in ibahah the act becomes halal; in rukhsah it remains unlawful in essence but is allowed due to coercion.
5
Conclude the Proof
Since the coerced act can be fard, haram, mubah, or rukhsah — all of which are categories of taklif (accountability) — the coerced person must be mukallaf.
If he were not mukallaf, these rulings would be meaningless.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from Hanafi usul al-fiqh establishing that a coerced person (mukrah) remains mukallaf (legally accountable) and his actions are subject to shari' rulings. The author presents a rational proof (dalil aqli): the fact that the coerced person's act can take one of four possible rulings (fard, haram, mubah, rukhsah) proves he is addressed by the Lawgiver (mukhatab). The Two Effects of Duress: Change of Attribution and Removal of Consent
Explains the two effects of duress and their application to contracts and acknowledgments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between complete and deficient duress and apply the Hanafi rule for each to contracts and acknowledgments.

Core Idea
Complete duress changes attribution of the act; deficient duress removes consent but not choice.

This lesson unpacks the foundational Hanafi distinction between complete (kamil) and deficient (qasir) duress. Understanding this distinction is critical for determining legal liability in coerced transactions and actions, as the effect ranges from shifting attribution to suspending contracts pending ratification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of duress
Read the passage to distinguish complete duress (force majeure eliminating choice) from deficient duress (threats that only remove consent).
Complete duress: e.g., threat of death or grievous harm. Deficient duress: e.g., threat of imprisonment or beating.
2
Analyze the effect of complete duress
Complete duress spoils choice entirely, making the coerced a tool. The act is attributed to the coercer if the coerced can be a tool (e.g., destroying property). Personal acts (eating, speech) remain attributed to the coerced.
The spoiled choice is like non-existent, so the coerced becomes a mere instrument.
3
Analyze the effect of deficient duress
Deficient duress removes consent (rida) but not choice (ikhtiyar). Thus, contracts requiring consent become suspended; actions not requiring consent (divorce, emancipation) are effective.
Consent is necessary for contracts like sale and lease; their validity depends on later ratification.
4
Apply the rule to acknowledgments
Acknowledgments under any duress are invalid because duress indicates falsehood; the acknowledged fact is presumed non-existent.
The duress itself is evidence that the acknowledgment is not truthful.
5
Examine the complete duress tool principle
When the coerced is a tool (e.g., forced to destroy property), the act is attributed to the coercer and he bears the ruling (e.g., liability for damage).
This applies only if the act admits agency; personal acts do not.

Its answer is that the effect of duress appears in two matters: (1) If the duress is complete, its effect appears in the change of attribution, i.e., the act that was attributed to the coerced person becomes attributed to the coercer, provided that act is capable of change and there is no impediment to changing attribution. The effect of duress only appears. (This is the answer to an implied question: If duress does not invalidate words and actions, then where does its effect appear?) Its answer is that the effect of duress appears in two matters: (1) If the duress is complete, its effect appears in the change of attribution, i.e., the act that was attributed to the coerced person becomes attributed to the coercer, provided that act is capable of change and there is no impediment to changing attribution. (2) If the duress is deficient, its effect appears in removing consent, not in removing choice; i.e., deficient duress removes consent, not choice, like when someone is forced by threat of imprisonment or beating; consent is removed but choice remains. Then they discuss the removal of consent: since duress removes consent, whether complete or deficient, all transactions that can be annulled and depend on consent become corrupted, like sale and lease; these contracts become void but their validity depends on consent, meaning if after duress ends the coerced person gives permission, they become valid. However, transactions where consent is not a condition, like divorce and emancipation, take effect as they do from a non-coerced person. The author says: all acknowledgments, whether under complete or deficient duress, whether of a revocable or irrevocable act, are not valid. For example, if someone is forced by threat of death or beating to acknowledge that he previously freed a slave, divorced his wife, or made a sale, etc., then no legal effect is established from such acknowledgment. Because an acknowledgment can be either true or false, and for truthfulness to be predominant, the acknowledgment is valid only if the thing acknowledged actually existed. Here, the thing acknowledged does not actually exist; the duress is evidence that he made the acknowledgment to save himself, and the thing acknowledged does not exist externally. Therefore, truthfulness does not prevail, so the acknowledgment is not valid. And when duress is connected: If in khul' there is duress regarding acceptance of wealth, the divorce occurs, but the wealth is not obligated. For example, a woman with whom marriage was consummated is forced to give khul' for 1000 rupees and she reluctantly accepts; the divorce occurs but the wealth is not obligated. Because duress, whether complete or deficient, eliminates consent to both the cause (the contract of khul') and the effect (accepting wealth). However, in duress, choice is not eliminated. Due to the presence of choice, the woman's acceptance of wealth is valid. Since her acceptance is valid, the divorce that depended on her acceptance occurs. But the obligation of wealth depends on consent, and in duress there is no consent, so it is as if the wealth was not mentioned; thus the divorce occurs without wealth being obligated. This is like a case where a man divorces his minor wife on payment; the divorce depends on the minor's acceptance; when she accepts, the divorce occurs but wealth is not obligated. Divorce occurs because she accepted; wealth is not obligated because the minor is not competent to obligate herself. The exact same situation applies in duress: due to the presence of choice, the woman's acceptance is valid, so divorce occurs, but the existence of wealth depends on consent, and in duress there is no consent, so wealth is not obligated. Contrary to hazl (jest): This is the answer to an implied question: when in divorce duress is joined with hazl (i.e., both have the same rank) and in both cases divorce occurs, so in duress over khul', wealth should also be obligated along with divorce, just as in hazl it is obligated by agreement. But the author says that in duress over khul', divorce occurs but wealth is not obligated. The answer is that there is a great difference between duress and hazl: because in hazl, there is consent to the cause (the contract) but not to the effect (the obligation of wealth), like option-of-condition prevents consent to the effect but not to the cause. In duress, there is no consent to either the cause or the effect. Since there is a difference, duress cannot be analogized to hazl. And when complete duress is connected to something wherein the doer can be a tool for another, like destroying life or property, the act is attributed to the coercer and its ruling is binding on him. Because complete duress spoils choice, and the spoiled in opposition to the sound is like non-existent. So the coerced becomes like one without choice, a tool for the coercer in what admits that. As for what does not admit it, its attribution to the coercer is not valid, so the opposition in entitlement to ruling does not occur, and it remains attributed to the corrupt choice. Commentary
This passage from the Urdu commentary on Al-Husami discusses the legal effects of duress (ikrah) in Hanafi fiqh. It begins by explaining that duress does not nullify actions but changes attribution for complete duress and removes consent for deficient duress. The author then details that contracts dependent on consent (like sale and lease) become suspended pending ratification, while actions not requiring consent (like divorce) are effective. Acknowledgment under duress is invalid because duress indicates falsehood. The discussion then focuses on khul' under duress: divorce occurs because the woman's acceptance is valid (choice remains), but the consideration is not owed because consent is absent. This is compared to the case of a minor wife, who similarly accepts but cannot be obligated. Finally, the text distinguishes between duress and jest (hazl): in jest, consent to the cause remains, so wealth is obligated; in duress, consent to both cause and effect is absent. The Arabic passage at the end reiterates the principle for complete duress: the coercer is liable for acts where the coerced is merely a tool, like destroying property, but not for personal acts like eating or speech.

Key Terms
الإكراه (al-ikrah): Compulsion that forces a person to act against his will, dividing into complete (kamil) and deficient (qasir).
الرضا (al-rida): Consent that is essential for some contracts; removed by any duress.
الاختيار (al-ikhtiyar): Legal capacity to choose, which remains in deficient duress.
الهزل (al-hazl): Jest or playfulness that does not indicate real intention.

Distinguishing Coercion from Jest: The Consent Distinction in Qiyas
Distinguishing coercion from jest in khul' divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi distinction between coercion (ikrah) and jest (hazl) in terms of consent to cause and ruling, and apply this distinction to evaluate the validity of qiyas between the two situations.

Core Idea
Coercion removes consent from both cause and ruling; jest only from ruling. Hence analogy between them fails.

In this passage, the commentator explains why coercion does not impose financial liability in a khul' divorce, even though both coercion and jest result in divorce. The key lies in the presence or absence of consent to the cause (sabab). Understanding this distinction is essential for applying qiyas correctly, especially when the effective cause (illa) differs between two situations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Under coercion, the divorce occurs but wealth is not obligatory. Note that the woman's acceptance is valid because choice remains.
Coercion does not nullify the cause, only consent to ruling.
2
Recognize the objection
An objector argues that since jest also results in divorce with wealth, coercion should be treated similarly by qiyas.
The objector assumes the two situations are comparable.
3
Analyze the distinction
In jest, the person consents to the cause (sabab) but not the ruling (hukm). In coercion, there is no consent to either.
Consent to cause is the key differentiator.
4
Apply the qiyas principle
For a valid qiyas, the illa must be shared. Here, the illa for wealth obligation is consent to cause, which is absent in coercion but present in jest.
Analogy fails because the illa differs.
5
Conclude
Therefore, wealth is not obligatory in coercion, upholding the Hanafi position.
The distinction is based on a precise analysis of consent.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling explaining the effect of complete coercion (ikrah kamil) on a khul' divorce. The author establishes that coercion does not invalidate the woman's acceptance because choice (ikhtiyar) remains present, albeit corrupted. Therefore, the divorce occurs. However, the obligation of the financial compensation (mal) is conditional upon genuine consent (rida), which is absent in coercion. Consequently, the divorce takes effect without the payment. The author then addresses a potential objection: since both coercion and jest result in divorce, one might think wealth should also be obligatory in coercion as it is in jest. The response clarifies the fundamental difference: in jest, the person consents to the cause (sabab) though not to the ruling (hukm), while in coercion, consent is absent from both. Therefore, analogy (qiyas) between coercion and jest is invalid. The example of a minor wife further illustrates the principle: the minor's acceptance effectuates divorce but cannot impose financial liability due to lack of legal capacity.

Key Terms
اکراہ (ikrah): Coercion that forces a person to act against their will, but choice remains corrupted.
ہزل (hazl): Jest or playful statement where the person intends the cause but not the legal ruling.
رضا (rida): Consent; in this context, it is the condition for the obligation of wealth in khul'.
سبب (sabab): The cause of the act, such as the pronouncement of divorce.
حکم (hukm): The legal ruling resulting from the act, such as the obligation of wealth.

The Hanafi Rule of Attribution Under Complete Coercion
Usul rule on attribution under ikrah kamil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which complete coercion transfers attribution of an act from the coerced to the coercer, and apply the rule to distinguish acts that admit instrumentality from those that do not.

Core Idea
Complete coercion transfers liability only when the coerced acts as a mere instrument for the coercer.

This passage from Al-Husami lays down a core usul principle: not every coerced act is attributed to the coercer. The key is whether the act admits the coerced being a tool (ālah) for another. By understanding this, you can correctly allocate legal responsibility in cases of duress, avoiding the error of burdening the victim or letting the real perpetrator escape liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the presence of complete coercion
Check whether the coercion is 'kāmil' – a threat of imminent harm to life or limb that overwhelms the will. Only such coercion can potentially transfer attribution.
Incomplete coercion (ikrāh nāqiṣ) does not transfer attribution; the act remains with the coerced.
2
Determine if the act admits instrumentality
Ask: can the coerced person be a mere conduit for the act? Acts like killing or destroying property can be done through a tool, so the coerced can be considered an instrument.
Personal acts like eating, sexual intercourse, or speech cannot be performed through another; the coerced is necessarily the direct agent.
3
Check whether the locus (mahall) changes
Even if the act type admits instrumentality, if making the coerced an instrument would change the subject matter of the offense (e.g., compelling a pilgrim to kill game – the ihram belongs to the coerced, not the coercer), attribution stays with the coerced.
The locus must remain identical; if it shifts, the coerced cannot be a tool for the coercer in that specific scenario.
4
Apply the rule: attribute or not
If the act admits instrumentality and the locus does not change, the act is attributed to the coercer, who bears the legal consequences. Otherwise, the act remains attributed to the coerced under corrupted choice.
The coercer's liability includes diyah (blood money) or compensation for property, while the coerced may face no liability due to duress.

Attribution of Actions Under Coercion: Hanafi Position and Shafi'i Dispute
Explains Hanafi rule and dispute with Shafi'i on coerced actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for coerced actions and explain why verbal transactions remain valid, citing the Shafi'i disagreement.

Core Idea
Coercion negates pleasure, not choice; the coerced remains legal actor except where financial loss transfers hukmi.

This passage explains the Hanafi school's position on coerced acts, contrasting with Imam al-Shafi'i. It uses three concrete examples—coerced killing, sale, and emancipation—to illustrate the principle that coercion does not eliminate choice but only negates pleasure. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining legal responsibility in contracts and torts under duress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational principle
Locate the statement that coercion does not eliminate choice (ikhtiyar) but negates pleasure (rida). This is the core of the Hanafi view.
The principle is stated near the end of the passage: 'coercion does not eliminate choice, but rather pleasure is negated by it, or choice is corrupted by it.'
2
Understand the 'instrument' argument
The passage argues that the coerced cannot be treated as a mere instrument (alah) of the coercer because that would change the locus of the act (mahall al-jinayah). Consider why this applies to killing and sale but not to financial loss in emancipation.
Key phrase: 'if he were made an instrument for another, the locus of the transgression would change.'
3

Work through each example: (1) Coerced killer is sinful because the act is a jinayah against religion; (2) Coerced delivery in sale remains attributed to the mukrah; (3) In coerced emancipation, the mukrah is the speaker but the financial loss transfers to the coercer. Note the reason: financial loss is separable and 'intelligible' (ma'qul).
The third case introduces a hukmi (legal) transfer, which is limited to acts that are ma'qul, not sensory.
4
Contrast with Shafi'i position
Identify al-Shafi'i's ruling: all coerced verbal transactions are void (laghw) because they lack genuine intent and choice. Coercion by imprisonment is equated to coercion by death. For actions, if coercion is complete, the legal effect is void from the actor.
The practical difference: Hanafi upholds validity of coerced contracts; Shafi'i voids them.
5
Extract the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence of the dispute: under Hanafi, a coerced sale is binding; under Shafi'i, it is void. This affects financial liabilities and property rights.
Memorise this: the Hanafi view is mufta bihi in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the Hanafi ruling on the attribution of acts performed under coercion (ikrah). The author, commenting on Al-Husami, begins by establishing that the coerced person (mukrah) is not considered a mere instrument (alah) of the coercer (mukri) in acts that are intrinsically sinful or pertain to his own person. If the mukrah were treated as an instrument, the locus of the transgression (mahall al-jinayah) would shift to the coercer, which is legally impossible because a person cannot transgress against another's ihram (here meaning inviolable state or liability). Hence, the act remains attributed to the mukrah. The text then provides three specific applications: (1) coerced killing renders the mukrah sinful because killing is a jinayah against the victim's religion (i.e., taking life), and the mukrah cannot be a mere tool; (2) coerced sale and delivery (taslim) remain binding on the mukrah because delivery is a disposal of his own property, and treating him as an instrument would turn the transaction into pure usurpation (ghasb); (3) coerced emancipation (itaq) is different: the mukrah is the speaker, but the financial loss (itlaf) is transferred to the coercer because the destruction of property is separable from the act of emancipation. The author then contrasts the Hanafi position with that of Imam al-Shafi'i, who holds that all verbal transactions under unjust coercion are void (laghw) because they lack genuine intent and choice. Al-Shafi'i equates coercion by imprisonment with coercion by killing. For coerced actions, al-Shafi'i rules that if coercion is complete, the act's legal effect is void from the actor; if possible, it is attributed to the coercer, otherwise it is utterly void. The Hanafi position, as stated, is that coercion does not eliminate choice (ikhtiyar) but only negates pleasure (rida) or corrupts choice. Thus, the mukrah remains legally accountable for his actions, except where the act is purely financial and separable, in which case the loss transfers to the coercer.

Transfer of Act is Hukmi Not Hissi
Stating the rule that transference of an act is legal (hukmi), not sensory

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two conditions required for the legal transference of an act under coercion and explain why the transference is hukmi, not hissi.

Core Idea
Act attribution under coercion is a legal construct (hukmi), not physical reality (hissi).

This passage establishes a foundational rule in Hanafi usul regarding the attribution of acts when coercion (ikrah) is present. The key insight is that the transference of the act's responsibility from the coerced to the coercer is not literal but legal. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying rulings in cases of coercion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
Recognize that the text asserts the transference is hukmi (legal) not hissi (sensory). Understand that this means the coercer is treated as the doer in law, not in physical reality.
Contrast with our everyday perception of who 'did' an act.
2
Extract the two conditions
Locate the two conditions stated: (1) the act must be rationally conceivable for the coercer, (2) the act must not have sensory existence from the coercer.
These conditions determine when the legal transference is valid.
3
Analyze condition 1
Consider why conceivability is required. If the act cannot be imagined as done by the coercer (e.g., a personal act like prayer), transference is impossible.
This condition filters out acts that inherently require personal intent.
4
Analyze condition 2
Explain why the act must not have sensory existence from the coercer. If the coercer physically performs the act, it is not transferred but is directly his, so the rule is not needed.
This condition preserves the distinction between physical and legal attribution.
5
Apply the rule
Apply the two conditions to a concrete example, such as forced killing. The killing is conceivable (condition 1 met), and the coercer does not physically kill (condition 2 met), so transference is valid.
This illustrates the practical operation of the rule.

Commentary
This passage establishes a foundational rule for understanding all previous discussions on coercion. The transference of an act's attribution from the coerced (mukrah) to the coercer (mukrih) is a legal (hukmi) construct, not a physical (hissi) reality. This means the coercer does not physically perform the act; rather, the law treats the act as if attributable to him for certain purposes. Two conditions must be met for this legal transference: first, the act must be such that it is rationally possible to conceive of the coercer doing it (e.g., a physical killing can be conceived, but an act like prayer cannot be transferred because it requires personal intent). Second, the act must not have a sensory existence from the coercer; i.e., the coercer should not physically do the act himself. This clarifies why in some cases (like murder) the act is not transferred entirely, but only certain effects are transferred to the coercer.

Key Terms
حکمی (ḥukmī): Legal/juridical in nature; based on legal convention rather than physical reality
حسی (ḥissī): Sensory/perceptible; physically real

Key Takeaway
The transference of an act under coercion is a legal construct (hukmi) requiring two conditions: conceivability for the coercer and absence of sensory existence from the coercer.
