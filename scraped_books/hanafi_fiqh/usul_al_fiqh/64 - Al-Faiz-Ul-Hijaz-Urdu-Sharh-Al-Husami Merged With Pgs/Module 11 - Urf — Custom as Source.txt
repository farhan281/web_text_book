The 'Urf-Based Distinction Between Shira' and Milk in Conditional Manumission
Explains ruling on buying half a slave then the other half

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that 'urf defines the scope of shira' and milk to determine the conditional manumission ruling, and evaluate when metaphorical usage (isti'ara) is accepted by the court.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, 'urf determines whether partial acquisition qualifies as 'purchase' or 'ownership', altering the condition's trigger.

This lesson unpacks a nuanced Hanafi usul ruling that distinguishes between 'shira' (purchase) and 'milk' (ownership) in a conditional manumission context. The distinction hinges on customary usage ('urf) and affects whether a slave becomes free when bought in two separate transactions. We will explore the legal reasoning, including the role of 'urf, metaphorical usage (isti'ara), and the difference between acceptance in conscience (diyana) and court (qada).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditional phrases and their 'urf-based interpretations
The passage presents two conditions: 'in ishtaraytu 'abdan fa huwa hurr' and 'in malaktu 'abdan fa huwa hurr'. Under 'urf, 'shira' applies to buying any part, while 'milk' requires complete possession at the moment of the statement. This is key to the different rulings.
Note that the condition uses an indefinite noun ('abdan).
2
Analyze the role of 'urf in defining the terms
In Hanafi usul, 'urf (custom) determines the lexical scope of words. Here, 'urf treats 'shira' as including partial purchase, so buying half by half counts as buying the slave. Conversely, 'milk' requires the whole object to be possessed; partial ownership is not called 'milk' in common parlance.
This is not a purely logical definition but a customary one.
3
Trace the ittisal (connection) allowing metaphorical usage
The passage states that shira' and milk are connected as cause and effect (shira' leads to milk). This ittisal allows isti'ara (metaphorical usage) in both directions. However, acceptance by the court depends on benefit to the speaker.
If the speaker uses one term intending the other, diyana accepts, but qada rejects when it benefits him due to suspicion of lying.
4
Differentiate between diyana and qada acceptance
When the metaphorical interpretation benefits the speaker (a concession), the court rejects his claim to that meaning because he is suspected of lying. If it is against his benefit, both diyana and qada accept. This is a principle derived from the text.
For example, saying 'shira' intending milk gives benefit (the second half becomes free), so court rejects; saying 'milk' intending shira' is against benefit, so accepted.
5
Apply the special case when the object is definite
If the condition uses a definite object (e.g., 'hadha al-'abd'), the 'urf-based distinction disappears because the condition refers to that specific slave. In both shira' and milk, the second half becomes free, as the condition is considered to have been fulfilled.
The indefinite/definite distinction crucially alters the ruling.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling that distinguishes between two conditional manumission phrases based on the 'urf (customary understanding) of shira' (purchase) and milk (ownership). The author first presents a case: if someone utters 'in ishtaraytu 'abdan fa huwa hurr' and buys half a slave, sells it, then buys the other half, the second half becomes free because 'urf considers him a buyer even of part of a slave. Conversely, if he says 'in malaktu 'abdan fa huwa hurr' and does the same, the second half does not become free because 'urf requires complete ownership at one time. This distinction rests on the fact that 'urf applies 'buyer' to someone who purchases any portion, but 'owner' only to one who possesses the entire slave. The passage then extends this to cases where the speaker uses one term intending the other. Since there is an actual connection (ittisal) between shira' and milk as cause and effect, metaphorical usage (isti'ara) is valid in both directions. However, the acceptance of this intention differs between diyana (conscience, religious obligation) and qada (court judgment). The rule is: if the speaker's interpretation would be for his own benefit (a concession), the court rejects it due to suspicion of lying, though diyana accepts it. If the interpretation is against his benefit, both accept it. A specific note restricts this difference to when the object is indefinite; if definite, the ruling is always the second half becomes free.

Key Terms
شراء (shira'): Purchase; in 'urf, it applies even to partial acquisition of a slave; the buyer is called 'buyer of the slave' by custom.
ملک (milk): Ownership; in 'urf, requires full possession of the entire object; partial ownership is not called milk in common usage.
استعارہ (isti'ara): Metaphorical usage of a word to mean something related; here, using shira' for milk or vice versa, valid due to ittisal (connection) between cause and effect.
عرف ('urf): Customary understanding that determines the meaning of words in legal contexts when no shar'i definition exists.

Cause and Effect in Metaphorical Usage: One-Directional Dependence
Explaining the second type of conceptual connection (cause-effect) in metaphorical usage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sabab and illa in Hanafi usul, and apply the rule that in cause-effect metaphor, only the cause can be used to imply the effect.

Core Idea
In cause-effect metaphor, the effect depends on the cause, so the cause can stand for the effect, but not vice versa.

This lesson unpacks the second type of conceptual connection (ittisal) in metaphorical usage: the cause-effect relationship. In Hanafi usul, a mere cause (sabab) is distinct from an illa, which is a direct cause placed for a ruling. The key insight is that the effect depends entirely on the cause, while the cause is independent. Therefore, when using metaphor, you may say the cause to imply the effect, but not the effect to imply the cause. This principle has practical consequences in legal speech, such as when words of emancipation are used to imply divorce.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish sabab and illa
Understand that illa is placed for the ruling and always produces it, while sabab merely leads to the ruling and may not always. The passage emphasizes that the second type deals with sabab mahd (mere cause), not illa.
Illa: 'i'taqa' always removes ownership. Sabab: 'i'taqa' may or may not remove usufruct depending on context.
2
Identify the one-directional dependency
In cause-effect, the effect (musabbab) depends on the cause (sabab) for its existence. The cause is independent. Therefore, the connection is one-sided.
The passage states: 'the connection of the branch with the original is like non-existent due to the original being independent.'
3
Apply the rule to legal examples
Analyse the example of 'anti hurra' said to a wife intending divorce. The word 'hurra' (cause) is used to imply divorce (effect). This is valid because divorce depends on the utterance.
The passage confirms: 'if someone says to his wife anti hurra intending divorce, it is valid.'
4
Recognize the invalid reverse
Saying 'anti taliq' to a slave-girl intending freedom is invalid because the effect (divorce) cannot imply the cause (emancipation). The cause is independent of the effect.
The passage explicitly states: 'saying effect meaning cause is not valid.'
5
Understand the grammatical analogy
The analogy with incomplete and complete sentences illustrates one-sided dependence: 'Hind taliq wa Zainab' – the second part depends on the first, but not vice versa.
This reinforces the principle that dependency dictates the direction of metaphorical usage.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a matn explaining the second type of conceptual connection (ittisal) in metaphorical usage. The author explains that the second type involves a connection between a cause (sabab) and its effect (musabbab). The key point is that this connection is one-directional: the effect depends on the cause, but the cause does not depend on the effect. Therefore, in metaphorical speech, one may use the cause to imply the effect (e.g., saying 'anti hurra' intending divorce), but not the effect to imply the cause (e.g., saying 'anti taliq' intending freedom). The reasoning is that the effect needs the cause for its existence, while the cause is independent. The author then draws an analogy with grammar: a complete sentence and an incomplete sentence when conjoined; the complete sentence is independent, but the incomplete one depends on it. Thus, the direction of dependence determines the validity of the metaphor. All examples are from Hanafi fiqh.

Key Terms
سبب (sabab): A cause that leads to a ruling indirectly; it is not specially placed for that ruling but leads to it by consequence.
مسبب (musabbab): The effect that results from the cause; it depends on the cause for its existence.
علة (illa): A cause specially designated by the Lawgiver to produce a ruling necessarily.

Five Indicators for Abandoning Literal Meaning (Haqiqah) in Hanafi Usul
Listing and explaining the five indicators for metaphorical interpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five indicators that necessitate abandoning the literal meaning (haqiqah) for metaphor (majaz) in Hanafi usul, and apply each indicator to a new example.

Core Idea
Literal meaning is abandoned only when one of five textual, customary, or contextual indicators demands a metaphorical reading.

In Hanafi usul, words are assumed to retain their literal meanings unless a clear indicator (dalalah) overrides. This lesson examines the five such indicators enumerated by the author: the context of speech, customary usage, the speaker's state, syntactical context, and the word's own limitations. Understanding these indicators is essential for correct istinbat — deriving rulings from the Quran and Sunnah without falling into falsehood or contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the default rule
In Hanafi usul, haqiqah (literal meaning) is the default. It is only abandoned when one of the five indicators is present. Identify the general principle: haqiqah is presumed unless there is a clear reason to depart.
2
Identify the five indicators
Name the five indicators from the passage: (1) dalalat mahall al-kalam (context of speech), (2) dalalat al-adah (customary usage), (3) dalalat ma'nan yarji'u ila al-mutakallim (meaning related to the speaker, e.g., yamin al-fawr), (4) dalalat siyaq al-nazm (syntactical context), (5) dalalat al-lafz fi nafsihi (the word itself, e.g., scope of 'meat' not including fish).
Memorize the Arabic terms and their English translations.
3
Examine the examples
For each indicator, study the examples given: Quranic verse 'The blind and the seeing are not equal' (context forces metaphor), hadith 'Actions are by intentions' (literal would be false), oaths in anger (speaker's state overrides), verse 'Let him who wills believe...' (syntactical context turns threat into warning), and oaths on meat/fruit (word itself limits scope). Note how the literal meaning would lead to falsehood or impossibility.
4
Apply the rule to a new scenario
Given a new statement (e.g., someone swears 'I will not drink' and then drinks water from a well), determine which indicator applies and whether the literal meaning is abandoned. Consider the custom and context.
Use the examples as templates.
5
Prioritize haqiqah when customarily used
Note the important exception: if the literal meaning is still customarily used (haqiqah musta'malah), it takes precedence over metaphorical according to Imam Abu Hanifah. This ensures that the default is not lightly displaced.

Commentary
This passage enumerates the five indicators that necessitate abandoning the literal meaning (haqiqah) for a metaphorical meaning (majaz). The author lists: indication from the speech context (mahall al-kalam), custom (al-adah), a meaning relating to the speaker (as in oaths of anger), syntactical context (siyaq al-nazm), and the word itself. Word-based examples: swearing off meat excludes fish because 'meat' does not customarily include fish. This aligns with Hanafi usul principles.

Key Terms
الحقيقة (al-haqiqah): The original, literal meaning of a word that is established by custom or linguistic convention.
المجاز (al-majaz): The figurative or metaphorical meaning of a word, used when the literal meaning is abandoned due to evidence.
دلالة محل الكلام (dalalat mahall al-kalam): The indication arising from the context of the speech, which prevents the literal meaning from being intended.
دلالة العادة (dalalat al-adah): Customary usage that determines the intended meaning, often overriding the literal sense.
يمين الفور (yamin al-fawr): An oath taken in a state of anger or sudden emotional outburst, indicating a metaphorical interpretation.

Custom and Ikhtilaf in Oath Interpretations: Fish and Grapes Example
Illustrating how urf determines word meanings in oaths with a fish and grapes example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that custom (urf) determines the meaning of words in oaths, and explain the ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding fish and grapes.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, custom overrides literal meaning in oaths, making fish not 'meat' and grapes not 'fruit' depending on the era's custom.

This passage from Al-Fayd al-Hijazi illustrates a key usul principle: the interpretation of words in oaths is governed by custom, not merely linguistic derivation. Using two concrete examples—fish under 'meat' (lahm) and grapes under 'fruit' (fakihah)—the author shows how Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn differed due to changes in custom over time. Understanding this will help you analyze how custom functions as a source of law in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the examples and rulings
Locate the two examples: fish/meat and grapes/fruit. Note the ruling of Imam Abu Hanifa (no oath violation) and the Sahibayn (oath violation).
The rulings opposite each other due to differing customs.
2
Understand the linguistic derivation argument
Imam Abu Hanifa uses the root meaning (maddah) of 'lahm' to exclude fish (blood-bearing land animals) and of 'fakihah' to exclude grapes (nutritional value beyond enjoyment).
This is a linguistic analysis (bi-al-maddah).
3
Apply the concept of ziyadah (addition)
The meaning of 'fakihah' is found in grapes but with an addition (nutrition), so grapes are not simply 'fruit' in the linguistic sense. Similarly, 'lahm' in fish is deficient.
Ziyadah in meaning can exclude the word's application.
4
Recognize the role of custom change
The mashayikh explain that the ikhtilaf between Imam and Sahibayn is due to change in custom (urf) over time. Each ruled according to the custom of their era.
Both positions are valid relative to their time.
5
Note the principle: oaths are based on custom
The tanbih (note) states that oaths depend on custom, so rulings vary by region. If custom includes fish under 'meat', then eating fish breaks the oath.
This is a practical rule for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on how custom (urf) affects the interpretation of words in oaths, illustrated by two examples and showing an ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn. The author first discusses the example of fish: although in literal meaning (wad') fish might be included in 'meat' (lahm), the root meaning (maddah) of the word lahm indicates blood-bearing land animals, excluding fish. So the swearer who says 'I will not eat meat' does not violate his oath by eating fish, because fish is not considered lahm in the linguistic sense. The second example is grapes and fruit (fakihah): fakihah implies something eaten for enjoyment without being a staple food; grapes have both enjoyment and nutrition, so grapes are fakihah with an addition. Imam Abu Hanifa rules that eating grapes does not break the oath, while the Sahibayn hold that it does. The mashayikh explain that this difference stems from the change of custom over time. A key principle (tanbih) is stated: oaths are based on custom, so the ruling varies by region. If a region's custom treats fish as meat, then eating fish would break the oath.

Key Terms
عرف (urf): Customary practice or common usage that determines the meaning of words in legal contexts such as oaths.
مادہ (maddah): The root letters of a word from which its core meaning is derived.
حانث (hanith): One who breaks his oath and becomes sinful.
زیادتی (ziyadah): Addition; when a word's meaning is found in a thing but with an extra attribute that excludes it from the word's application.
