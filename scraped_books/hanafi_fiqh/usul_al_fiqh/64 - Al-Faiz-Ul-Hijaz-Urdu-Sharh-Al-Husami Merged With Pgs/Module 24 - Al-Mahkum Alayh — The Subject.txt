Legal Capacity: Ahliyyah and Its Two Types
Defining ahliyyah, its division, and the basis of dhimma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ahliyyah, distinguish ahliyyat al-wujub from ahliyyat al-ada, and explain the Hanafi basis of dhimma from the primordial covenant.

Core Idea
Legal capacity divides into capacity to bear rights/duties and capacity to perform actions, rooted in the dhimma established by the primordial covenant.

In Hanafi usul, understanding legal capacity is essential before studying rulings on obligations. The concept tells us who can have rights and duties, and who can validly perform acts of worship and transactions. This lesson unpacks the two types of ahliyyah and their basis in the Quranic covenant.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its division
Locate the definition of ahliyyah and its two types: ahliyyat al-wujub and ahliyyat al-ada. Write down the key characteristics of each.
2
Grasp the basis of ahliyyat al-wujub
Note that ahliyyat al-wujub is based on dhimma, which every human possesses from birth. The text cites Quran 7:172 as the proof for this dhimma.
This is the primordial covenant (ʿahd alast).
3
Understand the partial dhimma of the fetus
The fetus has a partial dhimma: rights that benefit it (e.g., inheritance) are established, but duties are not imposed until after birth.
The text says 'قبل الانفصال هو من وجه جزء فلا يكون له ذمة كاملة'.
4
Analyze the principle that obligation is not an end
Obligation (wujub) is intended for performance (ada). If performance is impossible (e.g., child lacks choice), the obligation may be nullified.
The text uses the example of the child and the traveler's Friday prayer analogy.
5
Apply to the disbeliever and the child
The disbeliever is not obligated with worship because he lacks capacity for reward, but is obligated with faith. The child is not obligated with faith before understanding; after understanding, the essence of faith is obligatory but not performance.
This demonstrates the gradual nature of legal capacity.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of ahliyyah (legal capacity) according to the Hanafi school. The author first divides ahliyyah into two types: ahliyyat al-wujub (capacity to have rights and duties) and ahliyyat al-ada (capacity to perform actions with legal effect). The key basis for ahliyyat al-wujub is the existence of dhimma (legal personality), which every human possesses from birth by virtue of the primordial covenant (ʿahd alast) mentioned in Quran 7:172. This dhimma is initially 'suitable' (saliha) but not 'complete' (kamila) before birth, meaning the fetus can acquire rights that benefit it (like inheritance, lineage) but cannot be liable for duties (like payment of price). After birth, the dhimma becomes complete, enabling both rights and duties. However, the author explains that obligation (wujub) is not an end in itself; its purpose is performance (ada) by choice. Since a child lacks the ability to perform deliberately, many obligations that require intent are not imposed until maturity. Similarly, a disbeliever is not obligated with acts of worship because he lacks the capacity for reward, but he is obligated with faith because he can perform it and its ruling applies. The passage uses the example of a traveler performing Friday prayer as an analogy for the child's faith being valid without prior obligation. The overall reasoning demonstrates the Hanafi principle that legal capacity is graduated based on physical and intellectual readiness, and that obligation is linked to the ability to perform meaningfully.

Key Terms
أهلية الوجوب (ahliyyat al-wujub): The capacity to have rights established for one and duties imposed upon one.
أهلية الأداء (ahliyyat al-ada): The capacity to perform an action with legal effect, requiring intellect and ability.
الذمة (al-dhimma): A legal attribute in a person that makes them capable of bearing rights and duties.

The Minor as Agent: Responsibility Requires Guardian's Permission
Stating the Hanafi ruling on the minor's agency.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a minor's agency and identify the condition under which responsibility binds the minor.

Core Idea
A minor's agency does not bind him unless the guardian permits — responsibility without permission falls on the principal.

This lesson presents a key Hanafi principle regarding the legal capacity of a minor in contracts of agency. Understanding when a minor bears responsibility is essential for correctly applying the rules of guardianship and representation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act
Recognize that the act in question is a minor acting as an agent (tawakkul).
Agency involves undertaking duties like delivery or payment.
2
State the Default Ruling
Without guardian permission, the responsibility (uhdah) does not bind the minor. He is not liable for the duties of the agency.
The default is non-liability.
3
Identify the Condition for Binding
With the guardian's explicit permission (idhn al-wali), the responsibility becomes binding on the minor.
The guardian's permission compensates for the minor's deficient judgment.
4
Understand the Rationale
Agency involves potential harm (like bearing liability), and the minor lacks full capacity for such acts without guardian oversight.
This aligns with the Hanafi distinction between beneficial and harmful transactions.
5
Apply to a Scenario
Consider a minor sent to buy an item. Without guardian permission, if he damages the item, he is not liable. With permission, he is.
The condition is decisive.

Commentary
This passage states a Hanafi usul ruling on the legal capacity of an interdicted minor (sabi mahjur) in the context of agency (wakala). The minor does not have full legal competence for transactions that involve potential harm (like bearing responsibility for a contract). Therefore, if a minor accepts agency independently, the duties (like delivering the sold item or price, or handling defects) do not become binding on him; rather, they fall on the principal (muwakkil). However, if the minor acts with the guardian's permission, the guardian’s supervision compensates for the minor's deficiency in judgment, and then the responsibility binds the minor. The reasoning is that agency involves potential harm, and the minor is not authorized for such acts without the guardian – a principle consistent with the Hanafi position on the minor's partial capacity.

Key Terms
صبي (sabi): A minor child who is under interdiction (mahjur) and does not have full legal capacity for transactions.
عهدة (uhdah): The legal responsibility and liability that falls on an agent (e.g., to deliver the item or price).
ولي (wali): The guardian of the minor who has authority to permit transactions on his behalf.

The Legal Capacity of an Imbecile ('Atah) in Hanafi Jurisprudence
Defines 'atah and compares its rulings to those of a sane minor, addressing an objection about property liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'atah, distinguish its rulings from insanity and minority, and apply the Hanafi rule that an imbecile's valid acts are effective while harmful acts are not binding.

Core Idea
An imbecile is like a sane minor: valid acts accepted, harmful acts not binding, but property damage compensation remains due.

In this lesson, we examine the concept of 'atah (imbecility) as a legal impediment. The Hanafi jurists compare the imbecile to a minor of discerning intellect, and draw a key distinction between liability for harmful acts and compensation for property destruction. Understanding this distinction is essential for determining the legal capacity of such individuals.

This difference affects procedural rules in marriage.

So the ruling of an adult imbecile is like that of a sane minor. The child's initial state resembles madness, but in the final state the child has intellect, albeit deficient; similarly, the imbecile has a defect in intellect. Therefore, the adult imbecile is like a sane minor. Thus, 'atah does not prevent the validity of speech and action; just as the speech and actions of a sane child are valid, so are those of an imbecile, such as accepting Islam, accepting a gift, appointing an agent, etc. However, 'atah prevents liability; that is, something that causes him harm will not be binding upon him. Hence, the imbecile's divorce of his wife is not valid, nor his manumission of his slave, whether with the guardian's permission or without. Similarly, if an imbecile is made agent for a sale, he cannot be demanded to deliver the sold item, and in case of a defect appearing, he cannot be forced to return the item. In short, liability for harmful actions does not apply to him, just as it does not apply to a sane minor. As for the guarantee of consumed property: It is an answer to an objection, namely: When harmful things are not made binding upon the imbecile and the sane minor, then the guarantee for destroyed property should also not apply to them, because therein also is their harm, whereas guarantee is obligatory on them. Its answer is that the guarantee is not based on liability (dhimma), which is disputed regarding the imbecile, but rather guarantee is legislated as compensation for destroying inviolable property. The fact that the destroyer is an imbecile or a minor does not oppose the inviolability of the locus; i.e., even if the destroyer is a child or imbecile, that does not cancel the inviolability of someone's property. Therefore, guarantee is obligatory as compensation. This guarantee is for the inviolability of the property, not a punishment for the act; the punishment for the act does not come upon the imbecile. Hence, if he destroys the rights of Allah, the guarantee that comes upon him is a punishment for the act; that will not come upon the imbecile or sane minor. The address (khitab) is lifted from the imbecile just as from the child. Then there is insanity (junun) and imbecility ('atah). Insanity resembles the initial state of a child, and imbecility resembles the final state of a child. So the author is explaining the difference between the child and the insane, and between the child and the imbecile. He says that the difference between insanity and childhood is that insanity is unlimited; no time is fixed for its removal, while childhood is limited; after a period, childhood ends. He then elaborates: if the wife of an insane man becomes Muslim, Islam is presented to the insane man's parents, and the insane man is considered subject to his parents; if the parents accept, the marriage remains, otherwise the woman becomes separated (ba'in) from the insane man. The Ma'dhun Slave's Legal Capacity: Possession vs Ownership in Hanafi Usul
Hanafi usul dispute on slave capacity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi distinction between milk al-yad and milk al-ayn for the ma'dhun slave, apply the analogy of the agent (wakil) to determine the slave's capacity, and evaluate the Shafi'i counterargument.

Core Idea
Possession (qabd) is the purpose of transaction; ownership of substance is only a means, so a slave can possess even without owning.

This passage tackles a fundamental usul al-fiqh dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i over whether a slave permitted to trade (ma'dhun) can validly possess property. The Hanafi position hinges on a sharp distinction between possession and ownership, and uses an analogy to the agent (wakil) to explain the slave's partial capacity. You will see how this reasoning produces specific rulings in the master's death illness and in chains of permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
Pinpoint the exact point of disagreement: can a ma'dhun slave acquire valid possession (qabd) through his own transactions, even though he cannot own the substance?
The Shafi'i view denies any capacity to possess because ownership is impossible.
2
State the Hanafi position and its basis
The Hanafi position holds that the slave obtains milk al-yad (ownership of hand) but not milk al-ayn (ownership of substance). The proof is that the primary purpose of transaction is possession, which benefits even without ownership.
The master's permission makes the slave an agent only in ownership, not in possession.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i argument and Hanafi rebuttal
Shafi'i argues that since the slave cannot own, he cannot transact, because transaction causes ownership. The Hanafi response distinguishes means from ends: ownership is only a means to possession, so the absence of ownership does not negate the validity of possession.
This is a classic application of istihsan.
4
Apply the analogy of the wakil to the two scenarios
The text likens the ma'dhun slave to a wakil in ownership matters and continuation of permission. Apply this analogy: in the master's death illness, the slave's transactions are limited like a wakil's; in chain permission, withdrawal from the first does not affect the second.
The two companions (Sahibayn) differ on the effect of gross fraud in the death illness scenario.

It is (the case) whether free or slave, and a woman cannot be its owner. In a free man, both types of ownership are complete, therefore his blood-money is complete. A woman has only the first type of ownership, therefore her blood-money is half that of a man. In a slave, the first type of ownership is deficient and the second type is complete, therefore his blood-money is somewhat less than that of a free man. And this is with us: The author (may Allah have mercy on him) says that the slave's eligibility to transact in property is our doctrine, because the master has given him permission to transact. So his transaction is for himself as principal, not as the master's agent. Therefore, from his transaction, the original ruling of transaction, i.e., possession, belongs to him. As for ownership of the substance, it is not obtained by him; i.e., the slave becomes owner of possession and transaction but not owner of the substance. So ownership of substance is an additional thing beyond ownership of hand (i.e., possession). That is, the slave becomes eligible for transaction and possession, but he does not become owner of it. So ownership of substance is an additional thing; possession is the essence, because through possession a person can fulfill his need, even if he is not the owner. And if he is owner but does not have possession, he cannot benefit. In any case, he himself is the owner of transaction and possession; in this the slave is original. And as for ownership of substance, which is prescribed for reaching ownership of hand (i.e., possession), in this the master will be the agent of the ma'dhun slave. Therefore ownership is established for the master. Originally, ownership should also be established for the direct agent, i.e., the slave, but since the slave is not eligible for ownership, making the master his agent, ownership is established for the master. This passage is actually a response to Imam Shafi'i's argument. Imam Shafi'i's argument is that the slave is not eligible for transaction and possession either, just as he is not eligible for ownership. Therefore, his possession over his earnings is not his own possession, but rather possession by way of trust, like a depositary, because if the slave were eligible for transaction and possession, he would also be eligible for ownership, since transaction is a cause of ownership, and the slave is unanimously not eligible for ownership; therefore he will not be eligible for transaction and possession either. The response is that the primary purpose of transaction is possession, and this is obtained by the slave. Possession is the primary purpose because through possession a person can benefit from the thing; there is no need for proof of ownership for this. Therefore, ownership of substance is not necessary for this primary purpose, i.e., possession; rather it is a means to reach possession. So the non-existence of a specific means does not necessitate the non-existence of the purpose; rather it is possible that there is another means for the purpose. And for this reason we have made the slave: Since ownership is not established for the slave, but the master stands in the place of the slave, therefore the slave, in the ruling of ownership and in the continuation of permission—not in the ruling of transaction—will be like an agent. That is, the slave is original and independent in ownership of hand (i.e., possession) and ownership of transaction, but in the ruling of ownership of substance and continuation of permission, he will be non-independent like an agent. Therefore, if the slave buys something, its owner will be the master, just as when an agent buys, the principal becomes owner. So in ownership of substance, the slave is like an agent, and just as the principal can prevent the agent from transacting against his will if he wishes, similarly the master can prevent the ma'dhun slave from transacting against his will. So in the continuation of permission, the slave is like an agent. In the author's phrase, 'in the ruling of ownership' pertains to issues of the master's death illness, and 'in the ruling of continuation of permission' pertains to general issues of the ma'dhun slave. Now the meaning is that we have made the ma'dhun slave like an agent in the ruling of ownership in the issues of the master's death illness, and like an agent in the ruling of continuation of permission in the general issues of the ma'dhun. Example of the first: the master gave permission to the slave for trade, then the master fell ill with death illness, and the slave bought and sold with gross fraud or slight fraud, then the master died. If the master has debt, then the ma'dhun slave's transaction is absolutely invalid, because due to the debt, the creditors' right has become attached to this slave as well. And if there is no debt, then the slave's transaction will be effective in the master's one-third wealth, because the slave is like an agent, and an agent has only as much authority as the principal has. Therefore, in both these states, if the master himself had transacted, the same ruling would apply; so the agent will also have the same ruling. In summary, just as an agent's ruling changes in the master's state of illness, similarly the ma'dhun slave's ruling will change in the master's death illness. However, in the state of health, the ma'dhun slave is not like an agent. This is according to Imam Abu Hanifa. Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul discussion concerning the legal capacity of a ma'dhun slave—a slave permitted by his master to engage in trade. The text begins with a brief mention of blood-money (diya) for a free man, woman, and slave, contrasting their ownership capacities. It then transitions into a central dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i over whether such a slave can validly possess (qabd) and transact (tasarruf) without achieving full ownership (milk al-ayn). The Hanafi position, attributed to the author (likely al-Husami or the commentator), is that the slave, by virtue of the master's permission, acts as principal in possession and transaction; he obtains 'milk al-yad' (ownership of hand/possession) but not 'milk al-ayn' (ownership of the substance). The purpose of transaction is possession, which is sufficient for benefit, so ownership of the substance is not necessary. Imam Shafi'i's argument is that since the slave cannot own the substance, he also cannot possess or transact, because those lead to ownership. The Hanafi response distinguishes between the primary purpose (possession) and the means (ownership of substance), asserting that the slave can achieve the purpose without the means. The text then draws an analogy to a wakil (agent): in matters of ownership and continuation of permission, the ma'dhun slave is like a wakil, but in possession and transaction, he is principal. This is then applied to two scenarios: (1) when the master is in death illness (marad al-mawt) and the slave transacts with slight or gross fraud—according to Imam Abu Hanifa, the transaction is valid only in one-third of the master's wealth if no debt, and void if there is debt; the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) differentiate between slight fraud (same as Abu Hanifa) and gross fraud (void). (2) A chain of permission: the master permits the slave, who then permits his own slave; if the master withdraws permission from the first slave, the second is not affected; if the master dies, both become interdicted. The passage concludes with a brief note on blood inviolability: slavery affects the value (diya) but not the sanctity of life; retaliation (qisas) applies equally between free and slave. This is a quintessential Hanafi usul argument demonstrating istihsan (juristic preference) and the distinction between means and ends in legal capacity.

Key Terms
عبد ماذون (abd ma'dhun): A slave whom his master has permitted to engage in trade transactions.
ملک ید (milk al-yad): Ownership of hand, i.e., the right of possession and control over property, as distinct from ownership of the substance.
ملک عین (milk al-ayn): Ownership of the very substance of property, i.e., full proprietary ownership.
قبض (qabd): Physical possession or control over property, enabling benefit.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn statement 'وانقطعت الولايات كلها' (And all types of authority are severed). The commentator explains the distinction between two types of inviolability ('ismah): one derived from faith (which incurs sin when violated) and one derived from residence in Dar al-Islam (which incurs financial compensation). Slavery does not affect either type, so a slave's life is equally protected as a free person's. However, slavery does affect the amount of diyah (blood money): the slave's value is less than a free person's. The commentator then moves to the main point: slavery severs all forms of legal authority (wilaya). A slave cannot act on behalf of others—cannot perform marriage, be a judge, or be a witness. An exception arises when the slave's action first affects himself and only secondarily affects others. The example of giving aman (safe conduct) to a disbeliever during jihad is discussed: because the slave first waives his own share of booty (his own right), the secondary effect on others is permitted. This principle is analogous to a slave's testimony about the Ramadan moon: it first obligates him to fast, then secondarily informs others. The commentator then applies this principle: a slave's confession to hudud or qisas is valid because it first burdens himself, even if it destroys the master's property right. Similarly, confession to theft is valid for both ma'dhun and majur slaves. For the majur slave whose master denies, the three imams differ: Abu Hanifa holds immediate cutting and return, Abu Yusuf postpones the financial guarantee to after freedom, and Muhammad postpones both.

رقیت (riqq): Legal status of slavery that affects capacity (ahliyyah) but not inviolability (ismah).
عصمت (ismah): Legal inviolability that protects life and property; derived from faith (for sin) and from Dar al-Islam (for financial liability).

The Principle: Actions First Affecting the Slave Are Valid
Explanation of the exception to severed wilaya via the principle of self-affecting acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a slave's action is valid if it first binds himself, even if it secondarily affects the master.

Core Idea
A slave's action is valid if it first affects himself, even if it harms the master.

After establishing that slavery does not affect inviolability, the commentator turns to the main topic: authority (wilaya). The matn states that all types of authority are severed from the slave. But this raises a puzzle: if a slave lacks authority, how can he give aman (safe conduct) to an enemy during jihad? The answer unveils a key principle: the action must first affect the slave himself, and only secondarily affect others. This principle is established by analogy to a slave's testimony about the crescent moon, which first obligates him to fast.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule: wilaya is severed
The matn says all authorities are severed. A slave cannot act for others—no marriage, no judgeship, no testimony except as an exception.
2
Identify the apparent contradiction
Giving aman is an act of authority (makes the enemy safe from Muslims). How can a slave do it if he has no wilaya?
The commentator poses this as a question.
3
Understand the principle: self-affecting first
When the master permits the slave to fight, the slave gains a share in booty. Giving aman first waives his own share, then secondarily affects others. Thus it is valid because it starts with himself.
The key is the order of effect: self first, others second.
4
See the qiyas (analogy) to moon testimony
A slave's testimony about the Ramadan crescent is valid because it first obligates himself to fast, then informs others. This is the asl (original case) for the far' (new case) of aman.
Both actions share the illah: self-affecting first.
5
Generalize the principle
Any action of a slave that first affects himself and then secondarily affects the master's rights is valid.
This principle will be applied in the next lesson to confessions.

Explanation: Slavery does not affect the inviolability of blood. Therefore the blood of a slave is equally inviolable as that of a free person, because the inviolability whose violation incurs sin arises from faith, and the inviolability whose violation incurs compensation (diyah) arises from being in Dar al-Islam. If someone possesses both inviolabilities—being a Muslim and residing in Dar al-Islam—then killing him entails either diyah or qisas, and also kaffarah. If a Muslim resides in Dar al-Harb, then killing him only obligates kaffarah, because he has only one inviolability that entails compensation. In short, the inviolability that incurs sin is acquired from faith, and the inviolability that incurs compensation is acquired from Dar al-Islam. In terms of faith and being a resident of Dar al-Islam, a slave is like a free person. Hence slavery does not make any difference to his inviolability. However, the effect of slavery appears in the amount of value: i.e., the slave's value is slightly less than that of a free person, as previously mentioned: if the value of a murdered slave is ten thousand dirhams or more, then one-tenth less is due as diyah—i.e., 9990—so that his status remains lower than a free person. If his value is less than ten thousand, then the value is obligatory. Since the slave is like a free person in inviolability, if someone deliberately kills a slave, he is killed in qisas, just as a man is killed for killing a woman, because in inviolability the woman is also like the man. In any case, slavery does not affect inviolability. But the slave does not own his bodily usufruct; the Shari'ah has only made him owner of those acts of worship that it has excepted, such as prayer and fasting. Therefore, in Hajj and jihad the slave does not own the usufruct of his body because the Shari'ah has not excepted them. Hence without the master's permission, the slave cannot perform Hajj or jihad. However, if general mobilization (nafir 'amm) occurs—i.e., in the case of a sudden attack when the Commander of the Faithful issues a general order for jihad—then the slave does not need the master's permission. In any case, since slavery creates a deficiency in eligibility for jihad, the slave is not entitled to a full share of booty, whether he fights with the master's permission or without. However, the Imam may give a little as a gift, called radhkh, just as the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, gave to slaves as a gift at Khaybar. Thus the discussion of dhimma and capability is completed; the third of the three matters was wilaya, and now we are explaining it. 'And all types of authority are severed': From the slave, due to slavery, every kind of authority is severed, because slavery is a legal impediment (mujaz hukmi), meaning the slave is incapable of all dispositions by the command of the Shari'ah. Since the slave has no authority over himself, he cannot have authority over others. Therefore the slave cannot perform marriage for anyone, cannot be a judge, and cannot be a witness. There is a question: When every kind of authority is severed from the slave, how is it valid for a slave who is permitted to give aman (safe conduct) to a disbeliever in jihad? This is also a type of authority, because that disbeliever becomes a slave to the Muslims and his property becomes booty. By giving him aman, the Muslims' right over him is dropped, which is a disposition over others, called authority. The answer is: When the master gave the slave permission for jihad, the slave becomes a partner in the booty like other fighters, even if he does not get a full share but only a gift or radhkh—still, he has some share in the booty. Now when he gives aman to the disbeliever, first he makes a disposition regarding his own right, then its effect secondarily affects others. Therefore this is not counted under the chapter of authority. This is like the case of a slave giving testimony about the crescent of Ramadan: his testimony is valid even though he is not among those possessing authority, but the testimony is accepted because first he obligates fasting upon himself, then secondarily and subsequently this ruling extends to others. So every action of a slave that first affects the slave himself, then secondarily causes loss to the master's property, is valid. And upon this principle it is valid: On the principle that the slave's action that first binds him can then secondarily extend to others. If a slave confesses to hudud or qisas, it is valid, and the objection does not arise that the slave's confession destroys the master's right (i.e., the slave's physical person which is destroyed in qisas), because first the slave destroys his own right, and along with it the master's right is destroyed. It is correct that first something becomes binding on the slave, then it extends to others. Similarly, if a slave confesses to theft, it is valid, whether the stolen property has been destroyed or is still extant, whether the slave is ma'dhun (permitted to trade) or majur (hired out). In any case, the confession is valid. If the stolen property is extant, it must be returned to the one from whom it was stolen, and the hand is also cut. This is the ruling for the ma'dhun slave. Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn statement 'وانقطعت الولايات كلها' (And all types of authority are severed). The commentator explains the distinction between two types of inviolability ('ismah): one derived from faith (which incurs sin when violated) and one derived from residence in Dar al-Islam (which incurs financial compensation). Slavery does not affect either type, so a slave's life is equally protected as a free person's. However, slavery does affect the amount of diyah (blood money): the slave's value is less than a free person's. The commentator then moves to the main point: slavery severs all forms of legal authority (wilaya). A slave cannot act on behalf of others—cannot perform marriage, be a judge, or be a witness. An exception arises when the slave's action first affects himself and only secondarily affects others. The example of giving aman (safe conduct) to a disbeliever during jihad is discussed: because the slave first waives his own share of booty (his own right), the secondary effect on others is permitted. This principle is analogous to a slave's testimony about the Ramadan moon: it first obligates him to fast, then secondarily informs others. The commentator then applies this principle: a slave's confession to hudud or qisas is valid because it first burdens himself, even if it destroys the master's property right. Similarly, confession to theft is valid for both ma'dhun and majur slaves. For the majur slave whose master denies, the three imams differ: Abu Hanifa holds immediate cutting and return, Abu Yusuf postpones the financial guarantee to after freedom, and Muhammad postpones both.

Key Terms
ولایت (wilaya): Legal authority to act on behalf of others; severed by slavery except in actions that first affect the slave himself.
رضخ (radhkh): A discretionary gift from booty given by the Imam to slaves who participate in jihad.

Explanation: Slavery does not affect the inviolability of blood. Therefore the blood of a slave is equally inviolable as that of a free person, because the inviolability whose violation incurs sin arises from faith, and the inviolability whose violation incurs compensation (diyah) arises from being in Dar al-Islam. If someone possesses both inviolabilities—being a Muslim and residing in Dar al-Islam—then killing him entails either diyah or qisas, and also kaffarah. If a Muslim resides in Dar al-Harb, then killing him only obligates kaffarah, because he has only one inviolability that entails compensation. In short, the inviolability that incurs sin is acquired from faith, and the inviolability that incurs compensation is acquired from Dar al-Islam. In terms of faith and being a resident of Dar al-Islam, a slave is like a free person. Hence slavery does not make any difference to his inviolability. However, the effect of slavery appears in the amount of value: i.e., the slave's value is slightly less than that of a free person, as previously mentioned: if the value of a murdered slave is ten thousand dirhams or more, then one-tenth less is due as diyah—i.e., 9990—so that his status remains lower than a free person. If his value is less than ten thousand, then the value is obligatory. Since the slave is like a free person in inviolability, if someone deliberately kills a slave, he is killed in qisas, just as a man is killed for killing a woman, because in inviolability the woman is also like the man. In any case, slavery does not affect inviolability. But the slave does not own his bodily usufruct; the Shari'ah has only made him owner of those acts of worship that it has excepted, such as prayer and fasting. Therefore, in Hajj and jihad the slave does not own the usufruct of his body because the Shari'ah has not excepted them. Hence without the master's permission, the slave cannot perform Hajj or jihad. However, if general mobilization (nafir 'amm) occurs—i.e., in the case of a sudden attack when the Commander of the Faithful issues a general order for jihad—then the slave does not need the master's permission. In any case, since slavery creates a deficiency in eligibility for jihad, the slave is not entitled to a full share of booty, whether he fights with the master's permission or without. However, the Imam may give a little as a gift, called radhkh, just as the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, gave to slaves as a gift at Khaybar. Thus the discussion of dhimma and capability is completed; the third of the three matters was wilaya, and now we are explaining it. 'And all types of authority are severed': From the slave, due to slavery, every kind of authority is severed, because slavery is a legal impediment (mujaz hukmi), meaning the slave is incapable of all dispositions by the command of the Shari'ah. Since the slave has no authority over himself, he cannot have authority over others. Therefore the slave cannot perform marriage for anyone, cannot be a judge, and cannot be a witness. There is a question: When every kind of authority is severed from the slave, how is it valid for a slave who is permitted to give aman (safe conduct) to a disbeliever in jihad? This is also a type of authority, because that disbeliever becomes a slave to the Muslims and his property becomes booty. By giving him aman, the Muslims' right over him is dropped, which is a disposition over others, called authority. The answer is: When the master gave the slave permission for jihad, the slave becomes a partner in the booty like other fighters, even if he does not get a full share but only a gift or radhkh—still, he has some share in the booty. Now when he gives aman to the disbeliever, first he makes a disposition regarding his own right, then its effect secondarily affects others. Therefore this is not counted under the chapter of authority. This is like the case of a slave giving testimony about the crescent of Ramadan: his testimony is valid even though he is not among those possessing authority, but the testimony is accepted because first he obligates fasting upon himself, then secondarily and subsequently this ruling extends to others. So every action of a slave that first affects the slave himself, then secondarily causes loss to the master's property, is valid. And upon this principle it is valid: On the principle that the slave's action that first binds him can then secondarily extend to others. If a slave confesses to hudud or qisas, it is valid, and the objection does not arise that the slave's confession destroys the master's right (i.e., the slave's physical person which is destroyed in qisas), because first the slave destroys his own right, and along with it the master's right is destroyed. It is correct that first something becomes binding on the slave, then it extends to others. Similarly, if a slave confesses to theft, it is valid, whether the stolen property has been destroyed or is still extant, whether the slave is ma'dhun (permitted to trade) or majur (hired out). In any case, the confession is valid. Now if the stolen property has been destroyed, only the hand is cut; compensation is not obligatory because hand-cutting and compensation cannot be combined as punishments. If the stolen property is extant, it must be returned to the one from whom it was stolen, and the hand is also cut. This is the ruling for the ma'dhun slave. Regarding the majur slave, there is a difference among the imams. If a majur slave commits theft, we see whether the stolen property is extant or destroyed. If destroyed, hand-cutting occurs. If the property remains, we see whether the master confirms or denies. If the master confirms, then the majur slave's hand is cut and the property must be returned. If the master denies (falsifies), then according to Imam Abu Hanifa, both hand-cutting and return of property are obligatory in this case as well. According to Imam Abu Yusuf, the hand is cut now, but the guarantee of the stolen property becomes obligatory after his emancipation. Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn statement 'وانقطعت الولايات كلها' (And all types of authority are severed). The commentator explains the distinction between two types of inviolability ('ismah): one derived from faith (which incurs sin when violated) and one derived from residence in Dar al-Islam (which incurs financial compensation). Slavery does not affect either type, so a slave's life is equally protected as a free person's. However, slavery does affect the amount of diyah (blood money): the slave's value is less than a free person's. The commentator then moves to the main point: slavery severs all forms of legal authority (wilaya). A slave cannot act on behalf of others—cannot perform marriage, be a judge, or be a witness. An exception arises when the slave's action first affects himself and only secondarily affects others. The example of giving aman (safe conduct) to a disbeliever during jihad is discussed: because the slave first waives his own share of booty (his own right), the secondary effect on others is permitted. This principle is analogous to a slave's testimony about the Ramadan moon: it first obligates him to fast, then secondarily informs others. The commentator then applies this principle: a slave's confession to hudud or qisas is valid because it first burdens himself, even if it destroys the master's property right. Similarly, confession to theft is valid for both ma'dhun and majur slaves. For the majur slave whose master denies, the three imams differ: Abu Hanifa holds immediate cutting and return, Abu Yusuf postpones the financial guarantee to after freedom, and Muhammad postpones both.

Key Terms
مجور (majur): A slave hired out by his master; contrasted with ma'dhun (permitted to trade).
ماذون (ma'dhun): A slave permitted by his master to engage in trade.

Marad al-Mawt: A Legal Impediment Preserving Rights
Defines the legal impediment of terminal illness and its effects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define marad al-mawt, explain why it imposes a restriction (hajr) on property transactions, and distinguish between rescindable and non-rescindable transactions in this context.

Core Idea
Terminal illness restricts property transactions to protect heirs and creditors but does not remove legal capacity for worship or speech.

Illness is a natural human condition, but in Islamic law, when it becomes terminal, it triggers specific legal consequences to safeguard the rights of others. This lesson explores how the Hanafi school defines marad al-mawt and the rationale behind placing a partial restriction on the sick person's property dealings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the impediment
Recognise that the eighth impediment (aarid) discussed is illness, specifically the type that does not disrupt intellect (as opposed to insanity or coma).
The passage clarifies that the impediment here is marad al-mawt, not every illness.
2
Understand capacity preservation
Note that illness does not negate the capacity for obligations or speech; a sick person remains obligated for worship and capable of valid contracts like marriage and divorce.
This sets the baseline: the restriction is limited, not a total removal of ahliyyah.
3
Rationale for restriction
Because illness causes death, and death makes the property subject to the rights of heirs and creditors, a restriction is needed to protect those rights.
The reasoning is rational (dalil aqli): death ↔ succession → attachment of others' rights.
4
Distinguish transaction types
Classify transactions into two categories: those that can be rescinded (like gifts and underpriced sales) are initially valid but may be annulled; those that cannot be rescinded (like freeing a slave) are treated as suspended on death (tadbir).
The key criterion is the possibility of faskh (rescission).
5
Apply to emancipation
For a slave freed in marad al-mawt, the emancipation is like tadbir: the slave becomes free after death and must work to earn his value for the rights of heirs/creditors.
This shows the practical consequence of the ruling.

Translation: In any case, illness is not contrary to the capacity for ruling and expression. But when illness is a cause of death, and death is the cause of succession, then illness becomes among the causes for the right of the heir and creditor being attached to the property of the sick person. So restriction (hajr) is established due to illness when the illness is connected to death, attributed to the beginning of the illness, to the extent that it protects the right of the heir and creditor. So it is said: Every transaction that occurs from the sick person that has the possibility of rescission (faskh) — it is obligatory to consider its validity initially, then it is obligatory to break it and make amends when the need arises. And every transaction that occurs from the sick person and does not have the possibility of rescission — it will be considered like a transaction suspended on death, such as emancipation (itaq) when it occurs affecting the right of the creditor or heir, unlike the emancipation of a pawn, which is effective because the pawnbroker's right is in possession, not ownership. And the analogy (qiyas) was that the sick person would not be owner of a gift, nor of fulfilling financial rights for Allah, nor of his will, except that the Sharia allowed it from one-third of the wealth out of mercy. When the Sharia took custody of the will for the heirs and invalidated his will for them, then his will becomes invalid in form, meaning, reality, and resemblance. Thus the sale of the sick person to the heir is absolutely not correct according to Imam Abu Hanifa, may mercy be upon him, and his acknowledgment for the heir is invalid even if it is regarding the receipt of a debt of health. And good quality becomes valuable in the right of the heirs, just as good quality is valuable in the right of minors. Commentary: The eighth impediment is illness. Illness is the name of a state of the human body by which balance is lost. Insanity, coma, etc., are also illnesses, but here the intended illness is that which does not cause disruption to the intellect. So illness is not contrary to the capacity for obligatory rulings, nor contrary to the capacity for speech and expression. Thus the sick person will be obligated for all rulings that a healthy person is obligated for, except that concessions are given according to different situations. If the sick person is not able to stand in prayer, he prays sitting or by gesture. Similarly, the fasts of Ramadan are allowed to be postponed. However, the sick person is obligated; so he will be obligated for prayer, fasting, etc. Similarly, he is capable of expressing intentions with words, so his marriage, divorce, freeing a slave, etc., all acts are valid. A question arises: when the sick person still has capacity, why is restriction imposed on him in cases of terminal illness? The answer is being given: illness is the cause of death, and death is the cause of succession, i.e., upon a person's death, his capacity ends. Then his heirs become successors to his property. Since there is also debt on him, the right of the creditor is also attached to his property. So when the right of the heirs and creditor is attached to his property, then for the protection of both rights, restriction is imposed on the sick person so that his transaction does not become effective, lest both rights be lost. But this restriction is to the extent that protects the right. So in the amount of the creditor's debt, the sick person's transaction is not effective; and after the debt, the remaining two-thirds of the property is the right of the heirs — in that also the sick person's transaction is not effective. This restriction occurs in terminal illness, and terminal illness is known when he dies from that illness; otherwise it is not terminal illness. When he dies, the restriction is attributed to the beginning of the illness, i.e., at the time of death it is judged that his transaction is not effective from the time he became ill. Nevertheless, his being terminally ill depends on connection to death, and death is possible; he may die from this sickness or not. So what of the transaction he is doing now? The author is explaining a principle: in that transaction we look whether it has the possibility of rescission or not. If it has the possibility of rescission, like giving a gift, selling goods at a very low price, etc., then it becomes effective because there is no harm in this transaction if he dies and leaves a debt, that transaction can be rescinded to pay the debt and give to the heir. And if the transaction does not have the possibility of rescission, like freeing a slave when it affects the right of an heir or creditor, then that transaction will be considered like a transaction suspended on death, just as the master said to the slave: 'You are free after my death,' which is called tadbir. In any case, the sick person's emancipation when it affects the right of the heir or creditor — meaning the sick person freed a slave and the value of the slave exceeds one-third of the wealth, or the sick person has a debt that encompasses all his wealth — then this emancipation is affecting the right of the heir and creditor. Therefore its ruling is like that of a mudabbar, just as a mudabbar becomes free upon the master's death and after being free works to earn the amount of his value to pay the right of the heirs and creditors. Similarly, the slave freed in terminal illness becomes free after the master's death and works to earn the amount of his value for payment of the rights of heirs and creditors.

Commentary
This passage defines the legal impediment of terminal illness (marad al-mawt) in Hanafi fiqh. The author first clarifies that illness does not negate legal capacity (ahliyyah) for obligations or speech; a sick person remains obligated for worship and capable of valid contracts like marriage and divorce. However, when illness is connected to death, it triggers a restriction (hajr) on property transactions to protect the rights of heirs and creditors. The reason is that death causes succession, so the property effectively becomes attached to others' rights. The restriction applies retroactively from the onset of that terminal illness if death occurs. The passage distinguishes between two types of transactions: those that can be rescinded (like gifts and underpriced sales) are initially valid but may be annulled later to satisfy debts and heirs, while those that cannot be rescinded (like freeing a slave) are treated as suspended upon death (tadbir), taking effect only after death and requiring the freed slave to work off his value for the rights of heirs/creditors. The author also discusses specific rulings: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, a sale to an heir is invalid, and an acknowledgment of debt to an heir is invalid even if it was for a health-period debt. The quality of goods becomes valuable in the right of heirs similar to minors. The passage emphasizes that the restriction is only to the extent needed to protect rights—up to the amount of debt and two-thirds of remaining property for heirs.

Key Terms
عارض (aarid): A legal impediment that temporarily affects a person's capacity to perform acts; the eighth impediment discussed is illness.
حجر (hajr): A legal restriction preventing a person from freely disposing of his property, imposed to protect the rights of creditors and heirs.
مدبر (mudabbir): A slave whom the master promises freedom upon his death; the slave becomes free after the master's death and must work to pay off his value to the heirs if the estate is indebted.

Ignorance as an Excuse in Hanafi Usul: Eight Cases
Eight cases where ignorance excuses legal consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the eight cases where ignorance is a valid excuse in Hanafi usul al-fiqh and distinguish them from the case where it is not, applying the principle that jahl is an excuse only when the person could not have reasonably known.

Core Idea
Ignorance excuses only when knowledge is impossible, not when learning is neglected.

In Hanafi usul, ignorance (jahl) can suspend legal consequences, but only under specific conditions. This passage from a classical sharh enumerates eight scenarios—from agents to slaves to women—where ignorance excuses inaction or preserves rights. It then contrasts these with the case of a free adult woman whose ignorance of khiyar al-bulugh is not excused, because she is expected to learn the rulings. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the maxim that silence before knowledge does not imply consent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
Recognize that the passage deals with ignorance as an excuse (uzr) in various transactions and its effect on rights and obligations.
The passage is a collection of furu‘ (branches) illustrating a usul rule.
2
Extract the eight cases where ignorance is an excuse
List each case: (1) agent unaware of appointment, (2) slave unaware of permission to trade, (3) agent unaware of dismissal, (4) slave unaware of dismissal, (5) pre-emptor unaware of sale, (6) master unaware of slave's crime, (7) adult virgin woman unaware of father's marriage, (8) married slave-girl unaware of option upon emancipation.
In each case, the person's lack of knowledge prevents the legal consequence (e.g., transaction not binding, right preserved).
3
Identify the contrasting case where ignorance is NOT excused
Note the case of a free adult woman who, after majority, is unaware of her option of puberty (khiyar al-bulugh). Her ignorance is not an excuse because she is free and in an Islamic land, expected to learn.
This contrast highlights the condition of feasibility: if knowledge could have been acquired, ignorance is not excused.
4
Apply the principle to new scenarios
Using the rule that jahl is excusable only when the person could not have known, test other hypothetical cases (e.g., a traveler in a non-Muslim land unaware of local rulings).
The underlying illa (effective cause) is the impossibility of obtaining knowledge under the circumstances.

معلوم نہیں کہ اس کو خیار بلوغ ملتا ہے، یعنی بالغ ہونے کے بعد نکاح کو باقی رکھنے اور فسخ کرنے کا اختیار ملتا ہے، تو اس لڑکی کا یہ عدم علم اور جہل عذر نہ ہوگا، لہذا بالغ ہونے کے بعد اس کے سکوت سے نکاح لازم ہو جائے گا۔ باندی کا خیار عتق سے جہل عذر ہے اور بالغہ کا خیار بلوغ سے جہل عذر نہیں ہے، کیونکہ باندی کو تو آقا کی خدمت کی مشغولی احکام شریعت سیکھنے سے مانع ہے لیکن آزاد بالغہ کے لئے دار الاسلام میں دین و احکام سیکھنے سے کوئی چیز مانع نہیں ہے، لہذا اس کو سیکھنا چاہیے نہیں سیکھی تو اس نے کوتاہی کی ، لہذا اس کا جہل عذر نہ ہوگا۔
One appoints an agent for buying and selling but the agent does not know of it, or a master permits a slave to trade but the slave does not know: then their lack of knowledge and ignorance is an excuse. Therefore, if they performed any transaction before receiving notice, their transaction will not be binding on the principal or master. Similarly, if the principal dismisses the agent, or the master dismisses the slave, but they could not learn of the dismissal, then this is an excuse. Hence, if they performed any transaction before notice of dismissal, it will be binding on the principal/master. Likewise, the ignorance of the pre-emptor regarding the sale is an excuse: if someone sold his house and the pre-emptor did not know for a period, then his lack of knowledge and ignorance is an excuse, so after learning, he will have the right of pre-emption. Similarly, the master's ignorance of the slave's crime is an excuse: if the slave committed a crime and the master does not know, and without knowledge the master frees or sells the slave, then this ignorance is an excuse, so the master will not be considered as having chosen the ransom. That is, it will not be said that the master, by freeing or selling, consented to pay the ransom. Rather, the lesser of the slave's value and the arsh (blood money) will be obligatory on the master. For example, if the slave's value is five thousand and the penalty is four thousand, then the lesser (four thousand) is due from the master. If the master freed him without knowledge of the crime, it will not be considered that he agreed to pay the full ransom (the slave's value). Yes, if he freed knowing of the crime, then the full ransom would be obligatory on him. Similarly, the ignorance of an adult virgin woman regarding marriage is an excuse: if her father married her off and she did not know, then her silence before knowledge of the marriage is not considered consent; rather, after knowing, she will have the option of annulment. Likewise, the ignorance of a married slave-girl regarding the option upon emancipation is an excuse: if the master married off the slave-girl and then freed her, she gets the option upon emancipation; that is, after freedom she may either keep the marriage or dissolve it. But if the slave-girl does not know of the option upon emancipation, then her lack of knowledge and ignorance is an excuse, so after knowing she will have the option. On the contrary, if a guardian other than father or grandfather married off a minor girl, and after reaching majority she does not know that she has the option of puberty (i.e., after majority she has the choice to keep or dissolve the marriage), then this lack of knowledge and ignorance will not be an excuse; therefore, after majority her silence will make the marriage binding. The slave-girl's ignorance regarding the option upon emancipation is an excuse, but the adult free woman's ignorance regarding the option of puberty is not an excuse. This is because the slave-girl's engagement in serving her master prevents her from learning the rulings of Shari'ah, whereas for an adult free woman in an Islamic land, nothing prevents her from learning the religion and rulings; therefore she should learn; if she does not, she has been negligent, so her ignorance is not an excuse.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling regarding ignorance (jahl) as a valid excuse in various transactions according to the Hanafi madhhab. The author enumerates eight cases: (1) An agent unaware of his appointment; (2) A slave unaware of permission to trade; (3) An agent unaware of his dismissal; (4) A slave unaware of dismissal; (5) A pre-emptor unaware of a sale; (6) A master unaware of his slave's crime; (7) An adult virgin woman unaware of her father's marriage contract for her; (8) A married slave-girl unaware of her option upon emancipation. In all these cases, the ignorance prevents legal consequences from attaching until knowledge is acquired. The underlying principle is that silence before knowledge does not imply consent. The passage contrasts the eighth case with a free adult woman's ignorance of the option of puberty (khiyar al-bulugh): because she is free and in an Islamic land, she is expected to learn the relevant rulings, so her ignorance is not excused. The rulings are consistent with the Hanafi position that jahl is an excuse in cases where the person could not reasonably have known, but not when they are negligent.

Key Terms
الجهل (al-jahl): Lack of knowledge of a fact that, if known, would have caused a different legal outcome.
العذر (al-‘udhr): A valid excuse that suspends legal consequences until knowledge is obtained.

Effect of Intoxication on Legal Capacity
Explains the legal effect of intoxication on validity of actions and limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that intoxication preserves legal capacity for most actions and identify the exceptions (apostasy and confessions of pure divine rights) justified by istihsan.

Core Idea
Intoxication preserves capacity; actions are valid except apostasy and confessions of hudud Allah.

This passage addresses a key Hanafi usul rule: the intoxicated person (sukran) retains legal capacity (ahliyyah) despite diminished awareness. The Quranic address to the intoxicated (Surah an-Nisa, 4:43) proves they are mukallaf. Thus, their transactions and utterances (divorce, manumission, sales) are binding. However, by istihsan (juridical preference), apostasy is not valid because it requires intention, and confessions of pure divine punishments (hadd) are not accepted because intoxication implies retraction. Understanding this nuanced position helps you apply the rule correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Source Text
Locate the Quranic verse cited: 'Do not approach prayer while you are intoxicated' (4:43). This is the primary dalil that the intoxicated are capable of being addressed.
The verse is not directly about capacity but about prohibition, showing the intoxicated can receive commands.
2
Classify the Text
Categorize the verse as 'amm' (general) in Hanafi usul. It addresses all intoxicated persons without exception.
Hanafi rule: al-amm yufidu al-wujub (general command indicates obligation) and applies to all instances.
3
Apply the Hanafi Principle
Since the verse addresses the intoxicated, they must have the capacity to be addressed (ahliyyat al-ada). Hence, all rulings apply to them except where the text or istihsan creates an exception.
Capacity is not removed by intoxication; only insanity removes it entirely.
4
Derive the Ruling
Most actions (divorce, manumission, contracts) are valid. Exceptions: apostasy (requires intent, lacking here) and confession to pure divine punishments (intoxication signals retraction). These exceptions are based on istihsan overriding qiyas.
Istihsan here prevents punishing the intoxicated for words they do not affirm with intention.

Commentary
This passage discusses the legal effect of intoxication on a person's capacity and the validity of his actions. The author begins by contrasting intoxication with insanity: an insane person lacks legal capacity entirely, so no rulings apply. An intoxicated person, however, retains capacity, so Allah's address to him is valid, and all rulings become binding. Therefore, his transactions like divorce, manumission, and sales are effective. However, by istihsan (juridical preference), his apostasy is not considered valid because apostasy requires belief and intention, which are absent in intoxication. Similarly, his confession to crimes that are pure rights of Allah (like drinking, theft, fornication) is not accepted, because intoxication serves as a sign of retraction; the intoxicated cannot be relied upon to remain consistent. But for crimes that involve rights of servants (like slander or homicide), his confession is valid because retraction by the servant is not allowed in these cases, so intoxication cannot substitute for retraction. This shows a nuanced approach balancing deterrence and fairness.

اہلیت (ahliyyah): Legal capacity to acquire rights and obligations.
ردہ (riddah): Apostasy from Islam.
