Four Divisions of Nazm and Ma'na in Hanafi Usul
Introduces the fourfold classification of nazm and ma'na for Shari'ah rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fourfold classification of nazm and ma'na in Hanafi usul and explain its purpose in deriving Shari'ah rulings.

Core Idea
The Hanafi usul classifies Quranic texts into four divisions based on nazm and ma'na for ruling derivation.

This passage lays the foundation for a key Hanafi usul framework: understanding how the Quranic text is categorized when extracting legal rulings. It introduces the concept that the Quranic text relevant to ahkam is divided into four major divisions based on whether the discussion centers on wording (nazm) or meaning (ma'na). This classification is essential for systematically analyzing Quranic verses and applying the correct interpretive principles.

Guided Path
1
Understand the key terms
Familiarize yourself with the terms nazm (wording), ma'na (meaning), sigha (form), and lugha (language). Note that nazm refers to the wording/expression of a Quranic text, while ma'na refers to its meaning. Sigha and lugha are sub-categories of nazm indicating its morphological form and lexical aspect.
These terms are foundational for the entire classification.
2
Scope of the classification
Recognize that this classification is limited to the part of the Quran that relates to knowing the rulings of Shari'ah (ahkam). The author specifies that the divisions are 'in what pertains to knowing the rulings of Shari'ah', so not all Quranic content is covered.
The Quran also contains stories, beliefs, etc., which are not part of this classification.
3
Identify the number of divisions
The passage states that there are four primary divisions. Each division has four subcategories, making sixteen. However, the second division includes four counterparts (mutabaqat), resulting in a total of twenty. Grasp this counting carefully.
The exact nature of the counterparts is not fully explained here due to the passage being cut off.
4
Analyze the proof of enumeration (dalil al-hasr)
The author provides a logical proof for this enumeration: either the Quranic verse discusses solely meaning (ma'na) or solely wording (nazm). If it is about wording, it can be further divided based on usage or indication of meaning. This reasoning justifies the fourfold classification.
This dalil shows a binary-tree-like division, which is a common method in Hanafi usul.

The Four Levels of Clarity: Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, Muhkam
Defines and exemplifies four categories of clear speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four categories of clear speech (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) in Hanafi usul and apply the hierarchy to determine the weight of a textual evidence.

Core Idea
Textual clarity increases from zahir to muhkam — each level reduces the possibility of reinterpretation.

In Hanafi usul, not all clear texts are equally binding. This passage delineates the second division of clear speech: four levels of increasing clarity that determine whether a text can be qualified, interpreted, or abrogated. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for weighing conflicting evidences and deriving rulings correctly.

Guided Path
1
List the four categories in order of increasing clarity
Identify each category from the passage: Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, Muhkam. Note the sequence and that each subsequent level adds more clarity and restrictions.
Zahir is the lowest; Muhkam is the highest.
2
For each, identify the key property that increases clarity
Zahir: meaning appears from wording alone. Nass: meaning is deliberate from speaker (siq al-kalam li ajlihi). Mufassar: no possibility of takhsis (specification) or ta'wil (interpretation). Muhkam: additionally fortified against naskh (abrogation).
These properties are cumulative.
3
Note what each category still permits
Zahir and Nass permit takhsis and ta'wil. Mufassar permits naskh but not takhsis or ta'wil. Muhkam permits nothing — it is final.
This is the practical implication for a mujtahid.
4
Examine the Quranic examples given in the passage
Identify how the same verse 'marry...two and three and four' is zahir in permission (ibaha) and nass in number because the speech was brought for that number. The verse 'all of them together' is mufassar for the prostration of all angels.
A single verse can have multiple levels for different aspects.
5
Practice classifying a new text according to this hierarchy
Take any clear Quranic command (e.g., 'establish prayer') and decide whether it is zahir, nass, mufassar, or muhkam. Apply the criteria: can it be specified or interpreted? Can it be abrogated?
Use the definitions to determine the level.

And the second division in the aspects of clarification with that arrangement, and they are four: Az-Zahir: it is that whose intended meaning appears from the wording itself. An-Nass: it is that which has increased clarity over the zahir due to a meaning in the speaker, like His saying: 'then marry what is pleasant to you of women, two and three and four' – the verse. Commentary
This passage introduces the second division of clear speech (wujuh al-bayan) in Hanafi usul: four categories of increasing textual clarity. (1) Zahir: a word whose meaning is apparent from its wording alone, without requiring external context. Example: 'marry women' shows permission (ibaha) but remains open to specification. (2) Nass: a word that has more clarity than zahir because the speaker deliberately brought the speech to convey that specific meaning. The same verse 'two and three and four' is nass regarding the number of wives because the speech was specifically for that purpose. (3) Mufassar: a word whose clarity exceeds nass such that no possibility of specification (takhsis) or interpretation (ta'wil) remains. Example: 'the angels prostrated, all of them together'—the words 'all' and 'together' remove any possibility of some not prostrating. Its ruling is definitive (qat'i) obligation, but it may still be abrogated (naskh). (4) Muhkam: when mufassar becomes even stronger, immune to abrogation and alteration. This hierarchy is key in Hanafi jurisprudence for determining the strength of textual evidence.

Key Terms
ظاهر (zahir): A word whose meaning is apparent from its wording alone, but may admit specification (takhsis) or interpretation (ta'wil).
نص (nass): A word that is clearer than zahir because the speech is deliberately brought for that meaning (siq al-kalam li ajlihi).
مفسر (mufassar): A word so clear that it excludes any possibility of specification (takhsis) or interpretation (ta'wil), but may still be abrogated.
محکم (muhkam): A word that is fortified against abrogation and alteration; the highest level of clarity.

Distinguishing Zahir and Nass: Speaker Intent and Text Clarity
Defining Zahir and Nass with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a Quranic or hadith text as Zahir or Nass by analyzing whether the speaker intentionally used the text to convey that specific meaning.

Core Idea
Speaker intent elevates a text from Zahir to Nass, but both still allow takhsis and ta'wil.

In Hanafi usul, not all clear texts are equal. The first two levels, Zahir and Nass, both admit possibility of specification (takhsis) and interpretive modification (ta'wil), but Nass is more explicit because the speaker intentionally brought the speech to convey that meaning. This lesson unpacks the decision tree used to classify them, with the Quranic verse on marriage (4:3) as a key example.

Guided Path
1
Determine if takhsis/ta'wil is possible
If the text can be specified (takhsis) or reinterpreted (ta'wil), then it is either Zahir or Nass. If not, it belongs to Mufassar or Muhkam (covered in the next lesson).
This is the first branch of the decision tree.
2
Identify whether the speaker intended this meaning
Was the speech brought specifically to convey that meaning? If yes, it is Nass; if no, it is Zahir. The example: Quran 4:3 regarding marriage numbers.
Speaker intent is determined by context, emphasis, or purpose.
3
Apply to the example: Quran 4:3
The verse 'So marry what is good to you of women, two, three, and four' is Zahir for permissibility of marriage (hearer understands immediately), but Nass for the specific numbers because the numbers are the intended point.
The same text can have different classifications for different meanings.
4
Understand the implication
Both Zahir and Nass can be subject to takhsis and ta'wil, but Nass is stronger in clarity. In case of conflict, texts are considered equally authoritative unless other factors intervene.
The difference becomes important when two texts appear to conflict.

Then it will be seen whether there is any possibility of ta'wil and takhsis in it or not. If there is possibility of takhsis and ta'wil, then there are two forms: either the speech was brought to express that meaning or not. If the speech was not brought to express that meaning, it is called Zahir; if the speech was brought to express that meaning, it is called Nass. And if there is no possibility of takhsis and ta'wil, then also two forms: is there possibility of naskh and tabdil or not? If there is, it is Mufassar; if not, it is Muhkam. Now he is defining all of them. Az-Zahir: It is a speech whose meaning becomes known to the listener as soon as he hears it, i.e., he understands the meaning from the mere wording, provided he knows the language. An-Nass: It is a speech that has more clarity than Zahir, because its clarity is not only from the wording but also from a reason referring to the speaker, i.e., the speaker intentionally brought this speech to express that meaning, whereas in Zahir the speech is not intentionally brought to express the meaning. Example: 'So marry what is good to you of women, two, three, and four.' This speech is Zahir regarding the permissibility and lawful nature of marriage, because a native speaker understands that upon hearing it; and regarding the number, it is Nass because it was brought specifically to express the number. Al-Mufassar: It is what has increased in clarity over Nass in such a way that no possibility of takhsis and ta'wil remains, like Allah's saying: 'So the angels prostrated, all of them together.' Its ruling is obligation definitively, without possibility of takhsis or ta'wil, except that it admits the possibility of naskh. When it increases in strength and the intended meaning is made firm against change, it is called Muhkam. Commentary: Mufassar is speech that has more clarity than Nass; it has no possibility of takhsis or ta'wil, while Nass does. For example, the verse about the angels prostrating: it is Zahir regarding all angels prostrating, but there is a possibility of takhsis (some might have prostrated, not all); when 'kullahum' is added, it becomes Nass, removing that possibility. But there remains a possibility of ta'wil (they might have prostrated separately, not together); when 'ajma'un' is added, that possibility is removed, making it Mufassar. However, Mufassar admits the possibility of naskh, but that possibility is confined to the time of the Prophet (peace be upon him). After him, the entire Quran is Muhkam with no possibility of naskh or change. Question: How can this speech be Mufassar? It is Muhkam, because Allah is informing that all angels prostrated, and Allah's information cannot be abrogated, else it would entail lying. Answer: The original speech admits possibility of naskh, but due to an external factor (it being information from Allah) that possibility is removed. A better example is Allah's saying: 'Fight the polytheists completely' – this is a command, not information, so it admits naskh. The ruling of Mufassar is that the established rule is decisive and certain; there is no possibility of takhsis or ta'wil. Therefore, believing in and acting upon a ruling from Mufassar is obligatory. 'Except that it admits naskh': But note that the possibility of naskh was only during the time of the Prophet (peace be upon him). After him, even that possibility is gone, so Mufassar becomes Muhkam li-ghayrihi, because naskh occurs through revelation and revelation ceased after the Prophet. 'When it increases in power': Now defining Muhkam. Muhkam is speech whose meaning is stronger and firmer than Mufassar, with no possibility of naskh or change, like 'Allah is All-Knowing of everything' and 'Jihad continues until the Day of Judgment'. Benefit: There are two ways of having no possibility of naskh: (1) The speech by its nature does not admit naskh, like verses of tawhid and divine attributes – that is Muhkam li-dhatihi. (2) The possibility of naskh ended after the Prophet's departure – that is Muhkam li-ghayrihi. And the difference between these names appears only when they conflict, otherwise all are decisive.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed explanation of the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity in usul al-fiqh. The author is defining four categories: Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, and Muhkam. First, he establishes a decision tree: If a text can be subject to takhsis (specification) or ta'wil (interpretive modification), then look at the speaker's intent. If the text was not brought specifically for that meaning, it is Zahir; if it was, it is Nass. If the text cannot be subject to takhsis or ta'wil, then check if it can be abrogated (naskh). If it can, it is Mufassar; if not, it is Muhkam. Then he defines each. Zahir is a text whose meaning is immediately apparent to a competent speaker of the language, without need for external evidence. Nass is a text whose clarity is enhanced because the speaker intentionally brought it to convey that meaning; it is more explicit than Zahir. The example given is the Quranic verse about marrying women in twos, threes, and fours (4:3). Regarding the permissibility of marriage, the verse is Zahir; regarding the specified numbers, it is Nass because the numbers are the intended point. Mufassar is a text that has been made so clear that no possibility of takhsis or ta'wil remains. The example is the verse about the angels prostrating to Adam (15:30). The word 'kullahum' removes the possibility that only some prostrated, and 'ajma'un' removes the possibility that they prostrated separately. Thus the text becomes Mufassar. However, Mufassar still admits the possibility of naskh (abrogation). The author addresses a potential objection: the verse about angels is actually an information (khabar) from Allah, and information cannot be abrogated because that would imply falsehood. The response is that the verse is primarily a command (amr) in form, but as information it is not subject to naskh; however, the category of Mufassar is about texts that are commands, not information. The better example is the command to fight polytheists completely (9:36) which is a command and thus can be abrogated. Muhkam is the highest level, where even naskh is impossible. This can be either because the text inherently does not admit abrogation (like verses about Allah's attributes) or because abrogation became impossible after the death of the Prophet (peace be upon him). The latter is called Muhkam li-ghayrihi. The author concludes by noting that the distinctions between these categories matter only when there is an apparent conflict between texts; otherwise, all are authoritative.

Key Terms
الظاهر (az-Zahir): A text whose meaning is immediately understood from its wording alone by a competent speaker of the language, without the speaker intending that specific meaning.
النص (an-Nass): A text whose clarity is enhanced because the speaker intentionally used it to convey that meaning; it is more explicit than Zahir.

Mufassar, Muhkam, and the Confines of Naskh
Defining Mufassar and Muhkam with examples and nuance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish Mufassar from Muhkam and explain why Mufassar can still be abrogated (naskh) while Muhkam cannot.

Core Idea
Mufassar is immune to takhsis and ta'wil but not to naskh; Muhkam is immune to all change.

After Zahir and Nass come the higher levels of clarity: Mufassar and Muhkam. Mufassar is a text so clear that no specification or alternative interpretation remains possible, yet it can still be abrogated. Muhkam is the highest level, immune even to abrogation. This lesson explores the example of the angels' prostration (Quran 15:30) and the important distinction between Muhkam li-dhatihi and Muhkam li-ghayrihi.

Guided Path
1
Check if takhsis/ta'wil is possible
If the text has no possibility of takhsis or ta'wil, then it is either Mufassar or Muhkam. This is the second branch of the decision tree.
2
Check if naskh is possible
If naskh is possible, it is Mufassar. If not possible, it is Muhkam.
Naskh is possible via revelation during the time of the Prophet (SAW). After him, it is impossible.
3
Analyze the example: Quran 15:30
The verse 'So the angels prostrated, all of them together' (15:30) is analyzed: 'kullahum' removes takhsis, 'ajma'un' removes ta'wil, making it Mufassar. But as a command (to prostrate), it can be abrogated.
The verse is primarily information, but the example is given to show the structure; the author notes a better example is the command to fight polytheists.
4
Distinguish Muhkam li-dhatihi and li-ghayrihi
Muhkam li-dhatihi: texts that by nature do not admit naskh (e.g., verses about tawhid). Muhkam li-ghayrihi: texts that were Mufassar but became Muhkam after the Prophet's death because revelation ceased.

Then it will be seen whether there is any possibility of ta'wil and takhsis in it or not. If there is possibility of takhsis and ta'wil, then there are two forms: either the speech was brought to express that meaning or not. If the speech was not brought to express that meaning, it is called Zahir; if the speech was brought to express that meaning, it is called Nass. And if there is no possibility of takhsis and ta'wil, then also two forms: is there possibility of naskh and tabdil or not? If there is, it is Mufassar; if not, it is Muhkam. Now he is defining all of them. Az-Zahir: It is a speech whose meaning becomes known to the listener as soon as he hears it, i.e., he understands the meaning from the mere wording, provided he knows the language. An-Nass: It is a speech that has more clarity than Zahir, because its clarity is not only from the wording but also from a reason referring to the speaker, i.e., the speaker intentionally brought this speech to express that meaning, whereas in Zahir the speech is not intentionally brought to express the meaning. Example: 'So marry what is good to you of women, two, three, and four.' This speech is Zahir regarding the permissibility and lawful nature of marriage, because a native speaker understands that upon hearing it; and regarding the number, it is Nass because it was brought specifically to express the number. Al-Mufassar: It is what has increased in clarity over Nass in such a way that no possibility of takhsis and ta'wil remains, like Allah's saying: 'So the angels prostrated, all of them together.' Its ruling is obligation definitively, without possibility of takhsis or ta'wil, except that it admits the possibility of naskh. When it increases in strength and the intended meaning is made firm against change, it is called Muhkam. Commentary: Mufassar is speech that has more clarity than Nass; it has no possibility of takhsis or ta'wil, while Nass does. For example, the verse about the angels prostrating: it is Zahir regarding all angels prostrating, but there is a possibility of takhsis (some might have prostrated, not all); when 'kullahum' is added, it becomes Nass, removing that possibility. But there remains a possibility of ta'wil (they might have prostrated separately, not together); when 'ajma'un' is added, that possibility is removed, making it Mufassar. However, Mufassar admits the possibility of naskh, but that possibility is confined to the time of the Prophet (peace be upon him). After him, the entire Quran is Muhkam with no possibility of naskh or change. Question: How can this speech be Mufassar? It is Muhkam, because Allah is informing that all angels prostrated, and Allah's information cannot be abrogated, else it would entail lying. Answer: The original speech admits possibility of naskh, but due to an external factor (it being information from Allah) that possibility is removed. A better example is Allah's saying: 'Fight the polytheists completely' – this is a command, not information, so it admits naskh. The ruling of Mufassar is that the established rule is decisive and certain; there is no possibility of takhsis or ta'wil. Therefore, believing in and acting upon a ruling from Mufassar is obligatory. 'Except that it admits naskh': But note that the possibility of naskh was only during the time of the Prophet (peace be upon him). After him, even that possibility is gone, so Mufassar becomes Muhkam li-ghayrihi, because naskh occurs through revelation and revelation ceased after the Prophet. 'When it increases in power': Now defining Muhkam. Muhkam is speech whose meaning is stronger and firmer than Mufassar, with no possibility of naskh or change, like 'Allah is All-Knowing of everything' and 'Jihad continues until the Day of Judgment'. Benefit: There are two ways of having no possibility of naskh: (1) The speech by its nature does not admit naskh, like verses of tawhid and divine attributes – that is Muhkam li-dhatihi. (2) The possibility of naskh ended after the Prophet's departure – that is Muhkam li-ghayrihi. And the difference between these names appears only when they conflict, otherwise all are decisive.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed explanation of the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity in usul al-fiqh. The author is defining four categories: Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, and Muhkam. First, he establishes a decision tree: If a text can be subject to takhsis (specification) or ta'wil (interpretive modification), then look at the speaker's intent. If the text was not brought specifically for that meaning, it is Zahir; if it was, it is Nass. If the text cannot be subject to takhsis or ta'wil, then check if it can be abrogated (naskh). If it can, it is Mufassar; if not, it is Muhkam. Then he defines each. Zahir is a text whose meaning is immediately apparent to a competent speaker of the language, without need for external evidence. Nass is a text whose clarity is enhanced because the speaker intentionally brought it to convey that meaning; it is more explicit than Zahir. The example given is the Quranic verse about marrying women in twos, threes, and fours (4:3). Regarding the permissibility of marriage, the verse is Zahir; regarding the specified numbers, it is Nass because the numbers are the intended point. Mufassar is a text that has been made so clear that no possibility of takhsis or ta'wil remains. The example is the verse about the angels prostrating to Adam (15:30). The word 'kullahum' removes the possibility that only some prostrated, and 'ajma'un' removes the possibility that they prostrated separately. Thus the text becomes Mufassar. However, Mufassar still admits the possibility of naskh (abrogation). The author addresses a potential objection: the verse about angels is actually an information (khabar) from Allah, and information cannot be abrogated because that would imply falsehood. The response is that the verse is primarily a command (amr) in form, but as information it is not subject to naskh; however, the category of Mufassar is about texts that are commands, not information. The better example is the command to fight polytheists completely (9:36) which is a command and thus can be abrogated. Muhkam is the highest level, where even naskh is impossible. This can be either because the text inherently does not admit abrogation (like verses about Allah's attributes) or because abrogation became impossible after the death of the Prophet (peace be upon him). The latter is called Muhkam li-ghayrihi. The author concludes by noting that the distinctions between these categories matter only when there is an apparent conflict between texts; otherwise, all are authoritative.

Key Terms
المفسر (al-Mufassar): A text that is so clear that it excludes any possibility of takhsis or ta'wil, but still admits the possibility of naskh.
المحكم (al-Muhkam): A text that admits no possibility of takhsis, ta'wil, or naskh; it is firmly established against any change.

Mufassar and Muhkam: Definitions and Naskh Possibility
Defining mufassar and muhkam with examples and naskh possibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mufassar and muhkam, distinguish between them, and apply the Hanafi rule regarding naskh possibility.

Core Idea
Mufassar is the highest textual clarity possible before revelation ends; after the Prophet's death, it becomes muhkam.

In this passage, we explore two critical levels of textual clarity in Hanafi usul: mufassar and muhkam. While both texts are free from takhsis and tawil, mufassar still permits naskh during the prophetic era. The discussion clarifies why the verse about angelic prostration is initially mufassar but effectively muhkam after the cessation of revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining characteristics of mufassar
Note that mufassar is free from takhsis and tawil but allows naskh. This distinguishes it from nass and zahir.
2
Trace the example of the angel prostration verse
Understand how the progression from zahir to nass to mufassar occurs through the addition of 'kullahum' and 'ajma'un'.

3
Distinguish between muhkam li-dhatihi and muhkam li-ghayrihi
Muhkam li-dhatihi is inherently immune to naskh (e.g., verses on tawhid); muhkam li-ghayrihi becomes immune after revelation ceases.
4
Apply the ruling of mufassar
The ruling established by mufassar is definitive, requiring both belief and action, despite the theoretical possibility of naskh during the Prophet's time.
After the Prophet's death, the possibility of naskh is removed.

Understand why mufassar becomes muhkam after the Prophet's death
Naskh occurs only via revelation; after the Prophet's death, revelation ceased, so any remaining mufassar text is permanently binding.

Resolving Conflict Among Textual Categories (Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, Muhkam)
Explaining how to resolve conflict among textual categories by precedence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) to resolve apparent conflicts between textual evidences.

Core Idea
When texts conflict, the clearer category wins: muhkam > mufassar > nass > zahir.

In Hanafi usul, not all textual evidences carry the same weight. The categories of zahir, nass, mufassar, and muhkam each indicate a ruling with certainty, but their clarity differs. When two texts appear to conflict, the scholar must prefer the one with higher clarity. This lesson explains the hierarchy and demonstrates it through concrete examples, including the well-known dispute over the mustahada's wudu.

Guided Path
1
Understand the hierarchy of categories
Memorize the order from highest to lowest clarity: muhkam, mufassar, nass, zahir. Each category necessitates certainty, but when they conflict, the higher takes precedence.
This hierarchy is unique to Hanafi usul; other schools may use different criteria for tarjih.
2
Work through the zahir vs. nass example
Consider the verses on marriage: 'Uhilla lakum ma waraa dhalikum' (zahir, general permission) and 'Fankihoo ma taba lakum... mathna wa thulatha wa ruba' (nass, explicit limit of four). According to the hierarchy, nass overrides zahir, so marriage is limited to four.
3
Examine the nass vs. mufassar example (mustahada)
Two hadiths: 'Al-mustahadatu tatawadda li kulli salatin' (nass) and 'Al-mustahadatu tatawadda li waqti kulli salatin' (mufassar due to the explicit word 'waqt'). The mufassar is higher, so wudu is required for the time of each prayer, not each prayer itself.
This is the Hanafi position; the Shafi'i school follows the nass version.

Al-Khafi: The Hidden Text in Hanafi Usul
Defining al-khafi with an example from theft

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-khafi, distinguish it from other unclear texts by identifying the external factor, and apply the ruling that it requires ijtihad.

Core Idea
Khafi: meaning hidden by custom, not wording—requires ijtihad to apply.

In the Hanafi classification of clear and unclear texts, al-khafi is the opposite of zahir. While zahir is immediately clear, khafi's meaning is hidden due to an external reason such as customary terminology. Understanding khafi is crucial for correctly applying Quranic and Sunnah rulings to specific cases. This lesson unpacks the definition and illustrates it with the example of applying the theft verse to pickpockets and grave robbers.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of khafi
Khafi is a text whose intended meaning is hidden due to an external factor, not the wording. Note the condition: the obscurity comes from outside the text (e.g., custom, specialized terminology).
The wording itself is clear; the hiddenness stems from an external cause.
2
Distinguish from other categories
Compare khafi with mujmal (ambiguous wording) and mushkil (difficult meaning). Khafi is unique because the wording is clear, but application is hidden.
This distinction is essential for correct classification.
3
Work through the example
The verse on theft (Q. 5:38) uses the general term sariq (thief). However, pickpockets (tarrar) and grave robbers (nabbash) are known by distinct names. The verse's applicability to them is hidden (khafi) because of this custom.
Custom (urf) is the external factor here.
4
State the ruling for khafi
Since the meaning is not self-evident, ijtihad (legal reasoning) is required to determine whether the ruling applies. This contrasts with zahir, which is immediately operative.
The mujtahid must exert effort to resolve the hiddenness.

Al-Khafi: Hidden Text and Its Ruling
Defining khafi with its ruling and example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khafi, identify its cause, state its hukm, and apply the definition to a Quranic example.

Core Idea
Khafi text hides its meaning due to an external cause; seeking (talab) reveals it.

In Hanafi usul, texts are classified by clarity. Khafi is the first level of hiddenness, opposite of zahir. Unlike deeper obscurities, khafi can be resolved by active search. This lesson unpacks its definition and shows how it works in practice using the theft verse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its opposite
Recognize that khafi is defined as the opposite (ḍidd) of ẓāhir. This establishes the binary in the clarity hierarchy.
Ẓāhir is clear; khafī is hidden.
2
Analyze the cause of hiddenness
The hiddenness is due to an external factor (ʿāriḍ), not the wording itself. Identify that factor – e.g., customary usage that restricts a general term.
If the wording itself were unclear, it would be mushkil or mujmal, not khafī.
3
State the ruling (ḥukm) of khafī
The ruling is to seek (ṭalab) the intended meaning. This requires pondering whether the hiddenness stems from an increase or decrease in the essential meaning.
Increase → stronger inclusion; decrease → exclusion.
4
Apply to the theft verse example
The verse 'As-sāriq wa as-sāriqatu faqṭaʿū aydiyahumā' is clear for a common thief but hidden (khafī) for a pickpocket (ṭarrār) and grave-robber (nabbāsh). The external cause is that custom does not call them 'thief'.
Through seeking, we find pickpocket has increase (steals while awake), so included a fortiori; grave-robber has decrease (unprotected property), so excluded.
5
Distinguish khafī from higher obscurities
Khafī is the lightest hiddenness – resolved by a little effort. Higher levels (mushkil, mujmal, mutashābih) require more or are unsolvable by human effort.
This places khafī as the first of four hidden categories.

Understanding Mushkil: Speech Requiring Reflection after Seeking
Defining mushkil as speech requiring reflection after seeking due to resemblance with other meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a mushkil text, distinguish it from other levels of obscurity, and apply the Hanafi process of seeking (talab) and reflection (ta'ammul) to resolve it.

Core Idea
Mushkil requires deliberate reflection after seeking possible meanings because the word shares forms with other words (homonymy).

In our study of the Hanafi textual hierarchy, we have encountered clear texts (nass) and those obscured by external factors (khafi). Now we reach mushkil, where ambiguity is internal to the word itself. This lesson defines mushkil and demonstrates the two-step process necessary to uncover its intended meaning, using a Quranic example that carries significant fiqh implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of mushkil
Locate the definition in the Arabic text: 'الْمُشْكِلُ وَهُوَ مَالَا يَنَالُ الْمُرَادُ مِنْهُ إِلَّا بِالتَّأَمُلِ فِيهِ بَعْدَ الطَّلَب لِدُخُولِهِ فِي أَشْكَالِهِ'. Note the key components: 'لا ينال المراد' (meaning not attained), 'بالتأمل بعد الطلب' (by reflection after seeking), and 'لدخوله في أشكاله' (due to entry into its forms).
The term 'أشكاله' refers to similar words (homonyms).
2
Distinguish mushkil from khafi
Contrast mushkil with the previous level, khafi. While khafi's hiddenness comes from an external factor (like a missing context), mushkil's ambiguity is inherent: the word itself has multiple meanings (homonymy). This requires a deliberate two-step process: first seeking all possible meanings, then reflecting to select the correct one.
3
Analyze the ruling (hukm) of mushkil
The ruling is 'التأمل فيه بعد الطلب' (reflection after seeking). This means the scholar must first exhaust all possible meanings (talab), then apply reflection (ta'ammul) to disambiguate. This is not arbitrary but uses tools like context (qarinah), linguistic analysis, and fiqh principles.
In the Hanafi school, this process is essential for deriving rulings from apparent ambiguous texts.
4

Mujmal: Ambiguous Text Requiring Suspension
Defining mujmal and its ruling of suspension.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mujmal, state its ruling (tawaqquf), identify the example given, and distinguish it from mufassar.

Core Idea
Mujmal is speech with crowded meanings requiring suspension until clarification.

In the Hanafi textual hierarchy, mujmal represents the highest level of ambiguity. Unlike mufassar which is self-explanatory, mujmal leaves the intended meaning undetermined. The Hanafi ruling of tawaqquf (suspension) prevents arbitrary interpretation and demands patience for authoritative bayan (clarification). This lesson unpacks the definition and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposite
The passage begins by stating that mujmal is the opposite of mufassar. Recognize that mufassar is clear speech; mujmal is its counterpart.
Mufassar is one of the clearest levels; mujmal is one of the most ambiguous.
2
Understand the definition: crowding of meanings
Mujmal is defined as speech 'in which meanings are crowded together' (izdiham al-ma'ani). This crowding causes the intended meaning to be confused (ishtabaha).
The confusion is not due to deficiency but due to multiple plausible interpretations.
3
Recognize the condition for resolution
The intended meaning is not known except through clarification (bayan) from the speaker (or from the mujmil). No internal indicator can resolve it.
Bayan can come from the Sunnah, context, or additional revelation.
4
Apply the ruling: suspension
The ruling for mujmal is tawaqquf (suspension). One must withhold judgment, believing that a real meaning exists, until the bayan arrives.
Suspension is active; it requires belief in a haqiqi meaning, not rejection.
5
Analyze the example: ayat al-riba
The verse on riba (Quran 2:275-279) is given as an example. The term 'riba' in Arabic could refer to multiple types of increase. The Sunnah later clarified its precise scope.
This shows how a mujmal text operates; the clarification (bayan) came via the Prophet's explanations.

Four Types of Textual Inference (Istidlal)
Introduces the four valid methods of istidlal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the four types of istidlal from the nazm and classify given examples into each type.

Core Idea
Four valid ways to infer rulings from sacred texts: expression, indication, connotation, and requirement.

This passage is a foundational taxonomy in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It presents the four and only four valid methods for a mujtahid to derive legal rulings from the textual composition (nazm) of the Quran and Sunnah. Understanding these four—ibarah, isharah, dalalah, and iqtida'—is essential for correct istinbat and for distinguishing valid inference from invalid methods.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching category
Recognize that this passage deals with the fourth division of the book: 'ways of standing upon the rulings of the textual composition.' It is a meta-level classification of inference methods.
2
List the four types with Arabic names
Memorize the four terms: istidlal bi ibarat al-nass, istidlal bi isharat al-nass, istidlal bi dalalat al-nass, istidlal bi iqtida' al-nass. Their sequence is fixed in the text.
3
Determine the criterion for each type
For ibarah: the meaning was intentionally intended. For isharah: the text was not intentionally directed to that meaning but it is indicated. For dalalah: the meaning is linguistically understood (mafhum muwafaqah). For iqtida': the meaning is not linguistically understood but is necessary for the text to be valid legally or rationally.
4
Understand the boundary – invalid methods
Any method other than these four is considered istidlalat fasidah (invalid inference). Note that the Hanafi school is strict about limiting valid inference to these four.
5
Map classical examples to each type
Ibarah: 'Aqimu al-salah' – explicit command. Isharah: 'wa lahum azwaj mutahharah' – implicitly they are also pure for them. Dalalah: 'la taqul la huma uff' – linguistically implies prohibition of hitting. Iqtida': 'wa al-sariq wa al-sariqah faqta'u aydiyahuma' – requires conditions like nisab for the ruling to be valid.

Commentary
This passage is a usul definition introducing the fourth division of the book: the ways a mujtahid can infer rulings from the textual composition (nazm). There are four approved methods: (1) Istidlal bi ibarat al-nass: inference from the explicit wording, where the text was intentionally put forth to convey that specific meaning. (2) Istidlal bi isharat al-nass: inference from the indication of the text, where the meaning is not the primary intent but is implied indirectly. (3) Istidlal bi dalalat al-nass: inference from the connotation, where the meaning is understood linguistically from the text without being the primary intended meaning. (4) Istidlal bi iqtida' al-nass: inference from the requirement of the text, where a meaning is necessary for the text to be valid (legally or rationally), even though not explicitly stated or linguistically implied. Any other method of inference is considered invalid (istidlalat fasidah). The commentary explains each, clarifying the criterion: whether the meaning was intentionally intended, not intended but still indicated, linguistically understood, or necessary for the text's validity.

Key Terms
استدلال بعبارة النص (istidlal bi ibarat al-nass): Inference from the explicit wording of the text that was intentionally put to convey that meaning. Example: 'And establish prayer' directly orders prayer.
استدلال بإشارة النص (istidlal bi isharat al-nass): Inference from an indication in the text that is not the primary intent but is implied. Example: 'And for them are wives purified' implies they are also purified for them.
استدلال بدلالة النص (istidlal bi dalalat al-nass): Inference from the linguistic connotation of the text, where the meaning is understood from the language but not the primary intent. Example: 'Do not say to them 'uff'' implies prohibition of hitting.
استدلال باقتضاء النص (istidlal bi iqtida' al-nass): Inference from a meaning that is necessary for the text to be valid, even if not explicitly stated or linguistically implied. Example: 'The thief: cut off his hand' requires the hand to be at a certain joint and the theft to reach the nisab.

Distinguishing Naqis from Fasid: Time as Container vs. Integral to the Act
Explains why prayer at makruh times is naqis not fasid using qiyas with fasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between naqis (deficient) and fasid (invalid) by analyzing whether time is a container (zarf) or integral to the act, and evaluate the legal consequence (qada vs no qada) for prayer and fasting.

Core Idea
Time a container for prayer makes it naqis; time integral to fasting makes it fasid.

This passage from Al-Fayd al-Hijazi unpacks a key Hanafi usul principle: the same temporal defect (being a disliked time) affects different acts differently based on how the act relates to time. Understanding why prayer at sunrise is merely deficient while fasting on the day of Nahr is invalid is essential for correctly applying rulings and for grasping the deeper logic of Hanafi legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the acts and their rulings
Note that prayer at disliked times is ruled deficient (naqis) while fasting on the day of Nahr is ruled invalid (fasid). Determine the legal consequence for each if one begins and then breaks the act.
Prayer requires qada; fasting does not.
2
Analyze the relationship between time and the act
Determine whether time is a container (zarf) that surrounds the act but is not part of its essence, or whether it is integral (mi'yar) to the act's definition.
Time is a container for prayer; time is integral to fasting.
3
Apply the talil (legal reasoning)
Trace how the defect in the time transfers (or does not) to the act. If time is only a container, the defect makes the act deficient but not invalid. If time is integral, the defect makes the act invalid.
The cause (sabab) of obligation does not make the time part of the act.
4
Derive the legal consequence
Conclude that because prayer is deficient, commencing it creates liability (daman) for qada. Because the fast is invalid, commencing it does not create liability.
This is the practical difference in ruling.
5
Compare with the opposite case
Imagine if time were integral to prayer (like fasting) – then prayer would be invalid. This contrast clarifies the exact role of time in each act.
This comparative reasoning is central to Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage is a talil (legal reasoning) explaining why prayer performed at times such as sunrise and sunset is considered deficient (naqis) but not invalid (fasid), whereas fasting on the day of Nahr is invalid. The key distinction is that time is merely a container (zarf) for prayer; it does not enter into the reality of the prayer. The prayer is composed of specific actions and is not defined by the time. Therefore, the defect of the time (attribution to shaytan) does not render the prayer invalid, only deficient. However, time is integral to fasting; the fast is defined by the time period. Hence, the defect of the time (day of Nahr being prohibited for fasting) makes the fast invalid. The author clarifies that because prayer is deficient in these times, if one begins a prayer and then breaks it, he must make it up (qada), because the act is still considered a valid obligation (though deficient). In contrast, if one begins a fast on the day of Nahr, it is invalid from the start, so no qada is required. This ruling is from the Zahir al-Riwayah.

Key Terms
ناقص (naqis): Deficient; a prayer that is valid in essence but lacking in perfection due to a temporal defect.
فاسد (fasid): Invalid; an act that is fundamentally unsound and not legally acceptable.
ظرف (zarf): Container; time that surrounds the act but is not part of its essence or definition.
سبب (sabab): Cause; time is the cause for the obligation of prayer but not a constitutive element.

Summary of five khabar categories and their epistemic values

Core Idea
The Hanafi classification of khabar ranks reports from certain knowledge to permissible conjecture.

In Hanafi usul, not all reports from the Prophet carry the same authority. This passage categorises them into five distinct types, each with a specific epistemic weight that determines whether belief and action are required, recommended, or merely permitted. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for weighing evidence and deriving rulings.

Bayan al-Darurah: Definition and First Two Types
Defines bayan al-darurah and explains its first two types with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define bayan al-darurah, identify its four types, and explain how the first two types function as valid forms of clarification in Hanafi usul, using the Quranic verse and the silence of the Lawgiver as examples.

Core Idea
Bayan al-darurah: clarification through structural implication or contextual silence, not explicit speech.

In this lesson, we examine a key concept in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: bayan al-darurah (clarification out of necessity). Unlike ordinary bayan which uses explicit statements, bayan al-darurah operates through contextual cues and structural implications. The passage from Al-Husami defines this category and illustrates its first two types using a Quranic verse and the silence of the Prophet (ﷺ) and Companions. Understanding this concept is essential for properly deriving rulings from implicit evidence.

Guided Path
1
State the definition of bayan al-darurah
Read the definition: 'a type of bayan that occurs by means of something not originally designated for it.' Note that it arises out of necessity (darurah).
The necessity triggers the need for clarification when explicit speech is absent.
2
Identify the first type: in the meaning of mantuq bihi
Examine the Quranic example (4:11). The verse first establishes that both parents inherit (partnership), then specifies the mother's third. From this structure, the father's share (remainder) is implied. This clarification is 'in the meaning of an uttered statement' because it is derived from the structure itself.
This type relies on the organization of words, not mere silence.
3
Identify the second type: established by dalalat al-hal
Two sub-examples: (a) The Prophet's silence upon seeing permissible acts indicates permissibility. (b) The Companions' silence on not compensating bodily benefits in the walad al-maghrur case, in a context requiring explanation, serves as a valid bayan.
Context and need (hajat) are crucial for silence to be considered bayan.
4
Recognize the third and fourth types mentioned
The passage briefly introduces the third type (by necessity of removing deception) with examples of the pre-emptor's silence and the master's silence regarding a slave's trading. The fourth type is not detailed here.
These types will be explored in subsequent lessons.
5
Contrast with the Shafi'i position on silence
Note that some other schools require explicit speech (qawl) for bayan. The Hanafi school defends the validity of silence as bayan when supported by contextual need, citing the practice of the Salaf.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf (difference of opinion) within usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage explains 'Bayan al-Darurah' (بیان ضرورت), which is a type of clarification (bayan) that takes effect through something not originally intended for that purpose — such as silence or contextual circumstances. The author (Al-Husami) states that bayan al-darurah has four types, and this passage details the first two and introduces the third.

The first type is that which is 'in the meaning of the uttered statement' (فی معنی المنطوق بہ). The example is the verse from Surah An-Nisa (4:11): 'And his parents inherit him, so for his mother is the third.' The beginning of the verse ('his parents inherit him') establishes that both parents share in the inheritance, but does not specify shares. Then specifying the mother's share as one-third implicitly clarifies that the father gets the remaining two-thirds. This clarification is achieved through the structure of the statement itself, not by mere silence — it is considered like an explicit statement.

The second type is that which is established by the indication of the speaker's state (دلالة حال المتکلم). The example given is the silence of the Prophet (ﷺ) upon seeing permissible transactions (such as buying, selling, eating, drinking) — his silence without objection indicates that the act is permissible (ḥaqīqah, i.e., correct and allowed). This is a form of bayan because the context necessitates it. A further sub-type is the silence of the Companions (like Sayyiduna 'Umar) in a situation of need for clarification: the case of the 'walad al-maghrur' (child of the deceived). When a man was deceived into marrying a slave woman thinking she was free, 'Umar ruled that the child is free upon payment of its value, but he did not assess any compensation for the bodily benefits (manāfi') the man had enjoyed from the child. All the Companions remained silent on this point, indicating that bodily benefits are not subject to compensation (ḍamān). This silence, in a context requiring explanation, serves as a valid bayan.

The third type (introduced but not fully detailed here) is bayan by necessity of removing deception, exemplified by the silence of the pre-emptor (shafi') when he sees a property sold without objecting — his silence implies consent — and the silence of a master when he sees his slave engaging in trade — his silence implies permission.

In the Urdu commentary, the author elaborates on these points, emphasizing that silence can be a valid source of law when accompanied by contextual indications.

Key Terms
بيان الضرورة (bayan al-darurah): A type of clarification that occurs through means not originally intended for expression, such as silence or contextual circumstances, due to necessity.
منطوق به (mantuq bihi): The explicit uttered statement; bayan in the meaning of mantuq means the ruling is derived as if it were spoken.
دلالة حال (dalalat al-hal): Indication derived from the speaker's state or context, used to infer a ruling.

Rule of Waw with Complete and Incomplete Sentences
Explaining when waw requires partnership in the predicate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that 'waw' indicates absolute conjunction to determine whether a second sentence shares the predicate of the first, based on whether the second sentence is complete or incomplete.

Core Idea
Waw joins sentences; only an incomplete second sentence shares the predicate of the first.

The conjunction 'waw' (and) is common in legal speech. However, its legal effect depends on the structure of the sentences it joins. In this passage, the commentator explains when the second sentence shares the predicate of the first and when it does not. This is crucial for correctly applying rulings in utterances like divorce and permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sentences joined by waw
Look for the conjunction 'waw' between two clauses. Determine what each clause expresses (e.g., a divorce declaration or a condition).
The waw itself does not define the legal effect; the structure of the second clause does.
2
Check if the second sentence is complete or incomplete
A complete sentence (jaml kamilah) has both a subject and a predicate (or equivalent). An incomplete sentence (jaml naqisah) lacks a necessary element like a predicate.
Completeness is judged independently: does the second clause convey a full meaning without relying on the first?
3
Apply the rule: complete → no partnership; incomplete → partnership required
If the second sentence is complete, it does not share the first sentence's predicate. If incomplete, it must share the predicate to be meaningful.
The text states: 'When it is complete, the evidence for partnership disappears.'
4
Verify with the given examples
Examine 'Hādhihi ṭāliqun thalāthan wa hādhihi ṭāliqun' (complete → second wife gets one divorce) vs 'Hādhihi ṭāliqun wa hādhihi' (incomplete → both share predicate, each gets one divorce).
The first example is a complete sentence; the second lacks a predicate in the second clause.
5
Extend to conditional divorce statements
In 'In dakhalti al-dāra fa-anti ṭāliqun wa ṭāliqun', the second 'ṭāliqun' is incomplete (lacking a conditional clause), so it shares the same condition as the first, not a new one.
This avoids assuming an extra omitted condition; the second divorce is suspended on the same condition as the first.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory commentary (sharh) on the usul text Al-Husami. It examines a grammatical rule concerning the conjunction 'waw' and its effect on legal utterances, specifically in cases of divorce and marriage permission. The author first clarifies a prior discussion about two sisters and permission, refuting the claim that the invalidity of both marriages is due to waw indicating simultaneity. Instead, it is because the first utterance is complete on its own, but adding the second creates a dependency (like a condition) that forces the two permissions to occur together, resulting in the forbidden combination of two sisters. The central rule is then stated: when waw joins a complete sentence (with its predicate), the second sentence does not share in the predicate of the first. Evidence is given: 'This one is thrice divorced and this one is divorced' – the first wife gets three divorces, the second only one, because the second sentence is complete. The rule is contrasted with incomplete sentences, where sharing is required, as in 'This is divorced and this' (where the second lacks a predicate, so it shares, each getting one). This principle is also applied to conditional divorces: 'If you enter the house, you are divorced, and divorced' – the second divorce is linked to the same condition, not an independent one. The author emphasizes that the requirement for partnership stems from the incompleteness of the second sentence. The passage demonstrates how usul al-fiqh uses linguistic analysis to derive legal rulings, a hallmark of Hanafi methodology.

Key Terms
و (waw): The conjunction 'and'; in legal speech, it indicates absolute conjunction without implying simultaneity or sequence, unless context dictates.
جملة كاملة (jaml kamilah): A complete sentence that can stand alone and convey a full meaning, typically having both a subject and a predicate (or equivalent).
جملة ناقصة (jaml naqisah): An incomplete sentence that lacks a necessary element, such as a predicate, and requires sharing or connection to the previous sentence to be meaningful.
مشاركة في الخبر (musharakah fil-khabar): Partnership in the predicate; the second sentence's predicate is the same as the first sentence's predicate, so the legal effect (e.g., divorce) applies to both.
