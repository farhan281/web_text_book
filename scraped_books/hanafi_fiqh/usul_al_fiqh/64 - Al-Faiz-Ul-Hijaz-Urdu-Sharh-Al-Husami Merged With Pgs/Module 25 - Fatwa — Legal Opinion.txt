Transfer of Liability in Coerced Manumission
Applying ikrah rules to coerced freeing of a slave

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi conditions for transfer (intiqal) of legal responsibility to coerced acts, distinguishing between sensory and non-sensory dimensions.

Core Idea
Manumission stays with the speaker; liability shifts to the coercer.

This passage applies the Hanafi theory of ikrah (coercion) to coerced manumission, demonstrating how the same act can have multiple legal dimensions each attributed to different parties. The key is the two conditions for transfer: the act must be intellectually conceivable from the coercer and not sensory. The utterance (manumission) fails the second condition, so it stays with the coerced person; the financial destruction (itlaf) satisfies both, so it transfers to the coercer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two dimensions of coerced manumission
Recognise that the single act of freeing a slave has two aspects: the utterance (sensory) and the destruction of property (legal/conceptual). The ruling differs for each.
Utterance → manumission and wala'; destruction → liability for value.
2
Recall the two conditions for transfer (intiqal)
For an act to be attributed to someone other than the direct doer, it must be (1) intellectually conceivable (mutasawwir al-sudur) for that person to perform, and (2) not sensory (hissi). The first condition is about possibility; the second excludes acts like speech.
These conditions are Hanafi-specific.
3
Apply conditions to the utterance of manumission
The utterance is sensory (speech). It cannot transfer because the coercer cannot speak through the coerced person's tongue. Therefore, manumission and wala' remain with the coerced person.
Contrast with the coercer's liability.
4
Apply conditions to the destruction (itlaf)
The financial loss is not sensory; it is a legal consequence. It is conceivable that the coercer caused it. Both conditions are met, so liability transfers to the coercer, who must pay the slave's value.
Analogy: coerced killing of a slave – destruction transfers to coercer.

و انتقال امر حکمی ہے نہ کہ امیر حسی۔ مصنف عالی، اس تمہید پر یہ مسئلہ متفرع کر رہے ہیں کہ اگر کسی کو اکراہ کامل کے ذریعہ غلام آزاد کر نے پر مجبور کرنے کیا گیا، تو مکرہ ہی اعتاق کا تکلم کرنے والا ہوگا اور مکرہ کی طرف ہی اعتاق کی نسبت ہوگی حتی کہ غلام کی ولاء اسی کے لئے ہوگی، اور اس فعل اعتاق میں مکروہ کو مکرہ کا آلہ قرار دیکر اعتاق کی نسبت مکرہ کی طرف نہ کریں گے، کیونکہ نسبت و انتقال کے لئے پہلی شرط یہ تھی کہ وہ فعل مکرہ سے معقول و متصور الصدور ہو، اور یہاں یہ ممکن نہیں ہے کہ مکرہ کی زبان سے مکرہ کی طرف سے اعتناق کا صدور ہو سکے، اس لئے کہ انسان دوسرے کی زبان سے تکلم نہیں کر سکتا لیکن اعتاق کی وجہ سے مکرہ غلام کی قیمت کا ضامن ہوگا، کیونکہ عبد معتق کا اتلاف مکرہ کی طرف منسوب ہوسکتا ہے، اس میں اس کی دونوں شرطیں پائی جاتی ہیں، اس لئے کہ مالیت عبد کا اتلاف ایسا امر ہے جس کا صدور مکرہ سے ممکن ہے، متصور ہے، اور اس کا صدور حسا نہیں ہے، لہذا انتقال کی دونوں شرطوں کے پائے جانے کی وجہ سے اتلاف مکرہ کی طرف منتقل ہو جائے گا، لہذا ایسا ہو جائے گا گویا مالیت کو مکر و نے تلف کیا ہے ، اور مکر و صرف آلہ ہے، پس غلام کی مالیت کا ضمان مکرہ پر آئے گا۔ لانه منفصل عنه : - ایک سوالِ مقدر کا جواب ہے، جس کی تقریر یہ ہے کہ جب اعتناق مکرہ کی طرف منتقل نہیں ہوسکتا، تو پھرا تلاف جو اعتاق کے ضمن میں پایا جارہا ہے وہ بغیر عتق کے کیسے مکرہ کی طرف منسوب ہوگا ؟ جواب کا حاصل یہ ہے کہ اتلاف اور اعتاق لازم ملزوم نہیں ہیں کہ اعتاق نہ ہو تو اتلاف بھی نہ ہو، بلکہ بعض صورتوں میں دونوں ایک دوسرے سے الگ بھی ہو جاتے ہیں، جیسے کسی کے غلام کو قتل کر دیا، تو اتلاف تو ہے اعتاق نہیں ہے، اور اتلاف اپنی اصل ہی سے یعنی ابتدا ہی سے انتقال کا احتمال رکھتا ہے پس اگر کسی نے کسی کو اپنا غلام قتل کرنے پر مجبور کیا اور مکرہ نے غلام کو قتل کر دیا تو یہ قتل واتلاف مکرہ کی طرف منتقل ہوگا، مکر وصرف آلہ ہوگا، تو جب اتلاف ابتدا ہمکرہ کی طرف منتقل ہو سکتا ہے تو وہ اختلاف جو اعتاق کے ضمن میں متحقق ہوا ہے ، وہ بھی اعتماق سے جدا ہو کر مکرہ کی طرف منتقل ہو جائے گا۔ وهذا عندنا
And transfer is a legal matter, not a sensory one. The noble author derives this issue from the premise: if someone is compelled through complete coercion to free a slave, then the coerced person is the one who utters the manumission, and the manumission is attributed to him, such that the slave's wala' (clientage) is for him. In this act of manumission, we do not regard the coerced person as a tool of the coercer and attribute the manumission to the coercer, because the first condition for attribution and transfer is that the act be intellectually conceivable and possible for the coercer to perform, and here it is not possible for the manumission to occur from the coercer's tongue by the coerced person, because a person cannot speak with another's tongue. However, due to the manumission, the coercer becomes liable for the slave's value, because the destruction of the freed slave can be attributed to the coercer; both its conditions are present, for the destruction of the slave's value is an act whose occurrence from the coercer is possible and conceivable, and its occurrence is not sensory. Therefore, since both conditions of transfer are present, the destruction transfers to the coercer, so it becomes as if the coercer himself destroyed the value, and the coerced person is merely a tool. Thus, the liability for the slave's value falls upon the coercer. 'Because it is separate from it' is an answer to a presumed question, which is: when the manumission cannot transfer to the coercer, how can the destruction found within the manumission be attributed to the coercer without the manumission? The gist of the answer is that destruction and manumission are not inseparable such that if there is no manumission, there is no destruction. Rather, in some cases they separate from each other, such as killing someone's slave: there is destruction but no manumission. And destruction in its essence, from the beginning, has the potential for transfer. So if someone compels another to kill his slave and the coerced person kills the slave, this killing and destruction transfers to the coercer, and the coerced person is merely a tool. So when destruction can transfer from the beginning to the coercer, then the destruction that occurs within the manumission also separates from the manumission and transfers to the coercer. And this is our position.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed application of the principles of coercion (ikrah) to the specific case of coerced manumission. The author, commenting on the matn, explains that when a person is forced under complete coercion to free a slave, the act of uttering the manumission is attributed to the coerced person (mukrah), not to the coercer (mukrih). This is because the utterance is a speech act that must come from the speaker himself; no one can speak using another's tongue. Therefore, the legal consequences of the utterance, such as the wala' (clientage) of the slave, belong to the coerced person. However, the financial liability for the slave's value is a different matter. The destruction (itlaf) of property inherent in freeing the slave is an act that can be attributed to the coercer, because it is intellectually conceivable for the coercer to have caused the destruction, and it is not a sensory act requiring direct physical performance. The conditions for transfer (intiqal) are met: the act is conceivable (mutasawwir al-sudur) and it is not sensory (hissi). Therefore, the coercer is liable for the slave's value, as if he himself destroyed it. The text then addresses an objection: if the manumission itself does not transfer, how can the accompanying destruction transfer? The answer is that destruction and manumission are not necessarily linked; they can be separated. As proof, the author points to the case of killing another's slave: there is destruction without manumission. Thus, the destruction aspect can be isolated and attributed to the coercer. This nuanced ruling shows the Hanafi method of carefully distinguishing between the legal aspects of an act, attributing each to the appropriate party based on the conditions of transfer.

Key Terms
اکراہ کامل (ikrah kamil): Complete coercion that renders the act permissible (mubah) in Shariah, such that the coerced person is excused
اعتاق (i'taq): The act of freeing a slave; a verbal declaration that effects manumission
اتلاف (itlaf): Destruction of property; here, the financial loss caused by manumission
ولاء (wala'): Patronage/clientage relationship between the freed slave and the manumitter
ضمان (daman): Liability, obligation to compensate for loss
