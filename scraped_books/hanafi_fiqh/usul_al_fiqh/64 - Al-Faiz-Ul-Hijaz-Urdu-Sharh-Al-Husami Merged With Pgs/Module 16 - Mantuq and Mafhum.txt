'Ibarat al-Nass and Isharat al-Nass: Definitions and Precedence
Defining two types of textual indication and their relative strength

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify examples of 'ibarat al-nass and isharat al-nass, and apply the Hanafi rule that 'ibarah takes precedence over isharah when they conflict.

Core Idea
Ibarah is the text's intended meaning; isharah is implied. When they clash, the explicit wins.

In this lesson, we explore two key types of textual indication in the Hanafi usul: 'ibarat al-nass and isharat al-nass. The author defines them and explains that both are definitive in obligating a ruling, but when they conflict, 'ibarah is given precedence. We will work through the example of menstruation duration to see how this principle operates in practice.

Guided Path
1
Define 'ibarat al-nass
Identify the ruling or meaning that the text was explicitly intended to convey. This is the direct, primary indication.
Example: The verse about the Muhajirun is explicitly meant to give them a share in spoils.
2
Define isharat al-nass
Identify a ruling or meaning that is also derived from the text but was not the primary intention. It is an implied or secondary indication.
Example: The same verse implies the Muhajirun lost their property in Mecca.
3
Apply the definitions to a Quranic example
Read the verse 'For the poor emigrants...' and distinguish what is 'ibarah (explicit share) and what is isharah (implied loss of property).
This builds your ability to recognize each type in a real text.
4
Observe a conflict scenario
Consider two hadiths: one explicitly states the maximum menstruation is ten days ('ibarah), another implicitly suggests fifteen days (isharah).
The conflict arises because both indications appear mutually exclusive.
5
Apply the rule of precedence
Apply the Hanafi rule: when 'ibarah and isharah conflict, 'ibarah takes precedence. Therefore, the maximum is ten days.
This demonstrates how the usul principle resolves the apparent contradiction.

So this matter is known from 'ibarat al-nass because the purpose of bringing the verse is exactly this. But from the words of the verse, another matter is also known: that these emigrants left their properties in Mecca and the disbelievers seized them, so they became owners, and the ownership of the emigrants ceased because Allah called them 'poor'. Therefore, this ruling is known from isharat al-nass, because it is known from the words of the verse, but the verse was not brought to explain this. Then he says that 'ibarat al-nass and isharat al-nass are equal in obligating the ruling, i.e., both are definitive, but if there is conflict between them, then 'ibarat al-nass will be given precedence. Example of conflict: As in the hadith where women are called deficient in intellect and religion, that their intellect is half and their religion is half. It was asked why intellect and religion are half? So the reason was given: intellect is half because the testimony of two women equals one man, and religion is half because half their life they sit (during menses) not praying or fasting. So the purpose of bringing the hadith is to explain that women are deficient in intellect and religion. Therefore, this matter is known from 'ibarat al-nass. But from the isharat of this hadith, it is known that the maximum duration of menstruation is fifteen days, as is the school of Imam Shafi'i. Because the Prophet said: half their life they sit, due to menstruation they do not pray or fast, and life is counted by years. So half a year would be the menstruation period. And the year is counted by months, so half a month meaning fifteen days would be the menstruation period. Thus, by isharat al-nass, the maximum duration of menstruation is established as fifteen days. And there is another hadith that the minimum duration of menstruation is three days and the maximum is ten days. From this hadith, the maximum of ten days is known from 'ibarat al-nass because this hadith was brought to explain this ruling. Therefore, there is conflict between the 'ibarat of the second hadith and the isharat of the first hadith, so precedence is given to 'ibarat, and the maximum duration of menstruation is ten days.

Commentary
This passage defines two types of textual indication: 'ibarat al-nass and isharat al-nass. 'Ibarat al-nass is the explicit meaning intended by the text, while isharat al-nass is an implicit meaning derived from the same text but not the primary intention. The author provides the example of the verse about the Muhajireen: the explicit ruling (share in spoils) is 'ibarah, while the implication that they lost their property is isharah. Both are definitive (qat'i) in obligating a ruling, but when they conflict, 'ibarah takes precedence. The example of menstruation illustrates this: hadith A (women deficient in religion) implicitly suggests a 15-day maximum (Shafi'i view), but hadith B explicitly states 10 days (Hanafi view). Since hadith B is 'ibarah, it prevails over the isharah of hadith A. This demonstrates the Hanafi principle of preferring explicit over implicit textual meanings.

Key Terms
عبارۃ النص ('ibarat al-nass): The ruling and meaning that is understood from the speech, and the speech was intentionally meant for it.
اشارۃ النص (isharat al-nass): What is established by the text like the first, except that the speech was not intended for it.
تعارض (ta'arud): Conflict between two textual indications that yield contradictory rulings.

Dalalat al-Nass: Definition and Legal Weight
Defining dalalat al-nass as a type of textual indication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dalalat al-nass, distinguish it from isharat al-nass, and identify its legal weight in deriving rulings.

Core Idea
Dalalat al-nass is the linguistic implication of a text that yields a definitive ruling without personal reasoning.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, texts carry multiple layers of indication. The previous lessons covered 'ibarah and isharah. This lesson introduces dalalat al-nass, the third category. You will learn how a text's linguistic meaning can establish a ruling without ijtihad, and why this category carries such authority that it can even set hudud punishments and kaffarat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition's core
Locate the phrase 'what is established by the meaning of the text linguistically, not by deduction through personal opinion.' This tells you that the derivation does not require ijtihad (independent reasoning).
Key distinction: the implication is from language itself, not from analogical reasoning.
2
Examine the example
The Quranic prohibition of saying 'uff' to parents (17:23) leads to the prohibition of hitting them. Understand why this is a direct linguistic implication: if the mildest expression is forbidden, then a stronger harmful act is even more obviously forbidden.
This example shows how dalalat al-nass works: it follows from the literal meaning without needing to think about causes or analogies.
3
Compare with isharat al-nass
The text states that rulings from dalalat al-nass are like those from isharah, but with a key difference: dalalat al-nass can establish hudud and kaffarat even when there is conflict, whereas isharah cannot.
This gives dalalat al-nass higher authority. 'Conflict' here means a competing text or evidence.
4
Note the exception
The text says 'except when there is conflict, unlike isharah.' This means that if a stronger textual indication opposes the dalalat al-nass, it may be set aside. However, even then, it still can be used for hudud/kaffarat – the exception applies only to the comparison with isharah.
This nuance is important for understanding the hierarchy of textual indications.
5
Apply the concept to a new text
Try to identify other examples from the Quran or Sunnah where a ruling is derived by dalalat al-nass. For instance, the prohibition of 'pinching' (if that is forbidden, then striking is worse).
This prepares you for the practice tasks.

Commentary
This passage defines dalalat al-nass, the third type of textual indication. It refers to a ruling derived from the linguistic meaning of the text without any personal reasoning (ijtihad). The famous example is the Quranic prohibition of saying 'uff' to parents (17:23). Linguistically, if saying 'uff' (a mild expression of displeasure) is prohibited, then hitting them is even more prohibited. This is not a personal analogy but a direct linguistic implication. The text states that dalalat al-nass has the same authority as isharat al-nass, with one distinction: it can even be used to establish hudud (fixed punishments) and kaffarat (expiation), which isharah cannot do when there is conflict. This highlights its strength as a definitive proof (qat'i) in Hanafi usul.
