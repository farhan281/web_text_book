The Prophet's Ijtihad as a Source of Law
Ruling on the Prophet's ijtihad as a source of law

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the Prophet's ijtihad and evaluate the Ash'ari counter-argument using the dalil from Quran 53:3.

Core Idea
The Prophet's ijtihad, when confirmed by Allah, becomes a definitive proof like revelation.

This lesson examines a key usul al-fiqh dispute: Did the Prophet (peace be upon him) practice ijtihad? The Hanafi school affirms it, while the Ash'aris deny it based on Quran 53:3. Understanding this debate clarifies the scope of the Sunnah as a source of law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi claim
State the Hanafi position that the Prophet practiced ijtihad when revelation ceased, and that his confirmed ijtihad is a definitive proof.
Note the phrase 'al-sahih 'indana' indicates the preferred view.
2
Identify the opponent's claim and dalil
The Ash'aris and Mutakallimun argue that the Prophet never practiced ijtihad because of Quran 53:3, 'Nor does he speak from desire.'
3
Apply qiyas to ilham
The text compares ijtihad to ilham (inspiration), which is a definitive proof for the Prophet. Recognize the analogical reasoning: both are forms of inner revelation that are definitive in his case.
This is an example of qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis accept the Prophet's ijtihad as part of the Sunnah, while opponents require explicit revelation for any ruling.

And connected to the sunan is the explanation of the method of the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) in manifesting the rulings of Shari'ah through ijtihad. There is disagreement in this section, and the correct view in our madhhab is that he used to practice ijtihad when hope of revelation was cut off regarding an issue he was faced with, and he was not left upon error. So when he was confirmed upon something of that, it is a definitive indication of the ruling, unlike what comes from others of bayan bi al-ra'y (ijtihad). And it is analogous to ilham (inspiration), for it is a definitive proof in his case, though not in the case of others in that capacity. Explanation: Before the explanation, as a preamble, know that revelation (wahy) is of two types: (1) manifest revelation (wahy zahir), and (2) inner revelation (wahy batin). Manifest revelation has three forms: (1) Jibril conveys a ruling after explaining, like the revelation of the Qur'an; (2) occurs through Jibril's indication without explanation; (3) through ilham (inspiration) in the Prophet's heart. Inner revelation is that which is obtained through contemplation and ijtihad regarding specific rulings. Now the author says: inner revelation, i.e., the Prophet's ijtihad, is also attached to the Sunnah, meaning that whatever ruling is manifested through his ijtihad has the same status as the verbal and practical Sunnah. The author says: there is disagreement among scholars whether he practiced ijtihad in matters where no revelation had come down. The Ash'aris and Mutakallimun hold that he did not practice ijtihad, because Allah said about him: 'Nor does he speak from desire' (53:3). Therefore, ijtihad is not part of his practice.

Commentary
This passage discusses two topics connected to the Sunnah: the Prophet's ijtihad and the laws of previous prophets. On ijtihad, the Hanafi view (as per the author) is that the Prophet did practice ijtihad when no revelation came, and his ijtihad, if affirmed by Allah (by not correcting it), becomes definitive proof—unlike the ijtihad of others which is merely zanni. This is compared to ilham (divine inspiration), which is definitive for him but not for others. On the second topic, the laws of previous prophets (shara'i' man qablana), the correct view is that those laws become binding on us only if they are narrated by Allah or His Messenger without criticism; otherwise, they are not. The commentary adds a useful classification of wahy: manifest (zahir) includes revelation through Jibril with explanation, without explanation, and through inspiration; inner (batin) is through ijtihad. The author affirms that the Prophet's ijtihad has the same legal force as the explicit Sunnah.

Key Terms
اجتهاد (ijtihad): The Prophet's exertion of reasoning to derive a ruling when no revelation exists. When confirmed, it becomes definitive proof.
إلهام (ilham): Divine inspiration in the Prophet's heart, considered definitive proof for him but not for others.

The Binding Nature of Previous Shariahs
Ruling on the authority of previous prophets' laws

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that previous shariahs become binding on us when narrated without rejection by Allah or His Messenger.

Core Idea
A previous prophet's law becomes our law if Allah or His Messenger narrates it without criticism.

This lesson explores another source of law attached to the Sunnah: the divine laws of previous prophets (shara'i' man qablana). The Hanafi view is that such laws are binding on us only if they are mentioned in the Quran or Sunnah without rejection. This principle helps us understand how narratives from earlier prophets (e.g., stories of Ibrahim, Musa) are used in Islamic jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Define the concept
Shara'i' man qablana refers to the revealed laws of prophets before Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him).
E.g., the law of Musa or Isa as recorded in the Quran.
2
Identify the condition for binding
The text states the condition: whatever Allah or His Messenger narrates from those laws without rejection is binding on us.
'Min ghayri inkar' means without disapproval or correction.
3
Apply to examples
Consider a rule from previous scripture mentioned in the Quran: if it is not rejected or abrogated, it becomes part of our Shari'ah.
E.g., the story of the Israelites' punishment for murder (Quran 2:72-73) is not adopted because it was a specific ruling.

On ijtihad, the Hanafi view (as per the author) is that the Prophet did practice ijtihad when no revelation came, and his ijtihad, if affirmed by Allah (by not correcting it), becomes definitive proof—unlike the ijtihad of others which is merely zanni. This is compared to ilham (divine inspiration), which is definitive for him but not for others. On the second topic, the laws of previous prophets (shara'i' man qablana), the correct view is that those laws become binding on us only if they are narrated by Allah or His Messenger without criticism; otherwise, they are not. The commentary adds a useful classification of wahy: manifest (zahir) includes revelation through Jibril with explanation, without explanation, and through inspiration; inner (batin) is through ijtihad. The author affirms that the Prophet's ijtihad has the same legal force as the explicit Sunnah.

Key Terms
شرائع من قبله (shara'i' man qablana): The divine laws of previous prophets, which become binding on us if narrated without rejection by Allah or His Messenger.

Definition of Tarjih and the First Type: Tarjih bi Quwwat al-Athar
Definition of tarjih and exposition of the first type.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define tarjih, state its conditions, and explain the first type (tarjih bi quwwat al-athar) with its example from wiping the head.

Core Idea
Tarjih prefers by quality, not quantity; strength of effect is the first basis.

When evidences appear to conflict, the Hanafi method is not to reject one but to prefer the stronger through tarjih. This passage defines tarjih and introduces its first type: preference by the strength of the legal effect. Understanding this principle is essential for resolving apparent contradictions in legal proofs.

Guided Path
1
Define tarjih
Tarjih is the preference of one of two equivalent proofs over the other due to a superior quality (wasf) within the proof itself.
Notice the condition: the proofs must be equivalent (mithlayn) in strength initially.
2
Identify the trigger
Tarjih is invoked when mu'arada (opposition) occurs between two equally strong proofs.
Without mu'arada, no need for tarjih.
3
Note the limits
Tarjih is not based on number or type of proof (e.g., one qiyas over another, or Book over Sunnah) but by an internal strength (quwwa).
Think of the analogy: a person with multiple injuries is not automatically preferred over one with a single injury.
4
Understand the first type
Tarjih bi quwwat al-athar: preference by the strength of the legal effect (athar) that the proof carries. The athar is a meaning (ma'na) in the proof; the stronger effect is more deserving.
This is akin to istihsan overriding qiyas.
5
Apply the example
The head is wiped (mas'h) because the effect of indicating lightness (takhfif) is stronger than the effect of considering it a pillar (rukn) requiring repetition (takrar).
The effect of wiping in lightness applies to all cases where purification is not conceivable, e.g., tayammum.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of a new fasl (chapter) on tarjih, which is the method of resolving conflicts between evidences (mu'arada). The author defines tarjih as giving preference to one of two equivalent proofs over the other due to a superior quality (wasf). Importantly, tarjih is not done by mere number of proofs (e.g., qiyas is not preferred over another qiyas, nor is the Book preferred over the Sunnah simply by being the Book); rather, it is by strength within the proof itself. The first type of tarjih mentioned is by strength of effect (quwwat al-athar). The 'athar' here refers to the legal effect or implication that the proof carries. If one proof has a stronger effect in establishing the legal ruling, it is given preference. This is similar to how istihsan (juristic preference) is used to override qiyas. The second type is tarjih by strength of establishment (quwwat thabat) over the ruling that is witnessed (i.e., a ruling that is directly indicated). An example is given: in the issue of wiping the head in wudu, Hanafis argue that wiping is established because it indicates lightness (takhfif), which is more appropriate for the head, as opposed to considering it a pillar (rukn) requiring repetition. The effect of wiping in lightness applies to all cases where purification is not conceivable, like tayammum. This passage gives the first of four types of tarjih; the remaining three are presumably covered later.

Key Terms
ترجیح (tarjih): The process of preferring one of two equivalent proofs over the other due to a superior quality (wasf) within the proof
أثر (athar): The legal effect or implication resulting from a proof; strength of effect (quwwat al-athar) is one basis for tarjih
قوَّة الأثر (quwwat al-athar): A type of tarjih where preference is given to the proof whose legal effect is stronger or more directly indicative of the ruling
