Causes of Legislation (Asbab al-Shara'i)
Explains the Hanafi principle that rulings are attached to specific causes (asbab) and proves it with consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the role of sabab in Hanafi legal theory and explain how the ijma' on prayer obligation for the insane demonstrates that rulings are attached to causes.

Core Idea
A ruling is not directly from the command; it is caused by a sabab, and the command demands performance of what is already obligatory.

Have you ever wondered why prayer is obligatory even for a person who is sleeping or unconscious? The Hanafi school teaches that rulings are not directly created by the command (amr) but are instead attached to specific causes (asbab) designated by the Shari'ah. This passage from Al-Husami explains this foundational principle and proves it using the consensus (ijma') of the jurists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concept of sabab
Read the first part of the passage and note how it defines sabab as the occasioning factor for rulings. List the examples given: Hajj by the House, fasting by the month, prayer by times, etc.
Sabab is distinct from 'illah (effective cause).
2
Understand the role of the command (amr)
The passage states that the command does not create obligation; rather, it is the demand to perform what has already become obligatory due to a prior cause. Consider the sale example: the contract (sabab) obligates the price, and the command demands payment.
3
Analyze the proof from ijma'
The passage cites the consensus that prayer is obligatory on someone who is sleeping, insane, or unconscious for less than a day and night. Even though such a person cannot be addressed by the command, the obligation remains. This proves that the obligation is tied to the cause (time) and not the ability to receive the command.
Thubut and dalala of this ijma' are both qati' (certain).
4
Distinguish sabab from 'illah
In Hanafi usul, sabab is the external occasion (e.g., time for prayer), while 'illah is the underlying effective cause. This passage focuses on sabab as the basis for obligation.
This distinction is critical for understanding Hanafi istinbat.

Commentary
This passage introduces the important usul concept of 'asbab al-shara'i' (causes of legislation). It states that every ruling in the Shari'ah—whether foundational (usul) like faith, or branches (furu') like acts of worship, transactions, punishments, and expiations—is attached to a specific cause (sabab) that the Lawgiver has designated as the occasioning factor. For example, Hajj is caused by the House (Ka'bah), fasting by the month (Ramadan), prayer by its times, punishments by their respective causes (e.g., theft causing amputation), and kaffarah by an act that is between prohibition and permission (e.g., breaking an oath). Transactions are linked to the continuation of the world through their practice. Faith is caused by the proofs of the universe's temporal origin. The passage then explains the role of command (amr): it is not the primary cause of obligation; rather, it is the demand to perform what has already become obligatory due to a prior cause. For example, in a sale, the contract itself (sabab) obligates the price, and the command (the Shari'ah's general demand) simply enforces its payment. The proof for this causality-based system is the consensus (ijma') of the jurists that prayer is obligatory on a sleeping person, an insane person, or one unconscious for less than a day and night, even though they are not presently commanded due to their state; the obligation is attached to the cause (time) and remains even when the command cannot be directly addressed. This demonstrates that rulings are not directly from the command but via causes. This is a distinctively Hanafi approach emphasizing the role of causes (asbab) in legal obligation.

Key Terms
سبب (sabab): A cause or occasioning factor placed by the Shari'ah upon which a ruling is dependent.
شرائع (shara'i'): The laws or rulings of the Shari'ah, plural of shari'ah.
اجماع (ijma'): Consensus of the mujtahidun of the ummah on a legal ruling after the death of the Prophet.

Nafs al-Wujub vs Wujub al-Ada: Hanafi Response to Asbab Denial
Refutes objection that asbab are redundant after divine command

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between nafs al-wujub and wujub al-ada to resolve the objection of tahsil hasil against asbab.

Core Idea
Nafs al-wujub from sabab, wujub al-ada from command – two distinct obligations, no repetition.

Some usuliyyun argue that asbab (causes) are unnecessary because the divine command alone creates obligation. They claim that recognizing both leads to tahsil hasil (acquisition of the acquired). This lesson presents the Hanafi response: obligation has two aspects. Nafs al-wujub (the very existence of obligation) is created by the sabab, while wujub al-ada (obligation to perform) is created by the command. The proof is the scholarly consensus (ijma) that a person who sleeps, faints, or becomes insane for less than a day-night still owes qada of missed prayers, even without being directly addressed by any command during that time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
State the opposing argument: if command creates obligation, then asbab are redundant; invoking both leads to repetition or tahsil hasil.
2
Present the Hanafi distinction
Explain the two-part response: nafs al-wujub comes from sabab, wujub al-ada from command. They are separate aspects of obligation.
3
Analyze the ijma proof
Examine the consensus evidence: a person ineligible for command (asleep/unconscious/insane) still owes qada, proving that the obligation existed from sabab, not from command.
Qada is a branch of ada, so the existence of qada implies the prior existence of wujub al-ada.
4
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf
Determine the practical consequence: the Hanafi view allows obligation to arise without direct command (e.g., for an insane person), ensuring no missed obligations; the opposing view would require a command, potentially excusing such persons.
5
Confirm no repetition
Conclude that since nafs al-wujub and wujub al-ada are distinct, there is no tahsil hasil or repetition.

'And the command is only for obligation' - This is a response to an objection. Some usuliyyun completely deny asbab and say: when obligation is established through command, what need is there for asbab? If obligation is also established through asbab, then repetition would be necessary, and if both command and asbab are causes, then tahsil hasil (acquisition of the acquired) would be necessary. The author responds that here two things are separate: one is nafs al-wujub (obligation itself) and the other is wujub al-ada (obligation of performance). Nafs al-wujub occurs from sabab, and wujub al-ada occurs from command. For example, if someone makes a sale, the nafs wujub of the price upon the buyer occurs from the sale, and when the seller demands 'give me my price,' then wujub al-ada becomes obligatory from that. So nafs wujub occurred from the sabab (sale), and wujub al-ada from the demand (command) of the seller. And the proof of this principle (that nafs wujub comes from sabab and wujub al-ada from command) is that the fuqaha have consensus (ijma) on the following: if a person remains in a state of sleep, unconsciousness, or insanity for less than a day and night, for example covering the time of four prayers, then qada of those prayers is obligatory upon him, and qada is a branch of ada (performance). So it is known that ada was obligatory upon him, even though he was not eligible for command and address (khitab). Thus, it is known that obligation did not come from command, because he was not eligible for command and address. Inevitably, the obligation came from sabab. So this proves that nafs wujub occurs from sabab and wujub al-ada occurs from command. Therefore, the objection of repetition or tahsil hasil does not arise.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul dispute addressing an objection: if the divine command already creates obligation, then asbab are redundant and would lead to repetition. The author (presumably al-Husami or a commentator) responds by distinguishing between nafs al-wujub (the very existence of obligation) and wujub al-ada (the obligation to perform that which is already obligated). Nafs al-wujub is created by the sabab, while wujub al-ada is created by a command (amr or khitab). The proof is the scholarly consensus (ijma) that a person who sleeps, faints, or becomes insane for less than a full day-night still owes qada of missed prayers. Since they were not addressed by any command during their state, the obligation (wujub) must have come from the sabab (time entering), not from a direct command. This resolves the objection: sabab and command do not duplicate obligation; they create different aspects.

Key Terms
نفس وجوب (nafs al-wujub): The very existence of an obligation, created by the sabab.
وجوب ادا (wujub al-ada): The obligation to perform an act after it has become due, created by a command (amr).

Acceptance of Mursal: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Presents Hanafi acceptance of mursal and Shafi'i conditional acceptance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi basis for accepting mursal of Tabi‘i and evaluate the Shafi‘i conditions for acceptance.

Core Idea
The mursil’s omission indicates certainty; Hanafis accept unconditionally, Shafi‘is require external support.

The status of mursal hadith—where a narrator omits the link above him—has been a point of contention between schools. While all accept mursal of a Companion, the dispute arises for mursal of a Tabi‘i or Taba‘ Tabi‘i. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rationale of trust in the mursil’s certainty versus the Shafi‘i insistence on corroborating evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of mursal
Distinguish between mursal of a Companion (accepted by consensus) and mursal of those below the Companion (Tabi‘i, Taba‘ Tabi‘i).
The text divides mursal into three types but focuses on the latter two.
2
State the Hanafi position and its rational proof
Hanafis accept mursal of Tabi‘i and Taba‘ Tabi‘i. The proof: the mursil’s omission shows full confidence in the hadith; doubt would have led him to preserve the link.
This is a dalil aqli (rational argument).
3
Contrast with the Shafi‘i position
Imam al-Shafi‘i and the Zahiris reject such mursal unless supported by a definitive proof, sound analogy, general acceptance, or a known trustworthy chain.
These conditions reflect the Shafi‘i concern about unknown narrators in the missing link.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to Shafi‘i conditions
The Hanafi response: the mursil’s character and knowledge suffice; imposing additional conditions is excessive.
This highlights a methodological difference in evaluating hadith authenticity.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Hanafis use such mursal as binding proof in law; Shafi‘is only accept them with external corroboration.
This affects which hadiths are considered valid evidence for rulings.

As for the mursal from the Companion: The first type, i.e., mursal of a Companion, is interpreted as having heard (samā‘) meaning that the Companion heard the hadith from the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, and by consensus (bilā jamā‘) the mursal of a Companion is accepted. Expression by Silence in Situations of Need
Defines third type using examples of need and harm prevention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when silence constitutes a binding expression according to Hanafi usul and apply the principle to new scenarios.

Core Idea
Silence in a context of need is a binding expression to prevent harm.

In Hanafi usul, not all silence is equal. This lesson explores a critical principle: when a reasonable person would speak if something were wrong, their silence indicates consent or abandonment. This is used to prevent harm and uncertainty in practical affairs. We will examine examples from early Islamic practice and a key dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for silent expression
The silence must occur in a situation where expression is needed (mawḍi‘ al-ḥājah) or to prevent harm (daf‘ al-gharar). The person who remains silent must be in a position to speak and not prevented by duress or anger.
This condition distinguishes this type from mere silence.
2
Analyze the first example: the maghrūr case
Caliph Umar ruled that the deceived husband must pay the value of the children but not the bodily benefits earned. The Companions' silence on this part is taken as an expression that bodily benefits are not guaranteed.
The silence of the Companions in a situation needing expression is a form of tacit ijma.
3
Apply the principle to the preemptor (shafī‘)
If the preemptor knows of a sale and remains silent, his silence is interpreted as abandoning his right of pre-emption. This protects the buyer from harm.
The ruling is based on preventing harm to the buyer, not on a literal statement.
4
Examine the dispute over the master's silence
If a master sees his slave trading and is silent, the Hanafi school considers this permission. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees because silence could be due to anger. The Hanafi response: in a context of need, silence is consent.
This dispute illustrates the different weight given to contextual inference.
5
Derive the usul rule from these examples
The common thread: silence in a situation of need is treated as an expression (bayān) to eliminate harm and uncertainty. This is a recognized source of proof in Hanafi usul.
This principle is related to istihsan and preventing harm.

In Hanafi usul, a Companion's opinion is a proof that can override qiyas.

Scope of Valid Ijma': The Hanafi Position
Presents different views on the scope of valid consensus and states the Hanafi position

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whose consensus is binding and identify the views that are rejected in the text.

Core Idea
Consensus of any era's just, mujtahid scholars is binding, not limited to Sahaba, Madinah, or Ahl al-Bayt.

In this passage, the author opens the chapter on Ijma' (consensus) by listing several incorrect views that restrict consensus to specific groups. The Hanafi school affirms that consensus is a valid proof from any generation of scholars, provided they are just and qualified. We'll examine each dismissed view and understand the Hanafi criteria for valid consensus.

Whose Ijma' Counts? The Hanafi Position on Consensus
Opening of Ijma' chapter presenting three restrictive views and the Hanafi expansive view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three opposing views on whose Ijma' is authoritative, explain the Hanafi position, and evaluate the Hanafi response to the first two schools.

Core Idea
Ijma' is not limited to any specific group; the consensus of just and mujtahid scholars of every age is a binding proof.

In this opening of the chapter on Ijma', the author presents three schools that restrict consensus to specific groups: Companions, people of Medina, and the Prophet's family. The Hanafi school, however, adopts an expansive view: the consensus of just and mujtahid scholars of any age is a definitive proof. This lesson unpacks the dispute, examines the opponents' proofs, and shows how the Hanafi response avoids logical defects.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi ruling from the matn: the consensus of just and mujtahid scholars of every age is a proof (hujjah). Note the conditions: 'adalah and ijtihad.
The Hanafi school does not limit Ijma' to a specific era or location.
2
Identify the first opposing school and its dalil
Recognize the first school (Dawud al-Zahiri, Shia, one narration from Ahmad) that restricts Ijma' to Companions. Their proof is from Qur'anic verses like 'You are the best nation' and 'Thus We have made you a middle nation', which they claim are addressed to Companions only.
The matn does not name the schools explicitly, but the commentary identifies them.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response to the first school
The Hanafi response points out two defects: (1) If the verses are specific to Companions, then after their death, no Ijma' could occur because all Companions must agree – an impossibility after their passing. (2) Companions who embraced Islam after the revelation would be excluded, contradicting the notion that their consensus is considered.
This shows that restricting the verses leads to logical absurdities.
4
Identify the second opposing school and its dalil
The second school (Imam Malik) restricts Ijma' to the people of Medina. Their proof is the hadith 'Medina is like a bellows, it removes its impurities' and the special status of Medina as the Prophet's city.
5
Analyse the Hanafi response to the second school
The Hanafi response (implied in the matn) is that the hadith only shows the virtue of Medina, not that consensus is limited to its people. The Hanafi position accepts Ijma' from any region as long as the scholars are just and mujtahid.
The matn explicitly rejects restriction to a specific group.

Conditions of Ijma: Number, Persistence, and Opposition
Explains conditions for valid ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for valid ijma according to the Hanafi school and identify which types of opposition do not invalidate consensus.

Core Idea
Ijma is immediate upon agreement; no number condition; heretic opposition only matters if on a non-core issue.

Ijma, or consensus of mujtahids, is a primary source of Sharia. However, its validity depends on several conditions debated among scholars. This lesson examines the Hanafi view on the number of mujtahids required, whether persistence until death is needed, and how opposition from heretics (ahl al-hawa) and non-mujtahids affects the formation of ijma.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition regarding the number of mujtahids
Determine whether the text requires a minimum number or accepts any number, including a single mujtahid. Note the proof cited for the single-mujtahid view.
The Quranic verse 'Indeed Ibrahim was an ummah' is used to argue that one person can represent the ummah.
2
Analyze the condition of persistence until death
Understand the majority view that ijma is concluded immediately upon agreement, not after death. Identify the author's refutation.
The possibility of retraction during life does not delay the binding nature of ijma.
3
Assess the effect of opposition from heretics (ahl al-hawa)
Distinguish between opposition on the very issue of heresy and opposition on other matters. Note the classification of innovators leading to disbelief.
Mujassimah and Rawafid who believe in distortion of Quran are treated as disbelievers; their opposition is disregarded.
4
Apply the rule for opposition from non-mujtahids
Differentiate between matters requiring ijtihad (where only mujtahids' consensus matters) and transmitted matters (where laity's agreement is also necessary).
Examples: marriage, divorce vs. number of rak'ahs.
5
Synthesize the conditions for valid ijma
Summarize the conditions: no number condition, immediate binding, heretic opposition on core heresy ignored, lay opposition only matters in non-ijtihadi issues.
This gives a clear framework for evaluating claims of ijma.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the conditions and classifications of ijma (consensus) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author begins by addressing the condition of the number of mujtahids. The majority (جمہور) hold that no specific number is required for ijma to be valid; rather, the consensus of all mujtahids, however many, suffices. They argue that requiring a large number like tawatur is unnecessary because ijma itself is a special virtue of this ummah. The debate then moves to whether the statement of a single mujtahid in a given era can constitute ijma. Some say yes, citing the Quranic verse 'Indeed Ibrahim was an ummah' (Quran 16:120) where the word 'ummah' applies to one person. Others say no, because ijma linguistically implies a group. Next, the author refutes the condition that mujtahids must remain on their consensus until death. According to the majority, once they agree, ijma is immediately established, even if some later retract. Then, the effect of opposition from 'ahl al-hawa' (people of desires) is discussed. If their opposition is on the very issue that defines their heresy, it is disregarded; otherwise, it prevents ijma. Innovators whose innovation leads to disbelief (e.g., Mujassimah, Rawafid regarding Quranic distortion) are treated as disbelievers; their opposition is irrelevant. For those whose innovation does not lead to disbelief, there are several scholarly views. The author of al-Husami adds that if the innovator does not actively invite others to his innovation, his opposition is considered. Regarding opposition from non-mujtahids (laity and scholars who are not ahl al-ra'y), the rule depends on the subject: in matters requiring ijtihad (like marriage, divorce, trade), the agreement of mujtahids alone suffices; in transmitted matters (like text of Quran, number of rak'ahs), the agreement of all, including laity, is required because no ijtihad is involved. Finally, the passage classifies ijma into four ranks: (1) ijma of the Companions by explicit statement (the strongest); (2) ijma by explicit statement of some and silence of others (weaker because silence is less explicit); (3) ijma of later scholars on a ruling with no prior contrary opinion; (4) ijma of later scholars on a ruling where a prior contrary opinion existed. For the fourth rank, some scholars deny it is true ijma; the Hanafi view is that it is valid but carries different evidential weight—like mashhur hadith if no prior disagreement, and like sahih hadith if prior disagreement existed.

Key Terms
اجماع (ijma'): The consensus of all mujtahids of the Muslim community in a given era on a religious ruling.
مجتهد (mujtahid): A jurist qualified to derive rulings from primary sources directly.
اهل الهوى (ahl al-hawa): People of desires or heretics, those who follow their whims in matters of religion, often associated with bid'ah.
سكوت (sukut): Silence of a mujtahid after another has expressed an opinion, taken as tacit approval.

Classifies four ranks of ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the four ranks of ijma and compare their evidential strength in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Ijma of Companions by explicit text is strongest; silence-based consensus is weaker; ijma after prior disagreement is like sahih hadith.

Not all consensus is equal. The Hanafi school classifies ijma into four ranks based on the era, the explicitness of agreement, and the presence of prior disagreement. This lesson explores each rank and its weight in legal evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first and strongest rank
Recognize the ijma of the Companions with explicit text (nass). Understand why it is the strongest: no disagreement, includes Ahl al-Madinah and the Prophet's family.
This rank is considered qat'i (definitive).
2
Differentiate the second rank: explicit statement and silence
Note that when some Companions explicitly state a ruling and others remain silent, the consensus is weaker because silence is less explicit.
Sukut (silence) indicates tacit approval but is inferior to nass.
3
Examine the third rank: ijma of later scholars without prior opposition
This is the consensus of scholars after the Companions on a ruling where no earlier contrary opinion is known. It is compared to mashhur hadith.
Mashhur hadith is well-known but not mutawatir.
4
Analyze the fourth rank: ijma after prior disagreement
When later scholars agree on a ruling that had a previous contrary opinion, some scholars deny this as ijma. The Hanafi view accepts it but considers it like sahih hadith in strength.
The death of the previous opponent does not nullify his opinion, but the later consensus carries weight.
5
Apply the ranking to determine evidential weight
Given a specific ruling, identify which rank of ijma it falls under and what that implies for its binding nature.
First rank is binding (qat'i); second is also binding but weaker; third is binding; fourth is binding but with lower rank.

Has reached the level of tawatur, because with tawatur it is impossible for them to agree upon an error. And the proof of the majority is that the authority of ijma is a virtue and honor of this ummah, therefore there is no condition of number. Then the majority differ: if in a time there is only one mujtahid, is his statement a proof or not? Some say it is a proof and his statement ranks as ijma because there is no other mujtahid besides him, so the word 'ummah' applies to him; the proof is 'Indeed Ibrahim was an ummah' — the term 'ummah' is applied to a single individual, our master Ibrahim (peace be upon him). This is the position of the majority. The author refutes those who hold that persistence until death is a condition and that ijma is concluded only after death, because during life there is a possibility of retracting. 'Wa bi-mukhalafati ahli al-hawa': Opposition of the people of desires does not affect ijma, but their opposition does not affect the very matter due to which they are attributed to desires and misguidance. For example, ijma was concluded on the virtue of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, and the Rawafid oppose it; their opposition is not considered because their attribute of Rafd is due to their rejection of Abu Bakr's virtue. However, if the Rawafid oppose another issue, their opposition is considered such that ijma is not concluded. There is some detail: if a mujtahid's innovation leads to disbelief, he is like an unbeliever; his statement is not considered, like the Mujassimah who affirm a body for Allah, and the Rawafid who believe in distortion of the Quran. They are like disbelievers; their statement is not considered, so their opposition does not affect ijma. If his innovation does not lead to disbelief, there are three views: (1) his statement is absolutely not considered; (2) his statement is absolutely considered; (3) his statement is considered for himself but not for others. There is a fourth view, held by the author of al-Husami: if the innovator does not invite people to his innovation, even if he is known for it, then his opposition is considered. 'Wa la bi-mukhalafati man la ra'y lahu': The author says that in matters requiring opinion and ijtihad, where mujtahids have reached ijma, the opposition of the laity has no effect, nor does opposition of scholars who are not people of opinion (ahl al-ra'y), like rulings on marriage, divorce, and trade. In matters not requiring opinion and ijtihad, such as transmission of the Quran and number of rak'ahs, for ijma, the agreement of both scholars and laity is necessary, so if some laymen oppose, ijma is not concluded because in these matters mujtahids and non-mujtahids are equal; therefore, agreement of all is necessary, otherwise ijma is not concluded. Then ijma has ranks: the strongest is the ijma of the Companions with explicit text (nass), because there is no disagreement in it, and among them are the people of Madinah and the family of the Messenger (peace be upon him). Then that which is established by explicit text of some and silence of the rest, because silence in indicating approval is inferior to explicit text. Then the ijma of those after the Companions on a ruling in which no contrary opinion of their predecessors has appeared. Then their ijma on a statement in which there had been a prior contrary opinion. Scholars have differed on this category: some say this is not ijma because the death of the opponent does not nullify his statement. In our view, the ijma of scholars of every age is a proof both in matters where disagreement preceded and where it did not; however, in matters where no disagreement preceded, it is like the mashhur type of hadith, and in matters where disagreement preceded, it is like the sahih type of hadith.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the conditions and classifications of ijma (consensus) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author begins by addressing the condition of the number of mujtahids. The majority (جمہور) hold that no specific number is required for ijma to be valid; rather, the consensus of all mujtahids, however many, suffices. They argue that requiring a large number like tawatur is unnecessary because ijma itself is a special virtue of this ummah. The debate then moves to whether the statement of a single mujtahid in a given era can constitute ijma. Some say yes, citing the Quranic verse 'Indeed Ibrahim was an ummah' (Quran 16:120) where the word 'ummah' applies to one person. Others say no, because ijma linguistically implies a group. Next, the author refutes the condition that mujtahids must remain on their consensus until death. According to the majority, once they agree, ijma is immediately established, even if some later retract. Then, the effect of opposition from 'ahl al-hawa' (people of desires) is discussed. If their opposition is on the very issue that defines their heresy, it is disregarded; otherwise, it prevents ijma. Innovators whose innovation leads to disbelief (e.g., Mujassimah, Rawafid regarding Quranic distortion) are treated as disbelievers; their opposition is irrelevant. For those whose innovation does not lead to disbelief, there are several scholarly views. The author of al-Husami adds that if the innovator does not actively invite others to his innovation, his opposition is considered. Regarding opposition from non-mujtahids (laity and scholars who are not ahl al-ra'y), the rule depends on the subject: in matters requiring ijtihad (like marriage, divorce, trade), the agreement of mujtahids alone suffices; in transmitted matters (like text of Quran, number of rak'ahs), the agreement of all, including laity, is required because no ijtihad is involved. Finally, the passage classifies ijma into four ranks: (1) ijma of the Companions by explicit statement (the strongest); (2) ijma by explicit statement of some and silence of others (weaker because silence is less explicit); (3) ijma of later scholars on a ruling with no prior contrary opinion; (4) ijma of later scholars on a ruling where a prior contrary opinion existed. For the fourth rank, some scholars deny it is true ijma; the Hanafi view is that it is valid but carries different evidential weight—like mashhur hadith if no prior disagreement, and like sahih hadith if prior disagreement existed.

Key Terms
نص (nass): Clear, explicit text from the Quran or hadith that conveys an unequivocal ruling.
سكوت (sukut): Silence of a mujtahid after another has expressed an opinion, taken as tacit approval.
مشهور (mashhur): A category of hadith that is well-known but not mutawatir; it requires three or more narrators in each tier but does not reach the level of certainty.
صحيح (sahih): Authentic; for hadith, it means a rigorously sound chain and text. For ijma, it indicates a binding but not the highest rank.

The First Two Levels of Ijma: Explicit and Tacit Consensus
Classifies ijma into four ranks and details the first two, with arguments for and against tacit consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between explicit and tacit consensus and evaluate the Hanafi argument for the validity of tacit consensus.

Core Idea
Tacit consensus (ijma sukuti) is valid but weaker than explicit consensus, and its acceptance depends on rejecting alternative explanations for silence.

Consensus (ijma) is a primary source of Islamic law, but not all consensus carries the same weight. The Hanafi school classifies ijma into four ranks. The first two—explicit consensus (ijma sarih) and tacit consensus (ijma sukuti)—are foundational. Explicit consensus is definitive, while tacit consensus, based on the silence of scholars after a fatwa spreads, is accepted by the majority but disputed by Imam al-Shafi'i and others. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the epistemological hierarchy of proofs in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two ranks of consensus
First, read the passage and note the two types: explicit consensus (all Companions verbally agree) and tacit consensus (some state, others remain silent). Understand the criteria for each.
The passage mentions four ranks but details only the first two.
2
Understand explicit consensus (ijma sarih)
Recognize that explicit consensus is the strongest: it is definitive, like a mutawatir hadith, and its rejecter becomes a disbeliever. Example: the caliphate of Abu Bakr.
This rank is uncontested among scholars.
3
Understand tacit consensus (ijma sukuti)
Tacit consensus occurs when some scholars give a ruling and others remain silent after it spreads. The Hanafi position is that this silence indicates approval, making it valid consensus, though weaker than explicit.
The lower rank is due to silence being a weaker indicator than explicit verbal agreement.
4
Examine the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof rests on two points: (1) requiring explicit agreement from all is impractical and would prevent any consensus; (2) customarily, silence after a fatwa spreads indicates agreement because if it were false, mujtahids would speak out.
The first proof is practical necessity; the second is based on the default assumption about mujtahids.
5
Analyze the opposing argument and Hanafi response
The opposing argument (from al-Shafi'i et al.) lists alternative explanations for silence: awe, fear, or lack of opportunity. They cite Ibn Abbas's silence before Umar on ‘awl. The Hanafi response rejects these as contrary to the apparent state of mujtahids and questions the authenticity of the Ibn Abbas story.
The Hanafi response relies on the character of the Companions and earlier scholars.

This is the strongest consensus because there is no disagreement in it; it is the consensus of all Companions, including the people of Medina and the family of the Prophet. The second proof is that if the matter were not true, a mujtahid would not keep silent, because silence in such matters is forbidden; making the truth known is necessary. Since he remained silent, it proves the truth of the matter. Regarding tacit consensus, it is reported from Imam al-Shafi'i that this is not consensus at all and is not a proof. Among our scholars, Isa ibn Abban also held this view, as did Abu Bakr al-Baqillani of the Ash'aris, some Mu'tazila, and Dawud al-Zahiri. Their proof is that silence is not always evidence of consent and agreement. Sometimes a person remains silent out of awe of the speaker, sometimes due to the age or rank of the person opposite, sometimes because he has not had the opportunity to reflect on the issue. There are many instances where `Umar submitted to the truth. Therefore, it is incorrect that Ibn `Abbas remained silent out of fear.

Types of Tabi'in Consensus and the Dispute Over Post-Sahabi Ijma
Defines the third and fourth ranks of ijma and the dispute over the fourth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of Tabi'in consensus, explain why the fourth type is disputed, and evaluate the opposing argument.

Core Idea
Consensus of Tabi'in: strong as mashhur hadith, but when it overrides Sahaba disagreement, its validity is contested.

In the Hanafi usul framework, the consensus (ijma) of the Tabi'in holds significant authority, but not all types are equally accepted. This lesson explores two subcategories: the third rank—consensus on new matters—and the fourth rank—consensus on a matter where the Sahaba themselves differed. We then examine the scholarly dispute over the binding force of the fourth type, with the Zahiris and Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal rejecting it as a valid consensus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the third type
The third type of ijma is the consensus of the Tabi'in on a matter that never arose among the Sahaba. This is like a mashhur hadith in authority. The example given is istisna' (manufacturing contract).
Istisna' is a contract to order something custom-made, e.g., shoes.
2
Identify the fourth type
The fourth type is the consensus of the Tabi'in on a matter where the Sahaba themselves held differing opinions. The Tabi'in then agree on one of those opinions. The example is the sale of the umm walad (slave mother) – Ali allowed it, Umar forbade it, and later Tabi'in agreed on prohibition.
Umm walad: a slave woman who bears her master's child.
3
State the dispute over the fourth type
The Zahiris, Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal, and others argue that this fourth type is not a valid consensus because the opposing Sahaba's opinion remains valid even after their death. Death does not nullify a Sahaba's statement; otherwise, all four madhhabs would be invalidated.
The Hanafi stance is that the fourth type is a proof (hujjah) but not definitive (qat'i).
4
Analyze the opponents' dalil
The opponents' key argument: if an opposing Sahaba dies, his opinion does not become void. Therefore, full consensus of the entire ummah is not achieved, so the Tabi'in agreement is not binding ijma. They cite that otherwise, the four schools would be nullified (since earlier imams disagree).
This argument rests on the permanence of a Sahaba's qawl even after death.
5
Apply the Hanafi perspective
The Hanafis do not accept the fourth type as absolute ijma; rather, it is a proof (hujjah) akin to a solitary hadith. It can be used to override qiyas or prefer one view, but it does not achieve the certainty of consensus without prior disagreement.

The Transmission of Ijma: Mutawatir vs Ahad
Rule on transmission modes of ijma and their authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ijma transmitted by mutawatir and ahad chains in terms of epistemic and practical authority, and apply the Hanafi rule that ahad-transmitted ijma is binding in practice and takes precedence over qiyas.

Core Idea
Ijma transmitted by mutawatir is certain; ijma transmitted by ahed obligates action but not certainty.

This passage clarifies that the authority of transmitted ijma depends on its mode of transmission. If every generation's scholars unanimously report a consensus, it reaches the level of mutawatir—absolutely certain. If it comes through individual chains, it is like ahad hadith: certain in origin but only probabilistically transmitted. The Hanafi school still holds such ijma as binding in practice and overrides qiyas. Understanding this hierarchy prevents both overestimating and underestimating transmitted ijma.

Mutawatir yields certainty ('ilm yaqini). Ahad yields probability (zann) but remains authoritative.
The text says mutawatir is like a mutawatir hadith; ahad is like a single hadith.

Apply the practical consequences
Mutawatir ijma: denial is kufr. Ahad ijma: obligates action (wajib al-'amal) but not certainty.

Transmission of Ijma' and Its Levels of Certainty
Classifying ijma' by transmission type and evidentiary force

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify ijma' based on its mode of transmission (mutawatir vs ahad) and state the evidentiary value of each type.

Core Idea
Ijma' transmitted via mutawatir yields certainty; via ahad obligates action but not knowledge.

Having discussed the definition and conditions of ijma', the author now turns to a critical issue: how does the consensus of the Salaf reach us, and what is its epistemic weight? The passage distinguishes between two modes of transmission—tawatur and ahad—and draws a parallel to the transmission of hadith. This classification determines whether a reported ijma' is definitive (qat'i) or merely probable (zanni), and thus how it should be treated in legal inference. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying ijma' when encountering a claim of consensus.

Ijma' transmitted via ahad, when conflicting with qiyas, takes precedence over qiyas, just as khabar wahid does. Reason: both are certain in origin, while qiyas is purely zanni.
This is the rule of the majority of scholars (including Hanafis).

Distinguish the sub-types of ahad transmission
Within ahad, further subdivide: ijma' of the Successors on matters not disputed by the Companions is like khabar mashhur (tranquillity but not certainty); if disputed, it is like khabar wahid (action only).
This nuance is specific to the author's system.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on al-Muntakhab al-Husami discusses the transmission of ijma' and its classification based on how it reaches us. The author first presents a disagreement among scholars: some (including most Hanafis, Shafi'is, and the author himself) hold that ijma' does not require the agreement of the entire Ummah; rather, the consensus of the scholars of each era is sufficient. This view includes both cases—whether the ruling was disputed among the Companions or not. The text then explains that ijma' of the Salaf, when transmitted to us, can be divided into two main types based on transmission: (1) ijma' that comes through the continuous agreement of scholars of every era (similar to mutawatir hadith), which yields certainty and its denier becomes a disbeliever; and (2) ijma' that comes through individual chains (ahad), which is like khabar wahid—it obligates action but does not provide certainty, and its denier is not a disbeliever. An example of the first type is the consensus on the caliphate of Abu Bakr (ra). Examples of the second type include the consensus on four rak'as before Zuhr, praying Fajr at isfar, prohibition of marrying a sister during the iddah of her sister, and the confirmation of mahr by valid seclusion. The passage also clarifies that when ijma' transmitted via ahad conflicts with qiyas, it takes precedence because ijma' is certain in its origin (attributed to the infallible Ummah), whereas qiyas is based on opinion and is zanni. This mirrors the rule that khabar wahid takes precedence over qiyas.

Key Terms
اجماع السلف (ijmā‘ al-salaf): Consensus of the early generation (Companions and Successors) that is transmitted to later generations.
نقل متواتر (naql mutawātir): Transmission of ijma' through a mass of scholars in every era such that collusion on falsehood is impossible.
نقل آحاد (naql āḥād): Transmission of ijma' via individual or few chains, not reaching tawatur.
خبر مشہور (khabar mashhūr): A type of hadith that is initially ahad but later becomes widely known. The text equates some ijma' with this.

The Two Types of Intoxication and Their Effect on Legal Capacity
Defining sukr types and their impact on legal address

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between permissible and prohibited intoxication and apply the Hanafi rule that prohibited intoxication does not negate legal address, identifying the exceptions for apostasy and pure hudud confessions.

Core Idea
Prohibited intoxication does not void legal capacity; it is treated like full capacity except for apostasy and hudud by istihsan.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, intoxication (sukr) is a temporary defect of legal capacity. However, not all intoxication is treated equally. This passage from Al-Husami divides sukr into two types: permissible and prohibited, and explains how each affects the validity of legal dispositions and the binding of rulings. The key principle is that prohibited sukr does not negate the ability to be addressed by divine commands, as proven by the Qur'anic verse prohibiting approach to prayer while intoxicated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and its division
The passage divides sukr into two types based on the cause: permissible (from lawful means) and prohibited (from unlawful means). This is the starting point for legal analysis.
Permissible causes include medicine, duress, or dire necessity.
2
Locate the primary proof and operative phrase
The author cites Quran 4:43: 'O you who believe, do not approach prayer while you are intoxicated.' Identify the imperative verb and the condition (while intoxicated). This verse directly addresses the intoxicated person.
The verse proves that the intoxicated person is capable of receiving a legal address (khitab).
3
Derive the rule for prohibited sukr
From the proof, the Hanafi rule is that prohibited intoxication does not nullify any aspect of legal capacity (ahliyyah). All rulings of the law are binding, and all dispositions are valid.
This is the default rule (qiyas).
4
Identify the exceptions by istihsan
By istihsan (juristic preference), two exceptions are made: apostasy (riddah) by an intoxicated person is not considered valid, and confessions of pure divine penalties (hudud) are not accepted. The rationale is that the intoxicated person is unlikely to persist, so intoxication is treated as a retraction.
Istihsan overrides qiyas in these two cases.
5
Apply the rule to permissible sukr
Permissible intoxication is analogized to unconsciousness (ighma'); thus, all dispositions are invalid, and the person is not bound by rulings during that state.
This is because the cause is lawful and the person is excused.

Commentary
This passage discusses intoxication (sukr) as one of the defects of legal capacity (ahliyyah) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It begins by stating that sukr is of two types: (1) permissible sukr, which arises from lawful causes such as drinking medicine or being forced or compelled to drink; this type is treated like unconsciousness (ighmā'), and all legal dispositions such as divorce, manumission, and contracts do not take effect. (2) Prohibited sukr, which arises from unlawful consumption of intoxicants; this type does not negate the capacity to receive a legal address (khitāb). The author provides the Qur'anic proof: 'O you who have believed, do not approach prayer while you are intoxicated' (4:43). The verse itself addresses intoxicated persons, proving they are capable of being addressed. Therefore, for prohibited sukr, all rulings of the law apply, and all dispositions are valid and binding. Exceptions are made by istihsān for apostasy (riddah) and for confessions of pure divine penalties (hudūd). The reason is that an intoxicated person does not persist; thus sukr is treated as a retraction (rujū') and affects only what admits retraction. Apostasy requires true belief and intent, which the intoxicated lacks. Confession of hudūd (like drinking wine, theft, fornication) is not accepted because the intoxicated person's state suggests he would retract. In contrast, for penalties mixing rights of God and individual (e.g., qadhf, qiṣāṣ), the intoxication does not invalidate the confession because retraction is not honored in claims of individuals.

السكر (al-sukr): Intoxication, a temporary defect of legal capacity. Divided into permissible (like from medicine) and prohibited (like from wine).
الإغماء (al-ighma'): Unconsciousness; a state that nullifies legal capacity completely.
الردة (al-riddah): Apostasy; renunciation of Islam. In the intoxicated person, it is not considered valid by istihsān because it requires intent.

Hazl Invalidates Iqrar, Shuf'a Surrender, and Ibra': The Rule of Jest in Informative Acts
Explanation that hazl invalidates acknowledgments and similar acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the category of acts affected by hazl (informative statements) and explain the Hanafi reasoning (talil) for why hazl invalidates iqrar, shuf'a surrender, and ibra'.

Core Idea
Jest (hazl) negates existence, so it invalidates any statement that requires the existence of its subject.

In this lesson, we explore a key usul al-fiqh rule: the effect of jest (hazl) on certain legal acts. While we often think of hazl affecting contracts (insha), the Hanafi school extends its impact to informative statements (ikhbar) like confessions and releases. Understanding the reasoning behind this extension is crucial for grasping how intention and existence condition legal validity.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish insha' and ikhbar
Identify the two categories: insha' (contracts, dispositions) and ikhbar (statements that report a fact). The passage focuses on ikhbar.
Iqrar, shuf'a surrender, and ibra' are classified as ikhbar.
2
Identify the acts affected by hazl
List the acts: iqrar (confession), surrender of shuf'a after the first two demands, and ibra' (release of debtor). Note that these are informative acts that presuppose existence.
The surrender of shuf'a is considered an act of ikhbar because it communicates the relinquishment of a right.
3
Understand the talil (ratio legis)
The passage states that hazl indicates the non-existence of the thing informed about (mukhbar bihi). Since iqrar and similar acts are based on the existence of that thing, hazl negates the precondition and invalidates the act.
This is a rational inference (talil) not from a specific text but from the nature of hazl.
4
Apply the reasoning to shuf'a surrender and ibra'
Extend the same reasoning: hazl in a surrender of shuf'a or an ibra' shows that the right or debt does not exist, thus invalidating the act. The passage notes that the surrender is similar to what is invalidated by a condition of option.
The condition of option (khiyar al-shart) is a parallel because both involve negation by a condition.
5
Recognize the practical impact
This rule protects individuals from being bound by jest statements. It confirms that legal acts require serious intent (qasd) and existence of the subject matter.
The rule applies regardless of the subject's susceptibility to naskh (abrogation).
