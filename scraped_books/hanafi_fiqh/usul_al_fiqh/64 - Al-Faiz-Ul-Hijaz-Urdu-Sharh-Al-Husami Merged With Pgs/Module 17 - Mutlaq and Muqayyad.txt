Mutlaq and Muqayyad Across Incidents: The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Explaining the Hanafi rejection of carrying mutlaq to muqayyad across different incidents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that mutlaq is not carried to muqayyad across different incidents, and evaluate the Shafi'i counterargument based on qiyas.

Core Idea
Mutlaq is not automatically bound by muqayyad; each remains operative when possible.

You've learned that mutlaq and muqayyad are textual categories. Now we encounter a classic ikhtilaf: does a qualified ruling in one case (e.g., kaffarah for killing requiring a believing slave) extend to other cases of the same genus (e.g., zihar and oath kaffarah)? The Shafi'i school says yes, treating the descriptor as a condition. The Hanafi school says no, citing a concrete ruling that proves absolute and qualified can coexist without transfer. This lesson examines the arguments and the heavy proof.

Guided Path
1
State the Shafi'i position and its proof
Identify that the Shafi'i school carries mutlaq to muqayyad across different incidents of the same genus, treating the qualifying descriptor as a condition that negates the ruling when absent. Their proof is qiyas across all kaffarah.
The Shafi'i analogize from killing kaffarah (muqayyad) to zihar and oath kaffarah (mutlaq) because all are of one genus.
2
Identify the Hanafi position and its dalil
The Hanafi school holds that mutlaq is not carried to muqayyad even in the same incident as long as both rulings can be applied separately. Their dalil is the explicit ruling of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad on zihar kaffarah.
The ruling shows that the qualified fasting condition does not transfer to the absolute feeding.
3
Examine the Hanafi textual proof: the zihar ruling
Read the ruling: if a man has intercourse during the fast of zihar kaffarah, he must restart; if during the feeding, he does not restart. This distinguishes between the qualified (fast) and the absolute (feeding), proving that the condition of 'before physical contact' applies only where textually prescribed.
The condition is textually stated for freeing and fasting, not for feeding.
4
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
The ruling's distinction demonstrates that the absolute feeding remains unqualified even within the same incident (zihar). This refutes the Shafi'i claim that mutlaq must be carried to muqayyad within the same incident or genus.
If mutlaq were carried, feeding would also require being free from contact before completion.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Shafi'i requires a believing slave for all slave-freeing kaffarah; Hanafi requires a believing slave only for killing kaffarah, allowing any slave for zihar and oath kaffarah.
This affects the validity of the kaffarah in practice.

Commentary
This passage presents the Shafi'i argument that mutlaq (absolute) texts should be carried to muqayyad (qualified) texts even across different incidents, as exemplified by various kaffarah rulings. The Shafi'i reasoning treats the qualifying descriptor as equivalent to a condition, making the ruling dependent on its presence. The Hanafi response rejects this, asserting that mutlaq and muqayyad can each be applied separately as long as both are actionable. The Hanafi proof is a concrete ruling from Abu Hanifa and Muhammad regarding kaffarah for zihar: if a man has intercourse during the fast of expiation (which is qualified by 'before physical contact'), he must restart the fast, but if during the feeding (which is absolute without such a condition), he does not restart. This shows that the absolute feeding is not qualified by the condition of prior completion to physical contact, even though it is in the same incident (zihar). Thus, mutlaq is not automatically carried to muqayyad.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A textual expression indicating an essence without any qualifying descriptor, e.g., 'raqabah' (a slave).
مقید (muqayyad): A textual expression indicating an essence with a qualifying descriptor, e.g., 'raqabah mu'minah' (a believing slave).
رقبہ مومنہ (raqabah mu'minah): A believing slave; a qualified expression for the slave to be freed in kaffarah for killing.

The Rule of Mutlaq and Muqayyad: Separate Application When Possible
Expansion of the usul rule: mutlaq not carried to muqayyad when independent operation is possible.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that mutlaq and muqayyad texts remain independent when separate application is possible, and identify the condition for carrying the muqayyad to the mutlaq.

Core Idea
Mutlaq and muqayyad are applied separately unless contradiction arises—preserving the Lawgiver's wisdom.

This lesson examines a distinctive Hanafi principle in usul al-fiqh: when a text appears in both absolute (mutlaq) and restricted (muqayyad) forms, we do not automatically restrict the absolute with the restricted. Instead, we keep both operative if each can be applied independently. This preserves the specific legislative wisdom (hikmah) behind each formulation. Through two concrete examples—zihar expiation and sadaqat al-fitr—you will learn how to identify mutlaq and muqayyad rulings and apply the rule correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify mutlaq and muqayyad
Locate the two texts or rulings: one without qualification (mutlaq) and one with a specific condition or restriction (muqayyad). In the zihar example, freeing and fasting are muqayyad (by the condition 'before they touch'), while feeding is mutlaq.
The restriction may be in the text itself or implied by context.
2
Check if both can be applied independently
Determine whether each ruling can be acted upon in its own sphere without contradiction. If yes, keep them separate. If no (due to same subject and ruling), carry the muqayyad to the mutlaq.
3
Apply the rule to the zihar example
Since the feeding ruling has no condition, it remains absolute: intercourse during feeding does not invalidate it. The fasting ruling has the condition, so it must be restarted if intercourse occurs. Both are applied as they are.
4
Apply the rule to sadaqat al-fitr
Two hadiths: one absolute (pay for every slave) and one restricted (pay for Muslim slaves). Both causes are valid; we pay for non-Muslim slaves using the absolute hadith and for Muslim slaves using the restricted one.
5
State the Hanafi principle
Summarize: The absolute is not carried to the restricted if separate application is possible. This is because the Lawgiver's wisdom in making one ruling absolute (ease) and another restricted (difficulty) must be respected.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the usul rule regarding the interaction between mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (restricted) texts. The author, Al-Husami, presents a key Hanafi principle: when it is possible to act upon both the absolute and the restricted texts in their own spheres, they are not to be combined or one carried to the other. This preserves the distinct wisdom (hikmah) of the Lawgiver in making some rulings absolute and others restricted.

The passage provides two illustrative examples:

1. **Zihar (unlawful declaration) expiation:** The expiation for zihar has three sequential options: freeing a slave, fasting two months consecutively, or feeding sixty poor persons. The Qur'anic verse (58:3-4) includes the phrase 'min qabli an yatamāssā' (before they touch) for the first two options but not for the third. Thus, if a person chooses fasting and has intercourse during the fast, he must restart the fast because the condition of completing the fast before intercourse is violated. However, if he chooses feeding and has intercourse in the middle, he does not need to restart because no such condition was attached to feeding. Here, the rule of fasting is muqayyad (restricted by the condition), while the rule of feeding is mutlaq (absolute). Since both can be applied as they are, we do not carry the restriction of fasting over to feeding.

2. **Sadaqat al-fitr (charity of breaking the fast):** Two hadiths are cited. One (from Abu Sa'id al-Khudri) mentions paying for 'every slave' without qualification of religion. The other (from Ibn Umar) specifically says 'among the Muslims'. The first hadith is mutlaq regarding the cause (the 'head' of the slave), obligating sadaqah for both Muslim and non-Muslim slaves. The second is muqayyad, obligating only for Muslim slaves. Since there is no conflict in the causes—both hadiths create separate obligations for separate categories—we apply each as it is: absolute for absolute, restricted for restricted. Thus, a person pays sadaqat al-fitr for his non-Muslim slave based on the absolute hadith, and for his Muslim slave based on the restricted hadith. This demonstrates that when causes (asbāb) are distinct and non-competing, we do not harmonize by restricting the absolute.

The underlying rationale is that the Lawgiver's choice to make a ruling absolute or restricted carries intended legal effects (ta'līl). Restriction may impose difficulty, while absoluteness grants ease. If we automatically carry the restricted to the absolute, we lose the specific benefit of the absolute ruling. Therefore, the Hanafi method maintains both texts as operative in their own domains.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A text or ruling that is unrestricted; it applies to all individuals or situations without qualification.
مقيد (muqayyad): A text or ruling that is restricted by a condition or qualification, limiting its application.
حمل (haml): The act of carrying or interpreting one text in light of another; here, making the absolute conform to the restricted.
سبب (sabab): The cause or occasion that triggers a legal ruling; in sadaqat al-fitr, the 'head' (ra's) of the person supported.

Mutlaq and Muqayyad in the Cause: The Hanafi Rule
Explaining the Hanafi usul rule for mutlaq and muqayyad when the cause differs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that when mutlaq and muqayyad texts pertain to different causes (asbab), each text retains its independent ruling, and distinguish this from cases where the ruling is singular and conflation occurs.

Core Idea
Mutlaq and muqayyad in different causes: each binding; same cause: muqayyad qualifies mutlaq.

This lesson unpacks a key Hanafi distinction in Usul al-Fiqh: how to reconcile two texts—one absolute (mutlaq) and one restricted (muqayyad)—when they address the same act but different underlying causes. The example of sadaqat al-fitr from two hadiths reveals that the Hanafi school does not automatically subsume the mutlaq under the muqayyad. Rather, the determination hinges on whether the cause (sabab) is identical or different.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two texts and their causes
In the sadaqat al-fitr example, the first hadith says 'for every slave' (mutlaq cause: slave ownership/expenditure), the second says 'from the Muslims' (muqayyad cause: slave who is Muslim). The cause is the 'head' (ra's) of those under one's care.
The first hadith is narrated without the restriction 'Muslim'; the second includes it (Ibn Umar hadith).
2
Determine if the cause is the same or different
Hanafi analysis: the cause of obligation in the first hadith is any slave, in the second it is a Muslim slave. Since the cause differs (non-Muslim vs. Muslim slave), there is no conflict.
The cause here is the type of slave (kafir vs. Muslim), not the general obligation.
3
Apply the rule: each text operates independently
Mutlaq remains mutlaq, muqayyad remains muqayyad. Sadaqah is wajib for both non-Muslim and Muslim slaves under one's care. Conflation would only occur if the cause were identical.
This is the Hanafi position: no loading (haml) of mutlaq onto muqayyad when asbab differ.
4
Exception: when the ruling is singular (hukm wahid)
If there is only one ruling with the same cause, mutlaq is carried to muqayyad unanimously. Example: kaffarah yamin fasts – mutlaq 'three days' and muqayyad 'consecutive' are combined because the ruling (kaffarah) is the same.
This tanbih (warning) highlights that the rule is not absolute; it depends on the unity of hukm and sabab.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Usul al-Husami addresses a key Hanafi usul principle: when mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (restricted) occur in the cause (sabab) of a ruling, they are not conflated. Instead, each operates independently. The example given is sadaqat al-fitr: two hadiths exist—one says 'for every slave' (mutlaq), the other says 'from the Muslims' (muqayyad). The Hanafi view holds that both are valid causes: the first obligates sadaqah for all slaves (including non-Muslim), the second for Muslim slaves. They are not combined into one ruling. The author adds a tanbih: if the same single ruling is involved (e.g., the same expiation), then mutlaq is carried to muqayyad unanimously. The parallel is drawn with a conditional suspension: a ruling before its condition exists is both suspended (mu'allaq) and absolute (mursal) in a way, showing how two aspects can coexist without conflict.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A scriptural text that is absolute, without a qualifying restriction (qayd).
مقيد (muqayyad): A scriptural text that includes a qualifying restriction (qayd).
سبب (sabab): The cause or reason for which a ruling is prescribed; here, the 'ra's' (head) of those under one's care for sadaqat al-fitr.
محمول (mahmul): The process of carrying or subsuming a mutlaq text under a muqayyad text.

Probability of Conditional vs Immediate Rulings Before Existence
Expands on a usul rule using divorce example and qiyas to mutlaq/muqayyad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why, in Hanafi usul, a ruling before its existence can carry both probabilities of conditionality and immediacy, and analogize this to mutlaq and muqayyad.

Core Idea
Before a ruling exists, both conditional and immediate probabilities coexist without contradiction.

This lesson unpacks a foundational usul principle from the commentary on Al-Husami. Using the example of conditional versus immediate divorce, the author demonstrates that before a ruling actually occurs, its non-existence (al-‘adam al-aṣlī) allows both possibilities—ta‘līq and irsāl—to coexist. The same logic is then extended to mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (restricted) commands, which function as alternative causes for a single ruling.

Guided Path
1
Understand the divorce example
Recognize the two types of divorce: ta'liq (conditional) and tanjiz/irsal (immediate). The author uses this familiar fiqh scenario to illustrate a broader usul point.
2
Grasp the concept of 'adam asli
Before any pronouncement, the ruling is non-existent. This original non-existence (al-‘adam al-aṣlī) is a blank slate that carries no inherent determination.
Key: the ruling's non-existence is not due to the condition's non-fulfillment but due to the absence of any cause.
3
See how both probabilities coexist
After a conditional statement, the ruling remains non-existent until the condition occurs. In this state, the ruling can still be established by an immediate pronouncement (irsal) or by the condition (ta'liq). These are not contradictory because they are only potential at this stage.
4
Apply the same reasoning to mutlaq and muqayyad
Just as ta'liq and irsal are alternative causes for divorce, mutlaq and muqayyad are alternative causes for a ruling. Before the ruling is fixed, both textual forms are possible bases.
5
Recognize the qiyas (analogy)
The author draws an explicit qiyas: asl = conditional divorce, far' = mutlaq/muqayyad, illa = both are causes that can establish the ruling on an alternative basis (ala sabīl al-badalīyah).

Talaq, this is called conditional divorce, and there is also immediate divorce, which is when the husband gives divorce without condition, such as saying 'You are divorced', this is called immediate divorce. Both of these are causes of divorce. Now understand the analogy: The author says that in conditional divorce, the non-existence of the ruling is not due to the non-existence of the condition, but due to the non-existence of the cause, which you have read in detail. So in the case of conditional divorce, the ruling is non-existent. Then that ruling, before its existence and after the condition, carries both probabilities: the probability of being conditional and the probability of being immediate (i.e., unrestricted by condition). Therefore, if someone says to his wife, 'If you enter the house, you are divorced', then in this there are both probabilities of conditionality and immediacy. That is, there is a probability that divorce will occur at the time of entering the house and not before, and there is also a probability of immediacy, i.e., that before entering the house, the husband may give an immediate divorce. So in one ruling, there is probability of both immediacy and conditionality, because before the existence of the ruling, there is no contradiction between the probabilities of conditionality and immediacy. However, at the time of the existence of the ruling, there is necessarily a contradiction between conditionality and immediacy, i.e., it is not possible that one ruling is established both by immediacy and by conditionality; rather, the ruling (i.e., the occurrence of divorce) will be established by only one of them, either by conditionality or by immediacy. But before existence of the ruling, both probabilities can exist, because before existence, the ruling is suspended on a condition and related to a condition, so without the condition, the ruling is non-existent, like original non-existence. That is, the ruling is non-existent, in which there is probability that it is suspended on a condition, and also probability that it is immediate, in the sense that immediate divorce may occur. And the reason for this is that before the condition, the ruling's original non-existence had both probabilities: conditionality and immediacy. For example, initially the husband has both options: to give conditional divorce or to give immediate divorce. And it is agreed that after the condition, the original non-existence of the ruling does not change; rather, the ruling remains non-existent as it was before the condition. So when after the condition the original non-existence does not change, then just as before the condition (i.e., in the state of original non-existence) there were both probabilities of the ruling's occurrence (conditionality and immediacy), similarly after the condition, both probabilities will remain for the ruling's occurrence. So just as a single ruling before its existence and after the condition has the probability of being established both by immediacy and by conditionality, and there is no contradiction in this; rather, the ruling will be established by one of them on an alternative basis (ala sabīl al-badalīyah). Similarly, there is no contradiction between absolute (mutlaq) and restricted (muqayyad), because both are causes, so the ruling will be established on an alternative basis from both. This is the analogy.

Commentary
This passage is from a sharh on Al-Husami, explaining a key usul principle about the state of a ruling before its actual occurrence. The author uses the example of conditional divorce (ta'liq) versus immediate divorce (tanjiz). Before the condition occurs, the divorce ruling is non-existent (ma'dum), and this non-existence is similar to the original non-existence before any statement. In this state, both possibilities coexist: the divorce could be realized by the condition (ta'liq) or by an immediate pronouncement (irsal). This is not contradictory because the ruling only becomes determined at the moment of occurrence. The author draws an analogy with mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (restricted) commands: they are alternative causes for the same ruling, and before the ruling is established, both are possible. This is a fundamental concept in Hanafi usul, emphasizing that potentiality allows for multiple probabilities without contradiction.

Commentary
This passage defines the first of three types of time-bound commands (al-muqayyad bi al-waqt). Here, time has three roles regarding the obligated act (mamur bih): 1) Zarf (container): time is broader than the act; the act does not fill the entire time slot. For example, prayer time (e.g., Zuhr) lasts several hours, but the prayer takes only a few minutes; so time is a container. 2) Shart (condition): performance is impossible before the time begins and becomes invalid if the time ends before completion. For prayer, if one prays before the time, it is invalid; if the time expires (e.g., sunset for Asr) before completing, the prayer is missed. 3) Sabab (cause): the obligation is linked to time; the quality of time affects the obligation. For example, the last part of Asr time is considered 'deficient' (naqis) so the obligation is also considered less complete; if one prays at the end and the sun sets during prayer, the prayer becomes invalid because the obligation was meant for a complete time. Similarly, the author argues: rushing the act before time invalidates it because time is the cause. The example is the five daily prayers. The author also distinguishes this from a 'measure' (mi'yar) where the act fills the time (like fasting fills the whole day). Commentators add that this first type applies to prayers, not to fasting (which is the second type) or Hajj (which is the third, ambiguous type).

ظرف (zarf): Container: time extends beyond the act of worship; the act does not occupy the entire time.
شرط (shart): Condition: the act is invalid if performed before or after the specified time.
سبب (sabab): Cause: the time is the apparent cause for the obligation, influencing its perfection or deficiency.

The Particle 'Ila' and Inclusion of the Endpoint in Hanafi Usul
Explaining the meaning of 'ila' and the four views on endpoint inclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul criteria for determining whether the endpoint (ghayah) after 'ila' is included in the ruling, using examples from the Quran and hadith.

Core Idea
Whether the endpoint after 'ila' is included depends on its self-subsistence and whether the initial term encompasses it.

The particle 'ila' is fundamental in legal texts, indicating a limit. Hanafi usul distinguishes four general views on the inclusion of the endpoint, then refines them based on the nature of the limit. This lesson unpacks that classification, enabling you to interpret texts like the wudu verse and the fasting verse correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify Key Terms
Define 'ghayah' (the endpoint after 'ila') and 'moghayya' (the limited thing before 'ila'). Understand that 'ila' is placed for the end of a limit.
2
Summarize the Four Views
List the four positions: (1) absolute inclusion, (2) absolute exclusion, (3) inclusion if the endpoint is of the same genus, (4) exclusion if the endpoint is self-subsistent.
The fourth view is the one elaborated by the Hanafis.
3
Understand the Refined Classification
If the endpoint is self-subsistent (e.g., a wall), it is not included. If not self-subsistent, check whether the initial term encompasses it. If yes, it is included; if no, it is excluded.
Example: 'hands' naturally includes 'elbows'; 'fasting' does not include 'night'.
4
Apply to Quranic Examples
Analyze the wudu verse (Al-Ma'idah 5:6): 'your hands to the elbows' – the elbows are included because they are not self-subsistent and 'hands' encompasses them. Analyze the fasting verse (Al-Baqarah 2:187): 'complete the fast to the night' – the night is excluded because 'fast' does not encompass it.
5
