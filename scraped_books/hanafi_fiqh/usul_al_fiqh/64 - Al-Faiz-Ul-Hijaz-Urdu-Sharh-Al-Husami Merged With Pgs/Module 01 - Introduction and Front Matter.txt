Introducing Al-Fayḍ al-Ḥijāzī: A Hanafi Usul Commentary
Title page and author details

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the original text, author, and commentator, and explain the significance of this commentary within the Hanafi usul tradition.

Core Idea
An Urdu commentary on a Hanafi usul classic bridges centuries of scholarship.

This lesson introduces the book you are about to study: Al-Fayḍ al-Ḥijāzī, an Urdu commentary on Al-Muntakhab al-Ḥusāmī. Understanding its provenance—who wrote the original and who commented—grounds your study in the Hanafi scholarly chain. The original author, Ḥusām al-Dīn al-Akhsīkatī, was a Hanafi jurist of the 7th century AH, and the commentator, Mawlānā Ḥafiz al-Rahmān Pālanpūrī, taught Hadith and Tafsir in 20th-century India.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original text
Locate the named base text: Al-Muntakhab al-Ḥusāmī. Recognize it as a classical Hanafi usul work.
The title 'al-Muntakhab' means 'the selected', indicating it is an abridgment or selection.
2
Note the original author
Identify Ḥusām al-Dīn al-Akhsīkatī (d. 642 AH/1244 CE) as the author. He was a Hanafi scholar from Akhsikat (in modern Uzbekistan).
Death years may vary in manuscripts (642 vs 644); the correct date is 642.
3
Recognize the commentator
Mawlānā Ḥafiz al-Rahmān Pālanpūrī (d. 2000 CE) wrote the Urdu commentary. He was a teacher of Hadith and Tafsir at Idara Diniyat, Bombay.
This commentary makes usul accessible to Urdu-speaking students.
4
Understand the chain of transmission
The commentary is a sharh (explanation) that connects the classical matn to later students. It is part of the Hanafi tradition of teaching usul through commentaries.
Learning the chain helps establish the reliability of the work.

Introduction to Al-Fayd al-Hijazi and Its Scholarly Context
Title page and dedication of a Hanafi usul commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author, commentator, and institutional lineage of Al-Fayd al-Hijazi and explain how front matter secures the chain of knowledge transmission in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A title page is not just bibliographic data—it is the first link in the chain of knowledge transmission.

Every classical Islamic text begins with a title page that identifies the work and its author, but for the Hanafi tradition, this front matter carries deeper significance: it situates the text within a living chain of transmission. This lesson examines the title page and dedication of Al-Fayd al-Hijazi, an Urdu commentary on the foundational usul text Al-Muntakhab al-Husami. By understanding this bibliographic frame, you take the first step toward tracing how Hanafi usul has been taught and preserved from the classical period to the modern era.

Guided Path
1
Identify the work and its original author
Read the title page to find the book's name (Al-Fayd al-Hijazi) and the original author of the base text (Al-Muntakhab al-Husami by Husam al-Din al-Sikisi). Note that the commentary is in Urdu, reflecting the spread of Hanafi learning to South Asia.
The original author's full name includes the honorific 'Husam al-Din' (Sword of the Religion), common in medieval Islamic scholarship.
2
Recognize the commentator and the chain of transmission
The commentator, Hafiz al-Rahman Palanpuri, attributes his work to his teachers and institutions—Darul Uloom Kakosi and Darul Uloom Deoband. This shows how Hanafi usul has been transmitted through a network of scholars and seminaries.
Darul Uloom Deoband is a major center of Hanafi learning; its mention here anchors the commentary in a specific pedagogical tradition.
3
Analyze the dedication to understand the values of the tradition
The commentator dedicates the work to his parents and teachers, acknowledging their role in his intellectual formation. This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on gratitude to one's teachers (adab al-muta'allim) and the collective nature of knowledge.
Such dedications are not mere formality; they publicly affirm the chain (sanad) through which the author received and now transmits knowledge.

Navigating the Architecture of Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh
Table of contents of a Hanafi usul sharh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major topics covered in a classical Hanafi usul al-fiqh text and explain how the book's structure reflects the logical progression of the discipline.

Core Idea
A table of contents reveals the architecture of Hanafi usul al-fiqh: definitions, divisions, commands, prohibitions, and hadith sciences.

This lesson presents the table of contents (fihrist) from Al-Faiz-ul-Hijaz, an Urdu commentary on the Hanafi usul text Al-Muntakhab al-Husami. Rather than diving into a specific ruling or proof, this passage offers a bird's-eye view of the entire usul curriculum. By studying the table of contents, you will gain a mental map of how the topics are sequenced and connected, which will help you navigate the detailed lessons ahead.

Guided Path
1
Scan the major sections
Look at the headings: introduction, definitions of khass and amm, divisions of nass (text), command and prohibition, hadith classifications, conflict of evidences, and concluding topics. This reveals the classical progression from simple linguistic tools to complex evidentiary interactions.
The table of contents mirrors the typical order of a Hanafi usul textbook.
2
Identify the four main pillars
Notice the first heading: 'The sources of Sharia are four.' This is the foundational principle. Then the TOC moves to linguistic categories (khass, amm, haqiqah, majaz), then to commands and prohibitions, then to hadith science, and finally to conflict resolution (mu'aradah).
The four sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) are the bedrock.
3
Trace the flow from simple to complex
The TOC starts with basic definitions (specific, general, reality, metaphor), then moves to divisions of textual indication ('ibarah, isharah, dalalah, iqtida'), then to commands and prohibitions, then to sources (hadith types), then to conflict between proofs. This is a deliberate pedagogical sequence.
Each topic builds on the previous one.
4
Note the page numbers as signposts
Page numbers are provided for each heading. Use them to quickly locate discussions. For example, 'Discussion of command (amr)' begins around page 140. If you need to review the obligation of command, you can jump directly there.
The page numbers are approximate but useful for navigation.
5
Recognize the book's purpose
This is a commentary (sharh) on Al-Muntakhab al-Husami. The TOC shows that the commentary covers the entire matn, explaining each term and rule in Urdu. Understanding the TOC helps you see the full scope of the work.
The commentary is designed for intermediate students of Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A Hanafi scholar’s prayer and endorsement anchor the text in scholarly tradition.

This lesson introduces you to the opening pages of Al-Faiz-ul-Hijaz, an Urdu commentary on the classical Hanafi usul text Al-Husami. It is necessary before starting any discipline to know seven things so that the beginning of that discipline may be with insight: (1) Definition of the discipline (2) Subject matter of the discipline (3) Purpose and goal of the discipline (4) Rank of the discipline (5) Ruling of the discipline (6) Compilation of the discipline (7) Biography of the author. (1) Definition of Usul al-Fiqh: Usul al-Fiqh is a genitive compound composed of two parts: 'Usul' (the construct) and 'Fiqh' (the genitive). So in Usul al-Fiqh, it is necessary to keep in view the definition of both parts. Defining the two parts separately is called 'al-hadd al-idafi', and defining them together is called 'al-hadd al-haqiqi'. The analytical definition of Usul al-Fiqh: 'Usul' is the plural of 'asl', which lexically means 'that on which something else is built'. This science is called Usul al-Fiqh because upon it the foundation of Fiqh is established. The second part is 'Fiqh'. Lexically, Fiqh means to open a thing, to clarify. A Faqih is a scholar who clarifies the Sharia rulings. The terminological definition of Fiqh: 'It is knowledge of the subsidiary Sharia rulings from their detailed evidences' – knowing subsidiary Sharia rulings from detailed evidences. Rulings related to actions are called subsidiary rulings, and rulings related to belief are called primary rulings. The real definition of Usul al-Fiqh: Allama Kamal al-Din Ibn al-Humam defined it in al-Tahrir as: 'It is the comprehension of the rules by which one arrives at the extraction of Fiqh' – that Usul al-Fiqh is the name for knowing the rules through which Fiqh can be reached. (2) Subject matter of Usul al-Fiqh: The four proofs (Book, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) and the totality of rulings are the subject matter of Usul al-Fiqh. Some people say only the proofs are the subject matter, and some say only the rulings are the subject matter, but the preferred view is the first. And the subject matter of the science of Fiqh is the rulings along with their detailed proofs. So both the science of Fiqh and the science of Usul al-Fiqh pertain to the proofs, but the mode of relation is different: the relation of Fiqh to the proofs is in terms of extracting particular rulings from them, and the relation of Usul al-Fiqh to the proofs is in terms of the method of deduction and inference and the levels of authority. (3) Purpose and goal of Usul al-Fiqh: To know the Sharia rulings with their detailed evidences, and to know the rules of deriving issues. (4) Rank and status of Usul al-Fiqh: The rank of a science depends on the rank of its subject matter. Since the subject matter of Usul al-Fiqh is the Book of Allah, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas, and rulings – all of which are of high rank from a religious perspective – so the rank of this science is also high.

The Definition of Usul al-Fiqh: Partial and Complete
Defining Usul al-Fiqh through its components

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state both the partial (al-hadd al-idafi) and complete (al-hadd al-haqiqi) definitions of Usul al-Fiqh and explain why the discipline is named as such.

Core Idea
Usul al-Fiqh is the science of principles that enable a faqih to derive rulings from their detailed evidences.

Before delving into the principles of Usul al-Fiqh, we must understand what the discipline itself means. This lesson unpacks the compound term 'Usul al-Fiqh' by examining its two components separately and then together. You will learn why it is called the foundation of fiqh and how it differs from fiqh itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two components of the compound term
Recognize that 'Usul al-Fiqh' is an idafa construct: 'usul' (principles) is the first part, and 'fiqh' (jurisprudence) is the second. Each part must be defined separately for the partial definition.
The word 'usul' is plural of 'asl', meaning foundation.
2
Define 'usul' lexically and terminologically
Lexically, 'asl' means 'that on which something else is built'. In usul al-fiqh, it refers to the principles that serve as the foundation for deriving fiqh rulings.
This shows why the discipline is called 'usul al-fiqh' — it is the basis of fiqh.
3
Define 'fiqh' lexically and terminologically
Lexically, 'fiqh' means to open or clarify. Terminologically, it is 'knowledge of subsidiary legal rulings from their detailed evidences'. Note that it covers practical rulings (furu') not beliefs.
This distinguishes fiqh from kalam and other Islamic sciences.
4
Combine the two definitions into the complete definition
The complete definition (al-hadd al-haqiqi) as given by Ibn al-Humam: 'It is the comprehension of the principles by which one can reach the derivation of fiqh.' This captures the essence of the discipline.
This definition shows that Usul al-Fiqh is a tool for derivation, not the rulings themselves.

Introduction
Urdu commentary on al-Muntakhab al-Husami
Before starting any discipline, it is necessary to know seven things so that the study of that discipline can begin with insight: (1) Definition of the discipline, (2) Subject of the discipline, (3) Purpose and goal of the discipline, (4) Rank of the discipline, (5) Ruling of the discipline, (6) Compilation of the discipline, (7) Biography of the author. (1) Definition of Usul al-Fiqh: Usul al-Fiqh is a construct phrase composed of two parts: 'usul' (added) and 'fiqh' (added to). So in Usul al-Fiqh, it is necessary to keep in view the definition of both parts. The separate definition of each part is called al-hadd al-idafi, and defining both together is called al-hadd al-haqiqi. Al-hadd al-idafi of Usul al-Fiqh: 'usul' is the plural of 'asl', which lexically means 'that on which something else is built' [66], something on which another thing is based. The discipline of Usul al-Fiqh is called so because upon it the foundation of Fiqh is established. The second part is 'fiqh'. Lexically, fiqh means to open something, to clarify. A faqih is called that scholar who clarifies the legal rulings. The terminological definition of fiqh: 'It is knowledge of the subsidiary legal rulings derived from their detailed evidences.' The rulings that pertain to actions are called subsidiary rulings, and those that pertain to beliefs are called primary rulings. Al-hadd al-haqiqi of Usul al-Fiqh: Allamah Kamal al-Din Ibn al-Humam defined it in al-Tahrir as: 'It is the comprehension of the principles by which one can reach the derivation of fiqh.' That is, Usul al-Fiqh is the name for knowing the principles through which fiqh can be attained. (2) Subject of Usul al-Fiqh: The four sources (Book, Sunnah, Consensus, and Analogy) and the collection of rulings constitute the subject of Usul al-Fiqh. Some say only the sources are the subject, and some say only the rulings are the subject, but the preferred view is the first. And the subject of the discipline of Fiqh is the rulings along with their detailed evidences. So both Fiqh and Usul al-Fiqh relate to the sources, but the mode of relation is different: Fiqh's relation to the sources is such that particular rulings are extracted from them, while Usul al-Fiqh's relation to the sources is in terms of the method of derivation and deduction and ranks of authority. (3) Purpose and goal of Usul al-Fiqh: To know the legal rulings along with their detailed evidences, and to know the principles of deriving issues. (4) Rank and status of Usul al-Fiqh: Whatever the rank of its subject, that will be the rank of the discipline. Thus, the subject of Usul al-Fiqh is the Book of Allah, Sunnah, Consensus, Analogy, and rulings; all these things are of high rank religiously, so the rank of this discipline is also high. (5) Ruling of Usul al-Fiqh: Acquiring Usul al-Fiqh is a communal obligation (fard al-kifayah). (6) Compilation of Usul al-Fiqh: In the era of the Companions, just as other disciplines were not compiled, Usul al-Fiqh was also not compiled. After the era of the Companions, its compilation took place. Imam Abu Hanifah is the first compiler of fiqh, so certainly he laid the foundation of Usul al-Fiqh at the time of compiling fiqh, but from the Imam himself no writing in this discipline is available. However, his students Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad wrote books on Usul al-Fiqh, but they are rare; now it is difficult to find them. The first composition on Usul al-Fiqh is the Risalah of Imam al-Shafi'i, in which he wrote about commands and prohibitions, elucidation, and reports and abrogation etc. This treatise is essentially the introduction to his book al-Umm, which is considered the foundation of fiqh. At the end of the second century and the beginning of the third century, regular composition and compilation had begun, and in that period two excellent books appeared: Kitab al-Jadal and Makhadh al-Shar' by Shaykh Abu Mansur Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Maturidi (d. 23? H). Then in the fourth century, Kitab al-Usul by Shaykh Imam Abu Bakr al-Jassas (d. ???) and Kitab al-Dhakhirah by Shaykh Ahmad ibn Husayn known as Ibn Barran al-Farisi (d. 350 H) were authored. Then in the fifth century, ten books came into existence. In the sixth and seventh centuries, this series progressed further, and the book under consideration, al-Muntakhab al-Husami, is from this seventh century. (7) Biography of the author (may Allah bless him and grant him peace): The author's name is Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Umar, his title is Husam al-Din, and his kunyah is Abu Abdullah. He was attributed to Asyakit and thus called Asyakit. Asyakit is a famous and beautiful city of Ferghana, about which the author of al-Ansab wrote: 'It was one of its most pleasant and beautiful cities.' He was a perfect shaykh, a virtuous imam, a scholar of furu' and usul, and an expert in disputation and disagreement. Muhammad ibn Umar al-Hawabazi, Muhammad ibn Bukhari, and Fakhr al-Din Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Maymarfi among the distinguished scholars acquired fiqh from him. Works: His book al-Muntakhab al-Husami is the best, reliable, accepted, and widely used book of Usul al-Fiqh. Besides that, in refutation of Hujjat al-Islam Imam al-Ghazali's 'al-Mankhul', which is a criticism of the Great Imam, he wrote a fine treatise consisting of six chapters, in which he refuted every single statement of Imam al-Ghazali and also mentioned the noble virtues of the Imam. Death: He died on Saturday, 22/23 Dhu al-Qa'dah 143 H and was buried near Qadikhan in the cemetery of the judges. The lesson is: this world is not your abode; you came for another purpose.

Commentary
This passage is an introduction to the study of Usul al-Fiqh. The author begins by stating the seven prerequisites for any discipline: definition, subject, purpose, rank, ruling, compilation, and author biography. He then applies these to Usul al-Fiqh.

First, the definition of Usul al-Fiqh is given in two ways: the partial definition (al-hadd al-idafi) which defines its two components separately, and the complete definition (al-hadd al-haqiqi) which defines the compound. 'Usul' is the plural of 'asl', meaning that on which something else is built, so Usul al-Fiqh is the foundation for Fiqh. 'Fiqh' is defined terminologically as knowledge of subsidiary legal rulings from their detailed evidences. The complete definition, from Ibn al-Humam, is that Usul al-Fiqh is the comprehension of the principles by which one can derive fiqh.

Second, the subject (mawdu') of Usul al-Fiqh is the four sources (Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas) and also the rulings themselves. There is a difference of opinion: some say only the sources, some only the rulings, but the preferred view is the first (only sources). However, the text earlier included both. This reflects an internal discussion in the Hanafi tradition.

Third, the purpose is to know the legal rulings with their evidences and to learn the principles of derivation.

Fourth, the rank of Usul al-Fiqh is high because its subject matter is of high religious importance.

Fifth, the ruling is that acquiring it is a communal obligation (fard al-kifayah).

Sixth, the historical compilation is traced: after the Companions, Imam Abu Hanifah laid the foundation, his students wrote books that are now lost, and the first systematic work is Imam al-Shafi'i's Risalah. The text then lists key works by al-Maturidi, al-Jassas, Ibn Barran, and finally the book being commented on, al-Muntakhab al-Husami from the seventh century.

Seventh, the author's biography: Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Umar, known as Husam al-Din al-Asyakit, a scholar from Ferghana. He studied under notable teachers and taught many. His main work is al-Muntakhab al-Husami, and he also wrote a refutation of Ghazali's al-Mankhul. He died in 143 H.

All these points equip the student with the necessary orientation before delving into the detailed principles of Usul al-Fiqh.

Subject, Purpose, Rank, and Ruling of Usul al-Fiqh
Explaining the scope and importance of Usul al-Fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the subject (mawdu'), purpose, rank, and legal ruling of studying Usul al-Fiqh, and explain the preferred view regarding its subject.

Core Idea
The subject of Usul al-Fiqh is the four sources and the rulings; its acquisition is a communal obligation.

After defining the discipline, the passage turns to four essential aspects: what it studies (subject), why it is studied (purpose), its status among Islamic sciences (rank), and the obligation to learn it (ruling). These aspects contextualize Usul al-Fiqh within the broader framework of Islamic knowledge and motivate the student to engage with it seriously.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of Usul al-Fiqh
The passage presents two views: some say the subject is only the four sources (Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas), others say it includes rulings. The preferred view is the first: only the sources. However, note that the passage initially includes both sources and rulings, then gives preference.
This debate reflects different emphases: is usul about the sources themselves or about the process of deriving rulings from them?
2
Distinguish the subject of Usul al-Fiqh from that of Fiqh
Both disciplines relate to the sources, but differently: Fiqh extracts particular rulings from them, while Usul al-Fiqh studies the methods of derivation and the hierarchy of authority.
This distinction prevents confusion between the tool and the result.
3
State the purpose and goal
The purpose is twofold: (1) to know the legal rulings with their detailed evidences, and (2) to learn the principles of derivation. This underscores that usul is both theoretical and practical.
The purpose directly links to the definition: it is about both knowledge and method.
4
Determine the rank of Usul al-Fiqh
Since the subject (Book, Sunnah, etc.) is of high religious rank, the science itself is of high rank. This establishes its importance and encourages respect for the discipline.
Rank is derived from the honor of the subject matter.
5
Identify the legal ruling (hukm)
Acquiring Usul al-Fiqh is fard al-kifayah (communal obligation). This means that if some Muslims master it, the rest are absolved; but if no one does, the entire community is sinful.
This ruling motivates the study: it is not optional but a collective duty.

Introduction
Urdu commentary on al-Muntakhab al-Husami
Before starting any discipline, it is necessary to know seven things so that the study of that discipline can begin with insight: (1) Definition of the discipline, (2) Subject of the discipline, (3) Purpose and goal of the discipline, (4) Rank of the discipline, (5) Ruling of the discipline, (6) Compilation of the discipline, (7) Biography of the author. (1) Definition of Usul al-Fiqh: Usul al-Fiqh is a construct phrase composed of two parts: 'usul' (added) and 'fiqh' (added to). So in Usul al-Fiqh, it is necessary to keep in view the definition of both parts. The separate definition of each part is called al-hadd al-idafi, and defining both together is called al-hadd al-haqiqi. Al-hadd al-idafi of Usul al-Fiqh: 'usul' is the plural of 'asl', which lexically means 'that on which something else is built' [66], something on which another thing is based. The discipline of Usul al-Fiqh is called so because upon it the foundation of Fiqh is established. The second part is 'fiqh'. Lexically, fiqh means to open something, to clarify. A faqih is called that scholar who clarifies the legal rulings. The terminological definition of fiqh: 'It is knowledge of the subsidiary legal rulings derived from their detailed evidences.' The rulings that pertain to actions are called subsidiary rulings, and those that pertain to beliefs are called primary rulings. Al-hadd al-haqiqi of Usul al-Fiqh: Allamah Kamal al-Din Ibn al-Humam defined it in al-Tahrir as: 'It is the comprehension of the principles by which one can reach the derivation of fiqh.' That is, Usul al-Fiqh is the name for knowing the principles through which fiqh can be attained. (2) Subject of Usul al-Fiqh: The four sources (Book, Sunnah, Consensus, and Analogy) and the collection of rulings constitute the subject of Usul al-Fiqh. Some say only the sources are the subject, and some say only the rulings are the subject, but the preferred view is the first. And the subject of the discipline of Fiqh is the rulings along with their detailed evidences. So both Fiqh and Usul al-Fiqh relate to the sources, but the mode of relation is different: Fiqh's relation to the sources is such that particular rulings are extracted from them, while Usul al-Fiqh's relation to the sources is in terms of the method of derivation and deduction and ranks of authority. (3) Purpose and goal of Usul al-Fiqh: To know the legal rulings along with their detailed evidences, and to know the principles of deriving issues. (4) Rank and status of Usul al-Fiqh: Whatever the rank of its subject, that will be the rank of the discipline. Thus, the subject of Usul al-Fiqh is the Book of Allah, Sunnah, Consensus, Analogy, and rulings; all these things are of high rank religiously, so the rank of this discipline is also high. (5) Ruling of Usul al-Fiqh: Acquiring Usul al-Fiqh is a communal obligation (fard al-kifayah). (6) Compilation of Usul al-Fiqh: In the era of the Companions, just as other disciplines were not compiled, Usul al-Fiqh was also not compiled. After the era of the Companions, its compilation took place. Imam Abu Hanifah is the first compiler of fiqh, so certainly he laid the foundation of Usul al-Fiqh at the time of compiling fiqh, but from the Imam himself no writing in this discipline is available. However, his students Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad wrote books on Usul al-Fiqh, but they are rare; now it is difficult to find them. The first composition on Usul al-Fiqh is the Risalah of Imam al-Shafi'i, in which he wrote about commands and prohibitions, elucidation, and reports and abrogation etc. This treatise is essentially the introduction to his book al-Umm, which is considered the foundation of fiqh. At the end of the second century and the beginning of the third century, regular composition and compilation had begun, and in that period two excellent books appeared: Kitab al-Jadal and Makhadh al-Shar' by Shaykh Abu Mansur Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Maturidi (d. 23? H). Then in the fourth century, Kitab al-Usul by Shaykh Imam Abu Bakr al-Jassas (d. ???) and Kitab al-Dhakhirah by Shaykh Ahmad ibn Husayn known as Ibn Barran al-Farisi (d. 350 H) were authored. Then in the fifth century, ten books came into existence. In the sixth and seventh centuries, this series progressed further, and the book under consideration, al-Muntakhab al-Husami, is from this seventh century. (7) Biography of the author (may Allah bless him and grant him peace): The author's name is Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Umar, his title is Husam al-Din, and his kunyah is Abu Abdullah. He was attributed to Asyakit and thus called Asyakit. Asyakit is a famous and beautiful city of Ferghana, about which the author of al-Ansab wrote: 'It was one of its most pleasant and beautiful cities.' He was a perfect shaykh, a virtuous imam, a scholar of furu' and usul, and an expert in disputation and disagreement. Muhammad ibn Umar al-Hawabazi, Muhammad ibn Bukhari, and Fakhr al-Din Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Maymarfi among the distinguished scholars acquired fiqh from him. Works: His book al-Muntakhab al-Husami is the best, reliable, accepted, and widely used book of Usul al-Fiqh. Besides that, in refutation of Hujjat al-Islam Imam al-Ghazali's 'al-Mankhul', which is a criticism of the Great Imam, he wrote a fine treatise consisting of six chapters, in which he refuted every single statement of Imam al-Ghazali and also mentioned the noble virtues of the Imam. Death: He died on Saturday, 22/23 Dhu al-Qa'dah 143 H and was buried near Qadikhan in the cemetery of the judges. The lesson is: this world is not your abode; you came for another purpose.

Commentary
This passage is an introduction to the study of Usul al-Fiqh. The author begins by stating the seven prerequisites for any discipline: definition, subject, purpose, rank, ruling, compilation, and author biography. He then applies these to Usul al-Fiqh.

First, the definition of Usul al-Fiqh is given in two ways: the partial definition (al-hadd al-idafi) which defines its two components separately, and the complete definition (al-hadd al-haqiqi) which defines the compound. 'Usul' is the plural of 'asl', meaning that on which something else is built, so Usul al-Fiqh is the foundation for Fiqh. 'Fiqh' is defined terminologically as knowledge of subsidiary legal rulings from their detailed evidences. The complete definition, from Ibn al-Humam, is that Usul al-Fiqh is the comprehension of the principles by which one can derive fiqh.

Second, the subject (mawdu') of Usul al-Fiqh is the four sources (Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas) and also the rulings themselves. There is a difference of opinion: some say only the sources, some only the rulings, but the preferred view is the first (only sources). However, the text earlier included both. This reflects an internal discussion in the Hanafi tradition.

Third, the purpose is to know the legal rulings with their evidences and to learn the principles of derivation.

Fourth, the rank of Usul al-Fiqh is high because its subject matter is of high religious importance.

Fifth, the ruling is that acquiring it is a communal obligation (fard al-kifayah).

Sixth, the historical compilation is traced: after the Companions, Imam Abu Hanifah laid the foundation, his students wrote books that are now lost, and the first systematic work is Imam al-Shafi'i's Risalah. The text then lists key works by al-Maturidi, al-Jassas, Ibn Barran, and finally the book being commented on, al-Muntakhab al-Husami from the seventh century.

Seventh, the author's biography: Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Umar, known as Husam al-Din al-Asyakit, a scholar from Ferghana. He studied under notable teachers and taught many. His main work is al-Muntakhab al-Husami, and he also wrote a refutation of Ghazali's al-Mankhul. He died in 143 H.

All these points equip the student with the necessary orientation before delving into the detailed principles of Usul al-Fiqh.

Key Terms
ادلہ اربعہ (al-adillah al-arba'ah): The four sources: Book (Qur'an), Sunnah, Ijma' (consensus), and Qiyas (analogy).
فرض علی الکفایہ (fard al-kifayah): A communal obligation: if some Muslims perform it, the rest are absolved.

Compilation History and Biography of the Author
Historical development of Usul al-Fiqh and author biography

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to trace the major phases of the compilation of Usul al-Fiqh and identify the author of al-Muntakhab al-Husami, his teachers, works, and death date.

Core Idea
Usul al-Fiqh was first compiled by Imam al-Shafi'i; al-Muntakhab al-Husami is a seventh-century Hanafi classic.

Understanding the historical development of a discipline gives context to its content. This lesson covers how Usul al-Fiqh evolved from the era of the Companions to the seventh century, highlighting key works and scholars. It also introduces the author of our text, Husam al-Din al-Asyakit, his scholarly pedigree, and his important works.

Guided Path
1
Recall the early period (Companions and immediate successors)
In the time of the Companions, Usul al-Fiqh was not compiled as a separate discipline; it was practiced implicitly. After the Companions, compilation began.
Practical application preceded theoretical systematisation.
2
Identify the role of the early Hanafi school
Imam Abu Hanifah laid the foundations of usul when he compiled fiqh, but he did not write a dedicated usul work. His students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, wrote books on usul, but these are now lost.
This shows the Hanafi school's early contribution to usul, even if not extant.
3
Recognize Imam al-Shafi'i as the first compiler
The first surviving systematic work on Usul al-Fiqh is Imam al-Shafi'i's Risalah, which was originally the introduction to his al-Umm. It covers key topics like commands, prohibitions, bayan, and naskh.
Al-Shafi'i is often called the 'father of usul al-fiqh' for this reason.
4
Trace subsequent developments
From the late second/early third century, major works appeared: al-Maturidi's Kitab al-Jadal and Makhadh al-Shar', then al-Jassas's Kitab al-Usul and Ibn Barran's al-Dhakhirah in the fourth century. The fifth century saw ten books, and the sixth/seventh continued. Al-Muntakhab al-Husami belongs to the seventh century.
This timeline shows the growth of the discipline.
5
Learn the author's biography
The author is Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Umar, titled Husam al-Din, with kunyah Abu Abdullah, from Asyakit in Ferghana. He was a master of both furu' and usul, taught notable students, and wrote al-Muntakhab al-Husami and a refutation of al-Ghazali's al-Mankhul. He died on 22/23 Dhu al-Qa'dah 143 H (AH 743).
Note: The death year 143 H seems early; likely a misprint for 743 H (or 1342 CE).

Introduction
Urdu commentary on al-Muntakhab al-Husami
Before starting any discipline, it is necessary to know seven things so that the study of that discipline can begin with insight: (1) Definition of the discipline, (2) Subject of the discipline, (3) Purpose and goal of the discipline, (4) Rank of the discipline, (5) Ruling of the discipline, (6) Compilation of the discipline, (7) Biography of the author. (1) Definition of Usul al-Fiqh: Usul al-Fiqh is a construct phrase composed of two parts: 'usul' (added) and 'fiqh' (added to). So in Usul al-Fiqh, it is necessary to keep in view the definition of both parts. The separate definition of each part is called al-hadd al-idafi, and defining both together is called al-hadd al-haqiqi. Al-hadd al-idafi of Usul al-Fiqh: 'usul' is the plural of 'asl', which lexically means 'that on which something else is built' [66], something on which another thing is based. The discipline of Usul al-Fiqh is called so because upon it the foundation of Fiqh is established. The second part is 'fiqh'. Lexically, fiqh means to open something, to clarify. A faqih is called that scholar who clarifies the legal rulings. The terminological definition of fiqh: 'It is knowledge of the subsidiary legal rulings derived from their detailed evidences.' The rulings that pertain to actions are called subsidiary rulings, and those that pertain to beliefs are called primary rulings. Al-hadd al-haqiqi of Usul al-Fiqh: Allamah Kamal al-Din Ibn al-Humam defined it in al-Tahrir as: 'It is the comprehension of the principles by which one can reach the derivation of fiqh.' That is, Usul al-Fiqh is the name for knowing the principles through which fiqh can be attained. (2) Subject of Usul al-Fiqh: The four sources (Book, Sunnah, Consensus, and Analogy) and the collection of rulings constitute the subject of Usul al-Fiqh. Some say only the sources are the subject, and some say only the rulings are the subject, but the preferred view is the first. And the subject of the discipline of Fiqh is the rulings along with their detailed evidences. So both Fiqh and Usul al-Fiqh relate to the sources, but the mode of relation is different: Fiqh's relation to the sources is such that particular rulings are extracted from them, while Usul al-Fiqh's relation to the sources is in terms of the method of derivation and deduction and ranks of authority. (3) Purpose and goal of Usul al-Fiqh: To know the legal rulings along with their detailed evidences, and to know the principles of deriving issues. (4) Rank and status of Usul al-Fiqh: Whatever the rank of its subject, that will be the rank of the discipline. Thus, the subject of Usul al-Fiqh is the Book of Allah, Sunnah, Consensus, Analogy, and rulings; all these things are of high rank religiously, so the rank of this discipline is also high. (5) Ruling of Usul al-Fiqh: Acquiring Usul al-Fiqh is a communal obligation (fard al-kifayah). (6) Compilation of Usul al-Fiqh: In the era of the Companions, just as other disciplines were not compiled, Usul al-Fiqh was also not compiled. After the era of the Companions, its compilation took place. Imam Abu Hanifah is the first compiler of fiqh, so certainly he laid the foundation of Usul al-Fiqh at the time of compiling fiqh, but from the Imam himself no writing in this discipline is available. However, his students Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad wrote books on Usul al-Fiqh, but they are rare; now it is difficult to find them. The first composition on Usul al-Fiqh is the Risalah of Imam al-Shafi'i, in which he wrote about commands and prohibitions, elucidation, and reports and abrogation etc. This treatise is essentially the introduction to his book al-Umm, which is considered the foundation of fiqh. At the end of the second century and the beginning of the third century, regular composition and compilation had begun, and in that period two excellent books appeared: Kitab al-Jadal and Makhadh al-Shar' by Shaykh Abu Mansur Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Maturidi (d. 23? H). Then in the fourth century, Kitab al-Usul by Shaykh Imam Abu Bakr al-Jassas (d. ???) and Kitab al-Dhakhirah by Shaykh Ahmad ibn Husayn known as Ibn Barran al-Farisi (d. 350 H) were authored. Then in the fifth century, ten books came into existence. In the sixth and seventh centuries, this series progressed further, and the book under consideration, al-Muntakhab al-Husami, is from this seventh century. (7) Biography of the author (may Allah bless him and grant him peace): The author's name is Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Umar, his title is Husam al-Din, and his kunyah is Abu Abdullah. He was attributed to Asyakit and thus called Asyakit. Asyakit is a famous and beautiful city of Ferghana, about which the author of al-Ansab wrote: 'It was one of its most pleasant and beautiful cities.' He was a perfect shaykh, a virtuous imam, a scholar of furu' and usul, and an expert in disputation and disagreement. Muhammad ibn Umar al-Hawabazi, Muhammad ibn Bukhari, and Fakhr al-Din Muhammad ibn Ahmad al-Maymarfi among the distinguished scholars acquired fiqh from him. Works: His book al-Muntakhab al-Husami is the best, reliable, accepted, and widely used book of Usul al-Fiqh. Besides that, in refutation of Hujjat al-Islam Imam al-Ghazali's 'al-Mankhul', which is a criticism of the Great Imam, he wrote a fine treatise consisting of six chapters, in which he refuted every single statement of Imam al-Ghazali and also mentioned the noble virtues of the Imam. Death: He died on Saturday, 22/23 Dhu al-Qa'dah 143 H and was buried near Qadikhan in the cemetery of the judges. The lesson is: this world is not your abode; you came for another purpose.

Commentary
This passage is an introduction to the study of Usul al-Fiqh. The author begins by stating the seven prerequisites for any discipline: definition, subject, purpose, rank, ruling, compilation, and author biography. He then applies these to Usul al-Fiqh.

First, the definition of Usul al-Fiqh is given in two ways: the partial definition (al-hadd al-idafi) which defines its two components separately, and the complete definition (al-hadd al-haqiqi) which defines the compound. 'Usul' is the plural of 'asl', meaning that on which something else is built, so Usul al-Fiqh is the foundation for Fiqh. 'Fiqh' is defined terminologically as knowledge of subsidiary legal rulings from their detailed evidences. The complete definition, from Ibn al-Humam, is that Usul al-Fiqh is the comprehension of the principles by which one can derive fiqh.

Second, the subject (mawdu') of Usul al-Fiqh is the four sources (Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas) and also the rulings themselves. There is a difference of opinion: some say only the sources, some only the rulings, but the preferred view is the first (only sources). However, the text earlier included both. This reflects an internal discussion in the Hanafi tradition.

Third, the purpose is to know the legal rulings with their evidences and to learn the principles of derivation.

Fourth, the rank of Usul al-Fiqh is high because its subject matter is of high religious importance.

Fifth, the ruling is that acquiring it is a communal obligation (fard al-kifayah).

Sixth, the historical compilation is traced: after the Companions, Imam Abu Hanifah laid the foundation, his students wrote books that are now lost, and the first systematic work is Imam al-Shafi'i's Risalah. The text then lists key works by al-Maturidi, al-Jassas, Ibn Barran, and finally the book being commented on, al-Muntakhab al-Husami from the seventh century.

Seventh, the author's biography: Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Umar, known as Husam al-Din al-Asyakit, a scholar from Ferghana. He studied under notable teachers and taught many. His main work is al-Muntakhab al-Husami, and he also wrote a refutation of Ghazali's al-Mankhul. He died in 143 H.

All these points equip the student with the necessary orientation before delving into the detailed principles of Usul al-Fiqh.

Key Terms

المنتخب الحسامی (al-Muntakhab al-Husami): A Hanafi usul al-fiqh text by Husam al-Din al-Asyakit, from the seventh century.

Compilation of Usul al-Fiqh: A Historical Timeline
Historical timeline of usul compilation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key stages in the compilation of Usul al-Fiqh and name the first written work on the subject.

Core Idea
Usul al-Fiqh was not compiled in the Sahaba era; the first systematic work was Imam al-Shafi'i's Risalah.

This passage traces the history of usul compilation, showing how the science developed from oral foundations to written texts. Understanding this timeline helps you appreciate the authority and context of the texts you study, such as al-Muntakhab al-Husami, which appeared in the 7th century.

Guided Path
1
Identify the pre-compilation era
Note that during the time of the Companions, Usul al-Fiqh, like other sciences, was not yet compiled into written form. The science existed orally as part of the practice of ijtihad.
This is a key point: the absence of writing does not mean absence of knowledge.
2
Recognize the role of Imam Abu Hanifah and his students
Although Imam Abu Hanifah is credited with laying the foundations of fiqh and by extension usul, he did not produce a written work on usul. His students, Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad, did write usul books, but they are now rare.
3
Locate the first written work
The passage states that the first work on Usul al-Fiqh is the Risalah of Imam al-Shafi'i, which comprises topics like commands, prohibitions, bayan, khabar, and naskh. This Risalah serves as the introduction to his book al-Umm.
This marks the formal beginning of usul literature.
4
Trace later development through centuries
The author mentions key works from the 3rd to 7th centuries: al-Maturidi's Kitab al-Jadal and Makhidh al-Shar'i (3rd c.), al-Jassas's Kitab al-Usul and Ibn Baran's al-Dhakheerah (4th c.), and then a proliferation of books in the 5th-6th centuries, culminating in the 7th-century work al-Muntakhab al-Husami, which is the subject of study.
This chronology shows the continuous growth of usul scholarship.

(4) Compilation of Usul al-Fiqh: In the era of the Companions, just as other sciences were not compiled, likewise Usul al-Fiqh was not compiled. After the era of the Companions, its compilation took place. Imam Abu Hanifah (may Allah have mercy on him) is the first compiler of fiqh, so undoubtedly he laid the foundation of Usul al-Fiqh at the time of compiling fiqh, but no written work from him in this field remains. However, his students Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on them) wrote books on Usul al-Fiqh, but they are rare; now tracing them is difficult.

The first work on Usul al-Fiqh is the Risalah of Imam al-Shafi'i (may Allah have mercy on him), in which he wrote topics such as commands and prohibitions, Bayan, and Khabar and Naskh. This Risalah is actually the introduction to his book al-Umm, which is considered the foundation of fiqh. At the end of the second century and beginning of the third century, formal authorship and compilation began, and during this period two excellent books appeared: Kitab al-Jadal and Makhidh al-Shar'i by Shaykh Abu Mansur Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Maturidi (d. 323 H). Then in the fourth century, Kitab al-Usul by Shaykh Imam Abu Bakr al-Jassas (d. 370 H) and Kitab al-Dhakheerah by Shaykh Ahmad ibn Husayn, known as Ibn Baran al-Farsi (d. 350 H), were authored. Then in the fifth century, ten books came into existence. In the sixth and seventh centuries, this series progressed further, and the book under discussion, al-Muntakhab al-Husami, is from this seventh century.

(7) Biography of the author (may Allah bless him and grant him peace): The author's name is Muhammad ibn Muhammad ibn Umar, his title is Husam al-Din, and his kunyah is Abu Abdullah. He was attributed to the city of Akhsikath, so he was called al-Akhsikathi. Akhsikath is a famous and beautiful city of Farghana, about which the author of al-Ansab wrote: "It was one of the most pleasant and beautiful of its cities." He was a perfect shaykh, a virtuous imam, a scholar of furu' and usul, and an expert in debate and disagreement. Senior scholars such as Muhammad ibn Umar Nawabazi, Muhammad ibn Bukhari, and Fakhr al-Din Muhammad ibn Ahmad Maimarghi studied fiqh under him. Works: His book al-Muntakhab al-Husami is the best, authentic, and popular book on Usul al-Fiqh. Additionally, he wrote a refined treatise of six chapters in refutation of Hujjat al-Islam Imam Ghazali's al-Mankhul, which was based on disparagement of Imam A'zam, in which he refutes each statement of Imam Ghazali with evidence, and also mentions Imam's noble virtues.

Death: He passed away on Saturday, 22 or 23 Dhu al-Qi'dah 143 H? [actually 643 H?] and was buried in the cemetery of judges (Maqbarat al-Qudat) near Qadi Khan.

'Rasul': means messenger, a prophet sent with a new book and shariah. 'Muhammad': passive participle meaning praised; both Muhammad and Ahmad are names of the Prophet. His grandfather named him Muhammad, his mother Ahmad; he is called Muhammad on earth and Ahmad in heaven. Commentary
This passage is the opening of the commentary (al-Fayd al-Hijazi) on al-Husami's matn. The author begins with the basmalah and hamdalah, following the sunnah of the Prophet and the practice of earlier scholars. He then discusses the meaning and usage of key terms. 'Ba'd' and 'qabl' are explained as relational terms for time and place. 'Hamd' is defined technically as verbal praise for a voluntarily chosen good quality, whether a blessing or not. 'Salat' is defined in its linguistic and technical senses, and its meanings when attributed to Allah, angels, believers, and birds. 'Rasul' is defined as a prophet sent with a new book and shariah. 'Muhammad' is explained as a name meaning 'praised,' and its significance in heaven and earth. 'Al' is distinguished from 'ahl' in terms of usage: 'al' is used for nobles, only rational beings, and masculine, whereas 'ahl' is more general. The author notes Imam Abu Hanifa's opinion that the family of the Prophet (Al) includes those for whom sadaqah is forbidden and who have a share in booty, specifically the wives and children, but some extend it to all pious believers.

Key Terms
تسمیہ (tasmiyah): The act of saying 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim'.
تحمید (tahmid): The act of praising Allah, saying 'Alhamdulillah' or similar.
أما بعد (amma ba'd): An Arabic phrase meaning 'to proceed' or 'after this', used as a transition after the opening praise.
بعد (ba'd): A term meaning 'after', used for both time and place.
قبل (qabl): A term meaning 'before', used for both time and place.

Defining Key Terms: Hamd, Salat, Rasul, and Aal
Lexical and technical definitions of key terms in the opening lines of al-Husami.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the terms hamd, salat, rasul, and aal according to the Hanafi usul tradition, and distinguish between 'al' and 'ahl'.

Core Idea
Precise lexical definitions are foundational for correct legal reasoning.

In this passage, the commentator provides detailed linguistic and technical definitions of four key terms. These definitions are not mere philology; they affect how legal texts are interpreted.

Guided Path
1
Examine the definition of 'hamd'
Hamd is verbal praise for a voluntarily chosen good quality, whether a blessing or not.
This distinguishes it from shukr.
2
Analyze 'salat'
Salat linguistically means supplication; technically it is the specific acts of worship. Its meaning changes based on the attributor.
When attributed to Allah: mercy; to angels: seeking forgiveness; to believers: prayer; to birds: glorification.
3
Understand 'rasul'
A rasul is a prophet sent with a new book and shariah.
Distinct from nabi.
4
Learn about 'aal'
Aal originally from ahl, used for nobles, only rational beings, masculine. Imam Abu Hanifa defines Al Muhammad as those for whom sadaqah is forbidden and who have a share in booty.
Distinguish from ahl which is more general.
5
Note the distinctive features
Recognize the specific Hanafi usage, especially for 'aal'.

'Al' is distinguished from 'ahl' in terms of usage: 'al' is used for nobles, only rational beings, and masculine, whereas 'ahl' is more general. The author notes Imam Abu Hanifa's opinion that the family of the Prophet (Al) includes those for whom sadaqah is forbidden and who have a share in booty, specifically the wives and children, but some extend it to all pious believers.

Key Terms
حمد (hamd): Verbal praise for a voluntarily chosen good quality, whether a blessing or not.
صلوة (salat): Linguistically supplication; technically the specific acts of worship. When attributed to Allah: mercy; to angels: seeking forgiveness; to believers: seeking mercy and supplication; of birds: glorification.
رسول (rasul): A prophet sent with a new book and shariah.
آل (aal): The family or household, specifically those of noble status, used for rational beings and masculine; distinguished from ahl.
محمد (Muhammad): Passive participle meaning praised; one of the Prophet's names, meaning the praised one.
أهل (ahl): Family or people, used more generally than 'al', for rational and non-rational, masculine and feminine.
