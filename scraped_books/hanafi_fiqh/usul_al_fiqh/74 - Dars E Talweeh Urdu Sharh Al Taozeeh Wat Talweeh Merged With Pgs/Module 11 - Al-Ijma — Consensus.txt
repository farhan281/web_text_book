Ijma as a Definitive Proof: The Raising of Religion's Banners
Commentary on a line praising ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi view that ijma produces qat'i rulings and analyze the commentator's linguistic and critical remarks in this passage.

Core Idea
Ijma raises the banners of religion – a ruling established by consensus is permanently elevated and cannot be lowered.

This passage from the Sharh al-Talwih comments on a line from Al-Tawdih praising the role of ijma. The commentator explains that the banners of religion remain raised as long as mujtahids exert themselves to establish consensus. This underscores the definitive and permanent nature of ijma as a source of law. The passage also includes a linguistic analysis of the author's style and a subtle critique of the work's organization, showing the critical engagement typical of the Hanafi sharh tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key phrase and its legal implication
Locate 'ma rufi'a a'lam al-din' and note the commentator's interpretation that 'ma' means 'as long as' and that the elevation is caused by ijma. This sets the stage for the principle of ijma's permanence.
The phrase is not a direct proof but a statement about the effect of ijma.
2
Extract the ijma principle
Find the sentence: 'فإن الحكم المجمع عليه مرفوع لا يوضع' – the ruling agreed upon by consensus is elevated and not lowered. This reflects the Hanafi position that ijma produces qat'i, unassailable rulings.
This is the core legal takeaway.
3
Analyze the linguistic commentary
Examine the commentator's descriptions of the author: 'jaleel al-shan', 'bahir al-burhan', 'markooz'. These terms indicate the depth and strength of the author's work. Understand the lexical nuances.
This shows the commentator's engagement with the matn.
4
Identify the critique
Read the final part where the commentator alludes to 'additions that must be removed' and 'fragments that must be organized'. This is a subtle criticism of the matn's structure, characteristic of expansion_probe.
The critique is respectful but shows scholarly independence.
5
Formulate the usul rule
Synthesize: Ijma is a definitive proof (qat'i thubut and dalala). Rulings established by ijma are permanently binding and cannot be abrogated or lowered.
This is the key usul principle derived from the passage.

The commentator said: His saying: 'ma rufi'a' meaning as long as the banners of religion are raised high by the consensus of the mujtahids who exert their capacity in elevating the word of Allah and reviving the rites of religion. Indeed, a ruling upon which there is consensus is elevated, not lowered, and raised, not brought down.

Commentary:
We want to explain the text's statement: In 'ma rufi'a a'lam al-din', 'ma' is in the sense of 'madam' (as long as), and 'a'lam' is in the sense of 'rayat' (banners). How are the banners of religion raised by the consensus of the mujtahids? That is such: through the efforts of the mujtahids, the ways of religion are revived, and due to their beautiful endeavors, the word of Allah is elevated. Indeed, a ruling that is agreed upon (on which consensus has been made) is always high, it can never be lowered, it always remains established, it does not fall.

The commentator said: His saying: 'jaleel al-shan' i.e., great in matter, 'bahir al-burhan' i.e., overwhelming proof and surpassing it; 'markooz' i.e., buried, from 'rakaztu al-rumha' i.e., I thrust it into the ground. And 'kunooz' is buried wealth. And 'sukhoor' are large stones; he likened to them his difficult, strong expressions due to the difficulty of arriving at understanding of the meanings that are like precious jewels. 'Ramza' is a sign with lips or eyebrow; it is transitive with 'ila', so the original phrase is 'marmoz ila ghawamid', but the preposition was dropped and the verb made transitive, so 'ghawamid' becomes the subject. 'Al-nuktah' is the refined subtlety from 'nakata fi al-ard' with a stick: when one strikes the ground with it and it sinks into it, meaning he alluded to the hidden subtlety during his precise hints. 'Al-nazar' is looking at something with the corner of the eye, and 'al-lahaz' is looking at something with the corner of the eye, and 'al-lahaz' (with fatha) is the corner of the eye. And 'al-tanqih' is refinement; he says: 'naqah al-jidh'a wa shadhabtahu' when you cut off what spreads from its branches and is not in its core. And 'tanzim al-durar fi al-silk' is gathering them as they should be, orderly and well-arranged. And the speech is not devoid of some allusion that in the principles of Fakhr al-Islam there are additions that must be removed, and fragments that must be organized, and knots that must be untied, and that it is not built upon the rules of the intellect such that definitions and proofs observe their conditions mentioned in the science of logic. And in the divisions, there is overlapping of sections, and other things that the scholars pay attention to.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a line from the matn (likely Al-Tawdih) that praises the role of ijma in elevating the religion. The commentator explains 'ma rufi'a a'lam al-din' as 'as long as the banners of religion are raised.' The cause of this elevation is the consensus (ijma) of the mujtahids who strive to elevate the word of Allah and revive the rites of Islam. The passage emphasizes that a ruling established by ijma is permanent and cannot be abrogated or lowered; it remains firmly established. This reflects the high authority of ijma in Hanafi usul: it is a decisive proof (qat'i). The commentator then moves to praise the author of the matn (Fakhr al-Islam) by analyzing terms like 'jaleel al-shan' (great in station), 'bahir al-burhan' (overwhelming proof), and 'markooz' (buried deep), indicating that the author's expressions are profound like buried treasures. The commentator also alludes to some flaws in the work (additions, disorganization) that need refinement. This shows the critical yet respectful nature of the super-commentary (Talwih).

Key Terms
اجماع (ijma): Consensus of the mujtahids on a religious ruling; this passage emphasizes its definitive and permanent nature.
ما رفع اعلام الدين (ma rufi'a a'lam al-din): 'As long as the banners of religion are raised' — an expression denoting the perpetual elevation and strength of the religion through ijma.

Condition for Qiyas: Avoiding Ijma through Composite Consensus
States the condition that qiyas must avoid ijma and explains composite consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a qiyas is invalid due to composite ijma and apply the condition that qiyas must be based on a mujtahid's opinion.

Core Idea
If two or three mujtahid opinions exist on a matter, their composite consensus prevents any new qiyas.

In Hanafi usul, qiyas is a valid source of law, but it has conditions. One critical condition is that the qiyas must be the product of a mujtahid's ijtihad, not a violation of existing consensus. This lesson explores what happens when previous mujtahids have already issued opinions on the issue — the concept of composite ijma (ijtimā‘ murakkab) — and when a later mujtahid may still exercise ijtihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
The author states that qiyas must be arrived at by the opinion of a mujtahid. This is the condition being discussed.
The condition is set to avoid violating ijma.
2
Understand the rationale
The commentator explains that the condition is to avoid the accusation of going against ijma. Ijma is a binding proof (dalil qat'i).
Violating ijma is impermissible in Hanafi usul.
3
Define composite ijma
If two or three mujtahid opinions exist on an issue, they form 'composite consensus' (ijtimā‘ murakkab). The truth is confined within those opinions.
Even if the opinions differ, they collectively represent the truth.
4
Determine when new ijtihad is allowed
New ijtihad (and thus qiyas) is only permissible when either no prior opinion exists or only one opinion exists. In the case of composite ijma, a fourth opinion is invalid.
5
Apply the rule
Given a scenario, count the number of existing mujtahid opinions. If 2 or more, composite ijma applies and a new qiyas is invalid. If 0 or 1, a new qiyas may be attempted.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion probe clarifying a condition for qiyas: it must be based on a mujtahid’s opinion (رأى مجتهد) to avoid violating ijma. The commentator explains that if multiple mujtahid opinions exist on a matter, they form a 'composite ijma' (اجتماع مرکب), meaning the truth is confined to those opinions. A later mujtahid cannot introduce a new opinion; to do so would break the consensus. However, if no previous opinion exists or only one, then a contrary ijtihad is permissible. This rule ensures that qiyas does not contravene established scholarly consensus.

Key Terms
اجتماع مرکب (ijtimā‘ murakkab): Composite consensus: when two or three mujtahid opinions exist on an issue, they collectively represent the truth; no new opinion may be added.
اجماع (ijmā‘): Consensus of mujtahids on a ruling; here it is used in the context of composite consensus where multiple opinions confine the truth.
قیاس (qiyās): Analogical reasoning: a method of deriving rulings by applying the effective cause (illah) from a known case to a new case.
مجتہد (mujtahid): A jurist qualified to perform ijtihad and derive rulings from the sources.
