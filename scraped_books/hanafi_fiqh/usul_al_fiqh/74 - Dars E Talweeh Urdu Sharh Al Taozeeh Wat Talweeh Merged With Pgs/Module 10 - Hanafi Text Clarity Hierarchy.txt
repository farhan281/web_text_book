The Palace Analogy: Four Degrees of Textual Clarity
Drawing an analogy between parts of a palace and categories of legal texts (muhkam, nass, mutashabih, mujmal).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given scriptural text as muhkam, nass, mujmal, or mutashabih by applying the palace analogy and state the defining characteristic of each category.

Core Idea
Palace exterior = muhkam, interior = nass, hidden cabinet = mutashabih, moderate hiding = mujmal.

This passage uses a vivid analogy to cement the Hanafi classification of legal texts by clarity. A palace has parts visible to all, less visible, completely hidden, and moderately hidden — corresponding to four textual categories. Mastering this taxonomy is essential for correct istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the analogy
Picture a palace: the exterior is fully visible, interior rooms less so, hidden cabinets known only to the owner, and moderately hidden areas not completely concealed.
2
Map palace parts to textual categories
Exterior → muhkam (utmost clarity), interior → nass (clear but less), hidden cabinet → mutashabih (utmost concealment, only Allah knows), moderate hiding → mujmal (needs clarification).
3
Memorize operational definitions
Muhkam: no need for explanation; Nass: clear but may be specified; Mujmal: requires bayan from elsewhere; Mutashabih: true meaning known only to Allah.
4
Apply to real texts
For example, 'Establish prayer' (muhkam in obligation), 'Cut off the thief's hand' (muhkam but details in Sunnah), 'The Most Merciful rose over the Throne' (mutashabih).
The key difference between mujmal and mutashabih: mujmal can be clarified; mutashabih remains ambiguous as a test.

Commentary
In this passage, the commentator introduces a powerful pedagogical analogy (tashbih) to clarify the Hanafi classification of scriptural texts by degree of clarity. The analogy compares the 'palace of rulings' (qasr al-ahkam) to a physical palace. Just as a palace has areas of different visibility—some completely obvious (exterior), some less so (interior rooms), some utterly hidden (secret cabinets accessible only to the owner), and some moderately hidden—so too do the texts of the Qur'an and Hadith fall into four categories: muhkam (most clear, like the exterior), nass (less clear), mutashabih (most ambiguous, known only to Allah), and mujmal (moderately ambiguous). The passage then goes into detail: the term 'maksurat' (in the original matn) means 'confined', indicating that mutashabih is shrouded in concealment. The commentator firmly states the Hanafi position (and indeed the position of early Salaf) that the true interpretation (ta'wil) of mutashabih is known only to Allah. The purpose of revealing such ambiguous verses is a test (ibtilah) for scholars: they must resist the urge to delve into what is hidden and instead exercise restraint. This is a key usul principle: the mujmal requires clarification elsewhere, but mutashabih is left ambiguous by divine wisdom. The commentary also includes a brief recap of the earlier question about order, but the primary new material is this analogy and its explanation.

Key Terms
محكم (muhkam): A text that is utmost in clarity, like the exterior of a palace.
نص (nass): A text less clear than muhkam, like interior rooms.
متشابه (mutashabih): A text that is utmost in concealment; its true interpretation is known only to Allah.
مجمل (mujmal): A text that is moderately hidden, requiring clarification (bayan) from elsewhere.

The Unity of a Science's Subject Matter: The Condition of Munasabah
Critiquing the matn on the unity of a science's subject matter

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the concept of munasabah (proportionality) to determine whether multiple subject matters constitute a single science, using the examples from geometry, medicine, and usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Multiple subject matters in one science require proportionality (munasabah) — only then does unity hold.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, a foundational question is whether a single science can have more than one subject matter. The matn (Sadr al-Shariah) argued for strict unity, but the commentator (Taftazani) defends the majority view that multiple subjects are permissible if they share a unifying property. This lesson unpacks that condition and its implications for how we classify sciences like usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the matn's claim
The matn argues that a science must have a single subject matter; otherwise its issues become multiple and it splits into multiple sciences.
This claim is based on the idea that difference in subject matter causes difference in issues, which causes difference in sciences.
2
Identify the commentator's objection
The commentator says the matn's reasoning confuses mere multiplicity of issues with lack of proportionality. Mere multiplicity does not necessitate multiple sciences.
The commentator points out that every science has many issues; the key is whether those issues lack proportionality.
3
Understand the condition of munasabah
The majority (qawm) state that multiple things can be subject matters for a single science under the condition of their proportionality (shart tanasubiha).
Proportionality can be essential (dhati) like sharing a genus, or accidental (aradi) like sharing a common goal.
4
Analyze the examples
Geometry: line, surface, and educational body share the genus of quantity. Medicine: foods, medicines, elements, etc., share relation to health. Usul al-fiqh: sources and rulings share the goal of istinbat.
These examples illustrate both dhati and aradi proportionality.
5
Apply to the matn's own examples
The commentator shows that the matn's own examples (usul and logic) have multiple subject matters, contradicting his thesis.
For usul al-fiqh, the predicates of its issues are attached to multiple sources; for logic, conception and assent are multiple.

Commentary: From here, the commentator wants to refute the matn's investigation about the subject matter. He says: The author made a statement about the subject matter that is completely contrary to the majority, and anyone familiar with the majority's discourse sees it with astonishment. In summary, the matn wrote that it is incorrect for a science to have two or more subject matters; rather a science can have only one subject matter because what is investigated in a science is either an addition or not. If it is an addition, like usul al-fiqh, then we see that the states related to what is investigated are either related to the added (mudaf) or the added-to (mudaf ilayh). So at that time both become subject matters, as we see here that some states relate to the evidence and some accidents relate to the rulings; hence both become subject matters to avoid unwarranted preference. If what is investigated is an addition but the accidents and states are related to only one of the added and added-to, then the subject matter is one, like the subject matter of logic is the connection of conception and assent to an unknown conception and unknown assent, but the states relate to the added, so the subject matters are the connecting conception and assent, not the connected-to. And if it is not an addition, then the subject matter is one, like fiqh's subject matter is obligation and prohibition, because a science's being one or multiple depends on the subject matter's unity or multiplicity. If a science is one and its subject matters are many, then its issues become many, thereby it will not be one but rather multiple sciences. And if by it is meant their lack of proportionality, we do not affirm that mere multiplicity of subject matters necessitates that; rather that would be necessary only if the multiple subject matters were not proportional. And the authorities (qawm) stated that multiple things are only the subject matter of a single science under the condition of their proportionality. And the aspect of proportionality is their sharing in a essential property (dhati) like line, surface, and educational body for geometry, for they share in their genus which is quantity—i.e., continuous fixed quantum—or in an accidental property (aradi) like the human body and its parts, foods, medicines, elements, temperaments, etc., when made the subject matters of medicine, for they share in being related to health, which is the goal (ghayah) in that science. So it is known that they did not neglect observing a meaning that necessitates unity, and that no one may arbitrarily agree that fiqh and geometry are one science with subject matters the mukallaf's action and quantity. Moreover, in the examples he cited he contradicts himself, because the subject matter of usul is many things: the predicates of its issues are not essential accidents of the concept of proof but rather of the Book, Sunnah, Consensus, and Analogy individually and jointly for two or more; similarly, conception and assent for logic.' Commentary: From here, he wants to refute the matn in detail. The author said that difference of issues indicates difference of science. If you mean by difference of issues mere multiplicity, that occurs in every science because a science is not named after a few issues. And if you mean by difference of issues a multiplicity that lacks proportionality, indeed such multiplicity cannot form one science. But the majority necessarily observes proportionality. For example, the subject matter of geometry is line, surface, and educational body, but there is proportionality among them because they share in the genus of quantity. Similarly, the subject matter of medicine includes foods, medicines, temperaments, and elements, but there is sharing in accidental properties because they are all related to health, which is the goal of medicine, and the goal is accidental. Hence without proportionality, these authorities do not hold multiple subject matters. For example, if someone says that fiqh and geometry are one science because their subject matter is the mukallaf's action and quantity, it would be wrong because absolute action and quantity have no proportionality. Finally, he says that the examples the author presented contradict himself.

Commentary
This passage is a critical commentary on the earlier text (matn) from Al-Tawdih. The author of the matn had argued that a single science can have only one subject matter; otherwise it would lead to multiplicity of sciences. The commentator refutes this by presenting the view of the majority (jumhur) that multiple subject matters are permissible as long as they share a unifying relationship (munasabah), either essential (dhati) like line, surface, and body in geometry all sharing the genus of quantity, or accidental (aradi) like the various subjects of medicine all related to health as the goal (ghayah). The commentator points out that the matn's own examples contradict his thesis because the subject matter of usul al-fiqh is actually multiple (Book, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) and so is the subject matter of logic (conception and assent). The reasoning is based on rational argument (dalil aqli) and consensus of the authorities (qawm). The lesson here is a usul principle: the subject matter of a science can be multiple under the condition of proportionality.

Key Terms
موضوع العلم (mawdu' al-ilm): That which a science investigates; the subject matter from which its issues (masa'il) are derived. It may be one or multiple, with the condition that multiple subjects share a unifying property (munasabah).
مناسبة (munasabah): The property of proportionality or appropriateness that unifies multiple subject matters into a single science. It can be essential (dhati) like sharing a genus, or accidental (aradi) like sharing a common goal (ghayah).
