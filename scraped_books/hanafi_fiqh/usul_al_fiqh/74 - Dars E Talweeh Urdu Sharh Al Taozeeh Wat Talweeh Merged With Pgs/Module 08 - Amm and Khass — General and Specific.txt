Al-Kalim as Ism Jins: Linguistic Foundation in Hanafi Usul
Linguistic parsing of 'kalim' with gender and plural classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify 'al-kalim' as ism jins, justify its gender agreement flexibility using Quranic evidence, and identify the textual correction in the matn.

Core Idea
Al-kalim is a noun of genus, not a plural—its gender can be masculine or feminine depending on form or meaning.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text clarifies the linguistic nature of the word 'kalim'. Understanding whether it is a plural or a noun of genus (ism jins) has implications for how general commands (umum) are interpreted. The author uses Quranic examples to show that the same word can take both masculine and feminine adjectives, supporting the flexibility of gender agreement for ism jins. Additionally, a subtle correction of the matn is offered, highlighting the precision required in legal language.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic categories
Distinguish between ism jins (noun of genus), ism jam' (collective noun), and true plural based on the definitions given in the passage.
Ism jins applies to both few and many; ism jam' is for many with a different singular; true plural shares the same root.
2
Analyze the Quranic evidence
Examine the two Quranic verses (54:20 and 69:7) and note how the same word 'a'jazu' takes a masculine adjective in one and a feminine in the other.
This demonstrates that nouns with generic meaning can have variable gender agreement.
3
Apply the principle to 'kalim'
Explain why 'kalim' is classified as ism jins: it has a singular with ta (kalima), its pattern (fa'il) is not a plural pattern, and it is used for both few and many.
Thus, both tadhkir and ta'nith are permissible in its adjectives.
4
Critique the matn phrase
Identify why the commentator corrects 'in kana' to 'wa in kana'—the word 'in' implies doubt, but the nature of 'kalim' as ism jins is certain.
This shows the importance of precise wording in legal texts.

Commentary
This passage is a linguistic analysis of the word 'kalim' from a Hanafi usul perspective. The author explains that 'kalim' is a noun of genus (ism jins), used for both few and many. Its singular is formed by adding ta (kalima). Although the word is singular in form, it is often treated as plural because of its general meaning. Gender agreement can be either masculine (considering the form) or feminine (considering the meaning). The Quranic verse 'a'jazu nakhlin munqa'ir' uses masculine 'munqa'ir', while 'a'jazu nakhlin khawiya' uses feminine 'khawiya'. The word 'kalim' is not a true plural because its pattern (fa'il) is not a plural pattern; hence it is an ism jins. The commentator corrects a phrase in the matn: 'in kana' (if) should be 'wa in kana' (and if) because the nature of 'kalim' is certain, not doubtful. The subsequent Urdu commentary elaborates the differences between ism jam' (noun of multitude), ism jins, and plural. Ism jam' is for many with a different singular, ism jins is for few and many with no singular, plural shares the same root. The tashrih explains that 'yas'adu' (ascend) is used metaphorically for turning towards the exalted. 'Kalim' is used for three or more, hence feminine adjective is allowed. The author concludes that 'kalim' is ism jins like 'shamr' and 'rajab', not a real plural like 'nisab' and 'rabb'. The use of 'in' (if) in the matn is defective; it should be 'wa in' (and if) to indicate certainty.

Key Terms
الكلم (al-kalim): A noun of genus (ism jins) meaning speech; used for both few and many, its singular is kalima.
اسم الجنس (ism al-jins): A noun that applies to both few and many of its kind, without a singular from its own root.
تذكير وتأنيث (tadhkir wa ta'nith): Grammatical gender: masculine and feminine. For ism jins, both may be allowed depending on whether the word form or meaning is considered.

The Linguistic Basis of 'Al-Kalim' as Ism Jam' in Hanafi Usul
Linguistic analysis of the word 'الكلم' from a usul commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the morphological evidence that 'al-kalim' is an ism jam' and explain why the Hanafi commentator criticises the use of 'in' (if) in the matn.

Core Idea
'Al-kalim' is an ism jam' on pattern fa'l, not a true plural—this affects how we understand general terms in the sacred texts.

In this passage, the commentator of Al-Tawdih delves into the grammatical nature of the word 'al-kalim'. He demonstrates that it is an ism jam', a collective noun, based on morphological evidence and citations from Quranic usage. He then critiques the matn's phrase 'وإن كان جمعا' because once the ism jam' status is established, there should be no room for doubt. This lesson will guide you through this linguistic analysis, which is foundational for the Hanafi method of interpreting general (amm) texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the morphological pattern of 'al-kalim'
The word is on the pattern 'fa'l' (فَعْل). No true plural exists on this pattern. Compare to 'shimir' and 'rajab' (also ism jam') versus 'nisab' and 'rabbi' (true plurals).
This is a key clue: if a word on fa'l were a true plural, we would expect a corresponding singular form; none exists.
2
Examine the Quranic evidence for masculine/feminine treatment
The same word can take masculine or feminine adjectives depending on whether you consider meaning or word form. Example: 'أعجاز نخل منقعر' (masculine) and 'أعجاز نخل خاوية' (feminine). This flexibility supports ism jam' classification.
Allah uses both, illustrating that 'الكلم' can be treated either way.
3
Understand the matn's phrase 'وإن كان جمعا' and the commentator's critique
The matn used 'in' (if) which implies doubt. But if it is established that 'kalim' is an ism jam', there is no doubt; the commentator suggests using 'wa in kana' with waw for certainty.
The critique focuses on precision in usul texts; such linguistic exactness is crucial for deriving correct rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the phrase 'min mahamidi al-kalim' from the matn. The commentator begins by explaining that this phrase is literally a circumstantial accusative (hal) but functionally an elaboration (bayan) specifying what kind of praiseworthy words are meant. He draws a parallel with the Quranic verse about a good word being like a good tree, linking 'al-mahamid' (praiseworthy words) to 'al-kalim al-tayyib' (pure words) and noting that the root of praise (hamd) is faith and its fruit is righteous deeds. He then provides a lexical investigation of key terms such as 'haya' (faced), 'wajh al-rahman' (essence of the Merciful), 'mashra'at al-sama'' (watering place), and wind names like 'saba' (east wind) and 'dabur' (west wind). He describes how the west wind agitates clouds and the east wind disperses them, a metaphor for rain and growth.

The main point addressed here is a question: How can an indefinite noun (nakirah) like 'mahamid' serve as an elaboration for a definite noun (marifa) like 'al-kalim' which is universal (istighraq)? The commentator offers two answers. First, when a nakirah is qualified by an attribute (sifah), it becomes general. For example, 'imra'ah' (a woman) becomes 'kullu imra'ah' (every woman) when described as 'muhafiyah' (covering). Here, 'mahamid' is described by 'li-usuliha mashari' al-shar'' (for its principles are watering places of the law), so it becomes general and can elaborate 'al-kalim'. Second, the tanwin (nunation) on 'mahamid' indicates multiplicity (kathrah) according to the context, implying generality. Thus, the indefinite becomes equivalent to a general definite.

The commentator then distinguishes between 'hamd' (praise with the tongue) and 'shukr' (gratitude by any means), noting that 'al-kalim' (words) are suited to hamd because both involve speech. Finally, he explains that 'al-shar'' and 'al-shari'ah' are synonymous, referring to the revealed religion established by the Prophet, which is the subject of this work.

How an Indefinite Becomes General: Hanafi Linguistic Analysis of Bayan
Commentary defending Hanafi position on nakirah-ma'rifah bayan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that a nakirah qualified by an adjective or contextual takthir can become 'amm and serve as a bayan for a definite with lām al-istighrāq.

Core Idea
An indefinite can clarify a universal definite when context or an adjective generalizes it.

This passage addresses a common linguistic objection: how can an indefinite (nakirah) clarify a definite (ma'rifah) that includes all instances? The commentator provides two linguistic justifications rooted in Hanafi usul: the generalization through an adjective, and the contextual takthir (multiplication) indicated by tanwin. Understanding this principle is key to interpreting textual evidence in Hanafi fiqh, especially when phrases like 'min mahamid al-kalim' are used to define worship acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
Recognize the apparent contradiction between the indefinite 'mahamid' and the universal definite 'al-kalim' (with lām al-istighrāq). The critic argues that an indefinite cannot clarify a definite.
This objection is based on the default understanding that a nakirah is restricted, while a ma'rifah with lām istighrāq is all-encompassing.
2
Analyze the First Answer: Adjective Generalization
Examine the principle: when a nakirah is qualified by a specific adjective (sifat), it becomes general ('amm). The commentator uses the example of 'imra'ah' becoming 'all women of that type' when the adjective 'mahwiyyah' is added. In the text, 'mahamid' is qualified by 'li usuliha min mashari' al-shar'', thus it becomes general.
This principle is a recognized tool in Hanafi usul to expand the scope of a nakirah.
3
Analyze the Second Answer: Takthir via Tanwin
Understand that the tanwin (implied) on 'mahamid' is interpreted as indicating takthir (multiplication) due to the context (qarīnah). This yields a general meaning, making 'mahamid' compatible with 'al-kalim'.
Tanwin on plural forms is often used in Arabic to indicate 'many' or 'various', and here the context supports a general interpretation.
4
Verify Compatibility
Confirm that after applying either argument, the indefinite 'mahamid' now denotes generality, allowing it to serve as a bayan (clarifier) for the universal definite 'al-kalim'. The correspondence (mutabaqah) between bayan and mubayyan is re-established.
Both arguments are considered valid in Hanafi usul, and the commentator presents them as equally sound.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary (sharh) that addresses a linguistic and usul objection regarding the phrase 'min mahamid al-kalim'. The opponent argues that an indefinite (nakirah) cannot serve as a bayan (clarification) for a definite (ma'rifah) with lām al-istighrāq (all-inclusive definiteness). Second, the tanwin on 'mahamid' is interpreted as indicating takthir (multiplication) due to context, also yielding generality. Thus, it can clarify the universal 'al-kalim'. The commentator then explains lexical meanings: 'mahmada' is a verbal noun meaning praise, with 'hamd' specifically being praise by tongue, while 'shukr' is gratitude shown by any means. This suits the nature of 'kalim' (words) which are uttered. Further, 'shar'' and 'shari'ah' are synonymous, denoting the divine law. The author uses a beautiful metaphor: 'Mashari' al-Shar'' likens the Shari'ah to gardens with watering places, where the thirsty can drink pure water of mercy. Another metaphor compares acceptance ('qubul) to the rising sun and the east wind (saba) because of their life-giving and gathering properties. Finally, 'numuww' (growth) is defined as natural proportional increase, excluding pathological swelling. The discussion continues with the adjective phrase for 'mahamid' referencing the Quranic verse.

The Meaning of 'Asl' in Usul al-Fiqh: Literal vs. Technical
Commentary on the intended meaning of 'asl'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the literal meaning of 'asl' and explain why the Hanafi school retains it in 'usul al-fiqh' without transferring to the technical meaning 'dalil'.

Core Idea
'Asl' in 'usul al-fiqh' means 'foundation' literally; 'proof' is just one example of that foundation.

This passage is a commentary that clarifies the term 'asl' in the phrase 'usul al-fiqh'. The commentator argues that there is no need to treat 'asl' as a technical term for 'proof'. Instead, its literal meaning—'that upon which something else is built'—sufficently covers the intended concept. The addition of 'fiqh', an intellectual discipline, indicates an intellectual building, so the foundations of fiqh are its proofs. This approach avoids unnecessary deviation from the literal meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the technical meanings of 'asl'
The commentator lists other common uses: al-rajih (preponderant), al-qa'ida al-kulliyya (universal principle), and al-dalil (proof).
2
Recognize the commentator's defense of the literal meaning
The commentator notes that the author of the matn indicated no need to depart from the literal meaning because 'ibtina'' (building upon) includes both physical and intellectual sense.
3
Understand the two types of 'ibtina''
Physical building: upper wall on foundation, branches on trunk. Intellectual building: a ruling on its evidence. Both are instances of 'being built upon'.
The Arabic terms are explained in the lexical research.
4
Apply the idafah to fiqh as a qarina
Fiqh is an intellectual science, so the idafah (construct phrase) in 'usul al-fiqh' indicates that the 'ibtina'' is intellectual. Thus 'asl' here means 'that upon which fiqh is intellectually built'—i.e., the proofs.
The qarina avoids the need for technical transfer.
5
Conclude that the literal meaning suffices
The literal meaning 'foundation' includes the concept of proof when the perspective is intellectual. Therefore, there is no need to assume a technical meaning; the literal one covers the intended sense broadly.
This automatically repels the objection that the technical meaning is certainly intended.

3
Locate the contextual clue (qarinah)
The definition adds 'al-fiqh' (jurisprudence), which is an intellectual discipline. This qarinah indicates that the 'ibtina'' (building) is intellectual, not physical. Therefore, the literal meaning of 'asl' as foundation is restricted to intellectual foundations.
The addition (al-idafah) to 'fiqh' serves as a specifying clue.

Since the literal meaning can be intended and is specified by the qarinah, there is no need to resort to the metaphorical meaning. Thus 'asl' in the definition means 'that upon which something is built' in an intellectual sense, which is essentially the proofs (adillah).
The technical meaning is reached via the literal meaning, not as a separate term.
5

This reflects the Hanafi preference for linguistic precision.

The Two Types of Ibtina' and the Objection Refuted
Refuting the objection that ibtina' cannot be physical.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to differentiate between physical and intellectual building (ibtina') in Hanafi usul and explain the commentator's response to the objection that ibtina' is purely intellectual.

Core Idea
Ibtina' is a relation; its classification as physical or intellectual depends on the relata, not the relation itself.

In this continuation, the commentator addresses an objection: if 'ibtina'' (building upon) is a relation that exists only in the mind, how can it be divided into physical and intellectual? The response clarifies that the division is based on the nature of the two things related—if both are sensible, it is called physical building; if both are intelligible, it is intellectual. This nuanced analysis is essential for understanding how terms like 'asl' can encompass both concrete and abstract foundations.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector argues: 'ibtina'' is a relational concept (al-idafah), which has no external existence; it is purely intellectual. Therefore, dividing it into physical and intellectual is flawed because physical building would require external existence.
This is a philosophical objection based on the nature of relations.
2
Identify the commentator's first answer
The commentator responds that the division is based on the two sides (mutarafayn), i.e., the built thing (al-mabni) and the base (al-mabni 'alayhi). If both are sensible, the building is called physical; if both are intellectual, it is called intellectual.
The relation itself is intellectual, but the characterization refers to the relata.
3
Apply the first answer with examples
Example 1: Roof on walls – both are physical, so it is physical building. Example 2: Verb on its infinitive – both are audible (sensible in a broad sense), so also physical. Example 3: Ruling on proof – both are intellectual, so intellectual building.
The classification by relata is a standard Hanafi method.
4
Note the commentator's own truth (al-haqq)
The commentator adds that the ordering of a ruling on its proof is an example, not a definition, of intellectual building. True intellectual building includes metaphor on reality, particular rulings on universal rules, effects on causes, etc.
This refines the understanding: the example is for illustration, not exhaustive.
5
Synthesize the response
Thus, the division is valid and consistent with the nature of ibtina'. The objection fails because it confuses the relation itself with the attributes of the relata.
The response is a typical usul al-fiqh defense using linguistic and logical analysis.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the definition of Usul al-Fiqh, specifically analyzing the meaning of the word 'asl' (origin/foundation) in the phrase 'ma yabtani 'alayhi' (that upon which something is built). The commentator explains that some scholars held that 'asl' in this context means 'proof' (dalil) in the technical sense. However, the author (al-Tawdih) indicates that this technical meaning is not the default; the default is the literal (lughawi) meaning. The commentator argues that 'ibtina'' (building) can be either physical (like a roof on walls) or intellectual (like a ruling on its evidence). Since 'fiqh' is an intellectual discipline, the addition to fiqh shows that the building is intellectual, so 'asl' here means 'that upon which intellectual building rests', i.e., the evidence. Thus, the intended meaning is evidence, but it is derived from the literal sense, not from a separate technical usage. The commentator then addresses an objection: if the intended meaning is evidence (customary), why mention the literal meaning that includes physical building? The answer: the customary meaning is metaphorical, and metaphor is only resorted to when the literal meaning cannot be intended. Here, there is a contextual clue (qarinah) that the literal meaning (intellectual building) is intended, so metaphor is unnecessary. The passage then discusses a hypothetical objection about 'ibtina'' being a relation, thus not physical. The commentator responds that the division of 'ibtina'' into physical and intellectual is based on the nature of the two relata: if both are sensible, the building is called 'sensory'; if both are intellectual, it is 'intellectual'. The relation itself is intellectual, but the characterization is by the relata. This is a typical Hanafi usul analysis that prioritizes linguistic precision and uses contextual indicators to determine technical meanings.

Key Terms
اصل (asl): Literally: that upon which something else is built. In the definition of Usul al-Fiqh, it means the foundation (including both physical and intellectual, but here intellectually the proofs).
ابتناء (ibtina'): Building upon; relational concept that can be physical (sensible relata) or intellectual (rational relata).
حقيقت (haqiqah): Literal meaning; the primary, established meaning of a word.
مجاز (majaz): Metaphorical or transferred meaning; used only when the literal meaning is impossible or contradicted by context.

The Two Types of Imkan Aam and Their Role in Defining Jawaz
Definition and subdivision of imkan aam applied to forms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between imkan aam muqayyad bi janab al-wujud and imkan aam muqayyad bi janab al-adam, and explain why each is used in the fourth and fifth forms of ma laha wa ma alayha.

Core Idea
Jawaz means imkan, but its precise type shifts to ensure contrast with wujub or hurmah.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi technical term 'imkan' (possibility), a cornerstone of legal classification. You will learn its two subdivisions and how they serve to correctly define 'jawaz' in the context of the famous formulation 'ma laha wa ma alayha'. The passage clarifies that the meaning of jawaz varies depending on whether it is paired with obligation (wujub) or prohibition (hurmah).

Guided Path
1
Define imkan aam
Start with the basic definition: imkan aam is the negation of necessity on one side, i.e., either doing or not doing is not necessary.
This is the foundational notion of possibility.
2
Identify the two subdivisions
Imkan aam is divided into two: muqayyad bi janab al-wujud (existence may be necessary, non-existence not necessary) and muqayyad bi janab al-adam (non-existence may be necessary, existence not necessary). Classify examples like wajib, haram, and possibilities.
3
Connect jawaz to imkan
Understand that jawaz (permissibility) is equated with imkan. The specific type of imkan used determines which acts fall under 'yajuz' (it is permissible).
4
Apply to the fourth and fifth forms
In the fourth form (ma laha wa ma alayha i.e., what is permissible and what is obligatory), jawaz is taken as imkan khas (specific possibility) to exclude wajib. In the fifth form (what is permissible and what is prohibited), jawaz is taken as imkan aam muqayyad bi janab al-wujud to include wajib but exclude haram.
Contrast is the key: the chosen type must not conflate the paired categories.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Generality of 'act other than haram' is specified by context; 'prohibition' includes makruh tahrimi.

This lesson addresses two objections that may arise from the definitions in the previous lesson. The first objection concerns the fourth form: if 'act other than haram and makruh' is general and includes wajib, how can jawaz be imkan khas? The second objection deals with the fifth form: makruh tahrimi seems excluded from both 'yajuz' and 'yuhram', but the commentator shows that 'prohibition' includes makruh tahrimi because the definition is based on prohibition regardless of evidentiary strength.

2

3

5

Conclude that the Hanafi commentator resolves both objections by appealing to context (takhsis) and by carefully defining terms. The framework remains coherent and comprehensive.

The commentator said: His statement: 'It is said: Know that the Shafi'is defined fiqh as knowledge of the practical legal rulings from their detailed evidences. The explanation of that is that the object of knowledge is either a ruling or other than it. The ruling is either taken from the Sharia or not. What is taken from the Sharia either relates to the modality of an action or not. The practical is either such that knowledge of it is obtained from its detailed evidence to which the ruling is tied, or not. So knowledge encompassing all practical legal rulings obtained from their detailed evidences is fiqh. And knowledge of other than rulings – such as essences and attributes – is excluded; knowledge of rulings not taken from the Sharia, like rulings taken from reason (e.g., knowledge that the world is created), from sense (e.g., knowledge that fire burns), or from convention (e.g., knowledge that the subject is in the nominative) is excluded; knowledge of theoretical legal rulings, called creedal and foundational – such as that consensus is a proof and that faith is obligatory – is excluded; and also knowledge of Allah, of Jibril, and of the Messenger (peace be upon him) is excluded, as is the knowledge of the muqallid, because it is not obtained from detailed evidences.'

Analysis: He intends to analyze this definition and explain the benefits of its qualifications. So his statement 'the rulings' excludes knowledge of essences and attributes. His statement 'legal' excludes non-legal rulings such as knowledge that the world is created (rational), that fire burns (sensory), or that the subject is nominative (conventional). His statement 'practical' excludes theoretical creedal rulings such as that consensus is a proof and that faith is obligatory. His statement 'from their detailed evidences' excludes the knowledge of Allah, Jibril, the Messenger, and the knowledge of the muqallid, because it is not from detailed evidences.

The commentator said: His statement: 'It is possible that by ruling is meant' etc. The term 'hukm' is used customarily for attributing one thing to another, i.e., relating it by affirmation or negation. In the terminology of usul, it is the address of Allah ta'ala relating to the actions of the legally responsible persons by way of demand or option. In the terminology of logic, it is the perception that a relation exists or does not exist, and it is called tasdiq. That is not intended here because it is knowledge, and fiqh is not knowledge of the legal sciences. The verifiers hold that the second (usul meaning) is also not intended, otherwise mentioning 'legal' and 'practical' would be redundant. Rather, the intended meaning is the complete relation between two things, knowledge of which is tasdiq, and without it is tasawwur. And to this he alluded by his saying: 'It excludes conceptualizations and retains affirmations.' Thus fiqh is the affirmation of legal propositions relating to the modality of action, an affirmation obtained from the detailed evidences established in the Sharia for those propositions. The benefits of the qualifications are apparent on this estimation.

Commentary
This passage is an analysis of the Shafi'i definition of fiqh, presented by the commentator (Sharh al-Tawdih) to discuss its components and clarify its meaning. The Shafi'i definition states: 'Fiqh is knowledge of the practical legal rulings from their detailed evidences.' The commentator breaks this down by showing what each qualification excludes. First, 'al-ahkam' (the rulings) excludes knowledge of non-ruling matters such as essences and attributes. Second, 'al-shar'iyya' (legal) excludes rulings derived from reason, sense, or conventional usage. Third, 'al-'amaliyya' (practical) excludes theoretical creedal matters like consensus being a proof. Fourth, 'min adillatiha al-tafseeliyya' (from their detailed evidences) excludes direct knowledge of Allah, angels, prophets, and the knowledge of a muqallid (one who blindly follows without knowing the evidence). The commentator then discusses the term 'hukm' itself, noting its various usages in custom, usul al-fiqh, and logic. He concludes that in this definition, 'hukm' means a complete relation (nisba tamma) that yields tasdiq (affirmation), not just a concept. Thus fiqh is affirming legal propositions about actions based on detailed evidences. This analysis ensures the definition is precise and distinguishes fiqh from other types of knowledge.
