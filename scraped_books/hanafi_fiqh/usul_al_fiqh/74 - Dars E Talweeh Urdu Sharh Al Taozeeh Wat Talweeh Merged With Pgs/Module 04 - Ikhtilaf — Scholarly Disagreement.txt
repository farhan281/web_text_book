Resolving Ta'arud: Precedence of Basmalah over Hamd
Scholarly resolution of apparent contradiction between two hadiths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of ta'arud resolution (jam' and tarjih) to the conflict between tasmiyah and tahmid hadiths, and identify the role of ijma' 'amali as a proof.

Core Idea
When hadiths conflict, first reconcile by different interpretations; if needed, prefer the one with stronger proof like Qur'an and consensus.

You may have encountered teachings that both say 'begin with Bismillah' and 'begin with Alhamdulillah.' At first glance, they contradict. How do scholars resolve this? In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi approach: they reconcile by taking 'beginning' in two senses—real and customary—and then support the preference for basmalah with the Qur'anic order and the practical consensus of the entire Muslim community.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict
Recognize that two hadiths seem to instruct different ways to begin: one with basmalah, another with hamd. Acting on one seems to leave the other unapplied.
This is a classic ta'arud in Hanafi usul.
2
Attempt reconciliation (jam')
Consider interpreting 'beginning' in each hadith differently: one as real (haqiqi) beginning, the other as customary ('urfi) beginning. This removes the contradiction.
Jam' is the preferred first step over tarjih in Hanafi usul.
3
Examine the objection
The text raises a question: why not reverse the interpretations? That is, make hamd the real beginning and basmalah the customary beginning.
The objection tests the consistency of the initial reconciliation.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The author provides two proofs for the original interpretation: (1) the Qur'an places Bismillah before Alhamdulillah, (2) the entire ummah has always begun writings with Bismillah (ijma' 'amali).
These proofs strengthen one interpretation over the other, leading to tarjih.
5
Understand ijma' 'amali
Ijma' 'amali is consensus based on consistent practice, not verbal agreement. The Hanafi school accepts it as a valid source of law.
It differs from ijma' qauli required by some other schools.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between wahdat naw'iyya and wahdat shakhsiyya and apply this distinction to resolve objections regarding the i'jaz of the Quran.

Core Idea
The Quran's miracle is one in type, not in individual instance.

This lesson examines two objections raised against the claim that the miraculous way (tariq) of the Quran is singular. The responses clarify that the unity intended is of type (wahdat naw'iyya), not of individual (wahdat shakhsiyya), thereby preserving the uniqueness of the Quran's miracle while allowing for multiple levels of eloquence.

Both objections challenge the singularity of the Quran's miracle.
2

Understand that the answer restricts the discussion to human speech, excluding divine power, thus preserving the claim of i'jaz.
The term 'way' (tariq) is specified as relating to human eloquence.
3
Analyze the second objection and answer
Note that the objection references Miftah al-Ulum and Nihayat al-I'jaz. The answer introduces the key distinction between unity of type and unity of individual.
This distinction resolves the apparent contradiction.
4
Comprehend wahdat naw'iyya vs. shakhsiyya
Define both terms: naw'iyya means multiple instances share the same essential characteristic; shakhsiyya means a single unique instance.
The Quran's miracle is one in type, meaning all its aspects are part of a single kind of miracle.
5
Apply the distinction

Defending the Comprehensiveness of Asl: The Case of Fikr
Responding to an objection about fikr not being an asl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi response that ibtina' aqli is broad enough to include fikr as a legitimate asl, and evaluate why the objection fails.

Core Idea
Ibtina' aqli is not limited to rulings on evidence; it encompasses any rational dependence, including thought on its material.

In usul al-fiqh, the term 'asl' (foundation) is defined as 'that upon which something else is built.' An objector argues that fikr (thought) does not satisfy this definition because the relationship is neither sensory nor rational in the narrow sense of a ruling depending on evidence. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi response: ibtina' aqli is broader than the example used, and fikr does indeed have a rational foundation on its material (known matters).

Guided Path
1
Identify the objector's claim
The objector says that fikr (arrangement of known matters) does not have ibtina' (sensory or rational) upon known matters, so the definition of asl is not comprehensive.
The objector assumes ibtina' aqli is only the ordering of a ruling on its evidence.
2
Understand the two types of ibtina'
Recall the earlier distinction: ibtina' hissi (sensory) is physical superposition; ibtina' aqli (rational) is any dependency recognized by reason.
The objector limits ibtina' aqli to the author's illustrative example.
3
Examine the author's response
The author clarifies that the example (ruling on evidence) was not a definition but an illustration. Ibtina' aqli is a broad concept that includes the rational dependence of thought on its material (known matters).
Thus fikr does have an asl, and the definition remains comprehensive.
4
Apply to the definition of asl
The definition 'ma yabtani 'alayhi ghayruh' remains jami' (comprehensive) because ibtina' aqli covers cases like fikr, not just rulings.
The thamarah al-khilaf is the scope of asl in usul al-fiqh.
5
Evaluate the strength of the response
The response preserves the original definition without amendment by expanding the understanding of ibtina'. It relies on previously established principles.
The objection is invalid; the Hanafi position stands.

The Ash'ari Definition of Fiqh: Why It Would Include Ethics — A Hanafi Refutation
Refuting the Ash'ari position by highlighting an absurd consequence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi critique of the Ash'ari definition of fiqh and explain why including ethical knowledge in fiqh is an unacceptable consequence.

Core Idea
If all rulings are shar'i, ethics becomes fiqh — the Hanafi restriction to practical rulings saves the boundary.

This passage presents a critical refinement by a Hanafi commentator against the Ash'ari position that all rulings are entirely dependent on revelation. The commentator argues that if the Ash'ari definition of fiqh (knowledge of all actions supported by evidence) is accepted, then knowledge of ethical qualities like the goodness of generosity would become part of fiqh. This is problematic because such knowledge traditionally belongs to ethics (akhlaq), not fiqh. The Hanafi position distinguishes between shar'i rulings (known only through revelation) and rational rulings (known independently by reason), confining fiqh to the former type that are practical.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions of fiqh at stake
The Hanafi definition restricts fiqh to 'knowledge of practical shar'i rulings derived from detailed evidence.' The Ash'ari definition potentially includes any action supported by evidence, since they hold all rulings are shar'i.
The key difference: Hanafis add the condition 'practical' (amaliyya) to exclude ethics.
2
Understand the Ash'ari doctrine on reason and revelation
Ash'aris deny reason's capacity to know good and evil independently. Thus, the goodness of generosity and badness of miserliness would only be known through revelation, making them shar'i rulings.
Hanafis and Mu'tazila accept rational good and evil as independent sources.
3
Apply the Ash'ari definition to a concrete example
Take the goodness of generosity (husn al-jawd). According to Ash'aris, this is known only because the lawgiver addressed it. Their definition of fiqh would then include 'knowledge that generosity is good' as a ruling supported by evidence.
This leads to the absurdity that knowledge of generosity's goodness becomes a matter of fiqh.
4
Recognize the Hanafi objection: conflation with ethics
The commentator notes that such knowledge is unanimously classified under 'ilm al-akhlaq (ethics), not fiqh. The matn author himself had earlier separated ethics and fiqh.
The Ash'ari position would blur the boundary between fiqh and ethics, contradicting established consensus.
5
Grasp the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Under the Hanafi view, rulings known rationally (e.g., the praiseworthiness of generosity) are not part of fiqh; they remain ethical judgments. Under the Ash'ari view, they become fiqh, expanding the subject matter incorrectly.
This shows the importance of correctly defining the scope of fiqh.

Refining Definitions: The Dispute Over Razi's Qualifier in Fiqh
Responding to an objection against a qualifier in defining fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain and evaluate the dispute between Sadr al-Shariah and Taftazani regarding Imam Razi's qualifier in the definition of fiqh.

Core Idea
A qualifier can be explanatory (tawḍīḥī), not restrictive (iḥtirāzī) — knowing the intent prevents futile objections.

In the study of uṣūl al-fiqh, precise definitions are essential. When Imam al-Rāzī added a qualifier excluding self-evident matters from the definition of fiqh, some objected, thinking it unnecessary. This lesson unpacks the nuanced dispute between Sadr al-Sharīʿah and Taftazānī, revealing how a misunderstanding of a qualifier's function can lead to baseless criticism. You will learn to distinguish between restrictive and explanatory qualifiers and apply this insight to legal definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contested Qualifier
Find the specific phrase added by Imam Rāzī in the definition of fiqh: 'التي لا يعلم كونها من ضروريات الدين' (which are not known to be from the necessities of religion). Understand its context in al-Maḥṣūl.
The qualifier aims to exclude self-evident matters like the obligation of prayer.
2
Understand Sadr al-Sharīʿah's Objection
Grasp the matn's critique: the qualifier is futile because fiqh requires a faculty (malakah) of ijtihad, not mere knowledge of a few issues. Even someone who knows many rulings is not a faqīh without this faculty. So excluding self-evident matters is unnecessary.
Sadr al-Sharīʿah objects based on the nature of fiqh as a malakah.
3
Analyze Taftazānī's Defense
Taftazānī reframes the qualifier as explanatory (tawḍīḥī), not restrictive. He argues Rāzī's intent is to clarify that fiqh deals only with theoretical rulings, not to exclude anything that would otherwise be included.
This is a key methodological distinction: qualifiers can be for clarification, not restriction.
4
Evaluate the Thamarah al-Khilāf
The practical consequence is a more precise definition: fiqh is knowledge of non-self-evident Sharīʿah rulings derived through ijtihad. The qualifier prevents confusion with self-evident knowledge.
Both sides ultimately agree that self-evident knowledge is not fiqh; the dispute is only about the need for explicit exclusion.
5
Determine the Tarjīḥ
The text (Taftazānī) favors Rāzī's qualifier as valid and useful for clarity. Thus, the objection is unsuccessful. The accepted definition includes the qualifier for precision.
Tarjīḥ is based on the principle that explanatory additions are permissible and beneficial.

Objection: Definition of Fiqh Fails for Companions' Era
Objection to the definition of fiqh due to its failure to cover Companions' understanding.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the flaw in a definition of fiqh that includes consensus as a necessary component, and explain why such a definition fails to encompass the fiqh of the Companions.

Core Idea
Fiqh definitions must apply to all eras; requiring consensus excludes the Prophet’s time.

This passage presents a second objection to a proposed definition of fiqh. The definition includes both revealed rulings and those upon which consensus has been formed. However, during the lifetime of the Prophet (ﷺ), consensus did not exist because revelation was ongoing. This means the definition would not cover the fiqh of the Companions, who were the first to understand the Shari'ah. The commentator argues that to salvage the definition, one would have to make it conditional on the existence of consensus, which is an unacceptable inconsistency.

Definitions in usul must be comprehensive, consistent, and cover all valid instances. This objection teaches us to carefully define key terms to avoid excluding legitimate cases.

The Certainty of the Mujtahid's Conjecture

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on whether a mujtahid's zann can be considered qat'i and evaluate the two main approaches in resolving the apparent contradiction.

Core Idea
A mujtahid's conjecture can become certain in obligation even if the ruling itself remains conjectural in reality.

This passage tackles a fundamental epistemological question in Hanafi usul: how can a mujtahid's conjecture (zann) be regarded as certain knowledge (qat'i)? The objection is straightforward: zann admits the possibility of error, while certainty does not. The commentator presents two resolutions – one based on the maxim 'every mujtahid is correct' and another based on 'only one is correct' – and subjects both to critical scrutiny. The discussion reveals the Hanafi subtlety that certainty can attach to the obligation to act, even if the ruling itself remains conjectural in Allah's knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
Identify the contradiction: maẓnūn admits its opposite, ma'lūm does not, so how can a maẓnūn ruling be ma'lūm?
The objection is foundational: definitions seem to preclude any overlap.

3

The certainty shifts from reality to duty.

First critique: adopting the first view redefines fiqh as knowledge of obligation, not knowledge of rulings. Second critique: on the second view, a ruling established by zannī evidence cannot be truly qat'ī because qat'ī by definition does not admit non-existence.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul dispute concerning the epistemological status of a mujtahid's conjecture (zann). The commentator (sharih) begins by presenting a potential objection: al-maẓnūn is that which admits the possibility of its opposite, while al-ma'lūm is that which does not, so they are contradictory. How then can the mujtahid's conjecture be called certain knowledge? The reply is given in two scenarios. First, on the view that every mujtahid is correct (kull mujtahid muṣīb), the conjecture becomes certain through a qiyās: this ruling is known to be maẓnūn, and every ruling known to be maẓnūn for the mujtahid is certain in reality (thābit fī nafsi al-amr). So the minor premise and major premise yield the conclusion that the ruling is certain. Second, on the view that only one mujtahid is correct (al-muṣīb wāḥid), the conjecture becomes qaṭ'ī in terms of obligation to act (wājib al-'amal) or establishment by way of evidence (thābit bi-n-nazar ilā d-dalīl). Then the commentator critiques both: the first reduces fiqh to knowledge of obligation rather than knowledge of rulings; the second makes a ruling qaṭ'ī even though established by a zannī evidence, which is contradictory because qaṭ'ī by definition does not admit the possibility of non-existence. Finally, the commentator mentions a solution from some muhaqqiqūn in Sharḥ al-Minhāj: the ruling must be acted upon with certainty due to a qāṭi' evidence, and this leads to the ruling being known with certainty. However, the commentator objects: the claim that every ruling that must be acted upon with certainty is known to be Allah's ruling is exactly the point of dispute; one might act with certainty on a conjecture without it being certain in reality. The passage thus illustrates the nuanced Hanafi position that the mujtahid's zann has a kind of certainty in terms of obligation, while acknowledging the logical tension.

The Flaw in the Qiyas: Using a Disputed Premise as Proof
Critique of a qiyas on certainty of speculative rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of using a disputed premise as proof in qiyas and explain why speculative rulings remain zanni according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A disputed premise cannot serve as proof; acting upon a ruling does not make it certain.

This passage from a scholarly commentary critiques a qiyas presented by those who hold that every mujtahid is correct. They attempt to argue that speculative rulings derived by a mujtahid are certain because they must be acted upon with certainty. The Hanafi response exposes a fundamental usul principle: a premise that is itself the subject of controversy cannot be used as a premise in a proof. Furthermore, the obligation to act does not transform speculative knowledge into certainty.

The minor premise of the first qiyas is 'Every ruling that must be acted upon with certainty is known with certainty to be Allah's ruling.' This premise is precisely what the Hanafis reject. They accept the obligation to act but deny the certainty of knowledge.
The opponent assumes that the obligation implies certainty, but this is not granted.
3
Explain the Hanafi Critique
The Hanafi critique demonstrates that using a disputed premise as a proof is methodologically invalid. The opponent cannot build a proof on something that is itself in question. Moreover, the consequence of accepting the premise would make the rest of the argument circular or meaningless.
The text states: 'Using a disputed matter as a proof is invalid.'
4

If one were to assume that the mujtahid's speculative ruling is definitely Allah's ruling (the view of those who say every mujtahid is correct), then the entire qiyas becomes unnecessary. The certainty is already assumed, and the obligation to act adds nothing.

The Hanafi position is that speculative rulings remain zanni. They are obligatory to act upon due to the zann, but they do not become certain. Ijtihad can err, and the mujtahid's ruling is not necessarily the actual divine ruling.

Similarly, the subject of medicine is foods, medicines, humors, and organs—all share in an accident because they are all related to health, which is the purpose of medicine, and purpose is accidental. Therefore, without proportion, they would not accept multiplicity of subjects. For example, if someone says that fiqh and geometry are one science because their subject is the act of the legally responsible person and quantity, it would be wrong because the absolute act and quantity have no proportion. Finally, he says: the examples the author gave are against him. For instance, the subject of usul al-fiqh is not the general concept of proof but the individuals of proof, i.e., the Book, Sunnah, Consensus, and Qiyas. Then usul al-fiqh should not be called one science because its subject is four things. Similarly, the subject of logic is not the concept of conception and assent but their individuals: genus, species, differentia, syllogism, induction, and example. Then it would be wrong to count logic as one science.

Commentary
This passage is a refutation of an earlier claim in the matn (al-Tawdih) that multiplicity of subjects prevents a science from being one. The commentator argues that mere multiplicity does not destroy unity; the key is proportion (munasaba). The jumhur (majority of scholars) require proportionality among the subjects. He gives examples from geometry (line, surface, body all share in quantity) and medicine (body parts, foods, etc. all relate to health). If subjects are proportional, they can form one science. The commentator also points out a contradiction in the matn's examples: usul al-fiqh has multiple subjects (Book, Sunnah, etc.) but is considered one science. Similarly, logic has multiple subjects (genus, species, etc.) but is one science. Therefore, the matn's argument is inconsistent. The lesson emphasizes that unity of a science does not require a single subject; rather, subjects can be multiple if they share a common aspect (essential or accidental). This is a typical Hanafi usul al-fiqh discussion on the classification of sciences.

Key Terms
تناسب (munasaba): Proportionality or correlation among subjects of a science, either in essence (dhati) or accident (aradi), which makes them suitable to be studied under one science.
ذاتي (dhati): Essential attribute shared among subjects, e.g., genus (jins). In the passage, geometry subjects share the essential attribute of quantity.
عرضي (aradi): Accidental attribute shared among subjects, e.g., relation to a common goal. In the passage, medicine subjects share the accidental attribute of relation to health.
