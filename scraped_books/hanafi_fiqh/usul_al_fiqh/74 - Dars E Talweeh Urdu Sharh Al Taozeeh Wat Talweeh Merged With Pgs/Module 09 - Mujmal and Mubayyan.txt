The Comprehensive Meaning of Hamd in Sharia
Explaining that hamd includes belief, speech, and action

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain that hamd of Allah in the Sharia encompasses belief (i'tiqad), speech (qawl), and action (amal), and identify the Quranic evidence that supports this comprehensive meaning.

Core Idea
Hamd of Allah is not mere speech — it requires correct belief as its root and righteous deeds as its branches.

In the opening of many Islamic texts, the author begins with 'al-hamdu lillah'. But what does true praise of Allah entail? In this passage from Dars-e-Talweeh, the commentator expands the linguistic definition of hamd to include not just the tongue but the heart and limbs, drawing on Imam Razi's Tafsir and the Quranic verse from Surah Fatir. Understanding this comprehensive definition is essential for proper usul al-fiqh analysis of Quranic texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's claim
The commentator clarifies that while hamd linguistically is an act of the tongue, in the Sharia it includes three dimensions: belief (i'tiqad), speech (qawl), and action (amal).
This is the key expansion from the linguistic to the sharia meaning.
2
Analyze the metaphor
The commentator uses a tree metaphor: belief is the root (asl), righteous deeds are the branches (far'). Without belief, hamd is like a rotten tree with no stability; without deeds, it is like a tree without fruit.
This metaphor illustrates the necessity of both components.
3
Extract the Quranic evidence
The verse from Surah Fatir (35:10) is cited: 'To Him ascend the good words, and the righteous deed raises it.' The commentator notes the grammatical features: fronting of 'ilayhi' indicates exclusivity (only to Allah), and the present tense 'yas'adu' indicates continuity.
The evidence shows that hamd (good words) ascends only with righteous deeds.
4
Synthesize the definition
Combine the three components and the evidence into a complete definition: Hamd of Allah is praise that originates from correct belief, is expressed verbally, and is accompanied by righteous deeds that raise it to Allah.
This is the operative definition for further usul analysis.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) from Dars-e-Talweeh explaining the nature of praise (hamd) in the context of the author's opening words. The commentator begins by addressing a linguistic point about rare masadir, then dives into the core discussion: what constitutes true praise of Allah. He clarifies that although hamd linguistically is only an act of the tongue, in the sharia and in the Quranic context, it encompasses three dimensions: belief in the heart (i'tiqad), expression by the tongue (qawl), and action by the limbs (amal). This understanding is attributed to Imam Razi. The commentary uses the metaphor of a tree: belief is the root (asl) without which hamd is unstable and unacceptable, like a rotten tree; righteous deeds are the branches (far') without which the tree is barren. Both are essential. The evidence cited is Quran 35:10 ('To Him ascend the good words, and the righteous deed raises it') and a hadith indicating that without righteous deeds, hamd is not accepted. The commentary further explains the grammatical points of the verse: the fronting of 'ilayhi' indicates exclusivity (ikhtisas) that only Allah receives the ascending words, and the present tense verb yas'adu indicates continuity (dawam). Finally, the commentator notes that the author's connection of praises to some blessings highlights the importance of knowledge, particularly fiqh and usul al-din, which are the subjects of this book.

Defining 'Asl' with Perspective Restriction in Hanafi Usul
Commentary on defining idafah compounds and the relative definition of 'asl'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'asl' (principle) with its perspective restriction (qayd al-haythiyya) and explain why the matn author omitted defining 'idafah' separately.

Core Idea
Asl is relative — it is the basis for something else only from a specific perspective, not absolutely.

In this passage, the commentator (sharh) unpacks the matn author’s method for defining idafah compounds. He clarifies why the matn author defined mudaf and mudaf ilayh separately but left idafah itself undefined — because its meaning is self-evident. The passage then tackles a deeper challenge: if the proofs of fiqh (Quran, Sunnah, etc.) are themselves based on 'ilm al-tawhid, how can they be called 'asl' (principles) of fiqh? The answer introduces a critical nuance: the term 'asl' is relative and requires a perspective restriction. This lesson will equip you to define 'asl' correctly and understand how relative terms are handled in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Understand the commentary’s structure
The passage is a sharh on a matn definition. Identify the matn claim: mudaf and mudaf ilayh are defined separately. Then the commentator provides the reasoning (compounds require clarifying parts) and notes the omission of defining idafah.
2
Grasp the objection
The objection: if proofs of fiqh are themselves based on 'ilm al-tawhid, they cannot be asl. Understand why this is a problem — it would make the term asl contradictory.
3
Analyze the response
The response distinguishes perspectives: they are asl relative to fiqh, not absolutely. This resolves the contradiction.
The commentator uses 'bi-ḥasab al-iʿtibār' (consideration of perspective).
4
Extract the definition of asl with the perspective restriction
The commentator proposes: 'mā yabtanī ʿalayhi ghayruhu min ḥaythu yabtanī ʿalayhi ghayruhu' (that upon which something else is built, from the perspective of it being built upon it).
This is the core definition to memorize.
5
Apply the principle
Recognize that in relative matters like asl and farʿ, the perspective restriction is crucial to avoid confusion. For example, the same text can be an asl for fiqh but a farʿ for tawhid.

Commentary: The matn author said that when making a definition of an idafah (genitive construction), we will define each of the mudaf and mudaf ilayh separately. Here the commentator (sharh) explains the reason: because idafah involves a compound. Therefore, to understand every compound, it is necessary to clarify every unclear and ambiguous part of it. Here, even though the matn author defined the mudaf and mudaf ilayh, he should have also defined the idafah itself, because idafah for the compound is like a formal part. The gist of the answer is that they are called usul in relation to fiqh, even though they themselves are based on 'ilm al-tawhid and are considered branches (furu') in that regard, but that is from another perspective. Because in such relative matters, adding the restriction of perspective is necessary, but sometimes due to notoriety it is omitted.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the distinction between real and conventional essences and the two types of definition: real and nominal. The author first defines a real essence (mahiyyah haqiqiyyah) as something that has existence in reality (nafs al-amr), such as the essence of a human being, which exists externally. These essences have both essential properties (dhatiyyat) and accidental properties ('aradiyyat). A conventional essence (mahiyyah i'tibariyyah) is something whose existence depends on the consideration of a positor or a conceptual assumption, like genus, species, jurisprudence, or the root (asl). These have no real essences but only accidents. The commentary then explains that when a positor places a name, he has a conception (mutta'allaq) in mind. If that conception corresponds to a real essence, the definition that clarifies it by stating its essential or accidental properties is a real definition (ta'rif haqiqi). If the conception does not have external existence, or if we consider it merely as a concept, the definition is nominal (ta'rif ismi). Nominal definitions simply clarify the meaning of a name by using a more famous word (like 'al-ghadanfar is the lion') or by providing a detailed description (like 'asl is that upon which something else is built'). Non-existents (ma'dumat) can only have nominal definitions because they have no reality. Existents can have both types depending on whether we consider their real essence or their mere concept. The commentator then addresses an objection: the apparent wording might suggest that real essences are always defined by real definitions and conventional essences always by nominal definitions. He clarifies that a real essence can be considered from two perspectives: as a reality in itself (then its definition is real) or as a concept in the positor's mind (then its definition is nominal). Similarly, a conventional essence is always defined nominally because it has no reality. The nominal and real definitions may even coincide when the positor's conception is identical to the real essence, but the type of definition depends on whether we have knowledge of the existence of the thing: before knowing existence, it is nominal; after knowing existence, it becomes real. This nuanced analysis is important in Hanafi usul for understanding how terms are defined in legal texts.

ماہیت اعتباری (mahiyyah i'tibariyyah): An essence whose existence depends on the consideration and assumption of a positor, like terminological concepts (jins, naw', fiqh, asl).
تعریف حقیقی (ta'rif haqiqi): A definition that explains the real essence of a thing by its essential or accidental properties, answering the question seeking reality.
تعریف اسمی (ta'rif ismi): A definition that clarifies the concept of a name without describing the reality, often using a more famous word or a detailed paraphrase.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the distinction between real and conventional essences and the two types of definition: real and nominal. The author first defines a real essence (mahiyyah haqiqiyyah) as something that has existence in reality (nafs al-amr), such as the essence of a human being, which exists externally. These essences have both essential properties (dhatiyyat) and accidental properties ('aradiyyat). A conventional essence (mahiyyah i'tibariyyah) is something whose existence depends on the consideration of a positor or a conceptual assumption, like genus, species, jurisprudence, or the root (asl). These have no real essences but only accidents. The commentary then explains that when a positor places a name, he has a conception (mutta'allaq) in mind. If that conception corresponds to a real essence, the definition that clarifies it by stating its essential or accidental properties is a real definition (ta'rif haqiqi). If the conception does not have external existence, or if we consider it merely as a concept, the definition is nominal (ta'rif ismi). Nominal definitions simply clarify the meaning of a name by using a more famous word (like 'al-ghadanfar is the lion') or by providing a detailed description (like 'asl is that upon which something else is built'). Non-existents (ma'dumat) can only have nominal definitions because they have no reality. Existents can have both types depending on whether we consider their real essence or their mere concept. The commentator then addresses an objection: the apparent wording might suggest that real essences are always defined by real definitions and conventional essences always by nominal definitions. He clarifies that a real essence can be considered from two perspectives: as a reality in itself (then its definition is real) or as a concept in the positor's mind (then its definition is nominal). Similarly, a conventional essence is always defined nominally because it has no reality. The nominal and real definitions may even coincide when the positor's conception is identical to the real essence, but the type of definition depends on whether we have knowledge of the existence of the thing: before knowing existence, it is nominal; after knowing existence, it becomes real. This nuanced analysis is important in Hanafi usul for understanding how terms are defined in legal texts.

Key Terms
ماہیت حقیقی (mahiyyah haqiqiyyah): An essence that has existence in reality (nafs al-amr) and actuality, independent of any consideration.
ماہیت اعتباری (mahiyyah i'tibariyyah): An essence whose existence depends on the consideration and assumption of a positor, like terminological concepts (jins, naw', fiqh, asl).
تعریف حقیقی (ta'rif haqiqi): A definition that explains the real essence of a thing by its essential or accidental properties, answering the question seeking reality.
تعریف اسمی (ta'rif ismi): A definition that clarifies the concept of a name without describing the reality, often using a more famous word or a detailed paraphrase.

Resolving Apparent Contradictions in a Scholar's Usage
Objection that matn's own usage contradicts his criticism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and resolve an apparent contradiction in a scholar's usage of a technical term across different contexts, distinguishing between contextual explanation and technical definition.

Core Idea
Contextual explanations do not override technical definitions; consistency is maintained by recognizing pedagogical purpose.

This lesson addresses a common objection in usul: that a scholar's own words seem to contradict his critical stance. The objection here is that the matn of al-Tawdih and al-Talwih defined 'asl' as 'muhtaj ilayh' in Bab al-Majaz, yet he objected to Imam Razi for the same definition. We will learn how to resolve such apparent contradictions by distinguishing between contextual explanations and absolute technical definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent contradiction
Note that the matn defined 'asl' as 'muhtaj ilayh' in Bab al-Majaz but criticized Imam Razi for using that definition.
2
Understand the objector's claim
The objector claims that the matn is inconsistent and therefore his objection to Imam Razi is unsound.
3
Examine the context of the matn's usage
In Bab al-Majaz, the matn was explaining the difference between haqiqah and majaz, so he used a simplified, contextual definition for clarity.
4
Apply the distinction
Recognize that a technical definition in usul is broader and more precise, while a contextual explanation serves a specific pedagogical purpose.
5
Conclude no contradiction
The matn's usage in Bab al-Majaz does not represent his absolute technical definition; therefore, his criticism of Imam Razi stands on its own merits.

Refuting Four Meanings of 'al-Ahkam' in the Definition of Fiqh
Refuting four possible interpretations of 'al-ahkam' in the definition of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four possible interpretations of 'al-ahkam' in the definition of fiqh and apply the Hanafi argument to refute each.

Core Idea
Al-ahkam cannot mean all rulings, each ruling, a fraction, or any part—four refutations clarify the scope of fiqh.

In the definition of fiqh, the term 'al-ahkam' appears ambiguous. The Hanafi school objects to the Shafi'i definition by arguing that this term cannot be taken literally in any of its four possible senses. This lesson unpacks each possibility and shows why it fails, strengthening your ability to critically analyze legal definitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Four Possibilities
Recall the four possible meanings of 'al-ahkam' as presented in the passage: all rulings, each rulings, a specified fraction (e.g., half), or any unspecified part. Write them down.
The passage lists these explicitly as the four divisions to be refuted.
2
Understand the First Refutation
The first possibility (all rulings) is impossible because rulings are nearly endless and no human can know them all individually or as a detailed whole. This is supported by the statement 'they are almost endless'.
This refutation relies on the epistemological limitation of humans.
3
Understand the Second Refutation
The second possibility (each ruling) is invalid because even acknowledged fuqaha do not know every ruling. The example of Imam Malik being asked forty issues and saying 'I do not know' for thirty-six proves this.
This shows that knowing every ruling is not a requirement for being a faqih.
4
Understand the Third and Fourth Refutations
The third possibility (a specified fraction) fails because the whole (all rulings) is unknown in quantity, so its fractions are also unknown. The fourth possibility (any part) fails because it would allow a person knowing just one or two rulings to be called a faqih, which is not the case terminologically.
The third refutation is logical; the fourth is definitional.
5
Synthesize the Conclusion
All four interpretations are invalid. Therefore, the term 'al-ahkam' in the definition of fiqh must have a different intended meaning, or the definition itself needs revision. Hanafi usul thus refines the understanding of fiqh.
This analysis is a classic example of the precision required in usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of the word 'al-ahkam' in the definition of fiqh. The matn objects to the Shafi'i definition by arguing that the term 'al-ahkam' cannot refer to (1) all rulings, (2) each ruling, (3) a specific fraction, or (4) any unspecified part. The Haythiyya (Aspect) in Subject Definitions
Explaining the two types of haythiyya in the subject of a science

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of haythiyya in the definition of a science's subject and apply this distinction to identify whether a given haythiyya is part of the subject or an indicator of the accidents studied.

Core Idea
A haythiyya can be part of the subject or a pointer to the studied accidents – never both.

When defining a science, we often say it studies X from a certain aspect (haythiyya). But have you ever wondered what that aspect actually does? This lesson unpacks the two roles a haythiyya can play: one that is inseparable from the subject itself, and another that tells you what properties you'll investigate. Understanding this distinction is key to properly defining any Islamic science, including usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the role of haythiyya in a definition
Look at how the science is defined. The phrase 'from the aspect of X' is the haythiyya. Determine whether X is a property that the science investigates (like health in medicine) or a fundamental characteristic of the subject itself (like existence in metaphysics).
2
Distinguish type 1: haythiyya as part of subject
In sciences like metaphysics, the haythiyya 'existent qua existent' is part of the subject. The science does not investigate existence itself but the attributes that necessarily follow from existence (e.g., causality, necessity). The haythiyya here is not an accident studied but a condition of the subject.
The author (al-Tawdih) holds this type.
3
Distinguish type 2: haythiyya as indicator of accidents
In sciences like medicine, the haythiyya 'body qua healthy/ill' directly points to the accidents studied: health and illness. Here the haythiyya is not part of the subject but a description of the essential properties the science investigates.
This type makes the haythiyya a predicate, not a part of the subject.
4
Apply to a real science definition
Consider usul al-fiqh: defined as 'the knowledge of the proofs of fiqh from the aspect that they are proofs'. The haythiyya here is 'from the aspect that they are proofs'. Does it belong to type 1 or 2? The proofs are the subject, but we do not study the proofs' existence; we study their evidentiary force and classification. So it is type 1: the haythiyya is part of the subject's definition, but the accidents studied are the properties of proofs (e.g., general, specific, abrogating).
This shows the usefulness of the distinction.

Commentary
This passage clarifies the concept of 'haythiyya' (aspect) in the definition of a science's subject. The commentator distinguishes two types: (1) The haythiyya is part of the subject but not investigated as an accident (e.g., 'existent qua existent' in metaphysics—we do not investigate existence itself but attributes of existence). (2) The haythiyya is a description that indicates the essential accidents studied (e.g., 'body qua healthy/ill' in medicine—health and illness are the very accidents studied). Al-Tawdih (the matn) argues that in the first type, the haythiyya is part of the subject, so it cannot be a predicate; in the second, it is a statement of the accidents studied. This distinction helps correctly define a science's subject without circularity. The lesson is foundational for understanding how Islamic sciences (especially usul al-fiqh) define their subjects.

Key Terms
حيثية (haythiyya): The aspect or consideration from which a subject is studied; it may be part of the subject (like 'existent qua existent') or a descriptor of the accidents studied (like 'body qua healthy/ill').
