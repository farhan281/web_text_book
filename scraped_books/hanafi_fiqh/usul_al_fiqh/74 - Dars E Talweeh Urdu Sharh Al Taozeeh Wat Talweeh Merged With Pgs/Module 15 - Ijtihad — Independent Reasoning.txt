Shafi'i Fiqh: Knowledge Through Ijtihad
Explains that fiqh is limited to knowledge through ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Shafi'i definition of fiqh, explain why daruriyyat al-din are excluded, and compare it with the Hanafi understanding.

Core Idea
Fiqh is knowledge through ijtihad; what is necessarily known is not technically fiqh.

This passage from a Hanafi commentary explains the Shafi'i technical definition of fiqh. The Shafi'is restrict the term 'fiqh' to rulings obtained through inference (ijtihad), excluding matters known necessarily, like the obligation of prayer. The commentator defends this condition against objections, clarifying its importance. Understanding this distinction sharpens our analysis of how different schools define the scope of fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Find the precise Shafi'i definition of fiqh as presented: knowledge of shar'i rulings obtained through nazar and istidlal (reflection and inference).
2
Recognize the exclusion
Note that daruriyyat al-din (necessities of religion) like the obligation of prayer are explicitly excluded from this technical definition.
3
Understand the condition's purpose
The condition of acquisition (iktisab) ensures that only knowledge requiring effort is called fiqh; otherwise, anyone knowing a single necessary ruling would be a faqih.
4
Analyze the commentator's defense
The commentator argues that even knowing many rare inferential rulings does not automatically make one a faqih; the key is the method of acquisition.
5
Compare with Hanafi terminology
Hanafis also require ijtihad for fiqh but have a broader definition that includes necessary knowledge when derived; the difference is largely terminological.

لا يعد من
قال الشارح : قوله لا يزاد عليه المصطلح بين الشافعية أن العلم بالأحكام إنما يسمى فقها إذا كان حصوله بطريق النظر والاستدلال ، حتى أن العلم بوجوب الصلوة والصوم ، ونحو ذالك مما اشتهر كونه من الدين بالضرورة بحيث يعلمه المتدين وغيره - الفقه اصطلاحا ، ولهذا يذكرون قيد الاكتساب أو الاستدلال والإمام قيد في المحصول الأحكام بالتي لا يعلم كونها من الدين بالضرورة وقال: هو احتراز عن العلم بوجوب الصلاة والصوم أنه لا يسمى فقها معنى أنه لا يدخل في مسمى الفقه ولا يعد عنه على ما صرح بها الإمام فى قيد العملية، لا بمعنى أنه لم يحترز عنه لزم أن يكون العالم بمجرد وجوبهما فقها على ما فهمه الصنف، فاعترض بمنع لزوم ذلك بناء على أن الفقيه من له الفقه . والفقه ليس علما ببعض الأحكام . وإن قيل حتى يكون العالم بمسئلة أو مسئلتين فقيها بل العالم بمائة مسئلة غريبة استدلالية وحدها لا يسمى فقها ثم إذا كان اصطلاحهم على أن الـعـلـم بضروريات الدين ليس من الفقه ، فلا بد من إخراجها عن تعريفهم للفقه ، فلا يكون القيد المخرج لها ضائعا ، ولا القول : بكونها من الفقه صحيحا عندهم ، ولا اصطلاح على ذالك صالحا للاعتراض عليهم .

تشریح
It is not counted from

The commentator said: His statement 'not added'—the terminology among the Shafi'is is that knowledge of rulings is only called fiqh if it is obtained through the method of reflection and inference, so much so that knowledge of the obligation of prayer and fasting, and similar matters that are well-known as necessarily belonging to religion, such that both the religious and others know them, is not fiqh in terminology. That is why they mention the condition of acquisition or inference. Al-Imam (al-Ghazali?) restricted in al-Mahsul the rulings to those which are not known necessarily as part of religion, and he said: This is a precaution against knowledge of the obligation of prayer and fasting that it is not called fiqh, meaning it does not enter into the name of fiqh and is not counted from it, as the Imam explicitly stated regarding the condition of practicality. It is not that if he had not taken precaution, it would necessarily follow that the person who knows merely their obligation would be a faqih, as the author understood. He opposed by denying the necessity of that, based on the fact that the faqih is one who has fiqh, and fiqh is not knowledge of some rulings. Even if it were said that a person who knows one or two issues would be a faqih, rather a person who knows a hundred rare inferential issues alone is not called a faqih. Then, if their terminology is that knowledge of the necessarys of religion is not from fiqh, then it is necessary to exclude these from their definition of fiqh. So the condition that excludes them is not wasted, nor is the statement that they are part of fiqh correct according to them, nor is such terminology valid for objecting against them.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion by the commentator on the Shafi'i technical definition of fiqh. The Shafi'is hold that fiqh is only knowledge of shar'i rulings obtained through ijtihad and inference (ijtihad), and not knowledge that is necessarily known (daruri) like the obligation of prayer. The commentator defends this condition against a possible objection that such necessary knowledge might be excluded wrongly. He argues that even if someone knows many rare rulings through inference, he is still not necessarily a faqih unless he has the comprehensive ability. The key point is that the condition of ijtihad is not useless; it properly excludes necessary knowledge from the technical definition of fiqh. This distinction is important in Hanafi usul as well, though the Hanafis have a different terminology (they include necessary knowledge if it is derived from evidence? Actually the Hanafis also consider fiqh as knowledge through ijtihad, but they have a broader definition. The passage shows the Shafi'i view for comparison.

Key Terms
الاكتساب (al-iktisab): Acquisition of knowledge through effort and inference.
ضروريات الدين (daruriyyat al-din): Matters known necessarily as part of the religion, such as the obligation of prayer.

Key Takeaway
In Shafi'i technical usage, fiqh is only knowledge derived through ijtihad; matters necessarily known are excluded.
