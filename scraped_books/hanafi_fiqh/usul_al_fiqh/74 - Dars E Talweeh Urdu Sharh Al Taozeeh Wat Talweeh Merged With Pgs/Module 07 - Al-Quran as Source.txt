Using Linguistic Categories to Interpret Quranic Words: al-Kalim and al-Su'ud
Lexical analysis of Quranic terms with noun classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify Arabic nouns (ism jins, ism jam‘, jam‘) and apply these categories to interpret Quranic vocabulary according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A word's grammatical category determines its scope—never assume a plural without checking its singular.

This passage from the commentary on al-Talwih performs a precise lexical analysis of two Quranic terms: 'yas‘ad' (ascends) and 'al-kalim' (words). The commentator clarifies that 'kalim' is a genus noun, not a true plural, and distinguishes three fundamental noun categories. Understanding these categories is essential for any Hanafi usuli who wishes to derive rulings from the Quran accurately.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word and its root
Begin by isolating the word in question. For 'al-kalim', note that it is derived from 'kalimah'. For 'yas‘ad', note the root 'ص-ع-د'.
The root gives initial semantic clues.
2
Classify the noun type
Apply the three-category framework: ism jam‘ (singular from different word), jins (no singular), jam‘ (singular from same word). 'Al-kalim' is ism jins because it has no true singular in its own pattern.
The commentator gives the example of 'tamr/tamrah' to illustrate.
3
Check for morphological patterns
Examine the structure. The pattern 'fa‘īl' (as in 'kalim') is not a known plural pattern. This is a key argument that it is not a true plural.
Unlike 'tamr' which is on pattern 'fa‘l' (a plural pattern).
4
Observe syntactic agreement
Look at adjectives modifying the noun. The Quranic phrase 'al-kalim al-tayyib' uses a masculine singular adjective, confirming that 'kalim' is treated as a singular genus noun.
If it were a plural, the adjective would likely be feminine plural (kalimāt tayyibāt).
5
Apply to Quranic interpretation
Conclude that the term indicates the genus of good words, not a specific number. This affects rulings like the promise of ascension for every good word.
Hanafi usul uses such linguistic precision to define the scope of Quranic commands.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed lexical analysis of two key Quranic words: 'yas‘ad' (ascends) and 'al-kalim' (words). The commentator explains that 'su‘ūd' literally means physical upward movement, but here it is used metaphorically for approaching a being of higher rank—Allah. For 'al-kalim', he clarifies that it is a 'ism jins' (genus noun) rather than a true plural. He distinguishes three categories: ism jam‘ (collective noun with singular from a different word), jins (genus with no singular), and jam‘ (true plural with singular from the same word). He also notes the grammatical point that 'fa‘īl' is not a plural pattern, so 'kalim' is not a plural like 'tamr' or 'rakab'. The commentary then gives a Urdu explanation reinforcing these distinctions. This is a typical Hanafi usul approach: precise linguistic analysis to avoid misinterpretation of Quranic texts.

Key Terms
صعود (su‘ūd): Movement from lower to higher physically or metaphorically for elevation in rank.
الكلم (al-kalim): A genus noun (ism jins) meaning 'words', not a true plural; used for many but singular by pattern.
اسم جمع (ism jam‘): A noun that refers to a group but its singular is from a different word.
جنس (jins): A noun that refers to a category without a singular form.
جمع (jam‘): A true plural whose singular is from the same root word.

The Tree of Praise: Rooted in Belief and Fruited by Deeds
Commentary expanding metaphors of praise and acceptance with Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive from the Quranic verse (Fatir 35:10) and the commentary's metaphors that acceptance of praise requires both correct belief and righteous action.

Core Idea
Praise (hamd) is a tree: belief is its root, righteous deeds its branches—acceptance grows only from both.

How does one truly praise Allah? This lesson unpacks a classical commentary's metaphorical analysis of praise (hamd). The commentator draws from the Quran and hadith to show that verbal praise alone is insufficient; it must spring from firm belief and be accompanied by righteous deeds. We will explore the metaphors of watering places, east winds, and trees, and see how the verse 'To Him ascends good speech, and righteous work raises it' serves as the proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the metaphors
Locate three key metaphors in the text: 'mashari' al-shar' (watering places of the Sharia), 'qabool' as east wind and as acceptance, and 'mahamid' as a tree. Understand the relationships: the Sharia is like a garden, acceptance is like a beneficial wind, praise is a tree.
The commentary distinguishes between isti'arah takhyiliyyah (imaginative) and isti'arah makniyyah (implicit).
2
Analyze the two uses of qabool
The word 'qabool' is used first as the east wind (saba) and then as acceptance. The east wind is an imaginative metaphor for the source of mercy; acceptance is likened to that wind bringing life. Note the lexical dispute about its rarity.
The annotator challenges the claim that qabool (acceptance) is shadhdh (rare source form), citing similar words.
3
Examine the Quranic proof
The verse 'إليه يصعد الكلم الطيب والعمل الصالح يرفعه' (Fatir 35:10) is cited. Identify how the commentator uses it: the words 'al-kalim al-tayyib' (good speech) refers to belief and praise, and 'al-'amal al-salih' (righteous work) raises it. Acceptance depends on both.
The verse is qat'i al-thubut but zanni al-dalalah; the Hanafi school interprets it to indicate the necessity of both belief and action.
4
Trace the definition of hamd
The commentator quotes Imam al-Razi: hamd is not just verbal but includes belief (i'tiqad), speech (qawl), and action ('amal). Belief is the root, action is the branch. Without belief, praise is a rotten tree; without action, it bears no fruit.
This aligns with the Hanafi emphasis on combining belief and practice (usul and furu').
5
Synthesize the expansion
The passage expands the matn's brief metaphors into a full proof structure: the Sharia provides the sources (mashari'), acceptance is the wind that brings growth, and praise is the tree that must have roots (belief) and branches (deeds). The Quranic verse and hadith confirm that righteous work is essential for acceptance.
The expansion answers a potential question: why is action necessary for praise to be accepted?

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn's metaphorical language. The commentator first explains the metaphor of 'mashari' al-shar' (watering places of the Sharia) — the Sharia is likened to a garden that needs a watering place (mashra'), which provides pure water of mercy and divine pleasure. This is an imaginative metaphor where the Sharia is implicitly described as a watering place.

Next, the commentator explains the phrase 'to establish acceptance of worship' (li-ithbati qabul al-ibada). The matn uses the word 'qabool' in two senses: (1) as the east wind (saba), and (2) as acceptance. The first is an imaginative metaphor because the wind is associated with the rising place of the sun; the second is an implicit metaphor likening acceptance to the rising sun or a wind that brings fresh life and gathers clouds for rain. Thus acceptance is presented as a source of mercy and blessing.

Then, the commentator discusses the description of 'praises' (mahamid). The matn indicates that praises are like a tree whose roots are faith and belief, and branches are righteous deeds, alluding to the Quranic verse about a good word as a good tree.

A benefit (fa'idah) notes a difference of opinion: the commentator claims that 'qabool' (in the sense of acceptance) is a rare (shadhdh) source form with no parallel, but the annotator objects, citing examples like 'maloo' and 'waloo'.

Finally, the commentator provides a detailed investigation (tahqeeq) of praise (hamd). Citing Imam al-Razi, he explains that true praise is not just a verbal utterance but includes belief in Allah's perfect attributes, verbal expression, and righteous deeds. Belief is the root — without it, praise is like a rotten tree uprooted. Righteous action is the branch — without it, praise has no growth or acceptance. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four categories of Quranic texts (muhkam, nass, mutashabih, mujmal) according to the Hanafi school, and explain the purpose and epistemological status of mutashabih verses.

Core Idea
Quranic texts have degrees of clarity: muhkam (fully clear) to mutashabih (known only to Allah).

The Quran is not uniform in clarity; some verses are immediately understood, while others are deliberately ambiguous. The Hanafi school, following the verse 'None knows its interpretation except Allah' (3:7), holds that mutashabih verses are forever hidden from human knowledge. This lesson unpacks that classification and its rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories and the analogy
The passage introduces four categories via a house analogy: muhkam (completely apparent, like exterior), nass (less apparent), mutashabih (extremely hidden, like a cupboard known only to owner), mujmal (not that hidden, but not known to all). Note the relative degrees of clarity.
2
Define each category in Hanafi usul
Use the definitions in the key terms to differentiate muhkam (unequivocal), nass (clear but open to abrogation), mutashabih (known only to Allah), and mujmal (ambiguous, needing explanation).
3
Focus on mutashabih: the Hanafi position
The text states that according to the Hanafi school, mutashabih is surrounded by 'tents of concealment' such that its true meaning (ta'wil) is impossible to know. This is based on Quran 3:7. Note that this differs from other schools (e.g., Ash'aris) who allow scholarly interpretation.
4
Explain the purpose of mutashabih
The passage answers an implied objection: why reveal verses whose meaning cannot be known? One benefit is to test deeply rooted scholars by preventing them from pursuing their desire to know everything—just as the ignorant are tested to learn.
This is the ibtila' aspect.
5
Analyze the linguistic and analogical reasoning
The commentator uses the metaphor of restraining a horse's rein to illustrate that scholars must 'pull back' their minds from interpreting mutashabih. This shows that the Hanafi approach is not ignorance but disciplined submission.

Commentary
This passage explains the classification of Quranic texts by degree of clarity, using an extended analogy of a house. The author (commentator) is explaining the matn that mentions 'some divisions of the Book'. The analogy: a house has parts that are fully visible (outer walls), less visible (interior rooms), hidden (cupboards known only to owner), and somewhat hidden (not known to all). Similarly, Quranic rulings (or verses) have levels: muhkam (fully clear), nass (clear but less), mutashabih (completely hidden in meaning, known only to Allah), and mujmal (brief, not detailed). The text then quotes Taftazani's al-Talwih, stating that 'mutashabih' is surrounded by 'tents of concealment' such that its true interpretation is unknowable to humans. This is the Hanafi position based on verse 3:7. The purpose of such verses is to test the scholars, because they naturally desire to know everything; they are commanded to stop and not delve into the hidden meanings. This parallels the test of ignorant people who are commanded to learn. The passage includes linguistic notes for Arabic terms and a further explanation of Taftazani's words about restraining the mind, like pulling a horse's rein.

Key Terms
محکم (muhkam): A verse or text of the Qur'an that is clear and unequivocal in meaning, generally understood by all.
نص (nass): Clear text that yields a specific meaning but may have some degree of possibility for interpretation, lower than muhkam.
متشابہ (mutashabih): Verses whose ultimate meaning is known only to Allah; humans cannot ascertain their intended interpretation.
مجمل (mujmal): A text that is general or ambiguous in wording, requiring external clarification.

The Wisdom of Mutashabihat and the Test of Scholars
Shah of Al-Tawdih: explanation of the wisdom behind mutashabihat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on mutashabihat, identify the dalil from Quran 3:7, and analyse the wisdom behind their revelation.

Core Idea
Only Allah knows the true meaning of mutashabihat; their revelation tests the scholars' submission.

This passage from Allama Taftazani's commentary on Al-Tawdih addresses a key usul al-fiqh question: if mutashabihat cannot be interpreted, why were they revealed? Taftazani explains the Hanafi position that their true meaning is known only to Allah, and then presents the wisdom of testing the scholars. This lesson unpacks the proof and the underlying rationale.

Guided Path
1
Identify the implied objection
The passage begins by addressing a hidden question: if no one can know the meaning of mutashabihat, what benefit is there in revealing them? Recognize that this is a common challenge to the Hanafi position.
The question is based on the assumption that beneficial speech must be understood.
2
State the Hanafi position on ta'wil
Taftazani clarifies that the Hanafi school holds that the true interpretation (ta'wil) of mutashabihat is known only to Allah. This is supported by the verse Quran 3:7.
The verse is considered qat'i al-dalala by Hanafis.
3
Examine the dalil (Quran 3:7)
Quote the verse: 'وما يعلم تأويله إلا الله' (And none knows its interpretation except Allah). Analyze its wajh al-dalalah: the exception 'إلا الله' explicitly restricts knowledge to Allah.
This is a proof from nass, giving certainty.
4
Understand the madhhab dispute
Some scholars (non-Hanafis) argue that the rasikhun (those firm in knowledge) also know the ta'wil, based on an alternative reading of the verse. The Hanafi response is that the correct grammatical pause is after 'إلا الله', so the rasikhun only affirm belief without knowing the interpretation.
This is a ikhtilaf in qira'at and grammar.
5
Analyse the benefit: testing the scholars
The revelation of mutashabihat serves as a test for scholars: their desire to know everything is checked by having to accept a limit. This distinguishes the truly grounded scholars from those who speculate.
The test is tailored to the state of the scholar, just as the ignorant are tested to learn.

Definition of I'jaz and the Correct View of Quranic Miraculousness
Definitional clarification of I'jaz and defense of the eloquence-based view of Quranic I'jaz.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define I'jaz terminologically and identify the correct aspect of Quranic I'jaz according to the Hanafi school, distinguishing it from weaker views.

Core Idea
I'jaz means impossibility of imitation; the Quran's I'jaz is primarily due to its unmatched eloquence.

The passage clarifies a common confusion: the description 'conveying meaning in the most eloquent way' is a mode (wajh) of I'jaz, not its definition. True I'jaz is the quality that renders speech immune to opposition. The commentator then affirms that, despite differing opinions, the correct view holds that the Quran's miracle lies in its supreme eloquence and fluency (balaghah and fasadah). This lesson will help you precisely define I'jaz and understand the Hanafi position on the basis of Quranic inimitability.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish definition from mode
Read the matn's statement: 'Indeed I'jaz in speech is that the meaning is conveyed in a way that is more eloquent...' The commentator clarifies this is a description of a mode (wajh) of I'jaz, not its definition. Identify the exact line where he says 'this is not the definition'.
The definition must encompass the core meaning of rendering incapable.
2
State the correct definition
The commentator defines I'jaz as 'speech being such that it cannot be opposed or that something like it cannot be produced.' Note the root 'a'jaza' meaning to render incapable. Write down the terminological definition in your own words.
Linguistically, I'jaz derives from 'ajz' (inability).
3
List the differing opinions on the aspect (wajh) of Quranic I'jaz
The passage lists four opinions: (1) eloquence, (2) news of the unseen, (3) unique style, (4) divine prevention of opposition. Understand each and note why scholars differ despite agreeing on the fact of I'jaz.
The ikhtilaf is not on whether the Quran is miraculous, but on how it is miraculous.
4
Identify the correct view and its basis
The commentator declares that the correct opinion (al-ra'i al-sahih) is that the I'jaz is due to its ultimate eloquence and fluency. Recognize that this is the Hanafi position and that the other views are considered weaker (marjuh).
The Hanafi reliance on linguistic inimitability stems from the Quran's own challenge (tahaddi).

Distinguishing the Definition of I'jaz from Its Manifestation
Resolving a doubt about i'jaz definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the definition of i'jaz and its manifestation, and identify the correct reason for the Quran's inimitability according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
I'jaz is the impossibility of opposition, not mere eloquence; the Quran's i'jaz lies in its ultimate balaghah.

In this passage, the commentator resolves a common misunderstanding: the phrase 'conveying meaning in the most eloquent manner' is often mistaken for the very definition of i'jaz. But i'jaz is derived from 'a'jaza' meaning to incapacitate. The commentator clarifies that while there are various opinions on why the Quran is miraculous, the correct view is that its inimitability is due to its unparalleled eloquence and fluency (balaghah wa fasahah). This lesson unpacks the analytical steps to grasp this key usul point.

Guided Path
1
Identify the doubt
The original text states: 'That i'jaz in speech is to convey the meaning by a method that is the most eloquent...' A student might think this is the definition of i'jaz. Your first step is to recognise that this is a doubt that needs resolution.

I'jaz is derived from 'a'jaza' (to make incapable). Therefore, i'jaz in speech means that the speech cannot be opposed or matched – it renders the opponent incapable. This is the core meaning.
The linguistic origin grounds the technical definition.
3
Differentiate manifestation from definition
The phrase in question describes one manifestation of i'jaz (the most eloquent method), but it does not encompass the full definition. The definition is about the effect of incapacitating opposition, not merely the method.
This is the crucial analytical distinction.
4
Survey the differing opinions
Scholars agree the Quran is miraculous (mu'jiz) but differ on the precise aspect: eloquence, news of the unseen, unique style, or divine prevention of opposition. List these as background.
Each opinion has proponents, but only one is adopted in the Hanafi school.
5
Adopt the correct Hanafi view
The commentator declares as 'sahih' (correct) that the i'jaz of the Quran is due to its utmost balaghah and fasahah. This is the operative view in Hanafi usul.
This choice affects how the Quran's authority (hujjiyyah) is understood.

The Singularity of I'jaz and Multiplicity of Magic
Commentator explains word choice in base text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the author used the plural 'ahdab' for magic and the singular 'urwah' for i'jaz, and apply this linguistic method to analyze word choices in Hanafi usul texts.

Core Idea
I'jaz has one degree; magic has many. Hence singular for i'jaz, plural for magic.

In this passage, Allamah Taftazani unpacks a seemingly minor word choice in the original text: why plural 'ahdab' (fringes) for magic, and singular 'urwah' (handle) for i'jaz? The answer reveals a profound understanding of how linguistic form mirrors conceptual reality. By learning this, you will sharpen your sensitivity to precise word selection in classical texts, a key skill in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic contrast
Locate the two phrases 'ahdab al-sihr' and 'urwat al-i'jaz' in the text. Note that 'ahdab' is plural (fringes) and 'urwah' is singular (handle).
The contrast in number is the starting point.
2
Understand the meaning of each term
Look up the linguistic definitions: 'hadab' is the edge of a garment, weak and multiple; 'urwah' is the handle of a vessel, strong and single. The commentator explains that 'urwah' is stronger than 'hadab'.
The strength of the handle versus the weakness of fringes parallels the concepts they represent.
3
Map the concepts to i'jaz and sihr
I'jaz (inimitability of the Quran) is the highest degree of eloquence, unique and singular. Sihr (magic of speech) has multiple degrees of subtlety. Thus i'jaz takes the singular 'urwah' (strong, single), and sihr takes the plural 'ahdab' (many, weaker).
This mapping is explicit in the commentary.
4
Explore the commentator's rationale
Taftazani states that i'jaz is one level (the highest and near-highest together considered one), while sihr has multiple paths and ranks. Hence the plural for sihr and singular for i'jaz.
The rationale is based on the nature of the concepts, not arbitrary aesthetics.
5
Apply to broader usul
Recognize how such linguistic clues help us understand the structure of dalalat in Hanafi thought. For example, the singular 'amr' indicates obligation (wujub) because it is one clear command, while multiple expressions may indicate permissibility or recommendation.
This skill transfers to analyzing many usul principles.

The Four Sources of Usul al-Fiqh: Classification Methods
Defining the four types of evidences in Usul al-Fiqh with two classification methods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any Shar'i proof into one of the four sources using the two Hanafi methods (revelation vs. non-revelation; via Prophet vs. not via Prophet).

Core Idea
All Shar'i proofs fall into exactly one of four sources: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas.

This lesson establishes the foundational taxonomy of Islamic legal proofs. The text first distinguishes between the concept (mafhum) of Usul al-Fiqh and its extension (misdak)—the actual set of sources. It then presents two binary classification methods that together exhaustively capture all admissible proofs. Mastering this taxonomy is essential for understanding how Hanafi jurists prioritize and derive rulings.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish mafhum from misdaq
Recognize that the concept (mafhum) of Usul al-Fiqh was explained earlier; now the text explains its extension (misdak)—the actual objects that the concept applies to. Just as 'Abdullah' means 'servant of God' but its misdaq is a specific person, the misdaq of Usul al-Fiqh is the four sources.
2
Apply the first binary: revelation vs. non-revelation
Divide all Shar'i proofs into revelation (wahy) and non-revelation. Revelation is further subdivided into recited (Quran) and non-recited (Sunnah). Non-revelation is either the consensus of the entire ummah (Ijma') or analogy (Qiyas).
The first method is based on the nature of the proof: wahy vs. ghayr wahy.
3
Apply the second binary: through the Prophet or not
Alternatively, ask whether the proof reaches us via the Prophet (peace be upon him). If yes, check if its wording is miraculous (Quran) or not (Sunnah). If no, check if it requires infallibility of those who issued it (Ijma') or not (Qiyas).
The second method focuses on the chain of transmission and the attribute of infallibility.
4
Test each classification on a hypothetical case
For any given proof (e.g., a hadith ahad, a ruling by a single sahabi), run it through both binary trees. Confirm that every proof falls into exactly one of the four categories.
This step reinforces that the taxonomy is exhaustive and mutually exclusive.

Commentary
This passage defines the four sources of Islamic law as understood in Hanafi usul. It begins by clarifying that the concept (mafhum) of Usul al-Fiqh was explained earlier, and now the extension (misdak)—the actual objects that the concept applies to—is explained. The extension is limited to four types: the Book (Quran), the Sunnah (Prophetic tradition), Ijma' (consensus), and Qiyas (analogy). The text provides two methods to classify evidences. The first method divides proofs into revelation (wahy) and non-revelation. Revelation is either recited (the Quran) or non-recited (the Sunnah). Non-revelation is either the statement of the entire ummah (Ijma') or analogy (Qiyas). The second method focuses on whether the proof reaches us through the Prophet or not. If it does, it is either the Quran (if miraculous in wording) or Sunnah (if not). If it does not, it is either Ijma' (if from an infallible group) or Qiyas (since individual ijtihad is fallible). The Urdu commentary emphasizes that the four sources are exhaustive because all Shar'i proofs must fall into one of these categories. This is a foundational lesson in usul al-fiqh, memorized by students as the pillars of legal evidence.

Key Terms
كتاب (Kitab): The Quran, the recited revelation (wahy matluw) characterized by inimitability (i'jaz).
سنة (Sunnah): Non-recited revelation (wahy ghayr matluw), the words, actions, and approvals of the Prophet.
إجماع (Ijma'): Consensus of all mujtahid scholars of a particular era on a Shar'i ruling.
قياس (Qiyas): Analogical deduction; extending a ruling from an original case (asl) to a new case (far') based on a shared effective cause ('illa).

Why Usul Discusses Ijma and Qiyas but Not Quran and Sunnah
Question-answer clarifying the scope of usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the authority of the Quran and Sunnah is considered self-evident (badihi) in Hanafi usul, and identify the scope of usul al-fiqh as dealing only with acquired proofs (kashiyyat).

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, only non-self-evident proofs like ijma and qiyas require justification; Quran and Sunnah are assumed.

You may wonder: if usul al-fiqh is about proofs (adillah), why do scholars discuss the proof of ijma and qiyas but not the proof of the Quran and Sunnah? This passage from the Sharh al-Talwih answers by distinguishing between self-evident (badihi) and acquired (kashiyyat) proofs. Understanding this boundary is essential to grasp the proper scope of usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Read the question posed in the passage: Why are ijma and qiyas included as issues of usul, but not the Book and Sunnah? This is the starting point.
The objector assumes that if usul deals with proofs, it should cover all proofs uniformly.
2
Understand the distinction introduced
The answer distinguishes between two categories of proof: badihi (self-evident) and kashiyyat (acquired). Self-evident proofs require no justification; acquired proofs need demonstration.
This is a key epistemological division in Hanafi usul.
3
Apply the distinction to the examples
Quran and Sunnah are badihi because their authority is established in kalam and well-known. Therefore, they are not topics of debate within usul. Ijma and qiyas are kashiyyat and must be proven.
The passage also cites irregular recitations and solitary reports as non-self-evident proofs that are discussed.
4
Extend the reasoning to other proofs
Consider why scholars engage with khabar wahid and qira'at shadhdhah: they lack self-evident authority and require validation. This confirms the principle: usul only examines proofs that are not immediately obvious.
This clarifies the boundary of the discipline.

Commentary
This passage is a question-and-answer explaining why certain proofs are considered issues of usul al-fiqh while others are not. The commentator addresses a potential objection: if the subject matter of usul is adillah (proofs) and ahkam (rulings), then why are the proofs for ijma (consensus) and qiyas (analogical reasoning) discussed as usul issues, but not the proofs for the Quran and Sunnah? The answer is that the Quran and Sunnah are so well-established and self-evident (badihi) in their authority—confirmed in theology (kalam) and widely known among people—that they do not require proof within usul itself. In contrast, ijma and qiyas are acquired proofs (kashiyat) that need justification. This is also why usul scholars discuss irregular recitations (qira'at shadhdhah) and solitary reports (khabar wahid), as these are not self-evident and require validation. This clarifies the scope of usul al-fiqh: it deals with proofs that are not immediately obvious.

Key Terms
الكسبيات (al-kashiyyat): Matters that are acquired through reasoning and need proof, as opposed to self-evident truths.
البديهي (al-badihi): Self-evident truth that requires no proof.
