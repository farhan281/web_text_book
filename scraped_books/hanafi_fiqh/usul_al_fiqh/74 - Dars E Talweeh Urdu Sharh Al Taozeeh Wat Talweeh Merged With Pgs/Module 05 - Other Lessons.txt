Three Exegetical Interpretations of Hamd in Usul Texts
Commentator's expansion of 'first and second' in hamd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify three exegetical approaches to interpreting the phrase 'first and second' in the context of hamd and apply them to similar textual expansions in Hanafi usul works.

Core Idea
Hamd encompasses both God's essential perfection and His blessings; commentators expand phrases using multiple interpretive dimensions.

This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary (sharh) unpacks the seemingly simple phrase 'first and second' used in relation to hamd. The commentator presents three distinct interpretive frameworks, each grounded in rational or scriptural evidence. This is a hallmark of Hanafi usul methodology: where a text admits multiple layers, the commentator provides plausible readings. Understanding this expansion trains you in the art of tafsir-style analysis within usul texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base phrase
The phrase being expanded is 'first and second' (awwala wa thaniyan) as used by the original author in relation to hamd.
The commentator assumes the reader knows the original text.
2
Distinguish the three interpretations
The first interpretation distinguishes hamd for Allah's essence/attributes versus His blessings. The second reduces all blessings to origination and preservation. The third links hamd to 'first and last' in the Quranic verse.
Each interpretation has a distinct focus: ontological, categorical, and eschatological.
3
Note the evidence used
The first uses rational argument (kamaal dhat), the second cites specific Quranic surahs, and the third quotes a verse (al-Qasas 70).
This shows how usul commentators ground expansions in both reason and revelation.
4
Recognize the analytical purpose
Such expansions are not exhaustive but serve to deepen understanding of the original phrase, revealing the richness of a simple expression.
This method is used throughout usul works to unpack dense language.

Commentary
In this passage, the commentator (sharh) elaborates on the meaning of hamd in the author's phrase 'first and second' (awwala wa thaniyan). He presents three possible interpretations. First: Hamd is for God's essential perfection and attributes, and also for His blessings and gifts (including the authorship of the book). Second: All blessings reduce to two categories: origination (ijad) and preservation (ibqa'), and hamd is for both, citing specific surahs that begin with hamd and pointing to these categories. This expansion shows how usul texts deepen the meaning of foundational phrases.

إيجاد (ijad): Origination or bringing into existence

Three Interpretations of 'Awwalan wa Thaniyan' in the Praise
Explaining the rationale for the qualifiers 'firstly and secondly' in the author's praise

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain three Hanafi usul interpretations (wujuh) for the qualifiers 'firstly and secondly' in a textual phrase, and apply the method of tahmil al-wujuh to other texts.

Core Idea
The author's 'firstly and secondly' is deliberate; the commentator reveals its layered meanings through usul analysis.

This passage from the sharh addresses a subtle objection: why did the author add 'awwalan wa thaniyan' to 'Haamidan'? The commentator demonstrates that these qualifiers are not redundant but carry rich interpretive possibilities. By presenting three wujuh, he models the Hanafi usul method of exploring multiple valid interpretations (tahmil al-wujuh) to uncover the depth of a text. This lesson will walk you through his reasoning and its broader application.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base text and the objection
Locate the phrase under analysis: 'Haamidan lillahi awwalan wa thaniyan'. Understand the implicit objection: why are 'firstly and secondly' needed? This sets up the commentator's response.
The objection is not stated explicitly but implied by the commentator's defensive explanation.
2
Analyze the first interpretation (wajh)
The first interpretation separates praise for Allah's essence/attributes (first) from praise for His blessings (second). Note how the qualification differentiates two aspects of praise.
Linguistically, 'awwalan' and 'thaniyan' serve to distinguish categories.
3
Analyze the second interpretation
Here, blessings are categorized under creation (ijad) and preservation (ibqa'), each with a first and second phase. The commentator links this to Quranic surahs that begin with praise, showing thematic consistency.
This interpretation uses induction (istiqra) from Quranic patterns.
4
Note the third interpretation (partial)
The third interpretation connects the phrase to the Quranic verse 'wa lahu al-hamdu fi al-ula wa al-akhirah', interpreting 'first and last' as this world and the hereafter.
Even though the text cuts off, the third interpretation is mentioned in the translation.
5
Apply the method of tahmil al-wujuh
Recognize that the commentator's approach is a demonstration of the Hanafi usul principle that a text can bear multiple valid meanings (wujuh). Practice identifying possible interpretations for other textual phrases.
This method prevents hasty dismissal of wording as redundant.

Defending the Division of Ibtina: Sensory vs. Intellectual
Defending the classification of ibtina against a logical objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sensory (hissi) and intellectual (aqli) ibtina, explain the objection that ibtina is purely intellectual, and articulate the Hanafi resolution using the relata-based and correct-definition arguments.

Core Idea
Ibtina is sensory when relata are perceptible, intellectual when purely mental; the relation itself is not the basis.

A key conceptual distinction in Hanafi usul is the classification of 'ibtina' (building upon). An objection arises: since ibtina is a relation (a purely intellectual category), how can it be sensory? This lesson unpacks the objection and the Hanafi response, culminating in the correct understanding that the division is based on the perceptibility of the two things related, not the nature of the relation itself.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Objection
The objection states: ibtina is a relation (idafah), which exists only in the mind. Therefore, it cannot be divided into sensory and intellectual because all relations are intellectual. Grasp this initial claim.
2
Analyze the First Answer (Relata-Based)
The first response argues the division is based on the two sides (relata): if both are sensible (e.g., roof and walls), ibtina is sensory; if both are intellectual (e.g., ruling and its evidence), it is intellectual. Notice how this shifts focus from the relation to the relata.
3
Examine the Second Answer (Customary Definition)
The second answer invokes custom (urf): sensory ibtina means one thing physically placed on top of another. This excludes cases like verb and masdar (auditory but not physical stacking). However, this narrow definition fails to include those cases in either category, making the division incomplete.
4
Grasp the Correct Answer (Example vs. Definition)
The commentator clarifies that 'tarattub al-hukm ala dalilihi' (ordering of ruling upon evidence) is not a definition but an example of intellectual ibtina. The true definition is any purely mental relationship between two things, such as cause-effect, metaphor-reality, or rulings with universal rules. This preserves the division: sensory ibtina covers physical/auditory relationships; intellectual ibtina covers all mental relationships.

Why is it said that the intended (intellectual ibtina) and the non-intended (sensory ibtina) are also included? The reason why the mentioned objection does not arise is clear: customary meaning is metaphorical, and metaphor is resorted to when the literal meaning cannot be intended. Here, because there is a context indicating the literal meaning, the customary meaning is not needed. The commentator said: "If you say: The building of one thing upon another is an addition between them, and it is certainly an intellectual matter. I say: He meant by 'ibtina' that the two things are sensible, and it includes like the building of the roof upon the walls, and the derivation of a derived word from its source, like the verb from the infinitive. He meant what is considered in custom that the building of the roof upon the walls means it is built upon it and placed above it, which is perceived by sense. Thus, the derivation of the verb from the infinitive is excluded from the sensory and does not enter the intellectual according to its interpretation. Real vs. Nominal Definitions and Conditions of Tard and 'Aks
Explaining real vs nominal definitions and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between real and nominal definitions in Hanafi usul and apply the conditions of tard and 'aks to evaluate a definition's validity.

Core Idea
Real and nominal definitions are the same essence, differing only in epistemic context.

Definitions are foundational to precise legal reasoning. Hanafi usul distinguishes between real (haqiqi) and nominal (ismi) definitions based on whether existence is known. This lesson unpacks that distinction, the proper order of inquiry using interrogative particles, and the two conditions that make a definition valid: tard and 'aks. Understanding these tools sharpens your ability to formulate and critique definitions in fiqh and usul.

Guided Path
1
Map the three interrogative particles
Identify 'ma sharhih' (conceptual), 'hal basitah' (existential), and 'ma haqiqiyyah' (essential). Note the required order: first conceptual, then existential, then essential.
This ordering is critical; a definition given out of order may be nominal instead of real.
2
Distinguish real from nominal definition
A real definition answers 'ma haqiqiyyah' after existence is established; a nominal definition answers 'ma sharhih' before or without existential knowledge. The same essential quiddity can have both depending on epistemic context.
The commentator resolves the apparent conflict between matn and sharh by explaining that real and nominal definitions are 'united in essence, differing in consideration'.
3
Understand the condition of tard
Tard means that whenever the definition applies, the defined term applies. This ensures the definition is not too narrow (i.e., it excludes non-instances).
Formally: 'kullama wujida al-hadd wujida al-mahdud'.
4
Understand the condition of 'aks
'Aks means the converse: whenever the defined term applies, the definition applies. This ensures the definition is not too broad. Note two interpretations: positive converse (every instance of the defined term is an instance of the definition) and negative converse (if the definition is absent, the defined term is absent).
Both interpretations yield the same requirement: the definition must 'encompass all individuals of the defined term'.
5
Apply both conditions to a definition
Take a definition like 'insan is hayawan natiq'. Check tard: Is everything that is hayawan natiq also insan? Yes. Check 'aks: Is every insan also hayawan natiq? Yes. If either condition fails, the definition is invalid.
This ensures the definition is both necessary and sufficient.

Lexical investigation: 'Ma haqiqiyyah', 'ma sharhih', and 'hal basitah' are three interrogative tools. The difference between them is that 'ma haqiqiyyah' asks about the reality and quiddity of a thing, 'ma sharhih' asks for clarification of a thing's meaning and concept, 'hal basitah' asks about a thing's existence. Their order is: first, clarification is sought with 'ma sharhih' for something, then second, existence is determined with 'hal basitah', after existence is known, the reality of the thing is asked with 'ma haqiqiyyah'. For example: Someone asked 'ma al-insan?' clarify for us. We answered, for example, 'innahu dahik katib' (he is laughing, writing). Then the question became 'hal al-insan mawjud?' We said 'na'am mawjud' (yes, existent). Then the question became 'ma al-insan?' i.e., what is his reality? We answered 'innahu hayawan natiq' (he is a rational animal).

For example, in geometry, the definition of a triangle is 'shakl yuhit bihi thalathat adla'' meaning a shape enclosed by three lines. This definition is given, but when Euclid presents proofs and demonstrates its existence, then this same definition becomes real. As for 'aks, some took it from the converse of tard according to common understanding: it is making the predicate the subject while observing the same quantity, as is said: every human is laughing, and conversely, i.e., every laughing is human. So the result of tard is a universal judgment by the defined term on the definition, and 'aks is a universal judgment by the definition on the defined term. And some took it from the fact that the converse of affirmation is negation, and explained it as: whenever the definition is negated, the defined term is negated, i.e., everything to which the definition does not apply, the defined term does not apply to it. Commentary
This passage is an extended commentary on the nature of definitions (ta'rif) in usul al-fiqh, contrasting real (haqiqi) and nominal (ismi) definitions. It begins with a lexical investigation of three Arabic interrogative particles: 'ma haqiqiyyah' (asking about reality), 'ma sharhih' (asking for conceptual clarification), and 'hal basitah' (asking about existence). The author explains their proper order: first ask for conceptual meaning (ma sharhih), then verify existence (hal basitah), then inquire into reality (ma haqiqiyyah), illustrating with the example of defining 'insan' (human).

The passage then addresses an apparent contradiction between the matn (original text) and the sharh (commentary) regarding whether real definition applies only to real quiddity or also to conventional quiddity. The commentator resolves this by stating that real and nominal definitions are 'united in essence, differing in consideration' (mutahid bil-dhat mutaghayir bil-i'tibar). That is, the same essential quiddity can receive either a real or nominal definition depending on the epistemic context: if the definition is given after establishing existence (answering ma haqiqiyyah), it is real; if given before or without existential knowledge, it is nominal. The example of defining a triangle in geometry illustrates this: initially it is a nominal definition, but after Euclid's demonstrations, it becomes real.

Finally, the commentator states the two conditions for a valid definition (both real and nominal): tard (coextensiveness) and 'aks (converseness). Tard means that every instance of the definition is also an instance of the defined term. 'Aks means the converse: every instance of the defined term is also an instance of the definition. The commentator explains two interpretations of 'aks: one as the direct converse of tard (e.g., 'every human is laughing' and conversely 'every laughing is human'), and another as a negative principle: if the definition is absent, the defined term is absent. Both interpretations ensure the definition is neither too narrow nor too broad, but 'jami' li-afradi al-mahdud kulliha' (encompasses all individuals of the defined term).

This discussion is foundational for classical Hanafi usul, where precision in definitions is critical for legal reasoning. The distinction between real and nominal definitions is particularly relevant when dealing with legal terms that may have both a linguistic and a technical meaning. The conditions of tard and 'aks ensure that legal definitions correctly capture the intended category without over- or under-inclusion.

Key Terms
تعریف حقیقی (ta'rif haqiqi): Definition that reveals the real essence of a thing, given after its existence is known
تعریف اسمی (ta'rif ismi): Definition that explains the conceptual meaning without requiring existential knowledge
طرد (tard): Condition that the definition applies to everything the defined term applies to (coextensiveness in one direction)
عکس (aks): Condition that the defined term applies to everything the definition applies to (converseness); also interpreted as negation condition
ما حقیقہ (ma haqiqiyyah): Interrogative particle asking about the reality of a thing

Conditions of Definition: Tard and 'Aks
Explaining the conditions of comprehensiveness and exclusiveness in definitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the conditions of tard and 'aks for a valid definition, distinguish between real and nominal definitions, and apply these conditions to evaluate whether a given definition is comprehensive and exclusive.

Core Idea
A valid definition must be coextensive with the defined: it must include all instances and exclude all non-instances.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, constructing a proper definition is essential for precise legal reasoning. This lesson explores the two conditions that any definition, whether real (haqiqi) or nominal (ismi), must satisfy: tard (comprehensiveness) and 'aks (exclusiveness). Understanding these conditions will allow you to evaluate whether a definition correctly captures the essence of a term without being too broad or too narrow.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between real and nominal definitions
The passage explains that a definition can be real (haqiqi) or nominal (ismi) depending on perspective: real definitions consider the essence as existing in reality, while nominal definitions consider it as conceptualized. Identify the two perspectives and their relationship.
Real definitions answer 'what is it in reality' (ma haqiqiyya); nominal definitions answer 'what is explanatory' (ma sharaha).
2
Define tard (exclusiveness)
Tard ensures that every instance of the definition is also an instance of the defined. This prevents the definition from being too general (including non-defined items). Understand the phrase 'whenever the definition exists, the defined exists'.
Tard makes the definition exclusive (mani').
3
Define 'aks (comprehensiveness)
'Aks ensures that every instance of the defined is also an instance of the definition. This prevents the definition from being too narrow (excluding some defined items). Note the two interpretations given in the passage.
The logicians interpret 'aks as the positive converse; others interpret it as the negation condition. Both aim for comprehensiveness (jami').
4
Understand the two interpretations of 'aks
The passage presents two views: (1) 'aks is the converse of tard: whenever the defined applies, the definition applies; (2) 'aks is the negation: whenever the definition is absent, the defined is absent. Both achieve the same result.
The first interpretation is from the logicians, the second from some others. The result is that the definition encompasses all individuals of the defined.

Commentary
This passage discusses the two types of definition in Islamic logic and usul al-fiqh: real (haqiqi) and nominal (ismi) definitions, and the conditions required for a valid definition. The author explains that the same definition can be either real or nominal depending on the perspective – whether we consider the essence as existing in reality or as conceptualized. The key point is that both types must satisfy two conditions: tard (comprehensiveness) and 'aks (exclusiveness). Tard means that whenever the definition applies, the defined must also apply; this ensures the definition is not too general and excludes non-defined items. 'Aks has two interpretations: according to logicians, it is the converse of tard, i.e., whenever the defined applies, the definition must also apply, ensuring the definition is not too specific and covers all instances. Alternatively, some interpret 'aks as the negation of tard: whenever the definition is absent, the defined is absent. Both interpretations aim for the definition to be coextensive with the defined. The passage also distinguishes between a full definition (hadd) and a mere property (khassa): a property is exclusive but not comprehensive. This discussion is foundational for understanding how definitions are constructed and evaluated in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Key Terms
تعریف حقیقی (ta'rif haqiqi): A definition based on the essence as it exists in reality, answering 'what is it in reality' (ma haqiqiyya).
تعریف اسمی (ta'rif ismi): A definition based on the essence as conceptualized by the mind, answering 'what is explanatory' (ma sharaha).
طرد (tard): The condition that whenever the definition (hadd) applies, the defined (mahdud) must also apply; i.e., comprehensiveness.
عکس ('aks): The condition that whenever the defined applies, the definition must apply (according to logicians); or, the negation condition that whenever the definition is absent, the defined is absent (according to others). Both ensure exclusiveness.
جامع (jami'): Comprehensive: includes all individuals of the defined.
مانع (mani'): Exclusive: excludes all individuals not part of the defined.

Debate on definition of asl and condition of exclusivity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition of exclusivity (man') in definitions and evaluate whether it is necessary for nominal definitions according to the Hanafi majority.

Core Idea
A definition must exclude non-definienda, but nominal definitions may tolerate generality for pragmatic distinction.

This passage examines a foundational debate in Hanafi usul: must a definition be exclusive (mani')? The commentator challenges the author's condition of exclusivity, arguing that nominal definitions (like those in Arabic lexicons) often use broader terms. The author upholds the majority view that exclusivity is a condition, but does not reject the opposing view entirely. This lesson unpacks the arguments and the concept of asl, sharpening your ability to critique and refine legal definitions.

Distinguish cause, condition, and asl

The commentator argues that exclusivity is not required in nominal definitions. He cites Arabic lexicons (e.g., al-sa'danah defined as 'nabt') and the verifiers' statements that incomplete definitions may be broader to achieve limited distinction.

The author (al-Matin) adopts the position of the majority (jumhur) that exclusivity is a condition for a sound definition. However, he does not declare Imam Razi's definition absolutely wrong, only that it would be better if it were exclusive. This nuanced stance reflects the Hanafi usul principle of precision while acknowledging practical flexibility in nominal definitions.

The Twelve Shar'i Rulings: Classification and Derivation
Defines the twelve categories of hukm based on act and leave with prohibition levels.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the six primary categories of hukm (wajib, mandub, mubah, haram, makruh tahrimi, makruh tanzihi) and explain how each yields two rulings (act and leave) to form twelve.

Core Idea
The twelve rulings are not separate; they arise from six categories split by act and leave.

This lesson unpacks the classification of shar'i rulings according to the Hanafi school. The commentator shows that every permissible act either has equal act and leave (mubah), or one is preferable. Preference combined with prohibition yields the categories. Moreover, each category has two faces: doing and leaving, giving twelve total. This is a foundational structure for legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six categories
List the six categories: wajib (includes fard), mandub (includes sunnah/nafl), mubah, haram, makruh tahrimi, makruh tanzihi. Note that the commentator expressly includes fard under wajib and sunnah/nafl under mandub.
In Hanafi usul, wajib and fard are sometimes distinguished by strength of proof, but here they are merged for classification.
2
Understand the basis: preference and prohibition
For each category, determine whether the act or its leave is preferable, and whether the opposite is prohibited. Wajib: act preferable, leaving prohibited. Mandub: act preferable, leaving not prohibited. Mubah: equal. Haram: leave preferable, act prohibited (by qat'i dalil). Makruh tahrimi: leave preferable, act prohibited (by zanni dalil). Makruh tanzihi: leave preferable, act not prohibited.
The distinction between qat'i and zanni is crucial for separating haram from makruh tahrimi.
3
Apply the act/leave split
Recognize that each category has two aspects: performing the act, and omitting it. These are distinct rulings. For example, for wajib: doing it is obligatory, leaving it is haram (prohibited). For mandub: doing it is recommended, leaving it is permissible but discouraged, etc.
The split gives 6 × 2 = 12 rulings, as the commentator states.
4
Count the total
Compute: six categories times two (act and leave) equals twelve. Verify that each combination yields a unique hukm. For instance, 'act of wajib' is wajib, 'leaving wajib' is haram (since leaving is prohibited). 'Act of haram' is haram, 'leaving haram' is wajib (since leaving is preferable).
This demonstrates how the twelve are not arbitrary but derived from a simple matrix.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Makruh Tahrimi and Makruh Tanzihi
Discusses the internal Hanafi difference on the definition of makruh tahrimi and tanzihi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definitions of makruh tahrimi according to Imam Muhammad and the two shaykhs, and explain the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between the two views.

Core Idea
Makruh tahrimi: impermissible according to Imam Muhammad, but only blameworthy (closer to halal) according to the two shaykhs.

This lesson examines a key internal Hanafi dispute over the definition of makruh categories. The commentator aligns with Imam Muhammad, but acknowledges the view of the two shaykhs. Understanding this difference is crucial for applying these terms correctly in fatwa and for evaluating the weight of different authoritative opinions.

Guided Path

State Imam Muhammad's definition
Imam Muhammad defines makruh tahrimi as an act whose doing is prohibited by a speculative (zanni) proof; its doing is not permissible (la yajuz) and must be left like haram. Makruh tanzihi is an act whose leaving is preferable but doing is not prohibited; doing is permissible (yajuz).
This definition aligns with the author's (matn) treatment: makruh tahrimi falls under 'la yajuz'.

State the two shaykhs' definition
The two shaykhs (likely Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, or Abu Yusuf and Muhammad?) define makruh tahrimi as an act where leaving is preferable and doing is prohibited, but it is closer to haram; the doer deserves a deterrent short of hellfire. Makruh tanzihi is when it is closer to halal; the doer is not punished but leaves reward. They consider makruh tahrimi approximately permissible (yajuz).
Their view sees makruh tahrimi as less severe than haram, even though doing is prohibited.
3
Identify the point of contention
The core difference is whether makruh tahrimi is permissible (yajuz) or impermissible (la yajuz). Imam Muhammad says the act must be left; the two shaykhs say it is still permissible but blameworthy.
This hinges on the strength of the prohibition: speculative proof vs. definitive.
4
Find the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
According to Imam Muhammad, one must avoid makruh tahrimi acts; according to the two shaykhs, one may perform them but with blame. This affects fatwa: the mufti must decide which view to adopt.
The text favors Imam Muhammad's view as 'appropriate here' (munasib huna).

When Does Avoiding Haram Earn Reward?
Refining the concept of reward for avoiding haram.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between passive avoidance of haram and active self-restraint (nahy al-nafs), and apply the Hanafi usul rule that only wajib acts earn reward.

Core Idea
Mere avoidance of sin earns no reward; only struggling against temptation is a wajib that merits reward.

A common question arises: if avoiding sin is good, does it earn reward? An objector cites a Quranic verse promising Paradise to those who avoid major sins. The Hanafi commentator refines this: mere omission—like not committing zina while studying—cannot be rewarded, or we would receive infinite rewards each moment. Reward is only for an obligatory act, such as restraining oneself when all temptations are present. This distinction is critical in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the flawed inference
The objector assumes that avoiding haram always earns reward, citing a verse. Note the logical problem: if every moment of avoidance earned reward, reward would be infinite and meaningless.
This is a methodological error: conflating passive omission with active obedience.
2
Locate the author's refinement
The commentator distinguishes between 'tark al-haram' (mere avoidance) and 'nahy al-nafs 'inda tahiya al-asbab' (restraining the soul when means are ready). Only the latter is a wajib act and earns reward.
The author includes the latter as a wajib in the chapter on obligations.
3
Apply the distinction
Test any scenario: is the person merely not sinning, or are they actively resisting desire out of fear of Allah? The former is neutral, the latter is rewarded.
This principle prevents overcounting reward and clarifies the nature of obedience.

Distinguishing Imkan Khas and Imkan Am in Hanafi Usul
Linguistic investigation of jawaz in the fourth and fifth categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between imkan khas and imkan am and apply the correct meaning of jawaz in Hanafi classifications of actions based on contextual contrast.

Core Idea
Jawaz in usul shifts between imkan khas and imkan am to contrast with wujub or hurma.

This passage investigates the linguistic precision of the term 'jawaz' in two key categories of actions. It distinguishes between imkan khas (both sides equally possible) and imkan am (one side not necessary) and shows how the correct interpretation of jawaz depends on its contrast with wujub or hurma. The objection and response sharpen your ability to identify contextual clues.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of imkan
Note that imkan khas removes necessity from both sides (doing/not-doing are equal), while imkan am removes necessity from only one side. Imkan am is further subdivided based on which side remains possible.
Imkan am muqayyad bi janib al-wujud: existence possible; imkan am muqayyad bi janib al-'adam: non-existence possible.
2
Understand the contextual use of jawaz in the fourth form
In 'mā lahā wa mā 'alayhā' (what is permissible and obligatory for her), jawaz must contrast with wujub. This requires imkan khas, because imkan khas excludes necessity – both sides are possible. If jawaz meant imkan am, haram would enter, breaking the contrast.
The fourth form pairs jawaz with wujub; thus jawaz must exclude any hint of necessity.
3
Understand the contextual use of jawaz in the fifth form
In the fifth form (what is permissible and prohibited for her), jawaz contrasts with hurma (prohibition). Here jawaz must be imkan am muqayyad bi janib al-wujud, because hurma implies necessity of non-existence; to contrast, jawaz must allow existence. Imkan khas would exclude wajib, making the form incorrect.
The fifth form pairs jawaz with hurma; thus jawaz must include acts whose existence is possible (including wajib).
4
Analyze the objection and response
The objector claims that if jawaz means non-prevention of both act and omission (imkan khas), then doing other than haram (which includes wajib) would be permissible, but wajib is not merely permissible. The responder clarifies that the context (explicit mention of obligation) specifies that the wajib act falls under the 'obligatory' side, not the 'permissible' side.
This shows that a term's meaning can be narrowed by the context of the discussion.
5
Apply the definitions to new examples
Practice distinguishing imkan khas and imkan am in other Hanafi classifications, such as acts that are mubah (equal possibility) vs. mustahabb (possibility with preference). Recognize when jawaz is used in a technical usul sense versus a fiqh ruling sense.
These definitions are foundational for understanding the Hanafi categories of actions (ahkam al-khamsah).

Why 'min adillatiha' Modifies 'Ilm' Not 'Ahkam'
Explaining that 'min adillatiha' modifies 'ilm' not 'ahkam' to exclude muqallid's knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the phrase 'min adillatiha' in the Hanafi definition of fiqh must modify 'ilm' (knowledge) rather than 'ahkam' (rulings) and identify that this excludes the muqallid's knowledge from being considered fiqh.

Core Idea
Fiqh is knowledge directly derived from detailed evidences; the muqallid's knowledge, though correct, is not fiqh.

In defining fiqh, the Hanafi school uses the phrase 'min adillatiha' (from its evidences). A misunderstanding can arise if one reads this phrase as modifying 'ahkam' (rulings). This passage clarifies that it must modify 'ilm' (knowledge), thereby excluding the muqallid – one who follows a mujtahid without personally deriving rulings. Understanding this nuanced point sharpens your grasp of what qualifies as fiqh in the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and the contested phrase
Recall the Hanafi definition: al-fiqh huwa al-'ilm bi al-ahkam al-shar'iyyah al-'amaliyyah al-muktasab min adillatiha al-tafsiliyyah. The key phrase 'min adillatiha' is at the end.
The phrase can theoretically attach to 'ahkam' or to 'ilm'.
2
Recognize the consequence of attaching to 'ahkam'
If 'min adillatiha' modifies 'ahkam', then it would read 'knowledge of the rulings that come from their detailed evidences'. The muqallid knows such rulings (since he knows the mujtahid's derived rulings), so he would not be excluded.
This would defeat the purpose, as fiqh is meant for mujtahids.
3
Analyze the two arguments for attachment to 'ilm'
First: the evidence produces knowledge of the thing, not the thing itself. Second: rulings as divine address are eternal and cannot be 'obtained' from evidence. Therefore, 'min adillatiha' must attach to 'ilm', meaning only knowledge directly obtained from evidence counts as fiqh.
The muqallid's knowledge is based on the mujtahid's statement, not direct evidence.
4
Conclude the exclusion of the muqallid
Since the muqallid does not look into the detailed evidences himself, his knowledge is not 'muktasab min adillatiha al-tafsiliyyah'. Thus he does not have fiqh in the technical sense.
This does not demean the muqallid; it simply defines the term precisely.

Grasp the Base Argument
The base text argues: if an attribute exists due to another (non-essence), then either infinite regress occurs or the essence is completed by an external other. Both are impossible. This sets up the necessity of attributes being essential.
Infinite regress (tasalsul) is invalid; completion by external other implies deficiency.
4
Analyze the Objection to the Base Argument
An objector asks: what does 'another' mean? If it means an external separate entity, it's not necessary that the attribute attaches through it; if it means another attribute, then the need of some attributes for others does not imply deficiency in the essence. Appreciate the subtlety.
The objector distinguishes between two meanings of 'ghair' (other).
5
See the Commentator's Resolution
The commentator resolves by specifying: 'other' in the first argument refers to an external thing, leading to the defect of completion by another; in the preceding part (not quoted), 'other' referred to an attribute, leading to infinite regress. Together, the two senses force the conclusion that attributes must terminate at the essence.
By combining both arguments, the proof of essential attributes is complete.

Question: It is possible that the end of this attribute is not upon the essence but upon the primary essential accident; therefore multiplicity will not be proven, because the primary essential accident is one. Even if we accept multiplicity, there is no diversity, meaning they are not different in kind. Answer: When it is accidental by means of the primary essential accident, it will also be an essential accident, because that which attaches to the essence through an intermediary that is equal is also essential like the equal. When different kinds of accidents are established in one locus, they will inevitably be diverse, because different individuals cannot exist in one locus; individuation is established by difference of locus; being present in one locus will establish different species, as in Zayd's mind many knowledges are of different species because the locus is one. The knowledges present in the minds of Zayd, Amr, and Bakr will be individuals because the loci are different.
The commentator said: His saying: "And because it requires" is conjoined to the content of previous speech, meaning: If the attribute belongs to another, it is invalid because it necessitates the completion of the real One in His attributes by another, which is impossible because it causes deficiency in His essence and neediness in His perfections. And there is consideration in this: If by completion by another is meant a separate thing, then it is apparently not necessary, as it is possible that some attributes attach to other attributes. And if what is meant is more general, including the separate and the attribute, then we do not accept that the need of some attributes for others causes deficiency in the essence, for creation depends on knowledge, power, and will. And it is possible to make this specific to what the other is separate, and what preceded is specific to what the other is not separate; thus by their combination the desired is achieved, namely proving another essential attribute.
Commentary: They want to show the structure: "Because it requires" is conjoined to the content of the previous speech, meaning: If the attribute attaches due to another, first infinite regress would be necessary, and second, completion by another would be necessary, which is impossible because it entails deficiency in the essence and neediness in perfection. "And there is consideration..." etc. is an objection: If by completion by another you mean a separate thing, then it is not necessary that it be through the attachment of an attribute. And if by another you mean general, which includes the separate and the attribute, then we do not accept the deficiency, because some attributes are dependent on others, like creation depends on knowledge, power, and will. "It is possible..." etc. gives the answer: Here by "other" is meant a separate thing, due to which the defect of completion by another is necessary, while in the preceding part by "other" was meant the attribute, due to which the defect of infinite regress in principles was necessary. To stop infinite regress in attributes, ending at the essence is necessary; thus by the combination of both, the objective, i.e., proof of an essential attribute, is achieved.

Commentary
This passage is an advanced commentary from Dars-e-Talweeh on a passage in Al-Tawdih wa Al-Talwih regarding the proof of additional essential attributes (arad dhatiyya) in God. The discussion arises from an objection: perhaps an attribute does not terminate at the essence directly but at a primary essential accident (arad dhati awwal). If so, multiplicity of attributes would not be established because the primary accident is one, and even if multiple, they would not differ in type. The commentator (the sharh) responds: if an attribute attaches through the primary accident, it remains essential because whatever attaches via an intermediary equal to the essence is also essential. Different kinds of attributes in one locus produce diversity of types, just as multiple sciences in one mind differ in type while the same science in different minds differs in individual. Then the commentator explains the text: the earlier part argues that if an attribute is due to another (non-self), it leads to either infinite regress or completion by an external other—both impossible. An objection is raised: if 'other' means a separate external thing, it is not necessary; if it also includes attributes, the need of some attributes for others does not imply deficiency in the essence. The commentator resolves the confusion by distinguishing two uses of 'other': in the first argument it means an external thing, leading to deficiency; in the second it means another attribute, leading to infinite regress. Together, these two arguments force the conclusion that there must be an essential attribute that terminates at the essence, not at another attribute or external thing. This is a classic usul/istinbat exercise demonstrating how rational proofs (dalil aqli) are combined to establish a theological tenet.

Key Terms
عرض ذاتی (arad dhati): Essential accident — an attribute that inheres in the essence necessarily and is not caused externally.
تسلسل (tasalsul): Infinite regress — an endless chain of causes, considered impossible in proving attributes.
استکمال بالغیر (istikmal bil-ghair): Completion by another — the idea that God's attributes could be completed by an external cause, implying deficiency.
محل (mahall): Locus — the substratum in which accidents inhere; here, the essence or the mind.
