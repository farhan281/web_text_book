Three Types of Beginning and Reconciling Hadiths via Grammatical Analysis
Commentary on the grammatical construction of basmalah and definition of three types of beginning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of beginning (ibtida') in Hanafi usul and explain how the commentator uses the grammatical concept of hal to reconcile two apparently conflicting hadiths.

Core Idea
Reconciling conflicting hadiths requires understanding the types of beginning: real, customary, and relative.

This lesson delves into the opening lines of Al-Tawdih, where the author Sadr al-Shariah uses an unusual grammatical construction—placing 'hamid' (praising) as a circumstantial accusative (hal) instead of the conventional nominal or verbal sentence. The commentator explains that this choice is a deliberate reconciliation of two hadiths: one commanding beginning with Bismillah and another commanding beginning with praise. To understand this, we must first grasp the three types of beginning (ibtida') that the commentator introduces: real, customary, and relative. This grammatical-legal analysis is a hallmark of Hanafi usul methodology, where linguistic precision serves to harmonize textual proofs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the grammatical construction of the basmalah
The author writes 'Bismillah al-Rahman al-Rahim, hamid'—'hamid' is in the accusative as a hal (circumstantial) from the hidden pronoun in the preposition's object, meaning 'With the name of Allah I begin the book, praising Him.' Recognize that this differs from the common opening forms like 'al-hamdu lillah' (nominal) or 'ahmadu Allah' (verbal).
Hal describes the state of the subject (the hidden 'I') at the time of beginning.
2
Recognize the problem: two apparently conflicting hadiths
The commentator cites two hadiths: 'Every matter of importance that does not begin with Bismillah is maimed (abtar)' and 'Every matter of importance that does not begin with praise is truncated (ajdham).' Literally, one cannot fulfill both at the very start because only one thing can be first.
These hadiths are from different collections; the commentator treats them as equally authoritative.
3
Understand the solution: using hal to combine both
By making 'hamid' a hal, the author places Bismillah as the real beginning (ibtida' haqiqi) and praise as a customary or relative beginning (ibtida' 'urfi or idafi). Thus, both hadiths are fulfilled: the invocation is first in reality, and praise accompanies the beginning as a description of the agent's state.
The hal construction allows praise to be linked to the verbal action without being the first word.
4
Distinguish the three types of ibtida'
The commentator defines: (1) Real beginning (ibtida' haqiqi)—first in every respect, e.g., the letter Alif in the alphabet. (2) Customary beginning (ibtida' 'urfi)—what people customarily consider as the beginning, e.g., initial lessons of a book. (3) Relative beginning (ibtida' idafi)—prior to some things but later than others, e.g., being ahead in some respects but behind in others.
These categories are from usul and logic, not directly from the hadiths themselves.
5
Apply the classification to the basmalah and hamd
Bismillah is the real beginning (ibtida' haqiqi) because it is the first uttered word. 'Hamid' is a customary/relative beginning: it is not the first word but is associated with the beginning through the hal construction, fulfilling the hadith of praise. Thus, both hadiths are honored.
This shows how Hanafi usul uses linguistic analysis to resolve apparent textual conflicts.

The author of Al-Tawdih said: 'In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful, praising Allah' — 'praising' is a circumstantial accusative (hal) from the hidden pronoun in the preposition's object, i.e., 'With the name of Allah I begin the book, praising Him.' He chose the hal construction over what is customary among them, the nominal sentence or the verbal sentence, like 'al-hamdu lillah' or 'ahmadu Allah,' to equalize between praise and invocation, and to observe appropriateness between them. For it has come in hadith: 'Every matter of importance that does not begin with Bismillah is maimed, and every matter of importance that does not begin with praise is truncated.' So he tried to make praise a condition of the beginning, as a hal from it, just as the invocation occurred that way. However, he put the invocation first because the two texts are apparently conflicting, since beginning with one of the two precludes beginning with the other. It was possible to combine by putting one before the other, so the beginning is with it in reality and with the other in addition to what is other than it. Thus he acted according to the text that brings the invocation first, and the consensus held upon it. Linguistic research: 'al-mustakinn' lexically means hidden; here it means the hidden pronoun. 'Taswiyah' is the source of the verb 'sawwa' (to equalize), and it is an object for 'athara'. 'Ri'ayah' is coordinated with 'taswiyah'. 'The beginning with it in reality': Beginning is of three types: real beginning (ibtida' haqiqi), customary beginning (ibtida' 'urfi), and relative beginning (ibtida' idafi). Customary beginning: a thing is before the intended purpose, because people customarily count it as the beginning, like if you are still studying the initial lessons of Talwih and are asked 'How much of the book have you read?', you would answer 'I am still at the beginning.' Relative beginning: a thing is prior to some things even if it is later than others; it is also counted as a beginning. Then, just as 'Bismillah' together with its object (mutalliq) is a hal, similarly he made 'hamid' also a hal so that there is proportion. Commentary
This passage is the commentary (sharh) on the beginning of Al-Tawdih. It analyzes why the author (Sadr al-Shariah) used the word 'hamid' (praising) as a circumstantial accusative (hal) instead of the conventional nominal or verbal opening. The reason is to reconcile two hadiths: one requiring beginning with 'Bismillah' and another requiring beginning with praise. By placing 'Bismillah' at the start (real beginning) and making 'hamid' a hal (customary or relative beginning), both hadiths are honored. The commentary then provides linguistic definitions: 'al-mustakinn' (hidden pronoun), nominal vs. verbal sentence, and the types of 'ibtida'' (beginning): real, customary, and relative. It explains with examples: real beginning (letter Alif), customary (still in initial lessons), relative (prior to some but not all). Finally, a question-answer section clarifies why the author chose the unusual hal form: to equalize between the two hadiths and maintain symmetry with 'Bismillah' also being in hal form. This is typical Hanafi usul methodology—harmonizing conflicting proofs by a nuanced grammatical analysis.

Key Terms
ابتداء حقیقی (ibtida' haqiqi): Real beginning: a thing that is first in every respect, with no prior element, like the letter Alif in the alphabet.
ابتداء عرفی (ibtida' 'urfi): Customary beginning: what is considered a beginning by common custom, even if there is a prior element, e.g., initial lessons of a book.
ابتداء اضافی (ibtida' idafi): Relative beginning: a thing is prior to some things but later than others; counted as beginning relative to those after.
حال (hal): A circumstantial accusative; a word indicating the state of the subject when the action occurs.

Explaining 'Al-Kalim' with 'Min Mahamid' and the Hadith of Praises
Sharh explaining 'min mahamid' as a hal with hadith proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how the commentator uses a hadith to specify the meaning of 'al-kalim' and distinguish between hamd and shukr in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
The indefinite plural 'min mahamid' specifies the general 'al-kalim' as verbal praises, proven by a hadith.

This passage from the sharh explains why the commentator uses the phrase 'min mahamid' as a circumstantial qualifier (hal) for 'al-kalim'. By citing a hadith that lists specific words of praise, the commentator demonstrates that 'al-kalim' in context refers to these utterances. The lesson unpacks the linguistic reasoning and the distinction between verbal praise (hamd) and broader gratitude (shukr), key to understanding Hanafi usul's approach to textual specification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the grammatical structure
Recognize that 'min mahamid' is a hal (circumstantial qualifier) modifying 'al-kalim'. This construction provides a specific description of the general term.
A hal typically answers 'how?' or 'in what state?' and here it restricts the general 'al-kalim' to praises.
2
Understand the hadith's role
The commentator quotes a hadith where the Prophet lists the words 'SubhanAllah, Alhamdulillah, La ilaha illaAllah, Allahu Akbar' and describes their ascent. This hadith serves as evidence that 'al-kalim' refers to these verbal praises.
The hadith is a khabar wahid (solitary report), which Hanafi usul accepts as valid for explaining a mujmal (ambiguous) text.
3
Analyze the indefinite plural 'mahamid'
The indefinite plural is chosen because an indefinite noun can become general by description, as in 'a Kufic mirror' (a type of mirror). The indefiniteness also indicates multiplicity, suiting the generalization of 'al-kalim'.
This principle is Hanafi linguistic usul: 'al-nakirah ta'mmu bi al-wasf' (the indefinite becomes general by description).
4
Distinguish hamd from shukr
The commentator defines hamd as verbal praise for a benefit, while shukr includes verbal, practical, and belief-based gratitude. Since hamd is purely verbal, it is the appropriate description for 'al-kalim', which consists of spoken words.
This distinction is crucial in Hanafi usul: shukr is broader and involves the whole being, while hamd is just the tongue.

The Priority of Sunnah over Ijma: Definitive Sunnah Takes Precedence
Answering an objection about the relative hierarchy of Sunnah and Ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that definitive Sunnah precedes Ijma and explain the exception for non-definitive Sunnah.

Core Idea
Sunnah takes precedence over Ijma only when its transmission is definitive; doubt delays it.

This passage addresses a critical question in Hanafi usul: does the Sunnah always come before Ijma? The commentator clarifies that the order holds only for definitive Sunnah. Understanding this nuance prevents misapplication in fiqh when conflicting evidences arise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The question challenges the absolute order of sources: is Sunnah always before Ijma?
The objector claims the order is conditional.
2
Recognize the condition
The condition is that the Sunnah must be definitive (qat'i al-thubut). The commentator specifies 'matn al-sunnah' meaning the established text with certainty.
Definitive means mutawatir or mashhur.
3
Understand the exception
If there is doubt (zann) in authenticity, the Sunnah may be delayed. This occurs with ahad reports.
Ahad hadith lack certainty of transmission.
4
Apply the consequence
In practice, a qat'i hadith prevails over a claimed ijma; but an ahad hadith may be outweighed by ijma.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Sunnah precedes Ijma only when it is qat'i; otherwise, Ijma takes precedence.

If the Sunnah is zanni al-thubut (e.g., khabar wahid), then Ijma takes precedence because Ijma is qat'i.
This is because a zanni proof cannot override a qat'i proof.
4

The basis is the usul principle that qat'i proofs override zanni ones.
Ijma is considered qat'i due to the infallibility of the ummah's consensus.

Fasl al-Khitab and the Primacy of Sunnah Qawliyya
Explaining the term 'fasl al-khitab' and the rule that Sunnah is primarily qawl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi usul principle that Sunnah qawliyya takes precedence over Sunnah fi'liyya and identify the linguistic tool 'atf al-khass ala al-amm' used to emphasize this.

Core Idea
Sunnah qawliyya is the primary source for sharia because speech is definitive, while actions may be ambiguous.

In this passage, the commentator of Al-Tawdih and Al-Talwih unpacks the term 'fasl al-khitab' and highlights a key Hanafi usul principle: the verbal Sunnah (qawliyya) is the foundational basis for deriving most sharia rulings. He uses the rhetorical device of 'atf al-khass ala al-amm' (conjoining the specific to the general) to underscore the elevated status of prophetic speech over action. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correct istinbat (derivation) of ahkam.

Guided Path
1
Parse 'fasl al-khitab'
The commentator explains that 'fasl' is a verbal noun. It can mean either 'decisive' (active participle) or 'distinct' (passive participle). In both senses, it describes the Prophet's speech as clear and distinguishing truth from falsehood.
This dual reading shows the richness of the Arabic language.
2
Identify the conjunction (atf) as specific to general
The phrase 'fasl khitabih' is conjoined to the Sunnah of the Prophet. Since Sunnah includes both speech and action, and 'khitab' only refers to speech, this is an example of 'atf al-khass ala al-amm' – specifying a particular part of the general category.
Such conjunctions are rhetorically meaningful.
3
Explain the purpose of this conjunction
The purpose is to highlight the greatness and importance of Sunnah qawliyya. Sharia regulations are always established by explicit speech, and the authority of prophetic statements is universally accepted.
This is a methodological point in Hanafi usul.
4
Contrast Sunnah qawliyya and fi'liyya
Sunnah fi'liyya is subject to interpretation: the action might have been performed for a specific reason (e.g., necessity) and may not necessarily indicate a general ruling. Speech, on the other hand, is clear and definitive.
For example, the Prophet urinating while standing does not legislate the act; it may have been due to a medical reason.
5
Connect to broader Hanafi methodology
Hanafi usul prioritizes clear texts (nass) over ambiguous indications. The primacy of Sunnah qawliyya aligns with this: verbal commands from the Prophet are considered explicit (zahir) and thus carry greater weight.
This principle affects how Hanafi scholars evaluate hadith.

Then he notes that the phrase 'fasl khitabih' is conjoined to the Sunnah as a specific to a general (عطف الخاص على العام) to highlight the importance of verbal Sunnah (Sunnah qawliyya). The reason is that sharia rulings are primarily established through speech (Quran and Prophetic statements) which are definitive and universally accepted. In contrast, actions (Sunnah fi'liyya) can be ambiguous and may have been done for reasons other than legislation (e.g., urinating while standing due to necessity). Therefore, the primary source for deriving ahkam is the Sunnah qawliyya. Finally, the commentator comments on the phrase 'ma rafa'' meaning that rulings established by ijma' (consensus) are firmly raised and cannot be lowered, indicating the binding nature of ijma'.

The Objection to 'Zuhur' in the Definition of Fiqh
Presents fourth objection to 'zuhur' condition in definition of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the two interpretations of 'zuhur' in the definition of fiqh, and state why each leads to a logical problem in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
The condition of 'zuhur' (appearance) in defining fiqh is ambiguous: if it means universal knowledge, it is contradicted by the Companions; if it means majority prevalence, it is imprecise.

This passage presents a fourth objection to a definition that requires 'zuhur' (appearance) of revelation as a condition for a ruling to be considered fiqh. The commentator unpacks two possible meanings of 'zuhur' and shows that both lead to logical difficulties. Understanding this objection clarifies the Hanafi usul position on what constitutes fiqh and the role of widespread knowledge (shuhrah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two interpretations
The objection posits two possible meanings of 'zuhur': (1) universal knowledge among all people, and (2) predominant knowledge among the majority. Recognize both.
2
Analyze the first interpretation
If 'zuhur' means all people know the ruling, then many Companions (e.g., those who referred to Aisha) would not be jurists, which is false. The Companions' ignorance of some rulings did not affect their faqahat.
Connect to the example of Aisha being consulted.
3
Analyze the second interpretation
If 'zuhur' means the majority knows, then it is imprecise because of the large number of narrators and their dispersion. Moreover, a ruling from a solitary narration would not be fiqh until it becomes widespread, which is problematic.
Note the difficulty of determining when a ruling becomes 'widespread.'
4
Consider the consequence
Both interpretations render the definition defective. The definition fails to capture the reality of fiqh as understood by the Companions and the early scholars.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth objection concerning the term 'zuhur' (appearance) in the definition. If it means universal knowledge among all people, then the Companions themselves often did not know certain rulings (e.g., they consulted Aisha), yet they were still considered jurists. If it means prevalent knowledge among the majority, then this is vague and practically unworkable. Moreover, it would mean that a ruling from a solitary narration is not fiqh until it becomes widely known, which is problematic.

Key Terms
ظہور (zuhur): Appearance or prevalence of a ruling; ambiguous whether it means universal or majority knowledge.
فقاہت (faqahat): Juristic ability or understanding of fiqh.
