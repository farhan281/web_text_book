Four Types of Metaphor in Usul al-Fiqh
Explaining the extended metaphor from the niche and lamp

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of isti'arah (metaphor) in a legal text and explain how each functions in the interpretation of the sharh.

Core Idea
Metaphor types reveal the key lexical operations used to interpret legal texts in Hanafi usul.

The commentator expands the opening statement of the sharh with a multi-layered metaphor. Understanding the four types of isti'arah – makniyyah, musarrahah, takhyiliyyah, and tarshiḥiyyah – trains students to analyse the deep linguistic structure of legal language. This lesson unpacks each type and shows how they are identified in the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the metaphorical construction
Locate the key words in the sharh statement that form the extended metaphor: 'mishkāt' (niche), 'awqada' (kindled), 'sirāj' (lamp), and 'sunnah' (Sunnah).
The entire phrase is a single extended metaphor, but each word carries a distinct type.
2
Distinguish makniyyah versus musarrahah
For each word, check whether the mentioned term is the mushabbah (the thing being described) or the mushabbah bihi (the thing it is compared to). Makniyyah mentions mushabbah; musarrahah mentions mushabbah bihi.
In 'sunnah', the mushabbah is mentioned, so it is makniyyah. In 'sirāj', the mushabbah bihi (lamp) is mentioned, so it is musarrahah.
3
Recognise takhyiliyyah
If the word attributes a necessary characteristic of the mushabbah bihi to the mushabbah, it is takhyiliyyah. For example, 'mishkāt' (niche) assigns the property of containing to the Prophet's chest, which is a feature of a niche for a lamp.
Takhyiliyyah often involves concrete attributes transferred to an abstract concept.
4
Recognise tarshiḥiyyah
If the word assigns a suitable aspect or action of the mushabbah bihi to the mushabbah, it is tarshiḥiyyah. For 'awqada' (kindled), the action of kindling is suitable for a lamp, and it is attributed to the act of illuminating the Sunnah.
Tarshiḥiyyah often involves verbs or adjectives that fit the comparison.
5
Apply the classification to the text
Match each key word to its metaphor type as explicitly given in the passage: sunnah → makniyyah, mishkāt → takhyiliyyah, awqada → tarshiḥiyyah, sirāj → musarrahah.
Confirm your classification with the passage's 'Benefit' section.

Reconciling Conflicting Hadiths: The Hanafi Principle of al-Jam‘ bayna al-Nassayn
Commentator explains the grammatical-legal rationale behind an unusual opening phrase

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi usul principle of al-jam‘ bayna al-nassayn, identify when it applies, and apply it to reconcile two apparently contradictory hadiths using grammatical tools.

Core Idea
Reconciliation (jam‘) is preferred over abrogation: two conflicting texts can be harmonized without cancelling either.

In this lesson, we examine how a Hanafi commentator reconciled two hadiths—one requiring beginning important matters with 'Bismillah' and another requiring beginning with praise. Using the grammatical device of the circumstantial accusative (hal), he showed that both requirements can be fulfilled in a single act. This is a classic application of the usul principle that reconciliation (jam‘) takes precedence over abrogation (naskh) when possible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent conflict
Two hadiths each command beginning important matters: one with basmalah, one with hamd. Starting with one seems to omit the other, creating an apparent contradiction (ta'arud).
The hadiths: 'كل أمر ذى بال لم يبدأ فيه ببسم الله فهو أبتر' and 'كل أمر ذى بال لم يبدأ فيه بالحمد فهو أجذم'.
2
State the Hanafi preference for jam‘
Hanafi usul prioritizes reconciling (jam‘) over declaring one text abrogated (naskh) or giving preference (tarjih) when a plausible reconciliation exists.
This is based on the principle that it is better to act on both texts than to discard one.
3
Analyze the commentator's solution
The commentator uses the grammatical form 'حامدا' (praising) as a circumstantial accusative (hal) attached to the implied verb 'أبتدئ' (I begin). This makes praise a condition/description of the beginning, while the actual start is with the basmalah.
Thus the basmalah is the literal beginning (ibtida' haqiqi), and the hamd is included contextually via the hal.
4
Evaluate the proof
The solution relies on the dalal of the hadiths (their apparent command) and the consensus that basmalah takes precedence. The grammatical hal allows both commands to be fulfilled without contradiction.
The commentator also notes that the two hadiths have zanni thubut but qati' dalala.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Given two conflicting commands in fiqh (e.g., one stating a condition and another stating a different condition), try to reconcile them by identifying a grammatical or contextual link that allows both to be observed.
Always prefer jam‘ over naskh when possible.

Commentary
This passage is the commentary on the basmalah and hamdalah that begin the matn (Al-Tawdih). The commentator (the author of Al-Talwih) explains why the author of Al-Tawdih chose the unusual phrasing 'بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم حامدا' (In the name of Allah, the Most Merciful, the Most Compassionate, praising). Normally, one might say 'بسم الله الرحمن الرحيم الحمد لله' or 'أحمد الله' as separate sentences. By using 'حامدا' (praising) as a circumstantial accusative (hal) connected to the implied verb 'أبتدئ' (I begin), he unifies the act of beginning with both praising and saying 'Bismillah'. This reconciles two hadiths: one requiring beginning with 'Bismillah' and another requiring beginning with praise. Since they appear contradictory, the author combines them by making one the actual beginning (the basmalah) and the other a condition/description (the hamd). He relies on the text (Quran?) that gives precedence to basmalah, and on consensus. The commentator then gives a linguistic analysis of the terms 'mustakin', 'jumla ismiyya/fi'liyya', and explains that 'ibtida' haqiqi' means something that is truly the first in every respect, like the letter alif in the alphabet. This discussion shows the care with which the Hanafi usul scholars analyzed even the opening formulas of their works, applying usul principles to textual structure.

Key Terms
الحال (al-hal): A circumstantial accusative that describes the state of the subject or object when the action occurs; here 'حامدا' is the hal.
ابتداء حقيقي (ibtida' haqiqi): Real beginning: when something is first in every respect, without any precedence of another.
الجمع بين النصين (al-jam' bayna al-nassayn): Reconciling two apparently contradictory texts by giving precedence to one over the other with a common interpretation.

Resolving the Apparent Conflict Between Basmalah and Hamd: Types of Initiation and Authorial Intent
Commentary on the author's opening wording and reconciliation of two hadiths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of ibtida (haqiqi, urfi, idafi) and explain how the Hanafi author reconciled the two hadiths on beginning with basmalah and hamd.

Core Idea
Basmalah hadith = real initiation; hamd hadith = customary initiation — reconcilable via textual analysis and consensus.

When two prophetic hadiths command beginning important matters with both basmalah and hamd, how does an author decide which to place first? This passage from a classical sharh unveils a sophisticated solution: the hadiths apply to different types of initiation. In this lesson, we will dissect the three types of ibtida, follow the author's grammatical and stylistic choices, and see how he balances textual evidence with the consensus of the ummah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of ibtida
The commentator defines real, customary, and additional initiation. Real initiation is the absolute first; customary is relative to the task; additional is prior to some things but not others.
The example with 'reading Talweeh' illustrates customary initiation.
2
Analyze the author's grammatical structure
Understand why 'hamidan' is a hal (circumstantial) related to the hidden pronoun in 'abtadi'. The author deliberately chose a hal over the usual nominal or verbal sentence.
The hal construction creates equality between basmalah and hamd.
3
Reconcile the two hadiths
The basmalah hadith is applied to real initiation; the hamd hadith to customary initiation. This avoids contradiction while fulfilling both commands.
The Qur'anic order and practical consensus (ijma amali) further support placing basmalah first.
4
Examine the omission of the conjunction 'wa'
The author omitted 'wa' before 'hamidan' to prevent hamd from appearing subordinate to basmalah, preserving the intended equality.
The commentator also discusses grammatical nuances about the hal's subject in different manuscript versions.

Resolving the Impermissibility of Combining Haqiqah and Majaz in One Word
Answering the objection that the word 'ḥāmidan' would combine literal and figurative meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that haqiqah and majaz cannot be combined in a single word, and identify the technique of positing an omitted repetition to resolve apparent conflicts in scriptural interpretation.

Core Idea
A single word cannot carry both literal and figurative meanings; an omitted repetition preserves each meaning separately.

This passage from a commentary on al-Talweeh addresses a subtle objection: if the word 'ḥāmidan' in the third interpretation is meant both literally (praising now) and figuratively (intending future praise), that would combine haqiqah and majaz in one word, which is impermissible in Hanafi usul. The commentator shows that the correct reading posits a second instance of the word, so each carries one meaning. This teaches us a key method for resolving linguistic ambiguities in sacred texts.

Identify the objection
The objector claims that according to the third interpretation, 'ḥāmidan' must relate to both 'aūlā' (actual praise now) and 'thāniyā' (intention of future praise), thus requiring one word to carry literal and figurative meanings simultaneously.
This would violate a fundamental Hanafi linguistic principle.

State the Hanafi principle
In Hanafi usul, a word cannot be used in both its literal (ḥaqīqah) and figurative (majāz) meanings at the same time in a single occurrence.
The principle is derived from the need for clear and unambiguous expression in legal texts.

The commentator resolves the issue by positing an omitted (or understood) repetition of the word 'ḥāmidan' before 'thāniyā'. Thus, the full expression is 'ḥāmidan aūlā wa ḥāmidan thāniyā', with the first being literal and the second figurative.

Prohibition of Simultaneous Haqiqah and Majaz from One Word
Resolution of an objection about haqiqah and majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a single word cannot be used simultaneously in its real and metaphorical meanings (haqiqah and majaz) according to Hanafi usul, and identify the solution of implied repetition when grammatical context would otherwise force a violation.

Core Idea
A single word cannot carry both real and metaphorical meanings at once; if context demands both, an implied repetition resolves the conflict.

This lesson examines a subtle but important rule in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the prohibition of using one word in both its literal (haqiqah) and figurative (majaz) senses in a single instance. The passage comes from a commentary on a matn where the phrase 'حامدا أولا و ثانيا' raises a potential violation. The commentator shows how to preserve the rule through an implied repetition. Understanding this rule sharpens your ability to analyse textual ambiguities and avoid misinterpretations in legal texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic constraint
Note that 'حامدا' as a haal (circumstantial adverb) must show concurrence (muqaranat) with its factor (عامل). For 'أولا' (in this world), actual praise is concurrent; for 'ثانيا' (in the afterlife), only the intention of praise can be concurrent.
The factor (عامل) is implicit in the phrase, meaning 'أحمد الله' or similar.
2
Recognise the usul principle
Recall the Hanafi rule: It is impermissible to intend both haqiqah and majaz from a single word in one usage. A word must be assigned to only one category of meaning.
This principle is qat'i in Hanafi usul; violating it renders the interpretation invalid.
3
Understand the problem
If 'حامدا' is used once for both 'أولا' and 'ثانيا', the word would have to mean both 'praising (literally)' for the first and 'intending to praise (metaphorically)' for the second. This would violate the principle.
The apparent defect is not in the phrase itself but in the interpretation.
4
Apply the commentator's solution
Assume an implied second 'حامدا' before 'ثانيا', so the underlying phrase is 'حامدا أولا و حامدا ثانيا'. The first 'حامدا' takes haqiqah, the second takes majaz.
Implied repetition (تقدير) is a common tool in Arabic grammar to avoid prohibited usages.
5
Confirm the ruling
With the implied repetition, the phrase is valid and the prohibition is avoided. Each word carries only one type of meaning, preserving the usul principle.
This resolution shows how Hanafi scholars reconcile apparent conflicts between grammar and usul.

Grammatical analysis of prepositional usage in a matn commentary.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two justifications (tasamuh and tadmin) for the prepositional usage of 'waffaqa' with 'ba' and apply the concept of tadmin to analyze similar grammatical constructions in usul texts.

Core Idea
The use of 'ba' with 'waffaqa' is not an error; it is justified by either tasamuh or tadmin.

When reading classical usul texts, you may encounter prepositions that seem unusual. This passage from the Talweeh commentary addresses exactly such a case: the verb 'waffaqa' usually takes 'lam', but the author used 'ba'. The commentator offers two sophisticated justifications rooted in Arabic linguistics. Understanding these will sharpen your ability to parse and interpret the precise language of usul works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent problem
Recognize that the verb 'waffaqa' normally governs its object with the preposition 'lam' (تُوُفِّقَ لِكِتابَةِ الكِتاب). The author uses 'ba' instead ('bi-ta'lifi al-kitab'). This seems like a grammatical anomaly that needs explanation.
The expected construction is 'waffaqani li-ta'lifi al-kitab'.
2
Evaluate the first answer (tasamuh)
The commentator first suggests that the author may have taken leniency (tasamuh) with prepositions, prioritizing overall meaning over strict grammatical rules. This is common among scholars when the meaning remains clear.
Tasamuh implies the author did not consider the change worth attention.
3
Evaluate the second answer (tadmin)
The commentator then offers a more rigorous justification: tadmin (implication). Here, an implied verb (e.g., 'mushrifan', honoring) is supposed to govern the 'ba' phrase, while 'waffaqa' retains its literal meaning and governs its own preposition 'lam'. The full original phrase would be 'lima waffaqani Allah mushrifan bi-ta'lifi al-kitab'.
Tadmin preserves the original verb's valency and avoids metaphorical interpretation.
4
Compare the two justifications
Tasamuh is more practical; tadmin is more precise. The commentator presents both, showing flexibility in interpreting the author's language.
Both are acceptable in scholarly analysis.
5
Apply the concept generally
In any usul text, when you see a verb with an unexpected preposition, first consider tasamuh (leniency for meaning) and then look for an implied verb via tadmin. This method respects the text and avoids hasty criticism.
Practice this skill by examining other examples in the Talweeh.

Implicit Reference and the Sunnah of Tasmiyah
Answering an objection about the basmalah placement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how Hanafi usul validates implicit reference before explicit mention (idmar qabl dhikr) in textual contexts and apply the principle that intention (niyyah) suffices for acting upon the Sunnah of tasmiyah.

Core Idea
Implicit reference before the mention is valid when the referent is contextually known.

This lesson examines a subtle usul issue: can a text rely on an implied reference (idmar) before the referent is explicitly mentioned? The commentator defends the author's opening phrase 'ilayhi yas'adu' (to Him ascends) by showing that the implied subject 'Allah' is understood from context, even if not written. The objector raises a dilemma: if the author began with the basmalah, the referent is explicit; if not, he abandons the Sunnah. The commentator resolves this by distinguishing between the requirement of the Sunnah and the textual form of the book.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objection states that either the author began with the basmalah (tasmiyah) or not. If yes, there is no idmar qabl dhikr; if no, he violates the hadith 'every matter of importance'.
The hadith emphasizes beginning important matters with basmalah.
2
Analyze the commentator's answer
The commentator replies that acting upon the Sunnah does not require writing the basmalah as part of the book. It suffices to recite it, think it, or write it separately for blessing (tabarruk).
Thus the author could have intended the basmalah in his heart without writing it in the text.
3
Identify the usul principle at play
The principle is that implicit reference (idmar) is valid when the meaning is clear from context and intention (niyyah) can substitute for explicit textual inclusion.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on internal states (qalb) alongside external actions.
4
Apply the principle to the text
The phrase 'ilayhi yas'adu' contains a hidden pronoun referring to Allah. The referent is known from the context of praise and Islamic teachings. Therefore, idmar qabl dhikr is permissible.
The commentator also mentions that the referent exists in the believer's heart, reinforcing the role of intention.
5
Recognize the thamarah (practical result)
The practical result is that the textual integrity of the book is preserved (no need to write basmalah as part of the text) while the Sunnah is fulfilled through intention or separate action.
This also shows that the Hanafi school does not require the basmalah to be written at the start of every work for it to be valid.

Explanation: Allama Taftazani wants to verify the text: why is "ahdab" (plural) used with sihr and "urwah" (singular) with i'jaz? Before understanding this, know a preliminary matter: according to eloquence, speech has three degrees: high, middle, low. The lowest degree: if one speaks a little below it, according to rhetoricians it would be considered animal speech, although common people would call it human speech. The high degree and what is close to it are both miraculous (mu'jiz), and as far as being miraculous, they are counted as one degree. Then the middle has several levels. So the summary of the difference is: i'jaz has one degree, therefore the singular word "urwah" is used for it. And sihr means delicate grip, as in al-Sihah: "al-sihr: al-akhdh" (magic: seizing). In terminology, sihr is said when such qualities are considered in speech that the speech seizes like magic. This is why a person whose speech has effect is called a magician of expression.
