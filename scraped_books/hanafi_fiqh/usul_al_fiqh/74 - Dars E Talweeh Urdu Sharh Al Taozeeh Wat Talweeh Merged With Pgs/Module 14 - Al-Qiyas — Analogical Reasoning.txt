Qiyas as a Revealer: Defending the Definition of Hukm
Author addresses objection that definition excludes qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi legal principle that qiyas is a muzhir (revealer) and not a muthbit (establisher), and apply it to defend the definition of hukm as 'speech of Allah' against objections.

Core Idea
Qiyas reveals the divine ruling; it does not create it.

The definition of hukm as 'the speech of Allah' seems to exclude rulings derived through qiyas (analogy), because those rulings are not directly spoken by Allah. This objection challenges the very consistency of the definition. In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi response: qiyas is not an independent source that creates new rulings; rather, it makes apparent a ruling already latent in the primary sources. Understanding this distinction is essential for grasping the epistemological framework of Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Read the passage carefully and state the third objection against the definition of hukm. What does the objector claim about qiyas-based rulings?
The objector says such rulings are not covered by 'speech of Allah'.
2
Recognize the author's response
The author answers by distinguishing between muzhir and muthbit. Define each term and explain the key contrast.
Muzhir reveals what exists; muthbit creates new existence.
3
Extend the logic to sunnah and ijma
The author notes the same objection could be raised against rulings from sunnah and ijma. How does the author extend the same defense?
Both sunnah and ijma reveal or make known Allah's speech; they do not originate it.
4
Connect to the meaning of 'dalil'
Explain the phrase 'convenience being proofs of the rulings'. What does it mean for qiyas, sunnah, and ijma to be proofs?
They indicate the ruling; they do not create it.
5
Apply the principle to a new example
Consider a hypothetical qiyas: analogizing the prohibition of a new intoxicant to khamr. Show how this qiyas reveals a ruling rather than establishing a new one.
The ruling (prohibition) already exists in the divine law; qiyas uncovers its applicability.

Qiyas, Sunnah, and Ijma as Revealers of Divine Ruling
Defending hukm definition against objections from qiyas, sunnah, ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that qiyas, sunnah, and ijma are revealers (muzhir) of Allah's ruling and apply it to defend the definition of hukm.

Core Idea
Qiyas, Sunnah, and Ijma do not create rulings; they reveal what Allah has already ordained.

One might wonder: if a ruling is derived through qiyas (analogical reasoning) or based on the Sunnah or Ijma, can it truly be considered a divine address (khitab Allah)? This objection threatens the definition of hukm itself. In this passage, the commentator clarifies that these sources are not independent legislators but rather 'revealers' (muzhir) of a pre-existing divine ruling. Thus, every ruling ultimately traces back to Allah's address.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
Identify the third objection: the definition of hukm fails to cover rulings established by qiyas because they are not directly from Allah's address.
The same objection applies to Sunnah and Ijma.
2
Understand the response for qiyas
The answer: qiyas is not an establisher (muthbit) of a new ruling, but a revealer (muzhir). The ruling already exists in Allah's address; qiyas only uncovers it.
Muzhir means 'one who manifests or reveals'.
3
Extend to Sunnah and Ijma
The same logic applies: Sunnah and Ijma are also kashif (discoverers) of Allah's address, not creators. They are called 'evidences' (adillah) because they point to the ruling.
Thus the definition of hukm holds for all three sources.
4
Grasp the implication for the definition of hukm
Conclude that the definition of hukm as 'the address of Allah' is comprehensive: all rulings, whether derived from Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma, or qiyas, ultimately stem from divine address.
The key to the definition lies in the distinction between muthbit (establisher) and muzhir (revealer).

Objection: Definition Excludes Qiyasi Rulings
Presents third objection that qiyasi rulings are excluded from fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the third objection to the definition of fiqh as only revealed and consensual knowledge, and evaluate the commentator's response regarding qiyasi rulings.

Core Idea
A definition of fiqh that excludes qiyas cuts out most of its content – the commentator saves it by relativizing revelation.

This lesson examines a critical objection to a proposed definition of fiqh. The definition limits fiqh to knowledge directly from revelation or consensus. However, most fiqh rulings are derived through qiyas (analogy). The commentator offers a creative resolution that ties qiyas back to revelation for each individual mujtahid, but this leads to a radical relativism where each mujtahid's fiqh differs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's definition of fiqh
Recall that the definition under criticism limits fiqh to knowledge of what has appeared by revelation (waḥy) and what consensus (ijmāʿ) has been formed upon.
This is a narrow definition, focusing on direct textual sources.
2
Recognize the scope of qiyasi rulings
Qiyās (analogy) is a method to derive rulings for new cases based on an original case with a shared effective cause (ʿillah). Most fiqh issues are derived this way, not through direct revelation or consensus.
Thus, qiyās rulings constitute the majority of fiqh topics.
3
Understand the objection's force
If fiqh is defined only as revealed/consensual knowledge, then qiyās-based rulings are excluded. Yet the jurists treat them as fiqh. This creates a contradiction: the definition fails to cover the bulk of the discipline.
The objection is that the definition is too narrow.
4
Examine the commentator's saving interpretation
The commentator (al‑Taftāzānī in al‑Talwīḥ) suggests that for each mujtahid, his own qiyās-derived rulings are considered 'what has appeared by revelation' (mā ẓahara bihī nuzūl al‑waḥy) because they are based on ijtihād that is guided by the Shariʿah. Thus, they are fiqh for him.
This interpretation maintains the definition's wording but shifts its meaning.
5
Assess the consequence: relativism
If each mujtahid's qiyās is his own 'revelation', then fiqh becomes relative to each scholar. Two mujtahids may derive different rulings via qiyās, and both would be correct fiqh for themselves. This contradicts the idea of a single fiqh discipline.
The commentator's rescue comes at the cost of objectivity.

Status of Qiyas as a Source in Hanafi Usul
Discussing Qiyas as a partial source and presenting objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Qiyas is considered a derivative source (asl min wajh) in Hanafi usul and identify the five objections to this classification.

Core Idea
Qiyas is a source from one aspect (min wajh) because it derives its authority from Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma'.

In Hanafi usul, the primary sources of law are the Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma'—these are called 'absolute principles' (usul mutlaqa) because they are not derived from anything else. Qiyas, or analogical reasoning, is also a source but only 'from one aspect' (min wajh) because it always traces back to the other three. This passage explains that distinction and then presents five objections that challenge it, along with clarifications. Understanding this hierarchy is key to grasping how Hanafi jurists derive rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Absolute Sources
Recognize that Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma' are considered 'usul mutlaqa' (absolute principles) because they are independent and foundational. Nothing else is needed to make them authoritative.
2
Understand Qiyas as a Derivative Source
Note that Qiyas is called 'asl min wajh' (source from one aspect) and 'far' (branch) from another. The ruling in Qiyas is attributed to a cause (illah) that is extracted from one of the absolute sources, so it is not independent.
The passage gives examples: prohibition of sodomy (Quran), riba analogy (Hadith), prohibition of a slave woman's mother (Ijma').
3
Examine the Five Objections
The commentator lists five reasons why some might argue Qiyas should be an absolute source. Study each objection carefully: (1) definition of 'asl mutlaq' does not require independence, (2) proximate cause is more deserving of the name, (3) weak divisions are always singled out, (4) Qiyas changes rulings, and (5) Ijma' also depends on authority.
These are presented as 'اعتراض' (objections) against the classification.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Response (Implicit)
The passage does not explicitly rebut the objections, but the Hanafi position is clear: Qiyas remains derivative because its authority comes from the absolute sources. The commentary clarifies the objections but does not overturn the original classification.
The objections are theoretical; they help refine the definition of 'asl mutlaq'.
5
Evaluate the Significance for Legal Derivation
Understand that this classification affects how jurists use Qiyas. Since Qiyas is not absolute, its rulings are subject to verification against the primary sources. This keeps the derivation process rooted in revelation.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion on the classification of sources (usul) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, focusing on the status of Qiyas (analogical deduction) as a source. The author begins by clarifying that all evidences ultimately refer back to the four primary sources: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. He then explains that the first three are 'absolute and complete evidences' (adillah mutlaqah kamilah), while Qiyas is only a source 'from one aspect' (min wajh) because it derives its authority from the other three. To illustrate, the ruling in Qiyas is attributed to a cause (illah) extracted from Quran, Sunnah, or Ijma'. Hence Qiyas merely manifests the ruling by extending it to new cases, as when the prohibition of wine (specific) is extended to all intoxicants via the common cause of intoxication. The passage then presents, through the commentator (al-Sharih), five objections against this classification. The first objection argues that an 'absolute principle' (asl mutlaq) simply means something upon which another thing is built, regardless of it being itself built on something else—just as a father is a principle relative to his son, even though he is also a son. The second objection contends that the proximate cause (al-sabab al-qarib) is more deserving of the name 'cause' than the distant one, even if it is caused by the latter. The third objection notes that in any division, there are strong and weak parts, so the weak part should be singled out for clarity. The fourth objection states that Qiyas does more than generalize; it can also transfer a ruling in a way that creates a new form, which is the real meaning of being a complete principle. The fifth objection observes that Ijma' itself relies on authority (sanad), so it too should not be considered an absolute source. The commentary (Tashrih) then clarifies the first four objections with examples, showing that the critic's points challenge the view that Qiyas is only a partial source. However, the Hanafi position remains that Qiyas is not an independent absolute source but a derivative one, essential for extending the law.

Key Terms
اصل مطلق (asl mutlaq): An absolute and complete source that is not derived from another source and upon which other sources build.
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical deduction extending a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a shared cause (illah).
اجماع (ijma'): Consensus of the scholars of a generation on a legal ruling.

Why Qiyas Is Not an Absolute Source: Objections and Rebuttals
Addressing objections to qiyas not being an independent absolute source

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the five objections to qiyas as an independent source and explain the Hanafi rational refutations for each.

Core Idea
Qiyas reveals rulings from texts; it does not create them independently.

In Hanafi usul, qiyas (analogical reasoning) is a valid source of law, but it is not an absolute, independent source (asl mutlaq). Critics argue that if something can be based on another, it qualifies as a source, and since qiyas generates new rulings, it should be independent. This lesson unpacks five such objections and their systematic rebuttals, clarifying the nuanced Hanafi position that qiyas is a derivative tool, not a foundational source.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of 'asl' (source)
The term 'asl' is used in degrees (bi al-tashkik). An absolute source like the Quran is independent; a dependent source like qiyas relies on primary texts or consensus. Grasp this graded concept to follow the debate.
Hanafis emphasize that 'source' is not a binary but a spectrum.
2
Identify each objection and its underlying claim
The five objections claim: (1) dependency does not disqualify a source (like father), (2) the near cause is more deserving, (3) every division has degrees, (4) qiyas generalizes rulings independently, (5) if qiyas is dependent then ijma would also not be absolute. List each clearly.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to each objection
For each objection, state the commentator's rebuttal: (1) father's dependency is existential, not foundational; (2) qiyas is not even a near cause but a revealer; (3) real divisions need not show strength contrast; (4) qiyas does not establish rulings in reality but only manifests them; (5) ijma's need for a proof is for actualization, not authority.
4
Extract the core Hanafi position on qiyas
After the rebuttals, conclude that qiyas is a subordinate source (asl ghayr mustaqil) whose authority derives from text or consensus. It is not an independent originator of rulings.
5
Relate the dispute to practical istinbat
Understand that because qiyas is not absolute, a Hanafi jurist may prefer istihsan (juristic preference) over qiyas and must always anchor analogy in a textual or consensual 'illa. This shapes real fiqh derivation.

The Reality of Qiyas: Refuting the Claim that Qiyas Creates a New Ruling
Refutation of the objection that qiyas creates a new ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the real source of a ruling (nass) and the epistemological role of qiyas, and apply the distinction to defend the Hanafi view.

Core Idea
Qiyas does not create a new ruling; it only reveals in our knowledge what the text already established in reality.

This passage addresses a common objection: that qiyas gives a ruling a new form and proves it under a different description. The Hanafi response hinges on a crucial epistemological distinction: between reality (waqi) and human knowledge ('ilm). Understanding this distinction is key to grasping the Hanafi view of qiyas as a valid but subordinate source of law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Read the first sentence of the source: the opponent claims qiyas gives the ruling another form and proves it in another form. What exactly is being claimed?
The objection attempts to undermine qiyas by suggesting it modifies the ruling's essential nature.
2
Analyze the Hanafi distinction
The response asks the opponent to clarify 'another form'. If they mean in reality (waqi'), the claim is false because only the text (nass) can establish a ruling in reality. If they mean in our knowledge ('ilm), then qiyas merely reveals the ruling; it does not create it.
This distinction is a hallmark of Hanafi epistemology: the ruling's real source is the text, not qiyas.
3
Note the implication for qiyas' validity
Since qiyas only makes the ruling evident in our knowledge, it remains a valid tool—it does not compete with the text but rather derives from it. The objection fails to weaken qiyas.
The Hanafi school thus affirms qiyas as a legitimate source while maintaining the primacy of nass.

Structure of Qiyas: Propositions, Syllogisms, and the First Figure
Explains logical terminology and syllogism figures for fiqh reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a syllogism (minor, major, middle terms) and apply the first figure to derive a fiqh ruling from a universal rule.

Core Idea
The first figure syllogism connects a fiqh rule to a specific case via a middle term.

This passage introduces the logical structure underlying usul al-fiqh reasoning. You will learn how universal rules (qawa‘id kulliyya) serve as major premises in syllogisms, enabling you to derive rulings systematically. The commentator explains the terminology of propositions and syllogisms, then demonstrates with the example of Hajj.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposition and its parts
A proposition (qadiyya) has a subject (mahkum ‘alayh) and a predicate (mahkum bih). Recognize that the same sentence can be called a statement, premise, demand, conclusion, or problem depending on context.
All these refer to the same logical entity.
2
Map the syllogism components
In the example 'Hajj is obligatory because it is commanded by the Lawgiver', identify the minor term (Hajj), major term (obligatory), and middle term (commanded).
The middle term appears in both premises.
3
Understand the four figures
The position of the middle term determines the figure. Focus on the first figure (middle term is predicate in minor, subject in major) because it is the most direct for fiqh deduction.
Hanafi usul primarily uses the first figure.
4
Apply the universal rule as major premise
The major premise is a universal rule (qā‘ida kulliyya) from usul or derived from sources. The minor premise is an easily obtained specific fact (e.g., from Quran/Sunnah).
The major premise must be established by induction or other means.
5
Derive the fiqh ruling
Combine the minor and major premises via the middle term to produce the conclusion. In the example: 'Hajj is commanded' + 'Everything commanded is obligatory' → 'Hajj is obligatory'.
The conclusion is the fiqh ruling (hukm).

Seven Logical Terms and Syllogistic Structure in Usul
Sharh introducing seven logical terms and the structure of syllogistic proof in usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the seven logical terms, distinguish their aspects, and apply the syllogistic structure to derive a fiqh ruling.

Core Idea
Seven different terms refer to one essence: a statement capable of truth or falsity, differentiated by the aspect considered.

The commentator begins by clarifying seven key terms that often confuse students of usul al-fiqh. Although these terms share the same essential meaning—a composite statement subject to truth and falsity—each highlights a different aspect, such as containing a judgment or being part of a proof. Mastering this distinction is crucial for understanding how qiyas (syllogistic reasoning) operates to derive fiqh rulings from universal rules.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Seven Terms and Their Common Essence
List the seven terms: qadiyya, khabar, akhbar, muqaddima, matlub, natija, mas'ala. Note that each is a name for a meaningful composite statement that can be true or false. They differ only in the aspect considered (e.g., as containing a judgment, as conveying news, etc.).
This conceptual identity is a key point in Hanafi usul; do not treat them as separate entities.
2
Distinguish Each Term by Its Specific Aspect
For each term, identify the aspect that gives it its name: qadiyya (contains a judgment), khabar (possible truth/falsity), akhbar (conveys a judgment), muqaddima (part of a proof), matlub (sought from a proof), natija (obtained from a proof), mas'ala (something asked about).
The commentary gives a concise list—verify your understanding against the text.
3
Analyze the Parts of a Categorical Proposition
In a categorical proposition (qadiyya hamliyya), the subject is called mawdu' and the predicate mahmul. For a conclusion (natija), the subject is asghar and the predicate akbar.
Asghar generally has fewer individuals; akbar more. This will be used in the syllogism.
4
Deconstruct the Syllogism into Its Components
A proof (dalil) always has two premises. The premise containing asghar is sughra; the premise containing akbar is kubra. The term repeated in both premises is hadd awsat (middle term). Identify the four figures based on the position of the middle term.
The passage describes all four figures; focus on the first figure for the example.
5
Apply the Structure to the Hajj Example
Using the first figure example: 'Hajj is commanded by the Lawgiver' (sughra) and 'Everything commanded by the Lawgiver is obligatory' (kubra) yields 'Hajj is obligatory.' Verify that asghar is 'Hajj', akbar is 'obligatory', and hadd awsat is 'commanded by the Lawgiver'.
This illustrates how a universal rule (kubra) combined with an easily obtainable sughra produces a fiqh ruling.

He then explains the structure of a syllogism (qiyas): the conclusion (natija) has a subject (asghar) and predicate (akbar); a proof consists of two premises, sughra (containing asghar) and kubra (containing akbar); the repeated term is the middle term (hadd awsat). The four figures of syllogism are determined by the position of the middle term. He illustrates with the first figure example: 'Hajj is commanded by the Lawgiver' (sughra) and 'Everything commanded by the Lawgiver is obligatory' (kubra) yields 'Hajj is obligatory.' This demonstrates how a universal rule (kubra) combined with an easily obtainable sughra yields a fiqh ruling. Finally, he emphasizes that the attainment of universal rules requires investigating the states of evidences (ahwal al-adilla) and rulings (ahkam), as well as their conditions and restrictions, which are the subjects of usul al-fiqh.

Key Terms
قضیہ (qadiyya): A composite statement that contains a judgment, considered from the aspect of containing a judgment.
خبر (khabar): The same composite statement considered from the aspect of possibility of truth and falsity.
مقدمہ (muqaddima): A statement that is part of a proof (dalil), i.e., a premise.
حد اوسط (hadd awsat): The middle term repeated in both premises of a syllogism.
اصغر (asghar): The subject of the conclusion (natija).
اکبر (akbar): The predicate of the conclusion (natija).
صغری (sughra): The premise containing the subject (asghar) of the conclusion.
کبری (kubra): The premise containing the predicate (akbar) of the conclusion.

The Condition of Qiyas: Avoiding Contradiction of Ijma
Sharh explaining the condition of qiyas to avoid contradicting ijma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a new qiyas-based opinion contradicts ijma and apply the concept of ijma' murakkab to restrict ijtihad.

Core Idea
Composite ijma (ijma' murakkab) confines truth within existing opinions, barring new qiyas that contradicts them.

This passage from the Sharh explains a critical condition for using qiyas: it must not lead to an opinion that contradicts existing ijma. The commentator introduces the concept of 'composite consensus' (ijma' murakkab) to clarify when previous ijtihad restricts new reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
The matn states that qiyas must have led to the opinion of a mujtahid. Locate this condition in the Arabic text and understand its literal meaning.
The condition is 'ويكون القياس قد أدى إليه رأى مجتهد'.
2
Understand the reason
The commentator explains this condition is placed to avoid contradicting ijma. Why is that a concern?
Ijma is a stronger source than qiyas; violating it would invalidate the analogy.
3
Distinguish three scenarios
The passage contrasts cases: no prior ijtihad, single prior opinion, and multiple prior opinions (two or three). Each has different implications for new ijtihad.
Multiple prior opinions form ijma' murakkab.
4
Apply ijma' murakkab
If two or three opinions exist, the truth is within them. A fourth opinion via qiyas would contradict this composite consensus and is invalid.
This restricts ijtihad even though no single opinion is definitive.
5
Recognize scope
The condition only applies when previous ijtihad has produced multiple opinions. If only one opinion exists or none, qiyas can produce a contrary ruling.
This preserves the possibility of ijtihad in undetermined matters.

Commentary
This passage is a sharh on a condition for qiyas (analogy). The matn states that qiyas must have led to the opinion of a mujtahid. The commentator explains that this condition applies when there has been previous ijtihad on the issue, i.e., other mujtahids have expressed opinions. It is meant to avoid contradicting ijma (consensus). Specifically, when there are two or three opinions among mujtahids on a matter, this is called 'ijma' murakkab' (composite consensus), meaning the truth is confined within those existing opinions. If a later mujtahid tries to introduce a fourth opinion, it would violate this consensus and is therefore invalid. However, if no ijtihad has preceded at all, or only the opinion of a single mujtahid exists, then there is no consensus to violate, and a later mujtahid may freely exercise ijtihad even contrary to that single opinion.

Key Terms
اجماع مرکب (ijma' murakkab): Consensus that the truth is within two or more existing opinions of mujtahids on a matter, not outside them.
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning to derive a ruling from an original case based on a common effective cause (illa).
