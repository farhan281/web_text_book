Definition and Conditions of Idtirab vs Ikhtilaf
Defining mudtarib hadith and distinguishing it from general ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the term 'al-mudtarib' hadith, list its conditions, and distinguish it from general ikhtilaf by applying the condition of equal strength and irreconcilability.

Core Idea
A hadith is only mudtarib when contradictions are equal and unresolvable; otherwise, it is just ikhtilaf.

Hadith scholars often encounter narrations that seem contradictory. But not every difference of narration is a sign of weakness. This passage explains when a hadith is truly mudtarib (inconsistent) and when it is simply a case of general disagreement where one version can be preferred. Understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating hadith evidence in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitions
Read the passage to find the formal definition of mudtarib hadith and the definition of ikhtilaf. Note how they are distinguished.
2
Isolate the key condition
The passage emphasizes that idtirab requires the conflicting versions to be of equal strength. Identify the specific condition and see what happens if one version is preponderant.
3
Apply the condition to examples
The passage gives examples of when idtirab does and does not occur. Work through these examples: weak vs strong chain, naming vs anonimity, conflicting names. Determine which are mudtarib and why.

The Second Section: The Difference between Idtirab and Ikhtilaf. The mudtarib hadith: it is that which its narrator differed in, narrating it once in one way and another time in a contradictory way. Likewise, if two or more narrators are inconsistent in it, each narrating it in a way contradictory to the other. Among the conditions of idtirab is the equal weight of the conflicting narrations such that none of them is preponderant over another. As for when one of the narrations is preponderant, it is not called mudtarib, rather it is absolute ikhtilaf. Al-Iraqi said: 'If one of them is preponderant due to its narrator being more reliable or having longer companionship with the one from whom it is narrated, or other grounds of tarjih, then the term idtirab is not applied to the preponderant version, nor does it have its ruling; the ruling is given to the preponderant version.' This is well-known among hadith scholars, with no disagreement; thus we find al-Mubarakfuri saying: 'It is established in the principles of hadith that mere ikhtilaf does not necessitate idtirab; rather among its conditions is the equal weight of the aspects of disagreement, so when one of the sayings is preponderant, it is given precedence.' Accordingly, if one version is narrated through a weak chain and another through a strong chain, there is no idtirab and practice is upon the strong chain. Commentary
This passage is a definition and clarification of the terms idtirab (inconsistency) and ikhtilaf (disagreement) in hadith terminology, as a foundational topic for understanding ikhtilaf among fuqaha. The author defines mudtarib hadith as one where a single narrator contradicts himself, or multiple narrators contradict each other, in ways that cannot be reconciled. The key condition for idtirab is that the conflicting versions must be of equal strength; if one version is clearly stronger (due to the narrator's reliability, memory, or other factors), then it is not idtirab but a case of general ikhtilaf where the stronger version is accepted and the weaker is considered shadhdh (irregular) or munkar (rejected). The author supports this with quotes from al-Iraqi and al-Mubarakfuri, confirming that this is agreed upon by hadith scholars. The passage then elaborates with examples: if one version has a weak chain and another strong, no idtirab; if the versions can be harmonized (e.g., one says 'a man' and the other names him), there is no idtirab. Only when the contradictions are irreconcilable and equally strong does the hadith become mudtarib. This distinction is crucial in Hanafi usul because the strength of hadith evidence affects legal rulings (ahkam). The passage emphasizes that mere difference in transmission does not automatically render a hadith mudtarib; only balanced, unresolved contradictions do.

Key Terms
المضطرب (al-mudtarib): A hadith where one narrator or multiple narrators contradict each other in a way that cannot be reconciled and where the conflicting versions are of equal strength.
الاختلاف (al-ikhtilaf): General disagreement or variation in narrations, which may include cases of idtirab as well as cases where one version is preponderant.
الترجيح (al-tarjih): The process of giving preference to one narration over another based on factors such as the narrator's reliability, memory, or length of companionship with the source.
الشاذ (al-shadhdh): A narration that contradicts a more reliable narrator or chain; it is considered weak and not used as evidence.

The Effect of Disagreement on the 'Illah: Funeral Prayer in the Mosque
Usul dispute on the effect of 'illah in a specific fiqh ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two competing 'illahs in the Hanafi school regarding funeral prayer in the mosque and explain how each generates a different ruling.

Core Idea
The ruling on funeral prayer in the mosque depends on the 'illah: fear of soiling or the purpose of mosques.

This passage presents the internal Hanafi debate on performing funeral prayer inside a mosque. While the majority initially deem it makruh tahriman, later scholars refined the ruling by examining the legal cause ('illah). Understanding this process reveals how usul al-fiqh shapes fiqh rulings within a single madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base ruling and its level
Note that the majority Hanafi view holds funeral prayer in the mosque as makruh tahriman (prohibitively disliked). This is the starting point.
Makruh tahriman is distinct from haram; it is still sinful if done without valid reason.
2
Pinpoint the two proposed 'illahs
The text mentions two potential causes: (a) fear of soiling the mosque (خوف تلويث المسجد) and (b) that mosques are established for obligatory prayers (المساجد وجدت لصلاة المكتوبات).
These are derived from general principles, not from explicit textual proof.
3
See how each 'illah leads to a different ruling
If the 'illah is soiling, the dislike disappears when the deceased is outside (no risk of soiling). If the 'illah is the purpose of mosques, the dislike remains in all cases because the funeral prayer diverts from the mosque's primary function.
This shows how the 'illah determines the scope of the ruling.
4
Note the internal Hanafi ikhtilaf and tarjih
The majority of Hanafis initially adopted the second 'illah (purpose of mosques), but later scholars like al-Sarakhsi and Ibn Abidin preferred the first (fear of soiling), leading to the operative view that removes dislike when the deceased is outside.
Ibn Abidin states: 'وعليه العمل وهو المختار' — the practice and chosen view.
5
Compare with the Maliki position
The Maliki school also considers funeral prayer in the mosque as permissible (with tanzih) when the deceased is outside or when space is limited, but their reasoning is based on different textual proofs.
This highlights how different usul approaches yield similar results in some cases.

First Model: Ruling on funeral prayer in the mosque
The jurists differed on the ruling of funeral prayer in the mosque into four schools: First: Prayer for the deceased inside the mosque where congregational prayer is held is disliked with a prohibition-level dislike (makruh tahriman), whether the deceased and the worshippers are in the mosque, or the deceased is outside and the people inside, or the deceased is inside and the people outside. This is the position of the Hanafis. Some of their jurists said: the dislike is for tanzih (lesser dislike). Abu Yusuf - may Allah have mercy on him - excepted a mosque originally built for funeral prayers, so prayer in it is not disliked. They also have a narration: if the deceased is outside the mosque, it is not disliked. This goes back to their difference in determining the cause ('illah) of the dislike: is it fear of soiling the mosque, or that mosques were established for obligatory prayers? Those who adopt the second - and they are the majority of Hanafi jurists - maintain the dislike in all cases. Those who make the cause fear of soiling the mosque remove the dislike if the deceased is outside the mosque. On this basis this narration is derived; al-Sarakhsi inclined to this in al-Mabsut and al-Muhit, and Ibn Abidin said: 'Upon it is the practice and it is the chosen view.' This is also held by Malik, Ibn Abi Dhi'b, and the Hadiyya (a Zaydi subschool). Second: That the dislike is tanzihi (non-prohibition), and there is no harm in those inside the mosque praying upon the deceased if the deceased is outside, following the imam's prayer, and likewise if the outside of the mosque is crowded with its people. This is the well-known position of Malik.

Commentary
This passage presents the first two of four scholarly opinions on the ruling of performing funeral prayer (salat al-janazah) inside a mosque. The first opinion, held by the majority of Hanafis, considers it makruh tahriman (prohibitively disliked) regardless of the positions of the deceased and worshippers. However, there is internal Hanafi divergence based on the underlying cause ('illah): if the cause is fear of soiling the mosque, then when the deceased is outside, there is no dislike; if the cause is that mosques are designated for obligatory prayers, then the dislike remains in all cases. The author notes that Abu Yusuf excepted mosques specifically built for funerals, and that a narration removes dislike when the deceased is outside. The preferred view among later Hanafis (as per Ibn Abidin) is the one that removes dislike when the deceased is outside, and this is also supported by Malik and others. The second opinion, attributed to Malik in his well-known view, considers the dislike to be tanzihi (non-prohibitively disliked), and permits praying inside if the deceased is outside or if space is limited. The passage illustrates how differences in identifying the legal cause (ta'lil) lead to divergent rulings within a school.

Key Terms
كراهة تحريم (karāhah taḥrīm): A level of disapproval that is closer to prohibition; the action is sinful if done without valid reason.
علة (ʿillah): The legal cause or effective reason that justifies a ruling.
كراهة تنزيه (karāhah tanzīh): A level of disapproval that is not prohibitive; it is better to avoid but not sinful.

Reconciling Contradictory Hadith: Mortgagee's Benefit
Analyzing hadith and reconciling texts on mortgagee's rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principles of naskh and jam' to reconcile conflicting hadith regarding the mortgagee's right to benefit from mortgaged property.

Core Idea
Abrogation requires chronology; without it, reconciliation (jam') is the valid method.

This passage presents a classic usul al-fiqh problem: two seemingly contradictory hadith on the mortgagee's right to use the mortgaged property. The Hanafi school argues that the hadith permitting benefit (profit/loss to mortgagor) is not abrogated, but rather reconciled by conditioning permission on the mortgagor's default in maintenance. Understanding this reconciliation teaches us how to handle conflicting texts when chronology is unknown.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting hadith
Locate the hadith that states 'profit and loss belong to the mortgagor' (implying the mortgagee cannot benefit) and the hadith that allows riding and drinking milk in return for maintenance (implying benefit is allowed).
The first is used by the majority to prohibit benefit; the second is used by the Hanafis to allow it under conditions.
2
Examine the claim of abrogation (naskh)
Al-Tahawi claimed that the permission to benefit was abrogated by the prohibition of riba. Identify the reasoning behind this claim and then the Hanafi response that naskh requires knowledge of chronology.
The Hanafi rebuttal: without knowing which came first, we cannot assert naskh; probability does not establish rulings.
3
Apply the principle of jam' (reconciliation)
The Hanafi school reconciles the hadith by interpreting the permission as conditional: if the mortgagor fails to maintain the property, the mortgagee may spend and take compensation through use, provided no harm.
This preserves the force of both hadith without discarding either.
4
Evaluate the strength of the evidence
Analyze the chains of narration: the hadith prohibiting milk without permission is narrated mursal (weak), while the profit/loss hadith is used by the Hanafis. Note how the Hanafi school uses the weaker hadith as a supporting evidence along with the principle of preserving property.
The reconciliation is preferred over abrogation because it respects both texts.

Commentary
This passage analyzes the hadith regarding the mortgagee's right to benefit from the mortgaged property, a key point of disagreement among jurists. The author begins by stating the hadith that profit and loss belong to the mortgagor, which implies that maintenance is on the mortgagor and offspring belongs to him. The text then notes that opponents argue this hadith contradicts analogy (qiyas) in two ways: first, it permits riding and drinking for someone who is not the owner of the property without the owner's permission; second, it holds the mortgagee liable for maintenance instead of the value of the benefit. The author then examines the chains of narration, noting that the strongest version is mursal, as favored by al-Albani. Next, the meaning of 'ghalaqa' (forfeiture) is clarified, showing that Islam abolished the pre-Islamic practice of forfeiture. Maximum Lashes in Ta'zir: The Hadith of Abu Burda and Its Implications
Textual analysis of the hadith of Abu Burda and the juristic disagreement on ta'zir maximum lashes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hadith of Abu Burda as a dalil for the maximum ta'zir lashes and evaluate the three main juristic positions on the upper limit.

Core Idea
An authentic hadith sets ta'zir at ten lashes, but later jurists used qiyas to extend the limit based on the principle that ta'zir must not reach the fixed hadd.

In this passage, we examine how the hadith of Abu Burda, despite its apparent limit of ten lashes, became the foundation for multiple opinions on the maximum ta'zir punishment. We will trace the linguistic and technical definition of ta'zir, the authenticity debate, and the juristic reasoning that led to limits of 39, 19, and 79 lashes.

Condition for Accepting Ziyada: Adding a New Legal Ruling
Rule: ziyada must add a new hukm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a ziyada from a single narrator is accepted and apply the condition that it must add a new shar'i ruling.

Core Idea
A single narrator's addition binds only if it introduces a new legal ruling.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the reliability of a narrator is not enough to accept an additional wording in a hadith. There is a further condition: the addition must carry a new legal consequence (hukm shar'i). This lesson unpacks that condition, distinguishing between meaningful additions and mere embellishments.

Guided Path
1
Recall the concept of ziyada
Define ziyada as an extra wording or detail reported by one or a few narrators not found in other narrations of the same hadith.
Ziyada can be in text or meaning.
2
State the condition
From the text: 'It is not accepted unless it provides a legal ruling; if it does not provide a legal ruling, it is not considered.' Identify that the condition is that the addition must yield a new hukm shar'i.
The condition is attributed to Qadi Abd al-Wahhab and Ibn al-Qushayri.
3
Distinguish legal vs. non-legal additions
Consider examples: adding a time limit (e.g., three days for wiping over khuffs) adds a new ruling; adding a descriptive detail (e.g., 'with moist hands') that doesn't affect the ruling does not.
The key is whether the addition changes or adds an obligation, prohibition, permissibility, etc.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Given a hadith about prayer, a narrator adds 'he said Bismillah aloud.' Other narrations don't mention this. Does this add a new ruling? (Yes, if it indicates the sunnah of reciting aloud; No, if it's just a report of a single incident without legal consequence.)
Requires analyzing the legal import of the addition.

An addition in a transmitted text, if accepted, restricts the general ruling to specific conditions.

This lesson presents a fiqh ruling on tayammum that illustrates a key usul principle: how an additional condition in a text can narrow the scope of a general command. The ruling distinguishes between surfaces that are part of the ground and those that are removed. Understanding this distinction is essential for recognizing how accepted additions (ziyada) function in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

The Tasmiyah Dispute: Wajib or Sunnah?
Examines divergent legal rulings on saying Bismillah before wudu.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two positions on tasmiyah in wudu, identify the madhahib that hold each, and evaluate the impact of hadith grading on the ruling.

Core Idea
Tasmiyah before wudu: wajib for some Hanbalis, sunnah for Hanafis, Malikis, and Shafi'is due to hadith strength.

This passage presents a classic fiqh ikhtilaf on whether saying Bismillah (tasmiyah) before wudu is wajib or sunnah. The author surveys numerous hadith chains, noting their strengths and weaknesses, and then presents the positions of the major imams. Understanding this dispute illustrates how hadith criticism (jarh wa ta'dil) directly shapes legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two qawls
Recognize that the first position declares tasmiyah wajib, the second declares it sunnah. The passage states these as 'القول الأول' and 'القول الثاني'.
The first qawl includes two sub-positions about forgetfulness.
2
Note the madhahib affiliation
The wajib view is attributed to one narration from Imam Ahmad; the sunnah view is held by Abu Hanifah, Malik, al-Shafi'i, and another narration from Ahmad, plus the Zahiriyyah and others.
This shows that intra-madhhab differences exist (e.g., Ahmad has two narrations).
3
Examine the hadith evidence
The passage lists many hadith chains (from Ibn Umar, Abu Huraira, etc.) and mentions their weaknesses. Some scholars like Ibn al-Qayyim and Ibn Hajar consider them collectively strong.
This is a key usul point: how multiple weak chains can strengthen each other.
4
Apply the usul principle
For those who consider the hadith authentic enough, they derive wujub; for others, it remains sunnah. The Hanafi position relies on the weakness of individual chains.
The Hanafi usul principle: a hadith must be mutawatir or well-known to impose obligation.
5
Synthesize the practical consequence
According to the sunnah position, leaving tasmiyah intentionally or forgetfully does not invalidate wudu. According to the wajib position, forgetting requires compensatory acts, and intentional omission invalidates wudu.
This shows the importance of the ruling in daily practice.
