Mashhur Hadith as Takhsis of the Qur'an
Argument that a mashhur hadith can specify the general Quranic text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a mashhur hadith can specify (takhsis) the general (amm) text of the Qur'an, and distinguish it from the ruling on ahad hadith.

Core Idea
A hadith that reaches the level of mashhur can specify the general Qur'an, even if it appears to contradict the plain text.

This passage tackles a fundamental usul question: can a hadith that seemingly opposes the Qur'an be accepted to restrict its general application? The objectors claim the hadith is ahad and thus cannot override the Book. The majority (jumhur), including the Hanafis, respond that the hadith is at least mashhur due to its multiple chains, making it a valid bayan (clarification) and takhsis. This lesson unpacks the dispute and the reasoning behind each position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent conflict
Note that the hadith in question seems to contradict the clear Qur'anic verse on witnesses (2:282), which generally requires two male witnesses or a man and two women.
2
Recognize the objection
Opponents argue the hadith is ahad (solitary) and therefore cannot override a definitive Qur'anic text. This is a standard argument against using ahad reports for takhsis of the Qur'an.
3
Evaluate the jumhur response
The jumhur (including Hanafis) count the narrations: the hadith is transmitted from numerous Companions (Ibn Abbas, Abu Huraira, Jabir, etc.) through multiple chains, making it mashhur.
4
Apply the Hanafi principle
In Hanafi usul, a mashhur hadith, though not mutawatir, is still authoritative enough to specify the general Qur'an. It functions as bayan (clarification) and takhsis.
5
Consider the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: acceptance of the hadith modifies the general witness requirement in certain cases, affecting legal rulings on testimony.

Commentary
Passage type: Argumentation on the status of a hadith opposing the Quran. The author first reports Ibn Abd al-Barr's statement that this particular opinion (likely the content of the hadith) was not narrated from any Companion. Then he presents the objection to the hadith: it conflicts with the Quranic verse on witnessing (2:282), and the objection is that this hadith is ahad (solitary) and thus cannot override the Quran. The response (from the majority, jumhur) is that this hadith is at least mashhur (well-known), being narrated from many Companions such as Ibn Abbas, Abu Huraira, Jabir, etc. Because it is mashhur, it can function as a bayan (clarification) of the Quran and can be used to make takhsis (specification) of the general (amm) Quranic command. This illustrates the Hanafi usul principle that a mashhur hadith, though not mutawatir, has sufficient authority to specify the Quran, unlike a mere ahad hadith.

Key Terms
مشهور (mashhur): A hadith narrated from at least three different chains in each generation, but not reaching mutawatir; considered well-known.
آحاد (ahad): A hadith whose number of narrators does not reach the level of tawatur; solitary report.
بيان (bayan): Clarification or explanation of the Quranic text by the Sunnah.
تخصيص (takhsis): Specification of a general (amm) text, limiting its application.

Analysing the Hadith Evidence on Mortgagee's Use: A Usul Dispute
Usul analysis of a hadith dispute over mortgagee's use.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the usul issue of ibham (ambiguity) in the hadith 'الظهر يركب بنفقته' and evaluate how the Hanafi school resolves it through contextual interpretation.

Core Idea
The hadith permits use in exchange for expense, but ambiguity about the beneficiary fuels the Hanafi-majority dispute on mortgagee's use.

The passage presents a lively usul dispute over the mortgagee's right to benefit from the mortgaged property. At the heart of the debate are two hadiths. The first, 'الظهر يركب بنفقته', appears to allow use in return for expense. The second, 'لا يغلق الرهن', stresses that the owner reaps the benefit and bears the loss. The key usul question is: who is the intended beneficiary? The Hanafi school argues that the context clarifies it is the mortgagee, while the majority contends the hadith is ambiguous and thus insufficient to override the default prohibition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi Position and Its Conditions
From the first paragraph of the passage, extract the three conditions under which the Hanafi school allows the mortgagee to use the collateral. Note that if the conditions are omitted, even later permission is invalid.
The conditions are: stipulation in the contract, fixed period, and the loan not being a qard.
2
Locate the Primary Hadith Evidence
Find the hadith narrated by Abu Hurairah: 'الظهر يركب بنفقته...' Pay attention to its wording: it does not explicitly name the mortgagee or mortgagor.
The hadith is reported by multiple compilers, indicating its acceptance.
3
Apply the Usul Concept of Ibmam (Ambiguity)
Identify the ambiguous element: the word 'المنتفع' (beneficiary) is unspecified. In usul al-fiqh, this makes the text mujmal (ambiguous) regarding the agent. The Hanafi school uses a contextual indication (qarinah) to resolve the ambiguity.
The context: the mortgagor already owns the property and does not need religious permission to use it, so the hadith must refer to the mortgagee.
4
Compare Hanafi Interpretation with the Majority's Response
The passage quotes the majority (جمهور) as arguing that the hadith is ambiguous: it could refer to the mortgagor using his own property. This is a naqd (critique) of the Hanafi derivation. Understand that this disagreement is based on how much weight is given to external context.
The majority also cites the hadith 'لا يغلق الرهن' to support that the owner retains benefit and bears loss.
5
Evaluate the Strength of Each Interpretation
Consider the Hanafi response: the hadith explicitly links use with expense; if it were the mortgagor, the permission would be redundant because he already owns the property. Evaluate whether this contextual qarinah is strong enough to remove ambiguity.
The passage does not give a final tarjih, leaving it as an ongoing scholarly debate.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed madhhab dispute and textual analysis concerning the permissibility of the mortgagee (المرتهن) benefiting from the mortgaged property (الرهن). The text first presents the Hanafi position (implicitly from the first unlabeled paragraph) which permits the mortgagee to use the collateral under three strict conditions: the use must be stipulated in the contract, the period must be fixed, and the loan must not be a qard (interest-free loan). If the condition is omitted from the contract, even if the mortgagor later permits it, it is not permissible according to Hanafis. The text then lists the Shafi'i, Zaydi, Imami, and Hanbali (in one narration) positions, all of which generally prohibit the mortgagee from benefiting. The majority (جمهور) of the listed schools (Shafi'is, etc.) argue that the mortgagee's right is restricted to securing the debt; any use requires specific permission or compensation. The passage then presents the hadith evidence used by those who permit (the Hanafis): the famous hadith 'الظهر يركب بنفقته' which seems to allow riding and drinking milk of the mortgaged animal in exchange for expense. However, the majority responds by pointing out that the hadith does not specify who the beneficiary is (المُنتفع). It could be the mortgagor using his own property, or the mortgagee as compensation. Thus the hadith is ambiguous. The passage also examines a second hadith: 'لا يغلق الرهن' (the pledge is not foreclosed), which indicates that the owner retains the benefit and bears the loss, again not specifying the mortgagee. The extensive chain analysis at the end (from page 179) shows the scholarly effort to verify the hadith's authenticity and transmission. The Hanafi position is that the mortgagee's use is a separate issue from mere retention, and they rely on the first hadith combined with their conditions to establish permissibility. The passage exemplifies how differences in interpreting ambiguous textual evidence lead to juristic disagreement.

The Ruling on Ziyada That Changes I'rab
A usul rule on treating conflicting narrations due to a grammatical addition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the usul rule regarding a ziyada that changes the grammatical ending of a hadith, and distinguish the conditions under which it is accepted or rejected.

Core Idea
A ziyada that changes the i'rab causes ta'arud (conflict) and is rejected unless narrated in separate sessions or explicitly clarified.

In usul al-fiqh, when a hadith is narrated with and without an addition (ziyada), the addition may affect the grammatical structure (i'rab). Scholars differ on how to treat such additions. This lesson examines the ruling when the addition changes the i'rab, presenting the views of the majority (Ibn Hanbal), al-Razi, and Qadi Abd al-Jabbar.

Guided Path
1
Identify the majority rule
Understand that when a ziyada changes the i'rab, the two narrations are considered conflicting (ta'arud) and the addition is rejected. This is the view of the majority, including Ahmad ibn Hanbal in a related context, as reported by al-Hindi.
The change in i'rab signals a different syntactic structure, making reconciliation impossible.
2
Examine al-Razi's detailed criteria
Al-Razi distinguishes based on whether the narrator attributes the narrations to one or two sessions, and on the frequency of the addition. If attributed to two sessions, the addition is accepted regardless of i'rab change. If to one session, outcome depends on frequency: fewer additions rejected unless narrator clarifies forgetfulness; equal or more additions accepted.
Al-Razi's approach emphasizes the narrator's consistency and the possibility of forgetfulness.
3
Consider Qadi Abd al-Jabbar's distinction
Qadi Abd al-Jabbar accepts the addition if it affects meaning but not wording, and rejects it if it affects the i'rab (wording). This is a different criterion focusing on the type of change.
This is a middle ground between outright rejection and full acceptance.
4
Apply the rule to a scenario
Given a hypothetical case, determine whether the addition is accepted or rejected according to each scholar's view. Practice identifying the key factors: change in i'rab, single or multiple sessions, frequency of narration, and explicit statements.

The Effect of Restriction (Taqyid) on Absolute Commands: Sadaqat al-Fitr for Non-Muslim Slaves
Analyzing two positions on sadaqat al-fitr for non-Muslim slaves with hadith proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principles of mutlaq and muqayyad to resolve the dispute on sadaqat al-fitr for non-Muslim slaves, and evaluate the strength of the hadith evidence.

Core Idea
An absolute command remains general unless a valid restriction; a context-specific hadith does not restrict a general command.

The obligation of sadaqat al-fitr for a slave who is not Muslim is a classic case of usul al-fiqh. The Hanafis and others differ based on how they read two hadith: a general command from Abdullah ibn Tha'laba and a restricted wording in Ibn Umar's narration. This lesson will teach you to identify the principles at play and derive the Hanafi ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main hadith
Find the hadith of Abdullah ibn Tha'laba (general command) and the hadith of Abdullah ibn Umar which includes the phrase 'min al-muslimin' (from the Muslims).
The Ibn Tha'laba hadith is unconditional.
2
Classify each hadith as mutlaq or muqayyad
According to Hanafi usul, a general command is carried on its absolute meaning unless there is a valid restriction. Classify the Ibn Tha'laba hadith as mutlaq, and the Ibn Umar hadith as muqayyad.
The restriction in Ibn Umar is not a restriction of the general command, but a specific context.
3
Apply the principle: mutlaq stays unless muqayyad of equal scope
The Hanafi principle is that an absolute text remains applicable to all instances unless there is a restriction that explicitly delimits it. The Ibn Umar hadith does not restrict the Ibn Tha'laba hadith because it is about his own slave who was Muslim, so it does not negate the obligation for non-Muslim slaves.
The opposing school treats the Ibn Umar hadith as a takhsis of the general command.
4
Evaluate the third hadith (Ibn Abbas)
Supporters of the Hanafi position also cite a hadith of Ibn Abbas that explicitly includes 'Jew or Christian'. However, al-Daraqutni deemed it weak due to a narrator called Salam al-Tawil. Note that the Hanafis do not rely on this weak hadith but on the general command.
This hadith is not needed for the Hanafi proof.
5
Conclude the Hanafi ruling
Therefore, the Hanafi ruling is that sadaqat al-fitr is wajib for every slave, regardless of religion, based on the general command of the Ibn Tha'laba hadith. The opposing view is rejected because their restriction is not a valid restriction of the general command.
The thamarah is the practical consequence: one must pay zakat al-fitr for a non-Muslim slave.

They further support their position with a hadith from Ibn Abbas that explicitly includes Jews and Christians, but al-Daraqutni declares this hadith weak due to the narrator Salam al-Tawil, who is accused of fabricating hadith. Therefore, the strongest evidence remains the unrestricted command, and the Hanafi ruling is that sadaqat al-fitr is wajib for every slave, Muslim or not.

Key Terms
صدقة الفطر (sadaqat al-fitr): The obligatory charity given at the end of Ramadan, before the Eid prayer.
مطلق (mutlaq): An unqualified or absolute statement that applies to all cases without restriction.
مقيد (muqayyad): A restricted or qualified statement that limits the application of an absolute ruling.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school holds that sadaqat al-fitr is wajib for every slave, Muslim or not, based on the general command in the hadith of Abdullah ibn Tha'laba, which is not validly restricted by the Ibn Umar hadith.
