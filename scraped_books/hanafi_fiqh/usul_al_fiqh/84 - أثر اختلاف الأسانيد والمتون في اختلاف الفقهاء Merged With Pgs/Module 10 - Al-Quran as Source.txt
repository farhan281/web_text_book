Certainty of the Qur'an vs. Probability of Solitary Hadith
Introduces the principle of certainty of the Quran and uncertainty of solitary hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the epistemic status of the Qur'an and khabar al-ahad in Hanafi usul and explain the rationale for their differing certainty.

Core Idea
The Qur'an is absolutely certain; solitary hadith is only probable.

This lesson introduces a bedrock principle of Hanafi usul: the Qur'an is qat'ī al-thubūt (definitively established), while a solitary report (khabar al-āḥād) is only ẓannī (probable). This distinction shapes how we weigh evidence when a hadith appears to contradict the Qur'an. Let's see why even the most trustworthy narrators cannot impart certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two sources
The Qur'an and khabar al-ahad are the two categories being compared. Note that the Qur'an is wholly transmitted via tawatur.
2
Classify each epistemically
The Qur'an is qat'i (definitive) in its transmission (thubut). Khabar al-ahad is zanni (probable) because its chain does not reach tawatur.
This is agreed upon among Muslims.
3
Understand the reason for zanniyyah
Even trustworthy narrators can err, as Imam Ahmad famously stated. This possibility of error prevents certainty.
The principle applies even when narrators are thiqat.
4
Recognize the consequence
Because a khabar al-ahad is only zanni, it cannot override a qat'i text like the Qur'an without further evidence or interpretation.
This is a key rule when resolving apparent contradictions.

Hanafi Istinbat: Housing for Irrevocably Divorced Woman
Stating the Hanafi ruling with Quranic proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a general Quranic command ('amm) includes all individuals unless a specific exclusion (takhsis) is proven, and derive the ruling on maintenance for a woman divorced three times.

Core Idea
A Quranic command that is 'amm covers all within its scope until an exclusion is proven.

This lesson shows how the Hanafi school derives the obligation of housing and maintenance for a woman divorced three times (irrevocably) from the Quran. The key is their usul principle that a general command ('amm) addressed to 'women' includes both revocably and irrevocably divorced women. You will trace the istinbat chain from the Quranic text to the final ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source text
Locate the primary Quranic verses cited: Surah al-Talaq 1 and 6. The command 'lodge them where you dwell' (أَسْكُنُوهُنَّ) is the operative text.
The address in verse 1 ('when you divorce women') sets the general context.
2
Classify the text according to Hanafi usul
Determine the textual category of the command 'lodge them'. In Hanafi usul, the word 'women' (النِّسَاءَ) without restriction makes it 'amm (general). The command (amr) is a direct imperative.
Hanafis consider the general command as qat'i al-dalala until takhsis.
3
Apply the Hanafi usul rule
The Hanafi rule: al-amr yufidu al-wujub and al-amm yufidu al-wujub. Since the command is general, it obligates housing for every woman addressed, including the bā'in.
No evidence of takhsis was found to exclude the irrevocably divorced woman.
4
Derive the ruling
Therefore, the irrevocably divorced (bā'in) woman who is not pregnant is entitled to housing and maintenance during her iddah.
This is the first of three opinions mentioned; the Hanafis adopt it.

Narrated by Malik, al-Shafi'i, Abd al-Razzaq, al-Humaydi, Sa'id ibn Mansur, Ibn Sa'd, Ibn al-Ja'd, Ibn Abi Shayba, Ahmad, al-Darimi, Muslim, Abu Dawud, Ibn Majah, al-Tirmidhi, al-Nasa'i, al-Tahawi, Ibn Hibban, and others: Ibn al-Jarud, and al-Tabari. ... Commentary
This passage is a ruling from the Hanafi school concerning the obligation of maintenance and housing for a woman who has been divorced three times (irrevocable divorce) during her waiting period, provided she is not pregnant. The author first presents the issue: the jurists differ on whether a non-pregnant woman divorced irrevocably is entitled to maintenance and housing from her ex-husband. He then states the first opinion, which is the Hanafi position, that she is entitled to both. This opinion is attributed to several early authorities, including Umar, Ibn Umar, Ibn Mas'ud, and Aisha, as well as later jurists like al-Nakha'i and al-Thawri. The Hanafis' proof is derived from the opening verses of Surah al-Talaq (65:1), where the address 'when you divorce women' is general and includes both revocable and irrevocable divorce. Subsequently, in verse 65:6, the command 'lodge them where you dwell' is also general, applying to all divorced women. Therefore, both types of divorced women are entitled to housing during their iddah. The Hanafis argue that the generality of the verse (amm) establishes the obligation for all divorced women, and there is no evidence to exclude the irrevocably divorced woman. This ruling is part of the established position of the Hanafi school, as recorded in authoritative works such as al-Mabsut and Bada'i' al-Sana'i'.

Establishing the Mashhur Status of a Hadith
Listing companion narrators and their sources to establish mashhur status.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the method of establishing a hadith as mashhur by tracing its multiple chains of transmission and distinguish mashhur from ahad and mutawatir.

Core Idea
A hadith with multiple chains from different companions is mashhur—stronger than ahad, weaker than mutawatir.

In this passage, the author catalogs several companions who narrated a specific hadith and lists the hadith collections that recorded their narrations. This method is used to demonstrate that the hadith has achieved at least mashhur status, a key category in Hanafi usul that carries legal weight. Understanding how mashhur is established is essential for determining the authority of a hadith in legal derivation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the named companion narrators
Read the passage and list all companions mentioned: Ibn Abbas, Abu Hurairah, Abdullah ibn Umar, Hakim ibn Hizam, and Abu Barzah al-Aslami. These are independent chains.
At least five different companions.
2
For each companion, note the hadith sources cited
Observe the variety of books: Bukhari, Muslim, Abu Dawud, Tirmidhi, etc. This shows the hadith was widely recorded.
The more sources, the stronger the transmission.
3
Count the number of distinct companion chains
The passage lists five companions, each with a separate chain. That is multiple independent transmissions.
Even if one had a single chain, multiple companions elevate it.
4
Conclude that the hadith is at least mashhur
Based on the multiplicity, the author states it is 'at least mashhur'. Mashhur requires more than one chain but not mass transmission.
If it had many more chains it could be mutawatir, but here it is mashhur.

Legal Implications of Mashhur Hadith in Hanafi Usul
Stating the legal capacity of mashhur hadith (takhsis and ziyada).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a mashhur hadith can specify general Quranic verses and add rulings, and distinguish this from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Mashhur hadith can specify the Quran and add to it—ahad cannot.

After establishing the hadith as mashhur through multiple chains, the author points out the legal consequence: mashhur differs from ahad in that it can specify (takhsis) the Quran and add (ziyada) to its rulings. This is a key point of ikhtilaf between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools, rooted in their different approaches to the authority of hadith.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concepts of takhsis and ziyada
Takhsis is narrowing a general Quranic rule, e.g., 'Cut the hand of a thief' applies only to a certain value. Ziyada is adding an extra condition or ruling, e.g., requiring a minimum nisab.
Both change the apparent meaning of the Quranic text.
2
Recall the status of mashhur in Hanafi usul
Mashhur is between ahad and mutawatir; it provides zanni but strong evidence. Hanafis consider it sufficient to override the general Quranic meaning.
Ahad is weaker and cannot do this.
3
Note the Shafi'i counter-position
Shafi'is treat mashhur as still ahad unless it reaches tawatur; they allow ahad to specify only under conditions like being corroborated by qiyas.
This affects the practical rulings.
4
Apply to a concrete example
Quran says 'forbidden to you are your mothers, daughters...' (4:23). A mashhur hadith adds 'and a woman and her paternal aunt cannot be married together'. This is ziyada. Hanafis accept it; Shafi'is may require additional proof.
The difference leads to different fatwas.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of hadith chains and a critique of Ibn Hazm's position. The author argues that not all narrations from trustworthy narrators are authentic; defects ('illal) can occur even in the hadith of trustworthy narrators. The author refutes Ibn Hazm's claim by pointing to the agreement of four hadith imams on the error of Jarir ibn Hazim, which was based on careful examination. The author also clarifies that singularity (tafarrud) is not itself a defect but highlights potential errors. The author then examines multiple weak chains of a specific hadith about a gift to 'A'ishah and Hafsah, demonstrating each chain's weakness due to unreliable narrators. The conclusion is that the hadith does not meet the conditions of authenticity. This analysis is relevant to Hanafi usul because the authenticity of hadiths forms the basis for legal rulings.

Key Terms
العلة (al-'illah): A hidden defect in a hadith that undermines its authenticity, even if the chain appears sound.
الشذوذ (al-shudhudh): A narration that contradicts a more reliable narrator, considered a defect.
التفرد (al-tafarrud): When a narrator is the sole transmitter of a hadith; it is not a defect in itself but may highlight a defect.

Identifying Weak Chains: Practical Hadith Criticism in Hanafi Usul
Practical application of chain analysis to reject a hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify multiple types of defects in hadith chains from the example provided and state whether the hadith meets the conditions of authenticity.

Core Idea
A hadith with multiple weak chains is not strengthened if each chain suffers from its own defect; the overall authenticity depends on the strongest chain.

After establishing the theoretical principle that even trustworthy narrators can err, the author provides a concrete example: a hadith about a gift to 'A'ishah and Hafsah. The author examines several chains, each revealing different weaknesses such as unreliable narrators, singularity with hidden defects, and contradictions. This lesson walks through that analysis, showing how a Hanafi usuli would critically evaluate multiple chains to determine if a hadith can be used as evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main hadith and its multiple chains
The hadith in question is about a gift to 'A'ishah and Hafsah. List the chains mentioned: from Tabarani, Ibn Abi Shaybah, Bazzar, etc.
Each chain is presented as a separate narration.
2
Examine each chain's narrators for weakness
For each chain, note the narrator who is criticized (e.g., Ya'qub ibn Muhammad al-Zuhri, Hisham ibn 'Abdullah, Khusayf, Hammad ibn al-Walid, Muhammad ibn Abi Salamah).
The author cites specific criticisms from imams like Ahmad, al-Saji, Ibn Hibban, etc.
3
Determine the type of defect
Classify each weakness: some are weak due to the narrator being matruk, munkar al-hadith, or having singular narrations that are baseless.
Tafarrud is used here to highlight the defect, not as the defect itself.
4
Assess overall authenticity
Since all chains are weak and cannot support each other (due to independent defects), the hadith is not authentic.
The author concludes: 'The hadith is not authentically continuous and does not meet the conditions of authenticity.'

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of the hadith evidence for the obligation (wujub) of saying Bismillah at the start of wudu. The author presents the first opinion (wajib) and its supporters: Hasan al-Basri, Imam Ahmad (in one narration), Ishaq ibn Rahawayh, and the Zaydiyya. Their primary evidence is the addition of the phrase 'Bismillah' narrated by Ma'mar in the hadith of the Prophet: 'There is no prayer without wudu, and no wudu for one who does not mention the name of Allah upon it.' The author then lists the various chains through which this hadith is reported from numerous companions (Saeed ibn Zaid, Abu Hurairah, Ibn Umar, Ibn Mas'ud, Sahl ibn Sa'd, Abu Sa'id al-Khudri, Ali ibn Abi Talib, and Aisha), evaluating each chain for authenticity. Most chains are deemed weak due to issues like unknown narrators or disconnection. However, the author notes that despite individual weakness, the collective strength of the multiple chains may give the hadith some basis, as stated by Ibn Hajar and Abu Bakr ibn Abi Shaybah. The passage then transitions to a sub-issue: the ruling if one forgets the tasmiyah, stating there are two opinions. The second part of the text repeats the same hadith chains, likely to apply the same evidence to the sub-issue. The overall purpose is to show how variation in hadith chains (specifically the addition of a phrase by one narrator) leads to juristic disagreement on the fiqh ruling.

Key Terms
التسمية (al-tasmiyah): Mentioning the name of Allah, specifically saying 'Bismillah' at the start of wudu.
زيادة معمر (ziyadat Ma'mar): The additional phrase 'Bismillah' added by the narrator Ma'mar in the hadith chain.
ثِقَة حجة (thiqa hujja): A trustworthy narrator whose narration is considered authoritative proof.
