Hanafi Position on Khabar Wahid vs. Qiyas
Stating the Hanafi detailed stance on prioritization when khabar wahid conflicts with qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on prioritizing khabar wahid over qiyas and distinguish the early majority view from the later conditioned view.

Core Idea
Early Hanafis unconditionally prefer khabar wahid over qiyas; later Hanafis condition acceptance on narrator's fiqh expertise.

This passage clarifies a famous but oversimplified Hanafi rule: the condition that khabar wahid must not contradict qiyas. In fact, the school has detailed views. Most early Hanafis unconditionally gave precedence to the hadith, arguing that qiyas is ijtihad and where a text exists, ijtihad has no place. A later group, followed by many muta'akhkhirin, conditioned acceptance on the narrator's mastery of fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Define qiyas and its authority
Read the passage's definition: qiyas is analogical deduction, a valid proof linking rulings via a common cause. Understand that it requires reflection and ijtihad.
Qiyas yields zanni knowledge.
2
Identify the conflict scenario
The core issue: when a khabar wahid (solitary report) contradicts a qiyas-derived ruling, which is given priority? There is a famous claim that Hanafis require khabar not to oppose qiyas, but the text shows nuance.
Focus on the internal Hanafi disagreement, not external inter-madhhab disputes.
3
Distinguish the two Hanafi positions
First: most early Hanafis (أكثر المتقدمين) unconditionally prefer the khabar because 'لا اجتهاد في مورد النص'. Second: some early and many later Hanafis prefer qiyas but condition acceptance on the narrator's fiqh competence: narrator known as faqih → accept unconditionally; narrator only known for transmission → accept only if the khabar agrees with qiyas or another qiyas.
The text ends mid-sentence indicating continuation, but the core positions are clear.

ذهب جمهور عُلَماء الأمة إلى القول بحجية القياس، وأنه أحد أدلة الأحكام الشرعية ومصادرها في الفقه الإسلامي والقياس هُوَ: حمل معلوم على معلوم في إثبات حكم لهما، أو نفيه عنهما، بأمرٍ جامع بَيْنَهُما من إثبات حكم أو صفة أو نفيهما عنهما. لذا كَانَ مبتنى القياس النظر والإستنباط من تصرفات الشارع وربط الأحكام بعللها، فإذا عارض خبر الواحد القياس، فأي مِنْهُمَا يقدم موجبه عَلَى الآخر؟ اشتهر عن الحنفية اشتراط عدم مخالفة خبر الواحد للقياس حَتَّى يصح العمل بِهِ كدليل مستقل، والحق أن هَذَا الموطن لَيْسَ محل اتفاق بَيْنَهُمْ، بَلْ هناك تفصيل في مذهبهم على النحو الآتي: إذا تعارض خبر الآحاد مع القياس فأكثر المتقدمين من الحنفية على تقديم الخبر وافق القياس أو خالفه؛ لأن القياس اجتهاد ولا اجتهاد في مورد النص. وأما الَّذِيْنَ قالوا بتقديم القياس عَلَى خبر الواحد فهم بَعْض المتقدمين مِنْهُمْ، وتابعهم عَلَيْهِ كَثِير من المتأخرين ولكنهم لَمْ يقولوا بالرد بإطلاق، بل قسموا الرُّوَاة على قسمين: الأول: الرُّوَاة المعروفون بالضبط والفقه والاجتهاد، كالخلفاء الأربعة والعبادلة ومعاذ بن جبل وزيد بن ثابت - رضي الله عَنْهُمْ - فهؤلاء تقبل أخبارهم باتفاق. الثاني: الرُّوَاة الَّذِيْنَ اشتهروا بالرواية، وَلَمْ يعرفوا بالفقه والاجتهاد والفتيا، فإذا جاءوا بخبر الآحاد، فإن وافق القياس قبل، وإن خالف القياس ووافق قياساً آخر قبل
Fifth Section: Contradiction of Hadith with Qiyas. The majority of scholars of the Ummah affirm the authority of qiyas, and that it is one of the proofs of legal rulings and its sources in Islamic jurisprudence. Qiyas is: attaching a known to a known in affirming a ruling for both, or negating it from both, by a unifying factor between them from affirming a ruling or attribute or negating them from both. Therefore the foundation of qiyas is reflection and derivation from the actions of the Lawgiver and linking rulings to their causes. So if a khabar wahid (solitary report) contradicts qiyas, which one takes precedence? It is famously attributed to the Hanafis that they stipulate that a khabar wahid must not contradict qiyas in order to be acted upon as an independent proof. But the truth is that this is not a point of agreement among them; rather there is detail in their madhhab as follows: When khabar ahad conflicts with qiyas, most of the early Hanafis give precedence to the khabar, whether it agrees with qiyas or opposes it, because qiyas is ijtihad and there is no ijtihad when a text exists. As for those who said to give precedence to qiyas over khabar wahid, they are some of the early ones, and many later ones followed them. However they did not absolutely reject khabar wahid; rather they divided the narrators into two categories: First: narrators known for precision, jurisprudence, and ijtihad, such as the four caliphs, the 'Abdullahs, Mu'adh ibn Jabal, and Zayd ibn Thabit (may Allah be pleased with them) – their reports are accepted by agreement. Second: narrators known for transmission but not known for jurisprudence, ijtihad, and issuing fatwas – if they bring a khabar wahid, if it agrees with qiyas it is accepted, and if it contradicts qiyas but agrees with another qiyas it is accepted.

Commentary
This passage is a statement of a usul rule: the Hanafi position on the conflict between khabar wahid and qiyas. The author begins by defining qiyas and noting its authority. Then he presents the famous Hanafi condition that khabar wahid must not oppose qiyas to be acted upon. However, he clarifies that this is not unanimous. Most early Hanafis actually prefer the khabar over qiyas unconditionally, because a textual proof (nass) takes precedence over ijtihad. A group of later Hanafis (and some early ones) prefer qiyas but do not reject all khabar wahid; they distinguish between narrators known for fiqh and those only known for transmission. For the latter, they require the khabar to agree with qiyas or at least with some other qiyas. This shows the nuance within the Hanafi school on dealing with apparent conflicts between transmitted reports and rational analogy.

Key Terms
خبر الواحد (khabar al-wahid): A report transmitted by one or a few narrators that does not reach the level of tawatur.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical deduction: applying a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a shared cause.

The Hadith of Tasriyah: How an Explicit Text Overrides Analogical Reasoning (Istihsan)
Analyzing the dispute over the hadith allowing return with dates and the objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the five qiyas objections to the hadith of tasriyah and explain the Hanafi responses using istihsan.

Core Idea
An explicit hadith can override qiyas when it prevents harm or fixes a precise compensation.

This passage presents a classic Hanafi argument that a specific, authentic hadith cannot be set aside by analogical reasoning. The opponents raise five objections based on general principles, but the Hanafi response defends the hadith as operative, illustrating the principle of istihsan (juristic preference) over strict qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith and its ruling
Locate the hadith of Abu Hurairah and state the ruling it provides: the buyer's option to return with a sa' of dates.
The hadith is the primary dalil for the Hanafi position.
2
List the five qiyas objections
Extract each of the five arguments raised by opponents who claim the hadith contradicts qiyas.
Objections: no defect, kharaj principle, mithli compensation, value principle, fixed amount incommensurate.
3
Analyze each Hanafi rebuttal
For each objection, identify the Hanafi response: (1) fraud establishes option; (2) milk existed at contract; (3) milk is not mithli; (4) fixed amount cuts dispute; (5) mixing prevents precise compensation.
Focus on how each rebuttal defends the explicit text.
4
Understand the role of istihsan
Recognize that the Hanafis employ istihsan (juristic preference) to uphold the hadith over qiyas, prioritizing textual authority and practical benefit.
Istihsan is a secondary source that allows departure from qiyas for a stronger evidence or public interest.
5
Apply the principle to analogous cases
Consider how the same principle—a clear text overriding qiyas—applies to other rulings, such as fixed compensation for mixed goods.
The key is the existence of a specific, authentic text that provides a complete ruling.

And those who permitted (the return with a sa' of dates) argued with the hadith of Abu Hurairah that the Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'Do not tie the udders of camels and sheep; so whoever buys them after that, he has the choice of two options after milking them: if he wishes, he may keep it, and if he wishes, he may return it with a sa' of dates.' And those who said it is not permissible responded by saying that this hadith contradicts qiyas in several ways: 1. Returning a sold item without a defect or discrepancy in description is not established by the principles of Shari'ah; because tasriyah is not a defect, since sale requires soundness of the item, and lack of milk is not among defects that negate soundness; because milk is a yield, and its absence does not negate the attribute of soundness, so its scarcity is even more so. 2. The principle is that profit is with liability, so the milk that comes into existence with the buyer is not guaranteed, yet it has been ruled to be guaranteed. 3. The guaranteed item (milk) is a fungible (mithli) and the rule is that fungibles are compensated with their like, yet he compensated it with something other than its like. 4. In compensation, if one moves from the like, one moves to value; and the dates mentioned in the hadith are neither value nor like. 5. The guaranteed property is valued according to its amount, little or much; and the amount is specified here, which is the sa'. And they answered the first objection: that there is nothing in the principles of Shari'ah indicating that the causes of return are limited to these two matters; rather, the option is established for the buyer due to fraud, because the buyer saw the udder full of milk and thought that was its habit, so it is as if the seller had stipulated that for him; and when the contrary becomes clear, the right to return is established for him due to the absence of the implicit condition we have mentioned. And regarding the second: 'al-kharaj' is a term for yield, such as the earnings of a slave and the hire of an animal and the like. As for offspring and milk, they are not called kharaj; the common factor between them is that they are benefits; otherwise, the earning and yield that come into existence were not present at the time of the sale, but arose after possession. As for the milk here, it was present at the time of the contract, so it was part of the object of contract, and the sa' was not fixed by the Legislator as compensation for the milk that arises later, but rather it is compensation for the milk that was present at the time of the contract in the udder, so its guarantee is part of complete justice. Third: it is absolutely impossible to compensate with its like, because milk in the udder is preserved and is not subject to spoilage, but when milked it becomes subject to souring and spoilage. Fourth: if we were to entrust its estimation to them or to one of them, disputes would abound; so the Legislator eliminated dispute by a fixed amount that cannot be exceeded to cut off contention. Fifth: the milk that arises after the contract mixes with what was present at that time, and its amount is not known so that we could require its equivalent; it may be more or less, leading to riba.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed juridical analysis of a hadith regarding the option of returning an animal whose udder was tied (tasriyah) to give a false impression of abundant milk. The hadith grants the buyer a choice: keep the animal or return it with a sa' of dates. The opponents argue that this hadith contradicts qiyas on five grounds. The Hanafi response (based on istihsan) refutes each objection: (1) fraud establishes an option, not just defect or misdescription; (2) 'kharaj' does not include milk because milk existed at contract time; (3) milk cannot be compensated with its like due to perishability; (4) a fixed amount prevents litigation; (5) the mixing of pre- and post-contract milk necessitates a fixed measure to avoid riba. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that an explicit, authentic hadith can override analogical reasoning when necessary to prevent harm or ease transactions.

Key Terms
تصریة (tasriyah): The act of tying an animal's udder so that milk accumulates, giving a false impression of high milk yield.
صاع (sa'): A measure of volume, here specifically a sa' of dates as compensation.
خراج بالضمان (al-kharaj bi al-daman): Principle: profit is with liability; one who bears the risk also reaps the benefit.
