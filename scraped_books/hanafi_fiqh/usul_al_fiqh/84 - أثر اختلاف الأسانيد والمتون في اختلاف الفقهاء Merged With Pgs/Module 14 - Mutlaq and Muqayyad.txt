The Dispute on the Extent of Wiping in Tayammum
Presents three opinions on tayammum wiping and their evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of mutlaq/muqayyad to evaluate the Shafi'i proof for wiping to the elbows in tayammum and identify the weakness in the hadith cited.

Core Idea
The extent of wiping in tayammum hinges on whether 'yad' is taken as mutlaq (entire arm) or muqayyad (hand only).

This passage examines how the Shafi'i school derives the ruling that tayammum wiping extends to the elbows, using both the Quranic word 'yad' and a hadith. A critical usul analysis reveals that the hadith is weak (munqati') and that the application of the mutlaq term is contested. We will work through the evidence and see how these differences in textual interpretation generate ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
What is the precise disputed ruling? The extent of wiping in tayammum: is it to the elbows (Shafi'i) or to the wrists (other opinions)?
This is a fiqh ruling with a clear usul dimension.
2
Analyze the Shafi'i Quranic Proof
The Shafi'is argue that the word 'yad' in the Quran (5:6) is general and includes the entire arm up to the shoulder. They claim that in tayammum, this general meaning is restricted by analogy to wudu (which is to the elbows). Identify the usul operation: they treat 'yad' as mutlaq and then apply taqyid from another context.
Hanafi usul holds that mutlaq remains general until a specific dalil restricts it; here, the taqyid from wudu is not directly applicable to tayammum because they are separate acts.
3
Evaluate the Hadith Evidence
The Shafi'is also cite a hadith in which the Prophet wiped his forearms. The passage notes that this hadith is defective (ma'lul) due to discontinuity (inqita'): al-A'raj did not hear from Ibn al-Simmah. This weakens the proof.
In usul, a hadith that is munqati' is not acceptable as a primary proof (dalil) for establishing a ruling unless corroborated.
4
Identify Alternative Views
The passage mentions a third opinion (to the wrists) attributed to Ali, Ahmad, Zaydis, and Zahiris, and an old Shafi'i view (also to the wrists).
These views rely on the minimal application of 'yad' and the second hadith of Ammar (not quoted).
5
Determine the Hanafi Position
Based on usul, the Hanafi school holds that 'yad' in the tayammum verse is mutlaq and is restricted by the Sunnah (hadith of Ammar) to the hands and wrists. The Shafi'i proof is insufficient because the hadith is weak and the taqyid from wudu is not valid.
This illustrates how different usul methodologies lead to different fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage examines how differences in interpreting Quranic texts and hadith lead to varying fiqh rulings on tayammum. The Shafi'i position holds that wiping must reach the elbows, based on the general meaning of 'yad' in the verse from Surah al-Ma'idah (5:6). They argue that 'yad' in Arabic encompasses the entire arm including the shoulder, and thus the forearm is included. However, in tayammum, they restrict this to the wudu ruling (which is to the elbows). They also rely on a hadith where the Prophet (SAW) wiped his forearms. However, the hadith is considered munqati' (disconnected) because al-A'raj did not directly hear from Ibn al-Simmah, thus it is weak. From Imam al-Shafi'i's earlier (qadim) position, transmitted by Abu Thawr and al-Za'farani, he said it is to the wrists (kaw'ayn), but al-Nawawi rejected this attribution. The third opinion (to the wrist) is attributed to Ali (RA) and adopted by Imam Ahmad, the Zaydis, and the Zahiris, based on the minimal application of 'yad' and the second hadith of Ammar (which is not quoted here). This illustrates how variations in textual evidence and interpretative methods cause divergence even within a single madhhab, and across madhhabs.

Key Terms
المسح (al-mas'h): Wiping with wet hands, distinguished from washing in wudu.
اليد (al-yad): In this passage, the Shafi'is define it as including the entire arm up to the shoulder, while the third opinion takes the minimal meaning of palm to wrist.
الانقطاع (al-inqita'): Discontinuity in the chain of narration; a hadith defect where a narrator is missing or did not hear directly from the source.
