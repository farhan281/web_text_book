The Three Methods for Resolving Apparent Hadith Contradiction
Explains the three methods to resolve apparent hadith contradictions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three methods (jamʿ, naskh, tarjīḥ) for resolving apparent hadith contradictions and apply them in the correct hierarchical order according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
First reconcile, then abrogate, then prefer – the order is mandatory, not optional.

When you encounter two hadiths that seem to contradict, your instinct might be to pick one and discard the other. But Hanafi usul teaches that real contradiction between authentic proofs is impossible. This lesson introduces the three-step methodology that every mujtahid must follow: reconciliation, then abrogation, then preponderance. Mastering this hierarchy is essential for sound legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the presumption of harmony
Recognize that all legal rulings come from one divine source, so true contradiction between authentic proofs cannot occur. Apparent contradiction is only a surface-level impression that must be resolved.
This principle underpins the entire approach – never conclude two proofs are actually contradictory.
2
Identify the three methods
Name the three methods: al-jamʿ (reconciliation), al-naskh (abrogation), and al-tarjīḥ (preponderance). Note that they are not interchangeable options.
Each method has distinct conditions and triggers.
3
Apply the hierarchy
Apply the methods in the prescribed order: first attempt to reconcile (jamʿ) by interpreting both proofs in harmony; if impossible, determine if one proof abrogates the other (naskh); only if neither works, prefer one proof over the other (tarjīḥ).
Jamʿ is always prioritized because it gives effect to both proofs.
4
Analyze the rationale
Understand why jamʿ is first: it preserves both proofs, which is superior to neglecting one (as in naskh or tarjīḥ). The obligation of this sequence is derived from the principle that giving effect to two proofs is better than giving effect to one.
This reflects a core Hanafi usul value: حفظ الدليلين (preserving both evidences).
5
Distinguish between scholarly disciplines
Note that usul scholars (usuliyyun) handle this under 'al-Taʿāruḍ wa al-Tarjīḥ', while hadith scholars (muhaddithun) deal with it under 'Mukhtalif al-Ḥadīth'. Both groups converge on the same three methods.
This shows the interdisciplinary nature of the issue.

These approaches are not optional for the mujtahid; rather they are obligatory in their order. Deriving the Ruling on Forgetful Eating During Fasting
Ruling with proofs from Quran and Sunnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that forgetfulness is not intentional and is covered by general forgiveness, using Quranic and hadith evidence, to derive the ruling that forgetful eating does not invalidate fasting.

Core Idea
Forgetfulness is not intentional; thus it falls under the general principle that Allah forgives mistakes and forgetfulness.

In this lesson, we examine how the Hanafi school derives the ruling that a fasting person's fast remains valid if they eat or drink forgetfully. The derivation relies on two key proofs: a Quranic verse indicating accountability only for intentional acts of the heart, and a Prophetic hadith that the community is forgiven for mistake, forgetfulness, and coercion. By classifying these texts and applying usul principles, we see how the ruling emerges from the general principles of Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling under discussion
The passage states that the fasting person who eats or drinks forgetfully has a valid fast. Identify this as the ruling to be derived.
2
Extract the Quranic proof
Locate the verse: {But He will take you to account for what your hearts have earned}. Analyze the key phrase 'what your hearts have earned' to understand that only intentional acts are accountable.
The verse is from Surah al-Baqarah (2:225) but the passage does not give the full verse reference.
3
Extract the hadith proof
Identify the hadith: 'My community has been forgiven for mistake, forgetfulness, and what they are coerced into.' This is a general principle covering all acts.
This hadith is well-known and used across schools.
4
Apply the principle of generality (umum)
The hadith is general (amm) and includes all acts, including fasting. In Hanafi usul, a general text remains binding unless specified. Forgetfulness is explicitly mentioned, so it covers eating/drinking during fasting.
This demonstrates istinbat from general principles.
5
Derive the ruling
Since forgetfulness falls under the hadith's forgiveness and the Quranic principle excludes unintentional acts, the fast is valid and no compensation (qada or kaffarah) is required.
This is the majority opinion, as stated by the author.

Majority View on Naskh of Wudu Hadiths
Conclusion on naskh between two conflicting wudu hadiths

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of naskh to resolve conflicting hadiths by identifying the abrogating (nāsikh) and abrogated (mansūkh) texts.

Core Idea
When two authentic hadiths conflict, the later or stronger evidence abrogates the earlier.

In this passage, we see the conclusion of a classical debate: two hadiths on wudu nullification are both authentic, but the majority rules that one abrogates the other. As a student of usul, you must understand how Hanafi scholars apply naskh to resolve such conflicts. We will parse the reasoning behind the majority position and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting texts
Locate the hadiths of Busra and Talaq mentioned in the passage. Both deal with whether touching the penis nullifies wudu.
2
Confirm the authenticity (sihha) of both
The passage states the final outcome is the authenticity of both hadiths. This is a prerequisite for naskh – only authentic texts can abrogate or be abrogated.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle of naskh
The majority (jumhur) hold that Busra's hadith abrogates Talaq's. This is based on chronology or strength of evidence. In Hanafi usul, naskh of sunna by sunna is valid when the later text is established.
4
Examine the evidence for naskh
Ibn 'Abd al-Barr defends the Kufan (Hanafi) position by noting that the Companions acted on Busra's hadith, indicating it superseded the earlier practice.
5
Understand the practical outcome
The Hanafi ruling is that wudu is nullified by touching the penis. This is the operative fatwa derived from the naskh analysis.

Principles for Resolving Marfu' vs Mawquf Conflicts
Usul al-hadith principle and its critique

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the different positions on preferring marfu' over mawquf, critique the absolute preference with the author's case-by-case method, and apply the method to a hadith example.

Core Idea
No universal rule: hadith critics weigh each marfu'/mawquf case individually based on contextual evidence.

When a hadith is reported both as marfu' (raised to the Prophet) and mawquf (stopped at a Companion), which one is preferred? Some scholars, like Imam al-Nawawi, held that the marfu' should always be taken because its narrator affirms knowledge the others lack. But our author, following the practice of leading hadith critics, argues that this principle is not absolute. He shows through the example of the hadith on urine of a boy and a girl that scholars balance all evidence — sometimes preferring the marfu', sometimes the mawquf — depending on preponderators (qara'in).

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting narrations
Recognize that a hadith exists in both marfu' and mawquf forms, creating a conflict in attribution.
Example: hadith of Ali on urine of children.
2
Recall the default principle
The principle that the one who affirms (marfu') takes precedence over one who negates or stays silent, because the affirmer has knowledge the others lack.
This is the basis of Imam Nawawi's absolute preference.
3
Recognize the author's critique
The author argues that this principle is not absolute; leading critics do not apply a single rule but examine all evidence (qara'in) before deciding.
The author quotes Nawawi but then qualifies his view.
4
Apply the case-by-case approach to the example
Examine the hadith of Ali: some scholars preferred the marfu', some the mawquf, based on contextual factors like backing of other narrators.
The author refutes al-Bazzar's claim of uniqueness.
5
Summarize the methodological takeaway
Usul al-hadith prefers contextual weighting over rigid rules; the ruling depends on preponderators.
This aligns with Hanafi istihsan.

وَقَالَ الحافظ ابن حجر: إسناده صحيح إلا أنه اختلف في رفعه ووقفه، وَفِي وصله وإرساله، وَقَدْ رجح البُخَارِيّ صحته وكذا الدَّارَ قُطني)). والرواية المرفوعة رواها معاذ بن هشام، قَالَ: حَدَّثَني أبي، عن قتادة، عن أبي حرب بن أبي الأسود ، عن أبيه ، عن علي بن أبي طالب، مرفوعاً . قَالَ البزار: «هَذَا الحَدِيث لا نعلمه يروى عن النبي ، إلا من هَذَا الوجه بهذا الإسناد، وإنما أسنده معاذ بن هشام، عن أبيه، وَقَدْ رَواهُ غَيْر معاذ بن هشام، عن قتادة، عن أبي حرب، عن أبيه، عن علي، موقوفاً).

Chapter Three: Disagreement in Chain and Text. The single memorizer [is relied upon] based on preponderance of opinion, and when there is disagreement concerning what necessitates the authenticity of a hadith or its defect, recourse is to the statement of the greater number because they are further from error and oversight, and that is when they are equal in [preservation] and mastery. If they differ and the number is equal, then [recourse is] to the statement of the better memorizer and more precise. This is a rule agreed upon by hadith scholars. The fourth opinion: the mawquf is interpreted according to the narrator's school, and the musnad as his narration, so there is no contradiction. Imam Nawawi favored the first opinion among these and acted upon it in his works and often said it. What appears to me, from the practice of the brilliant hadith scholars and critics, is that they do not judge the hadith in which there is such disagreement at first glance, but they balance and compare, then judge the hadith appropriately. They may prefer the marfu' narration or the mawquf narration according to the preponderators and contextual evidence surrounding the narrations. So the ruling of hadith scholars in such matters does not fall under a general, consistent rule that encompasses all hadith. Therefore, what Imam Nawawi absolutely declared as preferred can be qualified as follows: The ruling is for the marfu' because its narrator is an affirmer while others are silent, and even if they are negators, the affirmer takes precedence over the negator because he knows what is hidden, unless the critic has evidence or contextual indications that make the mawquf preponderant. I will give examples of hadith that differed between marfu' and mawquf according to the preponderations of hadith scholars. An example of a hadith where both narrations are authentic: The hadith of Ali: 'Sprinkle from the urine of a boy and wash the urine of a girl.' Imam al-Tirmidhi said: Hisham al-Dastuwa'i raised it from Qatada, while Sa'id ibn Abi 'Aruba stopped it at Qatada and did not raise it. Al-Hafiz Ibn Hajar said: Its chain is authentic, but it differed regarding its raising and stopping, and its connection and interruption. Al-Bukhari and al-Daraqutni preferred its authenticity. The marfu' narration was narrated by Mu'adh ibn Hisham: He said: My father narrated to me from Qatada, from Abu Harb ibn Abi al-Aswad, from his father, from 'Ali ibn Abi Talib, marfu'. Al-Bazzar said: We do not know this hadith narrated from the Prophet except from this route with this chain; only Mu'adh ibn Hisham narrated it marfu' from his father, while others narrated it from Qatada, from Abu Harb, from his father, from 'Ali, mawquf. I say: Al-Bazzar's absolute statement that Mu'adh ibn Hisham was alone in raising it is incorrect, because Mu'adh was followed in that by 'Abd al-Samad ibn 'Abd al-Warith in Ahmad and al-Daraqutni. Therefore, al-Daraqutni's statement was more precise when he said: Qatada reported it from Abu Harb ibn Abi al-Aswad, from his father; Hisham ibn Abi 'Abd Allah raised it from his son Mu'adh's narration.

Commentary
This passage is from a work discussing how differences in hadith chains and texts impact juristic differences. The author begins by presenting a rule from hadith criticism: when there is disagreement about a hadith's authenticity or defect, the opinion of the majority of narrators is given weight, provided they are equal in precision. If numbers are equal, the opinion of the more precise and memorizer is taken. This rule is agreed upon by hadith scholars. The author then mentions a fourth opinion: that a mawquf (stopped at Companion) narration should be interpreted as reflecting the narrator's own school, while a musnad (continuous) narration is treated as his direct report, so there is no real contradiction. Imam Nawawi preferred the first opinion. However, the author argues that in practice, hadith scholars do not apply a single mechanical rule; they weigh each case based on contextual factors. They may prefer the marfu' (raised to Prophet) over mawquf or vice versa. The author qualifies Nawawi's unconditional preference for marfu': normally the affirmer (rafi') takes precedence over the negator, but if evidence points to the mawquf, it can be preferred. The author then provides an example: the hadith about sprinkling for a boy's urine and washing for a girl's. Tirmidhi noted that Hisham raised it to the Prophet while Sa'id stopped it at Qatada. Ibn Hajar said the chain is authentic but there is disagreement. Al-Bazzar claimed Mu'adh ibn Hisham was alone in raising it, but the author points out that Mu'adh was followed by another narrator, so al-Daraqutni's more precise statement acknowledges that Hisham (Mu'adh's father) raised it from Qatada. This shows that hadith critics do not simply rely on number or memorization alone but examine all evidence.

Key Terms
المرفوع (al-marfu'): A hadith attributed (raised) to the Prophet Muhammad, whether in statement, action, or approval.
الموقوف (al-mawquf): A hadith that is attributed to a Companion (stopped at the Companion), not going back to the Prophet.
المسند (al-musnad): A hadith with a continuous chain reaching back to the Prophet or a Companion, but here used for marfu' specifically.
القرائن (al-qara'in): Contextual evidence or supporting indicators that help determine the strength of a narration.
الموازنة (al-muwazanah): Balancing and comparing narrations by weighing evidence.
