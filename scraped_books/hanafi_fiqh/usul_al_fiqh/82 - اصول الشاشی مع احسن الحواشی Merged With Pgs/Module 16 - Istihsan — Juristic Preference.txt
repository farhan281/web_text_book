Implication (Iqtida') and the Necessity Principle
Explaining how the word 'i'tadi' implies talaq by necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of iqtida' (implication) to derive the fiqh ruling when an ambiguous command is specified by intention, and you will be able to identify the limitation imposed by darurah (necessity) in establishing only the minimum necessary ruling.

Core Idea
Implication establishes only what is necessary for validity, not extra attributes.

When a husband tells his wife 'i'tadi' (observe your waiting period) after consummation with the intention of divorce, the word itself is ambiguous. The Hanafi school uses the principle of iqtida' (implication) to infer a necessary precondition: divorce must have occurred for 'iddah to be meaningful. However, the necessity (darurah) only demands the minimum — a revocable divorce — and not an irrevocable one. This lesson walks you through the specific steps of deriving this ruling from the text, highlighting the crucial role of darurah in limiting implication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous term
The word 'i'tadi' has multiple possible meanings (counting favors, dirhams, or menstrual periods). Recognize that it is mujmal (ambiguous) and requires specification.
This ambiguity is the starting point for the istinbat.
2
Apply intention (niyyah) to specify
When the husband intends 'menstrual periods' (al-aqra'), the word is specified to mean 'observe the 'iddah of divorce.' The niyyah removes the ambiguity.
Intention (niyyah) is a valid method of takhsis (specification) for an ambiguous word.
3
Invoke iqtida' (implication)
The command to observe 'iddah logically requires a prior divorce to make sense. Therefore, divorce is established by implication (iqtida'), as if the husband said 'I divorced you, so observe the 'iddah.'
Without this implication, the command would be meaningless or contradictory.
4
Apply darurah (necessity) to limit scope
Only the minimum necessary to validate the command is established. A revocable (raj'i) divorce suffices for 'iddah; an irrevocable (ba'in) divorce is an extra attribute not needed.
Al-Marghinani states: 'The necessity is removed by the lower, so the higher is not established.'
5
Extract the final ruling
Thus, the outcome is one revocable divorce (talaq raj'i). No more than one divorce occurs, and it is not irrevocable.
This ruling is specific to the Hanafi school's application of iqtida' and darurah.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the Hanafi principle of 'iqtida' (implication) as applied to the command 'i'tadi' (observe your waiting period). The sharih explains that when a husband says 'i'tadi' after consummation with the intention of divorce, the word itself is ambiguous. It could mean various things, but when the intention specifies 'menstrual periods,' the command necessitates the existence of a divorce to make sense of the command (since 'iddah only occurs after divorce). Thus divorce is established by implication. However, only the necessary minimum is established: a revocable (raj'i) divorce, not an irrevocable (ba'in) one, because the necessity is only for the existence of divorce, not for its heightened form. The hashiya also clarifies that specifying an absolute term (like 'food' to a particular type) is specification (takhsis), not restriction (taqyid), and that extra attributes beyond necessity cannot be established by implication. This lesson guards against the misunderstanding that implication can establish more than what is strictly necessary, and it demonstrates how Hanafi usul treats ambiguous commands in legal contexts.

Key Terms
اقتضاء (iqtidā’): The implied meaning necessitated by the literal wording for the validity of the speech.
ضرورة (ḍarūrah): The necessity that makes the implied meaning valid; only the minimum to remove the necessity is established.

Cessation of Rulings When Their Cause Ceases
Illustrates the principle that a ruling ends when its 'illah ends, using examples from zakat, khums, and purification with vinegar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a ruling ceases when its 'illah (cause) ceases, and distinguish between hadath (ritual impurity) and khabath (actual impurity) in determining purification.

Core Idea
A ruling tied to a cause ends when that cause ends—this governs zakat shares, khums shares, and even purification.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi illustrates a fundamental Hanafi principle: rulings that are established by a specific cause (illah) cease when that cause no longer exists. The commentator provides three concrete examples from fiqh: the elimination of the 'mu'allafatu qulubuhum' category in zakat, the removal of the share for the Prophet's relatives (dhawi al-qurba) from khums, and the purification of actual impurity (khabath) using vinegar. Through these examples, you will learn how to identify the 'illah of a ruling and determine when a ruling expires.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Read the opening statement: 'the fall of the ruling due to the fall of its foundation and cause'. Recognize that this is the core usul maxim being demonstrated.
2
Analyze the First Example (Mu'allafah)
Examine why the category of 'those whose hearts are to be reconciled' was removed from zakat. Identify the ruling (giving them zakat) and its 'illah (weakness of Islam needing reconciliation). Note how when Islam became strong, the cause ceased, so the ruling ceased.
3
Analyze the Second Example (Dhawi al-Qurba)
Similarly, the share of the Prophet's relatives from khums was given for their support. After the Prophet's death, the need for their support ended, so their share ceased. Confirm the 'illah (support/nusrah) and its cessation.
4
Analyze the Third Example (Purification with Vinegar)
Consider the case of washing an impure garment with vinegar. The 'illah of impurity is the presence of the impurity itself. When the impurity is removed by vinegar, the cause ends, so the place becomes pure. Note that this applies only to khabath (actual impurity), not to hadath (ritual impurity).
5
Distinguish Hadath and Khabath
Understand the key distinction: hadath (ritual impurity) is a legal impurity that cannot be removed by vinegar; its purification requires water (or earth) as a specific act of worship. Khabath (actual impurity) is removed by any substance that eliminates it, because its cause is the physical presence of impurity.

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary on the principle that a ruling ceases when its cause ('illah) ceases. Three examples are provided: (1) The category of 'mu'allafatu qulubuhum' (those whose hearts are to be reconciled) in zakat was removed after the time of the Prophet because the cause—weakness of Islam needing reconciliation—ceased when Islam became strong. (2) The share of dhawi al-qurba (relatives of the Prophet) from the khums (fifth of spoils) was also removed after his death because the cause—their support for him—ended. (3) Washing an impure garment with vinegar: since vinegar removes the actual impurity, the garment becomes pure because the cause of impurity (the presence of impurity) has been removed. However, this does not apply to ritual impurity (hadath) like that requiring wudu or ghusl, because those purifications are only achieved by using a specific purifier (water) as prescribed by shari'ah, and the cause is not removal of impurity but the act of using the purifier. This distinction between khabath (actual impurity, which can be removed by any liquid) and hadath (ritual impurity, which requires water) is a key Hanafi usul point. The passage notes that this contradicts Imam al-Shafi'i's view, likely on the issue of purification with non-water liquids. The commentary thus reinforces the Hanafi position that rulings are tied to their causes, and when the cause ceases, the ruling ceases.

Key Terms
المؤلفة قلوبهم (al-mu'allafatu qulubuhum): Those whose hearts are to be reconciled—a category of zakat recipients who were given zakat to incline them to Islam or to strengthen the Muslims. Their share ceased after the cause (weakness of Islam) ended.
الحدث (al-hadath): Ritual impurity (e.g., requiring wudu or ghusl), which is a legal impurity that cannot be removed by vinegar or other liquids; only water (or pure earth for tayammum) can remove it.
الخبث (al-khabath): Actual impurity (najasa) such as blood, urine, etc. It is removed by physically eliminating the impurity, e.g., by washing with vinegar or water.
ذوي القربى (dhawi al-qurba): The relatives of the Prophet (peace be upon him). They were given a share from the khums (fifth of spoils) during his lifetime for support. After his death, their share ceased because the cause (support) ended.

Confirmed Taharri: Binding Effect and Exception for Changeable Matters
Explains the rule of confirmed taharri and its exception for changeable matters

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that a taharri confirmed by action is not invalidated by a later mere taharri, and distinguish between matters that admit change (like Qibla) and those that do not (like cloth impurity).

Core Idea
Once a taharri is confirmed by action, it is binding unless the subject matter admits change.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi (with its commentary) introduces a crucial rule in Hanafi usul: when a person exerts effort (taharri) to resolve a doubt and then acts on that exertion, a later mere change of opinion does not undo the first action. However, this rule has an exception: if the matter itself is one that can legitimately change (like the direction of prayer or the number of takbirs in Eid), then a new exertion can override the first, akin to abrogation. Understanding this distinction is vital for applying ijtihad in cases of repeated doubt.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basic case
Consider a traveler with two cloths, one pure and one impure. He does not know which is which. He performs taharri and prays Zuhr in one cloth. At Asr, his taharri now indicates the other cloth. What should he do?
This sets up the scenario where action confirms the first taharri.
2
State the rule for cloths
The passage rules that he must continue praying in the first cloth because his first taharri was confirmed by action. A later mere taharri cannot invalidate it.
Reason: the cloth's impurity does not transfer; the first action was valid.
3
Contrast with Qibla
If the same doubt occurs about Qibla direction, the rule is different. If after praying to one direction, his opinion changes, he should face the new direction for future prayers.
Why? Because Qibla admits change (it was abrogated from Jerusalem to the Ka'ba).
4
Identify the underlying principle
The difference hinges on whether the subject matter can legitimately change. Matters that do not admit change (like impurity in a cloth) cannot be overridden by a later ijtihad; doing so would imply multiple truths. Matters that admit change (like Qibla or number of takbirs) can be changed by a later ijtihad, analogous to naskh.
This is a key usul principle: action confirms the first ijtihad unless change is inherent.
5
Apply to a new case
The passage gives the example of an imam in Eid prayer who starts with one opinion on takbir numbers (e.g., Ibn Abbas's thirteen) and then changes to another (Ibn Mas'ud's nine). He acts on the new opinion for remaining rak'ahs because takbir numbers admit change.
The first rak'ah remains valid; the change applies prospectively.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi (with Ahsan al-Hawashi) is a rule about taharri (exertion to determine the ruling) and its effect when confirmed by action. The passage opens with the obligation of taharri for a traveler who has two cloths, one pure and one impure, and cannot determine which is which. The reason is that water has a substitute (tayammum) but cloth does not; therefore, he must exert effort to identify the pure one. Shar'an, once he performs taharri and confirms it by acting (e.g., praying in one cloth), a later mere taharri pointing to the other cloth does not invalidate the first. He must continue praying in the first cloth because it was confirmed by action. However, this rule does not apply to the Qibla direction. If a person exerts for Qibla and prays in one direction, then later his opinion changes and he believes another direction is correct, he should turn to the new direction. The difference is that Qibla admits change: it transitioned from Jerusalem to the Ka'ba. The commentary elaborates that in matters that do not admit change (like impurity in a cloth), allowing later contrary ijtihad would lead to multiplicity of truths, which is invalid. In matters admitting change, later ijtihad can override the first like abrogation without multiplicity. The passage then illustrates with the disagreement among the Companions on the number of takbirs in Eid prayer. If an imam starts with one opinion and then changes to another, he acts on the new opinion for remaining rak'ahs because the number of takbirs admits change, analogous to abrogation. The previous acts remain valid.

Key Terms
تحري (taharri): Exertion to determine the correct ruling or object in a case of doubt, based on available evidence.
تحري مؤكد بالعمل (taharri mu'akkad bil 'amal): Taharri that is confirmed and strengthened by subsequent action, which prevents it from being invalidated by a later mere taharri.
محتمل الانتقال (muhtamil al-intiqal): A matter that admits change or transfer, such that a later ijtihad can override the first like abrogation.

Istishab al-Hal: Defense Only, Not Imposition
Explaining the Hanafi rule that istishab is for defense only

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi use of istishab for defense (daf') vs. imposition (ilzam) and apply this distinction to three illustrative cases.

Core Idea
Istishab preserves the status quo; it cannot create new obligations.

This passage from al-Shashi's Usul, with commentary, establishes a key Hanafi usul principle: that istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) is a valid proof only for defensive purposes, not for imposing new legal effects. The principle is explained through three concrete examples that show how the presumption works in practice.

Guided Path
1
Define istishab and its scope
Understand the definition of istishab al-hal and the Hanafi limitation that it is valid only for defense (daf'), not for imposition (ilzam).
The Arabic terms are دفع (daf') and إلزام (ilzam).
2
Analyze the first case: unknown lineage person
A person of unknown lineage is claimed as a slave. If he is injured, the claimant cannot impose free person's blood money because that would be an imposition using istishab.
The presumption of freedom (istishab) can defend against the claim of slavery, but cannot impose a higher blood money.
3
Analyze the second case: menstruation beyond habit
A woman with a known menstrual habit bleeds beyond it. She returns to her habit and the excess is istihadah because imposing a change on the habit would be an imposition without evidence.
Istishab preserves the known habit; it does not extend the bleeding's ruling.
4
Analyze the third case: missing person
A missing person (mafqud) does not inherit from relatives who die during his absence, nor do they inherit from him. His life is presumed by istishab, but that presumption only defends his rights; it cannot impose inheritance obligations.
This case clearly shows istishab for defense only.

Chapter: Arguing without evidence. It is valid for defense not for imposition. Based on this we say: A person of unknown lineage, if someone claims him as a slave, then if he injures him, the blood money of a free man is not obligatory; because imposing the blood money of a free man is an imposition, so it is not established by istishab. Similarly, if a woman's bleeding exceeds ten days and she has a known habit, she returns to her habit and the excess is istihadah; because imposing the excess on her habit is an imposition. And if the bleeding connects with menstrual blood and istihadah blood and is ambiguous, if we rule nullification of the habit, we would be acting without evidence. Similarly, if she begins as a mustahadah at puberty, her habit is ten days. And the case of the missing person: he does not inherit from those who die during his absence, and others do not inherit from him; because his life is established by istishab, which is valid for defense not imposition. If it is objected that Abu Hanifa said 'no khums in amber', that is holding to absence of evidence, we respond: that is a text.

Commentary
This passage from al-Shashi's Usul, with the hashya of Ahsan al-Hawashi, expounds the Hanafi ruling on istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity). The core principle is that istishab is a valid proof (hujja) for defensive purposes (daf') but not for imposing new obligations (ilzam). The author provides three illustrative cases: (1) A person of unknown lineage claimed as a slave - if a free man injures him, he must pay the blood money of a free man; but if the claimant injures him, he does not pay the blood money of a free man because that would be imposing an obligation based on mere claim, which istishab (presumption of freedom) cannot support. (2) A woman with menstrual bleeding exceeding ten days - if she has a known habit, she retains that habit; any additional bleeding is considered istihadah (non-menstrual). If her bleeding pattern is ambiguous, the ruling follows the established habit to avoid acting without evidence. (3) The missing person (mafqud) - he does not inherit from relatives who die during his absence, and they do not inherit from him; his life is presumed by istishab, but that presumption only defends his own rights, it does not impose obligations on others. The hashya further clarifies that objections about the hadith on amber are answered by distinguishing between relying on absence of evidence (which is not proof) and relying on a specific text (nass). The Hanafi position is that istishab is a valid argument but only for defense, not for creating new legal effects.

Key Terms
استصحاب الحال (istishab al-hal): The presumption of continuity of a previously established state of affairs until evidence of change is presented.
دفع (daf'): Defense: using a proof to maintain the current status quo and prevent a change.
إلزام (ilzam): Imposition: using a proof to establish a new obligation or legal effect.
