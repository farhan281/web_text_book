Mutlaq from Quran Cannot Be Restricted by Khabar al-Wahid or Qiyas
States the Hanafi rule that absolute Quranic texts are not subject to restriction by ahad reports or analogy.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a mutlaq from the Quran is not restricted by khabar al-wahid or qiyas, and apply it to distinguish between permissible clarification and impermissible abrogation.

Core Idea
Absolute Quranic commands are definitive; adding conditions via ahad reports is abrogation, not clarification.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi lays down a foundational Hanafi usul rule: when the Quran speaks absolutely (mutlaq), and it is possible to act on that absolute meaning, no one may add conditions—like intention or order—based on a single-transmitter report or analogy. The Hanafis consider such additions to be abrogation (naskh) of a definitive text by a speculative one, which is invalid. This lesson unpacks the reasoning, contrasts it with the Shafi'i view, and applies it to the classic example of intention in wudu.

Guided Path
1
Define the absolute (mutlaq)
Identify the Hanafi definition: mutlaq is the common share among individuals of a quiddity without considering attributes. It is not ambiguous because it can be acted upon literally.
The passage defines mutlaq in footnote (1): 'the common share among individuals...'
2
State the rule
The absolute from the Quran cannot be restricted by khabar al-wahid or qiyas. Adding a condition would change the absolute, which is naskh.
3
Analyze the reasoning
Absoluteness is an intended attribute. Adding a condition removes that attribute, i.e., abrogation. The Quran is qat'i (definitive) while khabar al-wahid and qiyas are zanni (speculative). A speculative report cannot abrogate a definitive text.
The passage states: 'because the Book is definitive and khabar al-wahid and qiyas are speculative'.
4
Apply to wudu example
The verse 'wash your faces' is absolute. The hadith 'Actions are by intentions' seems to add intention. Hanafis interpret the hadith in a way that does not change the absolute washing, or reject its use for taqyid. Thus, wudu is valid without intention in the Hanafi school.
The passage discusses this example and also references the verse about water being 'purifying' absolutely.

Example: His statement: 'Wash your faces' [5:6]. The commanded act is absolute washing, so the condition of intention, order, continuity, and naming is not added by a report. But the report is acted upon in a way that does not change the ruling of the Book. (1) The absolute means the common share among individuals of a quiddity without regard to perfection, deficiency, or attribute. The quiddity and its individual are equal in it. It is said to be absolute. In our view, the absolute proceeds in its absoluteness and is not qualified by an attribute or restriction from opinion or hearing, nor is it carried upon the qualified except when combination is impossible. According to al-Shafi'i, it is carried upon the qualified. (2) Because absoluteness is an intended attribute in their speech, and adding to it would be abrogation and removal of the attribute of absoluteness. Hence it is not permissible to abrogate the Book in its essence or its attribute by khabar al-wahid or qiyas, because the Book is definitive and khabar al-wahid and qiyas are speculative. Contrary to al-Shafi'i, who permits adding by khabar al-wahid and qiyas and considers it a clarification of the absolute from the Book, because the absolute may be qualified by clarification. We respond: Clarification requires prior ambiguity, but there is no ambiguity in the absolute since it can be acted upon. If intention, order, etc. are stipulated by reports, it would not be absolute washing and would be abrogation of the absolute of the Book by ahad reports, which is not permissible. Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul rule regarding the absolute (mutlaq) from the Quran. The Hanafi position is that when a Quranic verse expresses an absolute command (like 'wash your faces' without qualification), and it is possible to act upon its literal absolute meaning, then it cannot be restricted by adding conditions (like intention, order, etc.) through ahad reports or qiyas. Doing so would effectively abrogate the absolute nature of the Quranic command, which is definitive (qatʿī) in its text (thubūtan) and meaning (dalālatan). Since khabar al-wahid and qiyas are speculative (ẓannī), they cannot abrogate or change a definitive text. This is a point of ikhtilaf with Imam al-Shafiʿi, who considers such additions as clarifications (bayān) of the absolute, because he views the absolute as potentially ambiguous. The Hanafi response is that the absolute is not ambiguous because it can be acted upon without further qualification; thus there is no need for clarification. The footnote (1) defines 'mutlaq' in Hanafi terminology: it is the common share among individuals of a quiddity without considering attributes. It runs on its absoluteness and is not bound by any restriction from opinion or transmitted evidence. The footnote (3) addresses a counterargument from the verse about prayer: the command to wash when rising to pray might imply that intention (to pray) is a condition. The Hanafi response uses another verse about water being purifying absolutely, showing that requiring intention would conflict with that absoluteness. Footnote (4) clarifies that if one were to make intention, order, etc. conditions based on reports, that would change the absolute washing to a qualified one, which is not permissible. Footnote (5) gives the hadith about intentions as an example of a khabar that might be considered, but it should be understood in a way that does not change the absolute Quranic command.

Key Terms
مطلق (muṭlaq): The common share among individuals of a quiddity without consideration of perfection, deficiency, or attribute; a word that denotes the essence without qualification.
مقيد (muqayyad): A word that includes an additional condition or attribute restricting the absolute.
خبر الواحد (khabar al-wāḥid): A report transmitted by a single chain or a number below tawātur; considered ẓannī in Hanafi usul.
زيادة (ziyādah): An addition to the absolute text, such as a condition; Hanafis consider this naskh if it changes the absolute.

The Hanafi Rule on Absolute Texts: Prohibition of Qualifying Mutlaq by Khabar al-Wahid
Explaining the Hanafi rule that absolute Quranic texts cannot be qualified by khabar al-wahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a mutlaq text from the Quran cannot be qualified by khabar al-wahid, and you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i position by analyzing the epistemological basis.

Core Idea
Qualifying a certain Quranic absolute with a speculative report is abrogation — impossible.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi position on absolute (mutlaq) texts from the Quran. The Hanafis hold that when a Quranic command is absolute and capable of being acted upon as is, it cannot be restricted or qualified by a solitary report (khabar al-wahid) or analogy. Adding a condition or an additional element would constitute abrogation, which requires certain evidence. This sets up a key disagreement with the Shafi'i school, which regards such addition as permissible explanation (bayan) rather than abrogation. We'll unpack the definition of mutlaq, the reasoning behind the Hanafi rule, and its application to two classic examples: wudu and the punishment for zina.

Guided Path
1
Define Mutlaq
Identify the definition of mutlaq according to the Hanafis: 'the common share among individuals of the essence without observing particular perfection, deficiency, or description.' Mutlaq is unambiguous and fully actionable.
From the hashiya: 'al-mutlaq huwa al-hissah al-shai'ah fi afrad al-mahiyyah min ghayri mulahazah khhusus kamal aw nuqsan aw wasf.'
2
State the Hanafi Rule
Recognize that the Hanafi rule is: a mutlaq from the Quran, when acting upon its absoluteness is possible, cannot be added to by khabar al-wahid or qiyas. Any addition is naskh (abrogation) of the attribute of absoluteness.
This rule derives from the principle that the Quran is qat'i (certain) and khabar al-wahid is zanni (speculative).
3
Understand the Epistemological Basis
Analyze the dalil: the Quran is qat'i in transmission and meaning; khabar al-wahid is zanni. Since abrogation requires evidence of equal or greater certainty, it is impermissible to abrogate a Quranic absolute with a speculative report.
Al-Shafi'i counters that adding to the absolute is bayan (explanation) not naskh, but Hanafis respond that bayan requires prior ambiguity (ijmal), and mutlaq has no ambiguity.
4
Apply to Examples
Work through the two examples in the passage: (1) Wudu (5:6) — the absolute command 'wash your faces' is fully satisfying; conditions like intention, order, continuity, and tasmiyah are sunan from hadith, not fard. (2) Zina (24:2) — the absolute command to flog 100 lashes is the complete hadd; the hadith adding exile for virgins does not make exile part of the hadd but is interpreted as ta'zir or sunnah.
The thamarah al-khilaf: in wudu, intention is sunnah (Hanafi) vs. wajib (Shafi'i). In zina, exile is ta'zir (Hanafi) vs. part of hadd (Shafi'i).

Section on the Absolute and Qualified: Our companions (Hanafis) held that an absolute (text) from the Book of Allah, when acting upon its absoluteness is possible, then adding to it by a solitary report or analogy is not permissible. Example: His statement: 'Wash your faces' [5:6]. So what is commanded is washing absolutely, so the condition of intention, order, continuity, and naming is not added to it by the report, but the report is acted upon in a way that does not change the ruling of the Book. So it is said: Absolute washing is obligatory by the ruling of the Book, and intention is Sunnah by the ruling of the report. Similarly, we said regarding His statement: 'The fornicatress and the fornicator, flog each one of them one hundred lashes' [24:2]: The Book made flogging a hundred a hadd (punishment) for zina, so exile is not added to it as a hadd due to his statement (peace be upon him): 'The virgin with the virgin: one hundred lashes and exile for a year.' Rather, the report is acted upon in a way that does not change the ruling of the Book. Then in the commentary: (1) His statement: [regarding the absolute] intended is the common share among individuals of the essence without observing particular perfection, deficiency, or description. So the essence and the diffuse individual from it are equivalent; it is called absolute. It has been defined as that which concerns the essence itself without its attributes. According to us, the absolute runs upon its absoluteness and is not restricted by a description or restriction from opinion or hearing, nor is it carried upon the qualified unless combining is impossible. According to al-Shafi'i (may mercy be upon him), it is carried upon it. Thus in 'al-Husul'. (2) His statement: [by solitary report etc.] Because absoluteness is an intended attribute in their speech, and adding to it is abrogation and removal of the attribute of absoluteness. So abrogating the Book in its origin or attribute by a solitary report or analogy is not permissible because the Book is certain, while the solitary report and analogy are speculative, contrary to al-Shafi'i (may Allah have mercy on him), for he permits addition by solitary report and analogy and makes it an explanation of the absolute from the Book, because the absolute is susceptible to qualification by explanation. We say: Explanation requires prior ambiguity, and there is no ambiguity in the absolute due to the possibility of acting upon it. Thus in 'al-Fusul'. Commentary
This passage is a combination of matn and hashiya (gloss) from Usul al-Shashi. The matn states the Hanafi position on the absolute (mutlaq) text from the Quran: if it can be acted upon as absolute, it cannot be restricted by a solitary report (khabar al-wahid) or analogy (qiyas). The commentary (hashiya) defines mutlaq as the common aspect of an essence without any qualification of perfection, deficiency, or attribute. According to the Hanafis, such a text remains on its absoluteness and is not restricted by any description from opinion or transmitted report. It is also not interpreted as restricted unless combining the absolute and restricted is impossible. The Shafi'i view is the opposite: the absolute is interpreted in light of the restricted. The matn provides two examples: the command to wash in wudu (5:6) and the punishment for zina (24:2). For washing, the absolute command requires only washing, and conditions like intention, order, continuity, and saying 'Bismillah' are not part of the obligation; they are sunan established by hadith but do not change the obligatory nature of the absolute command. For zina, the Quran specifies one hundred lashes as the sole hadd (punishment). The hadith adds exile for virgins, but the Hanafis do not consider exile as part of the hadd; it remains a discretionary punishment (ta'zir) or is interpreted in a way that does not alter the Quranic ruling. The reasoning is that the Quran is certain (qat'i) while solitary reports and analogy are speculative (zanni). Adding to an absolute text is abrogation, which cannot be done by speculative evidence. The commentary also addresses potential objections, such as the claim that the word 'when you rise for prayer' implies intention. The response is that intention would make water non-purifying without it, contradicting the absolute statement that water is purifying.

Mutlaq and Muqayyad: Preserving Quranic Absoluteness
Explaining Hanafi rule for mutlaq-muqayyad with two examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a mutlaq Quranic command cannot be restricted by a khabar wahid, and explain the reasoning using the examples of zina punishment and tawaf.

Core Idea
The Quran's absolute command cannot be narrowed by a solitary hadith – that would be abrogation.

In this lesson, we explore a key Hanafi usul principle: when an unqualified Quranic command meets a qualified statement in a solitary hadith, the Quranic text remains absolute and the hadith is interpreted independently. This principle prevents a weaker source (zanni) from overriding a stronger one (qat'i). We will examine two classic examples: the punishment for zina (flogging vs. flogging+exile) and the requirement of wudu for tawaf (condition vs. obligation).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic mutlaq
Find the Quranic verse that gives a command without any qualification. For example, flogging in Surah an-Nur:2 and tawaf in Surah al-Hajj:29.
2
Identify the hadith that seems to qualify
Locate the hadith that adds a condition or additional element, such as exile for zina or wudu for tawaf.
These are khabar wahid (solitary reports).
3
State the Hanafi principle
The mutlaq from the Quran is qat'i; the khabar is zanni. A zanni source cannot restrict a qat'i source. Therefore, the mutlaq must be preserved, and the khabar is interpreted in a way that does not change the Quranic ruling.
This prevents naskh (abrogation) by a weaker source.
4
Apply to the zina example
The Quran makes flogging a hadd (fixed deterrent). The hadith adds exile. If exile were part of the hadd, then flogging alone would not be a hadd, effectively abrogating the Quranic ruling. Therefore, flogging remains the sole hadd, and exile is a discretionary punishment (siyasa).
The hadd must be a complete deterrent; adding another element changes its nature.
5
Apply to the tawaf example
The Quran commands tawaf absolutely. The hadith 'Tawaf is prayer' implies wudu. If wudu were a condition (shart), then tawaf without wudu would be invalid, effectively abrogating the absolute command. Therefore, wudu is not a condition but an obligation (wajib) from the hadith, while tawaf remains valid without it.
The absoluteness of the Quranic command is preserved.

This passage is from the chapter on al-Mutlaq wa al-Muqayyad (the Unqualified and Qualified) in Usul al-Shashi. The author explains the Hanafi principle that when a mutlaq (unqualified) ruling from the Quran is faced with a muqayyad (qualified) ruling from a khabar wahid (solitary report), the mutlaq retains its absoluteness and the khabar is interpreted in a way that does not alter the Quranic ruling. The commentary elaborates with examples.

The first example is ghusl: the Quran commands it absolutely (mutlaq), while a hadith adds the condition of intention (niyyah). The Hanafis say intention is sunna, not obligatory, because imposing it would change the absolute obligation. Similarly for tayammum and wiping.

The main example is the punishment for zina. The Quran says: 'Flog each of one hundred lashes' – this is mutlaq. The hadith says: 'The virgin with the virgin: one hundred lashes and exile for a year.' The Hanafis argue that if exile were imposed as a hadd (fixed punishment), then flogging alone would cease to be a hadd, and that would effectively abrogate the Quranic mutlaq. Therefore, the hadith is interpreted such that flogging remains the hadd by the Book, and exile is a discretionary punishment (siyasa) left to the imam's opinion. The commentary clarifies that the hadd is the deterrent, and if the combination is made the hadd, then each part separately is not a hadd, leading to a form of abrogation – which is impermissible with a solitary report.

The second example is tawaf: the Quran commands tawaf of the Kaaba absolutely. The hadith 'Tawaf is prayer' implies that one must be in wudu. The Hanafis say that wudu is not a condition (shart) for the validity of tawaf, but rather an obligatory (wajib) act by the hadith, while the tawaf itself remains obligatory (fard) by the Quran. Thus the absoluteness is preserved.

The commentary also addresses a potential objection: that the condition of purity is deduced from the Quran itself via implication (dalalat al-nass) because tawaf is glorification and impurity is dishonor. The Hanafi response is that this reasoning would be binding only if one holds to the implication, but here they choose to follow the khabar for the obligation, not the condition.

Overall, the passage demonstrates the Hanafi usul principle that a khabar wahid cannot restrict a mutlaq from the Quran; it can only add an independent obligation without altering the Quranic ruling.

خبر الواحد (khabar al-wahid): A narration transmitted by one or a few chains that does not reach tawatur; its proof value is zanni.
حد (hadd): A fixed, divinely ordained punishment for certain crimes; it is deterrence in itself.

Commentary
This passage defines haqiqah (literal) and majaz (metaphorical) and addresses whether both can be intended from a single word in one instance. The author begins by stating that a word placed for a specific meaning is haqiqah, and if used for something else it becomes majaz. The crucial principle is that haqiqah and majaz cannot be combined in intending one word in one state. The hashiya explains the linguistic derivation of 'haqiqah' and 'wad'. It then presents a dispute: Imam al-Shafi‘i and some Mu‘tazilites argued that they can be combined, citing the example 'Do not marry what your father married' where both intercourse and contract could be intended. The Hanafi response (our companions) argues that a qarinah (contextual clue) that diverts from the literal is necessary for majaz; intending the literal with that qarinah is contradictory. They also reason that haqiqah is stable in its place while majaz is a departure, and a single thing cannot be both at once, analogizing a garment that cannot be both owned and borrowed. The hashiya then gives a fiqh example: the sa‘ (a measure) can be sold in disparity because it is not currency, thus not covered by the hadith prohibiting selling a dirham for two dirhams. The hadith reference is also discussed textually.

Key Terms
الحقيقة (al-haqiqah): A word used for the meaning for which it was originally coined (الموضوع له الأصلي).
المجاز (al-majaz): A word used for other than its original meaning with a relationship (علاقة) and a contextual clue (قرينة).
القرينة (al-qarinah): A contextual clue indicating that the literal meaning is not intended, diverting to a metaphorical meaning.

The Scope of 'Mamluk': Inclusion of Mukatab, Mudabbar, and Umm Walad
Ruling on the scope of 'mamluk' with implications for kaffarah and marriage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the term 'mamluk' as mutlaq and determine its scope of inclusion for different slave categories, apply the Hanafi principle of completeness of ownership to derive rulings, and evaluate the qiyas argument for marriage invalidation.

Core Idea
The absolute word 'mamluk' includes only those with complete ownership; partial slavery excludes.

This lesson from Al-Shashi's Usul with Hashiya examines the word 'mamluk' in the hadith 'كل مملوك فهو حر'. The Hanafi scholars distinguish between categories of slaves: mukatab (under contract), mudabbar (deferred manumission), umm walad (mother of master's child), and part-freed slave. The key is completeness of ownership (milk kamil). We will walk through the definitional analysis, the qiyas argument, and the practical application to expiation (kaffarah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its categories
Recognize that the word 'mamluk' is the focus. The text mentions four categories: mukatab, mudabbar, umm walad, and part-freed (mu'taq al-ba'd). Note the ruling: mukatab and part-freed are not included under the absolute 'mamluk'; mudabbar and umm walad are included.
This distinction is based on completeness of ownership (ملك تام) versus deficiency in slavery (نقصان في الرق).
2
Determine the cause (illa) for inclusion
The text states that mudabbar and umm walad are included because ownership (milk) over them is complete (كامل). In contrast, ownership over a mukatab is incomplete because he still has some rights and can earn. The deficiency is in the slavery (riqq) of mudabbar and umm walad, not in ownership.
Thus, the absolute word applies to those with complete ownership, even if their slavery will end later.
3
Analyze the qiyas on marriage invalidation
The text presents an analogy: if a wife completely owns her husband, marriage is invalid (asl). If she owns a mukatab husband, marriage is not invalid (far') because ownership is incomplete. This qiyas reinforces the principle that only complete ownership triggers the ruling of invalidation.
The illa is 'complete ownership' (الملك التام); its absence in the mukatab case prevents the ruling.
4
Apply to kaffarah (expiation)
The text explains that freeing a mukatab for kaffarah is valid because his slavery is complete (رق كامل). But freeing a mudabbar or umm walad is invalid because their slavery is deficient (رق ناقص) due to impending freedom upon death. The Quranic verse 'فتحرير رقبة' requires a slave with complete slavery.
Thus, the absolute term 'raqabah' in the verse is restricted to slaves with complete riqq.

Absolute vs Time-Bound Commands: The Hanafi Ruling of Tarakhi
Definition and ruling of absolute vs. time-bound commands

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a commanded act is mutlaq or muqayyad and apply the Hanafi ruling of tarakhi to determine obligation and permissibility of delay.

Core Idea
An absolute command must be performed within one's lifetime, not immediately—delay is permissible without sin until death.

Imagine you are told to give zakat but you delay it for a few months—does that make you sinful? According to the Hanafi school, not if you eventually pay before death. This passage from Usul al-Shashi explains the distinction between absolute commands (mutlaq) and time-bound commands (muqayyad), and establishes that for mutlaq commands, the obligation is to perform the act at any point within your lifetime. The author presents the majority Hanafi position, the dissenting views, and three rational proofs that support the permissibility of delay.

Guided Path
1
Classify the Commanded Act
Read the definitions: a mutlaq command is not tied to a specific time (e.g., 'Give zakat'), while a muqayyad command is tied to a time (e.g., 'Fast Ramadan'). Identify which category the commanded act in question falls into.
The key is whether the command is lost if the time passes. For mutlaq, it is not.
2
State the Hanafi Ruling for Mutlaq
The majority Hanafi position (mufta bihi) is that a mutlaq command is wajib 'ala al-tarakhi: obligatory but permissible to delay as long as it is performed within one's lifetime.
Sin only occurs if one dies without performing the act.
3
Understand the Proofs (Dalil)
The text provides three rational proofs: (1) The command demands the act in the future; specifying time would make it muqayyad. (2) The absolute command is for ease; imposing fawr creates hardship. (3) If fawr were required, performance after the immediate moment would be qada', not ada', violating consensus.
These proofs are aqli (rational), not naqli (textual).
4
Recognize the Ikhtilaf
Some scholars (e.g., Abu Yusuf in one narration, al-Karkhi, some Shafi'is) held fawr (immediacy) as a precaution. Note which schools or individuals hold each view and how the Hanafi school responds.
The Hanafi response: sin only upon death; delay is not negligence.
5
Apply the Thamarah (Practical Consequence)
Consider a real scenario: a person has the nisab for zakat for a full year but delays payment. Before they pay, their wealth is destroyed. According to the Hanafi position (tarakhi), the obligation falls off—no sin, no liability. If fawr were required, the obligation would remain and they would sin.
This illustrates the practical difference the ruling makes.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion from the Hanafi usul text 'Usul al-Shashi' with its commentary 'Ahsan al-Hawashi'. It opens the chapter on the two types of commanded acts (al-ma'mur bih): absolute (mutlaq) regarding time and time-bound (muqayyad). The author defines the mutlaq act as one whose performance is not attached to a specific time, such that missing that time would invalidate the command. Examples include zakat, 'ushr (tithe), sadaqat al-fitr, expiations (kaffarat), and absolute vows. The text then addresses the ruling of such absolute commands. The majority Hanafi position, and the correct one according to the author, is that performance of a mutlaq command is obligatory with postponement (wajib 'ala al-tarakhi), meaning it must be performed within one's lifetime but not immediately. Delay is permissible as long as the act is not missed entirely; only if one dies without performing it does one sin. The condition is tied to overwhelming probability (ghalabat al-zann) that one will live long enough, since sudden death is rare. The text also presents an internal Hanafi ikhtilaf: some Hanafis, including Abu Yusuf in one narration and al-Karkhi, held that it is immediate (fawr), as did some Shafi'is and the People of Hadith. The author provides three rational proofs for the tarakhi position: (1) The command demands the act in the future, and specifying a time would make it muqayyad; (2) The absolute command is for ease, and imposing fawr would create hardship; (3) If it were fawr, performance after the immediate moment would be considered qada' (make-up), not ada' (proper performance), which contradicts consensus. The commentary then applies this principle to practical examples: if someone has wealth for zakat and the nisab is destroyed before payment, the obligation falls off entirely if delay was permissible. Similarly, if an oath-breaker delays financial expiation and then becomes poor, fasting suffices. These demonstrate that the mukallaf is not considered negligent (mufarrat) by delaying a mutlaq command.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A commanded act not restricted to a specific time; missing that time does not invalidate the command.
مقيد (muqayyad): A commanded act restricted to a particular time, such that the command is lost if that time passes.
فور (fawr): Immediate performance of a commanded act without delay.
تراخي (tarakhi): Permissibility to delay performance of a commanded act as long as it is done within one's lifetime.

أقسام المأمور به: المطلق والمقيد بالوقت
Classification and rulings on time-specified vs. time-unspecified commanded acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between commanded acts with specified and unspecified times, and apply Hanafi usul principles to derive rulings on intention and substitution.

Core Idea
The Lawgiver's specification of time in a commanded act controls the servant's ability to designate time; intention must align with the Shari'ah's absolute or restricted nature.

In Hanafi usul, commands (al-mamur bihi) are divided into two fundamental types based on time specification. This classification has practical consequences for the validity of acts like making up missed fasts (qada') and fulfilling vows (nadhr). Understanding when the Shari'ah leaves time unrestricted—and when it binds the servant—is essential for correctly applying intention (niyyah) in worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of commanded acts
From the source text, note the dichotomy: acts for which the Lawgiver has specified a time (e.g., the month of Ramadan) and acts for which no time is specified (e.g., making up missed fasts). This classification drives all subsequent rulings.
2
Apply the ruling to time-unspecified acts
For a commanded act without a specified time (like qada' of Ramadan), the Shari'ah leaves the time absolutely (mutlaq). The servant cannot restrict it by his own designation. Therefore, specifying the intention (niyyat al-ta'yin) is required—a general intention of fasting is insufficient.
This is unlike the case of Ramadan fasts where the time is fixed and intention can be general during the month.
3
Analyze the nadhr scenario
If a person vows to fast a specific day, that day becomes his personal obligation. However, if he fasts that day with the intention of qada' or kaffara, it counts for the latter because the Shari'ah made those acts absolute. The servant's designation cannot override the Lawgiver's unrestricted ruling.
4
Distinguish the nafil case
The ruling differs for supererogatory (nafl) fasts. If one intends nafl on a day vowed for fasting, the fast counts for the nadhr instead. The reason: nafl is the servant's right—he can abandon it without sin—so the Shari'ah prioritizes the more binding obligation (nadhr) over the less binding one (nafl).
This shows that the servant's authority affects his own voluntary acts but cannot alter the Shari'ah's determinations for obligatory acts.
5
Derive the usul principle
The overall principle: For acts whose time is unspecified by the Lawgiver, the servant lacks authority to fix the time; intention must be specific to identify the obligation. For acts where time is specified, intention can be general. This illustrates the Hanafi axiom: 'The Lawgiver's unrestricted ruling cannot be restricted by the servant's action.'

Bayān al-Ḍarūra: The Principle of Necessity-Based Statement
Defines necessity-based statement with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Bayān al-Ḍarūra, identify its occurrence in legal texts, and apply it to infer unspecified shares in contracts and inheritances using Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
When a text specifies one part of a previously established whole, the remainder is implied by necessity — silence speaks.

In Hanafi usul, not all legal clarity comes from explicit words. Sometimes the very structure of a ruling forces a conclusion. This lesson introduces Bayān al-Ḍarūra — a statement made not by speech but by the logical necessity of the context. We will see how the Quranic inheritance verse and common contracts rely on this principle to fill in unspecified details, making contracts valid and rulings complete.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial ruling
Find the part of the text that establishes a shared or common situation, e.g., the inheritance verse that says both parents share.
This initial ruling sets the framework of partnership.
2
Locate the explicit specification
Identify where the text specifies one party's share, such as 'for his mother is the third'.
Specification must be of a part, not the whole.
3
Infer the remainder by necessity
Since the initial ruling implies a common share for all parties, specifying one forces the remainder upon the others.
This inference is unavoidable for coherence.
4
Recognize the role of silence
The unspecified party is not mentioned, but the context of the initial ruling and the explicit specification makes the silence a statement.
Silence alone does not create a statement; it must be combined with a prior explicit ruling.
5
Apply to other cases
Test the principle on mudaraba, muzara'a, bequests, and ambiguous divorce.
In each case, specifying one share implies the other.

Chapter: As for the statement of necessity – it is that which occurs by necessity of speech. The beginning of the verse established partnership between the parents in the inheritance without specifying each's share. Then specifying the mother's share as one-third indicates that the father receives the remainder. So it became a statement of the father's share by necessity. Similarly, we say: if we specify the profit share of the mudārib (agent) in a mudāraba contract, the owner's share becomes the remainder, and the contract is valid. Likewise if we specify the owner's share, it is a statement for the agent's share. The same ruling applies to muzāra'a (sharecropping). Also, if one bequeaths to two persons a thousand and then specifies one's share, that specifies the other's share. And if a man divorces one of his two wives and then has intercourse with one of them, that intercourse specifies the divorce.

Commentary
This passage defines Bayān al-Ḍarūra (statement by necessity), which is a legal clarification that occurs not through explicit speech but by the logical implication of the context. The primary example is the inheritance verse (Quran 4:11). The verse initially states that both parents share in the inheritance but then assigns the mother one-third. Since the beginning established common sharing, specifying the mother's share necessarily implies that the father gets the remainder (two-thirds). This is not explicit speech but is derived by necessity of the ruling. The author then extends this principle to contracts: in a mudāraba contract, if one party's profit share is specified, the other's share is understood by necessity. This is also applied to muzāra'a (sharecropping) and bequests. Additionally, if a man divorces one of his wives ambiguously and later has intercourse with one, that act indicates which wife was divorced (since intercourse with a divorced woman would be illegal; thus the one he had intercourse with is the undivorced one). The commentary from the hashiah emphasizes that such a statement is made by silence (sukūt) combined with the initial context, not by mere omission.

Key Terms
بيان الضرورة (Bayān al-Ḍarūra): A statement that occurs non-verbally, inferred by necessity from the context, such as specifying one party's share implying another's.
السكوت (al-Sukūt): Silence that, when combined with a prior context that establishes a general ruling, can serve as a statement implying the unspecified part.

Sabab as Proxy for 'Illa: Sleep, Seclusion, and Travel
Illustrating the principle with examples of sleep, valid seclusion, and travel

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three examples of sabab standing in for 'illa and explain the istihsan-based rationale for each.

Core Idea
Sleep, valid seclusion, and travel are sababs that stand in for hidden effective causes due to istihsan.

In the previous lesson, we learned the principle that a sabab (apparent cause) can be established in place of a hidden 'illa (effective cause) to avoid difficulty in ascertaining it. This lesson provides three concrete examples from Hanafi fiqh: sleep nullifies wudu, valid seclusion triggers mahr and 'idda, and travel grants concessions. Each illustrates how the law attaches rulings to an observable cause rather than requiring proof of the hidden reality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hidden effective cause
For each example, determine the real cause that is difficult to ascertain: for sleep, it is the actual exit of impurity (hadath); for seclusion, it is actual intercourse; for travel, it is actual hardship.
The hidden cause is uncertain but likely present.
2
Identify the apparent cause (sabab)
The law uses an observable event as a proxy: complete sleep (al-nawm al-kamil), valid seclusion (al-khalwa al-sahiha), and travel of a minimum distance (al-safar).
These are easily verifiable.
3
Explain the istihsan rationale
The reason (ta'lil) is 'تعذر الاطلاع' (difficulty of knowing the hidden cause). Istihsan dictates that rulings are based on the apparent cause to avoid hardship in evidence.
This is a form of istihsan.
4
State the legal effect
Each sabab triggers a ruling as if the hidden cause were present: sleep nullifies wudu; seclusion imposes full mahr and 'idda; travel permits qasr and iftar.
The ruling is called a 'hukm hukmi' (legal ruling based on a legal cause).
5
Address potential misconceptions
For sleep: it is not itself impurity but a legal event. For seclusion: it does not physically cause mahr but legally so. For travel: it is the distance, not the subjective hardship, that matters.
This prevents confusion between sabab and 'illa.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion from the previous lesson, providing three key examples of the principle that a sabab (apparent cause) is established in place of the hidden 'illa (effective cause). First, sleep is established in place of the actual event (hadath) that nullifies wudu; because the real exit of impurity is uncertain during sleep, but since sleep customarily leads to it, the law treats sleep itself as the cause of nullification. Second, valid seclusion (khalwa sahiha) is established in place of actual intercourse for the purposes of full dower (mahr) and the waiting period ('idda), because the actual occurrence of intercourse is hidden; even if there is certainty that no intercourse occurred, the legal consequences follow. Third, travel is established in place of actual hardship for the concessions of shortening prayer and breaking fast, because hardship is internal and varies among people; instead, the objective distance of travel is the criterion. The commentary in Ahsan al-Hawashi explains the rationale and addresses potential objections, such as the idea that sleep itself is not impurity but the law treats it as such for practical reasons.

Key Terms
النوم الكامل (al-nawm al-kamil): Complete sleep (where joints relax) which is a sabab for nullification of wudu.
الخلوة الصحيحة (al-khalwa al-sahiha): Valid seclusion free from legal, physical, and natural barriers to intercourse, which stands in for actual intercourse for mahr and 'idda.
السفر (al-safar): Journey of three days and nights (approximately 48 miles) which is a sabab for concessions of qasr and iftar.
