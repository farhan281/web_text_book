Meaning of Nikah in Oaths: Contract vs Intercourse
Dispute on whether 'nikah' in oaths means marriage contract or intercourse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul distinction between haqiqah and majaz to resolve an apparent contradiction between a legal ruling and the Quranic usage of the term 'nikah'.

Core Idea
In oaths, 'nikah' defaults to the marriage contract; Quranic use as intercourse is common figurative, not overriding.

This passage presents a seeming contradiction: the Hanafi school rules that in oaths, 'nikah' means the marriage contract, yet in the Quran it clearly means sexual intercourse. The commentator resolves this by appealing to the concept of common figurative usage (majaz muta'arif). We will dissect the reasoning and its implications for usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi rule on 'nikah' in oaths
Identify the lull (beginning of source): 'said our scholars: if one swears... that applies to the contract.' This is the unequivocal ruling.
Contract is the default meaning.
2
Identify the objection from the Quran
Recognize the objection (يرد): the Quran uses 'nikah' to mean intercourse (verses 2:230 and 4:22), which seems to contradict the oath ruling.
Objector might claim the oath should also mean intercourse.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The response (الجواب) explains that the Quranic usage is a common figurative (majaz muta'arif) that is both a legal figurative and literal in language. It does not override the literal in oaths unless context permits.
Key terms: 'majaz shar'i', 'haqiqa lughawiyya', 'majaz muta'arif'.
4
Classify the Quranic usage according to Hanafi usul
The commentator categorizes the Quranic meaning as a 'common figurative' – widespread and abundant in texts, but still figurative. It can be intended when the literal is also permissible.
This is a standard Hanafi classification.
5
Apply the principle to oath interpretation
In oaths, the default is the literal meaning (contract). The figurative meaning (intercourse) is possible only if the oath-taker intended it and the context supports it, but the default rule stands.
Practical implication: intercourse alone does not break an oath of 'I will not marry'.

Custom (Urf) Determines Figurative Meaning in Oaths
Examples showing that figurative meaning is applied by custom, not combining literal and figurative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that customary figurative meaning overrides literal in oaths, and distinguish it from combining literal and figurative.

Core Idea
Custom can make a figurative meaning so predominant that it replaces the literal in oaths.

This passage presents three oath examples where the literal meaning would be too narrow. The Hanafi school uses custom (urf) to adopt a broader figurative meaning, ensuring the oath reflects common understanding. The key insight is that this is not a forbidden combination of literal and figurative; rather, custom shifts the primary meaning entirely.

The ruling applies the figurative meaning exclusively. The literal is not considered, so no combination occurs.
4
Understand the principle
In Hanafi usul, when a figurative usage becomes widespread (majaz 'urfi), it takes precedence over the literal. This is not combining but substituting.

Custom (urf) is a valid source for interpreting ambiguous terms, especially in oaths where intent matters.

Literal vs Metaphorical Meaning in Oaths: The Hanafi Dispute
Dispute on the application of literal vs. metaphorical meaning in oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the two companions' positions on applying haqiqah vs. majaz in oaths, and explain the practical consequence of their difference on oath-breaking rulings.

Core Idea
For Abu Hanifa, majaz substitutes wording; for the companions, it substitutes the ruling – changing how oaths apply.

In Hanafi usul, both haqiqah and majaz are recognized, but a critical internal dispute arises over the nature of metaphorical substitution. Imam Abu Hanifa views majaz as a linguistic substitute – the word itself replaces the literal word – while his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) view majaz as a substitute in the legal ruling, not the wording. This seemingly abstract difference has concrete implications for how oaths are interpreted, as demonstrated by the classic examples of wheat and Euphrates water.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State clearly the views of Abu Hanifa and the two companions on interpreting oaths. Abu Hanifa holds that an oath refers to the literal meaning only; the companions hold that it extends to commonly understood metaphorical meanings.
Both positions are within the Hanafi school.
2
Understand the nature of substitution
Abu Hanifa considers majaz a substitute in the wording itself (khalaf fi haqq al-lafz). The companions consider it a substitute in the ruling (khalaf fi haqq al-hukm). This difference determines when majaz can be applied.
This is the core theoretical divergence.
3
Apply to the oath examples
Work through the wheat example: swearing not to eat 'this wheat.' According to Abu Hanifa, only eating the whole grain breaks the oath; according to the companions, eating bread also breaks it. Similarly for the Euphrates: Abu Hanifa requires drinking directly by lapping; the companions accept any method.
Note how the same wording yields different results.
4
Examine the case when literal meaning is impossible
If the literal meaning is impossible in itself, the companions say majaz cannot be used, and the speech becomes void. Abu Hanifa allows using majaz even if the literal is impossible, because the substitution is in wording.
This highlights the practical impact of the theoretical difference.
5
Relate to custom (urf)
Recognize that both sides base oaths on custom (urf), but they differ on whether frequent metaphorical usage constitutes a sufficient indicator (qarina) to override the literal. Abu Hanifa says no; the companions say yes.
Custom is central, but its evidentiary weight is disputed.

The Definition of Meat in Oaths: Fish and Locust Excluded
Ruling on oath not to eat meat regarding fish and locust

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'meat' (lahm) in the context of oaths and apply it to determine whether an oath is broken by eating fish or locust.

Core Idea
Meat in oaths means blood-generated flesh; fish and locust lack blood so they are not meat.

When someone swears not to eat meat, what exactly counts as 'meat'? This lesson unpacks a key Hanafi principle: in oaths, words are interpreted according to their technical legal meaning, not common parlance. The source passage establishes that meat (lahm) is defined as flesh generated from blood, excluding fish and locust. We'll explore the evidence, the property of blood, and the rebuttal to the Maliki view that fish meat is included based on the Quranic term 'fresh meat'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem
A person takes an oath: 'I will not eat meat.' He then eats fish or locust. Does he break his oath? The Hanafi answer: no, because these are not 'meat' in the relevant sense.
2
Extract the Hanafi definition of lahm
The passage states that meat is 'what is generated from blood' (ما يتولد من الدم). This is the technical definition used in oaths, not the everyday sense.
Pork and human flesh are generated from blood, so eating them breaks the oath.
3
Understand the property of blood
Blood has a specific property: it turns black when exposed to the sun. Fish blood turns white, so it is not considered blood in the legal sense. Hence fish meat is not generated from blood.
This is a physical test to distinguish true blood from other fluids.
4
Address the objection from the Quran
The Maliki school argues that Allah calls fish 'fresh meat' (lahman tariyyan) in Quran 16:14, so the word 'meat' should apply. The Hanafi response: that usage is metaphorical (majaz), not the literal technical meaning used in oaths.
The absolute term (mutlaq) refers to the perfect and complete instance; fish meat is deficient in firmness (shiddah) because it lacks blood.
5
Distinguish between literal and technical meanings
In oaths, words are interpreted according to the intended meaning of the oath-taker, which is based on customary usage (urf) and technical definitions, not mere linguistic literalness.

The Rule of 'Atf in Debts and Its Exception in Salam
Definition of 'atf rule with exception for salam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of 'atf (conjunction) to debts of makil/mawzun and distinguish when the rule does not apply based on 'urf.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, conjuncted amounts in debts become one unit for frequently traded goods, but not for rare ones like cloth.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi explains a key principle about grammatical conjunction ('atf) in debt contracts. The principle states that when two amounts are conjoined with waw, they are treated as a single unit. However, as the commentary refines, this rule is not absolute—it applies only to items commonly sold on credit, like measured or weighed goods, due to customary usage. For items that are rarely transacted as debts, such as cloth, the conjunction does not merge the amounts. Understanding this refinement is crucial for correctly interpreting debt obligations in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Read the matn's claim: 'The conjuncted and the conjuncted-to are like one entity.' This is the default rule for debts when two amounts are joined by waw.
The waw of 'atf is for joining, making the two items a single unit.
2
Locate the Exception
The hashiya states that this rule only applies to makil (measured) and mawzun (weighed) items. For items like cloth (thiyab), the rule does not apply because such items are not normally sold on credit except in salam.
The exception is based on 'urf (custom).
3
Understand the Reasoning
The reason ('illa) is custom: for frequently transacted goods, people commonly omit the explicative (mufassir) for the first conjunct and rely on one explanation for the whole. For rare items, this custom does not exist, so the original rule (each amount separate) remains.
The necessity (darura) of frequent speech drives the custom.
4
Apply the Distinction
Given a debt statement like '100 dirhams and 50 dirhams for wheat (makil)', treat as 150 total. But if for cloth (not makil/mawzun), treat as two separate debts: 100 and 50.
Check the type of item: if it's usually sold on credit, the conjunction merges.
5
Recognize the Usul Principle
This case illustrates how 'urf (custom) can restrict the application of a literal rule. The Hanafi school gives weight to 'urf in defining legal meanings.
The rule is not purely linguistic; it is shaped by practical usage.
