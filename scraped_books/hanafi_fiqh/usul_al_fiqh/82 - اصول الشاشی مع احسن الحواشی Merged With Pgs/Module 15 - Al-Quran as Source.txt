Janabah Does Not Invalidate Fasting: Istinbat from Quran and Hadith
Derivation of janabah's non-invalidation from Quran and hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the ruling that janabah does not invalidate fasting from the Quranic verse (2:187) and prophetic hadith, using Hanafi usul principles of nass and darurah.

Core Idea
Janabah does not break the fast because the Quran permits intimacy until dawn, making janabah inevitable at the fast's start.

This passage demonstrates the Hanafi method of istinbat (derivation) by examining how a fiqh ruling is extracted from the Quran and Sunnah. The ruling at hand is that being in a state of janabah (major impurity) does not invalidate one's fast. The author uses the verse from Surah al-Baqarah and a hadith from 'A'ishah and Umm Salamah to prove that the fast remains valid despite janabah, a ruling that is widely accepted across schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary source texts
Locate the Quranic verse (Al-Baqarah 2:187) and the prophetic hadith (Tirmidhi) that are cited. These are the two main proofs.
The verse permits intimacy up to dawn; the hadith shows the Prophet in janabah at dawn while fasting.
2
Classify the Quranic text as nass
The verse is a clear text (nass) on the permissibility of intercourse until dawn. This classification triggers Hanafi usul rules about conclusive meaning.
Nass has a clear, unambiguous meaning and is not open to interpretation.
3
Analyze the logical implication (talil)
The command to complete the fast from dawn (ثُمَّ أَتِمُّوا الصِّيَامَ) implies that the fast begins at dawn. Since intimacy is permitted until the last moment of night, one may enter dawn in janabah.
There is no temporal gap for ghusl between the end of the permitted act and the start of the fast.
4
Apply the principle of darurah (necessity)
Because ghusl is a gradual process and cannot be performed instantaneously, it is necessary that janabah at dawn does not invalidate the fast.
Darurah here refers to the unavoidable nature of janabah at the start of the fast.
5
Confirm with the hadith
The hadith provides direct evidence that the Prophet himself fasted while in janabah, confirming the deduction from the Quran.
The hadith is sahih (authentic) and acted upon by the majority of scholars.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary explaining the derivation (istinbat) of several fiqh rulings from Quranic texts and hadith. The author begins by discussing the chapter 'On Matters Related to Texts' (فصل في متعلقات النصوص). The main issue is the ruling on janabah (major ritual impurity) and its effect on the validity of fasting. The commentator shows how from the Quranic verse (Al-Baqarah 2:187) which permits sexual intercourse, eating, and drinking until dawn, and then commands completing the fast until night, we can deduce that janabah does not invalidate the fast. The reasoning is that since intercourse is allowed right up to the break of dawn, and there is no moment between the last permissible time and the start of the fast in which to perform ghusl, one must inevitably be in a state of janabah at the beginning of the fast. Therefore, the fast is valid despite janabah. This is supported by the hadith of the Prophet (peace be upon him) that he would be overtaken by dawn in a state of janabah from his wife and then fast. The commentary also mentions subsidiary rulings that follow from this principle: rinsing the mouth (madmadah) and nose (istinshaq) do not break the fast, tasting something without it going down the throat is permissible, and similarly wet dreams (ihtilam), cupping (hijamah), and oiling (idhan) do not break the fast. Finally, the definition of fasting is discussed: it is abstention from the three things (eating, drinking, sexual intercourse), though intention and capacity are also required but omitted here due to their fame. The lesson is a classic example of how Hanafi usul derives rulings through textual analysis and the principle of necessity (darurah).

Key Terms
الرفث (ar-rafath): Sexual intercourse and obscene speech; in the context of fasting, it refers to sexual intercourse.
الجنابة (al-janabah): State of major ritual impurity requiring ghusl.
المضمضة والاستنشاق (al-madmadah wa al-istinshaq): Rinsing the mouth and nose during wudu or ghusl.
الإمساك (al-imsak): Abstention from eating, drinking, and sexual intercourse.
الاحتلام (al-ihtilam): Wet dream.

Dalalat al-Nass: The Implication of the Text
Definition and example of Dalalat al-Nass

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dalalat al-nass and apply it to derive the prohibition of harming parents from the Quranic verse.

Core Idea
The text's implication extends the ruling to all cases sharing the same cause.

In this lesson, we explore how the Quranic prohibition of saying 'uff' to parents implies the prohibition of any act that harms them, illustrating the Hanafi concept of dalalat al-nass. This is a foundational tool in Hanafi usul, bridging explicit text and its necessary consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal wording
Read the verse: 'So do not say to them 'uff'' (al-Isra 17:23). This is the explicit text (عبارة النص). The ruling is prohibition of uttering 'uff'.
The literal meaning is limited to saying the word 'uff'.
2
Recognize the underlying cause
The context of the verse is honoring parents and avoiding harm. Any Arabic speaker immediately understands that the reason for the prohibition is to prevent causing harm (الأذى) to parents.
The cause (علة) is harm, which is manifest and appropriate.
3
Extend the ruling to all similar acts
Since the cause (harm) is present in acts like beating, verbal abuse, or harsh treatment, those acts are also prohibited by the indication of the text (دلالة النص).
This extension is immediate and necessary, not based on analogical reasoning.
4
Distinguish from qiyas
Unlike qiyas (analogy), where the cause is identified through ijtihad, دلالة النص is self-evident to native speakers and does not require additional reasoning. It is classified as a direct textual implication.
Hanafis consider it decisive (قطعي) and not speculative.

Commentary
This passage is from a commentary (sharh) on Usul al-Shashi, explaining the concept of 'دلالة النص' (the indication of the text). The author begins by defining دلالة النص as the implied meaning that a native Arabic speaker immediately understands from the context, beyond the literal wording. The first example is the Quranic verse 'فَلاَ تَقُل لَّهُمَا أُفٍّ' (So do not say to them 'uff'). Literally, the text prohibits saying 'uff' to parents. However, any Arabic speaker understands that the underlying reason is to avoid causing harm or pain to parents. Therefore, by دلالة النص, this prohibition extends to any act that harms parents, such as beating, verbal abuse, or using them harshly. The harm (الأذى) is the cause (علة) that links the original ruling (prohibition of 'uff') to new cases. The passage then discusses that دلالة النص is decisive (قطعي) in its authority, comparable to the explicit text itself. This is supported by the fact that early scholars accepted it even before the formalization of qiyas (analogy). The commentary provides examples: from the hadith of Ma'iz, the stoning of an adulterer who is muhsan is established by the text for Ma'iz, but by دلالة النص for all others in the same condition. Similarly, the obligation of kaffarah for intercourse during Ramadan daytime is from the text of the hadith, while the same obligation for eating and drinking is derived by indication. The passage also addresses a hypothetical community that considers saying 'uff' as a compliment; for them, the prohibition would not apply because the cause (harm) is absent, even though the literal text is general. Another example is the verse 'وَذَرُوا الْبَيْعَ' (and leave off sale) at the Friday prayer call. The literal prohibition is against selling, but the real purpose is to avoid anything that hinders attending the prayer. Thus, if a sale does not hinder attendance (e.g., if the buyer and seller are in a ship heading to the mosque), it is not prohibited. This reinforces the principle that the ruling revolves around the cause in existence and non-existence. The passage emphasizes that understanding دلالة النص is crucial for correct legal reasoning, and it is a foundational tool in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Key Terms
دلالة النص (dalālat al-naṣṣ): The implied meaning of a text that is immediately understood by native speakers, extending the ruling beyond the literal wording based on the underlying cause.
علة (ʿilla): The underlying reason or cause for a legal ruling; here, for example, causing harm (الأذى) is the 'illa for the prohibition of 'uff' and all similar acts.

The Authority of Dalalat al-Nass: Decisive Evidence in Hanafi Usul
Arguments for the decisiveness of Dalalat al-Nass and its applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that dalalat al-nass is qat'i (decisive) and evaluate the opposing view that it is zanni (speculative).

Core Idea
Hanafis consider dalalat al-nass as decisive and equivalent to the explicit text, even for hudud and qisas.

In this lesson, we delve into the authoritative status of dalalat al-nass. The passage asserts that this type of indication is decisive (قطعي), known to every Arabic speaker, and was accepted before the formalization of qiyas. We examine the evidence for this view and the opposing perspective of the Mutakallimun and some Shafi'is, highlighting the practical implications in deriving criminal penalties and expiations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi claim about dalalat al-nass
The passage states that dalalat al-nass is قطعية (decisive) and لا ينكرها منكر (never denied). It is equivalent to the explicit text in establishing rulings, even for hudud and qisas.
This is a strong epistemological claim: the implication is as binding as the literal text.
2
Examine the evidence for decisiveness
The evidence includes: (a) the understanding is immediate for Arabic speakers, (b) it was practiced before qiyas, (c) no one denies it. Examples include stoning for all muhsan adulterers from the Ma'iz hadith, and kaffarah for eating/drinking in Ramadan from the hadith on intercourse.
The cause (علة) is self-evident, making the extension necessary.
3
Understand the opposing view
The Mutakallimun and some Shafi'is consider dalalat al-nass as zanni (speculative) because it involves inference beyond the literal words. They treat it as a form of qiyas.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf in usul.
4
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafi response is that the meaning is not inferred through reasoning but is immediately understood by anyone familiar with the language. The cause is manifest and cannot be ignored. Therefore, it is not speculative but decisive.
The difference hinges on whether the implication is considered 'part of the text' or 'additional reasoning'.
5
Apply to examples: kaffarah and stoning
In the hadith of the Bedouin, the Prophet explicitly obligated kaffarah for intercourse. By dalalat al-nass, the same kaffarah applies to eating and drinking because the cause (intentional violation of the fast) is the same. Similarly, the stoning of Ma'iz is explicit, and by indication, all muhsan adulterers are stoned.
The practical consequence is that Hanafi scholars can apply hudud punishments based on this indication.

Commentary
This passage is from a commentary (sharh) on Usul al-Shashi, explaining the concept of 'دلالة النص' (the indication of the text). The author begins by defining دلالة النص as the implied meaning that a native Arabic speaker immediately understands from the context, beyond the literal wording. The first example is the Quranic verse 'فَلاَ تَقُل لَّهُمَا أُفٍّ' (So do not say to them 'uff'). Literally, the text prohibits saying 'uff' to parents. However, any Arabic speaker understands that the underlying reason is to avoid causing harm or pain to parents. Therefore, by دلالة النص, this prohibition extends to any act that harms parents, such as beating, verbal abuse, or using them harshly. The harm (الأذى) is the cause (علة) that links the original ruling (prohibition of 'uff') to new cases. The passage then discusses that دلالة النص is decisive (قطعي) in its authority, comparable to the explicit text itself. This is supported by the fact that early scholars accepted it even before the formalization of qiyas (analogy). The commentary provides examples: from the hadith of Ma'iz, the stoning of an adulterer who is muhsan is established by the text for Ma'iz, but by دلالة النص for all others in the same condition. Similarly, the obligation of kaffarah for intercourse during Ramadan daytime is from the text of the hadith, while the same obligation for eating and drinking is derived by indication. The passage also addresses a hypothetical community that considers saying 'uff' as a compliment; for them, the prohibition would not apply because the cause (harm) is absent, even though the literal text is general. Another example is the verse 'وَذَرُوا الْبَيْعَ' (and leave off sale) at the Friday prayer call. The literal prohibition is against selling, but the real purpose is to avoid anything that hinders attending the prayer. Thus, if a sale does not hinder attendance (e.g., if the buyer and seller are in a ship heading to the mosque), it is not prohibited. This reinforces the principle that the ruling revolves around the cause in existence and non-existence. The passage emphasizes that understanding دلالة النص is crucial for correct legal reasoning, and it is a foundational tool in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Key Terms
دلالة النص (dalālat al-naṣṣ): The implied meaning of a text that is immediately understood by native speakers, extending the ruling beyond the literal wording based on the underlying cause.
علة (ʿilla): The underlying reason or cause for a legal ruling; here, for example, causing harm (الأذى) is the 'illa for the prohibition of 'uff' and all similar acts.
قطعي (qatʿī): Decisive, certain; a ruling or evidence that is definitive and not open to doubt.
ظني (ẓannī): Speculative; a ruling or evidence that is probable but not certain.

Command Implies Goodness: Two Types of Commanded Acts
Explanation of the principle that command implies goodness and classification of commanded acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between acts good in themselves (hasan li-aynihi) and acts good for another (hasan li-ghayrihi), and apply their respective rulings based on the presence or absence of the external purpose (wasita).

Core Idea
A commanded act may be good in itself or good for another; its ruling depends on whether the external purpose is achieved by the act itself.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi explores a key principle: the command implies goodness, but not all commanded acts are good intrinsically. The text distinguishes between acts good in themselves and those good only because they lead to something else. It then explains two subtypes of the latter, each with different legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of goodness
Examine how the passage divides commanded acts: some are good in themselves (hasan li-aynihi) and others are good for the sake of something else (hasan li-ghayrihi). Recognize that the focus is on the second category.
The passage does not elaborate on hasan li-aynihi; it assumes the reader knows that acts like faith are intrinsically good.
2
Analyze the first subtype of hasan li-ghayrihi
Look at examples like sa'y to Jumu'ah and wudu. The passage states they are good because they lead to an external purpose (Jumu'ah prayer, prayer itself), but the commanded act itself does not achieve that purpose directly. Understand the ruling: if the purpose is not achieved (e.g., missing Jumu'ah after sa'y), the commanded act must be repeated or drops.
The wasita (external purpose) is not attained by the same act; hence, its failure voids the basis for the command.
3
Analyze the second subtype of hasan li-ghayrihi
Consider hudud, qisas, and jihad. These acts are also good for another (deterrence, justice, repelling evil), but here the external purpose is achieved directly by the act itself. For example, executing a hadd punishment inherently achieves deterrence. Ruling: if the underlying cause (e.g., crime) disappears, the commanded act is no longer required.
The text emphasizes that jihad continues indefinitely because its cause (disbelief) never completely ceases.
4
Apply the distinction to the evidence
Review the Quranic evidence (al-Jumu'ah:9, al-Ma'idah:6) for the first subtype and the hadith ('Jihad continues until the Day of Judgment') for the second. Understand how each proof supports the respective ruling.
The Quranic verses show commands for acts that are means, not ends. The hadith shows that the cause of jihad is perpetual, so the command remains.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi with its commentary (Ahsan al-Hawashi) discusses the fundamental usul principle that a command implies the goodness of the commanded act. The text first establishes that when a commanded act is missed, it must be made up (qada). It then divides commanded acts into two types: those good in themselves (hasan li-aynihi) and those good for the sake of something else (hasan li-ghayrihi). The focus here is on the second type, which is further subdivided based on how the external purpose is achieved.

The first subtype (like sa'y to Jumu'ah and wudu for prayer) are good not intrinsically but because they lead to an external good (Jumu'ah and prayer). Their ruling is that they become obligatory when their purpose is intended, but if the purpose is not achieved (e.g., missing Jumu'ah after sa'y, or breaking wudu before prayer), the commanded act loses its basis and must be repeated or is dropped. The text gives examples: if someone is coerced away from the mosque, they must repeat sa'y; if someone breaks wudu, they must renew it.

The second subtype (hudud, qisas, jihad) are also good for the sake of something else (deterrence, justice, repelling evil), but with a key difference: the external purpose is achieved directly by the commanded act itself. For instance, the act of executing a hadd punishment inherently achieves deterrence; the act of jihad inherently repels evil. Thus, if the underlying cause (crime, disbelief) disappears, the commanded act is no longer required. However, the text notes that jihad is continuous until the Day of Judgment, so its cause never ceases entirely.

The commentary (hashiya) clarifies nuances, such as that the 'closeness' of the second subtype to being good in itself does not make it so, and that the means (wasila) in the first subtype is not achieved by the commanded act itself, unlike the second. The text uses Quranic evidence (al-Jumu'ah:9, al-Ma'idah:6) and the hadith on jihad's perpetuity.

The Hanafi Principle of Nahy on Shar'i Acts: Validity in Essence
Explaining the Hanafi principle that prohibition does not nullify legitimacy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that nahy on shar'i acts does not affect the act's fundamental legitimacy, and distinguish between acts whose completion is possible without sin and those where it is not.

Core Idea
Prohibition on a worship act does not make it invalid; the act remains valid in essence, only its performance under certain conditions is prohibited.

This lesson explores a foundational Hanafi rule in usul al-fiqh: when a shar'i act (like prayer or fasting) is prohibited in a specific context, the act itself remains legitimate. This principle leads to practical rulings on vows, supererogatory prayers, and even marital relations. We will trace the reasoning, examine the proof from the Quran, and see how the Hanafi school differs from others.

Guided Path
1

First, extract the principle from the opening: prohibition (nahy) does not negate the fundamental legitimacy (mashru'iyya) of a shar'i act. The act remains valid in essence; the prohibition only attaches due to an external description (like time or state).
Example: wine ownership remains, but disposition is prohibited.
2

List the three examples: vow to fast on Nahr/Tashreeq, vow to pray at disliked times, and supererogatory prayer at prohibited times. For each, note how the Hanafi school applies the rule.

The passage distinguishes between starting a nafl prayer at a prohibited time (binding) and starting a nafl fast on the day of Nahr (not binding). Combining Multiple Qira'at: The Principle of Tawfiq
Introduces the principle of combining multiple qira'at with the example of al-Ma'idah 5:6.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to APPLY the Hanafi usul principle of tawfiq (reconciliation) to resolve apparent contradictions between two qira'at of a Quranic verse, and IDENTIFY the resulting fiqh ruling.

Core Idea
When a text has two authentic readings, the Hanafi school combines them by assigning each to a different context rather than discarding one.

This passage introduces a foundational usul rule: when a single text (especially a Quranic verse) is transmitted with two different readings (qira'at), the preferred approach is to act in a way that incorporates both, rather than selecting one and discarding the other. The classic example is the verse on wudu in Surah al-Ma'idah. By reconciling the two readings through contextual specification, the Hanafi school derives a comprehensive ruling that honors both transmissions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two qira'at
Find the two authentic readings of the verse. In Quran 5:6, the word 'arjul' is read with nasb (accusative: 'wa arjulakum') and with khafd (genitive: 'wa arjulikum').
Both readings are mutawatir, so both are qat'i in transmission.
2
Determine the literal meaning of each reading
The nasb reading ('wa arjulakum') is connected to the verb 'wash' from earlier in the verse, thus it commands washing the feet. The khafd reading ('wa arjulikum') is connected to the words 'wipe your heads', thus it commands wiping the feet (or possibly the footgear).
The contradiction is clear: washing vs. wiping.
3
Recognize the need to apply both readings
Since both readings are authentic and part of the Quran, the Hanafi usul rule is that we cannot simply discard one. We must find a way to give effect to both.
This is the principle of tawfiq before considering naskh or tarjih.
4
Apply tawfiq by contextual specification (takhsis)
Interpret each reading as applying to a different circumstance. The khafd reading (wiping) is specified to apply when the person is wearing khuffs (leather socks), where wiping over khuffs is sufficient. The nasb reading (washing) applies when the feet are bare.
This specification is based on other proofs from the Sunnah about wiping over khuffs.
5
Derive the comprehensive fiqh ruling
The combined ruling: when barefoot, wash the feet; when wearing khuffs, wipe over them. Both qira'at are operative, each in its own context.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Principle of reconciling dual readings (qira'atayn)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of prioritizing both Quranic readings to reconcile apparent contradictions and derive a fiqh ruling.

Core Idea
Give effect to both readings; reconcile by assigning each to a different context.

When a Quranic verse is recited in two different ways, the Hanafi school does not reject one in favor of the other. Instead, it seeks a reconciliation (jam') that allows both readings to be operative. This lesson explores that process using the classic example of 'wa arjulakum' in the wudu verse (5:6). Understanding this method is key to appreciating how Hanafi usul derives rulings from the Qur'an while respecting the integrity of all authentic qira'at.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two readings
Locate the word 'arjulakum' in the verse and note that it is read with nasb (accusative) and with khafd (genitive).
The nasb reading connects the feet to the washed items (faces, hands); the khafd reading connects them to the wiped item (head).
2
State the Hanafi reconciliation principle
When a text has two valid readings, it is preferable to give effect to both rather than ignore one. The principle is: 'al-'amal bi'l-qira'atayn awla min al-ihmal' (acting on both readings is better than neglecting one).
3
Apply the principle to the wudu verse
The khafd reading is assigned to the case of wearing khuffs (leather socks), where wiping over the khuff fulfills the obligation. The nasb reading is assigned to bare feet, requiring washing.
This reconciliation gives effect to both readings without contradiction.
4
Address the grammatical objections
Two objections are raised: (1) the genitive may be due to adjacency, not genuine conjunction; (2) the accusative may be on the position of 'ru'usikum' to avoid an intervening phrase. The Hanafi answers: (1) 'to the ankles' limits the place of wiping, not the act; (2) intervening phrases can serve a purpose, here indicating order.
5
Note the later dispute on the basis of wiping over khuffs
Some Hanafi scholars argue that the reconciliation proves wiping over khuffs is established by the Qur'an. The majority, however, hold that it is established by the Sunnah, with the khafd reading interpreted metaphorically or due to adjacency.
This shows that even within the Hanafi school, the strength of the reconciliation is debated.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (hashiya) expanding on a section of the matn of Usul al-Shashi concerning the correct method of using texts (tarq al-murad bi al-nusus). It provides four concrete examples of weak 'tamassuk' (clinging to a text) where the opponent attempts to prove a disputed ruling by citing a text that does not address the point of contention. The commentator systematically shows that in each case, the text is 'sākit 'anhu' (silent about the disputed point), making the tamassuk invalid. 

Example 1 (Blood and Vinegar): The opponent argues that since the hadith says 'wash the blood with water,' using vinegar to remove impurity is not valid because the command specifies water. The Hanafi response distinguishes between the physical removal of impurity (which is agreed upon) and the legal purity of the spot after removal with vinegar. The text only commands washing with water when blood is present; it is silent on whether the spot becomes pure if the blood is removed by another substance. Hence, the tamassuk is weak.

Example 2 (Zakat Sheep): The opponent uses the hadith 'in forty sheep, one sheep' to argue that paying the value (qīma) instead of the animal is not allowed. The Hanafi position agrees that the obligation is the sheep, but the text does not address whether paying its value suffices. Since the cause (ʿilla) for the obligation is meeting the poor's need, which is equally fulfilled by the value, the value is acceptable. The text is silent on mode of payment.

Example 3 (Umrah Obligation): The opponent quotes 'And complete the Hajj and Umrah' to argue that Umrah is obligatory initially. The commentary clarifies that the verse addresses completion after starting (i.e., once one has entered ihram), not the initial obligation. The text is silent on initial obligation, and the dispute between Hanafis and Shafi‘is on this point is not resolved by this verse. 

Example 4 (Defective Sale): The opponent uses the prohibition 'do not sell a dirham for two dirhams' to argue that a defective sale does not confer ownership because it is prohibited. The Hanafi counter argues that the text only prohibits the transaction; it is silent on the legal effect (ownership after possession). Since ownership is a separate ruling, the prohibition alone does not negate it. The text is silent, so tamassuk is weak.

Throughout, the commentary emphasizes that valid tamassuk requires the text to address the exact point in dispute. This lesson trains the student to critically evaluate how texts are cited in legal arguments, a core skill in Hanafi usul.

Key Terms
النص ساكت عنه (al-nass sākit ‘anhu): The text does not address or rule upon the specific point in dispute.
التمسك (tamassuk): Clinging to a text as evidence for a ruling, often in a way that extends beyond the text's explicit scope.

The Letter Ba for Attachment and Its Fiqh Application in Sale
Linguistic definition and legal application of the letter ba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi linguistic principle that the letter ba indicates attachment (ilṣāq) to determine which item in a sale contract is the price and which is the sold object.

Core Idea
Ba marks the price in a sale because the subsidiary must attach to the foundation.

In this lesson, we explore the letter ba (باء) — a simple particle that carries profound legal consequences. The Hanafi school insists that its primary meaning in Arabic is ilṣāq (attachment), based on consensus of lexicographers. This linguistic reality shapes how we interpret contracts: when ba enters on one of the two items in a sale, that item is identified as the price (subsidiary) and the other as the sold object (foundation). Understanding this principle will help you correctly classify the elements of a sale and avoid common mistakes in contract language.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning of ba
State that the letter ba is a particle (ḥarf jarr) whose primary meaning in Arabic is ilṣāq (attachment), by consensus of linguists. Other meanings (tabʿīḍ, ziyāda) are metaphorical and secondary.
The consensus (ijmāʿ ahl al-lugha) is the proof for this meaning.
2
Recognize the relationship between sold object and price
Understand that in a sale, the sold object (mabīʿ) is the foundation (aṣl) and the price (thaman) is subsidiary (tabaʿ). Evidence: destruction of the sold object voids the sale, but destruction of the price does not.
The price is a condition (shart) for the sale, not the primary object.
3
Apply the attachment principle
The principle: the subsidiary should be attached to the foundation, not vice versa. Since ba indicates attachment, when it enters on an item in a sale, that item is the subsidiary (price) attached to the foundation (sold object).
This is a logical deduction (talīl) from the meaning of ba.
4
Work through the examples
Examine the two scenarios: (1) 'I sold you this slave for a kurr of wheat' → ba enters on the kurr, so kurr is price, slave is sold object. (2) 'I sold you a kurr of wheat for this slave' → ba enters on the slave, so slave is price, kurr is sold object.
The ba in the second example is implicitly on 'this slave' after the verb.
5
Derive the fiqh ruling
Conclude: the item on which ba enters is the price (thaman). Therefore, it is permissible to substitute (exchange) the price before receipt, since the price is subsidiary and not the primary object of the sale.
This ruling is muftā bihī in the Hanafi madhhab.

Presenting Reports to the Quran and Sunnah (al-'Ard)
Three types of narrators and the rule of presenting reports to Quran and Sunnah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of 'ard (presenting reports to the Quran and well-known Sunnah) to identify and reject contradictory ahad reports.

Core Idea
A report that contradicts the Quran or well-known Sunnah must be rejected — the Quran takes precedence over ahad hadith.

This passage establishes a foundational rule of Hanafi usul al-fiqh: when a report (khabar) conflicts with the Quran or well-known Sunnah, it is set aside. The rule is justified by the potential unreliability of narrators, as narrated from Ali ibn Abi Talib. Two concrete examples — the hadith on touching the penis and the hadith on marriage without a guardian — demonstrate how the Quran overrides apparent meanings of reports.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Identify the principle of 'ard: present any khabar to the Quran and well-known Sunnah; accept if it agrees, reject if it contradicts.
2
Identify the three narrator types
From Ali's statement: a sincere believer who understands, an ignorant Bedouin who may change meaning unintentionally, and a hypocrite who fabricates. This motivates the rule.
3
Analyze the first example (touching the penis)
Show how Quran 9:108 praises those who perform istinja (involving touching private parts), contradicting the hadith 'Whoever touches his penis let him perform wudu.' The Hanafi conclusion: touching does not invalidate wudu.
The istinbat chain from the lesson illustrates this.
4
Analyze the second example (marriage without guardian)
Show how Quran 2:232 instructs guardians not to prevent women from marrying, contradicting the hadith that such marriage is invalid. Hanafi ruling: a woman can marry without a guardian (subject to other conditions).
5
Conclude the Hanafi ruling
Both ahad hadith are set aside in favor of the Quran. The principle is that the Quran takes precedence over ahad reports when there is a clear contradiction.

Commentary
This passage establishes a foundational usul rule: when a report (khabar) conflicts with the Quran or well-known Sunnah, it must be rejected. The author justifies this by citing a report from Ali ibn Abi Talib categorizing narrators into three types: a reliable believer, an ignorant Bedouin who may change meaning unintentionally, and a hypocrite who fabricates. These examples show that the Quran takes precedence over ahad reports.

The Mujtahid's Priority: Quran First, Then Sunnah
Mandates the mujtahid to derive rulings from Quran first, then Sunnah, then other sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the obligation of the mujtahid to seek rulings first from the Quran, then the Sunnah, and explain the basis for this hierarchy in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
The mujtahid must seek the ruling in the Quran before any other source.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi establishes a foundational principle of Hanafi ijtihad. The mujtahid is not free to begin with his own opinion or analogy; he must first exhaust the primary revealed sources. The Quran, being qat'í al-thubut, takes precedence. Only if no clear ruling is found there does he turn to the Sunnah, starting with the most authentic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the duty
Recognize that the passage begins with 'الواجب على المجتهد' – a declaration of obligation. This is not optional; it is a binding requirement.
2
Note the sequence
The ruling must be sought first from the Quran, then from the Sunnah. The word 'ثم' indicates a strict order. The mujtahid does not move to the next source until the Quran yields no clear ruling.
The sequence is chronological and hierarchical.
3
Understand the scope
The phrase 'بصريح النص أو دلالته' indicates that both explicit texts (sarih al-nass) and implicit indications (dalalah) from these sources are valid. The mujtahid must consider all types of textual signification.
Hanafis recognize four types of dalalah: ibarah, isharah, dalalah, iqtida.
4
Relate to Hanafi methodology
This order reflects the hierarchy of evidence in Hanafi usul. The Quran is at the top due to its certainty of transmission (qat'i al-thubut) and meaning (qat'i al-dalalah in most verses). The Sunnah, while authoritative, is categorized into mutawatir, mashhur, and ahad, with only the first two being qat'i.
This prioritization is a key feature distinguishing Hanafi methodology from some other schools.

Core Idea
The traveler's concession is derived by direct linguistic indication from the Quranic principle of ease, not by analogy.

This lesson explores a key Usul al-Fiqh method: deriving a specific ruling from a general textual principle. The source passage from Usul al-Shashi, with its commentary, demonstrates how the Hanafi school uses the Quranic verse 'Allah intends ease for you' to justify the traveler's breaking fast, and defends this derivation against critics who argue it must be qiyas. Guided Path
1

The First Part of Time Establishes Obligation
Explains how the first part of time causes obligation and how the person's state affects it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the first part of time is the cause for obligation and apply the Hanafi rule for transferring causality to later parts of time when performance is delayed.

Core Idea
The first part of time triggers the obligation; delay transfers the cause to later parts.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi explains a fundamental Hanafi principle: obligation (wujub) is established by entering the time, not by its passage. The first part of the time is the direct cause. If you do not perform at once, the causality transfers to the next part, and so on until the end. This ensures you can pray early without waiting. The text also considers the condition of the person (capacity) and the quality of the time (complete or deficient).

Guided Path
1
Identify the key claim
The text states: 'Obligation is established by entering the time' and 'the first part is a cause for obligation.' Pinpoint the direct link between the entry of time and the creation of duty.
This is the core sabab (cause) principle.
2
Understand the transfer mechanism
If one does not perform in the first part, the causality transfers to the second part, then third, etc., until the last part. This transfer allows the obligation to become fixed even if delayed.
Two ways: transfer or consideration of the entire time? The text mentions two ways.
3
Consider the condition of the servant
The obligation depends on the person's capacity (ahliyyah) at the relevant part of time. If a child becomes adult, or a disbeliever converts, or a menstruating woman becomes pure during the time, the prayer becomes obligatory.
Capacity at the time of obligation—not at the start—determines the duty.
4
Note the quality of time
If the part of time is complete (not a forbidden time), the duty is complete and must be performed completely. If the part is deficient (e.g., sunrise), the duty is deficient, but one can still perform it completely.
Completeness and deficiency of the cause transfer to the obligation.
5
Apply to examples
Traveler/resident scenario: if one is traveler at start but resident at end, pray 4; if resident at start and traveler at end, pray 2. Similarly, loss of capacity (hayd, nifas) in the last part drops the obligation.
The end of time is decisive for the person's status.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi deals with how Shariah obligations are attached to their causes, specifically focusing on time-based obligations like prayer. The author explains that obligation (wujub) is established by entering the time (dukhul al-waqt), not by the complete passage of time. The first part (juz' awwal) of the time is the cause (sabab) for obligation. If one does not perform in the first part, the causality transfers to subsequent parts until the last part, where it becomes firmly established. This ensures that one can perform the obligation early in the time without waiting for the end. The condition of the servant (hal al-'abd) is considered: if a person gains capacity (ahliyyah) during the time (e.g., a child reaches puberty, a disbeliever accepts Islam, or a menstruating woman becomes pure), the prayer becomes obligatory. Conversely, if one loses capacity (e.g., through menstruation, postpartum bleeding, insanity, or unconsciousness) in the last part, the prayer drops. Travelers and residents have different prayer counts based on their status at the end of the time. The quality of the part (kamal/nuqsa) also matters: if the part is complete (e.g., not a forbidden time), the duty is complete and should be performed completely; performing it in a deficient time (e.g., at sunrise) is not valid. The footnotes expand on these points: Footnote (1) argues against making obligation dependent on the entire time; Footnote (2) explains the two ways of transferring causality; Footnote (3) discusses how completeness/deficiency of the cause affects the obligation; Footnote (4) defines 'first part' precisely. The second-half footnotes address performing prayers at disliked times. The Hanafis reconcile hadiths: while there is a hadith about catching Asr before sunset, many hadiths forbid prayer during sunrise, sunset, and noon. The Hanafis resort to qiyas (analogy) to prefer validity for Asr and invalidity for Fajr. The Shafi'is differentiate between obligatory and supererogatory prayers, but the Hanafis argue that the indefinite in a negative context is general. The author defends the Hanafi method of using qiyas after apparent conflict.

Key Terms
سَبَب (sabab): The cause that triggers a Shariah ruling; here, the first part of time is the cause for obligation.
وُجُوب (wujūb): The legal obligation to perform an act; it is established by the presence of the sabab (cause).
نَقْلُ السَّبَبِيَّةِ (naql al-sababiyyah): The transfer of causality from the first part of time to subsequent parts if the obligation is not performed in the first part.

Hanafi Ruling on Obligatory Prayers during Forbidden Times
Explains the Hanafi ruling on obligatory prayers in forbidden times and the usul debate with Shafi'is.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on performing obligatory prayers during forbidden times (sunrise, sunset, noon) and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the usul principle of ta'āruḍ and qiyas.

Core Idea
Obligatory Asr prayer is valid in its disliked time; obligatory Fajr is invalid if performed after sunrise; qiyas resolves hadith conflict.

This passage from the footnotes of Usul al-Shashi addresses a major ikhtilaf: can obligatory prayers be performed during the three forbidden times (sunrise, sunset, zenith)? The Hanafis differentiate between Asr and Fajr based on qiyas after apparent conflict of hadiths. The Shafi'is hold that the prohibition only applies to supererogatory prayers. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi reasoning, the use of qiyas after ta'āruḍ, and the rebuttal to Shafi'i objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
The issue is whether obligatory prayers (fara'id) are valid when performed during times when prayer is prohibited (e.g., after Fajr until sunrise, after Asr until sunset, at noon). Hanafis say some are valid (Asr) and some invalid (Fajr). Shafi'is say all valid.
The text focuses on this dispute in the long footnote.
2
Identify the Hanafi evidence
Hanafis rely on hadiths that forbid prayer at those times (e.g., 'No prayer after Fajr until sunrise...') and argue these are general (āmm) including obligatory prayers. They also use qiyas: via analogy, Asr at sunset is valid because its time is still remaining; Fajr after sunrise is invalid because its time has gone.
The text mentions 'many hadiths with mutawatir meaning'.
3
Identify the Shafi'i evidence
Shafi'is cite the hadith: 'Whoever catches a rak'ah before sunset has caught Asr' and argue the prohibition is specific to supererogatory prayers. They see no conflict because the prohibition hadiths are about supererogatory prayers only.
The text states: 'خص الشافعية أحاديث النهي بالنوافل لا بالفرائض'.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Hanafis respond: The indefinite in a negative context (like 'لا صلاة') is general, covering all prayers. There is no specific exception (mukhassis) to exclude obligatory prayers. Since the hadith conflict, they resort to qiyas, which supports validity for Asr and invalidity for Fajr.
The text says: 'هو بلا مخصص فلا يسمع'.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah)
Hanafis: If someone prays Asr after its time has become disliked (sunset soon), the prayer is valid but makruh? Actually, the text says it is valid because the time is still present. Fajr after sunrise is invalid and must be made up. Shafi'is: both are valid.
The thamarah is a real difference in fiqh rulings affecting millions of Muslims.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi deals with how Shariah obligations are attached to their causes, specifically focusing on time-based obligations like prayer. The author explains that obligation (wujub) is established by entering the time (dukhul al-waqt), not by the complete passage of time. The first part (juz' awwal) of the time is the cause (sabab) for obligation. If one does not perform in the first part, the causality transfers to subsequent parts until the last part, where it becomes firmly established. This ensures that one can perform the obligation early in the time without waiting for the end. The condition of the servant (hal al-'abd) is considered: if a person gains capacity (ahliyyah) during the time (e.g., a child reaches puberty, a disbeliever accepts Islam, or a menstruating woman becomes pure), the prayer becomes obligatory. Conversely, if one loses capacity (e.g., through menstruation, postpartum bleeding, insanity, or unconsciousness) in the last part, the prayer drops. Travelers and residents have different prayer counts based on their status at the end of the time. The quality of the part (kamal/nuqsa) also matters: if the part is complete (e.g., not a forbidden time), the duty is complete and should be performed completely; performing it in a deficient time (e.g., at sunrise) is not valid. The footnotes expand on these points: Footnote (1) argues against making obligation dependent on the entire time; Footnote (2) explains the two ways of transferring causality; Footnote (3) discusses how completeness/deficiency of the cause affects the obligation; Footnote (4) defines 'first part' precisely. The second-half footnotes address performing prayers at disliked times. The Hanafis reconcile hadiths: while there is a hadith about catching Asr before sunset, many hadiths forbid prayer during sunrise, sunset, and noon. The Hanafis resort to qiyas (analogy) to prefer validity for Asr and invalidity for Fajr. The Shafi'is differentiate between obligatory and supererogatory prayers, but the Hanafis argue that the indefinite in a negative context is general. The author defends the Hanafi method of using qiyas after apparent conflict.

Key Terms
تَعَارُض (ta'āruḍ): Apparent conflict between two evidence (here, hadiths).
قِيَاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning used to derive a ruling when clear text is lacking or conflicting.
مُخَصِّص (mukhassis): A specification that restricts a general ruling; here, the Shafi'is lack a mukhassis for their specification.

Deriving the Ruling of Compulsion (Ikrah) from Quranic Exception
Explaining the ruling of ikrah and its effect on prohibitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Quranic exception (istithna') in Surah al-An'am 6:119 to derive the ruling of permissibility for prohibited acts under compulsion, and explain the sin of refusing the permissible.

Core Idea
Necessity makes the forbidden permissible; refusing the permissible under coercion is like suicide.

This lesson explores a powerful example of istinbat (legal derivation) from the Qur'an. The text addresses a life-or-death scenario: when a person is coerced under threat of death to eat carrion or drink wine, what is the ruling? The answer is rooted in an exception clause in Surah al-An'am, which serves as a key principle in Hanafi usul. Understanding this derivation will refine your ability to locate and apply legal exceptions in sacred texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its default ruling
The acts in question are eating carrion and drinking wine. The default ruling for these acts is haram (prohibited). The lesson introduces a scenario of compulsion (ikrah) which may change the ruling.
Default ruling is from explicit prohibitions in the Qur'an and Sunnah.
2
Locate the Quranic evidence
The author cites Qur'an 6:119: 'وَقَدْ فَصَّلَ لَكُم مَّا حَرَّمَ عَلَيْكُمْ إِلَّا مَا اضْطُرِرْتُمْ' (And He has detailed for you what He has forbidden to you, except what you are compelled to). This verse provides the foundation for the exception.
The verse is general in scope but contains an explicit exception.
3
Analyze the textual structure: the istithna'
The word 'إِلَّا' (except) is an exception particle. It excludes from the object of 'حَرَّمَ' (forbidden) those things to which one is compelled. This means the prohibition does not apply to those items under compulsion.
In Hanafi usul, an exception from a general prohibition indicates permissibility (ibahah) for the excepted case.
4
Derive the ruling for the coerced person
Since the prohibition is removed, the coerced person may eat carrion or drink wine; it becomes permissible (mubah) for them. The text emphasizes that the act is not merely excused but legally permissible.
The ruling is derived directly from the exception, making it a clear textual proof (dalil naqli).
5
Apply the derived ruling to the refusal scenario
If the coerced person refuses to consume the permissible item and is killed, he is sinful. The text compares this to suicide because he chose death over that which Allāh had made lawful for him in that situation.
This shows that the obligation to preserve life overrides the general prohibition; refusing leads to sin.
