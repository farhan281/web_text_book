Kinayah in Hudud: The Requirement of Explicit Wording
Explains that hudud penalties are not established by kinayah confessions due to shubha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that hudud are averted by shubuhat to identify when a confession or accusation is insufficient for a hadd penalty, and distinguish sarih from kinayah in the context of hudud.

Core Idea
Hudud are averted by doubt: only explicit (sarih) confession or accusation establishes a hadd; kinayah creates shubha and is insufficient.

This passage from Usul al-Fiqh applies a foundational Hanafi principle: hudud (fixed penalties) are averted by doubts. The commentator explains that kinayah (allusion) expressions introduce ambiguity and thus cannot be the basis for imposing a hadd. We will explore the linguistic and legal rationale: why sarih (explicit) wording is required, and how this rule applies to confessions of zina, deaf-mute gestures, and even the response 'sadaqta' in a qadhf case.

Guided Path
1
State the general principle
Recognize that hudud are averted by doubts (shubuhat) based on the hadith 'Idra'u al-hudud bi al-shubuhat'. This is a cardinal rule in Hanafi fiqh.
Key hadith: 'Avert hudud by doubts.'
2
Identify the application to kinayah
Understand that kinayah expressions inherently involve shubha because their meaning depends on intention and context, lacking the clarity of sarih.
Sarih is 'complete in conveying the intended meaning'; kinayah is 'deficient' and requires niyyah.
3
Analyze the examples given
Work through each example: (1) confessing 'I had intercourse with so-and-so' (kinayah) → no hadd; (2) deaf-mute confession by gesture → no hadd; (3) saying 'sadaqta' to a slanderer → kinayah → no hadd for qadhf.
The 'sadaqta' example shows that even a response can be kinayah due to multiple possible interpretations.
4
Extract the rationale
Link the examples back to the principle: hudud require certainty (yaqin). Kinayah introduces probability (zann), which is insufficient. This is why sarih is mandated.
The commentary states: 'in kinayah there is doubt and deficiency in establishing its result due to concealment.'
5
Apply to a new scenario
Consider whether a confession like 'I committed an act that deserves punishment' would be sufficient. Apply the same reasoning: it is kinayah because it does not specify the crime, thus shubha remains, and the hadd is not established.
This tests understanding of the principle's scope.

Commentary
This passage applies the ruling on kinayah to the field of hudud (fixed penalties). The commentator notes that hudud are averted by doubts (shubuhat) based on the hadith 'Idra'u al-hudud bi al-shubuhat' (Avert hudud by doubts). Since kinayah expressions involve ambiguity and doubt, they are insufficient to establish hudud. Several examples are given: if a person confesses to zina by saying 'I had intercourse with so-and-so' (which is kinayah, not explicit like 'I committed zina'), the hadd is not applied. Similarly, if a deaf-mute confesses by gesture, the hadd is not applied because it is ambiguous. The text also discusses the case of qadhf (false accusation of zina): if someone accuses another of zina and a third party says 'You spoke truth' (صدقت), this statement is considered a kinayah form of slander because it can be interpreted in multiple ways (e.g., you told the truth about something else, or you were truthful in the past but now you are lying). Therefore, the hadd of qadhf is not imposed on the one who says 'sadaqta'. The commentary emphasizes that the principle behind these rulings is that explicit wording (sarih) is necessary for establishing hudud because it eliminates doubt, whereas kinayah leaves room for interpretation and thus does not meet the stringent requirements for penalties.

Key Terms
حد (hadd): A fixed penalty prescribed by Shari'ah for specific crimes such as zina, theft, and qadhf.
صريح (sarih): Explicit wording that leaves no ambiguity; required for establishing hudud.
شبهة (shubha): A doubt or ambiguity that prevents the imposition of a hadd penalty.

Abandoning Literal Meaning Due to Siyaq al-Kalam
Demonstrating how contextual clues override literal meaning, with the example of 'descend'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a contextual clue (qarina) overrides the literal meaning (haqiqa) of a command in usul al-fiqh, and apply this to determine the ruling in Hanafi cases.

Core Idea
Context can override the literal meaning of a word, changing the legal ruling.

This lesson introduces the third way literal meanings are abandoned: through siyaq al-kalam (context of speech). The Hanafi school emphasizes that contextual clues (qara'in) can divert a word from its haqiqa to a majaz or specific intended meaning. We will examine the classic example from al-Siyar al-Kabir: the command 'descend' with and without a qualifying condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the command and its literal meaning
The command is 'descend' (إنزل). Its literal meaning is a request or permission to physically descend.
2
Recognize the contextual clue
The addition 'if you are a man' (إن كنت رجلا) is a qarina that alters the intent.
Note that 'being a man' is already apparent, so the condition must indicate a different purpose.
3
Determine the new meaning
The speaker is not really permitting descent but challenging the addressee's manhood. The intended meaning is a taunt, not permission.
4
Apply the new meaning to the ruling
When the harbi descends without condition, he is granted aman; with condition, he is not, because the command was not meant to grant permission.
This shows that the legal effect depends on the intended meaning, not the literal one.
5
Generalize the principle
Siyaq al-kalam can override haqiqa; the legal outcome changes based on contextual cues.
This principle is foundational in Hanafi usul for interpreting commands.

The commentary explains that the condition 'if you are a man' shows the context overrides the literal meaning, as being a man is already apparent, so the condition indicates a different intent. This principle is fundamental in Hanafi usul: siyaq al-kalam can divert a word from its haqiqah to a majaz or specific meaning.

Following the Prophet's Actions: Muwazabah and Specificity
Expansion of usul rule on following the Prophet's actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that the Prophet's action only obligates following when it is continuously performed (muwazabah) and not proven to be specific to him, and identify evidence of specificity.

Core Idea
The Prophet's action alone does not obligate; only continuous practice without omission and without specificity does.

In the previous lesson, we learned that the command form (أمر) obligates action. But what about the Prophet's own actions? Do they automatically become obligatory for us? This lesson explores the Hanafi answer: not automatically. The Prophet's action is not like his imperative speech; following his actions is only obligatory when he continuously performed them without ever omitting them, and when there is no evidence that the action was specific to him. We will examine the key concepts of muwazabah and dafil al-ikhtisas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its conditions
Read the opening statement: 'Following his actions is only obligatory upon continuous practice and absence of evidence of specification.' Identify the two conditions for obligation: muwazabah (continuous performance without omission) and no dafil al-ikhtisas (specificity evidence).
2
Analyze the proof: the hadith of the sandals
The Prophet removed his sandals in prayer; the companions imitated; he disapproved. Why does this prove that his action alone does not obligate? Because if following were obligatory, he would not have disapproved.
The hadith shows the absence of wujub from mere action.
3
Distinguish between action and command
Address the counter-argument from 'Pray as you have seen me praying.' The passage explains that following in that case is by the command (imperative صَلُّوا), not by the mere action. This reconciles the hadith with the rule.
The imperative creates obligation, not the action itself.
4
Refine the concept of muwazabah
The hashiya clarifies: 'continuous practice' means never omitting the action. If he sometimes omitted it, it indicates sunnah. Only if he never omitted it does it indicate wujub, because if it were merely recommended, he would have left it at least once to teach permissibility.
Muwazabah without omission is the key indicator of obligation.
5
Apply the rule
Given a scenario (e.g., the Prophet always performed the duha prayer without ever missing it), determine whether it is obligatory. Consider specificity: duha was specific to him, so despite continuous performance, it is not obligatory for us.
Apply both conditions: muwazabah and no specificity.

Commentary
This passage is an usul rule expansion, detailing the Hanafi position on following the Prophet's actions (taqlid al-af'al). The base text establishes that the specificity of command to the imperative form means the Prophet's action does not create obligation by itself. This is a standard Hanafi usul principle distinguishing between actions that are sunnah (recommended) and those that are wajib (obligatory) based on consistency.

Commentary
This is a usul definition passage explaining the concept of 'bayan al-hal' (clarification by the situation). The matn defines it by giving examples from the Lawgiver (the Prophet) and from everyday legal situations. The core principle is that silence (sukut) in a context where clarification is needed constitutes a form of legal clarification (bayan). The first and most important example is the Prophet's silence when witnessing an action among his companions; since he is obligated to forbid evil (according to the Quranic verses quoted), his silence indicates that the action is permissible (mubah). This is a source of law in usul. The other examples are analogical extensions: the pre-emptor's silence indicates consent to the sale, the virgin's silence indicates consent to marriage (due to shyness preventing verbal acceptance but not rejection), and the master's silence when his slave trades in the market indicates permission. The commentator elaborates on each: for the Prophet's silence, he provides rational and scriptural proofs; for the virgin, he explains the psychological reasoning; for the master, he addresses a subtle point about the conjunction 'and' versus 'or'. The authority weight of this passage is from the Zahir al-Riwayah as it is a standard usul topic.

سكوت (sukut): Silence; in legal context, when from a qualified person, it can indicate approval or consent.

Silence in a Place of Need as Legal Expression (Bayan)
Explaining the principle that silence in a place of need is equivalent to bayan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi usul principle that silence in a context of need is equivalent to bayan, apply it to transactions and judicial cases, and evaluate the Shafi'i objection.

Core Idea
Silence in a context of need is legally treated as consent; otherwise, harm and deception would result.

This lesson unpacks a foundational Hanafi usul principle: silence (sukut) when there is a genuine need for clarification is considered as an expression of consent or clarification (bayan). The passage from the commentary on Al-Shashi's Usul applies this principle to three areas: authorizing transactions, the refusal to take an oath in court (nukul), and the formation of consensus (ijma' sukuti). Understanding this principle requires grasping the rationale of preventing harm (darar) and deception (gharar), which overrides the ambiguity inherent in silence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
State the core principle: 'silence in a place of need is like bayan' (al-sukut fi mawdi' al-hajah k-al-bayan). Recognize that it is a rule of interpretation in Hanafi usul.
2
Analyse the First Application: Transactions
Examine how the principle is applied when a person remains silent while others deal with his property. The silence is deemed permission (idhn). Understand the Hanafi proof: the hadith prohibiting harm and deception.
Contrast with Shafi'i view: silence is ambiguous and cannot be taken as consent.
3
Analyse the Second Application: Nukul (Refusal of Oath)
Look at the case where a defendant refuses to take an oath. The Hanafi school treats this silence as either confession (iqrar) or tender (badhl) of the claim. Identify the difference between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the six items (e.g., marriage).
The practical consequence is whether istihlaf (oath administration) is permitted.
4
Analyse the Third Application: Ijma' Sukuti
Understand how silence of scholars after hearing an opinion, after a period of reflection (three days), constitutes silent consensus. The rationale: silence about the truth is not permissible for a scholar, so it indicates consent.
This is a valid proof (hujja) in Hanafi usul.
5
Restrict the Principle: The Need Condition
Note that the principle applies only 'fi mawdi' al-hajah' (in a place of need). Without need, silence does not indicate anything. Example: the Lawgiver's silence about expiation in the thief/murderer verses does not imply dropping it.
The need is determined by the context; it must be a situation where clarification is expected.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Shashi's Usul discusses a foundational Hanafi principle: silence in a context of need (sukut fi mawdi' al-hajah) is legally treated as an expression of consent or clarification (bayan). The author opens by stating the section heading 'As for the clarification of the state' and immediately applies the principle to three areas. First, in transactions: when a person is silent while others deal with his property, that silence is considered permission (idhn) for the transaction, authorizing it. This is because people rely on such silence to know consent, and if it were not treated as permission, the silence would deceive those dealing, leading to harm (gharar), which is prohibited by hadith. The passage then addresses the Shafi'i objection that silence could be due to anger or disgust, hence cannot be definitive evidence. The Hanafi response is that necessity (haja) requires treating it as permission to prevent harm, supported by the hadith 'Harm and reciprocating harm are not allowed' and 'Whoever deceives us is not of us.' Second, in judicial cases: when a defendant refuses to take an oath (nukul) after being asked to deny a claim, this silence is considered either as confession (iqrar) according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (Sahibayn) or as tender of the claim (badl) according to Abu Hanifa. This difference leads to a practical consequence: in six specific items (like marriage, divorce), Abu Hanifa does not allow istihlaf (administering the oath to the plaintiff) because tender is not possible in those, while the Sahibayn allow it because confession works. Third, in the formation of consensus (ijma'): when a qualified scholar speaks on a legal issue and other scholars of the age remain silent after knowledge and reflection (typically three days), that silence is considered consent, forming a silent consensus (ijma' sukuti) that is valid proof of the ruling. The passage emphasizes that the silent person is like a 'mute devil' if they know the truth and stay quiet—hence their silence is taken as agreement. The commentary further restricts the principle: silence is only bayan when there is a genuine need. Without such need, as in the case of the general texts about cutting off the thief's hand—which do not mention indemnity or expiation—silence does not imply any additional ruling. Thus, the principle is utilitarian: it prevents harm and upholds the integrity of legal dealings.

سكوت في موضع الحاجة (sukut fi mawdi' al-hajah): Silence in a place of need; considered as bayan (clarification) according to Hanafi usul.
إجماع سكوتي (ijma' sukuti): Silent consensus: when some scholars state an opinion and other scholars remain silent after knowledge and reflection, their silence is taken as consent.
بيان (bayan): Clarification; making a ruling known. Here, silence in a context of need is equated to bayan.
نكل (nukul): Refusal to take an oath (in court); such refusal is treated as silence that implies confession or tender of the claim.

The Three Types of Khabar: Mutawatir, Mashhur, and Ahad
Defines three categories of prophetic reports based on certainty of transmission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and define the three categories of khabar (mutawatir, mashhur, ahad) in Hanafi usul, and state the epistemological weight of each.

Core Idea
Prophetic reports are not all equal: mutawatir yields certainty, mashhur near-certainty, ahad probability.

In Hanafi usul, the Sunnah is a primary source of law, but not all reports of the Prophet (PBUH) carry the same authority. The scholars classified khabar into three levels based on the number of narrators and the certainty of transmission. Understanding this classification is essential for properly weighing hadith evidence in legal reasoning.

The author begins by affirming that the Sunnah is like the Quran in being binding. Locate the Quranic verse reference (4:80) implied in the text.
The text states 'من أطاعه فقد أطاع الله', a direct quote from Quran 4:80.
2
Recognize need for classification
Why must khabar be divided into types? Because the transmission process varies in certainty; some reports are beyond doubt, others contain varying degrees of uncertainty.
3
Define the three categories
For each category, identify its defining feature: mutawatir (no doubt, large group at every level), mashhur (slight doubt, originally few but later widespread), ahad (strong probability and doubt, few narrators).
The text explicitly states the three divisions.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi establishes the classification of transmitted reports (khabar) from the Prophet (PBUH). The author first affirms that the Sunnah holds the same binding authority as the Quran, supported by verses like 'Nor does he speak from [his own] desire; it is only revelation revealed' (Quran 53:3-4) and 'Whatever the Messenger gives you, take it' (Quran 59:7). The same textual categories (khas, aam, mujmal, etc.) that apply to Quranic verses also apply to hadiths because the Prophet's words are revelation and share the same literary qualities. However, the transmission of hadiths varies in certainty, leading to three divisions: mutawatir (mass-transmitted, yielding certain knowledge), mashhur (well-known but with minimal doubt, yielding near-certainty), and ahad (single-narrator, yielding probability but not certainty). The definition of mutawatir given here includes two essential conditions: (1) the narrators must be so numerous at every level that collusion on falsehood is inconceivable, and (2) this condition of crowd must persist from the beginning of the chain to the end. The author does not require additional conditions such as diversity of locations or exact number of narrators, following the majority (jumhur) of scholars. This classification is fundamental in Hanafi usul for determining the epistemological weight of hadiths in legal derivation.

Key Terms
الخبر (al-khabar): What is transmitted from the Prophet (PBUH) by way of report, including his sayings, actions, and tacit approvals.
المتواتر (al-mutawatir): A report transmitted by a group at every level such that collusion on falsehood is impossible, yielding certain knowledge.
المشهور (al-mashhur): A report that was originally transmitted by a few (two or three) in the first generation but later became widely known; it carries a slight doubt (shubha) but is still authoritative.
الآحاد (al-ahad): A report transmitted by one or a few narrators in each generation, carrying strong probability and doubt; it is zanni (probable) and not certain.

Types of Khabar: Mutawatir, Mashhur, and Ahad
Defining three types of khabar and their epistemic-legal force

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mutawatir, mashhur, and ahad in terms of their definitions, epistemological force, and legal implications, and apply the Hanafi rule that khabar wahid obligates action without certainty.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, mutawatir gives certainty, mashhur gives tranquility, and ahad obligates action—each has a distinct legal-epistemic weight.

This lesson examines the foundational classification of reports (akhbar) in Hanafi legal theory. The passage from Usul al-Shashi defines three categories of khabar based on transmission frequency and epistemic force. Understanding these distinctions is critical for evaluating how different hadiths become sources of law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories and their definitions
Read the passage and list the three types: mutawatir (large group transmission that precludes collusion), mashhur (starts as ahad but becomes widely accepted), and khabar wahid (transmitted by one or a few). Note the examples given.

Understand that the Hanafi school, as per the passage, gives precedence to a khabar wahid from a reliable narrator over qiyas. This is based on the practice of the Companions.

Commentary
This passage is a foundational text on the classification of reports (khabar) in Hanafi usul. It opens with the three categories: mutawatir, mashhur, and ahad. The author defines mutawatir as a report transmitted by such a large number that it is impossible for them to conspire to lie, and it reaches the listener directly through successive generations. The example given is the transmission of the Quran and the details of prayer and zakat—these are known definitively. The mashhur is a report that starts as ahad but later becomes widely accepted in the second and third Islamic centuries, such that the ummah receives it with acceptance, giving it the strength of mutawatir in terms of action. Examples include the hadith on wiping over leather socks and the stoning penalty. Both mutawatir and mashhur necessitate action, and the passage underlines that there is no scholarly disagreement on this point; the controversy only concerns ahad reports. For mutawatir, its denial amounts to disbelief; for mashhur, denial is a blameworthy innovation (bid'ah) because it implies error in the consensus of the early community. Then the text defines khabar wahid: a report transmitted by one or a few individuals in each generation, not reaching the level of mashhur. It obliges action in practical legal matters, not in creed, provided that the narrator is Muslim, just (adl), precise (dabit), sane, and the chain is unbroken. The passage also emphasizes that the narrator's reliability is crucial; a narrator known for knowledge and ijtihad (like the senior Companions) is given preference over qiyas (analogy), meaning that their authentic report outweighs analogical reasoning. This is a key Hanafi principle: the report of a qualified narrator takes precedence over qiyas, contrary to some other schools like Maliki which prefer qiyas over ahad reports due to the possibility of error in narration.

Khabar is Stronger than Qiyas: The Hanafi Usul Principle
Explains the principle that hadith, even if weak, takes precedence over qiyas with examples from Imam Muhammad's riwaya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that khabar (prophetic report) is stronger than qiyas (analogical reasoning) and apply this principle to evaluate legal proofs.

Core Idea
Khabar is stronger than qiyas because its source (the Prophet's speech) is certain, while qiyas's illa is uncertain.

In Hanafi usul, the strength of legal evidence is hierarchical. Among the sources, khabar (even if ahad or weak in transmission) takes precedence over qiyas. This lesson explains the rationale behind this rule, provides key examples from the Companions and Imam Muhammad, and defends the Hanafi methodology against criticism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
The passage asserts that khabar (a prophetic report) is stronger than qiyas (analogical reasoning). Note the explicit comparative phrase: 'al-khabar aqwa min al-qiyas'.
2
Examine the key examples
Two main examples are given: the hadith of the Bedouin laughing in prayer (requiring repetition of wudu and prayer) and the hadith of vomiting/nosebleed (nullifying wudu). In both cases, the apparent qiyas would not require nullification, but the hadith overrides it.
3
Analyze the reasoning for preference
The author explains: doubt in qiyas is about the 'illa (whether it truly is the cause of the ruling), whereas doubt in khabar is only about its transmission chain (its asl is certain, being the Prophet's words). Therefore, khabar is fundamentally stronger.
This is a key usul principle: certainty in origin outweighs doubt in application.
4
Trace the Companions' practice
Examples from Umar (fetus, fingers), Ibn Umar (muzara'ah), and others show that they left their own qiyas-based opinions when they encountered a hadith. Imam Muhammad also narrated hadiths (e.g., delaying women) and left qiyas.
This demonstrates the principle's application across generations.
5
Apply the principle
Given a new case where a weak hadith contradicts a strong qiyas, identify which evidence is preferred and justify using the rationale from this lesson.
The principle is not absolute; if the narrator is not a faqih and the hadith is very weak, qiyas may be preferred (as per later usul).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from Usul al-Fiqh on the relative strength of Khabar (prophetic report) vs Qiyas (analogy). The author, through commentary, provides multiple examples from the Hanafi tradition where Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani and other Companions preferred a hadith over the apparent qiyas. Key examples: 1) Hadith of the Bedouin laughing in prayer requiring repetition of wudu and prayer, despite qiyas not requiring it since no impurity exits. 2) Hadith of vomiting/nosebleed nullifying wudu, again contrary to qiyas. 3) Hadith of delaying women in prayer alignment. 4) The author also cites Umar and Ibn Umar leaving their own opinions for hadith. The reasoning is that doubt in qiyas is about the 'illa (effective cause) — whether the ruling is truly based on that description — while doubt in khabar is only in the chain, not in the source (the Prophet's words). Therefore, khabar is fundamentally stronger. The author defends Abu Hanifah against critics who claim he preferred weak hadith over qiyas, arguing that this is correct methodology.

Key Terms
الخبر (al-khabar): In this context, a prophetic report (hadith) that is the basis for a ruling.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning to derive a ruling from an original case.

Sajdat al-Sahw: Hadith vs. Qiyas in Hanafi Usul
Dispute on sajdat al-sahw timing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school follows the explicit hadith over qiyas in the case of sajdat al-sahw, and identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions.

Core Idea
When a clear hadith contradicts qiyas, the Hanafi school takes the hadith, even if qiyas seems logical.

This passage examines a classic usul dispute: does explicit textual evidence (nas) always override analogical reasoning (qiyas)? The case study is the timing of the prostrations for forgetfulness (sajdat al-sahw) — should they be performed before or after the salam? The Hanafi school, citing a direct hadith, rules after salam, while the Shafi'i school prefers qiyas and rules before salam. Understanding this dispute reveals how each school weighs different sources of law.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi ruling on when sajdat al-sahw is performed: after the salam, based on the explicit hadith.
The hadith is: 'For every forgetfulness, two prostrations after the salam.'
2
Identify the opposing school and their proof
The Shafi'i school (and others) rule before the salam, using qiyas: the prostration compensates for an omission, so it should be within the prayer.
Qiyas: the compensator takes the place of the omitted part, which is inside the prayer.
3
Analyse the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: the hadith is explicit and takes precedence over qiyas. The Prophet's command is the primary source.
This illustrates the principle that a clear nas (nass qati) overrides qiyas.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: If a worshipper performs sajdat al-sahw before salam, the prayer is invalid according to Hanafis, but valid according to Shafi'is.
Also note Malik's compromise: for increase after salam, for deficiency before.
5
Trace the authority chain
The passage lists many Companions and early scholars supporting the Hanafi position (after salam) and those supporting the Shafi'i position (before salam).
The Hanafi list includes Ali, Ibn Mas'ud, Ibn Abbas, etc.

Commentary
This passage presents a specific example of a hadith that contradicts qiyas: the hadith stating that the prostrations for forgetfulness (sajdat al-sahw) are performed after the salam. The author (commentator) notes that qiyas would suggest they be done before salam, as the Shafi'i school holds, because the prostration compensates for an omission and should be within the prayer. However, the Hanafi school follows the explicit hadith and places them after salam, supported by many Companions and tabi'un. The passage also notes Malik's compromise: after salam for increase, before for deficiency. This illustrates the principle that a clear hadith takes precedence over qiyas, even if the hadith is from a non-faqih narrator (though here the narrators include many fuqaha).

Second Category of Narrators: Preference of Qiyas over Their Reports
Defines the second category of narrators and the rule for conflict with qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that qiyas is preferred over hadith from a non-faqih narrator and explain the reasoning based on doubt in transmission.

Core Idea
When a non-faqih narrator's hadith contradicts qiyas, qiyas prevails due to greater doubt in the hadith's text.

In Hanafi usul, not all reliable narrators are equal. Those who are trustworthy but lack juristic expertise (non-fuqaha) are treated with caution when their isolated reports conflict with qiyas. This lesson examines that rule, its rationale, and its application through classical examples from Abu Hurairah and the musarra case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the second category of narrators
Recognize that the narrator is 'adil and dabit but not a faqih (mujtahid). Examples from the text: Abu Hurairah, Anas ibn Malik, Uqbah ibn Amr.
These narrators are reliable in memory and integrity but lack the depth of understanding to transmit meaning accurately.
2
State the rule for conflict with qiyas
If the hadith agrees with qiyas, it is acted upon without issue. But if it contradicts, qiyas is preferred and the hadith is set aside.
The key phrase: 'وإن خالفه كان العمل بالقياس أولى' (if it contradicts, acting on qiyas is preferable).
3
Examine the examples provided
First example: Abu Hurairah's hadith 'wudu from fire' was countered by Ibn Abbas using qiyas. Second: the musarra hadith (returning a previously milked animal with a sa' of dates) was abandoned by Hanafis for qiyas.
These show the rule in action, not that all hadith from these narrators are rejected.
4
Analyze the reasoning (talil)
Two sources of doubt in the non-faqih's hadith: doubt in the chain (common to all ahadith) and doubt in the text due to transmission by meaning. A non-faqih may not accurately capture the Prophet's intended meaning. Qiyas only has doubt in the 'illa (cause). Thus the doubt is greater in the hadith, so qiyas is preferred.
This distinguishes from a faqih narrator whose understanding reduces text doubt.
5
Contrast with faqih narrators
If the narrator is a faqih (like Aisha or Ibn Abbas), his hadith takes precedence over qiyas even if contradictory. The ability to accurately transmit meaning removes the extra doubt.
This shows the hierarchy is not anti-hadith but based on reliability of meaning transmission.

Commentary
This passage establishes a key usul rule in the Hanafi school: the second category of narrators are those who are reliable (adil, dabit) but are not jurists (fuqaha). For such narrators, if their hadith agrees with qiyas, it is acted upon; but if it contradicts qiyas, then qiyas is preferred. The commentator provides two examples from Abu Hurairah: the hadith 'wudu from what fire touches' was rejected by Ibn Abbas using qiyas, and Abu Hurairah's hadith on 'musarra' (a previously milked animal) was abandoned by Hanafis for qiyas. The reasoning is that non-faqih narrators are prone to error in transmitting hadith by meaning, leading to potential mistakes in understanding or wording. This introduces doubt in the text itself, in addition to chain doubts (which exist for all ahadith). Qiyas, on the other hand, only has doubt in the 'illa (cause). Therefore, when a non-faqih's hadith opposes qiyas, the doubt is greater in the hadith, so qiyas is preferred.

Key Terms
الراوي غير الفقيه (al-rawi ghayr al-faqih): A narrator who is reliable in memory and integrity but lacks the juristic expertise to accurately convey the intended meaning of hadith.
المصراة (al-musarra): A female animal (sheep, cow, etc.) that has been milked and then left unmilked for a while, causing the udder to swell and appear full, misleading a buyer.

Conflict of Khabar Ahad with Quran: The Case of Touching the Private Part
Analyzing the conflict between a khabar ahad and a clear Quranic verse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a khabar ahad contradicted by a clear Quranic text is rejected, and identify the derivation steps (muarada and tarjih) in the case of touching the private part.

Core Idea
A khabar ahad cannot override a clear Quranic text; the Quran's qat'i nature takes precedence.

In this lesson, we examine how Hanafi usul handles an apparent conflict between a khabar ahad (the hadith 'Whoever touches his private part, let him perform wudu') and a clear Quranic verse (9:108 praising the people of Quba for their purification). The Hanafi method involves presenting the hadith to the Quran, finding contradiction, and then giving preference to the Quran because of its qat'i transmission and clarity. This case illustrates a key principle: when a zanni report contradicts a qat'i text, the qat'i prevails. We will walk through the derivation chain and the inter-madhhab dispute with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting texts
Locate the khabar ahad (hadith) that commands wudu after touching the private part, and the Quranic verse (9:108) that praises purification involving touch. Note the apparent contradiction: the hadith implies touching causes hadath, but the verse praises an act that includes touching as purification.
The hadith is a khabar ahad (zanni al-thubut). The Quran is qat'i al-thubut and its meaning is clear (zahir).
2
Understand the Hanafi argument for rejection
The people of Quba used to clean themselves after relieving themselves by using stones and then washing with water, which necessarily involved touching their private parts. The Quran calls this act 'taharah' (purification). If touching caused hadath, the act would be a defilement (tanjis), not purification. Therefore, the hadith contradicts the Quran and must be rejected.
The key is that the Quranic praise is absolute and does not distinguish between ritual and physical purity.
3
Examine the Shafi'i objection
The Shafi'i school objects that the purification in the verse refers only to physical impurity (najasah haqiqiyyah), not ritual impurity (hadath). So touching could still cause hadath without contradicting the verse. Also, they argue that even if touching causes hadath, the act of cleaning might still be praiseworthy as a means to perform wudu.
This objection is based on a different understanding of the scope of the Quranic praise.
4
Apply the Hanafi tarjih principle
The Hanafi response counters that the Quranic praise is general ('ala al-itlaq), and if touching were a hadath, the people of Quba would not deserve such praise. Since the Quran is qat'i and the hadith is zanni, the Quran takes precedence. Thus, the hadith is set aside, and the ruling is that touching does not break wudu.
This is a classic example of tarjih based on the strength of the evidence (qat'i vs zanni).

Conditions for Accepting Khabar al-Wahid in Hanafi Usul
Explaining rules for when khabar al-wahid is accepted or rejected with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi conditions for accepting or rejecting a khabar al-wahid, specifically whether it contradicts the apparent (zahir) or concerns matters of general need ('umum al-balwa).

Core Idea
A khabar al-wahid that contradicts an established principle or deals with general need must be widely known; otherwise it is rejected.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi lays out the Hanafi criteria for accepting a solitary report. It uses the example of a judgment with one witness and an oath to show that a report cannot overturn a well-known hadith. It then introduces the crucial concept that matters of general need require widespread transmission; otherwise the report is considered unreliable. Understanding these conditions is essential for weighing evidences in Hanafi methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base rule
The Hanafi school rejects a khabar al-wahid if it contradicts the apparent (zahir) or a well-known (mashhur) report. The text states: 'when a solitary report comes out contradicting the apparent, it is not acted upon.'
The 'apparent' refers to established principles or common practice known to the Companions.
2
Apply to the first example
The ruling allowing judgment with one witness and an oath contradicts the mashhur hadith 'The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the denier.' Since the hadith attributes the oath to the denier, a judgment that uses the oath for the claimant opposes this principle, so the solitary report is rejected.
The hadith is considered mashhur (well-known) and serves as the established principle.
3
Recognize a form of contradiction
One specific form of contradicting the apparent is when a report about a matter of general need ('umum al-balwa) does not become famous among the early generations. If an authentic ruling would have been widely needed, it should have been transmitted widely; its lack of fame indicates it is not reliable.
'Umum al-balwa means the ruling affects all or most Muslims regularly.
4
Analyze the fosterage examples
If a person reports that fosterage occurred after the marriage (supervening), his news is accepted because it does not contradict the apparent fame of the marriage. But if he reports that the marriage itself was invalid due to prior fosterage, his news is rejected because it contradicts the apparent (the publicly known marriage).
The distinction hinges on whether the news matches or opposes the established public knowledge (zahir).
5
Summarize the Hanafi principle
In Hanafi usul, a khabar al-wahid is accepted only if it does not oppose the apparent (zahir) or a mashhur hadith, and if it concerns general need, it must have been widely transmitted among the early generations. Otherwise, it is rejected as unreliable.
This is not a rejection of the chain but a methodological preference based on evidentiary strength.

Core Idea
Khabar al-wahid is proof in four categories, but not for Allah's punishments — those require certainty.

Khabar al-Wahid's Four Categories of Acceptance in Hanafi Usul
States the four categories where khabar al-wahid is accepted with proofs and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and differentiate the four categories where khabar al-wahid is accepted as proof, and state the conditions (number, justice) for each category.

Core Idea
Khabar al-wahid's acceptance depends on the nature of the right and the degree of obligation — not uniform across all legal matters.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi presents the Hanafi classification of khabar al-wahid into four types based on the right involved (right of Allah or the servant) and whether there is a pure or mixed obligation (ilzam). Understanding this taxonomy is essential for knowing when a single-narrator report suffices as proof in different legal contexts, from worship to transactions to penal matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
List the four types as mentioned in the text: 1) Pure right of Allah not a punishment, 2) Pure right of servant with pure obligation, 3) Pure right of servant without obligation, 4) Pure right of servant with mixed obligation.
The categories are distinguished by the nature of the right (Ḥaqq Allāh vs. Ḥaqq al-ʿAbd) and the presence or absence of ilzām (obligation).
2
Examine the conditions for each
For category 1: khabar al-wahid is accepted unconditionally (no requirement of number or justice). For category 2: requires both number (at least two witnesses) and justice (ʿadāla). For category 3: accepted even from a single narrator, whether just or fāsiq. For category 4: requires either number or justice (according to Abu Hanifa).
The conditions reflect the strength of the right and the likelihood of disputes.
3
Trace the proofs for each category
Category 1 is supported by the Prophet's acceptance of the Bedouin's testimony for Ramadan. Category 2 is based on Quranic verses requiring two just witnesses (2:282, 65:2). Category 3 is based on the Prophet's acceptance of gifts from both just and sinful informants. Category 4 is derived from examples like dismissal of an agent (ʿazl) and restriction (ḥajr).
The proofs are dalāʾil naqliyya (transmitted evidence).
4
Note the exceptional views and ikhtilāf
Al-Karkhī and others hold that khabar al-wahid is not accepted in penal punishments (ḥudūd, qisās) even if they are pure rights of Allah, because such punishments are repelled by doubts. Also, some scholars (unnamed) argue that khabar al-wahid is not accepted in initiating worship (ibtidāʾ ʿibāda) but only in branches.
The Hanafi position (majority) accepts khabar al-wahid in both original and derivative acts of worship, citing the Bedouin hadith.
5
Apply the taxonomy to concrete cases
Given a scenario, determine which category applies and what conditions (number, justice) are required. Example: Testimony for the start of Ramadan falls under category 1; a dispute over a debt under category 2; receipt of a gift under category 3; removal of an agent under category 4.
This application integrates the theoretical rule with practical fiqh cases.

Commentary
This passage is a usul rule from Usul al-Shashi stating the conditions for accepting khabar al-wahid (single-narrator report) as proof. The author divides rights into four categories based on the nature of the right and the degree of obligation. The first category (pure right of Allah, not a punishment) accepts khabar al-wahid unconditionally regarding faith? The second category (pure right of the servant with pure obligation) requires both number (at least two witnesses) and justice. The third category (pure right of the servant without obligation) accepts khabar al-wahid from a just or even a sinful person. The fourth category (mixed obligation) accepts khabar al-wahid conditional on either number or justice. The passage then provides proofs: the hadith of the Bedouin for the first category, Quranic verses requiring two just witnesses for the second, and the practice of the Prophet accepting gifts from both just and sinful informants for the third. The commentary (hashiya) expands on these categories, discussing exceptions and differences of opinion, such as the Karakhi's view that khabar al-wahid is not accepted in penal punishments.

Deriving the 'Illa for Wudu from Sleep
Deriving the 'illa for wudu from a hadith through talil and qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extract the 'illa (effective cause) from a Sunnah text, justify it using Hanafi usul, and apply qiyas to extend the ruling to new cases.

Core Idea
Only sleep that causes relaxation of joints nullifies wudu; the hadith explicitly excludes other positions.

In this lesson, we examine how the Hanafi school derives the cause for wudu nullification from a specific hadith about sleep. The text presents a concrete example of talil (identifying the 'illa) and qiyas (extending the ruling). We will walk through the derivation step by step, and then compare the Hanafi position with that of other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general ruling from the hadith
The hadith states that wudu is not required for certain sleep positions (standing, sitting, bowing, prostrating) but required for sleeping on the side. This identifies the specific nature of the ruling.
2
Extract the 'illa from the text
The hadith adds 'for when he sleeps on his side, his joints relax.' This phrase indicates the cause: relaxation of joints (istirkha' al-mafasil).
The word 'فإنّه' (for indeed) indicates causal linkage.
3
Verify the 'illa using Hanafi usul criteria
The 'illa must be a consistent attribute. Here, relaxation of joints is the only attribute that differentiates the specified positions from the exempted ones. It is suitable for ta'deeya (extension).
Check that the 'illa is not idiosyncratic to the original case.
4
Apply qiyas to similar cases
Any sleep position that involves relaxation of joints—such as sleeping while leaning or reclining—also nullifies wudu, because the 'illa is present.
The ruling extends via qiyas, but the hadith explicitly exempts other positions so no further extension beyond the 'illa.
5
Compare with other schools
Note the differences: Shafi'i and Maliki schools have broader interpretations. The Hanafi position sticks strictly to the explicit text and the derived 'illa.
The dispute is a legitimate ikhtilaf in usul methodology.

Sunnah of Completion: Prolongation vs Repetition in Ghusl
Defines the usul principle of sunniyyat al-takmil via the rule of prolongation vs repetition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi usul principle that completion (takmil) is achieved by prolongation within the place of obligation, and apply it to distinguish the sunnah in ghusl (repetition) from the sunnah in masah (covering).

Core Idea
The sunnah completes the obligation by prolonging the act in its place; repetition substitutes only when prolongation is impossible.

This lesson unpacks a foundational Hanafi usul concept: how sunnah acts complete obligatory ones. Using ghusl and masah as case studies, we see that the principle of 'prolongation within the place of obligation' governs the form of sunnah acts. When the entire place is occupied (as in ghusl), repetition becomes the necessary substitute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its category
Recognize that the passage defines the property of 'sunniyyat al-takmil' – a sunnah that perfects the obligation.
This is a usul definition, not a fiqh ruling.
2
State the usul principle of takmil
The principle: completion (takmil) of an obligatory act is achieved by prolonging (iṭālah) the act within its place of obligation (maḥall).
This is derived from prayer, where standing, bowing, prostration are prolonged.
3
Apply to ghusl
In ghusl, the obligation (washing the whole body) occupies the entire maḥall, so prolongation would be outside the place. Therefore, the sunnah is fulfilled by repetition (takrār).
Repetition is a substitute (khalaf) for the original (aṣl).
4
Apply to masah
For wiping the head (masah), only part of the head is obligatory. Since the maḥall is not fully occupied, prolongation is possible via covering the entire head (isti‘āb).
This shows the rule's flexibility based on the maḥall.
5
Understand the exception
The principle is not absolute: when the maḥall is fully occupied, repetition becomes the default. This is a necessary adaptation (ḍarūrah) to preserve the purpose of completion.
The text uses 'faḥama' (understand) to emphasize careful application.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi with its commentary (Ahsan al-Hawashi) addresses two separate but related usul issues. First, it discusses the nature of sunnah acts in ritual purification: in ghusl, the sunnah is fulfilled by repeating the wash (since the entire body is the place of obligation, prolongation is impossible), while in wiping the head (masah), the sunnah is fulfilled by covering the entire head (isti‘ab). This is because the underlying principle (asl) is that sunnahs complete the obligatory acts, and completion occurs by prolonging the action within the place of obligation, not by repetition. However, in ghusl the obligation covers the whole body, leaving no room for prolongation, so repetition substitutes as the means of completion. Second, it discusses the condition of hand-to-hand exchange (taqabud) in ribawi sales: for foodstuffs, taqabud is required to prevent deferred sale; for currencies (gold and silver), the Hanafi position is that mere specification in the contract is not sufficient—actual physical possession (qabd) is required for the contract to be binding. The text asserts that taqabud is not a condition in currency exchange, rather specification (ta‘yin) is the condition, though currencies are not specified without possession. The argument is grounded in the hadith of riba ('hand to hand') and the principle of blocking the means to usury.

Key Terms
التقابض (al-taqābuḍ): Hand-to-hand exchange, the mutual physical delivery of the two countervalues in a sale contract.
سنية التكميل (sunniyyat al-takmīl): The property of being a sunnah that completes the obligation; such sunnah acts are legislated to perfect the obligatory act.

Hand-to-Hand Exchange in Currency Sales: The Hanafi Doctrine of Specification by Possession
Defines the condition of taqabud in sarf with cross-madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that taqabud is not a condition in sarf, rather specification (ta'yin) is, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
In currency exchange, Hanafis require possession for specification, not immediate hand-to-hand exchange.

This lesson examines a key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on currency exchange (sarf). The Hanafi position distinguishes between foodstuffs (where taqabud is required) and currencies (where specification by possession suffices). The underlying usul principle is that currencies are not specified by mere verbal designation.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
The passage addresses whether hand-to-hand exchange (taqābuḍ) is a condition in currency exchange (sarf). The hadith of riba requires 'hand to hand' for ribawi items.
This is a well-known point of ikhtilaf.
2
Present the Hanafi position
Hanafi: taqabud is not a condition for currencies; rather, their specification (ta'yin) is the condition. Currencies are not specified by verbal designation but only by physical possession (qabḍ).
Hence, the contract is valid but binding only upon possession.
3
Present the Shafi'i position
Shafi'i: The hadith's command of 'hand to hand' applies to all ribawi items, including currencies. Therefore, immediate taqabud is required for the contract to be valid.
Without it, the contract is fasid (invalid).
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Hanafis interpret the hadith as requiring taqabud for the purpose of specification. Since currencies cannot be specified without possession, taqabud is not an independent condition but rather the means of specification.
The text says 'la nusallim' (we do not concede) the opposing view.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Under Hanafi law, a currency exchange contract agreed verbally but without immediate exchange is valid (though binding only upon possession). Under Shafi'i law, such a contract would be invalid from the outset.
This affects daily transactions like currency exchange.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi with its commentary (Ahsan al-Hawashi) addresses two separate but related usul issues. First, it discusses the nature of sunnah acts in ritual purification: in ghusl, the sunnah is fulfilled by repeating the wash (since the entire body is the place of obligation, prolongation is impossible), while in wiping the head (masah), the sunnah is fulfilled by covering the entire head (isti‘ab). This is because the underlying principle (asl) is that sunnahs complete the obligatory acts, and completion occurs by prolonging the action within the place of obligation, not by repetition. However, in ghusl the obligation covers the whole body, leaving no room for prolongation, so repetition substitutes as the means of completion. Second, it discusses the condition of hand-to-hand exchange (taqabud) in ribawi sales: for foodstuffs, taqabud is required to prevent deferred sale; for currencies (gold and silver), the Hanafi position is that mere specification in the contract is not sufficient—actual physical possession (qabd) is required for the contract to be binding. The text asserts that taqabud is not a condition in currency exchange, rather specification (ta‘yin) is the condition, though currencies are not specified without possession. The argument is grounded in the hadith of riba ('hand to hand') and the principle of blocking the means to usury.
