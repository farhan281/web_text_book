Three Types of Haqiqah (Reality) in Usul al-Fiqh
Classifying haqiqah into three types with ruling on metaphor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any literal meaning (haqiqah) as impossible, abandoned, or used and determine whether to resort to metaphor or prioritize the literal meaning.

Core Idea
When literal meaning is impossible or abandoned, metaphor is used; when used, haqiqah is default.

In usul al-fiqh, words have literal (haqiqah) and figurative (majaz) meanings. This lesson breaks down three categories of literal meaning, guiding you in choosing between literal and metaphorical interpretations. Understanding these categories is essential for correctly deriving rulings from the Quran and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word and its literal meaning
Determine the common understanding of the term in the language.
2
Check possibility of the literal meaning
Is the literal meaning impossible in reality? If so, it is muta'adhdhirah (impossible).
Example: 'eating a tree' is impossible.
3
Check usage in custom
If the literal meaning is possible but abandoned in common custom, it is mahjurah (abandoned). If commonly used, it is musta'malah (used).
Example of abandoned: 'placing foot' for entering in oaths. Example of used: 'water' for the liquid.
4
Apply the ruling
For muta'adhdhirah and mahjurah, one resorts to metaphor (majaz) by consensus. For musta'malah, haqiqah is preferred over majaz.
The rule is applied unless the speaker intended the literal meaning.

Commentary
This passage defines the three categories of haqiqah (literal meaning) in usul al-fiqh. 1) Impossible (muta'adhdhirah): The literal meaning cannot be applied at all, e.g., 'eating a tree'—trees are not eaten literally. 2) Abandoned (mahjurah): The literal meaning is not used in common custom, though theoretically possible, e.g., in oaths 'placing the foot' for entering. 3) Used (musta'malah): The literal meaning is commonly used, e.g., 'water' for the liquid. In the first two types, scholars unanimously resort to the metaphorical meaning if no intention of reality is present. A fourth category, 'left' (matrukah), is sometimes added: the literal meaning is possible but rare, and here there is disagreement: Abu Hanifa prefers the reality, while the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) prefer the metaphor if the latter is well-known. The passage notes that the ruling applies 'by the two customs' i.e., based on common and specific custom.

Key Terms
متعذرة (muta'adhdhirah): A literal meaning that is impossible to apply (e.g., 'eating a tree').
مهجورة (mahjurah): A literal meaning that is abandoned in custom, though possible (e.g., 'placing foot' for entry).
مستعملة (musta'malah): A literal meaning that is commonly used (e.g., 'water' for the liquid).
متروكة (matrukah): A literal meaning that is possible but rarely intended (a subtype of the above).

The Four Levels of Textual Clarity: Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, Muhkam
Defines and distinguishes four categories of textual clarity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four Hanafi categories of textual clarity (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) and apply the criteria of tawil, takhsis, and naskh to classify a given text.

Core Idea
Clarity in sacred texts varies: zahir is apparent, nass is framed, mufassar is unequivocal, muhkam is permanent.

In Hanafi usul, not all clear texts are equally binding. The jurists have developed a hierarchy of clarity: from zahir (apparent) to nass (framed), mufassar (unequivocal), and muhkam (unchangeable). This lesson unpacks these four categories using the foundational definitions from Usul al-Shashi and their application to a Quranic example on sale and usury. Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for correct derivation of rulings (istinbat).

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining criteria for each category
From the definitions, note what each category allows or excludes: zahir - no reflection needed, allows tawil and takhsis; nass - framed for a meaning, still allows tawil and takhsis; mufassar - no tawil or takhsis, allows naskh; muhkam - no tawil, takhsis, or naskh.
These criteria are the key to classification.
2
Apply the criteria to the Quranic example (2:275)
The verse 'And Allah has permitted sale and forbidden usury' is used to illustrate nass: it is framed to distinguish sale from usury, refuting the disbelievers' claim. Recognize why it is called nass (framed for that purpose) and zahir (the permissibility of sale is apparent without reflection).
The verse serves as a single example that can be classified in multiple ways depending on the aspect considered.
3
Analyze the early vs. later Hanafi views on the relationship between categories
According to the early scholars, the categories overlap in existence but differ in concept (e.g., a single text can be both zahir and nass depending on the aspect). The later scholars made them mutually exclusive: a text is either zahir (not framed) or nass (framed), etc. Understand which view became the operative one for fatwa.
The footnotes in the passage explain this shift; the later view is now standard.
4
Test your understanding with a new text
Given a verse like 'And eat and drink until the white thread becomes distinct to you from the black thread of dawn' (2:187), classify it: 'white thread' is zahir for dawn, but the context frames it as nass for the intended meaning. Determine if it could be mufassar (no) or muhkam (depends).
This exercise reinforces the criteria.

Zahir is understood by mere hearing; nass is the specific purpose of the speech.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces the Hanafi classification of legal texts based on clarity. Core Idea
Al-nass is explicit and unambiguous; al-zahir is apparent but open to interpretation — yet both require action.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, not all clear texts are equal. Some are explicit (nass), leaving no room for alternative meaning, while others are apparent (zahir) but admit the possibility of a secondary interpretation. Both, however, are binding in practice. This lesson unpacks the distinction through Quranic verses and a hadith, showing how each category functions in legal derivation.

Guided Path

2
Classify the text as nass or zahir
Determine whether the text is explicit and unambiguous in its ruling (nass) or clear but potentially open to a metaphorical interpretation (zahir). Identify which part is which.
A text can have both nass and zahir aspects for different elements.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule of wujub al-'amal
Recall that the passage states action upon both nass and zahir is obligatory (wujub al-'amal) even if they admit the possibility of another meaning. This is a core rule in Hanafi usul.
This rule is analogous to the relationship between haqiqah and majaz.
4
Derive the legal result
State the specific ruling that follows from the text. For the hadith, the nass part gives the emancipation ruling; the zahir part gives the ownership ruling.
Both rulings are binding unless there is a specific evidence to the contrary.

Zahir and Nass: Definitions and Precedence in Hanafi Usul
Defining zahir and nass and establishing that nass overrides zahir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a text as zahir or nass, apply the Hanafi rule that nass overrides zahir, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether zahir yields certainty or probability.

Core Idea
Nass overrides zahir because nass is the intended purpose of speech and admits no alternative interpretation.

In Hanafi usul, not all clear texts are equal. The categories of zahir (manifest) and nass (definitive) form a hierarchy that determines how a mufti resolves apparent conflicts. This lesson unpacks the definitions, the critical rule that nass takes precedence, and a classical debate over whether zahir from the Quran and mutawatir hadith yields certainty or mere probability.

Guided Path
1
Define zahir and nass from the source
Read the passage to extract the precise meaning of each term. Zahir is a word that indicates its meaning but allows the possibility of another interpretation (like metaphor or specification). Nass is a word that is unequivocally placed for its meaning and admits no such possibility.
The source uses the example: 'Whoever owns a mahram relative becomes free' – this is nass for freedom (no alternative), but zahir for ownership.
2
Identify the key distinction: possibility of alternative interpretation
The presence or absence of ihtimal (possibility) is the decisive factor. For nass, the possibility is absent; for zahir, it exists but is considered remote by some scholars.
This distinction is the basis for the hierarchy.
3
Understand the precedence rule: nass overrides zahir
When a zahir text and a nass text conflict, the nass rules because it is the intended purpose of the speech (maqsud al-kalam) and carries no probability of other meanings.
The passage applies this to the divorce example: a wife's statement 'I have made myself irrevocably divorced' is nass for divorce but zahir for irrevocability, so the divorce is revocable.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on the epistemic status of zahir
Some Hanafis (e.g., al-Maturidi) hold that even zahir from Quran and mutawatir only produces probability (zann). Others (the Iraqi school) hold it produces certainty (qat'). This stems from whether the possibility of metaphor or specification is considered weighty.
The passage notes that this difference affects whether a ruling from a zahir text is considered absolutely binding or only probable.
5

Review the hadith of 'Uraynah: the command 'Drink from their urine' is zahir for permissibility, but the command 'Avoid urine' is nass for prohibition. The nass prevails. This illustrates the practical operation of the hierarchy.
The same method applies to any conflict between a text that admits interpretation (zahir) and one that does not (nass).

This passage from al-Shashi's Usul defines two key terms: al-zahir (the manifest) and al-nass (the definitive). Al-zahir is a word that indicates its meaning primarily but admits the possibility of another interpretation (like metaphor). Al-nass is a word that is unequivocally placed for its meaning and does not admit such possibility. The author provides examples: the hadith 'Whoever owns a mahram relative becomes free' is nass for the entitlement to freedom, but only zahir for the establishment of ownership. It is for this reason that Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) said: 'The Prophet (peace be upon him) left this world without explaining to us the categories of riba.' So by his saying: 'it is not subject to interpretation (ta'wil) and specification (takhsis)' excludes the second kind, for it is not mufassar because it was not reached by a decisive explanation, and for this reason there is disagreement among great scholars concerning it. (1) His saying: ['then the angels prostrated']—for the saying of Allah Most High: 'So the angels prostrated, all of them together' (Al-Hijr 15:30). The word 'angels' is zahir (apparent) in generality, but the possibility of specification exists. The door of specification is closed by His saying 'all of them', then the possibility of differentiation in prostration remained, so the door of interpretation (ta'wil) is closed by His saying 'together', meaning they prostrated not at different times. Thus it becomes mufassar. (2) His saying: ['all of them']—because the word 'all' denotes encompassment and inclusion, thus the generality of the plural is emphasized and the possibility of specification is cut off by it. If it is said: The word 'all' is among the expressions of 'amm, and every 'amm is subject to specification, so how is the door of specification closed by it while the possibility of a specifying factor remains in itself? The answer: When the word 'all' is used after a general noun, the possibility of specification is cut off, otherwise it would contradict its intended purpose, which is to emphasize inclusion and strengthen the ruling. Just as when you say 'Zayd came', it can be metaphorical, but if you say 'Zayd himself came', metaphor is prevented. This is from al-Ma'din. (3) His saying: ['if he said... etc.']—if it is said: The mufassar is subject to abrogation (naskh), but this issue is not subject to it because it is known that abrogation does not apply in the speech of people, so it would be muhkam (unequivocal). Commentary
This passage defines 'المفسّر' (mufassar) as a type of clear text that has been decisively explained by the speaker, leaving no room for ta'wil (interpretive preference) or takhsis (specification). The commentary distinguishes between decisive explanation (like the obligation of prayer and zakat, which are clearly defined in quantity and manner) and non-decisive explanation (like the prohibition of riba, which Umar said was not fully explained by the Prophet). Only the former qualifies as mufassar. The prime example from the Quran is verse 15:30: 'فَسَجَدَ الْمَلَائِكَةُ كُلُّهُمْ أَجْمَعُونَ'. The word 'ملائكة' is general (amm) and could be specified (some angels), but 'كلهم' closes the door of specification. Then the possibility that they prostrated at different times remains, but 'أجمعون' closes that door too, making the verse mufassar—it definitively means all angels prostrated together. The commentary also addresses an objection about abrogation: if mufassar is so clear, can it be abrogated? It discusses that in human speech, mufassar is not subject to naskh, but it can be considered mufassar from one aspect and not from another. Finally, a fiqh example: 'تزوجت فلانة شهراً بكذا' – the word 'تزوجت' is zahir in marriage, but the mention of a month may imply a temporary marriage; however, since the explanation is not decisive, it may not be mufassar.

Key Terms
المفسّر (al-mufassar): A text whose intended meaning is made clear by a decisive explanation from the speaker, leaving no room for interpretation or specification.
تأويل (ta'wil): Interpretation that gives a meaning other than the apparent one based on a valid indication.
تخصيص (takhsis): Specificating a general term by excluding some of its individuals.

Mufassar and Muhkam: Precedence and Finality in Hanafi Textual Hierarchy
Defining mufassar and muhkam with examples from marriage and debt acknowledgment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mufassar and muhkam, apply the rule of precedence over nass, and identify when a text is mufassar or muhkam based on the removal of interpretive doubt.

Core Idea
Mufassar removes doubt, muhkam removes doubt and abrogation—both are above nass but muhkam is final.

In Hanafi usul, texts are ranked by clarity. You have learned about zahir and nass. Now we climb higher: mufassar, which clarifies an ambiguous nass, and muhkam, which is so strong it cannot be overridden. This lesson unpacks both using the classic examples of marriage (nikah) and acknowledgment (iqrar).

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the base text and its level
Locate the initial statement that carries a ruling. Determine if it is nass (explicit) or zahir (apparent). In the examples, 'I married' is nass for marriage but ambiguous for duration; 'I owe one thousand' is nass for liability.
The base text has a possible interpretative scope.
2
2. Identify the clarifying addition
Look for the qualifying phrase that removes the ambiguity: 'for a month' (in marriage) or 'from the price of this slave' (in debt). This addition is the bayan (clarification).
The addition must be explicit and specific.
3
3. Determine if the addition removes one possible interpretation
Assess whether the addition eliminates a plausible alternative meaning. If yes, the combined text becomes mufassar (explained). In the debt example, 'from the price of this slave' removes the possibility that the debt is unconditional or for another cause.
Mufassar is stronger than nass because interpretive doubt is removed.
4
4. Check if the resulting text is beyond interpretation and abrogation
Determine if the mufassar text is also immune to further specification or abrogation. If it cannot be overridden, it is muhkam (established). True muhkam is rare in human rulings; examples from the Quran (e.g., God's knowledge) are muhkam.
Muhkam is the highest level; mufassar may still be abrogated or further specified.
5
5. Apply the precedence rule
When a text is mufassar, it takes precedence over the base nass. For example, the debt is due only upon receipt of the slave, not immediately. Muhkam takes precedence over mufassar. Both require acting upon them.
The practical ruling changes based on the classification.

The Four Opposites of Textual Categories
Explaining the four opposing categories to zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four opposite categories to the clear textual categories (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) and explain the concept of taqabul in Hanafi usul as a gradational opposition.

Core Idea
Each clear text has a corresponding unclear opposite: clarity and obscurity are opposites in the Hanafi hierarchy.

This passage reveals that the four well-known clear textual categories—zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam—each have an opposite category among the unclear texts. The concept of opposition (taqabul) here is not logical contradiction but a gradational spectrum of clarity. Understanding this duality helps jurists determine which texts take precedence and how certain their indications are.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four clear categories
Recall the four categories of clear texts: zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam. These are the first set of categories in the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity.
They are ordered from least to most clear.
2
Understand the meaning of taqabul in this context
The passage says the four opposites 'oppose' the clear ones regarding clarity. The hashiah explains that opposition (taqabul) can be of four types; here it is opposition of contradictories—clarity vs. obscurity.
It is not contraries (two positive attributes) but contradictories (one is the absence of the other).
3
Identify the four opposite categories
The four unclear categories that correspond to the clear ones are: khafi (hidden) opposite to zahir, mushkil (difficult) opposite to nass, mujmal (ambiguous) opposite to mufassar, and mutashabih (unclear) opposite to muhkam.
They are less certain in their indication.
4
Apply the framework to classify a text
When you encounter a text, first determine its level of clarity. Then identify its opposite to know which other category it might become if further explanation is given.
Example: a mujmal text's opposite is mufassar; once explained, it becomes mufassar.

Commentary
This brief but crucial passage introduces the concept that the four categories of clear texts—zahir, nass, mufassar, and muhkam—each have an opposite category among the unclear texts: khafi (hidden), mushkil (difficult), mujmal (ambiguous), and mutashabih (unclear). The 'opposition' here is not logical but gradational: clarity vs. obscurity. The hashiah by the commentator (likely from al-Ma'din) elaborates that the term 'opposition' (taqabul) has four technical meanings in logic: (1) contradictories (like existence and non-existence), (2) contraries (two positive attributes that cannot coexist, like black and white), (3) correlatives (like father and son), and (4) privation and possession (like ability and inability). However, here the intended sense is the first: the clear categories and their vague counterparts are like contradictories in that one excludes the other. The Hanafi usul tradition uses this framework to classify texts by their degree of clarity and the certainty of their indication (dalala). The practical effect is that rulings derived from clearer categories take precedence and are more binding.

The Four Opposite Textual Categories in Hanafi Usul
Defines the four opposite textual categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the four opposite textual categories (al-khafi, al-mushkil, al-mujmal, al-mutashabih) and distinguish them from their clear counterparts based on the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity.

Core Idea
Hidden texts vary: al-khafi by accident, al-mushkil by wording, al-mujmal needs revelation, al-mutashabih is unknowable.

The Hanafi school classifies texts along a spectrum of clarity and ambiguity. After establishing the four clear levels (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam), this passage introduces their opposites: texts whose meanings are hidden to varying degrees. Understanding these categories is essential for identifying when a text can be applied directly and when it requires interpretation, analogy, or external clarification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four pairs of opposites
The passage states that each of the four clear categories has a corresponding opposite: zahir ↔ khafi, nass ↔ mushkil, mufassar ↔ mujmal, muhkam ↔ mutashabih.
These opposites are equal in rank: the hiddenness of the latter mirrors the clarity of the former.
2
Define al-khafi
Al-khafi is hidden due to an accidental cause, not the wording itself. For example, the Qur'anic command regarding theft (sariqa) is clear in wording, but its application to pickpockets (tarar) and grave-robbers (nabbash) is hidden because they are known by different names.
The hiddenness is resolved through ijtihad or qiyas.
3
Define al-mushkil
Al-mushkil is hidden due to the wording itself, requiring reflection and analogical reasoning. The passage uses the analogy of a man who left his homeland and becomes mixed with lookalikes.
Mushkil has two degrees: hiddenness in itself and then confusion with similars.
4
Define al-mujmal
Al-mujmal is so ambiguous that it cannot be understood through reflection alone; it requires a clarification (bayan) from the Lawgiver.
Example: the Qur'anic command 'establish prayer' (aqimu al-salah) without details on how.
5
Define al-mutashabih
Al-mutashabih is the highest level of hiddenness; it cannot be clarified at all by any means. Its true meaning is known only to Allah.
Example: verses about the attributes of Allah that must be accepted as is (tafwid).

Al-Khafi: The Hidden by External Factor
Defines and exemplifies al-khafi with the thief/pickpocket/grave robber case and the resulting ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a text as khafi, explain why its hiddenness stems from an external factor, and apply the Hanafi ruling distinguishing the pickpocket from the grave robber.

Core Idea
A text can be clear in wording but hidden in application when an external factor masks its intended scope.

In Hanafi usul, not all hiddenness is alike. Al-Khafi is a text whose wording is clear but whose application to certain cases is obscured by an external factor—like a change in the agent's name or a shift in context. This lesson unpacks the definition using the famous thief/pickpocket/grave robber example, showing how the Quranic verse on theft is zahir for the ordinary thief but khafi for the pickpocket and grave robber. It also introduces the key principle that penalties are repelled by doubts.

Guided Path
1
Define al-Khafi
Understand that al-Khafi is a text whose intended meaning is hidden due to an external factor (e.g., the subject being designated by a different name), not from ambiguity in the wording itself.
The wording remains zahir; hiddenness arises from an external cause.
2
Analyze the Quranic example
Examine the verse on theft (al-Ma'idah 5:38). It is apparent for the ordinary thief (sariq) but hidden for the pickpocket (tarrar) and grave robber (nabbash) because they are known by other names, indicating an increase or decrease in the meaning of theft.
The tarrar takes while victim is awake; the nabbash takes from a dead person.
3

For the tarrar, the increase in meaning (complete theft) warrants the hadd penalty. For the nabbash, the deficiency introduces doubt (shubha), so the penalty is dropped based on the maxim 'al-hudood tudra'u bil-shubuhat'.
This is the Hanafi position; Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i disagree.
4
Evaluate the dispute
Review the opposing proof: the hadith 'Whoever robs a grave, we cut off his hand.' The Hanafi response interprets it as a political punishment or discretionary penalty, not the fixed hadd.
The word 'qata'na' in the hadith may mean discretionary punishment.
5
Conclude with tarjih
The Hanafi view is relied upon because of the strength of the doubt-based evidence and the principle of repelling penalties.
The mufta bihi is the Hanafi position: no cutting for the nabbash.

Commentary
This passage from 'Usul al-Shashi' with its gloss 'Ahsan al-Hawashi' discusses the classification of texts according to clarity, known as 'al-Mutaqabilat' (the opposites). The matn (main text) defines four pairs: Zahir (apparent) vs Khafi (hidden); Nass (explicit) vs Mushkil (problematic); Mufassar (explained) vs Mujmal (ambiguous); Muhkam (firm) vs Mutashabih (unclear). The focus here is on the first two. Al-Khafi is a text whose meaning is inherently clear from its wording but becomes hidden due to an external factor, such as a term being replaced by a specific name. The example given is the Quranic verse on cutting the hand of the thief. It is clear for the ordinary thief (sariq) but hidden for the pickpocket (tarar) and grave robber (nabbash) because they are known by different names. The Hanafi ruling distinguishes between them: the pickpocket, who performs a more complete form of theft while the victim is awake, incurs the hadd penalty; the grave robber, who steals from a dead person, does not because of doubt (shubha). This is a key Hanafi position, and the text includes a dispute with Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i, who argue for the penalty in all cases based on a hadith. The Hanafi response interprets that hadith as administrative punishment, not the fixed penalty. The second type, al-Mushkil, is more ambiguous: the meaning is unclear even after inquiry, requiring deeper reflection. The example is a vow not to eat 'idam' (a condiment eaten with bread). The word is clear for vinegar and molasses but problematic for meat, eggs, and cheese. The text discusses an ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad regarding whether these items fall under 'idam'. The gloss (hashiya) clarifies definitions and expands on the reasoning, emphasizing that penalties are repelled by doubts and that such disputes stem from different interpretations of textual and linguistic evidence. Al-Mushkil: The Problematic Text Requiring Reflection
Defines al-mushkil and exemplifies it with the idam case and the resulting Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mushkil, distinguish it from al-khafi, and apply the Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding the term 'idam' to resolve ambiguity through reflection (ta'ammul).

Core Idea
When a word's meaning is lost among similars, only careful reflection can reveal the intended ruling.

Al-Mushkil is a text whose meaning is even more hidden than al-khafi: after initial inquiry, the word still has multiple semblances, requiring deeper reflection to distinguish the correct application. The classic example in this passage is the vow not to eat 'idam' (a condiment). The word is clear for vinegar and molasses but problematic for meat, eggs, and cheese. This lesson walks through the Hanafi dispute on whether these items fall under 'idam', demonstrating how mushkil is resolved by reflecting on the underlying meaning (ma'na) of the term.

Guided Path
1
Define al-Mushkil
A text whose meaning is hidden to such a degree that after initial inquiry, it becomes entangled with similar concepts, so reflection (ta'ammul) is needed to distinguish the intended meaning from its similars.
Mushkil is more severe than khafi; khafi requires inquiry (talab), mushkil requires inquiry then reflection.
2
Compare with al-Khafi
Al-Khafi is hidden by an external factor but can be resolved by seeking additional information. Al-Mushkil is hidden because the word itself has multiple possible referents that look alike.
The example: the word 'sariq' is khafi for the nabbash because of an external designation; but 'idam' is mushkil because its meaning overlaps with many food items.
3
Analyze the 'idam' example
If someone swears not to eat 'idam', the term is clear for vinegar and molasses (the typical condiments). For meat, eggs, and cheese, it is mushkil because they are not typically dipped into bread.
The key is whether these items share the essential meaning of 'idam' as something that accompanies bread (muwadamah).
4
Trace the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Abu Hanifa and the apparent view of Abu Yusuf hold that meat, eggs, and cheese do not fall under 'idam' without intention. Muhammad (and another narration from Abu Yusuf) hold that they do, because they accompany bread.
The dispute turns on whether 'idam' requires mixing/dipping or just accompaniment.
5
Apply the resolution method
To resolve mushkil, one must investigate the essential meaning (ma'na) through reflection. The Hanafi view relies on the common usage and the concept of 'idam' as something that is dipped or mixed, not just eaten alongside.
The hadith 'The best idam of Paradise is meat' is considered weak or metaphorical by the majority.

Al-Mushkil: Definition and Application to the Iddam Dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mushkil, distinguish it from khafi, and apply its resolution process to a fiqh dispute involving an oath.

Core Idea
Mushkil is a text whose meaning is hidden by similarity to other cases, requiring both seeking and deep reflection to resolve.

In Hanafi usul, clarity levels form a hierarchy. Mushkil sits one level below khafi: it is not merely hidden, but its intended meaning is obscured by resemblance to other similar instances. This lesson unpacks the definition, contrasts it with khafi, and examines a classic dispute where the mushkil status of 'iddam' (condiment) determines whether an oath is broken.

2

The oath not to eat iddam is zaahir for khall (vinegar) and dibs (date syrup) because they are typical dips. Understand why they are unambiguous: they fit the common usage of iddam as a subsidiary eaten with bread.

3

These items can be eaten with bread but are not normally dipped. The word 'iddam' is mushkil here: it could include them or not. One must seek the intended meaning and reflect.
The concealment arises because these items partly share the meaning of iddam but lack the typical dipping characteristic.
4

Abu Hanifa (and apparent view of Abu Yusuf) excludes them because bread is not dipped into them; they lack the essential subsidiary quality. Muhammad (and one narration from Abu Yusuf) includes them based on derivation from 'muwadamah' (compatibility) and a hadith about meat in Paradise.

5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
If someone swears not to eat iddam, then eats meat without any dip, is the oath broken? According to Abu Hanifa: no. According to Muhammad: yes. This shows the real-world impact of the usul classification.
The tarjih is not stated definitively, but Abu Hanifa's view is the apparent one from Abu Yusuf's school.

The Hanafi View on the Rasikhun and the Interpretation of Mutashabih
Discusses the ikhtilaf on the interpretation of mutashabih, focusing on Quran 3:7.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether the rasikhun know the interpretation of mutashabih, evaluate the evidence from Quran 3:7, and compare the opposing Shafi'i/Mu'tazila argument.

Core Idea
The rasikhun do not know the certain interpretation; they submit, saying 'we believe in it'.

This passage addresses a famous ikhtilaf: whether the 'rasikhun' (those firmly grounded in knowledge) know the interpretation of mutashabih verses. The Hanafi school holds that only Allah knows the true interpretation, while the rasikhun submit without full understanding. This lesson unpacks the Quranic evidence (3:7) and the linguistic dispute behind the difference.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position and its Quranic basis
The Hanafi position is that only Allah knows the interpretation of mutashabih. The key verse is 3:7: 'وما يعلم تأويله إلا الله'. In Hanafi reading, one stops at 'إلا الله', making the following phrase 'والراسخون في العلم' a new sentence praising their submission.
This stopping is known as 'waqf'.
2
Identify the opposing view
The Shafi'i and Mu'tazila read 'والراسخون' as conjoined to 'الله', so the meaning is 'only Allah and the rasikhun know its interpretation'. They see the rasikhun as capable of understanding the intended meaning speculatively.
This reading does not require a stop at 'إلا الله'.
3
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafi response is that this is a verbal dispute. Those who say the rasikhun know mean speculative knowledge (zanni), not certain knowledge (qat'i). The Hanafi position refers to certain knowledge. The commentary cites Ibn Sam'ani that the majority of the Salaf, including Ibn Abbas, held the stopping view.
The dispute reduces to what is meant by 'ilm'.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafis avoid interpreting verses of divine attributes (mutashabih) literally and refrain from ta'wil, while Shafi'is may allow speculative interpretation to protect against anthropomorphism. Both schools agree that the apparent literal meaning is not intended for Allah.
The difference is in methodology, not in core belief.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The passage sides with the Hanafi view, citing the majority of the Salaf and the soundest narration from Ibn Abbas. The commentator concludes that the dispute is verbal, but the Hanafi position (stopping) is preferred for its caution.
The preferred view is to follow the overwhelming majority.

Commentary
This passage defines two key categories of unclear texts in Hanafi usul: mujmal (summary) and mutashabih (ambiguous). Mujmal is a text that has multiple possible meanings and cannot be understood without an external explanation from the speaker. The example given is the Quranic verse 'Haram al-riba' (2:279), where the word 'riba' literally means increase, but the intended meaning is a specific increase (without exchange in transactions of same-type measured goods). This cannot be deduced by mere reflection. Mutashabih is even more obscure; its intended meaning cannot be known in this world by the Muslim community. The example is the disconnected letters (e.g., 'Alif Lam Mim'). The passage distinguishes between two types of mutashabih: those whose meaning is unknown entirely (like the letters) and those whose linguistic meaning is known but the intended meaning of Allah contradicts the apparent sense (like 'Hand of Allah'), requiring belief without modality. The ruling for both is to affirm that what Allah intends is true, awaiting explanation. The commentary then addresses a classical ikhtilaf: whether the 'rasikhun' (those firmly grounded in knowledge) know the interpretation of mutashabih. The Hanafi position (and the author's) is that they do not—only Allah knows—based on the stopping at 'illa Allah' in 3:7. The Shafi'i view (and Mu'tazila) is that they do know. The commentator resolves this as a verbal dispute: those who say they know mean a speculative understanding, while those who deny mean certain knowledge. The passage sides with the Hanafi view, citing Ibn Sam'ani and the majority of the Salaf.

الراسخون (al-rasikhun): Those firmly grounded in knowledge; the scholars who submit to Allah's revelation
التأويل (al-ta'wil): Interpretation; in this context, the true meaning of mutashabih verses that only Allah knows with certainty.

Definition of Muqtada (Implication of a Text)
Defining and explaining the concept of 'muqtada' in usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the concept of muqtada, distinguish it from muqaddar and mahdhuf, and apply it to derive implied elements in Quranic verses and legal statements.

Core Idea
Muqtada is an essential addition to a text, without which the text's own meaning is unsound.

In Hanafi usul, not every meaning is directly uttered. Sometimes the text implicitly requires an additional element for its own coherence. This lesson unpacks the concept of 'muqtada' – the necessary implied addition – and shows how it differs from mere deletion or implicit inclusion. You will learn to recognize muqtada in legal texts and avoid confusion with related concepts.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the definition of muqtada
Identify what muqtada is: an addition to the text without which the text's own meaning cannot be realized. The text implicitly demands it for its own validity.
The phrase 'liyasiḥha fī nafsihi ma'nāhu' (for its own meaning to be sound) is key.
2
Distinguish three related terms: muqaddar, mahdhuf, muqtada
Muqaddar corrects speech linguistically, legally, or rationally; mahdhuf is purely linguistic deletion; muqtada is required by reason and law. Understand their scopes as per the commentary.
Many usulis consider mahdhuf a type of muqtada.
3
Analyze the talaq example
In 'You are divorced' (أَنْتِ طَالِق), the word 'tāliq' is an attribute (na't). An attribute requires a source (masdar) – the act of divorce (talāq) – for its meaning to be complete. Thus, talāq is muqtada.
This shows how a legal statement implies an underlying action.
4
Apply to Quranic example: 'freeing a slave'
The verse 'fataḥrīru raqabatin' (the freeing of a slave) requires an additional element: the slave must be owned, because freeing is impossible without ownership. Here, 'owned' (mamlūka) is muqtada.
The hadith 'There is no emancipation in what one does not own' supports the necessity of ownership.
5
Differentiate muqtada from connotation (dalāla)
Muqtada is necessary for the text's own meaning; connotation (dalāla) is separate – the text can be sound without its connotation. Example: 'freeing a slave' is valid even without explicitly stating the slave is owned, but the meaning of 'freeing' implies ownership as muqtada.
Understanding this distinction avoids mixing necessary implications with mere optional connotations.

Distinction Between Muqtada, Mahdhuf, and Muqaddar
Explaining the types of implicit meanings in text interpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between muqtada, mahdhuf, and muqaddar in usul al-fiqh and identify which linguistic rulings apply to each.

Core Idea
Muqtada is a logical necessity for valid speech; mahdhuf/muqaddar is a linguistic omission treated as spoken.

In Hanafi usul, not every implicit meaning in a text carries the same weight. The muqtada is a logically necessary meaning that the text depends on for validity, but it is not a word and may not be intended by the speaker. In contrast, mahdhuf and muqaddar refer to words omitted from syntax but estimated by context, and they behave like spoken words in legal analysis. This distinction is crucial for correctly deriving rulings from Quran, hadith, and juristic texts.

Guided Path
1
Classify the implicit element
Given a textual phrase, determine whether an implicit element is a logical necessity for the validity of the statement (muqtada) or a word that has been omitted from the syntactic structure (mahdhuf/muqaddar).
Muqtada is not a word; it is a meaning that may not even be in the speaker's mind.
2
Analyze the type of indication
Identify whether the indication of the text to the implicit element is a logical implication (dalala iltizamiyya) or a contextual estimation (qarina). Muqtada is indicated by logical necessity; mahdhuf is indicated by a contextual clue.
Sadr al-Shari'ah: muqtada is a necessary prior to the necessitated.
3
Apply linguistic rules
Once you identify the implicit element as mahdhuf/muqaddar, all linguistic rules (taqyid, itlaq, umum, khusus, etc.) apply to it as if it were spoken. For muqtada, these rules do not apply because it is not a word.
Example: In 'Sell it for me,' the muqtada includes the existence of a buyer, but that buyer is not linguistically restricted.
4
Test with an example
Take a sample text: 'The thief, male or female, cut off their hands' (Quran 5:38). The word 'hand' is a mahdhuf? Actually, the verse has 'hand' but consider the muqtada: the existence of a theft that meets the nisab. The muqtada is not linguistically expressed but logically necessary.
This exercises the distinction in a real fiqh context.
5
Conclude the distinction
Summarize: muqtada is a logical necessity (dalala iltizamiyya a'amm), not a mental estimation; mahdhuf is a linguistic estimation treated as if spoken. This distinction affects how far we can extend rulings from the text.
The Hanafi school relies on this to limit excessive interpretation.

Unlike the muqtada, for it is a consequential indication, a general logical necessity, not a mental [linguistic] one.
Source pages p. 90

Commentary
This passage is a usul definition clarifying the distinction between three types of implicit meanings in texts: the muqtada (required meaning), mahdhuf (deleted), and muqaddar (estimated). Sadr al-Shari'ah's view is explained. Muqtada is a logical necessity for the validity of the statement, e.g., in 'Sell it for me' the muqtada is the existence of a buyer and seller. It is not a word but a meaning, and the speaker may not even intend it explicitly. In contrast, mahdhuf is a word that is omitted from the sentence but whose meaning is still indicated and estimated by context; it is treated as if it were spoken, and all linguistic rules apply to it. Muqtada is different because it is a logical implication (dalala iltizamiyya) that is general (a'amm) and not a mental estimation.

Key Terms
مقتضى (muqtada): A meaning necessarily implied by the text for its validity, but not linguistically estimated.
محذوف (mahdhuf): A word omitted from the syntactic structure but estimated (muqaddar) based on context.
مقدر (muqaddar): The estimated word that fills the syntactic gap; it is like the spoken word.

When Does the Owner's Right Cease in Usurped Property?
Matn and hashya on cessation of owner's right in usurped property.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three factors that cut off the owner's right in a usurped property according to Zahir al-Riwayah and apply them to new scenarios.

Core Idea
The owner's right in a usurped property remains unless the change removes its name and primary benefit or makes separation impossible.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi tackles a key issue in the law of usurpation (ghasp): when does the rightful owner lose the claim to his property after it has been altered? The matn gives clear examples, and the hashya extracts the underlying legal criteria. By parsing the text, we uncover three precise factors that determine whether the owner's right is cut off or remains intact.

Guided Path
1
Identify the examples
List the three examples from the matn: silver struck into dirhams, silver nuggets made into dinars, and a sheep slaughtered. Note the common feature: in each case, the property undergoes a change but retains its original name (e.g., 'silver' remains silver).
2
Extract the condition from the matn
The matn states the ruling applies 'because the property has not changed such that its name disappears.' This is the operative condition: the right continues as long as the original name and primary benefit remain.
The hashya later specifies what 'disappearance of name' entails.
3
Parse the hashya's three factors
The hashya lists exactly three factors that cut off the owner's right: (1) change that removes the name and greatest benefits, (2) inseparable mixing with the usurper's property, (3) mixing that makes separation difficult. Note that these are exhaustive; no other factors suffice in Zahir al-Riwayah.
Memorize these three as they appear in legal texts.
4
Apply the factors to the matn examples
Check each example against the three factors: in all three, the name (silver, sheep) remains, the greatest benefits (value, meat) persist, and there is no mixing. Therefore, the owner's right is not cut. This confirms the rule.
5
Generalize the ruling
Formulate the usul principle: the owner's right in a usurped property is presumptively continuous and is only severed by one of the three specific causes. This is a clear application of the Hanafi rule 'certainty is not removed by doubt'.
This principle governs many furu' cases in ghasp.

The Particle 'حتى' as a Limit in Oaths
Analysis of 'حتى' as a ghaya in oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the condition for interpreting 'حتى' as a ghaya and derive the ruling (breaking of oath) from a given example.

Core Idea
In oaths, 'حتى' operates as a limit only if the action is extendable and the endpoint is plausible.

The particle 'حتى' (until) often appears in oath formulations to set a limit. Understanding when it functions as a genuine limit (ghaya) is crucial for determining whether an oath has been broken. This lesson examines the classical Hanafi analysis from Al-Shashi, focusing on the conditions that make 'حتى' operative as a limit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the particle and its possible meanings
Recognize that 'حتى' can mean 'until' (limit), 'so that' (causative), or 'and' (conjunction). The default is the limit meaning.
The passage states 'الأصل هو الأول' (the original is the first).
2
Check if the preceding action is extendable
Determine whether the verb in the oath can be repeated or continued over time. For example, beating can be extended; a single action like 'I will not enter' cannot.
The example uses 'ضرب' (beating) which is extendable.
3
Check if the following event is a suitable endpoint
The event after 'حتى' must naturally cause cessation of the action. Intercession, shouting, or nightfall are plausible reasons to stop beating.
The text says 'تصلح غاية للضرب' (are suitable as a limit for beating).
4
Apply the condition to the example
Take the oath: 'My slave is free if I do not beat you until so-and-so intercedes.' Since beating can be extended and intercession is a plausible stop, 'حتى' operates as a limit.
5
State the ruling
If the person stops beating before intercession occurs, he breaks his oath (حنث). He must continue beating until the limit or break the oath.
Breaking the oath incurs kaffarah (expiation).
