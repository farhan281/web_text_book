The Hanafi Interpretation of حتى in Oaths
Explains the Hanafi interpretation of حتى in conditional oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that حتى in oaths is interpreted as غاية, جزاء, or عطف محض based on context, and determine the legal outcome in oath scenarios.

Core Idea
حتى in oaths is not always 'until'; it takes the meaning of purpose when the clause is a suitable result.

The particle حتى in Arabic carries multiple meanings, and its interpretation in oaths can change the legal outcome dramatically. In Hanafi usul, حتى is treated contextually: if the following clause can logically serve as a result of the first, it is read as a purpose (لام كي); if not, it may become a pure conjunction. This lesson unpacks the rule through two canonical examples from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the particle and its literal meaning
Recognize حتى in the oath and note that its default meaning is 'until' (غاية), indicating an endpoint.
2
Apply the Hanafi principle of contextual reinterpretation
If the clause after حتى is suitable as a result (جزاء) of the preceding condition, abandon the literal meaning and interpret حتى as 'in order that' (لام كي).
The first clause must be a cause and the second a suitable effect.
3
Analyze the first example (different subjects)
In 'If I do not come to you until you feed me', feeding is not an endpoint of coming but a motive; it is a result. Thus حتى becomes لام كي – the condition is only 'coming that results in feeding'. If he comes but is not fed, the oath is not broken.
The focus is on the coming being for the purpose of feeding; feeding is not part of the condition.
4
Analyze the second example (same subject)
In 'If I do not come until I eat lunch' (both actions by the same person), the result meaning is impossible (one cannot recompense oneself). So حتى reverts to pure conjunction (عطف محض), making the condition the combined act of coming and eating. If he comes without eating, the oath is broken.
Pure conjunction treats حتى like فاء or ثم – no special meaning.
5
Summarize the decision tree
The interpretation of حتى depends on the logical relationship: if the second clause is a suitable result, interpret as جزاء (لام كي); if not, interpret as عطف محض. The literal غاية is only retained when it is the only plausible reading.
This is a Hanafi-specific rule; other schools may not apply this contextual analysis.

Chapter: 'حتى' for the end. If the second thing is suitable as an end and the first is a cause and the second a result, it is interpreted as the result, and its real meaning (the end) is abandoned. Example: Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on him) said: If someone says to another: 'My slave is free if I do not come to you until you feed me,' and he comes but he does not feed him, he does not break the oath because feeding is not suitable as an end for coming; rather it is an incentive to increase coming and is suitable as a result, so it is interpreted as a result, meaning the lām of purpose, so it is as if he said: 'If I do not come to you with a coming whose recompense is feeding,' and if this is impossible because the second is not suitable as a result for the first, it is interpreted as pure conjunction. Example: Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on him) said: If someone says: 'My slave is free if I do not come to you until I eat lunch at your place today,' or 'if you do not come to me until you eat lunch at my place today,' and he comes but does not eat lunch with him that day, he breaks the oath because when each of the two actions is attributed to the same person, his action cannot be a recompense for his action, so it is interpreted as pure conjunction, and thus the total is the condition for fulfillment.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi deals with the interpretation of the particle 'حتى' (until) in conditional oaths. The base ruling: if the clause after 'حتى' can logically serve as a result (جزاء) of the condition (the first clause), and the first clause is a cause and the second a result, then 'حتى' takes the meaning of 'لام كي' (purpose – 'in order that'). The literal meaning of 'حتى' (endpoint) is abandoned. The first example illustrates: if someone swears 'if I do not come to you until you feed me', the feeding is not an endpoint of coming but a motive; it becomes a result, so the oath is not broken if he comes but is not fed. However, if the result meaning is impossible (e.g., both actions by the same person), then 'حتى' is interpreted as pure conjunction (عطف محض, like فاء or ثم), making the combination of actions the condition. The second example: 'if I do not come until I eat lunch' – the same person cannot recompense himself, so the oath condition is the conjunction of coming and eating. If he comes without eating, the oath is broken. This analysis is unique to Hanafi jurisprudence; it uses linguistic reasoning to determine the legal effect of oaths.

Key Terms
حتى (ḥattā): A particle that can mean 'until' (غاية) or 'in order that' (لام كي), or pure conjunction depending on context in oaths.
غاية (ghāyah): The end or limit; the literal meaning of حتى indicating the endpoint of an action.
جزاء (jazā’): Recompense or result; the figurative meaning assigned to حتى when the second clause is a suitable consequence of the first.
عطف محض (ʿaṭf maḥḍ): Pure conjunction; interpreting حتى as a simple connective (like فاء or ثم) without implying غاية or جزاء.
