Mushtarak and Muawwal: Definitions and Fiqh Applications
Defines mushtarak, muawwal, and their rulings with fiqh examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mushtarak and muawwal, explain the ruling of muawwal, and apply the concept to fiqh examples such as sale with unspecified currency and allocation of debt for zakat.

Core Idea
A mushtarak word becomes muawwal by predominant evidence and must be acted upon despite possible error.

In this passage from Usul al-Shashi, we encounter two pivotal terms: mushtarak (a word with multiple meanings) and muawwal (the interpreted meaning based on predominant evidence). The author explains that acting on a muawwal is obligatory even though error remains possible, and then illustrates with concrete fiqh examples involving currency, Quranic terms, and zakat obligations. Understanding this concept is essential for deriving rulings from ambiguous texts.

Guided Path
1
Define mushtarak and muawwal
Identify the basic definitions: mushtarak is a single word coined for multiple meanings; muawwal is a mushtarak whose meaning is preferred by predominant evidence (ghalib al-ra'y).
Hanafi usul allows mushtarak even under a single coinage.
2
Identify the ruling of muawwal
State the ruling: acting upon a muawwal is wajib (obligatory), despite the possibility of error. This is because predominant opinion (zann ghalib) is a valid basis for fiqh rulings.
Compare with qat'i evidence that yields mufassar.
3
Analyze the fiqh examples
Examine the two examples: (1) When a price is unspecified in a sale, interpretation follows the prevalent currency; if none is prevalent, the sale is void. (2) 'Qur' in the Quran is interpreted as menstruation (Hanafi view); 'nikah' in divorce context is interpreted as intercourse.
These show how mushtarak is resolved through predominant usage or context.
4
Apply the principle to zakat derivation
Work through Imam Muhammad's derivation: a debt is allocated to the lighter asset (e.g., dirhams over sheep) to resolve ambiguity. This affects zakat liability – zakat is due on the other asset.
This is a practical application of muawwal in furu' fiqh.
5
Distinguish muawwal from mufassar
Note that if the speaker clarifies the intended meaning with conclusive evidence (bayan qat'i), the word becomes mufassar and is no longer open to interpretation.
This limits the scope of ta'wil.

Commentary
This passage is a definition of two key usul terms: mushtarak (a word with multiple meanings) and muawwal (the interpreted meaning based on predominant evidence). The author begins by stating that a mushtarak becomes muawwal through ghalib al-ra'y (predominant opinion), and the ruling is that acting upon the muawwal is obligatory, though it carries the possibility of error. Examples follow: in a sale where the price is unspecified but a currency is prevalent, the transaction is valid by interpreting it as that currency; if currencies are equally common, the sale is void due to ambiguity. Similarly, 'qur'' in the Quran is interpreted as menstruation (Hanafi view), and 'nikah' in the verse about remarrying after divorce is interpreted as intercourse (not the contract). The next principle applies the same reasoning: when a debtor has multiple assets, the debt is allocated to the easiest to liquidate (e.g., dirhams over livestock) to avoid ambiguity in zakat liability. Finally, if the speaker clarifies which meaning is intended with conclusive evidence, the word ceases to be muawwal and becomes mufassar (explicitly explained).

Al-Mujmal: Text Requiring Speaker's Clarification
Defining al-mujmal with the Quranic prohibition of riba as an example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mujmal, identify its conditions, and apply the Hanafi rule that mujmal requires external bayan from the speaker.

Core Idea
A mujmal text is ambiguous but can be clarified by the speaker; it is not forever unknown.

In this lesson, we examine the concept of al-mujmal, a text that is ambiguous due to multiple possible meanings yet can be resolved through explanation from the speaker. The Quranic prohibition of riba serves as a classic example: the word itself means increase, but the intended legal meaning requires specification. We will also distinguish mujmal from mushkil and mutashabih.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition of mujmal
Memorize the Arabic definition: 'ما احتمل وجوها فصار بحال لا يوقف على المراد به إلا ببيان من قبل المتكلم'. Note the key elements: multiple possibilities, inability to determine intent without speaker's clarification.
2
Identify the components
Break down the definition: (a) it admits multiple aspects (احتمل وجوها), (b) it reaches a state where the intended meaning cannot be known, (c) except by a statement from the speaker (بيان من المتكلم).
3
Analyze the riba example
The word riba literally means increase. The Quranic prohibition is mujmal because the intended meaning—a specific increase (free from compensation in sales of measured/weighed items of the same kind)—is not indicated by the word alone.
4
Distinguish from mushkil and mutashabih
Mushkil can be resolved by reflection; mutashabih cannot be clarified at all. Mujmal can be clarified by external bayan. The footnote emphasizes that mujmal accepts clarification, while mutashabih (like al-muqatta'at) does not.
5
Apply the definition
Given a text, check: does it have multiple possible meanings? Is there a qarina (contextual clue) to specify? If no qarina, it is mujmal. The ruling: you must seek bayan from the speaker and cannot act upon it before clarification.

Mujmal, Mutashabbih, and the Ta'wil Dispute
Defines the ruling and dispute over mujmal and mutashabbih.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish mujmal from mutashabbih, state the Hanafi ruling on belief in their meanings, and evaluate the dispute over who knows the ta'wil of mutashabbih verses.

Core Idea
Believe the intended meaning is true; for mutashabbih, only Allah knows the definitive interpretation.

When the Quran or Sunnah uses ambiguous language, how do we respond? This lesson examines two categories of unclear texts: mujmal (ambiguous requiring explanation) and mutashabbih (unclear in meaning but impossible in apparent sense for Allah). The Hanafi school insists on affirming the reality without modality, and the dispute over the interpretation of mutashabbih verses is resolved by distinguishing certain knowledge (Allah alone) from probable knowledge (scholars may deduce).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two text types
Distinguish mujmal (ambiguous, explanation may come in this world) from mutashabbih (apparent meaning impossible for Allah, explanation deferred to Hereafter). Use examples like 'Face of Allah' and 'Istiwa'.
Mujmal can become clear via bayan; mutashabbih is known in reality but not modality.
2
State the ruling for both
The ruling is to believe that whatever Allah intends by these texts is true, even if we do not understand it fully. For mujmal, wait for explanation; for mutashabbih, affirm without modality.
This is an obligation of 'aqidah, not just fiqh.
3
Analyze the ta'wil dispute
Read verse 3:7 and examine the two readings: stop at 'Allah' (Hanafi) vs. conjoin 'and those firmly grounded' (Shafi'i). Understand the linguistic basis and the implications.
The difference is in qira'ah (pause) and grammar (waqf/ibtida).
4
Resolve the dispute verbally
The author notes this is a verbal dispute: pro-knowledge scholars mean probable (zanni) interpretation, anti-knowledge scholars mean certain (qat'i) interpretation. Both are correct in their own senses.
This resolution avoids takfir or extremism.
5
Apply the Hanafi position to a new verse
Take another mutashabbih verse (e.g., 'Yad Allah' - Hand of Allah). Affirm that Allah has a hand in a manner befitting Him, without modality, and conclude that its true ta'wil is known only to Allah.
This is the practice of tafwid (consignment).

Abandoning Literal Meaning by Context: Dalalat Mahall al-Kalam
Explains abandonment of literal meaning by context of speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when contextual indication (dalalat mahall al-kalam) prevents literal meaning and apply the Hanafi rule to shift to metaphorical interpretation.

Core Idea
When literal meaning leads to falsehood, context compels metaphor — dalalat mahall al-kalam shifts the ruling.

This passage introduces the fifth way a literal meaning (haqiqah) is abandoned: through the context of the speech (dalalat mahall al-kalam). When the literal reading is impossible or leads to falsehood, the Hanafi school interprets the speech metaphorically (majaz). The passage provides three concrete examples that demonstrate this principle, including the famous hadith 'Actions are by intentions' and its implications for the requirement of intention in acts of worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Recognize that the text describes the fifth cause for abandoning literal meaning: the context of speech (dalalat mahall al-kalam). Understand that this principle applies when the literal meaning cannot be true.
This is a core Hanafi usul tool for ta'wil (interpretive shift).
2
Examine the marriage example
Consider the case of a free woman where the words 'sale,' 'gift,' 'transfer,' or 'charity' are used to contract marriage. Literally, these do not denote marriage. The context (intent to marry) forces a metaphorical reading.
A free woman cannot be sold, so literal sale is impossible.
3
Examine the emancipation example
When a master says 'This is my son' to a slave of known lineage or older age, the literal meaning is false. The context indicates emancipation (i.e., 'this is my freedman').
The impossibility of literal paternity directs interpretation.
4
Analyze the hadith example
The hadith 'Verily actions are by intentions' literally means actions do not exist without intention, which is false (e.g., involuntary actions). The context forces a metaphorical interpretation: reward or ruling of actions are by intentions.
This is a critical example debated between Hanafis and Shafi'is.

Seven Types of Bayan (Clarification) in Hanafi Usul
Lists and explains the seven types of clarification (bayan) as derived by induction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the seven types of bayan as defined by al-Shashi, distinguish between bayan al-taqrir and bayan al-tafsir, and explain the logic behind the multi-tier classification.

Core Idea
Bayan is not only explanation; it includes confirmation, alteration, and even abrogation of meanings.

In this lesson, we delve into the foundational Hanafi concept of 'bayan' (clarification). While often understood simply as 'explanation', bayan encompasses several distinct modes by which the intended meaning of a divine text is made known. Imam al-Shashi, in his Usul, enumerates seven types, each serving a unique role in resolving ambiguity, confirming apparent meanings, or modifying rulings. This classification is a classic memorization anchor in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Define bayan linguistically and technically
Understand that bayan means manifestation or clarification, and in usul it refers to the act of making the intended meaning clear, whether by speech or action. The Prophet’s actions in prayer and Hajj are classic examples of bayan by action.
Bayan can be by qawl (speech) or fi'l (action).
2
Memorize the seven types from the text
The text lists: 1) Bayan al-Taqrir, 2) Bayan al-Tafsir, 3) Bayan al-Taghyir, 4) Bayan al-Darurah, 5) Bayan al-Hal, 6) Bayan al-'Atf, 7) Bayan al-Tabdil. Commit these names and their rough meanings to memory.
Use the mnemonic: Taqrir, Tafsir, Taghyir, Darurah, Hal, 'Atf, Tabdil.
3
Understand the underlying classification tree
The text explains that bayan is first divided into verbal (mantuq) and non-verbal. Within verbal, it is divided into clarification of the meaning of the speech or its necessary implication. Further subdivisions lead to the seven types. Trace this tree to see why each type is distinct.
The classification is by induction (istiqra) and is approximate.
4
Identify the role of bayan al-taqrir vs bayan al-tafsir
Bayan al-taqrir confirms an already apparent meaning to cut off a remote possibility (e.g., metaphor or particularization). Bayan al-tafsir explains an ambiguous term (mujmal or mushtarak). Recognize that taqrir is for when the meaning is known but needs reinforcement, while tafsir is for when the meaning is unknown.
The text says taqrir is for 'when the meaning is apparent but admits another possibility'.
5
Compare the seven-fold division with the five-fold division of other usulis
Most usulis group bayan into five categories, making bayan al-hal and bayan al-'atf subtypes of bayan al-darurah. Al-Shashi’s division is said to be 'closer to understanding' while the other is 'more beneficial'. Understand that the essence is the same, but the tree structure differs.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf in classification methodology.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of 'bayan' (clarification) in usul al-fiqh. The author (al-Shashi) states that bayan is of seven types, derived by induction. The commentator expands: bayan is expressing what is in the mind to make the truth known, and it can be by speech or action (citing the Prophet's actions in prayer and Hajj). The first type (taqrir) is explained: when a word has an apparent meaning but could be misinterpreted, the intended meaning is stressed or confirmed (e.g., by repeating or emphasizing) to eliminate that remote possibility. This is necessary despite the word being 'zahir' (apparent), because there is still a chance of metaphorical interpretation or particularization.

Key Terms

بيان تقرير (bayan al-taqrīr): Confirmation of a word's apparent meaning to cut off any remote possibility of interpretation (e.g., metaphor or particularization).
بيان تغيير (bayan al-taghyīr): Alteration of the ruling through exceptions, conditions, or limits (e.g., 'except' in a general statement).
بيان تفسير (bayan al-tafsīr): Explanation of an ambiguous (mujmal or mushtarak) word to clarify its intended meaning.
بيان تبديل (bayan al-tabdīl): Substitution or abrogation (naskh) of a ruling by a later revelation.

Bayan al-Darurah: Implied Legal Shares
Definition and examples of Bayan al-Darurah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a context where a legal share is implied by necessity and apply the Hanafi principle of Bayan al-Darurah to determine the missing share in inheritance, contracts, and wills.

Core Idea
When one share is explicit, the other is implied—silence plus context speaks.

In Hanafi usul, not every silence is ambiguous. Sometimes the law speaks by implication. This lesson explores 'Bayan al-Darurah'—explanation by necessity—where stating one party's share necessarily determines the other's. We will see how this applies to the Quranic inheritance verse, profit-sharing contracts, and bequests.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit statement
Find the part of the legal text that clearly specifies a share or ruling.
2
Recognize the implied missing part
Determine what must be necessarily true for the legal effect to be complete.
3
Confirm contextual necessity
Ensure there is a contextual indicator (qarinah) that compels the implication, not mere silence.
The opening of the verse establishes partnership between parents.
4
Apply to other scenarios
Use the same reasoning for mudarabah, muzara'ah, and bequests.

فصل وأما بيان الضرورة فمثاله في قوله تعالى: ﴿وَوَرِثَهُ أَبَوَاهُ فَلأُمِّهِ الثُّلُثُ﴾ [النساء: 11] أوجب الشركة بين الأبوين ثم بين نصيب الأم فصار بيانا لنصيب الأب، وإلا لزم الوراثة بلا نصيب. وهذا البيان لم يحصل بمجرد السكوت من نصيب الأب، بل بالسكوت مع إثبات صدر الكلام الشركة وتخصيص الأم بالثلث. فكأنه قال: فلأمه الثلث ولأبيه الباقي، وهذا إنما حصل بمجرد السكوت مع إثبات صدر الكلام الشركة وتخصيص ما نصيب الأم بالثلث. وكذلك لو بينا نصيب رب المال صحت الشركة، وكذلك لو بينا نصيب المضارب صحت الشركة، والشركة فيه شرط لصحة العقد، فصار كأنه قال: لك نصف الربح ولي نصفه. وكذلك الحكم في المزارعة. ولو أوصى لفلان وفلان بألف ثم بين نصيب أحدهما كان ذلك بيانا لنصيب الآخر. ولو طلق إحدى امرأتيه ثم وطئ إحداهما كان ذلك بيانا للطلاق في الأخرى. بخلاف الوطئ في العتق المبهم عند أبي حنيفة؛ لأن حل الوطئ في الإماء يثبت بطريقين فلا يتعين جهة الملك باعتبار حل الوطئ. وعندهما لا فرق بين الوطئ في العتق المبهم والطلاق المبهم. والفرق لأبي حنيفة أن المقصود الأصلي من النكاح الولد وقصد الولد بالوطئ يدل على استبقاء الملك في الموطوءة وصيانة للولد، أما الأمة فالمقصود من وطئها قضاء الشهوة دون الولد فلا يدل وطئها على استبقاء الملك في الأمة الموطوءة.
Section: On Bayan al-Darurah (Explanation by Necessity). Its example is in the saying of Allah Most High: 'And if he is inherited by his parents, then for his mother is one-third' [al-Nisa' 4:11]. It established partnership between the two parents, then (specified) the share of the mother, so it became an explanation for the father's share. Otherwise, inheritance without a share would be necessary. This explanation did not occur by mere silence regarding the father's share, but by silence together with the establishment of partnership by the opening of the speech and the specification of the mother for one-third. So it is as if He said: 'For his mother is one-third and for his father is the remainder.' And this occurred merely by silence with the establishment of partnership by the opening of the speech and the specification of the mother's share. Similarly, if we specify the share of the owner of capital, the partnership is valid; and similarly if we specify the share of the mudarib, the partnership is valid. Partnership is a condition for the validity of the contract, so it is as if he said: 'For you is half the profit and for me is half.' Similarly is the ruling in muzara'ah. And if someone bequeaths to Zayd and 'Amr a thousand dirhams, then specifies the share of one of them, that is an explanation for the share of the other. And if a man divorces one of his two wives, then has intercourse with one of them, that is an explanation for the divorce of the other. Contrary to intercourse in ambiguous manumission according to Abu Hanifah, because the permissibility of intercourse with slave women is established by two routes, so the basis of ownership is not determined by virtue of the permissibility of intercourse. According to them (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), there is no difference between intercourse in ambiguous manumission and ambiguous divorce. The distinction for Abu Hanifah is that the primary purpose of marriage is offspring, and the intention of offspring by intercourse indicates preservation of ownership in the one with whom intercourse occurred and safeguarding of the offspring. As for the slave woman, the purpose of intercourse with her is satisfaction of desire, not offspring, so intercourse with her does not indicate preservation of ownership in the slave woman with whom intercourse occurred.

Commentary
This passage is a section on 'Bayan al-Darurah' (Explanation by Necessity) – a principle in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It explains that when a legal statement explicitly specifies the share of one party, the share of the other party is necessarily implied, even if not mentioned, because otherwise the legal effect would be incomplete. The primary example is from the Quranic verse on inheritance: when Allah specifies one-third for the mother, the father's share (the remainder) is automatically known, even though not stated. This is not mere silence; it is a 'necessary explanation' (bayan bi al-darurah) because the context requires it. The passage then applies this principle to several fiqhi cases: (1) In a mudarabah contract, if only the share of the owner of capital is specified, the share of the mudarib is understood by necessity, and vice versa. (2) Similarly in muzara'ah (sharecropping). (3) In a will, specifying the share of one legatee implies the share of the other. (4) In ambiguous divorce where a man says 'one of you is divorced' then has intercourse with one, that intercourse is taken as an indication that the other is divorced, because the natural disposition avoids intercourse with a divorced woman. However, this principle does not apply in the case of ambiguous manumission of slave women according to Imam Abu Hanifah, because intercourse with a slave woman is permissible by two routes (ownership and marriage after manumission), so intercourse does not clearly indicate which status is intended. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree and treat it like the divorce case. The Hanafi position is that the primary purpose of marriage is offspring, while with a slave woman it is desire, hence the difference. This passage shows the Hanafi emphasis on contextual indicators (qara'in) in legal interpretation.

Key Terms
بيان الضرورة (bayan al-darurah): A statement that is not explicit but is necessarily understood from the context due to the legal requirement that all shares be determined.

Ambiguous Manumission and Intercourse: A Hanafi Dispute
Dispute on applying Bayan al-Darurah to ambiguous manumission

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between ambiguous divorce and ambiguous manumission according to Abu Hanifah, and evaluate the reasoning of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Intercourse with a slave woman does not clarify ambiguous manumission because its purpose is desire, not offspring—unlike marriage.

In this lesson we examine a specific dispute within the Hanafi school. When a man says 'One of my two slave women is free' and then has intercourse with one, does that indicate which one is freed? Abu Hanifah says no, while his two companions say yes. The key lies in the purpose of intercourse: in marriage it indicates preservation of the bond for offspring, but with a slave woman it is merely for pleasure.

Guided Path
1
State the disputed case
A man ambiguously frees one of two slave women, then has intercourse with one. Does this clarify which is free?
2
Present Abu Hanifah's position
He says no, because intercourse with a slave is permissible by two routes (ownership or marriage after manumission), so it does not indicate which status is intended.
3
Present the Sahibayn's position
They say yes, analogizing to divorce: intercourse with one ambiguous wife indicates the other is divorced.
4
Analyze the distinction
Abu Hanifah distinguishes based on purpose: marriage aims at offspring, so intercourse preserves the marriage bond; slave intercourse aims at desire, so it does not indicate preservation.
5
Determine the practical consequence
According to Abu Hanifah, both remain in ambiguity; according to the Sahibayn, the one not touched is free.
