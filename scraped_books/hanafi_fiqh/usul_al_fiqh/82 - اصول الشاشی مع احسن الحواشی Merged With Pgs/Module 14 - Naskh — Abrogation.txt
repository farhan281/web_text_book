Deriving Fasting Validity while Junub from Quranic Implication
Derives permissibility of fasting while junub from implication of fasting verse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul concept of isharah (implication) to derive the ruling that fasting while junub is valid from Quran 2:187.

Core Idea
Permission of intercourse until dawn necessarily implies fasting can begin with janabah.

This lesson explores a classic Hanafi usul example where a ruling is derived not from explicit text but from a necessary implication (isharah). The source passage analyzes the fasting verse (2:187) and demonstrates how the permission of sexual relations until dawn logically entails the validity of fasting in a state of major impurity (janabah). You will learn to trace this derivation through textual analysis and prophetic practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the operative text
Locate the specific Quranic phrase that permits intercourse until dawn. This is the foundation of the derivation.
The phrase is 'until the white thread of dawn becomes clear'.
2
Recognize the necessary implication
Since intercourse is lawful until the very last moment before dawn, and performing ghusl is gradual, one cannot avoid janabah at dawn. Therefore, the fast must be valid despite janabah.
This step uses the concept of 'talil' – reasoning about the nature of ghusl.
3
Confirm with prophetic practice
The hadith of Aisha and Umm Salamah (marfu') states that the Prophet would sometimes be junub at fajr, then bathe and fast. This hadith is cited as supporting evidence.
The hadith is considered sahih by al-Tirmidhi.
4
Identify the usul category of the text
The verse is classified as 'zahir' (clear) regarding the permissibility of intercourse, and the derived ruling is an 'isharah' (implication).
Zahir is a textual category that allows for additional inferred rulings.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of 'dalalah bi al-isharah' with a second example from the fasting verse (2:187). The verse permits sexual intercourse during the nights of Ramadan until dawn. The Hanafis derive from this permission that a person who has had intercourse may begin the fast while still in a state of major impurity (janabah). The reasoning: since intercourse is allowed until the very last moment of the night, and performing ghusl (ritual bath) takes time and is not instantaneous, it is impossible to be pure at the exact moment dawn breaks. Therefore, it is a necessary consequence that one may be junub at the start of the fast. The passage also cites a hadith from the Prophet (peace be upon him) confirming that he would sometimes be junub at fajr and then bathe and fast. This hadith is used as supporting evidence. The commentary emphasizes that the verse's permission of intercourse until dawn is explicit (nass) for the permissibility of intercourse, and the validity of fasting while junub is an implied ruling (isharah) derived from the necessity of the situation. The Hanafi school holds that fasting is valid despite janabah, and the matter is not considered a sin. This is the position of the majority (including Shafi'i, Ahmad, Ishaq) as noted in the hadith commentary. The passage thus illustrates how a ruling can be deduced from the necessary implications of a text, even when not explicitly stated.

Key Terms
الجنابة (al-janabah): The state of major ritual impurity resulting from sexual intercourse or ejaculation, requiring ghusl.
الرفث (al-rafath): Sexual intercourse; in the context, specifically marital intercourse.

Muqtada by Necessity: Divorce and Conditional Oaths
Deriving rulings for talaq and conditional oaths using iqtida' by necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of iqtida' bi qadr al-darura to conditional divorce and oath statements, and distinguish the Hanafi view from the Shafi'i view on specification by intention.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, muqtada is estimated only to the extent of necessity — intention cannot specify it.

This passage applies a crucial usul principle: whenever a word is implied by necessity (iqtida'), it is estimated only as much as needed. The Hanafi school uses this to derive rulings in divorce and oaths. You will see how a speaker's hidden intention cannot restrict a muqtada, unlike the Shafi'i view where intention can specify a muqaddar in conscience.

Guided Path
1
Identify the statement requiring interpretation
Examine the two statements: 'You are divorced three' and 'If you eat, you are divorced and my slave is free.' Determine what word is missing for the statement to be meaningful.
In usul, we look for what the utterance necessarily implies.
2
Determine the muqtada (implied word)
In the first, the word 'divorce' is implied by the number 'three' — but the speaker intends only one. In the second, the verb 'eat' implies a food object. The muqtada is the minimum needed for the utterance to be coherent.
Hanafi: muqtada is not an actual word but a requirement of the verb.
3
Apply taqdir bi qadr al-darura
Estimate the muqtada only to the extent of necessity: one divorce suffices, and any food satisfies the need. Do not add more than needed.
Necessity (darura) is the yardstick; it is removed by the minimum.
4
Evaluate the role of intention (niyya)
Hanafi: intention cannot specify a muqtada because muqtada is not a word that can be made general or specific. Shafi'i: the implied word is muqaddar (estimated), which is general and can be specified by intention diyanatan but not qada'an.
This is the core khilaf.
5
State the final rulings and thamarah al-khilaf
Hanafi: In triple divorce, only one divorce occurs. In conditional eating, divorce occurs on any eating regardless of intention. Shafi'i: In conscience, divorce is limited to the intended number or food; judicially, it is not.
The practical difference is the scope of the divorce.

Commentary
This passage applies the principle of iqtida' by necessity to two fiqhi cases. First, if a husband says 'You are divorced three' but intends only one divorce, only one divorce occurs because the word 'divorce' is estimated (muqaddar) by iqtida' only to the extent necessary, and necessity is fulfilled by one. The intention of three does not take effect. Second, if a husband says 'If you eat, then you are divorced' and intends a specific type of food (e.g., bread not watermelon), the divorce occurs upon any eating because the muqtada is 'food' as a general requirement of the verb 'eat'. The muqtada is estimated by necessity (any food), so intending a specific food does not restrict it. The passage then contrasts the Shafi'i view: they consider 'food' as muqaddar (estimated word) which is general and can be specified by intention in conscience, but judicially the intention is not accepted to ease litigation. The Hanafi view is that it is muqtada, not muqaddar, so specification by intention is invalid entirely.

Key Terms
ضرورة (darura): Necessity that limits the establishment of the muqtada to the minimum required.
نيّة (niyya): Intention; here considered for specifying a general meaning.
اقتضاء (iqtida'): The requirement of a word or phrase for the utterance to be meaningful; something implied by necessity.
تقدير (taqdir): Estimation of an omitted word or meaning to complete the utterance.

Effect of Prohibition on Legal Acts: Hanafi Usul Principle
Explaining that prohibition on shari acts implies their continued validity, unlike physical acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a prohibition on a shar'i act does not negate its legal validity, and distinguish between cases where prohibition implies continued validity and cases where it implies complete negation (naskh/nafy).

Core Idea
Prohibition on a shar'i act does not invalidate it; it only makes it haram.

In this passage from Usul al-Shashi, we encounter a foundational Hanafi principle: when the Lawgiver prohibits a legally recognized act (shar'i), that act remains valid as a legal construct. The prohibition only affects its permissibility (making it haram), not its existence as a valid contract or worship. This principle is derived from the impossibility of commanding the incapable—an argument based on the nature of legal obligation. The passage then applies this rule to examples like a defective sale and a vow to fast on a prohibited day, and addresses an important exception: when the essence of the act is incompatible with the prohibition, the prohibition actually negates the act entirely, as in certain prohibited marriages.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between shar'i and hissi acts
Identify the two categories: shar'i acts (like sale, marriage, prayer) whose legal validity depends on revelation, and hissi acts (like adultery, murder) whose moral value is known before revelation. The Hanafi rule applies only to the first category.
Hissi acts are not affected by prohibition in terms of their possibility; they occur regardless.
2
State the Hanafi principle
Al-nahy 'ala al-fi'l al-shar'i la yanfi al-shar'iyyah. The prohibition does not negate the legal validity of the act. Prove it: if it did, it would be like commanding the incapable (telling a blind person not to see), which is impossible for Allah.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli).
3
Apply to the defective sale example
The hadith prohibits a sale with a condition (bay' wa shart). According to the Hanafi school, the sale remains valid (conveys ownership) but is forbidden (haram) and must be annulled. The prohibition is due to the condition, not the essence of the sale.
The sale is 'fasid' (defective) but not 'batil' (void).
4
Apply to the vow to fast on day of Nahr
Fasting on that day is a shar'i act (valid in essence). The prohibition is due to an external description (turning from Allah's hospitality). Thus, the vow is valid according to the Hanafis, unlike al-Shafi'i who holds the vow invalid because the day itself is prohibited.
The disagreement illustrates the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).
5
Identify the exception: marriage prohibitions
Not all prohibitions on shar'i acts imply validity. In marriages prohibited by essence (e.g., marrying mother, polytheist women), the prohibition is interpreted as negation/abrogation (nafy/naskh) because the essence of marriage (permissibility of sexual relations) is incompatible with the prohibition. Thus, these marriages are entirely void.
The key is: can the act's essence coexist with the prohibition? If not, the prohibition negates the act.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi with the hashya Ahsan al-Hawashi discusses the legal principle regarding the effect of prohibition (nahy) on legally recognized acts (al-af'al al-shar'iyyah). The passage type is an usul rule. The foundational rule is that when a prohibition is issued on a legally valid act (like sale, lease, vow), the act remains valid as a legal construct; the prohibition only affects the ruling (making it forbidden/haram), not the act's existence as a valid contract. This is because if prohibition completely nullified the act's validity, it would be akin to commanding an incapable person (like telling a blind person 'do not see'), which is impossible for Allah. In contrast, physical acts (al-af'al al-hissiyyah) like adultery or murder are not dependent on legal validity for their existence; prohibition does not negate their possibility, it only labels them as forbidden. The hashya clarifies that physical acts are those whose moral judgment (husn/qubh) is known inherently before revelation and remain unchanged. The passage then examines examples: a defective sale due to an invalid condition (e.g., selling a slave with a condition of service), which is prohibited by hadith 'Nahi 'an bay'i wa shart', yet the sale itself is valid (conveys ownership) but must be annulled because of the attached prohibition. Similarly, a vow to fast on the day of Nahr is valid in its essence (fasting is legislated), but the prohibition is due to the external description (neglecting Allah's hospitality). The hashya notes a difference with al-Shafi'i regarding the validity of such a vow. The discussion then addresses an objection: if prohibition implies continued validity, what about marriages that are prohibited (e.g., marriage to polytheists, marriage without witnesses)? The response is that in those cases, the prohibition is interpreted as negation/abrogation (nafy/naskh) rather than a restriction on a valid act, because the essence of marriage (permissibility of sexual relations) is incompatible with the prohibition (its prohibition). Since they cannot coexist, the prohibition nullifies the very validity of the act. The hadith 'La nikaha illa bi shuhud' is considered abrogation (it negates the act's existence), not a prohibition on a valid act. Thus, the general principle holds: prohibition on a shari act implies its continued validity unless the essence of the act is incompatible with the prohibition.

Key Terms
النهي (al-nahy): Divine address demanding abandonment of an act (talab al-tark); in usul, it is a demand to refrain from an act.
الأفعال الشرعية (al-af'al al-shar'iyyah): Acts whose legal validity depends on revelation (like sale, marriage, prayer). Their existence as legal constructs depends on the Lawgiver's recognition.
الأفعال الحسية (al-af'al al-hissiyyah): Physical acts that exist independently of legal recognition, like adultery or murder. Their moral value (good/bad) is known before revelation.

Reconciling Variant Quranic Readings: The Case of Menstruation (Hayd)
Applying usul principle to reconcile variant readings in hayd verse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of al-'amal bi al-qira'atayn to reconcile the two readings of the hayd verse and derive the ruling on intercourse without ghusl when bleeding stops at ten days.

Core Idea
Act on both readings; assign each to a different scenario to give effect to both texts.

The verse 'hatta yathurna' (until they are purified) can be read with tashdid (yatthurna) or takhfif (yathurna). The Hanafi school does not choose one over the other; instead, each reading is applied to a distinct factual scenario. This lesson walks through the reasoning for assigning the lightened reading to the maximum duration of menstruation (ten days) and the emphasized reading to shorter durations, and why this matters for the permissibility of intercourse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two variant readings and their linguistic implications
Recognize that the tashdid reading (yatthurna) implies complete purification requiring ghusl, while the takhfif reading (yathurna) implies mere clearance from menstruation without full bath.
The difference in meaning is foundational to the legal assignment.
2
State the Hanafi principle for reconciling variant readings
The principle is 'al-'amal bi al-qira'atayn' — give effect to both readings as much as possible, rather than neglecting one.
This contrasts with the Shafi'i approach that prioritizes explicit over implicit.
3
Apply the principle to the two scenarios based on duration of bleeding
If bleeding stops exactly at ten days (maximum menstrual period), apply the takhfif reading – no ghusl needed. If it stops before ten days, apply the tashdid reading – ghusl required.
The rationale: after ten days, return of blood is impossible; before ten, possibility remains.
4
Address the objection from Zufar and al-Shafi'i
They argue that the explicit tashdid reading should override the implicit takhfif, requiring ghusl always. The Hanafi response: acting only on tashdid would neglect takhfif, violating the principle of giving effect to both.
The thamarah (practical consequence) is clear: Hanafis permit intercourse after ten-day cessation without ghusl.

Commentary
This passage applies the usul principle of reconciling variant Quranic readings to a fiqh ruling on menstruation. The verse 'hatta yathurna' (al-Baqarah 2:222) has two readings: one with tashdid (yatthurna) and one with takhfif (yathurna). An objector (representing Zufar and al-Shafi'i) argues that tashdid is explicit, takhfif is implicit, and explicit takes precedence, so ghusl is always required. The Hanafi response: acting only on tashdid negates takhfif; the principle is to give effect to both as much as possible. Thus, if bleeding stops at ten days, intercourse is allowed without ghusl; if before ten, ghusl is required. This is a classic demonstration of Hanafi usul preferring reconciliation over prioritizing one text.

Naskh Not Permissible from Servants: Applications and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Explaining rule that naskh is invalid from servants and its consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that naskh is not permissible from servants to identify which qualifying statements constitute invalid abrogation (tabdil) versus permissible clarification (taghyir), and evaluate the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the two companions.

Core Idea
Naskh is exclusive to the Lawgiver; any human retraction that abrogates a prior statement is invalid.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi establishes a foundational principle: only the Lawgiver (Allah and His Messenger) can abrogate rulings. Since humans lack this authority, any attempt to 'retract' or 'except' that effectively abrogates a prior legal declaration is invalid. The text then examines several applications: the invalidity of 'istithna' al-kull an al-kull' (excepting the whole from the whole), the impermissibility of retracting an acknowledgment, divorce, or manumission, and a key intra-Hanafi dispute about whether adding a defect to a debt acknowledgment constitutes naskh or permissible clarification.

Guided Path
1
State the General Rule
Identify the central principle: naskh (abrogation) is only permissible from the Lawgiver, not from servants. This is derived from the fact that only the Lawgiver has authority to terminate His own commands.
The text says: 'ولا يجوز ذلك من العباد' — it is not permissible from the servants.
2
Apply to Istithna' al-Kull an al-Kull
Understand why excepting all wives when only those named exist is invalid. This 'exception of the whole from the whole' abrogates the original statement entirely, thus it is naskh and invalid.
Example: saying 'my wives are divorced except Zaynab, Amrah, and Sa'adah' when he has no other wives.
3
Apply to Ruju' in Declarations
Recognize that retracting an acknowledgment (iqrar), divorce (talaq), or manumission (itaq) is akin to naskh and thus prohibited. Once these are executed, they cannot be undone.
The text explicitly prohibits retraction: 'ولا يجوز الرجوع عن الإقرار والطلاق والعتاق'.
4
Analyze the Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Compare Abu Hanifa's view (adding 'counterfeit' to a debt acknowledgment is tabdil/invalid) with the two companions' view (it is taghyir/valid if connected). This dispute hinges on whether the original statement implies soundness.
Abu Hanifa says the contract implies the object is free from defects, so adding a defect is a retraction.
5
Extract the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Determine the practical consequence: under Abu Hanifa, the acknowledgment is void and he owes 1000 good dirhams; under the two companions, the acknowledgment stands and he owes 1000 counterfeit dirhams.
This shows how the usul principle directly affects fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage addresses the usul principle that naskh (abrogation of a ruling) is an act exclusive to the Lawgiver (Allah and His Messenger). It is not permissible for servants (humans) to abrogate their own statements. This principle has several furu' (branches): First, 'istithna' al-kull 'an al-kull' (excepting the whole from the whole) is invalid because it effectively abrogates the entire original statement. For example, if a man says 'my wives are divorced except Zaynab, Amrah, and Sa'adah' and he has no other wives, the exception is invalid — all are divorced — because it is a form of retraction. Second, retraction of an acknowledgment (iqrar), divorce (talaq), or manumission (itaq) is not permitted because these are acts that once executed cannot be undone; attempting to retract is like abrogation. Third, the text presents a dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding certain qualifying statements. If someone acknowledges a debt of 'a thousand' and then adds 'and they are counterfeit,' the Hanafi school splits: according to the two companions, this is a statement of change (taghyir) clarifying the description, hence valid if said in the same breath (connected). According to Abu Hanifa, it is a statement of replacement (tabdil), i.e., abrogation of the initial acknowledgment, so it is invalid even if connected because a contract of exchange requires the object to be free from defects, and counterfeitness is a defect — thus it amounts to retraction. Similarly, if someone acknowledges a price for a slave girl but adds that he did not receive her and she is lost, Abu Hanifa considers this a tabdil because the acknowledgment of the price's obligation implies receipt (since if the goods perish before receipt the sale is void). Thus, the additional statement is a retraction, which is impermissible. The passage demonstrates how the usul rule on naskh directly impacts practical legal statements.

Resolving Ta'arud Between Two Qur'anic Verses: Resorting to the Sunnah

Core Idea
When two Qur'anic verses conflict and no chronology is known, both are set aside in favor of the Sunnah.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, when a mujtahid encounters two apparently contradictory Qur'anic verses, he first attempts to reconcile them or determine which is later (nasikh). If chronology is unknown, the verses cannot be arbitrarily preferred. The text teaches us to resort to the Sunnah as a stronger evidence, using a classic example from prayer: the conflict between the verse commanding recitation and the verse commanding silence, resolved by the hadith that the imam's recitation suffices for the follower.
