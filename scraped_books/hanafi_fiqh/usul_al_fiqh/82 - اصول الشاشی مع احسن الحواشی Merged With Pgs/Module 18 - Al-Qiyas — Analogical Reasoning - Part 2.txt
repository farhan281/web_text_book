Tathlith of Masah in Wudu is Not Sunnah
Stating the ruling that masah is a rukn so its tathlith is not sunnah, using qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a rukn (pillar) does not have a sunnah method of repetition, using qiyas to derive the ruling for masah in wudu.

Core Idea
Masah is a rukn — tathlith is not sunnah, unlike ghusl.

This passage presents a Hanafi usul argument against the claim that repeating the wiping (masah) three times in wudu is sunnah. The opponent analogizes masah to washing (ghusl), where tathlith is sunnah. The Hanafi response uses qiyas to show that masah is analogous to wiping over khuffs and tayammum, where tathlith is not prescribed. By classifying masah as a rukn (pillar), the Hanafi school establishes a general principle: a rukn in wudu is performed once, and its repetition is not considered sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposing claim
The opponent says tathlith of masah is sunnah by analogy to ghusl. Recognize that this is a qiyas claim.
The quote 'كَالْغُسْلِ' indicates the opponent's analogy.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi response ('قُلْنَا') asserts that masah is a rukn, so tathlith is not sunnah.
The term 'ركن' is key.
3
Locate the Hanafi dalil
They use qiyas to masah over khuffs and tayammum. These are acts where tathlith is not sunnah.
The phrase 'كَمَسْحِ الْخُفِّ وَالتَّيَمُّمِ' provides the analogy.
4
Break down the qiyas
Asl: masah over khuffs/tayammum (ruling: tathlith not sunnah). Far': masah in wudu. Illa: both are wiping acts, not washing.
The illa is that both are acts of wiping, and the hukm of not repeating is attached to wiping.
5
Conclude the ruling
Therefore, tathlith of masah in wudu is not sunnah. The practical implication: masah is done once.
This conclusion follows from the qiyas.

Rulings Attach to Their Causes: The Principle of Asbab
Explaining that Shariah rulings are linked to their apparent causes as indicators.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi concept of sabab as a sign (amara) for a ruling and apply the principle that rulings attach to their apparent causes to determine when an act becomes obligatory.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, a cause (sabab) is a sign that reveals hidden obligation, not an efficient cause that produces it.

How do we know when an act becomes obligatory? Obligation (wujub) is a hidden divine attribute. The Lawgiver provides apparent signs (asbab) that reveal when the obligation applies. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi view that these signs are not the real causes but markers set by Allah, using the example of prayer and time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its apparent cause
Locate the ruling (hukm) and the apparent cause (sabab) mentioned in the passage. For prayer, the ruling is wujub and the sabab is the entry of time.
The passage uses 'سبب وجوب الصلاة الوقت' – the cause for the obligation of prayer is time.
2
Distinguish real cause from apparent cause
Understand that the real cause (al-illa al-haqiqiyya) is Allah's eternal command, while the apparent cause (sabab) is a created sign that indicates the ruling. The passage calls asbab 'أمارات ومعرفات كاشفة' (signs and identifiers).
The commentary says: 'السبب الحقيقي لنفس الوجوب هو الإيجاب القديم' – the real cause of obligation is the eternal command.
3
Apply the principle to the prayer example
The command to pray is not addressed before time enters, proving that time is the cause. This shows that the ruling attaches to the sabab.
The passage states: 'فسبب وجوب الصلاة الوقت بدليل أن الخطاب بأداء الصلاة لا يتوجه قبل دخول الوقت'.
4
Address the objection: cause precedes effect
If time is the cause, how can performance occur within it? The solution: the sabab is the specific moment (the beginning of time) that precedes performance, not the whole duration.
The commentary: 'السبب الجزء الذي ينفصل به الأداء وهو مقدم على الأداء' – the cause is the part that separates performance.
5
Support with the Quranic dalil
Cite verse 17:78 and analyze the lam of causation. The lam indicates that time is the cause for the command. Timing does not contradict causation.
The verse: 'أَقِمِ الصَّلَاةَ لِدُلُوكِ الشَّمْسِ' – the lam is for ta'lil.

Invalidity of Arguing from Absence of Cause
Establishing that lack of a known cause does not prove lack of ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of inferring the absence of a ruling from the absence of a specific cause and apply the Hanafi rejection of this reasoning.

Core Idea
The absence of a known cause does not prove the absence of a ruling—another cause may exist.

This passage from Uṣūl al-Shāshī addresses a flawed reasoning method: some argue that since a particular cause (ʿilla) is absent, the corresponding ruling (ḥukm) must also be absent. The Hanafi school decisively rejects this as invalid because the ruling might be established by a different cause. The text provides three concrete examples from fiqh to illustrate the fallacy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the flawed inference
The passage starts by defining the invalid method: استدلال بعدم العلة على عدم الحكم. Recognize that this means: 'Because cause X is absent, therefore ruling Y is absent.'
This is a logical fallacy in uṣūl because a ruling can have multiple causes.
2
Analyze the first example (vomit)
Some argue that vomit does not break wuḍūʾ because it does not exit from the two passages. The Ḥanafī response: nullification can also occur from other exits, based on a ḥadīth ( e.g., 'al-wuḍūʾ min kulli dami sāʾil').
The absence of one cause (exit from passages) does not negate the existence of another cause.
3
Analyze the second example (brother's freedom)
A brother does not become free by his brother because there is no lineage (wilād). But freedom can be established by other relationships (e.g., muṣāhara). So lack of lineage is not decisive.
The ruling of freedom has multiple potential causes.
4
Analyze the third example (joint murder)
When a father and a minor jointly kill someone, the minor is not liable because of lack of capacity, but the father is liable despite the minor's lack of liability. The father's liability is based on his own act, not the minor's.
The questioner’s attempt to infer the father’s non‑liability from the minor’s non‑liability is invalid.
5
Extract the uṣūl principle
The common lesson: عدم العلة لا يستلزم عدم الحكم. The absence of a specific cause does not entail the absence of the ruling. The Ḥanafī school requires that the claimant produce positive evidence for their ruling.
This principle prevents the misuse of silence or absence as proof.

Reasoning from Absence of Cause (Al-Istidlal bi 'Adam al-Illa)
Explains condition for valid reasoning from absence of cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition under which reasoning from absence of cause (al-istidlal bi 'adam al-illa) is valid in Hanafi usul, and apply it to examples.

Core Idea
When the cause is exclusive, its absence proves the absence of the ruling; otherwise, it's a fallacy.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi addresses a common logical error in legal reasoning: inferring that a ruling is absent simply because a particular cause is absent. However, as the text explains, this inference is valid only when the cause is exclusive (munhasira) to that ruling. Two Hanafi examples from Imam Muhammad illustrate the correct application of this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the flawed reasoning pattern
The passage begins by presenting the pattern being criticized: reasoning from the absence of a cause to the absence of a ruling, e.g., 'He did not die because he did not fall from the roof.' Recognize that this pattern is generally invalid unless a condition is met.
The flaw is that multiple causes can produce the same ruling.
2
Pinpoint the condition for validity
The passage states that the reasoning becomes valid when the cause is exclusive (munhasira) to the ruling. Look for the phrase 'إذا كانت العلة منحصرة' (when the cause is exclusive). This is the key condition.
Exclusivity means no other cause can produce that ruling.
3
Understand the logical structure
When the cause is exclusive, it becomes a necessary condition (lazim) for the ruling. The reasoning is then: from the absence of the necessary condition to the absence of the conditioned thing (intifā' al-lāzim yadullu 'alā intifā' al-malzūm).
This is valid logic only when the lazim is specific to the malzum.
4
Apply to the first example: offspring of usurped female
The example: 'The offspring of a usurped female is not liable for compensation because it is not usurped.' Here, usurpation (ghasb) is the exclusive cause for liability; thus, its absence proves no liability.
The offspring was never physically taken, so the cause is absent.
5
Apply to the second example: witnesses of retaliation
The example: 'There is no retaliation on the witness in the case of witnesses of retaliation when they retract, because he is not a killer.' Killing (qatl) is the exclusive cause for retaliation; hence, its absence proves no retaliation.
The witnesses did not perform the killing, so the cause is absent.

Chapter: Argument without proof. Reasoning from the absence of the cause to the absence of the ruling: this is like saying, 'So-and-so did not die because he did not fall from the roof' – except when the cause of the ruling is exclusive to a meaning, then that meaning is a specific necessary condition with the absence of other causes. An example is what was related from Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on him) that he said: 'The offspring of a usurped female is not liable for compensation because it is not usurped, and there is no retaliation against the witness in the case of the witnesses of retaliation when they retract, because he is not a killer; and that is because usurpation is necessary for the liability of usurpation, and killing is necessary for the existence of retaliation.'

Commentary
This passage discusses a flawed method of reasoning (istidlal) that is invalid as proof. The fallacy is reasoning from the absence of a cause (illa) to the absence of a ruling (hukm) – e.g., 'He did not die because he did not fall from a roof.' This is invalid because there could be other causes for death. However, if the cause is exclusively (munhasira) tied to the ruling, then its absence does prove the absence of the ruling. The author gives two Hanafi examples: (1) The offspring of a usurped female (like a slave girl) is not subject to liability (not maddamun) because it was never usurped (the usurper did not take possession of it); (2) Witnesses who retract after retaliation is carried out are not liable to retaliation because they are not the actual killers. In both cases, the cause (ghasb or qatl) is exclusive to the ruling.

Key Terms
العلة (al-illa): The cause or effective attribute that brings about a ruling.
الانحصار (al-inhisar): Exclusivity of the cause; when there is only one cause for a ruling.

Deriving the Ruling of No Khums on 'Anbar via Qiyas
Deriving fiqh ruling through qiyas jaliy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of qiyas jaliy to derive the fiqh ruling that there is no khums on sea products like ambergris, using the Quranic verse on ghanimah as the original case.

Core Idea
Sea products like ambergris are exempt from khums because they are not spoils taken by force.

In this passage from Usul al-Shashi, Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani questions Imam Abu Hanifa about the absence of khums on 'anbar (ambergris). Abu Hanifa responds by drawing an analogy (qiyas) to fish: just as there is no khums on fish because it comes from the sea, which is not subject to the military effort that justifies khums, so too is there no khums on 'anbar. The commentary expands on this reasoning, grounding it in the Quranic verse that restricts khums to wealth obtained by spurring horses and camels. This lesson will walk you through the istinbat chain: from the general Quranic principle, through taqyid and qiyas, to the specific ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Original Ruling (Asl)
The established ruling is that khums is obligatory on ghanimah (spoils taken by force from enemies). This is derived from Quranic and Prophetic texts. Here, the asl is the general obligation of khums, but it is restricted by the condition of taking by force.
In Hanafi usul, the asl must have a clear ruling and a known illa.
2
Analyze the Textual Source and Its Category
The Quranic verse (59:6) mentions that fay' (and by analogy ghanimah) is acquired without military effort. The text is 'amm (general) but is qualified by the phrase 'what you spurred horses and mounts for', indicating that khums is only on wealth taken by force. This is taqyid (restriction).
The verse is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalala according to Hanafi usul.
3
Construct the Qiyas (Analogy)
The far' (new case) is 'anbar (ambergris), extracted from the sea. The illa (effective cause) shared with fish is that both are obtained from the sea, which is not subjugated by military force. Since fish and water have no khums, by analogy 'anbar also has no khums.
Qiyas jaliy is valid when the illa is clear and the new case shares it directly.
4
Verify the Common Attribute (Tahqiq al-Manat)
Confirm that 'anbar is indeed extracted from the sea and that it was never in the hands of enemies. This verification solidifies the analogy. The commentator also extends the ruling to pearls and other sea products.
Tahqiq al-manat is a mental operation to confirm the presence of the illa in the far'.
5
Derive the Ruling
Since no text (nass) overrides the qiyas, the ruling is that there is no khums on 'anbar. This is the Hanafi position, as stated by Abu Hanifa. The ruling is mufta bihi (operative for fatwa).
The passage emphasizes that the analogy is so strong that even without a specific text, it is binding.
