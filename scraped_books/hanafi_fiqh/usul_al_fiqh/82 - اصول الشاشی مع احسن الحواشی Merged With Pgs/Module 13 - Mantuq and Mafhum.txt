Ibarat al-Nass and Isharah al-Nass: Distinct Correlates of the Text
Defining and distinguishing the four correlates of texts: expression, indication, implication, and requirement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'ibarat al-nass and 'isharah al-nass, and apply the Hanafi principle that isharah al-nass is a valid source for deriving rulings.

Core Idea
The direct expression gives the primary ruling; the indication reveals a secondary ruling from the same words.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, understanding how a textual statement conveys meaning is fundamental. This passage introduces two key correlates of the text: the direct expression (ibarah) and the indirect indication (isharah). The distinction is not merely terminological; it determines whether a derived ruling is considered directly from the text or from a secondary implication. Through the example of the verse on the poor emigrants, you will see how isharah yields a binding rule for property transfer in dar al-harb.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four correlates mentioned
The text lists four correlates: expression, indication, implication, and requirement. Focus first on the two defined here.
2
Define 'ibarat al-nass
It is the meaning for which the speech was primarily intended and explicitly expressed. This is the most direct form of textual evidence.
Hanafis consider this qat'i (definitive) in meaning.
3
Define 'isharah al-nass
A meaning established by the wording itself without any added element, but not apparent from every aspect – it requires reflection to extract.
It is a valid source but zanni (probable) in dalalah.
4
Analyze the example from al-Hashr 59:8
The verse primarily (ibarah) establishes that the spoils belong to the poor emigrants. The word 'poor' (fuqara) secondarily indicates (isharah) that their property was lost permanently, implying transfer of ownership to the disbeliever.
The isharah here relies on the literal meaning of 'poverty'.
5
Identify the fiqh consequence of the isharah
The Hanafi school uses this isharah to rule that a disbeliever's seizure of Muslim property in dar al-harb causes ownership to transfer. Al-Shafi'i rejects this by viewing 'poor' as metaphorical.
The thamarah al-khilaf is a practical difference in property law.

Commentary
This passage is from Al-Shashi's Usul al-Fiqh, explaining the different ways in which a religious text (nass) conveys legal rulings. The author introduces four correlates (muta'alliqat): 'ibarah (direct expression), isharah (indication), dalalah (implication), and iqtida' (requirement). He then defines the first two. 'Ibarah al-nass is the primary meaning for which the text was revealed, the obvious intent. Isharah al-nass is a secondary meaning derived from the wording without any implicit addition, but it is not as obvious as the direct expression; it requires reflection. The example from Surah al-Hashr (59:8) illustrates this: the verse primarily states that the spoils are for the poor emigrants. This directly establishes their entitlement. However, by indicating their poverty through the wording 'poor emigrants', the verse secondarily indicates that their poverty resulted from the loss of their properties to the disbelievers, implying that the disbeliever's seizure of Muslim property causes ownership to transfer. This is an 'isharah' ruling. The Hanafi school accepts such indications as valid sources for deriving rulings, unlike some other schools that may dismiss them as mere metaphors. The author then notes that further rulings, such as the issue of domination (istila') and the merchant's purchase, are derived from this indication.

Key Terms
عبارة النص (ibarat al-nass): The meaning for which the speech was primarily intended and explicitly expressed.
إشارة النص (isharah al-nass): A meaning established by the wording of the text without any implied addition, but not apparent from every aspect because the text was not brought specifically for it.
دلالة النص (dalalah al-nass): Implication of the text; a meaning derived from the rationale or context, not from the wording itself.
اقتضاء النص (iqtida' al-nass): Requirement of the text; a meaning that must be assumed to make the text meaningful.

The Principle of Legal Implication (Iqtida') in Hanafi Usul
Explaining iqtida' as a usul tool with fiqh examples and internal dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-muqtadā, apply it to a fiqh scenario, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding the implication of possession in gifts.

Core Idea
A text may legally imply an element without which the ruling is impossible — and that implied element is treated as explicitly stated.

Legal texts often require an implied addition to achieve their intended legal effect. The Hanafi school recognizes this as al-muqtadā — an implied element necessary for the text's legal validity. In this lesson, we will unpack its definition, see it in action through a classic example involving sale and emancipation, and examine a critical dispute within the Hanafi school on whether physical acts like possession can also be implied. Understanding iqtida' sharpens your ability to derive rulings that are not explicitly stated but legally necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit text and the legal problem
Start with the example: 'Sell me your slave for 1000 dirhams' followed by 'I have freed him.' Why can't the freeing be valid without implying a prior sale? Because freeing a non-owned slave is invalid. The explicit statement requires an implicit sale to make sense legally.
The key is the Prophet's hadith: 'There is no freeing for what the son of Adam does not own.'
2
Define al-muqtadā and distinguish it from al-mahdhūf
Al-muqtadā is an addition to the text without which the text's legal meaning is not realized. It is legally necessary. In contrast, al-mahdhūf is linguistically necessary for correctness of speech but not for legal validity. The author (al-Shashi) does not restrict the definition, following Qadi Abu Zayd.
Later scholars like Fakhr al-Islam distinguished based on the purpose: legal vs. linguistic.
3
Apply the rule: what is implied in the example?
The command 'Free him on my behalf' implies a sale ('Sell him to me') and agency ('then be my agent to free'). The sale is established by necessity, and since acceptance is a pillar (rukn) of sale, acceptance is also implied. The commanded's response 'I freed him' is interpreted as 'I sold, then became agent, then freed.'
The implication is based on the phrase 'on my behalf' which indicates ownership must transfer first.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute: Abu Yusuf vs. Abu Hanifa/Muhammad
If the command is 'Free your slave on my behalf for nothing' (a gift scenario without compensation), Abu Yusuf argues that possession (qabd) is implied because the gift cannot be completed without it, just as acceptance is implied in sale. Abu Hanifa and Muhammad disagree: possession is a condition (shart), not a pillar (rukn), and conditions are external. Physical acts like possession cannot be implied as subsidiary to a verbal statement.
The dispute hinges on the distinction between rukn and shart, and whether the implied element must be of the same type (verbal) as the implier.
5
Conclude with the relied-upon position and the epistemological weight
The text sides with Abu Hanifa and Muhammad: possession is not implied. This is the muftā bihī position within the Hanafi school. The argument rests on the principle that al-muqtadi (the implier) is principal, so the implied must be of the same type.
This demonstrates the Hanafi method of ta'līl (reasoning) by distinguishing between essential and external elements.

Chapter on the Entailments of Texts. By 'entailments' is meant what pertains to the text from implication and the like. Here is a discussion on al-muqtada. If a man says to another: 'Sell it to me for one thousand dirhams,' and he says: 'I have freed him,' the emancipation occurs on behalf of the commander, and one thousand is obligatory upon him. If the command was intended as expiation, it occurs according to his intention. That is because his saying: 'Free him on my behalf' implies the meaning: 'Sell him to me for one thousand, then be my agent in freeing him, so free him on my behalf.' Thus the sale is established by way of implication, and acceptance is likewise established because it is a pillar in the chapter of sale. For this reason, Abu Yusuf said: 'If he says: Free your slave on my behalf for nothing, and he says: I have freed him, the emancipation occurs on behalf of the commander, and this becomes an implication of gift and agency, and there is no need for possession because it is like acceptance in sale.' But we say: acceptance is a pillar in sale; if we establish the sale by implication, we necessarily establish acceptance, unlike possession in gift, which is not a pillar but a condition, and a condition is external to the thing itself. Know that most of our early Hanafi scholars and the followers of al-Shafi'i did not distinguish between types of the implicit. However, Shaykh Fakhr al-Islam and most later scholars, except Qadi Abu Zayd, distinguished: what is established to correct the speech linguistically is al-mahdhuuf, and what is established to correct the speech legally is al-muqtada. They considered 'You are divorced' and 'I divorce you' as muqtada, and 'Divorce' as mahdhuuf. According to their school, it becomes difficult to differentiate between 'You are divorced' and 'Divorce.' The author (al-Shashi) was absolute in his definition and did not restrict the addition to 'legally or rationally' when he said: 'It is an addition to the text without which the meaning of the text is not realized,' and he did not differentiate between them. Hence he defined it as the Qadi defined it, and according to his school, there is no need for differentiation, as in al-Fusul. (1) His words: 'The emancipation occurs... etc.' because al-muqtada is like the spoken word in definitiveness; so the issuance of this word from him is like the issuance of purchase from him intentionally, not under duress. The validity of the meaning of this statement depends on the existence of the sale by implication, because his word 'on my behalf' indicates it; since emancipation on behalf of a non-owner is not legally valid, and ownership is only established through sale because he explicitly mentioned compensation with 'one thousand.' Therefore, this statement implies the validity of an estimated sale: 'Sell him to me for one thousand, then be my agent, and free him on my behalf through agency.' Thus agency is also implied like the sale, because emancipation after ownership is not valid from the owner without agency, as there is no real emancipator except the owner or his agent. Ownership requires a cause, and the mention of compensation indicates sale, so the sale is considered by implication. When the sale is established by implication, acceptance is also established because freeing on behalf of the commander depends on acceptance as it is a pillar in sale. Similarly, the words of the commanded: 'I have freed him' imply the meaning: 'I sold him to you for one thousand, then I became your agent and freed him.' The commander's statement: 'Sell him to me for one thousand dirhams' is a command to sell, not an offer, and no sale or purchase is established by this implication because it is merely a command. (4) His words: 'and there is no need for possession... etc.' meaning in the second gift by implication; because when the gift is established by implication, and a gift is only complete with possession, possession is established by implication, as in al-Ma'din. Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi defines and illustrates the concept of al-muqtadā (implied meaning) in legal texts. It begins with the example: when someone says to another 'Sell me your slave for 1000 dirhams' and the other responds 'I free him,' the freeing is valid on behalf of the commander, and the 1000 dirhams are owed. This is because the statement 'Free him on my behalf' legally implies a preceding sale and agency. The sale and acceptance are established by necessity to make the freeing valid, since freeing a non-owned slave is invalid. The text then cites a dispute: Abu Yusuf argues that if the command is 'Free your slave on my behalf for nothing,' the freeing implies a gift and agency, and possession (qabd) is not needed by implication. But Abu Hanifa and Muhammad respond that acceptance is a pillar (rukn) of sale, so it is implied, whereas possession is only a condition (shart) of gift, and conditions are external; a physical act cannot be implied as subsidiary to a verbal act. The passage also discusses the distinction between muqtadā and mahdhūf (elision): some scholars say mahdhūf is for linguistic correctness, muqtadā for legal correctness. The author (al-Shashi) does not restrict the definition and follows Qadi Abu Zayd, defining muqtadā as an addition without which the text's meaning is not realized. The commentary (hashiya) elaborates on each point, quoting from al-Ma'din and al-Fusul, and ends with a note on the necessary implication of textual structure.

Key Terms
المقتضى (al-muqtadā): What is implied by the text to complete its legal meaning; an addition without which the text's legal significance is not realized.
المحذوف (al-mahdhūf): What is omitted from the text for linguistic correctness, not legal necessity.
الاقتضاء (al-iqtiḍā'): The process by which a text necessitates an implied element for its legal validity.
الركن (al-rukn): A constitutive part of an act; without it the act does not exist legally.
الشرط (al-sharṭ): An external condition necessary for the act's validity but not part of its essence.
