Introducing Usul al-Shashi: A Classic Hanafi Usul Primer
Title page with author and publisher info

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author and commentator of Usul al-Shashi and explain the relationship between a matn and its hashya in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
Usul al-Shashi is a concise yet foundational primer in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, explained by Ahsan al-Hawashi for clarity.

This lesson examines the title page of Usul al-Shashi, a classical Hanafi text on usul al-fiqh. While this page contains no legal rulings, it provides essential bibliographic context. Understanding the author (Imam Nizam al-Din al-Shashi), the commentator (Hafiz Muhammad Barakat Allah al-Kanawi), and the publishing body helps situate the work within the Hanafi scholarly tradition. We will also distinguish between a matn (base text) and a hashya (supercommentary).

Guided Path
1
Identify the book and author
On the title page, the book is titled 'Usul al-Fashi' (a variant spelling of 'Usul al-Shashi'). The author is identified as 'Imam Nizam al-Din al-Shashi'. Note that the Arabic uses 'للشيخ' meaning 'by the Shaykh'.
The author, Imam Nizam al-Din al-Shashi (d. 344/955), was a Hanafi jurist and usul scholar.
2
Recognize the hashya (commentary)
The page mentions 'Ahsan al-Hawashi' by Hafiz Muhammad Barakat Allah al-Kanawi. This is a supercommentary on al-Shashi's text. A hashya glosses and explains the matn, often providing additional insights and clarifications.
Hafiz Muhammad Barakat Allah al-Kanawi (d. 1412/1991?) was a modern Hanafi scholar who made the text more accessible.
3
Note the publisher and its significance
The book is published by Maktabat al-Madina in Karachi under the 'Madina al-Ilmiyya' council (affiliated with Dawat-e-Islami). This indicates the text's continued use in contemporary Hanafi educational circles.
The publication details help trace the transmission and dissemination of the work.

How to Read a Critical Hanafi Edition
Describes editorial features and methodology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four key editorial features described in the preface and apply them to evaluate the reliability of a critical edition of a classical Hanafi usul text.

Core Idea
A critical edition's methodology determines its trustworthiness—know the editor's choices before you rely on the text.

This preface from the editors of 'Usul al-Shashi with Ahsan al-Hawashi' reveals their deliberate choices to make the text accessible without sacrificing accuracy. As a student of Hanafi usul, understanding these editorial decisions is crucial for using the edition correctly. The preface outlines four main features: easy-to-read formatting, source referencing for Quran and Hadith, modern script with punctuation, and interlinear glosses for difficult terms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the editorial claims
Read the four numbered points in the preface. Each states a specific action the editors took to improve the text. List them clearly.
Focus on the verbs: 'presented', 'referenced', 'adhered', 'committed'.
2
Examine the bracket system
The editors use flower brackets {...} for Quranic verses and small angle brackets «...» for hadiths. Note this distinction—it prevents confusion between primary sources.
Check the actual text of Usul al-Shashi to see how this is applied.
3
Check the source verification
The editors claim to have referenced hadiths from their original books. This means they did not simply copy from the matn but verified citations. This increases reliability.
In scholarly work, always verify that hadiths are attributed to their original sources.
4
Evaluate the script and symbols
The use of modern Arabic script and stop symbols is meant to reduce reading errors. Consider how this affects your study—does it help or might it introduce modern conventions?
Old manuscripts often lack punctuation; modern editions add it for clarity but may sometimes misinterpret.
5
Assess the interlinear glosses
Difficult words and technical terms are explained between the lines in simple language. This is a pedagogical tool but may sometimes oversimplify. Use it as a starting point, not a final authority.
Always cross-check glosses with a reliable dictionary or commentary (sharh).

Introduction to Usul al-Shashi: The Commentator's Method and the Role of Quranic Proofs
Opening commentary on the basmala and first lines of the matn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key linguistic and legal concepts used by the commentator in the introduction and apply the Hanafi method of istinbat to the Quranic verses cited.

Core Idea
The commentator uses Quranic verses as proofs for linguistic and legal points, demonstrating that even introductory remarks derive authority from revelation.

This lesson examines the opening of the commentary on Usul al-Shashi, where the commentator explains the basmala and the first line of praise. You will see how Hanafi usul integrates linguistic analysis (like the type of addition in 'generous address') with scriptural proofs from the Quran. Understanding this introduction frames the entire work's methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of the commentator's opening
The passage begins with basmala (In the name of God...), then praise of God, then prayer upon the Prophet. This typical triad frames the work as a sacred endeavor.
Notice the commentator argues that the basmala's 'ba' is for causation (sababiyyah).
2
Analyze the linguistic point: 'al-idhafah al-bayaniyyah'
In 'bikarimi khitabih' (with His generous address), the addition of 'generous' to 'address' is an explanatory addition (idhafah bayaniyyah) like 'jard qatifah'. The commentator proves this with Quranic verses showing the address is generous.
Proof: 'And you are the highest' (Al Imran 3:139) indicates the generosity of address to believers.
3
Define the key term 'mustanbitin' and list the methods of istinbat
The commentator defines mustanbitin as mujtahids who extract rulings from texts by wording (ibarah), implication (dalalah), indication (isharah), requirement (iqtida'), and analogy (qiyas). This is the core Hanafi theory of istinbat.
These five methods are fundamental to Hanafi usul and distinguish it from other schools.
4
Evaluate the debated point: does the hadith 'when He is mentioned, I am mentioned with Him' imply generality?
A hypothetical objector claims the hadith is general, but the commentator argues it is a particular statement (qadiyyah muhmalah fi quwwat al-juz'iyyah), so it does not convey 'umum. Therefore, the Prophet is not always mentioned alongside God.
The commentator uses Quranic evidence: verse 'And We raised for you your remembrance' (Al-Sharh 94:4) supports the hadith's interpretation.

The Four Sources of Usul al-Fiqh
Opening of al-Shashi's Usul defining the discipline and its four sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four foundational sources of Hanafi usul al-fiqh as defined by al-Shashi and explain their hierarchical relationship.

Core Idea
Usul al-fiqh rests on four primary sources: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas — from these all rulings are derived.

This lesson introduces al-Shashi's celebrated work on Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author opens by praising Allah and sending peace upon Imam Abu Hanifa, signalling his madhhab. He then defines the discipline as comprising four sources that are the foundation for all legal derivation. The subsequent commentary elaborates on each phrase, clarifying the scope and nature of these sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four sources
Locate in the Arabic text where al-Shashi lists the four foundational sources: Kitab Allah, Sunnat Rasulih, Ijma' al-Ummah, and al-Qiyas. Understand that these are the primary tools of usul al-fiqh.
The sources are explicitly enumerated after 'fa inna usul al-fiqh arba'ah'.
2
Understand the purpose of studying these sources
The passage states that research in these divisions is necessary 'to know thereby the method of deriving rulings from the texts and analogies.' Recognize that usul al-fiqh is the methodology.
The goal is not mere knowledge of sources but the ability to extract ahkam.
3
Distinguish definitive from speculative sources
From the commentary's explanation of the limitation to four, note that ijma' is classified as definitive (qat'i) while qiyas is speculative (zanni). This hierarchy is crucial in Hanafi usul.
The commentary states: 'the second [source other than Lawgiver's words] is either definitive (consensus) or speculative (analogy).'
4
Recognize the Hanafi identity marker
Al-Shashi specifically sends peace upon Abu Hanifa, indicating his madhhab. This establishes the work as a Hanafi usul text.
The later comment clarifies this intention: 'He singled out Abu Hanifa with prayer of peace to show that the author is Hanafi.'

Praise be to Allah who elevated the rank of the believers through His generous address, and raised the degree of the sincere ones for their faith, and singled out the extractors from among the learned in the meanings of His Book with increased correctness and reward. And blessings be upon the Prophet, his family, and his Companions, and peace be upon Abu Hanifa and his beloved ones. Afterwards, indeed the principles of jurisprudence are four: the Book of Allah, the Sunnah of His Messenger, the consensus of the community, and analogy. Therefore, it is necessary to research each of these divisions to know thereby the method of deriving rulings from the texts and analogies.
(1) His saying: [Praise be to Allah] He began after the auspiciousness of the basmalah with praising Allah, the Exalted. Praise is the description with beauty for a voluntary beauty, literally or effectively, like the attributes of the Creator, and the lam in it is for the genus or generality.
(2) His saying: [With His generous address] The idafa is of the type 'jardu qatifah', and the evidence for it is His saying: 'And you are the highest' [Al-Imran:139]. A generous thing is everything whose goodness and benefit is abundant. It is said: 'a generous book', 'generous provision', 'generous reward'. Mentioning the attribute generous excludes the address to the disbelievers like His saying: 'O mankind' [Al-Baqarah:21] and 'O disbelievers' [Al-Kafirun:1]. He added the general to the specific for clarification and specification, as in their saying: 'jardu qatifah' meaning 'a generous thing' of the type of address.
(3) His saying: [The extractors] i.e., the mujtahids who expend their effort in extracting issues from the texts by their wording, indication, implication, requirement, and by analogy to the loci of the texts.
(4) His saying: [With increased correctness] is an attachment to 'singled out', and 'al-mazid' is a verbal noun meaning He singled them out with an increase of hitting the truth; because the rare and the majority hit the truth, unlike non-mujtahid scholars.
(5) His saying: [And His reward] i.e., He singled them out with an increase of reward; because they deserve two rewards when correct and one reward when wrong. This is in 'al-Multaqat'.
(6) His saying: [And blessings] is a creative act of compliance to His saying: 'Send blessings upon him...' [Al-Ahzab:56]; because he is the explainer of the rules of legal issues and the nodes of legal rulings; or to complete the praise, because all the blessings he receives are through his mediation and blessing, peace be upon him. When he mentioned the true benefactor, he mentioned blessings upon the intermediary to be gratitude to Allah; because 'Whoever is not grateful to people is not grateful to Allah'; or acting upon his saying, peace be upon him: 'Allah honored me with special favors, one of them is: when I am mentioned, I am mentioned with Him.' This is the interpretation of His saying: 'And raised high for you your remembrance' [Ash-Sharh:4]. If you say: 'How many places mention Allah without mentioning him, like the basmalah, takbir, slaughtering, etc.?' I say: 'When I am mentioned, I am mentioned with Him' is a general proposition in the strength of a particular, so it does not imply generality.
Then:
(1) His saying: [And peace] There is disagreement about whether blessings and peace upon other than prophets are permissible. Some held it disliked, some prohibited. The majority held it is not permissible initially and independently, but followingly it is permissible. Meaning: It is permissible to say 'Blessings and peace be upon Muhammad and Abu Hanifa' but not 'Blessings and peace be upon Abu Hanifa' alone.
(2) His saying: [Upon Abu Hanifa] He mentioned him for the beauty of the opening, and followed him with the Companions as an indication that he is among the Followers closer to correctness as they did not disagree in his seeing Anas ibn Malik, may Allah be pleased with him. He singled out Abu Hanifa with prayer of peace to show that the author, may Allah have mercy on him, is Hanafi in madhhab.
(3) His saying: [Principles of jurisprudence] In it, the idafa of 'principles' to 'jurisprudence' is in the sense of lam, which indicates specification; these evidences besides analogy are not specific to jurisprudence, but they are proofs from the principles of religion, i.e., theology as well. So he should have said: 'Principles of Sharia', which is more general.
(4) His saying: [Four] The best thing said about the reason for limitation is that legal evidences are either the words of the Lawgiver or the statements of others. The first is either from Allah or the Messenger; the second is either definitive (consensus) or speculative (analogy).
(5) His saying: [The Book of Allah] It is the Qur'an, about five hundred verses related to rulings. Similarly, the Sunnah is about five hundred thousand.
(6) His saying: [Consensus of the community] The lam is for the covenant, i.e., consensus of the community of our Messenger, may Allah bless him and grant him peace; because consensus of other communities is not a proof.
(7) His saying: [Method of deriving rulings] The idafa of 'method' to 'derivation' is explanatory, or in the sense of lam, i.e., a method for deriving rulings; from adding the masdar to the object: the method of the mujtahid's derivation of rulings.

Commentary
This passage is the introduction (mukaddimah) of Imam al-Shashi's famous work on Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author begins by praising Allah and sending blessings upon the Prophet and his Companions, specifically sending peace upon Imam Abu Hanifa to indicate his madhhab. He then defines usul al-fiqh as comprising four foundational sources: the Quran, the Sunnah of the Prophet, the consensus of the Muslim community (ijma'), and analogical reasoning (qiyas). The hashiya (super-commentary) by Muhammad Ahsan al-Khayrabadi explains each phrase. Notably, the hashiya discusses the definition of 'usul al-fiqh' and notes that the sources (except qiyas) are also used in theology, suggesting the term 'usul al-shar'' might be broader. The passage establishes the standard Hanafi enumeration of legal sources, emphasizing that all ruling derivation must return to these four. The mention of 'mujtahids' highlights the centrality of ijtihad in extracting rulings. The commentary also clarifies that consensus is specific to the ummah of Prophet Muhammad and that qiyas is a speculative source while consensus is definitive. This opening sets the agenda for the entire work: a detailed examination of each of the four sources.

Key Terms
أصول الفقه (usul al-fiqh): The four sources of Islamic law: Quran, Sunnah, Consensus, and Analogy.
المستنبطين (al-mustanbitin): The mujtahids who extract rulings from the texts using various methods.
الإصابة (al-isaba): Correctness in hitting the truth or correct ruling.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning from an established ruling to a new case based on common cause ('illah).

Learning Objective

Fiqh rulings are derived from four sources: Allah's words (Quran), the Prophet's words (Sunnah), the consensus of the ummah (Ijma), and analogical reasoning (Qiyas).

This opening passage of Usul al-Shashi lays out the foundational framework of Hanafi usul al-fiqh. This lesson introduces you to the structure of Usul al-Shashi, a foundational Hanafi text in usul al-fiqh. The table of contents reveals the book's logical flow from primary to secondary sources of law. Understanding this roadmap is essential before diving into the detailed discussions.

Commentary
This page is the table of contents (fihrist) of the classical Hanafi usul al-fiqh work 'Usul al-Shashi', with the marginal commentary 'Ahsan al-Hawashi'. It lists the major sections and chapters of the book, providing page numbers for reference. The work is divided into four main investigations (mabāhith): the first on the Book of Allah (Quran), the second on the Sunnah, the third on Ijma (consensus), and the fourth on Qiyas (analogy). The table also includes sections on rulings, causes, preventers, and other fundamental usul concepts. This TOC gives the student an overview of the scope and organization of the work, which is a standard text in the Hanafi usul curriculum.
