The Hanafi Position on a Woman's Marriage Without a Guardian: An Usul al-Fiqh Analysis
Presenting the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on a woman contracting her own marriage, with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf and the preferred fatwa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of takhsis al-amm bi al-sunnah to the hadith about a woman's marriage without a guardian and derive the Hanafi ruling on validity.

Core Idea
A woman's marriage without a guardian is valid only if the husband is kufu — the hadith is takhsis for the general capacity.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi presents the celebrated dispute between the Hanafi school and the Shafi'i/Maliki schools regarding a woman's capacity to contract her own marriage. At stake is not only a fiqh ruling but a fundamental usul al-fiqh question: how do we reconcile a general Qur'anic principle with a specific hadith that appears to restrict it? In this lesson, you will trace the Hanafi usul methodology that supports the position of Imam Abu Hanifa and the preferred fatwa within the school.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi positions and their variants
Identify the three main views within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa's absolute permissibility, Abu Yusuf's early restriction, and Muhammad al-Shaybani's suspended contract. Note the preferred view (al-mukhtar) from al-Hasan: valid only if the groom is a suitable match (kufu).
The kufu condition is key to the Hanafi usul response.
2
Identify the Shafi'i/Maliki position and their primary dalil
State that Malik and al-Shafi'i hold a woman cannot contract her own marriage at all. Their proof is the hadith: 'Any woman who marries herself without her guardian's permission, her marriage is invalid.'
The hadith is marfu' and appears in various collections.
3
Analyze the Hanafi usul response to the hadith
Explain that the Hanafi school treats the hadith as a specific restriction (takhsis) on the general capacity of women to contract, not as an absolute prohibition. The hadith is limited to cases where the guardian objects or where the husband is not kufu, based on other proofs (Qur'an, qiyas).
This is a classic application of takhsis al-amm bi al-sunnah.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Explore the practical consequences: if a woman marries without a guardian and the husband divorced her thrice, she may remarry him after an intervening marriage in Hanafi fiqh, but not in Shafi'i (since the first marriage is void).
The footnote discusses the precautionary view of later Shafi'is.
5
Evaluate the tarjih and the mufta bihi
Explain why the kufu condition became the chosen position for fatwa: it balances the woman's capacity with the guardian's interest and reflects the strongest evidence.
Most mashayikh adopted this view.

Commentary
This passage presents the intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab dispute regarding a woman contracting her own marriage without a guardian. It begins with a summary of the positions within the Hanafi school: Abu Hanifa holds that a mature woman may marry herself absolutely; Muhammad al-Shaybani initially says it is suspended (mawquf), although a retraction is reported; Abu Yusuf's early view requires a guardian, but he later retracted to absolute permission. The preferred position for fatwa (al-mukhtar) is that of Abu Hanifa and the later narrations, that it is valid only if the husband is a suitable match (kufu). Malik and al-Shafi'i hold that a woman cannot conclude her own marriage at all. The text then cites the hadith 'Any woman who marries herself without her guardian's permission, her marriage is invalid' as a Shafi'i proof. The Hanafi response uses usul al-fiqh: the hadith is taken by the Shafi'is as a general proof, but Hanafis interpret it as specific to cases where the guardian objects, or they consider it weak or overridden by other proofs. The passage then notes a practical branch: the consequences regarding the validity of intercourse, dower, etc. The later Shafi'i scholars (mutakhkhirin) adopt a precautionary measure (ihtiyat) and do not permit remarriage after triple divorce, whereas the early Shafi'is did. This shows how usul differences lead to fiqh differences.

Key Terms
ولي (wali): Guardian, typically a male relative (father, brother, etc.) who has authority over a woman's marriage.
كفو (kufu): Suitable match, i.e., the husband should be compatible in lineage, religion, etc.
تخصيص (takhsis): Restriction of a general term (amm) by a specific evidence.

The Limits of Metaphor: Two Test Cases
Applies the haqiqah/majaz ikhtilaf to two new examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that impossible literal meaning may lead to metaphor only if no inherent contradiction exists, and identify cases where metaphor is rejected.

Core Idea
Literal impossibility doesn't always trigger metaphor; inherent contradiction blocks it.

In the previous lesson, we explored the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn over whether a statement whose literal meaning is impossible can be rescued through metaphor. Here, the author applies that dispute to two concrete examples: a debt owed 'or on this wall' and a husband calling his wife 'my daughter.' These cases reveal a crucial refinement: not every impossible literal meaning leads to a valid metaphor—sometimes the required relationship is itself contradictory, and the speech becomes لغو or legally inert. Understanding this boundary is essential for proper legal interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two examples
The passage gives two scenarios: (1) someone says 'a thousand is owed to me or on this wall'—the wall cannot be a debtor. (2) a husband says to his wife 'this is my daughter'—the wife cannot be his biological daughter. Both have impossible literal meanings.
2
Understand the Sahibayn's default response
For both examples, according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, when the literal meaning is impossible, the speech is meaningless (لغو) and has no legal effect. No debt is established; no divorce occurs.
Their principle: if the literal meaning is impossible, no metaphor is salvageable.
3
Apply Abu Hanifa's exception to the first example
Abu Hanifa argues that in the debt example, a metaphorical reading is possible: 'on' can indicate obligation on the person. Thus the debt is established on the speaker. Metaphor rescues the speech.
The relationship (علاقة) of obligation is plausible.
4
Analyze the second example: why metaphor fails
Abu Hanifa rejects metaphor for 'my daughter' because daughterhood (بنتية) is inherently contradictory to marriage (نكاح). The relationship needed for metaphor—a connection between the literal and intended meanings—is absent due to تنافي (contradiction).
Thus, even Abu Hanifa agrees with the Sahibayn in outcome (no divorce), but for a different reason: not لغو but contradictory relationship.
5
Derive the refined principle
Impossible literal meaning → metaphor is allowed only if a coherent non-contradictory relationship exists. If the literal concept and intended meaning are mutually exclusive, metaphor fails, and the speech is either لغو (Sahibayn) or legally irrelevant (Abu Hanifa).
This principle prevents unjustified legal consequences from imaginative speech.

Commentary
This passage applies the previously established ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn to two new examples. The first is when someone says 'He owes me a thousand or on this wall.' The literal meaning is that the debt is either on the person or on the wall, but the wall cannot be a debtor. According to the Sahibayn, since the literal meaning is impossible (a wall cannot owe), the speech is لغو and no debt is proved. According to Abu Hanifa, metaphor is resorted to, and it is interpreted as meaning that the debt is owed by the person (i.e., 'on' implies the person, not the wall). The second example is a man saying to his wife 'This is my daughter.' The hawashi clarifies that this cannot be taken as a metaphorical divorce (as one might analogize from the slave case), because daughterhood is inherently contradictory to marriage. Since the literal meaning (true daughterhood) is impossible due to the known lineage of the wife, some might think it becomes metaphor for divorce. But the text explains that the contradiction (تنافي) between daughterhood and marriage prevents the metaphorical use for divorce; the relationship (علاقة) is lacking. Thus, the wife is not divorced. This shows that not every impossible literal meaning leads to a valid metaphor; the metaphorical relationship must also be coherent.

Key Terms
لغو (laghw): Nonsensical speech that has no legal effect because the literal meaning is impossible and no valid metaphor is accepted.
تنافي (tanafi): Contradiction or incompatibility between two concepts, such as daughterhood and marriage, which prevents metaphorical transfer.

Implicit Divorce Words: Bain and Haram as Kinayah
Resolves an objection regarding words like bain and haram being kinayah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school treats words like 'bain' and 'haram' as kinayah that effect irrevocable divorce, and evaluate the Shafi'i counterargument.

Core Idea
Kinayah words carry their literal meanings; 'separation' implies irrevocable divorce.

This lesson unpacks a classic Hanafi usul dispute: when a husband says to his wife 'You are bain (separated)' or 'You are haram (forbidden)', what type of divorce results? The source passage from the commentary on Usul al-Shashi answers a presumed objection: if these are kinayah, shouldn't they produce revocable divorce like explicit talaq? The Hanafi response reveals a deep principle about the interaction of linguistic meaning and legal intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection states that if these words are kinayah for divorce, they should operate like explicit 'talaq' and produce a revocable divorce (rajʿī), as Shafiʿīs hold. Recognize that this is a qiyas: kinayah → like sarih → revocable.
The objection relies on the assumption that all kinayah for divorce function identically.
2
State the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: these words have independent literal realities (ḥaqīqah) of separation and prohibition. They are called kinayah only because they are ambiguous in intent (iḥtimāl), not because they are euphemisms for talaq. Once intent is established, the literal meaning dictates irrevocable divorce.
The key distinction: kinayah in terms of ambiguity vs. kinayah as substitute.
3
Analyze the linguistic basis
The text explains that these words operate by their own implications (mūjabāt). The term 'bainūnāh' literally means complete separation; 'taḥrīm' means prohibition. These inherently sever the marriage bond, unlike 'talaq' which can be revocable. This is a semantic argument rooted in the Arabic language.
Hanafi usul gives weight to the literal meaning (ḥaqīqah) even in ambiguous contexts.
4
Compare with the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i (and the Companions Umar and Ibn Mas'ud) held that such words are kinayah for divorce and thus produce a revocable divorce. Their evidence: these words are not the explicit formula 'talaq', so they should be treated as any other kinayah.
The dispute is essentially about the strength of the literal meaning versus the classification as kinayah.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarat al-khilaf)
Hanafi: immediate irrevocable divorce (bā'in) → no reconciliation during iddah without a new marriage contract. Shafi'i: revocable divorce (rajʿī) → husband can take his wife back within the waiting period.
This difference directly affects the couple's ability to reunite.

Commentary
This passage addresses a critical dispute in Hanafi divorce law. The matn states that words like 'بائن' (separated) and 'حرام' (forbidden) are considered kināyah (implicit) in divorce. However, they still effect a divorce, but specifically an irrevocable divorce (بائن), not a revocable one (رجعي). The hashya explains the objection: if these words are merely kināyah, they should act like other kināyah and produce a revocable divorce (like the word 'طلاق' does). The Shafi'i school indeed holds that such words are kināyah for divorce and result in revocable divorce. The Hanafi answer is that these words have their own literal meanings (separation, prohibition) which imply a stronger severance of the marital bond. They are called kināyah only because they are ambiguous—they could mean separation from sin or from marriage, etc. But once the context or intention points to divorce, the intrinsic meaning of these words—severance—applies, which is the very definition of irrevocable divorce (بائن). This is a nuanced application of the usul principle that words can have multiple layers: they are kināyah in terms of needing intention, but their semantic weight determines the type of divorce. The Hanafi reliance on the literal meanings of these words (rather than treating them as mere substitutes for 'طلاق') shows the importance of linguistic analysis in istinbat.

Key Terms
البينونة (al-baynūnah): Separation or disconnection; in divorce law it refers to a state where the marriage is completely severed, preventing reconciliation without a new contract.
التحريم (al-taḥrīm): Prohibition; when used in divorce context, it implies a strong interdiction similar to the prohibition of marriage.

Textual Hierarchy: Nass vs. Mu'awwal in the Ushr Dispute
Explains the Hanafi proof for ushr and the rebuttal of the Sahibayn's hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a nass (clear text) takes precedence over a mu'awwal (interpretable text) to resolve a conflict of evidences in the obligation of ushr on crops.

Core Idea
A clear text (nass) outweighs an interpretable text (mu'awwal) — even a weak hadith cannot override definitive evidence.

This passage presents a classic usul dispute: does the hadith 'ليس في الخضراوات صدقة' negate ushr on vegetables? The Hanafi school argues that this hadith is mu'awwal (open to interpretation), while the hadith 'ما سقته السماء ففيه العشر' is a nass (clear text). The principle of textual hierarchy (nass > mu'awwal) decides the ruling. Through this lesson, you will learn how to weigh conflicting texts in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Conflicting Texts
Locate the two hadiths cited: 'ما سقته السماء ففيه العشر' (obligating ushr on all rain-fed crops) and 'ليس في الخضراوات صدقة' (seeming to negate charity on vegetables). Note that the dispute centers on which takes precedence.
The first hadith is presented as a nass, the second as mu'awwal.
2
Classify Each Text in Hanafi Usul
Determine the textual category: the first hadith is nass (clear, unambiguous, qat'i dalala). The second is mu'awwal (interpretable because 'صدقة' has multiple meanings: zakat, ushr, voluntary charity). Also note the weakness of the second hadith in transmission.
Hanafi usul: nass is a level above zahir and cannot be overridden by a mu'awwal.
3
Apply the Tarjih Principle
The principle: al-nass yuqaddam 'ala al-mu'awwal (the clear text outweighs the interpretable). Since the first hadith is definitive in meaning and the second is not, the ruling derived from the nass is preferred.
The tarjih is based on strength of dalala, not merely on number of narrators.
4

Core Idea
In oaths of striking, the effective cause is pain; without pain, the oath is not broken.

This lesson explores a key usul al-fiqh principle: when a person swears not to strike, what exactly does the oath prohibit? The Hanafi school looks beyond the literal act to the underlying cause (illah) – causing pain. By examining various scenarios, we will see how the presence or absence of pain determines whether the oath is broken.

Distinguishing Rukn from Shart in Iqtida': Qabul vs Qabd
Analyzing whether possession (qabd) is established by iqtida' in a gift

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of iqtida' to distinguish between components established by necessity (rukun) and those that are not (shurut).

Core Idea
Iqtida' establishes only pillars, not conditions; necessity is measured by the extent of necessity.

In Hanafi usul, when speech necessarily implies an element for its validity, that element is deemed present by iqtida'. But not all essential elements are pillars. A Command to Perform an Act Does Not Imply Repetition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a command to perform an act is satisfied by one performance and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument regarding repetition.

Core Idea
A command to perform an act is satisfied by one performance — it neither requires nor permits repetition.

This passage establishes a foundational principle of Hanafi usul al-fiqh: when a command orders an action (e.g., divorce, marriage), it is fulfilled by performing that action once. Repetition is not required by the command itself; any repeated performance comes from external explanation (bayan). The text provides concrete examples and addresses objections, particularly from the Shafi'i school.

The main debate: does a command (amr) to perform an act imply that the act must be done repeatedly (takrar)? First, understand the definition of takrar: doing an act and then returning to it after completion.
Persistence in faith (e.g., staying Muslim) is not takrar; it is continuity in compliance.

Study the three examples: a command to divorce a wife, to arrange a marriage for oneself, and to command a slave to marry. In each case, the command is exhausted by one performance.

Critics point to prayer: bowing and prostrating are repeated in every rakah. How does this not prove that commands imply repetition? This is a key distinction between the command itself and its explanation.

Three views are presented: (1) Some Shafi'is (al-Muzani) hold that the command obligates repetition unless evidence prevents it. (2) Other Shafi'is say it does not obligate but carries the possibility of repetition. (3) The correct Hanafi position: the command neither obligates nor carries the possibility of repetition, whether the command is absolute, conditional, or qualified.
Pay attention to the different implications for the mufta bihi ruling.

Use the principle to derive rulings for other commands, e.g., the command to give zakat or to fast. The command is satisfied by the minimum that counts as compliance, but intention (niyyah) can expand it to cover the entire type.

Commentary
This is a ruling passage (usul rule) establishing that a command to perform an act does not require repetition. The author first gives practical examples: a command to divorce given to an agent is fulfilled once; even if the wife is remarried, the agent cannot divorce again with the same command. Similarly, a command to arrange a marriage (e.g., 'Marry me to a woman') is satisfied by one marriage; and a command to a slave to marry is obeyed by one marriage. The commentary then addresses a potential objection about the repetition of bowing and prostrations in prayer, responding that these are due to the Prophet's explanation (bayan) removing ambiguity. The commentary also presents the various scholarly positions: (1) Some, like al-Muzani and some Shafi'is, hold that the command obligates repetition unless there is evidence to the contrary. (2) Some Shafi'is say it does not obligate but carries the possibility of repetition. (3) Some Hanafi scholars initially said that absolute commands do not imply or carry repetition, but conditional or qualified commands (e.g., 'if you are junub, purify yourselves') do repeat. However, the correct and chosen Hanafi position (المذهب الصحيح المختار عندنا) is that no command—whether absolute, conditional, or qualified—implies or carries repetition. The command is satisfied by performing the minimum of the act that counts as compliance, though it also potentially covers the whole type by intention.

Key Terms
التكرار (al-takrar): Doing an act and then, after finishing it, returning to it again. The text discusses whether the command itself entails such repetition.
الأمر بالفعل (al-amr bi al-fi'l): A command that specifically orders the performance of an action (e.g., 'Divorce', 'Marry'). It does not imply repetition.
المذهب الصحيح المختار (al-madhhab al-sahih al-mukhtar): The correct and chosen position in the Hanafi school, which is that the command does not imply or carry repetition.

The Usul Rule: Ada as the Original, Qada as Substitute
Defining the categories of wajib: original performance vs substitute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi usul principle that the original wajib is ada (performance) and that qada (substitute) is only allowed when ada is impossible, and identify the textual category of this rule.

Core Idea
The original obligation is to perform directly; substitute is only a fallback when performance is impossible.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi establishes a foundational principle in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the wajib (obligation) imposed by a command (amr) is originally to be performed as commanded (ada). Only when actual performance becomes impossible does the law require a substitute (qada). This principle governs not only acts of worship but also civil transactions and torts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule from the text
The passage begins 'الواجب بحكم الأمر نوعان' and explains that qada is only considered when the original is impossible. Read the first few lines to extract the rule.
2
Analyze the linguistic basis of 'khalf' (substitute)
The commentator notes that the substitute is only resorted to when the original is unattainable. This is a lexical principle inherent in the term 'خلف'.
The rule is not just a juristic construct but has linguistic grounding.
3
Apply the rule to simple cases: deposit, agency, usurpation
The text gives examples: in these contracts, the specific item is determined (يتعين) because the substitute (like value) is not allowed. This shows the rule in action.
The rule is applied consistently across different contracts.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi with its hashia discusses the foundational usul principle that the original obligation (wajib) by command is of two types: performance (ada) and substitute (qada). The principle is that the origin in all obligations is actual performance; substitute is only resorted to when performance is impossible. This is applied to various fiqh rulings. The commentary expounds on the principle and clarifies its application in cases of ghasb (usurpation) and sale with defects. A key point is the dispute with Imam Shafi'i regarding the obligation on a usurper: Shafi'i holds that the usurper must return the actual item even if it has changed significantly, while Hanafis hold that if the change is drastic (i.e., name and major benefits lost), the usurper becomes owner and must pay the value. The hashia provides detailed reasoning for the Hanafi position, referencing works like al-Ma'din and clarifying that the ruling depends on whether the original item (ayn) remains in essence. The principle of ada being preferred over qada is also applied to the buyer's option in case of a defect in a purchased item.

Applied Dispute: Usurpation with Drastic Change – Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Applying the ada/qada rule to ghasb with drastic change, including the inter-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the ghāsib's liability after drastic change, contrast it with the Shafi'i view, and apply the usul principle of 'ada before qada' to derive this ruling.

Core Idea
When a usurper destroys the identity of an item, its return becomes impossible; the obligation switches from returning the actual item to paying its value.

This passage applies the general rule about ada and qada to the case of ghasb (usurpation). When a usurper so transforms the usurped item that it loses its name and primary benefits (taghayyur fāḥish), the Hanafi school holds that the original owner loses ownership and the usurper becomes owner of the new object, with an obligation to pay its value (qīma). Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees, requiring the return of the changed item plus compensation for the deficiency. The commentary defends the Hanafi view using the principle that performance (ada) of returning the original is impossible, so substitute (qada) by value is necessary.

Guided Path
1
State the Shafi'i position from the text
Identify the section that begins with 'الشافعي: الواجب على الغاصب رد العين المغصوبة وإن تغيرت تغيرا فاحشا ويجب الأرش'. Read the translation to understand the position.
2
Identify the Hanafi response and its basis
The commentary explains that when the change is drastic (name and benefits lost), the original item is considered non-existent, so the usurper's act of transformation creates a new property right.
The response uses the principle that ada is impossible, so qada is required.
3
Trace the underlying usul principle
The principle: ada is the original; qada is only when ada impossible. Here, ada (returning the original wheat) is impossible because it no longer exists as wheat after grinding.
4
Work through the examples listed in the text
The text lists specific cases: grinding wheat, building with a beam, roasting a sheep, pressing grapes, planting wheat. In each, the Hanafi rule applies.
These are all applications of the same principle.
5
Identify the istihsan element in the Hanafi ruling
The commentary addresses the objection that the ruling rewards wrongdoing by distinguishing between the destructive and creative aspects of the act. Ownership is based on the creation, not the destruction.
This shows how istihsan can override a pure qiyas.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi with its hashia discusses the foundational usul principle that the original obligation (wajib) by command is of two types: performance (ada) and substitute (qada). The principle is that the origin in all obligations is actual performance; substitute is only resorted to when performance is impossible. This is applied to various fiqh rulings. The commentary expounds on the principle and clarifies its application in cases of ghasb (usurpation) and sale with defects. A key point is the dispute with Imam Shafi'i regarding the obligation on a usurper: Shafi'i holds that the usurper must return the actual item even if it has changed significantly, while Hanafis hold that if the change is drastic (i.e., name and major benefits lost), the usurper becomes owner and must pay the value. The hashia provides detailed reasoning for the Hanafi position, referencing works like al-Ma'din and clarifying that the ruling depends on whether the original item (ayn) remains in essence. The principle of ada being preferred over qada is also applied to the buyer's option in case of a defect in a purchased item.

Key Terms
أداء (ada): Performance of the original obligation as commanded; direct fulfillment.
قضاء (qada): Substitute performance when actual performance is impossible.
غصب (ghasb): Usurpation; taking another's property without right.
تغير فاحش (taghayyur fahish): Drastic change where the name and primary benefits of the item are lost.

Guarantee of Usurped Property: Mithli, Qimi, and Usufructs
Dispute on guarantee of mithli, qimi, and usufructs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi positions on valuation timing for usurped mithli goods and explain the Hanafi rationale for not guaranteeing usufructs.

Core Idea
A usurped mithli is replaced by its like; when impossible, value is assessed — but Hanafi masters differ on the valuation date, and usufructs are not guaranteed.

In this lesson, we examine a key usul dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the guarantee of usurped property. The text distinguishes between mithli (replaceable) and qimi (irreplaceable) goods, and discusses whether usufructs (manafi‘) are compensable. This dispute has practical implications for property law and reflects deeper principles about obligation and impossibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and categories
The passage discusses guarantee (daman) for usurped property. First, determine whether the property is mithli (has a standard like) or qimi (no like). For mithli, the guarantor must deliver the like; if impossible, value is paid. The timing of valuation is contested.
Mithli goods are fungibles like wheat, oil, etc.; qimi are unique items like a slave.
2
State the three Hanafi positions on valuation timing
Abu Hanifa: value on day of litigation. Abu Yusuf: value on day of usurpation. Muhammad: value on day the like becomes unavailable (cut off from people's hands). The reasoning ties to when inability to deliver the like is established.
Each imam focuses on a different moment as decisive for the switch from like to value.
3
Analyze the reasoning for usufructs
For things without any like (usufructs), guarantee by like is impossible, and guarantee by substance is also impossible because substance does not resemble usufruct. Thus, usufructs are not guaranteed. This is the Hanafi position, opposed by al-Shafi'i.
Usufructs are considered non-enduring accidents (a‘rad lā tabqā) in Hanafi usul.
4
Contrast with Shafi'i and note the thamarah al-khilaf
Al-Shafi'i argues usufructs are legally valuable (muttaqawwima) as seen in lease contracts, so they should be guaranteed. The practical consequence: under Hanafi law, using a usurped property without damage incurs no financial liability for the usufruct; under Shafi'i, it does.
The dispute is built on different characterizations of usufructs: accident vs. independent benefit.

Commentary

Thus, using a usurped slave or house does not oblige payment for the usufruct, unlike the Shafi'i school. The passage explains the reasoning: usufructs are ephemeral, not reproducible, and cannot be equated to tangible property. This ruling is based on istihsan and the nature of usufructs as non-enduring accidents.

Dispute on Waw in Wife's Statement 'Divorce Me and You Have a Thousand'
Presenting the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on interpreting waw in a divorce contract.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between conjunctive and circumstantial uses of the waw in a divorce statement, and apply each interpretation to derive the fiqh ruling.

Core Idea
The waw in 'Divorce me and you have a thousand' can be conjunctive or circumstantial, changing whether the wife owes payment.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi presents a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding the interpretation of the waw in a wife's divorce statement. The dispute centers on whether the waw is a simple conjunction (عطف) or a circumstantial clause (حال) that creates a condition. Understanding this dispute is key to appreciating how usul al-fiqh principles shape the fiqh outcomes in contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the text and the disputed particle
Read the wife's statement 'طلقني ولك ألف' and focus on the waw. Determine the possible meanings: conjunction (عطف) or circumstance (حال).
The waw is a multifunctional particle in Arabic; its legal interpretation depends on context and usul principles.
2
State Abu Hanifa's interpretation and ruling
According to Abu Hanifa, the waw is conjunctive, so the mention of a thousand is not a condition. Thus, no payment is due from the wife. The divorce is revocable (raj'i)
Abu Hanifa argues that divorce is originally gratuitous; mentioning money would change it to khul' or an oath, but the waw does not create a condition.
3
State the Sahibayn's interpretation and ruling
The Sahibayn interpret the waw as circumstantial (حال), which functions as a condition. Thus, the wife must pay a thousand dirhams, and the divorce becomes irrevocable (ba'in).
They analogize to a hire contract: 'Carry this goods and you have a dirham' where the dirham is a substitute.
4
Compare the usul principles behind each view
Identify the usul principles: Abu Hanifa relies on the default conjunction and the nature of divorce; the Sahibayn rely on the circumstantial meaning and the analogy to compensative contracts.
The dispute highlights the role of qiyas (analogy) and the classification of particles in usul al-fiqh.

Commentary

The relied upon position (mufta bihi) in the Hanafi school is often the view of the Sahibayn in this case.

Dispute on Basis for Number of Divorces: Wife's Status vs Husband's
Explains Hanafi view with proofs and Shafi'i rebuttal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of amm (general) to the hadith on slave woman's divorce and evaluate the Shafi'i counterargument using the Companions' practice.

Core Idea
The number of divorces depends on the wife's status, not the husband's — the definite article in 'al-ama' makes the ruling general for all slave women.

This lesson delves into a classic usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on the basis for determining the number of divorces a husband can pronounce. The Hanafi school argues that the crucial factor is the wife's freedom or slavery, while the Shafi'i school looks to the husband's status. We will trace the proofs from the Sunnah, analyze the linguistic arguments, and see how the Companions' practice is used to rebut the opposing view.

Guided Path
1
State the agreed premise and the point of dispute
Both schools agree that a free woman is entitled to three divorces and a slave woman to two. The disagreement is whether this number is determined by the wife's or husband's status.
2
Identify the Hanafi proof: the hadith 'طلاق الأمة ثنتان'
The Hanafi rely on the hadith 'The divorce of a slave woman is two.' The definite article 'al' in 'al-ama' indicates the genus, making the ruling applicable to all slave women. This directly ties the number to the wife's status.
The hadith also states the 'iddah is two menses, further showing the wife's status governs.
3
Identify the Shafi'i proof: the hadith 'الطلاق بالرجال والعدة بالنساء'
The Shafi'i school uses this hadith to argue that the number of divorces is determined by the husband's status (free or slave), since divorce is associated with men, while the waiting period is associated with women.
They interpret the hadith as defining the quantity of divorces based on the husband's condition.
4
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafi rebuttal has two parts: (1) The Companions, including the narrator Zayd ibn Thabit, did not use this hadith in their discussions on this issue, indicating it is either weak or abrogated. (2) If accepted, it can be interpreted to mean that the initiation of divorce is by men, not that the number depends on their status. The explicit hadith on the slave woman establishes the number based on the wife's status.
The Hanafi response uses the practice of the Companions as a criterion for evaluating the strength of the Shafi'i proof.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and the mufta bihi
The practical difference: If a free man marries a slave woman, Hanafis say he has two divorces; Shafi'is say three. If a slave man marries a free woman, Hanafis say three; Shafi'is say two. The text clearly adopts the Hanafi position as correct.
The Hanafi position is the mufta bihi in the Hanafi school, supported by the explicit hadith and the Companions' consensus (sukuti).

Hanafi vs Zufar on 'Ila' in Talaq
Usul dispute on 'ila' in talaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle regarding the word 'ila' in talaq to distinguish between immediate and deferred divorce, and evaluate the counter-argument of Imam Zufar.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, 'ila' in talaq delays the effect entirely, unlike in other contracts where it defers but does not prevent immediate establishment.

This lesson examines a classic usul dispute on the legal effect of the particle 'ila' (to/until) when used in a talaq pronouncement. The Hanafi school holds that if a husband says 'anta taliq ila shahr' without intention, divorce does not occur until the month ends. Imam Zufar disagrees, arguing that 'ila' merely defers the full effect but does not prevent the divorce from taking effect immediately. The difference hinges on the nature of talaq as an isqat (removal) versus an ithbat (affirmation).

1

2
Identify the Hanafi proof

Key: isqat vs ithbat
3
Identify Zufar's proof
Zufar argues that 'ila' indicates deferment, and deferment does not prevent original establishment, like a deferred debt still exists as a debt.
4

The Hanafis respond that 'ila' here enters upon the origin of divorce, and the origin of divorce can be delayed by conditioning, unlike the price in a sale which cannot be conditioned.
Talaq's origin is isqat, which is susceptible to ta'liq.
5

The practical consequence: without intention, Hanafi says no immediate divorce; Zufar says immediate divorce. This affects the wife's status and waiting period.

The Rule of Ba in Conditional Manumission: Truthful vs. Absolute News
Applies the rule of ba to conditional manumission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of ba attachment to distinguish between conditional manumission based on truthful news and absolute news.

Core Idea
Ba attaches the condition to the object: 'inform of arrival' requires true arrival; 'inform that he arrived' triggers on any report.

In this lesson, we examine how the particle ba in Arabic conditional statements determines the legal outcome. The Hanafi school analyses the attachment (talā'uq) of the action to its object. By contrasting two similar phrases, we see how a shift in ba's attachment changes the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two formulations
Locate the two phrases: 'If you inform me of the arrival of so-and-so' and 'If you inform me that so-and-so has arrived'.
The first uses 'bi-qadūm' (with arrival); the second uses 'anna' (that).
2
Analyze the ba attachment
In the first, ba attaches the news to the arrival (event). In the second, ba attaches the news to the report (act of informing).
Attachment (ilṣāq) is key.
3
Apply the rule
If ba attaches to the event, the condition requires the event's truth. If to the report, any report suffices.
4
Derive the ruling
False news in the first does not free; false news in the second frees the slave.
5
Note the underlying principle
Ba indicates attachment, and the legal effect depends on what it attaches to.
This is a fundamental usul principle.

Commentary
This passage applies the rule of ba for ilṣāq to conditional statements. The first scenario: a master says to his slave, 'If you inform me of the arrival of Zaid, you are free.' Ta'līq: Cause or Preventer? Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Defines bayān al-taghayyur and presents Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on ta'līq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi and Shāfiʿī positions on taʿlīq and explain the practical consequence (thamarah) of the dispute.

Core Idea
In ta'līq, Hanafis say the condition creates the cause; Shāfiʿīs say the condition blocks the effect.

This lesson examines the concept of bayān al-taghayyur (clarification by alteration) and the key dispute between the Hanafis and Shāfiʿīs regarding conditional clauses (taʿlīq). The core question: does a conditional declaration become a legal cause immediately or only upon fulfillment of the condition? Understanding this difference is essential for deriving rulings in cases where the condition never occurs.

Guided Path
1
Define bayān al-taghayyur
Understand that bayān al-taghayyur is a clarification that changes the original meaning; its two main examples are ta'līq (conditional clauses) and istithnā' (exceptions).
The change is by way of clarification, not contradiction.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafis hold that a conditional declaration (e.g., 'You are free if you enter the house') only becomes a cause (sabab) when the condition occurs. Before that, it has no causal effect.
The non-existence of the effect is due to the original non-existence, not a preventer.
3
State the Shāfiʿī position
Al-Shāfiʿī holds that the declaration is a cause in the present, but the condition acts as a preventer (māniʿ) that blocks the effect until the condition is fulfilled.
If not for the condition, the effect would occur immediately.
4
Compare the evidence (dalīl)
The Hanafis use a qiyas: just as a declaration addressed to an animal does not become a cause because it is not the proper place, similarly a conditional declaration does not become a cause until the condition is met. The Shāfiʿīs argue that since the effect would occur without the condition, the declaration must be a cause now.
Both arguments are rational (dalīl ʿaqlī).
5
Identify the practical consequence
If the condition never occurs (e.g., wife dies before entering), Hanafis rule no effect; Shāfiʿīs would likely rule effect because the cause already existed.
This difference affects fatwa in real cases.

Commentary
This passage defines 'bayān al-taghayyur' (clarification by alteration), where the meaning of the original statement is changed by subsequent clarification. The two primary examples are ta'līq (conditional statements) and istithnā' (exceptions). The author then presents a famous dispute between the Hanafis and al-Shāfi'ī regarding the effect of conditions on legal causes. For the Hanafis, when someone says 'You are free if you enter the house', the declaration 'You are free' does not become a legal cause (sabab) for emancipation until the condition is fulfilled; it has no effect before that. The Shāfi'ī view, attributed to al-Shāfi'ī, holds that the declaration is a cause in the present moment (immediately effective as a cause), but the condition acts as a preventer (māni') that blocks the effect until the condition occurs. The practical difference (fā'idat al-khilāf) lies in intermediate rulings: for example, if the condition is impossible or the declarant dies before the condition, the Hanafis would rule that no divorce or emancipation occurs, while the Shāfi'īs might consider it effective because the cause already existed. The commentary (hashiyah) elaborates on each position. It explains ta'līq using the example of emancipation: the phrase 'You are free' normally necessitates immediate freedom, but when followed by a condition, the effect is delayed. Similarly, exception (istithnā') changes the obligation from full to partial. The Hanafi argument is that the declaration does not even become a cause until the condition exists, likening it to an utterance that never reaches its proper place (mahall). The Shāfi'ī argument is that the declaration is already a cause but its effect is suspended by the condition. This disagreement reflects deeper usulî differences about the temporal relationship between cause and effect.

التعليق (al-ta'līq): A conditional statement where the effect is suspended on a future condition, e.g., 'If you enter the house, you are divorced.'
الاستثناء (al-istithnā'): An exception that reduces the scope of a statement, e.g., 'I owe a thousand except one hundred.'

Absence of Condition Is Not a Preventer: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Explains why absence of condition does not act as a preventer in Hanafi usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that absence of a condition does not prevent a ruling from being established by other textual evidence, and evaluate the Shafi'i counterargument based on mafhum al-mukhalafa.

Core Idea
Absence of a condition does not block a ruling if other texts support it.

This lesson tackles a foundational usul disagreement: when the Qur'an conditions a ruling on a specific situation (e.g., marrying a slave woman only when unable to marry a free woman), does the absence of that condition absolutely negate the ruling, or does it merely show that this particular text does not apply? The Hanafis adopt the latter view, while the Shafi'is accept the implication of the opposite (mafhum al-mukhalafa). We will work through two classic examples from the source text to see how this principle works in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two case examples
The text gives two cases: (1) marrying a slave woman when one can afford a free woman, and (2) maintenance (nafaqa) for a divorced woman who is not pregnant. In both, the Qur'an explicitly conditions the ruling: permission to marry a slave woman depends on inability to marry a free woman; maintenance for a divorced woman depends on her being pregnant.
These cases are used to illustrate the Shafi'i and Hanafi positions.
2
Contrast the Shafi'i and Hanafi reasoning
According to al-Shafi'i, the conditioned verses imply that when the condition is absent, the opposite ruling applies: e.g., if you can afford a free woman, you may not marry a slave woman. The Hanafis say the condition only specifies when this particular text applies; its absence does not negate the ruling altogether—other general texts may establish it.
The Shafi'i reasoning is based on mafhum al-mukhalafa.
3
Locate the underlying usul disagreement
The hashya clarifies that the dispute stems from the validity of mafhum al-mukhalafa. Hanafis generally reject it as a source of law, while Shafi'is accept it. Hence, the condition in the Qur'an is seen by Hanafis as merely a restriction on the scope of that specific text, not as a universal prohibition.
This is a root difference in usul methodology.
4
Apply the Hanafi principle to each case
For the slave marriage case: the general verse 'Marry what seems good to you of women' (Q. 4:3) permits marriage to any woman, including slave women, unless a specific prohibition applies. The conditioned verse (Q. 4:25) only addresses those who cannot afford free women; it does not forbid others. So a man who can afford a free woman may still marry a slave woman. For the nafaqa case: the general obligation to provide for divorced women (Q. 2:241 or Q. 65:6 general part) applies; the condition of pregnancy only specifies that pregnancy triggers a special obligation, not that non-pregnancy removes all obligation.
The Hanafi position is that the ruling can be established by other evidence (general verses).
5
State the practical difference (thamarah)
The practical outcome: Hanafi school allows a man who can afford a free woman to marry a slave woman; Shafi'i forbids it. Hanafi school allows a non-pregnant divorced woman to receive nafaqa from other sources (e.g., mutual agreement or general maintenance); Shafi'i gives her none.
This shows the real-world impact of the usul disagreement.

The Ta'yin al-Niyyah Dispute: Ramadan vs Qada
A Hanafi response to Shafi'i qiyas on intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a shari' specification (ta'yin shar'i) removes the need for the servant's specific intention, and distinguish when ta'yin is required.

Core Idea
When shariah specifies, the servant need not; when shariah is silent, the servant must specify.

In this passage, the Hanafi school responds to a Shafi'i objection about intention in Ramadan fasting. The Shafi'is argue by qiyas that just as qada fasts require specific intention, so does Ramadan, since both are fard. The Hanafis counter that shariah itself provides the specification for Ramadan via a hadith, so the worshipper's conscious specification is unnecessary. Understanding this dispute illuminates a key usul principle: the interaction between divine specification and human intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Shafi'i claim
What is the Shafi'i argument? They state that Ramadan fasting, being a compulsory fast (fard), requires specific intention (ta'yin al-niyyah) just like qada fasting. The basis is qiyas: both are fard fasts, so they should share the same rule.
The Shafi'i position is that intention must be directed to the specific fast (Ramadan) for it to be valid.
2
Extract the Hanafi counter-dalil
The Hanafis respond by citing a hadith: 'When Shaban passes, there is no fast except for Ramadan.' They argue that this provides a specification from shariah that the only possible fast after Shaban is Ramadan. Thus the time itself indicates the specific fast, and the worshipper's own specification is not needed.
This is a naqli (textual) proof that overrides the qiyas.
3
Analyze the distinction from qada
Why does qada still require ta'yin? Because there is no hadith or other shari' evidence that specifies which qada fast is being performed. The time does not automatically determine the makeup fast; the servant must consciously intend which missed day he is replacing.
This distinction is based on the presence or absence of a shari' specification.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: In the Hanafi school, if someone in Ramadan merely intends to fast without specifying Ramadan, the fast is valid as a Ramadan fast. In the Shafi'i school, such an intention would not count for Ramadan; the fast would be invalid as a fard and might become nafl.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary of Usul al-Shashi, explaining the chapter on 'Directed Questions' (al-as'ilah al-mutawajjihah). These are objections raised by a questioner against an opponent's legal analogy. The text defines and illustrates five main types: 1) Qalb (reversal) – the questioner turns the opponent's own analogy against him by using the same principle to derive the opposite ruling. 2) 'Aks (inverse) – the questioner uses the opponent's original analog to prove the opposite of what the opponent claims, forcing the opponent to differentiate between the original and the branch. 3) Fasad al-Wad' (invalidity of the description) – the questioner shows that the description chosen as the cause is not suitable because it has an effect opposite to the claimed ruling, based on a clear text or consensus. 4) Naqd (refutation) – the questioner shows that the cause exists (e.g., purity) but the claimed ruling (e.g., requirement of intention) is absent in other cases (e.g., washing a garment or vessel), thus undermining the cause. 5) Mu'arada (counter-argument) – the questioner presents an alternative analogy that conflicts with the opponent's ruling, preventing its establishment without directly invalidating the opponent's proof. The text also distinguishes between contradiction (tanaqud) which invalidates the proof itself, and counter-argument which only blocks the ruling. The commentary includes examples from Hanafi fiqh, such as the dispute about zakat on women's jewelry, the effect of Islam on marriage, the requirement of intention in wudu vs. washing a garment, and the argument about tayammum. The Hanafi position is consistently defended against Shafi'i objections, emphasizing that legal purity (tahara hukmiyya) is a non-intelligible meaning requiring intention, unlike tangible purity.

Key Terms
الأسولة المتوجهة (al-as'ilah al-mutawajjihah): Directed questions or objections raised by a questioner against an opponent's reasoning in a legal debate, aiming to refute or challenge the claimed analogy.
العكس (al-'aks): Linguistically: to return a thing to its first state. Terminologically: the questioner adheres to the opponent's original analog to argue for the opposite ruling, forcing the opponent to differentiate.
فساد الوضع (fasad al-wad'): Invalidity of the description: the questioner shows that the description chosen as the 'illa is inappropriate because it is proven to have an effect opposite to the claimed ruling by a text or ijma.
النقض (al-naqd): Refutation: existence of the claimed 'illa with absence of the ruling in another case, breaking the claimed causal link.
