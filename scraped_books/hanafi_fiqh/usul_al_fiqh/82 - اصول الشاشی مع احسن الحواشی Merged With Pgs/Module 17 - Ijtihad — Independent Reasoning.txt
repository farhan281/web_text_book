The Command of the Wise and the Reality of Good and Evil: A Hanafi–Māturīdī Perspective
Fasl on command, goodness, and the nature of moral value

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three meanings of ḥusn and qubḥ, identify the Māturīdī (Hanafi) position in the dispute over their third meaning, and explain the difference between the Māturīdī and Muʻtazilī views.

Core Idea
The command of the Wise indicates goodness, but whether goodness is rational or revealed is a defining usul dispute.

This fasl from al-Usul al-Shashi tackles a foundational question: does the very fact that Allah commands something prove that the act is good? The answer hinges on how we define 'goodness' (ḥusn) and 'evil' (qubḥ). The passage breaks down three meanings of these terms and locates the major theological-legal disagreement between the Ashʻarīs and the Māturīdīs (the school of the Hanafīs). Understanding this dispute is critical for grasping the Hanafī theory of value and obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn and its core claim
The matn states: 'The command for something indicates the goodness of the commanded thing if the commander is wise.' The commentary expands on who qualifies as a wise commander and why wisdom precludes commanding evil.
Note that the claim is conditional on the commander being wise (ḥakīm).
2
Define the three meanings of ḥusn and qubḥ
The passage lists three meanings: (1) perfection vs. deficiency, (2) suitability to worldly purpose vs. opposition, (3) deserving praise/reward or blame/punishment. The first two are agreed to be rational. Only the third is disputed.
Memorise these three meanings; they recur in usul discussions.
3
Locate the dispute on the third meaning
The Ashʻarī position: the third meaning is purely sharʻī (defined by revelation). The Māturīdī (Hanafī) and Muʻtazilī position: it is ʻaqlī (rational/real). Understand the evidence each side presents.
Pay attention to the nuance: Māturīdīs and Muʻtazila agree on reality but differ on the consequences.
4
Distinguish the Māturīdī view from the Muʻtazilī view
The passage explicitly differentiates: Māturīdīs hold that inherent goodness/evil does not necessitate a divine ruling; Muʻtazila hold that it does. This is a critical nuance often missed.
This difference has practical implications for the status of acts before revelation (whether obligation can be rational).
5
Synthesise the Hanafī-Māturīdī position
The Hanafī school (Māturīdī theology) affirms that acts have real, rational moral qualities. Revelation discloses these qualities; it does not create them arbitrarily. However, Allah is not obliged to command the good or prohibit the evil — He does so by His wisdom and will.
This position balances rationalism with divine sovereignty.

Because the command is to indicate that the commanded thing ought to exist, so that necessitates its goodness, and the wise one does not seek the existence of the ugly because it contradicts wisdom. (1) He said: [Hakiman... etc.] meaning: a possessor of wisdom who does not command people except with wisdom and does not command foolishness. And it only necessitates the goodness of the commanded act because the command by definition is a request, and the wise one only requests acts that have wisdom and benefit, which is the good. And 'good' and 'evil' are used in three meanings: first: the attribute of perfection and deficiency like knowledge and ignorance, justice and injustice, courage and cowardice; second: suitability to worldly purpose and its opposition – both these meanings are rational, not shar'i, by agreement; third: deserving praise and reward or blame and punishment, and it is in this that there is dispute. According to al-Ash'ari, it is shar'i: all acts like belief in Allah, disbelief, prayer, adultery, etc., before revelation are equal; no act deserves reward or punishment. The Lawgiver made some deserving reward and commanded them, and some deserving punishment and prohibited them. According to us, i.e., the Maturidis, and the Mu'tazila, it is rational and real, not dependent on revelation: an act is good or evil in reality without consideration of any judge. So what is good, the Lawgiver commanded; what is evil, He prohibited, because the commander is wise and does not command indecency. Thus the Lawgiver revealed the good and evil inherent in acts, just as medicine reveals the benefit and harm inherent in drugs. Intellect may fathom real good and evil, like the goodness of truthful speech and the evil of lying harm, or may not, like the goodness of fasting on the last day of Ramadan and the evil of fasting on the first day of Shawwal – intellect has no means to these, but revelation reveals them. The difference between our school and the Mu'tazila is: according to us, the goodness and evil of acts do not necessitate a judgment from Allah, but rather become a cause for deserving judgment from Allah who does not prefer the inferior. According to the Mu'tazila, goodness and evil necessitate judgment; even without revelation, acts and agents would necessitate judgments. So an act suitable for permissibility would be absolutely permissible, and so on. This is what the verifiers have stated, and the proofs of the difference are detailed in the longer works. (2) He said: [So it necessitated its goodness. They differed whether the goodness of the commanded thing is shar'i or rational. And some said it is rational, i.e., its goodness is known rationally.]

Commentary
This passage is a usul al-fiqh dispute about whether the command of a wise person indicates the inherent goodness of the commanded act, and whether goodness (ḥusn) and evil (qubḥ) are determined by revelation (sharʻī) or by reason (ʻaqlī). The author begins by stating the matn: "The command for something indicates the goodness of the commanded thing if the commander is wise." The reason is that a wise commander commands only what is good, as evil contradicts wisdom. The commentary then defines three meanings of ḥusn and qubḥ: (1) perfection vs. deficiency (e.g., knowledge vs. ignorance) – rational by agreement; (2) suitability to worldly aims vs. opposition – also rational by agreement; (3) deserving praise/reward or blame/punishment – this is where the dispute lies. The Ashʻarīs hold that this meaning is purely sharʻī: before revelation, acts are neutral; the Lawgiver arbitrarily assigns ḥusn and qubḥ by command and prohibition. If He commanded evil, it would become good. The Māturīdīs (our school) and Muʻtazila hold that ḥusn and qubḥ in this sense are real and rational: acts inherently deserve reward or punishment, and the Lawgiver reveals this inherent nature. However, the Māturīdīs differ from the Muʻtazila in that they do not say that inherent goodness or evil necessitates a judgment from Allah; rather, it is a ground for deserving judgment, but Allah is not obliged. The Muʻtazila say that the act itself necessitates the ruling even without revelation. This passage is crucial for understanding the Hanafi-Māturīdī epistemology of moral value and its distinction from both Ashʻarism and extreme rationalism.

Key Terms
الحسن (al-ḥusn): Goodness: has three meanings: (1) perfection, (2) suitability to worldly purpose, (3) deserving praise/reward. The third is disputed.
القبح (al-qubḥ): Evil: opposite of ḥusn; three meanings with third meaning disputed.
أمر حكيم (amr ḥakīm): Command of a wise one: refers to Allah, His Messenger, just rulers, and qualified scholars; not oppressors or fools.

Obligation to Seek Ruling from Quran First, Then Sunnah
States the obligation of the mujtahid to seek ruling from Quran then Sunnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hierarchical order of textual sources in Hanafi usul and explain why the Quran is given precedence over the Sunnah and why authentic hadith are given precedence over qiyas.

Core Idea
The mujtahid must first seek the ruling from the Quran; then from the Sunnah; only then other sources.

This lesson establishes a fundamental rule of Hanafi usul: the mandatory hierarchy of sources. When a new incident occurs, the mujtahid must begin with the Quran, the strongest and definitive divine speech. Only if no ruling is found there, does he proceed to the Sunnah. This order is not optional but wajib, and the commentator strongly warns against those who reverse this priority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mandatory first source
From the text, the first source is the Quran. The mujtahid must seek the ruling there before any other source.
The Quran is given precedence because it is the strongest evidence (aqwa al-dalail) and definitive (qati).
2
Understand why Quran is primary
The Quran is qati in transmission (thubut) and in its indication (dalala), being divine speech. This makes it superior to the Sunnah which is zanni in transmission for ahad hadith.
3
Recognize the hierarchy after Quran
After the Quran, the well-known (mashhura) Sunnah, then ahad hadith. Mutawatir hadith is like the Quran. Even authentic ahad hadith are given precedence over qiyas.
The text also mentions that mursal, munqati', and even weak hadith are preferred over personal opinion.
4
Identify the polemical context
The commentator criticizes the Zahiriyya (literalists) who prioritize hadith over Quran. The Hanafi position defends the primacy of the Quran.
This reflects a historical debate about source hierarchy.

Commentary
This passage is an usul rule stating the obligation of the mujtahid when a new incident arises. The author of the hashiya expands on the matn by explaining the hierarchy of sources: first the Quran, then the Sunnah. The commentary strongly criticizes those who prioritize hadith over the Quran, identifying them as 'literalists' (zahiriyya) of the time who are actually misguided. The rule is that the mujtahid must seek the ruling from the Quran first; if he finds it there, he does not go beyond it because the Quran is the strongest evidence (aqwa al-dalail), being definitive (qati) and divine speech. Then after the Quran, the well-known (mashhura) Sunnah, then ahad hadith. Mutawatir hadith is treated like the Quran. Authentic ahad hadith are given precedence over qiyas (analogical reasoning). Even mursal and munqati' hadith, and weak hadith according to some narrations, are preferred over personal opinion (ra'y). The commentary also defines ijtihad: it is exertion of effort to derive rulings, and the mujtahid must have comprehensive knowledge of the Quran and Sunnah in terms of language, legal principles, and the various types of indications. This is a typical Hanafi usul position emphasizing textual primacy.

الظاهرية (al-zahiriyya): A group that prioritizes hadith over Quran, called 'non-muqallids' and 'unitarians', described as misguided.

Resolving Conflicting Evidences: The Hanafi Hierarchy and Taharri
Methodology for a mujtahid when faced with conflicting Quranic verses, hadiths, or analogies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi hierarchy for resolving conflicting evidences (Quranic verses, hadiths, analogies) and distinguish the Hanafi requirement of taharri from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
When evidences conflict, a Hanafi mujtahid follows a strict hierarchy: Quran→Sunnah→Companions→Qiyas, and for conflicting qiyas, he performs taharri.

When two evidences conflict, the mujtahid must determine which to follow. The Hanafi school provides a clear hierarchy, moving from the Quran to the Sunnah, then to the Companions' reports, then to analogy. For two conflicting analogies, there is no external source, so the mujtahid must seek inner guidance (taharri). This lesson unpacks that methodology and the debate with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of conflict
Determine whether the conflict is between two Quranic verses, two hadiths, or two analogies. Each case has a different next step.
The hierarchy is sequential: you only move to the next source if the conflict cannot be resolved at the current level.
2
Apply the correct source
If Quranic verses conflict, incline to the Sunnah because it interprets the Quran. If hadiths conflict, incline to the reports of the Companions and then to sound analogy. If analogies conflict, perform taharri (seek the heart's inclination) and act on one.
For hadiths, the Companions' reports (athar) are preferred over analogy, unless a clear analogy overrides them.
3
Understand the underlying reasoning
The Sunnah is the strongest proof after the Quran. The Companions' reports are preferred because they witnessed revelation. Taharri is used only when no external evidence remains.
The hadith 'Fear the intuition of the believer' is cited to justify taharri.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that when two analogies conflict, the mujtahid may choose any one arbitrarily because each is a proof. The Hanafi school requires taharri because the heart's inclination is itself a form of guidance.

The Irreversibility of Taharrī Confirmed by Action

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Once a taharrī is acted upon, it cannot be undone by a later taharrī, except for matters like qibla where change is possible.

This lesson explores a key principle in Hanafi usul: the stability of rulings derived from ijtihad after they have been acted upon. The passage you are about to read clarifies that a taharrī-based decision, once accompanied by action, gains a form of legal finality that a mere change of opinion cannot reverse. An important exception to this rule is the qibla direction, which is inherently subject to change through fresh ijtihad.
