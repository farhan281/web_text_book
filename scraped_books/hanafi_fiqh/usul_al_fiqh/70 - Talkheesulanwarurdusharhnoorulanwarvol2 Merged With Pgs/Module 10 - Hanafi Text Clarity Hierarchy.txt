Zahir: Definition and Ruling in Hanafi Usul
Introduces second division of Quran and defines Zahir with its ruling and example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Zahir as per Hanafi usul, state its ruling (wajib al-amal qat'an), and distinguish it from Nass.

Core Idea
Zahir: clear from wording; acting upon it is obligatory with certainty.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi classification of Quranic texts by clarity. The first category, Zahir, is defined as speech whose intended meaning is apparent from its wording alone. The author explains that acting upon this apparent meaning is obligatory and certain (qat'i). We also begin to see the next category, Nass, which is even clearer but from the speaker's intention, not the wording.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories of clarity
The text states the second division of the Book of Allah yields four: Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, Muhkam. Note the hierarchy.
This classification is unique to Hanafi usul.
2
Define Zahir
From the Arabic: 'اسم الكلام ظهر المراد به للسامع بصيغته' — the intended meaning is apparent from its wording. Understand that 'sighah' refers to the linguistic form.
3
State the ruling of Zahir
'وجوب العمل بالذي ظهر معناه على سبيل القطع واليقين' — acting upon it is obligatory with certainty. In Hanafi usul, Zahir is qat'i al-dalala.
This means denial of a ruling derived from a Zahir text may be kufr if the ruling is definitive.
4
Analyze the example
The verse 'Ahallallahu al-bay'a wa harrama al-riba' is clear in indicating permissibility of trade and prohibition of usury. The wording directly shows the ruling.
This verse is considered Zahir in the permissibility of trade and prohibition of riba.
5
Distinguish Zahir from Nass
Nass is clearer, but its clarity comes from the speaker's intention (qarina), not the wording. This distinction is unique to Hanafis.
The definition of Nass in the passage is incomplete; the full definition includes that Nass has an additional indication from the speaker's context.

Az-Zahir: Definition and Ruling in Hanafi Usul
Defining zahir as text clear from its wording and stating its ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define az-Zahir, state its ruling (wujub al-amal bil-qat'i wal-yaqin), and apply the definition to identify zahir texts.

Core Idea
Zahir: a text clear from its wording; acting on it is obligatory and definitive.

In Hanafi usul, texts are classified by clarity. The first level is az-Zahir, where the meaning is apparent from the wording itself, requiring no external context. This lesson unpacks the definition and the binding ruling that follows, setting the foundation for higher levels of clarity.

Guided Path
1
Parse the definition of zahir
Break down the Arabic definition: 'az-Zahir is the utterance whose intended meaning is apparent to the listener by its wording (sighah).' Note that the clarity comes from the wording itself, not from context or external evidence.
The term 'sighah' refers to the linguistic form.
2
Identify the ruling (hukm)
The passage states: 'Its ruling is the obligation to act upon the meaning that appears, in a definitive and certain manner.' This means that when a text is zahir, its apparent ruling must be followed with certainty (qat'i), not merely as a probability.
This does not preclude the possibility of takhsis (specification) by a stronger text.
3
Apply the example
The verse 'Allah has permitted trade and prohibited usury' is given as an example. It is zahir in indicating that trade is permissible and usury is prohibited. The apparent meaning is clear from the words 'halala' and 'harrama'.
This does not mean all trade is permitted; it is open to specification (e.g., prohibited trade like gharar).
4
Distinguish from higher levels
In Hanafi usul, zahir is the lowest level of clarity. Unlike nass, mufassar, or muhkam, zahir can be subject to ta'wil (interpretation) or takhsis by another evidence. The ruling is nevertheless definitive until a specific exclusion is proven.
Memorize the hierarchy: zahir → nass → mufassar → muhkam.

Nass: Definition and Ruling
Defining nass as clearer than zahir due to speaker's intention, not wording

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define nass, distinguish it from zahir based on the source of clarity, and apply its ruling of obligatory action with possible tawil.

Core Idea
Nass gains clarity from speaker's intent, not wording, yet still permits interpretation.

The Hanafi classification of texts begins with zahir and moves to nass. While zahir derives clarity from its wording, nass achieves greater clarity through an external indication from the speaker's context. This lesson defines nass and explains its legal ruling, using the Quranic verse on polygyny as a key example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its definition
Locate the definition of nass in the passage. Note that it is defined by its increased clarity over zahir and the source of that clarity (the speaker's meaning, not the wording).
The Arabic phrase 'بمعنى من المتكلم لا في نفس الصيغة' is crucial.
2
Understand the source of clarity
Contrast nass with zahir: zahir is clear due to the wording itself; nass is clearer because of an external contextual clue from the speaker. The example of the marriage verse shows how the Companions' inquiry provided that context.
3
Grasp the ruling of nass
The passage states its ruling: 'وجوب العمل بما وضح' – it is obligatory to act on what is made clear. However, this is not absolute; the possibility of tawil (interpretation) remains within the scope of metaphorical usage.
Tawil can be specification for general nass or figurative interpretation for literal nass.
4
Apply to the given example
Analyze the verse 'فانكحوا ما طاب لكم من النساء مثنى وثلاث ورباع'. The verse is zahir on permissibility of marriage and nass on the number of wives because it was revealed in response to a question about quantity. This dual classification illustrates how context elevates clarity.

Al-Mufassar: Definition and Ruling
Defining mufassar as text even clearer than nass, excluding interpretation and specification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-Mufassar, distinguish it from nass, and state its ruling according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Mufassar is so clear that neither interpretation nor specification is possible, yet abrogation remains a possibility.

Having understood nass as a text with a clear meaning but open to interpretation, we now ascend to the third level: al-Mufassar. This category represents a text so explicit that no possible interpretation or specification can alter its meaning. However, unlike muhkam, it can still be abrogated by a later revelation. This lesson defines mufassar and its ruling.

Guided Path
1
Position mufassar in the hierarchy
Recall that in Hanafi usul, texts are ranked by clarity: zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam. Mufassar is clearer than nass but not as permanent as muhkam.
Muhkam is the highest, not susceptible to abrogation.
2
Define mufassar linguistically
The term 'mufassar' comes from 'tafsir', meaning to explain. It is a text that expounds itself clearly.
3
Identify the exclusions
Mufassar excludes the possibility of tawil (interpretation) and takhsis (specification). This means its meaning is unambiguous and cannot be restricted or reinterpreted.
Tawil is explaining a text in a way contrary to its apparent meaning; takhsis is excluding some instances from a general ruling.
4
Identify what remains
Despite its clarity, mufassar remains open to naskh (abrogation) by a later revealed text of equal or higher authority.
Naskh can only occur through revelation, not through ijtihad.
5
State the ruling
The ruling of mufassar is that it is obligatory to act upon it (wajib al-'amal bihi) as long as no abrogating text is established.
This obligation is immediate and binding.

Definition and Ruling of Mufassar
Defines Mufassar and its ruling with an example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Mufassar, distinguish it from Nass and Muhkam, and apply its ruling with the possibility of abrogation.

Core Idea
Mufassar text is so clear that ta'wil and takhsis are impossible — but it can still be abrogated.

In the Hanafi hierarchy of clear texts, Mufassar occupies the third level above Nass. It is a text whose clarity is so heightened that no possibility of reinterpretation (ta'wil) or specification (takhsis) remains. Yet, unlike Muhkam, it remains susceptible to abrogation (naskh). This lesson unpacks the definition, ruling, and a Quranic example that illustrates how a text progresses from Zahir to Nass to Mufassar.

Guided Path
1
State the definition of Mufassar
Read the Arabic definition: 'ما ازداد وضوحاً على النص على وجه لا يبقى معه احتمال التاويل والتخصيص.' Identify the key conditions: increased clarity over Nass, and no possibility of ta'wil or takhsis.
Mufassar is clearer than Nass, but not as definitive as Muhkam.
2
Identify its position in the Hanafi hierarchy
Hanafi usul ranks texts: Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, Muhkam. Mufassar is above Nass because it excludes ta'wil and takhsis.
Other schools may have different hierarchies.
3
Note the ruling (hukm) of Mufassar
The text states: 'وحكمه وجوب العمل به على احتمال النسخ' – acting upon it is obligatory, but the possibility of abrogation remains.
This distinguishes it from Muhkam, which cannot be abrogated.
4
Analyze the Quranic example
The verse 'فسجد الملائكة كلهم اجمعون' is used. Initially 'الملائكة' is Zahir, then 'كلهم' removes the possibility that some angels didn't prostrate, and 'اجمعون' removes the possibility of non-simultaneity. Thus it becomes Mufassar.
Each qualifying word adds clarity, eliminating interpretive possibilities.
5
Distinguish Mufassar from Muhkam
Both have no possibility of ta'wil or takhsis, but Mufassar can be abrogated, while Muhkam is definitive and immune to naskh.
Mufassar is still subject to historical abrogation if a later text overrides it.

Commentary
This passage defines the third level in the Hanafi hierarchy of clear texts: Mufassar. The author defines Mufassar as text that is clearer than Nass to an extent that no possibility of ta'wil (reinterpretation) or takhsis (specification) remains. However, unlike Muhkam, Mufassar can still be abrogated (naskh). The ruling is that acting upon it is obligatory, but the possibility of abrogation remains. The example given is the verse about the angels prostrating to Adam. Initially, the verse is Zahir (clear but open to interpretation) because it mentions the angels prostrating, but it does not specify which angels. Then 'kulluhum' (all of them) clarifies that all angels prostrated, removing the possibility that some did not. Finally, 'ajma'un' (together) removes the possibility that they prostrated at different times. Thus the verse becomes Mufassar, leaving no room for ta'wil or takhsis, but it could still be abrogated by a later revelation. Understanding Mushkil: Definition and Ruling
Defining the usul term mushkil and its legal ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mushkil in Hanafi usul, identify its distinguishing features, and apply the prescribed method for resolving its ambiguity.

Core Idea
Mushkil is a text whose ambiguity comes from resemblance to multiple meanings; the scholar must first assume the literal meaning, then investigate until clarity.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, texts are categorized by clarity. The category of mushkil represents an intermediate level of obscurity: it is ambiguous because it resembles many other possible interpretations (ashkal). Unlike mujmal, which lacks detail, mushkil's ambiguity is inherent in its form. Understanding its definition and the prescribed method of dealing with it is crucial for proper ijtihad.

Guided Path
1
Define mushkil literally and technically
The Arabic root shakl means 'form'. Literally, mushkil means 'that which enters into ashkal (similar forms)'. Technically, it is a text that is ambiguous because it resembles many other possible interpretations, making it difficult to determine the intended meaning without investigation. Note the contrast with khafi: khafi's ambiguity is due to an external cause; mushkil's ambiguity is inherent in the text's similarity to other forms.
Contrast with khafi: khafi's ambiguity is due to an external cause; mushkil's ambiguity is inherent in the text's similarity to other forms.
2
Identify the cause (sabab) of mushkil
The cause of mushkil is the multiple possible forms (ashkal) that the text resembles. The text itself is not uniquely clear; it shares its form with other meanings. For example, the Arabic word 'qara'a' can mean both 'to read' and 'to collect'. Such a word becomes mushkil when context does not immediately resolve the ambiguity.
The condition (shart) is that the text is mixed with similar forms; if it has a unique unambiguous form, it is not mushkil.
3

The ruling is two-part: first, assume the literal meaning (haqiqah) as the intended one; second, engage in seeking (talab) and deep reflection (ta'ammul) until the intended meaning becomes clear. This is a procedural duty for the mujtahid. It is not permissible to abandon the literal meaning without evidence or to jump to qiyas prematurely.
The hadith 'al-bayyi'ani bi al-khiyar ma lam yatafarraqa' is an example: assume literal physical separation first, then investigate if separation of speech is intended.
4
Distinguish mushkil from other categories
In the Hanafi hierarchy of clarity, mushkil is above mujmal (ambiguous due to lack of detail) and below nass (clear text). It is similar to khafi but the cause of ambiguity differs. While both require ijtihad, mushkil often requires deeper linguistic analysis because the ambiguity is rooted in the word's multiple possible meanings.
A common mistake is confusing mushkil with mujmal. Re-read the definition: mushkil's ambiguity comes from resemblance, not from lack of detail.

Mutashabih: Definition and Types
Defines mutashabih, its ruling, and types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mutashabih according to Hanafi usul, distinguish its two types, and apply the legal ruling of tafwid when encountering such texts.

Core Idea
Mutashabih texts must be accepted as true without seeking their intended meaning — only Allah knows it.

This passage introduces the important concept of mutashabih in Hanafi usul. As students of Islamic legal theory, you must know which Quranic and hadith texts fall into this category and how to treat them legally. The ruling is not to attempt interpretation but to believe them as true. We will break down the two subtypes: mutashabih al-ma'na and mutashabih al-murad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of mutashabih
Locate the key phrase 'ma inqata'a raja' ma'rifat al-murad minhu' – hope of knowing its intended meaning is cut off. Understand that this implies complete inaccessibility.
The term 'mutashabih' literally means 'resembling' but here takes a technical sense.
2
State the ruling (hukm) of mutashabih
The ruling is 'i'tiqad al-haqiqah qabl al-isabah' – to believe it is true before understanding it correctly. This means affirming its truth without requiring knowledge of how.
This contrasts with other categories where understanding is possible.
3
Distinguish the two types
Identify mutashabih al-ma'na (meaning itself unknown, e.g., huruf muqatta'at) and mutashabih al-murad (lexical meaning known but intended meaning known only to Allah, e.g., yadullah).
In mutashabih al-murad, the language is clear but referent is hidden.
4
Locate mutashabih in the hierarchy of textual ambiguity
From the passage, note that mutashabih is contrasted with mushkil (hidden due to external cause, solvable by reflection) and mujmal (hidden due to wording, but explanation exists). Mutashabih has no explanation available.
This hierarchy is key in Hanafi usul for determining the strength of evidence.

As for mutashabih, it is a term for that which hope of knowing its intended meaning is cut off. Its ruling is to believe in its truth before [reaching] its correct understanding. Example: like the mutashabihaat; hope of reaching its intended meaning is completely cut off; it is necessary to have belief in its truth. Mutashabih has two types: (1) mutashabih al-ma'na: that whose meaning is not known at all, like the isolated letters (huruf muqatta'at). (2) mutashabih al-murad: that whose meaning is known but its intended meaning is not known except to Allah, like the intended meaning of 'yadullah', 'wajhullah', and similar words. By consideration of hiddenness (khafa), the reason for [classifying] the second type is due to an external cause not the wording. If [the hiddenness] can be perceived through reflection, it is mushkil. If its meaning is hidden due to the wording itself, and explanation exists from the speaker, it is mujmal. If not [i.e., no explanation exists], then it is mutashabih.

Commentary
This passage defines mutashabih (ambiguous/unclear text) according to Hanafi usul. Mutashabih is text whose intended meaning is completely inaccessible to human understanding. The ruling is to believe it as true (since it is from Allah) but not to attempt to interpret it literally or ask about its modality. Two types: (1) Mutashabih al-ma'na: meaning itself is unknown, e.g., the disjointed letters at the start of some Quranic surahs (al-huruf al-muqatta'ah). (2) Mutashabih al-murad: the lexical meaning is known (e.g., 'yad' means hand) but the intended meaning (Allah's attribute) is known only to Him. The passage then presents a classification based on hiddenness (khafa): if hidden due to external cause and can be understood by reflection, it is mushkil (difficult); if hidden due to wording itself and explanation exists, it is mujmal; if no explanation exists, it is mutashabih. This hierarchy is important in Hanafi usul for determining the strength of textual evidence.

Key Terms
متشابه (mutashabih): Text for which hope of knowing its intended meaning is cut off.
متشابه المعنی (mutashabih al-ma'na): That whose meaning is not known at all, e.g., huruf muqatta'at.
متشابه المراد (mutashabih al-murad): That whose lexical meaning is known but intended meaning is known only to Allah.

Definition of Sarih (Explicit Speech)
Defines sarih in Hanafi usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given expression is sarih or kinayah based on clarity of meaning.

Core Idea
Sarih speech is unmistakably clear in meaning, whether literal or metaphorical.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, classifying words as sarih (explicit) or kinayah (allusive) is critical for deriving rulings. This lesson defines sarih precisely, showing that clarity of meaning—not literalness—is the key feature. Understanding this prevents misapplication when interpreting legal texts.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition components
Read the Arabic definition: sarih is speech whose intended meaning appears with clear manifestation (zuhur bayyin). Note that it includes both haqiqah and majaz.
The word 'zuhur' signifies manifest clarity, not hidden meaning.
2
Distinguish sarih from kinayah
Sarih is the opposite of kinayah (allusion). In kinayah, the intended meaning is not immediately clear and requires context. Sarih requires no such inference.
For example, 'He divorced' is sarih for divorce, whereas 'He is on his own' might be kinayah.
3
Recognize the scope: haqiqah and majaz
The definition explicitly includes both literal (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz) uses, as long as the meaning is clearly apparent. Metaphor is not a barrier to being sarih.
A common mistake is to think sarih only applies to literal statements.
4
Apply to a sample text
Consider the Quranic verse 'فاغسلوا وجوهكم' (So wash your faces). The command is sarih because the intended meaning—obligation to wash—is unmistakable.
This shows how sarih triggers immediate ruling (wujub) without further interpretation.
