The Four Divisions of Words in Usul al-Fiqh
Listing four classification systems of words

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify words according to the four Hanafi usul al-fiqh divisions: scope, clarity, literal/figurative nature, and method of inference.

Core Idea
Words in usul are classified by scope, clarity, usage, and inference method—each revealing a layer of legal meaning.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the precise meaning of words is the gateway to deriving rulings. This lesson introduces four essential classification systems that categorize words based on how they convey legal meaning. Master these divisions and you unlock the jurist's toolkit for analyzing the Quran, Sunnah, and other sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four divisions
The passage lists four distinct categorization systems. The first is based on scope (khass/amm/mushtarak/mu'awwal), the second on clarity (zahir/nass/mufassar/muhkam and their opposites), the third on literal vs. figurative (haqiqah/majaz/sarih/kinayah), and the fourth on method of inference from texts (ibarah/isharah/dalalah/iqtida).
Memorize these four categories—they are the backbone of Hanafi textual analysis.
2
Learn the types under each division
Under scope: specific (khass), general ('amm), shared (mushtarak), and interpreted (mu'awwal). Under clarity: apparent (zahir), explicit (nass), clearly defined (mufassar), and firm (muhkam); their opposites are hidden (khafi), difficult (mushkil), ambiguous (mujmal), and mutashabih. Under usage: literal (haqiqah), metaphorical (majaz), explicit (sarih), and allusive (kinayah). Under inference: by text (ibarah), by indication (isharah), by signification (dalalah), by requirement (iqtida).
Some lists include three or five—Hanafis adopt four for each division.
3
Understand the linking rationale
The final sentence explains that the purpose of these divisions is to classify how the mujtahid extracts rulings: the source is either revelation (Quran, Sunnah) or non-revelation (Ijma, Qiyas), and the words are either recited (matluw) or non-recited. This connects classification to practical istinbat.
This rationale justifies why the divisions are set the way they are.
4
Apply the classification to a sample word
Take the word 'salah' from the Quran. It is general ('amm) in scope? Actually it's a common noun—mushtarak perhaps? In the context of 'aqimu al-salah' it is general. In clarity it is zahir. In usage it is haqiqah (literal). In inference it falls under ibarah al-nass.
This exercise trains you to see how the classifications operate together.

ظاہر (zahir): A word whose meaning is apparent but still open to the possibility of an alternative interpretation.

مفسر (mufassar): A word that is clearly defined, leaving no ambiguity in its meaning.
محکم (muhkam): A word that is firmly established and not subject to abrogation.

مجاز (majaz): Figurative usage of a word; it is carried away from its original meaning.
صریح (sarih): A word that is explicit and unambiguous.
کنایہ (kinayah): A word that alludes to something indirectly, not explicitly.

استدلال باشارة النص (istidlal bi-isharat al-nass): Inference from the indication or implication of the text, not the explicit words.
استدلال بدلالته النص (istidlal bi-dalalat al-nass): Inference from the signification or connotation of the text.
استدلال باقتضاء النص (istidlal bi-iqtida' al-nass): Inference from what the text requires or necessitates for its own validity.

Qada Resembling Ada: A Legal Classification
Defines qada resembling ada with example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'qada resembling ada', distinguish its two aspects (haqiqatan and hukman), and apply the rule that catching the imam in ruku' counts as a full congregational rak'ah.

Core Idea
Qada that legally resembles ada is treated as ada—catching the imam in ruku' equals a full congregational rak'ah.

In Hanafi usul, the term 'qada' generally refers to making up a missed act. However, there is a special category called 'qada resembling ada' (قضاء مشابه بالا داء), where the missed act, though performed late, is legally considered as if it were performed on time. This lesson unpacks that definition and its practical application in congregational prayer.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of qada and ada
Qada is making up missed acts, ada is performing on time. The passage introduces a subtype where the two converge.
2
Identify the two dimensions: haqiqatan and hukman
The definition states that the resemblance can be in reality (haqiqatan) or in legal effect (hukman). The example given is only hukman.
Real resemblance would require the act to be performed within its time, which is impossible for qada.
3
Apply the example: catching the imam in ruku'
In Hanafi fiqh, if a person joins the imam while he is in ruku', that rak'ah is counted as performed in congregation, even though the person missed the beginning. This is qada resembling ada in legal effect.
The person's catch of ruku' is considered as if they performed the entire rak'ah on time for the purpose of congregation.

Definition of Mu'awwal: The Preponderant Homonym
Defining the interpreted word as a preponderant homonym

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mu'awwal, distinguish it from mujmal, and apply its classification in Hanafi usul analysis.

Core Idea
Mu'awwal is a homonym whose strongest meaning is known by preponderance, not certainty.

In Hanafi usul, understanding the degrees of textual clarity is essential. Mu'awwal is a key category: it is a word that originally carried multiple meanings, but through careful analysis one meaning becomes preponderant based on a strong indication (ghalib al-ra'y). This lesson unpacks the concise definition from our Urdu textbook.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Arabic definition
Locate the phrase: 'فما ترجح من المشترك بعض وجوهه بغالب الرأى'. This is the core definition.
The word 'ما' is mawsoolah; the predicate is implicit.
2
Break down the components
Decompose: 'مشترك' (homonym), 'ترجح' (became preponderant), 'بعض وجوهه' (some of its meanings), 'بغالب الرأى' (by strong opinion/preponderance of thought).
Ghalib al-ra'y is probabilistic, not certain.
3
Understand the process
The mu'awwal is the result of ta'weel: the scholar examines the mushtarak, applies qara'in (indicators), and one meaning outweighs others.
This is distinct from tawaqquf (suspending judgment) where no preference is found.
4
Distinguish from similar terms
Contrast with mujmal (no preferred meaning) and muhkam (clear and decisive). Mu'awwal is zanni (speculative) in dalalah.
5
Apply in usul
When analyzing a dalil, if the word is mu'awwal, the ruling derived is also zanni. Hanafi usul often relies on mu'awwal texts for non-qat'i obligations.

Haqiqat Musta'malah: Literal or Customary?
Defining haqiqat musta'malah and presenting the Hanafi-Sahibayn ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define haqiqat musta'malah, state its conditions, and compare the Hanafi and Sahibayn positions on whether haqiqah or majaz is primary.

Core Idea
When a literal meaning is easy to apply and not abandoned, Hanafi says it's primary; Sahibayn prefer the customary metaphor.

This lesson introduces the concept of haqiqat musta'malah, an important term in Hanafi usul al-fiqh that describes a word whose literal meaning remains operative because it is easy to act upon and has not been abandoned in custom. The passage from Talkhees al-Anwar also records a key intra-Hanafi dispute: Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the literal meaning (haqiqah) is primary, while his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, consider the customary metaphorical meaning (majaz) to take precedence. Understanding this disagreement helps us evaluate ambiguous expressions in the sources.

Guided Path
1
Define the term
Read the source Arabic carefully. Identify what 'haqiqat musta'malah' means: a literal meaning that is both easy to act upon and not abandoned in custom. Note that it is also called 'majaz muta'arraf'.
The term 'musta'malah' indicates that it is 'used' or 'operative'.
2
Analyze the conditions
Break down the definition into two conditions: (1) ease of acting upon the literal meaning, and (2) the literal meaning is not abandoned in custom. If either condition fails, the word becomes a metaphor (majaz).
These conditions form the shart and mani in the legal definition.
3
Map the intra-Hanafi dispute
Identify the two positions: Imam Abu Hanifa considers the literal meaning to be primary (awwal); the Sahibayn consider the metaphorical meaning to be primary. This disagreement arises from different weight given to original usage versus prevailing custom.
The dispute is not about whether both are possible, but which takes precedence when both are plausible.
4
Apply the dispute to an example
Consider a word like 'yad' (hand). Literally it means the physical hand; customarily it is used metaphorically to mean 'ownership' or 'control'. According to Hanafi, the literal meaning is primary; according to Sahibayn, the customary metaphorical meaning is primary if it is more prevalent.
This example illustrates the thamarah al-khilaf.
5
Evaluate the thamarah
Reflect on how this dispute impacts the interpretation of ambiguous expressions in the Qur'an and Sunnah. In Hanafi fiqh, when a word can be taken literally or metaphorically, the Imam's view leads to a literal interpretation unless custom has clearly abandoned it. The Sahibayn's view gives more weight to the widely understood meaning in custom.
This disagreement is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.

The Five Qara'in for Abandoning the Literal Meaning
Listing the five qara'in for leaving haqiqah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the five contextual indicators (qara'in) that necessitate abandoning the literal meaning (haqiqah) according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
The literal meaning is abandoned when custom, word-internal evidence, verbal context, speaker's state, or place of speech demand it.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, a word's literal meaning (haqiqah) is the default, but certain contextual indicators (qara'in) can require interpreting it metaphorically (majaz). This passage enumerates five such indicators, each rooted in linguistic or situational context. Understanding these qara'in is essential for correct istinbat (derivation of rulings) from revealed texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first qarinah: custom and habit
Consider words used in a community where a non-literal meaning is so common that the literal meaning is effectively abandoned. Example: 'zakat' in Islamic law normally means a specific charitable obligation, not its literal 'growth'.
This qarinah relies on 'urf (custom) as a source of interpretation.
2
Identify the second qarinah: internal evidence within the word
Look for clues within the word itself, such as its morphological or philological structure, that suggest the literal meaning is not intended. For instance, a verb form that implies exaggeration might indicate a metaphorical extension.
This is often linked to the word's own dalalah (indication).
3
Identify the third qarinah: verbal context (siyaq al-kalam)
Analyze the surrounding words and sentences. The overall discourse may make it clear that a literal reading is impossible or absurd. Example: 'And ask the village' (Quran 12:82) – the context shows 'people of the village' is meant.
Siyaq al-kalam is a key tool in tafsir and usul.
4
Identify the fourth qarinah: speaker's condition
Consider the speaker's state or circumstances. If the speaker is known for using hyperbole or is in a situation where literal meaning cannot apply, this indicates a metaphorical reading. Example: a person in a state of extreme anger saying 'I'm dying of rage' is not literally dying.
This qarinah depends on qarinat al-mutakallim (context of the speaker).
5
Identify the fifth qarinah: place of speech (mahall al-kalam)
The physical or situational context where the speech occurs can override literal meaning. For example, if a command is given in a mosque setting, the tone may imply spiritual rather than mundane intent.
Mahall al-kalam includes time, place, and audience.

These five are recognized in Hanafi usul as valid grounds for interpreting a word metaphorically.

Definition and Ruling of Sarih (Explicit Speech)
Defining sarih and its legal ruling that it binds without intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify sarih speech and explain that its legal effect occurs without requiring intention.

Core Idea
Sarih speech binds by utterance alone — intention is irrelevant.

In Hanafi usul, some words carry their meaning so clearly that the law treats the utterance as decisive. Learning Objective

Core Idea

In Hanafi usul, a dispensation (rukhsah) is a concession granted despite the presence of a prohibitive cause. However, not all dispensations are equal; some are stronger than others. Classification of Khabar: Mutawatir, Mashhur, Wahid
Classifies khabar based on chain completeness and certainty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given khabar as mutawatir, mashhur, or wahid by assessing its chain completeness (ittisal) and the certainty of its form and meaning (shabah).

Core Idea
A khabar's certainty is determined by its chain (ittisal) and the breadth of its certainty (shabah).

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, reports from the Prophet (ﷺ) are divided into three categories based on how they are transmitted. This lesson breaks down the classification into mutawatir, mashhur, and wahid using two key criteria: the completeness of the chain of narrators (ittisal) and the extent to which the report resembles tawatur (shabah). Understanding this taxonomy is essential for evaluating the probative value of hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first criterion: ittisal
Determine if the chain of narrators is complete (unbroken and numerous) or incomplete.
Ittisal refers to both continuity and sufficiency of narrators at each level.
2
If ittisal is complete, examine shabah
Shabah is the resemblance of the report to tawatur in certainty. Check whether both the form (sura) and meaning (ma'na) are certain, or only the meaning.
Form refers to exact wording; meaning refers to the conveyed message.
3
Classify as mutawatir if shabah in both form and meaning
If ittisal is complete and the report is certain in both wording and meaning, it is mutawatir yielding certain knowledge.
Mutawatir requires a very large number of narrators at each generation.
4
Classify as mashhur if shabah in meaning only
If ittisal is complete but only the meaning is certain (wordings vary), it is mashhur – stronger than ahad but weaker than mutawatir.
Mashhur is accepted by Hanafis as a source of near-certainty.
5
If ittisal is incomplete, classify as khabar wahid
If the chain lacks completeness (gaps or insufficient narrators), the report is wahid, requiring further scrutiny before being used in istinbat.
Wahid does not yield certain knowledge but may be acted upon under conditions.

Core Idea
Bayan is how the divine text conveys its ruling: three types reinforce, clarify, or redirect meaning.

In Hanafi usul, every text from the Quran and Sunnah relates to its meaning through one of five types of bayan. This lesson explains the first three: bayan taqrir (emphatic confirmation), bayan tafsir (clarification of ambiguity), and bayan taghyir (redirection from apparent meaning). Understanding these categories is essential for correctly deriving rulings from revealed texts.

Core Idea
Revelation includes not only angel-delivered messages but also divine inspiration and the Prophet's ijtihad, all considered binding.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, revelation (wahy) is broader than the Quran and explicit prophetic statements.
