Metaphorical Dispensation (Rukhsat Majaziyyah) and Its Two Types
Definition and classification of metaphorical dispensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define metaphorical dispensation, distinguish its two types, and explain why acting upon a complete metaphorical dispensation is sinful.

Core Idea
A metaphorical dispensation is not a true concession; acting upon it is sinful.

In Hanafi usul, not every easing of a ruling is a genuine dispensation (rukhsah). Some rulings are called 'rukhsah' only metaphorically because they were removed entirely from the Ummah. This passage defines metaphorical dispensation, splits it into complete and incomplete types, and clarifies their legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of rukhsat majaziyyah
Locate the statement that defines metaphorical dispensation: it is where the azimah (stricter ruling) is absent, so the term rukhsah is applied metaphorically.
Contrast this with true dispensation where the azimah still exists but a concession is given.
2
Recognize the two types
The text divides metaphorical dispensation into two: complete and incomplete. Identify the Arabic phrases for each.
Complete: 'ما وضع عنا من الاصرار والاغلال'; Incomplete: 'ما سقط عن العباد مع كونه مشروعا في الجملة'.
3
Analyze the complete type
List the examples given: cutting off wrong-doing limbs, inability to purify with earth, burning zakat and war booty. Understand that these were obligations on previous nations but lifted from the Ummah.
These are no longer part of Shari'ah; practicing them now would be sinful.
4
Understand why it is metaphorical
Note that calling these rulings 'rukhsah' is metaphorical because they are not concessions from a present stricter ruling; the stricter ruling itself is gone.
The term rukhsah implies a concession while the azimah remains; here the azimah is absent.
5
Contrast with incomplete metaphorical dispensation
The incomplete type refers to rulings that are still legislated in general but are dropped in specific circumstances. Recognize that it is not fully explained here.
This distinction helps in correctly applying the term rukhsah in usul discussions.

Metaphorical dispensation: That in which the azimah (stricter ruling) is lost, so in contrast it will be metaphorical dispensation; applying the term 'rukhsah' to them is metaphorical. Metaphorical dispensation has two types: (1) Complete metaphorical dispensation, (2) Incomplete metaphorical dispensation. (1) Complete metaphorical dispensation: 'As for the most complete of the two types of metaphor, it is that which has been removed from us of hardship and burdens.' That type consists of hardships and burdens which were legislated for previous communities but were dropped from the Ummah of the Prophet (peace be upon him), e.g., cutting off the wrong-doing limbs, not obtaining purity with earth (tayammum), burning zakat and war booty in fire, etc. It is named metaphorical dispensation; acting upon them for us is sinful. The second type, incomplete metaphorical dispensation: 'That which is dropped from the servants despite being legislated in general.'

Commentary
This passage introduces metaphorical dispensation (rukhsat majaziyyah), which applies when the stricter ruling (azimah) is altogether absent, so the concession is merely metaphorical. It is divided into two types. The first, complete metaphorical dispensation, consists of severe obligations that were placed on previous nations but were lifted for the Ummah of the Prophet (e.g., cutting off limbs for mistakes, inability to purify with soil, burning zakat and spoils). These are called 'rukhsah' metaphorically; practicing them would be sinful for Muslims. The second, incomplete metaphorical dispensation, refers to rulings that are legislated in general but are dropped from the servants for some reason, like certain exemptions. This classification helps us understand that not all concessions are true dispensations; some are merely rhetorical.

Key Terms
رخصت مجازیہ (rukhsat majaziyyah): A dispensation that is not a true concession; it refers to rulings that were removed from the Ummah, and calling it rukhsah is metaphorical.
مجاز (majaz): Metaphorical usage of a term; here applied to dispensation that is not genuine.
اصرار و اغلال (israr wa aghlal): Hardships and burdens (from previous nations) that were lifted from this Ummah.

Naskh: Definition, Sources, and Types
Defining naskh, its permissibility, and the three types of mansukh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define naskh, identify its permissible sources in Hanafi usul, and distinguish the three types of mansukh using the examples provided.

Core Idea
Naskh in Hanafi usul is limited to revealed texts — neither qiyas nor ijma can abrogate a Qur'anic or sunnatic ruling.

Abrogation (naskh) is a key mechanism in Islamic law whereby a later revelation supersedes an earlier ruling. This lesson lays out the Hanafi position on what can and cannot serve as a source of abrogation, and then categorises the types of legal texts that may be abrogated. Understanding these categories is essential for correctly interpreting apparent contradictions between scriptural sources.

Guided Path
1
Define naskh linguistically and legally
Identify the linguistic meaning (tabdil, change) and then the legal meaning as abrogation of a ruling by a later revealed text.
The passage explicitly says 'tabdil' is the linguistic meaning.
2
Identify the limitation on sources
Note that Hanafis restrict naskh to Quran and Sunnah only, excluding qiyas and ijma.
This is a distinctive Hanafi principle.
3
Enumerate the four source interactions
List the four combinations: Quran abrogating Quran, Quran abrogated by Sunnah, Sunnah abrogated by Sunnah, Sunnah abrogated by Quran.
These are the only permissible forms of naskh.
4
Distinguish the three categories of mansukh
Understand the difference between: (1) both recitation and ruling abrogated, (2) ruling abrogated but recitation remains, (3) recitation abrogated but ruling remains.
Each category has a distinct example in the passage.
5
Apply the categories to the given examples
Match each example from the passage to its correct category and verify your understanding.
E.g., the stoning verse is an example of category 3.

Benefit (5): Statement of tabdil: It is naskh in language. In lexicon, tabdil is called naskh. Abrogation cannot be made in the Book of Allah and the Sunnah by qiyas or ijma; rather, a Book can be abrogated by a Sunnah, and a Sunnah by a Book. It has four types: (1) abrogation of Book by Book, (2) abrogation of Book by Sunnah, (3) abrogation of Sunnah by Sunnah, (4) abrogation of Sunnah by Book. Clarification by examples of types of mansukh: (1) Abrogation of recitation and ruling: both recitation and ruling are abrogated, like Surah al-Ahzab was equal to Surah al-Baqarah in length, it had two hundred or three hundred verses, now only 73 verses remain; the recitation and ruling of the remaining verses are abrogated. (2) Abrogation of ruling without recitation: the ruling is abrogated while recitation remains, like 'lakum dinukum waliya din' – its ruling is abrogated by the verse of jihad, but its recitation remains. (3) Abrogation of recitation without ruling: recitation is abrogated while the ruling remains, like 'ash-shaykh wa ash-shaykha idha zanaya farijumoohuma nakalan min Allah' – its recitation is abrogated but the ruling remains.

Commentary
This passage defines naskh (abrogation) as linguistically 'tabdil' (change). It then states a key Hanafi principle: abrogation cannot be established by qiyas (analogy) or ijma (consensus); it must come from a revealed source – either the Quran or Sunnah. Four types are enumerated: (1) Quran abrogating Quran, (2) Quran abrogated by Sunnah, (3) Sunnah abrogated by Sunnah, (4) Sunnah abrogated by Quran. The passage then illustrates three categories of mansukh (abrogated text): (1) both recitation and ruling abrogated (e.g., part of Surah al-Ahzab), (2) ruling abrogated but recitation remains (e.g., the verse 'lakum dinukum...' whose ruling of tolerance is abrogated by jihad verses), (3) recitation abrogated but ruling remains (e.g., the stoning verse for married adulterers). These examples clarify the concept of naskh in Hanafi usul.

Key Terms
نسخ (naskh): Linguistically 'tabdil' (change); legally, abrogation of a ruling by a later revealed text.
منسوخ التلاوة و الحکم (mansūkh at-tilāwa wa al-ḥukm): Both the recitation and the ruling of the verse are abrogated.
منسوخ الحکم دون التلاوة (mansūkh al-ḥukm dūn at-tilāwa): The ruling is abrogated, but the recitation remains in the Quran.
منسوخ التلاوة دون الحکم (mansūkh at-tilāwa dūn al-ḥukm): The recitation is abrogated, but the ruling remains enforceable.

The Third Type of Naskh: Recitation Abrogated, Ruling Intact
Explains the third type of naskh with example from hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a case of naskh al-tilawah duna al-hukm and explain why the ruling remains binding despite the text being abrogated.

Core Idea
Abrogation can remove a verse's recitation without affecting its legal force — the ruling remains operative.

This passage introduces the third type of naskh according to Hanafi usul. While many assume abrogation always removes both text and ruling, here we see that a verse may be removed from the Quran yet its legal command continues. The classic example is the stoning verse. This lesson will clarify the mechanism and the evidence for this type of naskh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of naskh
From the passage, note that the third type is 'mansukh al-tilawah duna al-hukm' — recitation abrogated, ruling remaining.
2
Examine the example
The passage gives the stoning verse: 'al-shaykh wa al-shaykhah idha zanaya fa-ijmuuhuma.' Recognize that this text is not in the current Quran.
This is a hadith transmitted by multiple Companions.
3
Analyze the evidence
Understand that the ruling of stoning is established by the sunnah (mutawatir hadith) and ijma' of the Companions, not by the recited text.
The thubut of the hadith is zanni, but the dalala is qati.
4
Understand the implication
Realize that naskh can be partial: only the recitation is removed, while the ruling remains operative. This shows that abrogation does not always entail complete cancellation.
5
Note the scholarly difference
Some scholars reject this type of naskh. The Hanafi school accepts it based on the overwhelming evidence.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf.

Commentary
This passage presents the third type of abrogation (naskh) according to Hanafi classification. In this type, the recital text of a verse is removed from the Quran, but the legal ruling it contained continues to be binding. The example given is the so-called 'stoning verse' (ayah al-rajm), which is not found in the written Quran today but its ruling (stoning for married adulterers) remains operative. This shows that abrogation does not always affect the ruling; it may affect only the textual form.

Key Terms
منسوخ التلاوة دون الحكم (mansukh al-tilawah duna al-hukm): A type of naskh where the recited text of a Quranic verse is removed but its legal ruling remains in effect.
