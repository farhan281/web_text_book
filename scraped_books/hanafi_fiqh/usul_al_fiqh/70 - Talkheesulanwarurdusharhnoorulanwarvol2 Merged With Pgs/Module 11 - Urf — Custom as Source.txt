Contexts for Abandoning Haqiqah in Favor of Majaz
Explains two contexts where haqiqah is abandoned for majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a literal meaning is ta'zur or mahjurah and apply the rule to determine the metaphorical meaning in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Literal meaning is abandoned only when impossible or when custom has abandoned it.

In Hanafi usul, when interpreting oaths and other utterances, the default is to take the literal meaning (haqiqah). However, there are two well-defined exceptions where the literal meaning must be set aside in favor of the metaphorical meaning (majaz). This lesson unpacks those two exceptions—haqiqah ta'zur and haqiqah mahjurah—and shows how custom and practicality shape legal interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning (haqiqah) of the utterance
State the plain, lexical meaning of the key term in the oath or statement. For example, in 'I will not eat from this tree,' the literal meaning of 'eat from this tree' is consuming part of the tree itself.
The literal meaning is the default starting point.
2
Assess feasibility of acting on the literal meaning
Determine whether it is practically possible to act on that literal meaning. For 'eat from the tree,' is it physically possible to eat the tree? No—it is impossible. For 'place my foot in the house,' it is possible.
Impossibility (ta'zur) is the first basis for abandoning haqiqah.
3
Check if custom (urf) has abandoned the literal meaning
Even if the literal meaning is possible, ask whether the speech community (custom) has ceased to use that meaning in that context. For 'place my foot,' custom means 'enter,' not physically place a foot. If custom has abandoned the literal, it becomes haqiqah mahjurah.
Custom must be established and consistent.
4
Apply the metaphorical meaning (majaz)
Once either ta'zur or mahjurah is established, interpret the utterance according to the customary metaphorical meaning. For the tree, the majaz is 'eat from the fruit of the tree'; for the foot, the majaz is 'enter the house.'
The metaphorical meaning is what is used in legal rulings.
5
Derive the ruling for the oath
Determine whether the person has violated the oath (hanith) based on the metaphorical meaning. If they eat the fruit, they break the oath; if they enter the house, they break it.
The oath is interpreted by the majaz, not the haqiqah.

Contexts for abandoning haqiqah and [using] majaz: (1) Haqiqah that is difficult (ta'zur): This is when acting upon the literal meaning of a word is difficult, like 'I will not eat from this tree.' He will become a sinner (hanith) — acting upon it is difficult. In this case, the literal meaning is abandoned and the metaphorical meaning is acted upon. (2) Abandoned haqiqah (mahjurah): This is when acting upon the literal meaning is not difficult, but custom and habit have abandoned it. For example, 'I will not place my foot in so-and-so's house.' The literal meaning of placing the foot is easy, but custom and habit have abandoned acting upon it. Here too, the literal meaning is abandoned and the metaphorical meaning is acted upon.

Commentary
This passage discusses two scenarios where the literal meaning (haqiqah) is abandoned in favor of metaphorical meaning (majaz) in oaths (yamin). In the first case, 'haqiqah ta'zur', the literal meaning is impractical or impossible to fulfill. For example, if someone swears 'I will not eat from this tree,' the literal meaning (eating from the tree itself) is impossible, so it is interpreted metaphorically as not eating from the fruit of the tree. The person becomes a sinner (hanith) if he violates the metaphorical meaning. In the second case, 'haqiqah mahjurah', the literal meaning is possible but custom has abandoned it. For example, 'I will not place my foot in so-and-so's house' — literally one could place a foot, but custom understands it as 'I will not enter the house.' Both cases illustrate that in legal interpretation, when literal meaning is either impossible or abandoned by custom, the metaphorical meaning is given effect. This is a key principle in Hanafi usul for interpreting oaths and similar utterances.

Key Terms
حقیقت تعذره (haqiqah ta'zur): A literal meaning that is difficult or impossible to act upon.
حقیقت مهجوره (haqiqah mahjurah): A literal meaning abandoned by custom even though possible.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi usul, literal meaning is abandoned only when it is impossible (haqiqah ta'zur) or when custom has abandoned it (haqiqah mahjurah), and the metaphorical meaning then governs.
