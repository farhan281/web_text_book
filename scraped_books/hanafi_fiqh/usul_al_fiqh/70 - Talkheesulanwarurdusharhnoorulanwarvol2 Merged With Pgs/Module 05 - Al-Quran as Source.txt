The Four Fundamental Sources of Shari'ah
Listing the four fundamental sources of Shari'ah with brief explanations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four sources of Shari'ah according to Hanafi usul and identify the specific portion of the Quran and Sunnah used for legal derivation.

Core Idea
Islamic law derives from four sources: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas; only 500 Quranic verses and 3000 hadith are directly legal.

This passage lays the foundation of Hanafi usul al-fiqh by listing the four primary sources of Shari'ah. It clarifies that not every Quranic verse or hadith is a source of law—only about five hundred verses and three thousand hadith contain legal rulings. This distinction is crucial for understanding how jurists approach textual evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four sources
List the four sources in order: Kitab Allah, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas. Understand that they form a hierarchy—scholars first look to the Quran, then Sunnah, then Ijma', and finally Qiyas.
2
Understand the scope of the Quran as a source
Recall that the term 'Kitab Allah' here refers specifically to the ayat al-ahkam, approximately 500 verses that directly give legal rulings. The remaining Quranic verses are for other purposes such as stories, warnings, and refutation.
This prevents the misconception that every verse is a legal ruling.
3
Understand the scope of the Sunnah as a source
Similarly, 'Sunnah' refers to the approximately 3000 hadith from which jurists have derived rulings. Not every hadith carries legal weight; many relate to non-legal matters.
This is the basis for distinguishing legal from non-legal hadith.
4
Define Ijma' in the Hanafi context
Ijma' is the consensus of mujtahid scholars of any time, not limited to a specific century or city. This definition is broader than some other schools.
Contrast with definitions that restrict Ijma' to a particular generation.
5
Recognize the role of Qiyas
Qiyas is used when no ruling is found in the first three sources. It is a legitimate and necessary method, and accepting it is obligatory.
Qiyas is the fourth source and must be followed once correctly applied.

Introducing the four primary sources of law

Core Idea
The Hanafi school recognizes four sources: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas, in that order of priority.

Every legal system has its foundations. In Islamic law, the Hanafi school identifies four primary sources that provide rulings. This lesson introduces these sources, clarifying that not every Quranic verse or hadith is legal in nature—only specific ones are used for derivation. Understanding this framework is essential before delving into how each source is applied.

Defining the Quran: Revelation, Transmission, and Composition
Defining the Quran and its components

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of the Quran, explain its three essential components (revelation, written in mus'hafs, mutawatir transmission), and distinguish it from hadith.

Core Idea
The Quran is the divinely revealed word of Allah, transmitted mutawatir, written in mus'hafs, and consists of both words and meanings.

After establishing the four sources, the author turns to the first and most important source: the Quran. The definition he provides is precise and carries significant legal weight. This definition determines what constitutes binding proof (qat'i) in Hanafi usul. By understanding these components, you will grasp why the Quran is considered definitive in both transmission and meaning, unlike ahad hadith which are speculative in transmission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the components of the definition
From the Arabic text, extract the three key elements: (1) revealed to the Prophet, (2) written in the mus'hafs, (3) transmitted mutawatir without doubt.
These three components are the minimum for something to be considered Quran.
2
Understand 'mutawatir' and its significance
Mutawatir means transmission by such a large number in each generation that collusion on a lie is impossible. This makes the Quran qat'i al-thubut (definitively established).
In contrast, most hadiths are ahad (single-chain) and thus zanni al-thubut.
3
Recognize that the Quran is both words and meanings
The text explicitly states that the Quran is the name of the combination of words and meanings, not just one. This refutes the idea that only the meaning is the Quran.
This is a crucial point distinguishing the Quran from hadith qudsi, which is meaning from Allah but wording from the Prophet.
4
Note the divisions mentioned
The author mentions that the Quran is divided into four divisions based on word and meaning. While not detailed here, this indicates a framework for analyzing Quranic verses.
These divisions likely refer to categories like apparent (zahir), explicit (nass), etc., which will be covered later.

Classification of Quranic Expressions into Four Divisions
Explaining the four-fold classification of Quranic expressions based on lafz and ma'na

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four divisions of Quranic expressions and classify a given Quranic term under the correct division by its scope, clarity, literalness, and derivation method.

Core Idea
The Quran's words are analyzed by scope, clarity, literalness, and ruling derivation – four distinct lenses for istinbat.

This lesson introduces the foundational classification of Quranic expressions used in Hanafi usul. The author outlines four ways to categorize the words of the Quran: by the scope of meaning (specific/general), by clarity (apparent/explicit/detailed/perfect), by literal versus figurative usage, and by the method of deriving rulings (direct wording, indication, inference, necessity). Understanding these divisions is critical for correctly interpreting the Quran and deriving ahkam.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four divisions
Recognize that the author lists four distinct divisions based on different aspects: scope, clarity, literalness, and derivation method. Each division contains four subcategories.
These divisions are not mutually exclusive; a single expression can be analyzed under all four.
2
Analyze the first division: scope of meaning
The first division categorizes words by whether they indicate a single specific meaning (khaass), encompass all instances (aam), have multiple unrelated meanings (mushtarak), or have been reinterpreted from literal to secondary meaning (muawwal).
Hanafi usul: aam is qat'i until takhsis.
3
Analyze the second division: clarity hierarchy
The second division orders words by clarity: zaahir (apparent, subject to interpretation), nass (unequivocal), mufassar (explicitly detailed), muhkam (perfectly clear, cannot be abrogated). Their opposites are khafiyy, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih.
This hierarchy is used to determine which daleel takes priority.
4
Analyze the third division: literal vs figurative

The Four Sources of Islamic Law (Adillah)
Explaining the limitation of legal proofs to four sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four sources of Islamic law according to the Hanafi school (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) and explain the reasoning behind their limitation based on revelation and non-revelation.

Core Idea
All legal proofs derive from either revelation (recited or unrecited) or from consensus and analogy — only four valid sources.

This lesson explains why the Hanafi school limits authoritative proofs to four: the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas. The reasoning is based on the nature of the evidence: it either comes from revelation (recited as Quran or non-recited as Sunnah) or from non-revelation, which includes the agreement of the Ummah (Ijma) or analogical reasoning (Qiyas). This classification provides a comprehensive framework for all legal derivation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the general reasoning
The author states that all legal proofs are either from revelation (wahy) or from non-revelation. This is the foundational dichotomy.
Non-revelation here means human reasoning (ijma and qiyas).
2
Divide revelation into two categories
Revelation is either recited (matluww) – the Quran – or non-recited (ghayr matluww) – the Sunnah. Both are divine in origin.
The Quran is transmitted mutawatir; the Sunnah may be ahad.
3
Divide non-revelation into two categories
Non-revelation is either the agreement of all scholars (Ijma) or the reasoning of some (Qiyas). Both are based on human ijtihad.
Ijma is infallible; qiyas is a tool for extending rulings.
4
Identify the four sources
The four sources are: Kitabullah (Quran), Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. These are the primary adillah in Hanafi usul.
Other sources like istihsan are secondary and often subsumed under qiyas.
5
Recognize this as the Hanafi framework
This categorization is specific to the Hanafi school; other schools may include additional sources like maslaha mursalah or istihsan as primary.
The text presents a logic for limiting proofs to four.
