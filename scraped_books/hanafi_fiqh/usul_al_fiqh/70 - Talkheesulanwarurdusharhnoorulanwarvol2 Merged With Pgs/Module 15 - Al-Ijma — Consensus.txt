Definition of Ijma': Linguistic and Technical
Linguistic and technical definition of ijma'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi technical definition of ijma' and identify its essential components.

Core Idea
Ijma' is the agreement of righteous mujtahids, not all Muslims.

This lesson introduces the fourth primary source of Islamic law: ijma' (consensus). The passage provides both the linguistic and technical meanings, highlighting the key conditions that make an agreement binding. Understanding this definition is crucial for recognizing which consensus carries legal authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning
The text states that ijma' literally means agreement (ittifaq) or union. Note that the lexical sense is broader than the technical.
Linguistic meaning is general; technical meaning is specific.
2
Break down the technical definition
The definition has four key components: 'agreement' (ittifaq), 'mujtahids' (qualified scholars), 'righteous' (salihin), and 'from the ummah of Muhammad'. Each restricts the scope of ijma'.
The term 'mujtahid' implies one capable of independent legal reasoning.
3
Note the conditions
The mujtahids must be righteous (salihin) — meaning they are upright practitioners of Islam. They must also be from the ummah of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him), excluding previous communities.
Righteousness excludes sinners whose agreement carries no weight.
4
Understand the legal effect
Ijma' is a binding proof (hujjah) that produces definitive rulings. Once established, it must be followed.
Hanafi usul considers ijma' as qat'i al-thubut.

Ijma' Defined and Its Two Types
Defining Ijma' and its two types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of Ijma' and distinguish its two types: Azima and Rukhsat, identifying the conditions for valid Ijma'.

Core Idea
Ijma' is the binding consensus of all righteous mujtahids; its two types reflect full vs partial agreement.

Ijma' (consensus) is a primary source of Islamic law, second only to Quran and Sunnah. This passage from Talkhis al-Anwar defines Ijma' from the Hanafi perspective and introduces a unique division into Azima (firm consensus) and Rukhsat (concessionary consensus). Understanding this definition is foundational for grasping how Hanafi scholars derive and validate rulings through consensus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition components
Break down the technical definition into its essential elements: who (righteous mujtahids of the Ummah), what (agreement on a verbal or practical matter), when (in any single era). Note that the definition specifies 'mujtahids' not all Muslims, and 'righteous' (not fasiq).
The linguistic meaning is simply 'agreement'.
2
Understand the two divisions
Azima requires that all mujtahids in that era explicitly agree, either through speech (e.g., saying 'ajma'na ala hadha') or through action. Rukhsat is when only some agree, while others disagree or remain silent.
This division is specific to some Hanafi texts; others consider only unanimous agreement as true Ijma'.
3
List the conditions for validity
The passage mentions conditions: (1) the mujtahid must be pious and righteous, not a sinner or follower of desires; (2) being a Companion is not necessary; (3) being from Ahl al-Bayt or Ahl al-Madinah is not a requirement (though mentioned).
The condition of righteousness ensures that the consensus is from authoritative scholars.

Understanding Ijma: Definition, Types, Conditions and Levels in Hanafi Usul
Definition, types, conditions, and levels of Ijma in Hanafi usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Ijma according to Hanafi usul, distinguish its two types (azimah/rukhsah), state the conditions for its validity, and apply the ruling on its levels to determine whether a denier is considered a disbeliever.

Core Idea
Ijma is a definitive proof (qat'i) in Hanafi usul, but its binding force varies by the level of consensus.

Ijma (consensus) is a primary source of Islamic law in the Hanafi school. This lesson unpacks the classical definition, the conditions required for a valid consensus, and the different levels of authority that Ijma carries. Understanding these nuances is crucial for correctly applying Ijma as a legal proof and avoiding common misconceptions about who is bound by it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and its key components
The text defines Ijma as the agreement of the people of knowledge and piety (ahl al-ilm wa ahl al-taqwa) of the Ummah of Muhammad (PBUH) on a verbal or practical matter in any era. Note the qualifications: 'ilm' and 'taqwa' exclude ignorant or sinful individuals.
The definition focuses on qualified scholars, not all Muslims.
2
Distinguish between the two types: azimah and rukhsah
Azimah requires unanimous agreement of all mujtahids, expressed through explicit statement or collective action. Rukhsah is partial agreement where only some mujtahids agree; this is not considered a binding Ijma.
The type affects the ruling's strength and the status of a denier.
3
List the conditions for validity (shurut al-sihha)
The mujtahid must be righteous, not a sinner or follower of desires. It is not required that he be a Companion, from the Ahl al-Bayt, or from Madinah. These conditions ensure the integrity of the consensus.
Contrast with other schools that may require Prophetic descent or geographic location.
4
Apply the condition for occurrence (shart al-in'iqad)
All mujtahids must agree; even one dissenter prevents the formation of Ijma. This is the unanimity condition.
Rukhsah, by definition, lacks unanimity, so it is not true Ijma.
5
Analyse the levels of Ijma and their rulings on deniers
The passage describes at least two levels: (1) the Ijma of the Companions (strongest, like Quran/mutawatir), denier becomes a kafir; (2) Ijma where some Companions explicitly state a view and others remain silent (weaker, still qat'i but denier not kafir).
The practical difference: declaring someone a kafir is reserved only for the highest level.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul text on Ijma (consensus). It begins by defining Ijma as the agreement of the qualified scholars of the Ummah of Muhammad (peace be upon him) on a religious matter, whether verbal or practical, in any era. The definition emphasizes that the scholars must be 'ahl al-ilm wa ahl al-taqwa' (people of knowledge and piety). The author then divides Ijma into two types based on its pillar (rukn): 1) Azimat: when all mujtahids unanimously agree, either through their explicit statement (e.g., saying 'we have agreed') or through their collective action. 2) Rukhsat: when only some mujtahids agree while others do not; this is not a complete Ijma but a form of concession. The passage then lists conditions for the validity (ahammiyyat) of Ijma: the mujtahid must be righteous and not a sinner; it is not required that he be a Companion, from Ahl al-Bayt, or from Madinah. For the actual occurrence (inqi’ad) of Ijma, unanimous agreement of all mujtahids is necessary; if even one dissents, Ijma does not take place. The ruling (hukm) of Ijma is that it establishes a matter in Shariah with certainty (qat’i). The author then discusses the levels (maratib) of Ijma. The strongest is the Ijma of the Companions, which is like a Quranic verse or mutawatir hadith; its denier becomes an unbeliever. The author cites that the scholars of Bukhara and Lich used this to declare the Rawafid (a group of Shia) as unbelievers for denying the caliphate of Abu Bakr. The second level is where some Companions explicitly state an opinion and others remain silent (sukut), indicating tacit agreement. This type is not as strong; its denier is not deemed an unbeliever even if the evidence is definitive. The example given is the issue of three divorces in one sitting, established by a statement of Umar (may Allah be pleased with him). The passage cuts off after mentioning this example. The text follows the Hanafi legal theory where Ijma is a source of law and its strength varies by the composition of the mujtahids.

Commentary
This passage is from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh text discussing the conditions, ruling, and types of Ijma' (consensus). It first states the condition that Ijma' requires the agreement of all qualified mujtahids; any dissent prevents consensus. The ruling of Ijma' is that it renders the matter definitively established in Shari'ah, carrying certainty. The text then categorizes Ijma' into four types based on the category of scholars involved and the extent of agreement:

1. Ijma' of the Companions on a matter where all agree, and no dissenter is known. This is the strongest, like a Quranic verse or mutawatir hadith; its denier is considered a disbeliever. The scholars of Bukhara and Samarkand applied this to declare the Rawafid (a group that rejected the caliphate of Abu Bakr) as disbelievers because the Ijma' of the Companions on Abu Bakr's caliphate is of this type.

2. Ijma' where some Companions explicitly state a ruling and others remain silent without expressing opposition (sukut al-taqrir). This silence is considered tacit approval. Its denier is not a disbeliever, even though the evidence is definitive. The example given is the triple divorce (three divorces in one sitting) – 'Umar ibn al-Khattab explicitly enforced it as three, and other Companions remained silent, thus forming a valid Ijma'.

3. Ijma' of the generation after the Companions (Tabi'un and later) on a matter where no difference among the Companions is known. This type of Ijma' is like a mashhur (well-known) hadith; it provides tranquility (itminan) but not absolute certainty. It is a probable (zanni) proof.

4. Ijma' of the later scholars on a matter where there was known disagreement among the Companions. This is the weakest form, equivalent to a khabar wahid (isolated hadith); it obligates action but does not yield certainty, and it takes precedence over qiyas (analogy) if they conflict.

Finally, the text notes that the transmission of Ijma' itself requires Ijma': if the consensus of early scholars reaches us via a mutawatir (mass-transmitted) chain, it is treated like mutawatir hadith; if via ahad (isolated) chains, it is like khabar wahid.

2. Ijma' where some Companions explicitly state a ruling and others remain silent without expressing opposition (sukut al-taqrir). This silence is considered tacit approval. Its denier is not a disbeliever, even though the evidence is definitive. The example given is the triple divorce (three divorces in one sitting) – 'Umar ibn al-Khattab explicitly enforced it as three, and other Companions remained silent, thus forming a valid Ijma'.

3. Ijma' of the generation after the Companions (Tabi'un and later) on a matter where no difference among the Companions is known. This type of Ijma' is like a mashhur (well-known) hadith; it provides tranquility (itminan) but not absolute certainty. It is a probable (zanni) proof.

4. Ijma' of the later scholars on a matter where there was known disagreement among the Companions. This is the weakest form, equivalent to a khabar wahid (isolated hadith); it obligates action but does not yield certainty, and it takes precedence over qiyas (analogy) if they conflict.

Finally, the text notes that the transmission of Ijma' itself requires Ijma': if the consensus of early scholars reaches us via a mutawatir (mass-transmitted) chain, it is treated like mutawatir hadith; if via ahad (isolated) chains, it is like khabar wahid.
