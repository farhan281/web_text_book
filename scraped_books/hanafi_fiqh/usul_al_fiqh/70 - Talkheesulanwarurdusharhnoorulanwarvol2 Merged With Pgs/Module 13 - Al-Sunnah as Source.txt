Defining Sunnah linguistically and terminologically, and introducing its four divisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Sunnah linguistically and terminologically in the Hanafi usul tradition, and identify its four components and four divisions.

Core Idea
Sunnah in Hanafi usul is every attribution to the Prophet — sayings, actions, attributes, and tacit approvals — a primary source of law.

Having completed the chapters on the divisions of the Book of Allah, we now turn to the second primary source of Islamic law: the Sunnah. This lesson establishes the foundational definition of Sunnah in both linguistic and terminological senses, and outlines the four broad divisions under which it will be studied. Understanding this definition is crucial for correctly identifying what constitutes prophetic evidence in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Differentiate linguistic and terminological meanings
The linguistic meaning of Sunnah is 'path' or 'way'. The terminological meaning in Hanafi usul is all that is attributed to the Prophet (peace be upon him). Recognize that the technical definition is narrower and specific to legal theory.
The linguistic meaning is broader and can refer to any custom, but in usul it is restricted to prophetic attributions.
2
Identify the four components of terminological Sunnah
The passage lists four types: qawl (sayings), fi'l (actions), sifah (attributes), and taqrir (tacit approvals). Understand each component: attributes include physical and moral characteristics; tacit approvals are actions done in the Prophet's presence without objection.
Some schools may exclude attributes, but the Hanafi school includes them as part of Sunnah.
3
Recognize the four divisions foreshadowed
The author mentions four divisions: (1) how Sunnah has reached us (connected vs. disconnected), (2) how it is disconnected (types of broken chains), (3) the locus of the report (e.g., actions vs. sayings), and (4) a fourth division not fully stated here. This is a preview for later elaboration.
Detailed study of these divisions will follow in subsequent chapters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of khabar mutawatir and explain why it yields certain knowledge.

Core Idea
Mutawatir report yields certain knowledge because collusion on falsehood is impossible.

In Hanafi usul, reports are classified by their transmission. The highest category is khabar mutawatir, a report transmitted by so many narrators that falsehood is inconceivable. This lesson defines it and explains its binding certainty.

The definition focuses on the impossibility of collusion, not a fixed number.
2
Analyze the conditions
Two conditions: narrators in every era are uncountable, and it is impossible to imagine them conspiring to lie.
The condition is the impossibility of collusion, which may vary with context.
3
Recognize the examples
The author gives classic Hanafi examples: the five daily prayers and the Quran itself. These are known through mutawatir transmission.
These examples illustrate practical certain knowledge.
4
Understand the evidentiary value
Khabar mutawatir produces 'ilm yaqini (certain knowledge) akin to direct observation, categorized as 'ilm daruri (necessary knowledge).
This contrasts with ahad reports, which yield only probable knowledge (zann).

Definition and Ruling of Khabar Mutawatir
Defines mutawatir and states that it yields certainty akin to direct observation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khabar mutawatir, identify its ruling (ilm yaqini daruri), and explain that no specific number of narrators is required.

Core Idea
Khabar mutawatir yields certain knowledge equal to direct observation — no minimum number required.

In Hanafi usul, not all reports carry equal weight. Some are so massively transmitted that doubt is impossible. This lesson introduces khabar mutawatir, the highest category of transmitted knowledge, and clarifies its defining features and legal effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and genus
Recognize that khabar mutawatir is a type of khabar (report). The definition locates it within the category of transmitted information.
2
Extract defining characteristics
Two key features: (1) narrated by a group too large to count, (2) collusion upon falsehood is impossible. These are jointly sufficient.
The impossibility of collusion is based on sheer numbers and diversity of chains.
3
Analyze the ruling (hukm)
The passage states it 'yojibu al-ilm al-yaqini ka al-iyan' – it necessitates certain knowledge like direct observation, which is necessary (daruri).
This means the knowledge is immediate and indubitable, not requiring inference.
4
Examine the examples
The five daily prayers and the Quran are cited. Both are transmitted through such massive continuity that their content is undeniable.
These examples show that mutawatir applies to both practices (mutawatir amali) and texts (mutawatir lafzi).
5
Note the absence of a fixed number
The passage explicitly says 'adad shart nahin hai' – number is not a condition. The only condition is impossibility of collusion.
This distinguishes the Hanafi view from some other usul schools that specify a minimum number.

Khabar Mashhur: Definition and Epistemic Status
Defines mashhur and explains its epistemic status below mutawatir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khabar mashhur, explain its epistemic status (ilm al-itminan), and distinguish it from mutawatir and ahad.

Core Idea
Originally ahad, then tawatur in later generations—knowledge of itminan, between mutawatir and ahad.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi concept of khabar mashhur, a unique category of hadith transmission. Unlike mutawatir, which is certain from the start, a mashhur report begins as ahad but later becomes so widely transmitted that collusion on falsehood is impossible. This gives it an epistemic status of 'ilm al-itminan—knowledge of satisfaction—which is binding but not daruri (necessary). Understanding this distinction is crucial for Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the temporal structure
Note that mashhur is defined by its history: originally ahad in the first century, then reaching tawatur in later centuries.
This is the key distinguishing feature from mutawatir.
2
Analyze the condition for mashhur
The condition is that in later generations, the number of narrators makes collusion on falsehood impossible.
This echoes the condition for tawatur but is reached later.
3
Determine the epistemic output
Mashhur yields 'ilm al-itminan, which is between yaqin (mutawatir) and zann (ahad).
Itminan is close to certainty but not daruri.
4
State the ruling on its acceptance
Accepting mashhur is obligatory; denial is fisq and misguidance, but not kufr.
Because it is not at the level of daruri yaqin.
5
Contrast with mutawatir and ahad
Understand its rank in the hierarchy: lower than mutawatir, higher than ahad.
This ranking affects legal reasoning and ijma'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khabar wahid according to Hanafi usul and state its ruling regarding action and certainty.

Core Idea
Khabar wahid obligates action but does not produce certain knowledge.

In Hanafi usul, reports are categorized by their transmission. Among them, khabar wahid is a key category that binds Muslims to act upon it, even though it does not provide certainty. This lesson defines khabar wahid and explains why it carries legal weight despite lacking epistemic certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms in the definition
Locate the terms that define khabar wahid: 'every report', 'one or two or more', 'below mashhur and mutawatir'.
2
Understand the condition of being below mashhur/mutawatir
The report must not reach the level of mashhur or mutawatir in the first three generations (Sahaba, Tabi'un, Tabi' al-Tabi'in).
3
Note the irrelevance of later narrators
Once it is established as below mashhur/mutawatir, the number of narrators later does not change its category.
This is a key Hanafi principle.
4
State the ruling
Khabar wahid obligates action (wajib al-'amal) but does not produce certain knowledge (yaqin).
It is binding for practice, not for belief.
5
Contrast with the Quran
The Quran gives yaqin (certainty), whereas khabar wahid does not, yet both can establish legal obligations in the Hanafi school.
The difference is epistemic, not legal.

Classification of Narrators of Khabar Wahid in Hanafi Usul
Classification of narrators of khabar wahid and their rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify narrators of khabar wahid into known (ma'ruf) and unknown (majhul) and state the ruling on their narrations.

Core Idea
A narrator known for both fiqh and 'adala takes precedence over qiyas; one known only for 'adala is rejected if his report contradicts qiyas.

The reliability of khabar wahid depends on the narrator's standing. Hanafi usul provides a detailed taxonomy: first dividing narrators into known (ma'ruf) and unknown (majhul), then subdividing each with specific consequences for whether the narration is preferred over qiyas, rejected, or merely permissible to act upon. This lesson unpacks that taxonomy and its application.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the overarching classification
Identify that every narrator of khabar wahid is either ma'ruf (known) or majhul (unknown). This is the primary division.
The passage uses 'hasr' (exclusive enumeration) to assert that no other possibility exists.
2
Distinguish between the two types of ma'ruf narrators
If the narrator is ma'ruf, he is either 'ma'ruf bi al-fiqh wa al-'adala' (known for jurisprudence and justice) or 'ma'ruf bi al-'adala' (known only for justice). Note the different rulings: the first type's narration is muqaddam over qiyas unconditionally; the second type's narration is presented over qiyas but rejected if it contradicts qiyas.
The key distinction is knowledge of fiqh: it adds weight to the narration.
3
Identify the five subcategories of majhul narrators
When the narrator is majhul (unknown), the passage lists five possibilities based on the stance of the Salaf: (1) unanimous acceptance, (2) some accepted, (3) criticized but narration still taken, (4) silence, (5) narration never reached the Salaf. Note the ruling for each group.
The first three are treated like a narrator known only for justice; the fourth is rejected; the fifth is permissible to act upon if not contrary to qiyas.
4
Apply the rulings to example scenarios
Given a hypothetical narration, classify the narrator and determine the ruling (muqaddam, rejected, permissible, etc.) based on the criteria in this passage.
This step bridges theory to practice in usul analysis.

The Eight Matters That Do Not Disqualify a Narrator in Hanafi Usul
Lists eight factors not causing jarh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the eight matters that do not cause rejection of a narrator and explain the Hanafi reasoning for each.

Core Idea
Tadlis, irsal, humor, young age, and even excessive fiqh engagement do not automatically disqualify a narrator in Hanafi usul.

This passage from Talkhees al-Anwar lists eight specific circumstances that might be thought to disqualify a narrator, but the Hanafi school clarifies that they are not valid grounds for rejection. Understanding this list helps you correctly evaluate narrator credibility and avoid hasty judgments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the eight items
Read through the Arabic text and spot each of the eight matters mentioned. Note that the numbering may be inconsistent (item 6 is labeled as 1, and items 7 and 8 are present but poorly numbered).
The passage lists tadlis, talqis, rakḍ al-dābba, irsal, mizah, young age (not less than 5), and excessive fiqh engagement.
2
Define each matter
For each item, state its definition: tadlis is concealing defects in isnad, talqis is misrepresenting the teacher's name, etc. Refer to the key terms for help.
Some items like rakḍ al-dābba (racing animals) are less common; understand that some scholars considered it a defect but the Hanafi school rejects that view.
3
Understand the Hanafi reasoning
For each matter, note why it is not considered a defect by the Hanafis. For example, irsal is not a defect because the school accepts mursal hadith from trustworthy narrators. Mizah is permissible within Shari'ah limits as practiced by the Prophet.
The reasoning often hinges on the principle that these factors do not necessarily indicate dishonesty or poor memory.
4
Recognize implicit ikhtilaf
The passage mentions that 'some people' consider rakḍ al-dābba as a cause for rejection, showing that there is scholarly disagreement. Similarly, other schools may have stricter views on tadlis or irsal.
This ikhtilaf is legitimate; the Hanafi position is clear, but students should know that others differ.
5
Apply the rule to narrator evaluation
When assessing a narrator, do not automatically reject them solely because they engage in tadlis, irsal, etc. Evaluate the specific context and consider the overall reliability of the narrator.
For example, a narrator who occasionally uses tadlis for a valid reason may still be accepted if his honesty and memory are otherwise sound.

Types of Prophetic Actions (Sunnah Fi'liyyah)
Classifying the Prophet's actions into four legal categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any given prophetic action into one of the four Hanafi categories (mubah, mustahabb, wajib, fard) based on the known or unknown intent of the Prophet, and apply the default rule of permissibility when the intent is unknown.

Core Idea
The Prophet's actions are at least permissible; their higher status depends on evidence of intent.

In Usul al-Fiqh, understanding the legal value of the Prophet's actions (sunnah fi'liyyah) is essential for deriving rulings. Hanafi scholars have categorized these actions into four levels: mubah, mustahabb, wajib, and fard. This lesson examines how to identify the ruling for each prophetic act based on the known or unknown aspect (jihah) of the action, and the default ruling of ibaha when no other evidence exists.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the four categories
Learn the names (mubah, mustahabb, wajib, fard) and their approximate meanings. Mubah: permissible, no reward or punishment. Mustahabb: recommended, reward for doing, no punishment for leaving. Wajib: obligatory but with zanni evidence. Fard: obligatory with qati evidence.
The distinction between wajib and fard is unique to Hanafis but not used in this specific classification.
2
Identify the key principle
Understand that the ruling for us depends on the Prophet's intent (jihah). If his intent is known (e.g., he performed an action to teach obligation), we follow that. If unknown, default to mubah.
The principle is based on the impossibility of the Prophet doing makruh or haram.
3
Apply the principle to examples
Given a reported action (e.g., the Prophet eating watermelon), determine if evidence shows whether it was done as an obligation, recommendation, or mere permission. If no evidence, treat as mubah.
4
Recall the dalil
The proof is that it is impossible for the Prophet to have done something makruh or haram, so all actions are at least mubah until evidence elevates them.
This is a rational argument (dalil aqli) based on the Prophet's infallibility.
5
Note the distinction from other sunnah types
This classification applies only to actions (sunnah fi'liyyah). Statements (sunnah qawliyyah) and tacit approvals (sunnah taqririyyah) have their own rules.

Commentary
This passage categorizes the Prophet's actions (sunnah fi'liyyah) into four legal categories: mubah (permissible), mustahabb (recommended), wajib (obligatory), and fard (incumbent). The ruling for us is that we must follow them with the same intent as the Prophet. If the intent is unknown, we treat them as mubah (the minimal level). The proof given is that it is impossible for the Prophet to have done something makruh (disliked) or haram (forbidden). This principle ensures that all of his actions are at least permissible and may carry higher status based on evidence.

Key Terms
سنت فعلیہ (sunnah fi'liyyah): The actions of the Prophet (peace be upon him) that serve as a model for the community.
