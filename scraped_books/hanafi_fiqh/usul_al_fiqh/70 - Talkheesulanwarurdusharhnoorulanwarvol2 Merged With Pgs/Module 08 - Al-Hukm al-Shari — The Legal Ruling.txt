The Definition and Default Ruling of Amr (Command) in Hanafi Usul
Defining amr, its default obligation, and the dua modes of fulfillment.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define amr according to Hanafi usul, state that its default hukm is wujub, and distinguish between ada and qada in fulfilling a command.

Core Idea
A command (amr) from a superior to an inferior imposes obligation by default, not recommendation or permission.

This passage from Talkhees Ul Anwar lays the foundation for understanding legislative commands (amr) in usul al-fiqh. The author defines amr, establishes its default ruling as obligation, and then explains the two modes of fulfilling a commanded act: ada (exact performance) and qada (substitute performance). This distinction is crucial for determining when an act is considered discharged or requires repetition.

The definition excludes requests from equals or inferiors.
2

This holds even if the command comes after a prohibition.

This is a rational proof from the consequence.

Commentary
This lesson enters the discussion on amr (command). The author first gives a fā'idah: amr and nahy are not separate categories in usul; they are types of khaas because they each specify an action or its negation. Then he defines amr linguistically: it is the speech of a superior (musta'li) saying to another 'if'al' (do). This definition excludes requests from equals or inferiors. The legal ruling (hukm) of amr is that it imposes obligation (wujub), not recommendation (nadb), permission (ibahah), or suspension (tawaqquf). This applies whether the command comes after a prior prohibition or not. For example, if the law says 'do not eat' then later says 'eat', the command still indicates obligation. The proof is that the one who abandons a command deserves punishment (wa'id), which is the marker of obligation. After establishing that the amr necessitates obligation, the author explains that fulfilling a command can be done in two ways: ada (exact performance of the obligated act as required) and qada (performance of its substitute, e.g., making up a missed prayer). Ada is further divided into three: complete ada (fulfilling all conditions and pillars), deficient ada (missing some recommended aspects but still valid), and ada similar to qada (when the performance is after the due time but still considered ada in some contexts).

Key Terms
امر (amr): A command issued by a superior to an inferior using the imperative form 'if'al'.
اداء (ada): Performing the exact commanded act as required, in its proper time and manner.
قضاء (qada): Performing a substitute for the commanded act when the original is missed, such as making up a prayer.

Defining the ruling of command and its default nature

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the default ruling of a command (amr) in Hanafi usul and explain why it excludes recommendation, permissibility, and suspension.

Core Idea
A command from the Lawgiver defaults to obligation because abandoning it incurs divine threat.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle of Hanafi legal theory: every command (amr) in the primary sources yields obligation. Without this principle, a jurist might incorrectly assign a lower level of obligation, leading to errors in deriving rulings.

Guided Path

The default is wujub – obligation.
2
Understand what amr does not indicate
The passage explicitly excludes recommendation (nadb), permissibility (ibaha), and suspension (tawaqquf). Note why each is excluded.
The reason given: the one who abandons a command merits threat of punishment, which only applies to obligations.
3
Apply the principle to a new text
Take any imperative from the Quran or Sunnah (e.g., 'Establish prayer'). According to this rule, what is the default ruling?
Assume obligation unless there is a contextual indicator (qarinah) to shift it.

The requirement (ruling) of command is obligation. Not recommendation, permissibility, or suspension, whether that ruling is after prohibition or before it. That is, the ruling established by command will be obligatory to act upon. Recommended, permissible, and suspension cannot be the requirement of command because the one who abandons the command deserves threat (of punishment). The ruling of command has two types: (1) Ada' (performance) (2) Qada' (compensation). (1) Ada': It is the delivery of the very same thing that is obligatory by command. And it delivers exactly that thing which is made obligatory by command. (2) Qada': It is the delivery of the like of what is obligatory by it. And it is to deliver the like of what is obligatory by command. Ada' has three types: (1) Complete ada' (2) Deficient ada' (3) Ada' resembling qada'. (1) Complete ada': To perform something in the exact manner that the Lawgiver prescribed it, like performing the five daily prayers with congregation. (2) Deficient ada': Not to perform it in the manner prescribed by the Law, but to perform it with some deficiency or excess, like the prayer of a person praying alone. (3) Ada' resembling qada': Not to perform it in the manner that the Lawgiver made it obligatory, like the prayer of a latecomer. (1) Qada' like the rational: That qada' whose similarity to the original is understood by reason, without regard to the Law. Example: like making up a fast with a fast. (2) Qada' like the non-rational: That qada' whose similarity to the original is understood only by the Law, and reason is incapable of understanding it. Types of Performance and Compensation in Hanafi Usul
Categorizing performance and compensation of obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ada' (performance) and qada' (compensation), and identify the three subtypes of each based on their relation to the original command.

Core Idea
Fulfilling a command can be exact (ada') or by substitute (qada'), each with degrees of completeness.

After establishing that a command indicates obligation, the passage explains how to fulfill that obligation. Performance (ada') is doing exactly what was commanded, while compensation (qada') is doing a like act. Each has three subtypes depending on completeness and mode of resemblance. This classification helps in determining whether an act satisfies the demand or requires something else.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish ada' and qada'
Ada' is delivering the very same act (عين الواجب) that was commanded. Qada' is delivering a similar act (مثل الواجب) when the original cannot be performed.
The key difference: exact versus similar.
2
Study the three types of ada'
Complete ada': performed exactly as prescribed (e.g., congregational prayer). Deficient ada': performed with some deficiency (e.g., individual prayer). Ada' resembling qada': performed in a way that differs from the original prescription (e.g., latecomer's prayer).
The categories depend on how closely the performance matches the commanded manner.
3
Study the three types of qada'
Rationally similar: the substitute's likeness is understood by reason (e.g., fasting for a fast). Non-rationally similar: likeness known only from shari'ah (e.g., fidyah for a fast). Qada' resembling ada': a substitute that resembles the original performance (not explicitly defined in passage, but likely a reverse of the prior).
The basis for similarity: reason or revelation.

The Three Types of Adā' (Performance) in Hanafi Usul
Defines three types of performance in fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify any instance of performing an obligation into one of the three types of adā' and explain the basis for each classification.

Core Idea
Performance is not binary; Hanafi usul identifies three types: perfect, deficient, and performance-similar-to-make-up.

In Hanafi legal theory, fulfilling an obligation (adā') is not simply a matter of doing it versus missing it. The Lawgiver's prescription includes both the timing and the method. This lesson unpacks the three categories of adā' that determine whether your act is considered complete, incomplete, or even arguably a make-up.

Guided Path
1
Understand the basis: time and method
The Lawgiver (Shāriʿ) stipulates not only when an act must be done but also how (method, e.g., congregation for obligatory prayers). Any adā' must be evaluated on both time and method.
2
Identify complete performance (adā' kāmil)
Find when the act is performed exactly as prescribed in both time and method. Example: five daily prayers in congregation.
This is the ideal and most rewarding form.
3
Identify deficient performance (adā' qāṣir)
Find when the act is performed on time but the method deviates (e.g., praying alone). The obligation is fulfilled but with deficiency.
4
Identify performance similar to make-up (adā' mushābih bil-qadā')
Find when the method changes so significantly that it resembles a make-up but still occurs within time (e.g., latecomer joining imam; their prayer is considered adā' but with adjustments).
This category blurs the line between performance and make-up.

The Three Types of Qadā' (Make-up) in Hanafi Usul
Defines three types of make-up in fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a make-up act into one of the three types of qadā' and explain the basis for each classification.

Core Idea
Make-up (qadā') is not always like-for-like; Hanafi usul distinguishes rational, non-rational, and performance-like make-up.

When an obligation is missed, it must be made up (qadā'). However, the manner of make-up is not always identical to the original act. Hanafi jurists have identified three types of qadā' based on how the make-up relates to the original: one that reason can analogize, one that only revelation can justify, and one that resembles performance itself.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scope: qadā' compensates for missed obligations
Qadā' is performing an obligatory act after its prescribed time has elapsed. The key question is: what form should the make-up take?
2
Identify Qadā' mithl ma'qul (rational make-up)
The make-up resembles the original in a way that reason can understand, independently of Shari'ah. Example: making up a missed fast with another fast.
Reason sees that fasting another day compensates for a missed fast.
3
Identify Qadā' mithl ghayr ma'qul (non-rational make-up)
The make-up does not resemble the original in a rational sense; only Shari'ah specifies the substitute. Example: making up a fast with fidyah (feeding the poor).
Reason would not naturally think that feeding substitutes for fasting.
4
Identify Qadā' mushābih bil-adā' (make-up resembling performance)
This is a make-up that carries the meaning of performance (adā') either literally or legally. Example: catching the imam in ruku' counts as catching the whole rak'ah, even though part of it is technically make-up.
This category shows that some acts during performance are treated as make-up, blurring the line.
5
Apply to legal scenarios
Given a missed obligation and the Shari'ah's prescription for its make-up, determine which category it falls into.
This helps in understanding the wisdom behind certain rulings.

Commentary
This passage presents a conceptual classification of commanded acts (m'amur bihi) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It first divides acts based on the source of their goodness (husn): whether goodness is inherent (hasan li 'aynihi) or acquired from an external factor (hasan li ghayrihi).

(2) goodness that may drop due to an excuse, like prayer being waived during menstruation. (3) acts that share qualities with both types, like zakat: outwardly it seems wasteful, but its inner goodness (helping the poor) makes it beloved to Allah, so it is attached to hasan li 'aynihi in meaning.

(3) the goodness comes from a condition of the act, such as power (qudra).

Complete power (qudra kamila) includes ease, like ownership of nisab for zakat, but with the condition of a full year (hawlan al-hawl), making it easier. Definition and Classification of Prohibition (Nahy)
Defines prohibition and classifies by evil type

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define nahy and classify prohibited acts into the four categories of evil (qabih li'aynihi wad'an, qabih li'aynihi shar'an, qabih li ghayrihi wasfan, qabih li ghayrihi mujawiran) with examples.

Core Idea
Prohibition is a demand to refrain; its basis is the evil in the act either inherently or by attachment.

In this lesson, we unpack the precise definition of prohibition (nahy) as a command to refrain from a superior to an inferior. Then we explore a key classification: some acts are evil in themselves, while others become evil due to an external factor. Understanding this distinction is vital for Hanafi usul al-fiqh because it determines whether a prohibited act is intrinsically corrupt (fasid) or merely forbidden without corrupting the act's essence, and it affects rulings on invalidity and sin.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the definition
Learn the exact wording: 'Nahy is the statement of a speaker to another in a manner of superiority: Do not do.' Understand that it requires the speaker to be superior (musta'li) and the command to be a demand to abstain.
The condition of superiority distinguishes nahy from a simple request or advice.
2
Identify the two main categories
Recognize that nahy divides based on the nature of the evil (qabih) that triggers the prohibition. First, evil in itself (qabih li'aynihi): the act is inherently bad. Second, evil due to something else (qabih li ghayrihi): the act is permissible in itself but becomes evil because of an external factor.
This classification is fundamental in Hanafi usul for determining whether an act is intrinsically corrupt (fasid) or merely prohibited but valid.
3
Subdivide evil in itself
Evil in itself has two subtypes: (a) by nature (wad'an) – reason itself recognizes the evil, like disbelief (kufr); (b) by revelation (shar'an) – the Shari'ah declares it evil, even if reason cannot fully grasp it.
Example of evil by reason: kufr shukr al-mun'im (ingratitude to the Benefactor) is inherently evil by nature.
4
Subdivide evil due to something else
Evil due to something else also has two subtypes: (a) by attribute (wasfan) – the evil is inseparable from the act, like fasting on the Day of Sacrifice (the act of fasting is good, but the attribute of being on that day makes it prohibited); (b) by adjacency (mujawiran) – the evil is not inseparable but sometimes separates, like selling during Friday prayer call (the act of selling is permissible in itself but becomes prohibited when the call is made; after the prayer the prohibition lifts).
The passage cuts off, but standard Hanafi texts provide the example of bay' during adhan al-jumu'ah.

Distinguishing Sensory and Legal Actions in Hanafi Usul
Defines two action categories with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify any given action as either a sensory action (fi'l hissī) or a legal action (fi'l shar'ī) based on the Hanafi usul framework.

Core Idea
Sensory actions keep their pre-revelation meaning; legal actions are redefined by the Shariah.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, not all actions are treated equally. Some actions have meanings that are fixed by language and reason, unchanged by revelation. Others receive entirely new technical definitions from the Shariah. This distinction is fundamental for understanding how rulings are derived: a sensory action is ruled upon based on its known nature, while a legal action is ruled upon based on its Shariah-defined meaning. This lesson will give you the criteria to classify any action correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base meaning
Determine the meaning of the action in ordinary language and reason, before considering revelation. Is the action understood by common sense and custom?
Use pre-Islamic understanding as a reference point.
2
Check for Shariah transformation
Ask whether the Shariah has assigned a new, technical meaning to the action that differs from its original linguistic meaning. Does the Shariah define its conditions, pillars, or essence?
Legal actions often have specific conditions (shurut) and pillars (arkan) not present in the ordinary meaning.
3
Classify accordingly
If the meaning remains the same after revelation, it is a sensory action (fi'l hissī). If the meaning has been transformed, it is a legal action (fi'l shar'ī).
Examples: adultery (zina) remains 'unlawful sexual intercourse' – sensory; prayer (salat) becomes 'specific recitations and movements' – legal.
4
Note the usul implication
For sensory actions, the Shariah merely prohibits or permits them based on their inherent nature. For legal actions, the Shariah defines the action itself, so rulings derive from the Shariah definition, not the linguistic one.
This affects how we apply general commands and prohibitions (e.g., the command to pray refers to the legal, not linguistic, meaning).

Linguistic definition of rukhasat: al-yusr wa al-suhulah - ease and convenience.
Technical definition of rukhasat: Turning a ruling from difficulty to ease due to an excuse in the obligated person.
It is turning a ruling from difficulty to ease due to an excuse in the obligated person.
Rukhasat has two types.
(1) Rukhasat haqiqah (real concession)
(2) Rukhasat majaz (metaphorical concession).
Then each of these two has two types. Total makes four types.

Rukhasat haqiqah: It is that in which the azimat (original ruling) remains capable of being acted upon, i.e., whenever the azimat is established, the rukhasat also becomes a reality. Rukhasat haqiqah has two types.

The first type of rukhasat haqiqah: Ahag (stronger). As for the stronger type of real concession: it is what is deemed permissible. The first type, ahag, which is the strongest, is considered permissible. Example: like uttering a word of disbelief under duress. Even though all texts prohibit disbelief and it is forbidden, under duress one will not be held accountable for uttering a word of disbelief.

Ruling of the first type of rukhasat haqiqah: Its ruling is that taking the azimat is better, such that if the coerced person endures and is killed in the situation of duress, he is a martyr. The ruling of the first type is that acting upon the azimat is better, even if the coerced person endures and is killed in the situation of duress, he will be a martyr.

Second type of rukhasat haqiqah: Ghayr ahag (less strong). Explanation: And the second is what is deemed permissible while the cause exists, but its ruling is delayed. And the second type is that despite the existence of the cause, it is considered permissible, but its ruling is deferred. This type is weaker in strength than the first type, therefore it is called ghayr ahag. Example: like a traveler breaking the fast. The cause of prohibition, i.e., the existence of Ramadan, exists for him as well, but breaking the fast is still permissible, but its ruling is deferred, meaning after the journey ends, in residency, he makes it up.

Ruling of the second type of rukhasat haqiqah: Its ruling is that taking the azimat is better because its cause is complete. And its ruling is that acting upon the azimat is better than (the rukhasat).

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of rukhasat (concession) in usul al-fiqh. The author first gives the linguistic definition: ease and convenience. Then the technical definition: turning a ruling from difficulty to ease due to an excuse in the obligated person. Essentially, rukhasat is a dispensation granted because of a legitimate excuse.

The author then divides rukhasat into two main types: real (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz). Each of these is further subdivided into two, making four total. The passage focuses on real concession and its two subtypes.

Real concession (rukhsat haqiqah) is defined as that in which the azimat (the original ruling of determination) remains capable of being acted upon; that is, the azimat is not abrogated but remains valid. The first subtype is 'ahag' (stronger), which is deemed permissible (mubah). The example given is uttering a word of disbelief under coercion (ikrah). Even though the original ruling prohibits disbelief, under duress one is not held accountable. The ruling is that it is better to adhere to the azimat (i.e., to refrain from uttering disbelief and endure hardship), and if the coerced person is patient and killed, he is a martyr.

The second subtype is 'ghayr ahag' (less strong), where the cause for prohibition exists but the ruling is deferred. The example is a traveler breaking his fast in Ramadan; the cause (Ramadan) exists, but it is permissible to break the fast due to travel, and the ruling of qada (make-up) is deferred. This type is weaker because the cause of prohibition is still present, unlike the first type where the cause (prohibition of disbelief) is practically overridden by the excuse.

Important: In both cases, adhering to the original ruling (azimat) is considered better (awla). The concession is permissible but not preferred. This reflects the Hanafi principle that concessions are allowed due to necessity but the original ruling remains superior.

Key Terms
رخصت (rukhsat): A concession granted due to an excuse, turning a ruling from difficulty to ease.
عزیمت (azimat): The original, general ruling that applies normally without concession.
احق (ahaq): The stronger type of real concession where the cause of prohibition is removed by the excuse.
غیر احق (ghayr ahaq): The weaker type of real concession where the cause of prohibition remains but the ruling is deferred.

Classification of Sunnah Fi'liyyah and Ruling on Following It
Lists four categories of Prophetic actions and the ruling for emulating them

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four categories of sunnah fi'liyyah according to the Hanafi school, and apply the rule for emulating Prophetic actions based on known or unknown intention.

Core Idea
A Prophetic action's legal status for us mirrors the Prophet's intention; when unknown, default to mubah.

When we encounter an action performed by the Prophet (peace be upon him), how do we determine whether it is obligatory, recommended, or merely permissible for us to follow? The Hanafi school provides a clear classification based on the intention (jihah) with which the Prophet performed the act. This lesson explains the four categories and the default ruling when the intention is unknown.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
The passage lists four categories of Prophetic actions with respect to their legal ruling for us: mubah (permissible), mustahabb (recommended), wajib (obligatory), and fard (obligatory, sometimes synonymous with wajib). Recognize these as distinct levels.
Hanafis sometimes differentiate fard and wajib; here both are listed, but the principle applies to both.
2
Understand the governing principle: intention (jihah)
The ruling for us is determined by the intention (jihah) with which the Prophet performed the action. If his intention is known, we must follow the same level of obligation.
Jihah refers to the legal intent behind the action (e.g., whether he did it as an obligation or as a recommendation).
3
Apply the default rule when intention is unknown
If the Prophet's intention behind an action is not known, we assign the lowest possible ruling: mubah (permissible). This is the default because we cannot impose a higher obligation without evidence.
This ensures we do not overburden ourselves with obligations that are not clearly established.
4
Appreciate the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The proof for defaulting to mubah is rational: it is impossible that the Prophet performed a makruh (disliked) or haram (forbidden) action. Since we know he never did such acts, the only remaining possibility for an unknown intention is that the action is at most mubah.
This proof is a form of istidlal bi al-aql (rational inference) common in Hanafi usul.

The types of Sunnah Fi'liyyah, i.e., according to the ruling of the blessed actions of the Holy Prophet (peace be upon him) that apply to us, are four types: (1) Mubah (2) Mustahabb (3) Wajib (4) Fard. The ruling for the mentioned types: We are bound to act on those actions upon which the Prophet (peace be upon him) acted with the same intention (direction), and for those actions whose intention is not known, we will act upon them by placing them on the lowest level (mubah). Proof: Because it is not possible that the Prophet (peace be upon him) acted upon a makruh or haram action.

Commentary
This passage categorizes the Prophet's actions (sunnah fi'liyyah) in terms of their legal implications for us. The four categories are: (1) Mubah: actions that are permissible and not recommended. (2) Mustahabb: recommended actions. (3) Wajib: obligatory actions. (4) Fard: obligatory actions (some scholars differentiate, but here both are listed). The ruling is that we should emulate the Prophet's actions according to the intention (jihah) with which he performed them. If the intention is unknown, we assume the lowest level (mubah) because the Prophet never did anything makruh or haram. This is a rational proof: it is inconceivable that the Prophet would do a disliked or forbidden act.

Key Terms
سنت فعلیہ (sunnah fi'liyyah): The actions of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) that serve as a source of law.
جہت (jihah): The intention or direction with which the Prophet performed an action, determining its legal status for followers.
اباحت (ibahah): Permissibility; the default ruling when a Prophetic action's intention is unknown.
