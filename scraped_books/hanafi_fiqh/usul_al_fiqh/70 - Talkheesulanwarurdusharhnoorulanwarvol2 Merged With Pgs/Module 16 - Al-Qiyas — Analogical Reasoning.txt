In this lesson, we explore a central tool of Hanafi jurisprudence: Qiyas, or analogical reasoning. Definition of Qiyas: Linguistic and Technical
Defines qiyas linguistically and technically, explains asl and far'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and technical definitions of qiyas and identify its essential components (asl, far', 'illah).

Core Idea
Qiyas is the extension of a ruling from an original case to a new case due to a common effective cause ('illah).

This passage introduces qiyas (analogical reasoning), a cornerstone of Hanafi legal methodology. Understanding its precise definition and components is essential before you can apply qiyas to derive rulings. We will break down the linguistic meaning, technical formulation, and the key terms asl, far', and 'illah.

Guided Path
1
Learn the linguistic meaning
Qiyas linguistically means measurement or estimation, as in 'measure the shoe with the shoe'. This sets the stage for the technical term.
The verb qāsa (قاس) is used similarly.
2
Understand the technical definition
The technical definition is 'extending the ruling from the original (asl) to the branch (far') due to a common 'illah'. This is the core of qiyas.
3
Identify the three essential components
Identify asl (the original case with a known ruling), far' (the new case), and 'illah (the common effective cause that justifies the extension).
The asl is also called maqīs 'alayh; the far' is also called maqīs.
4
Recognize the condition of 'illah
The 'illah must be common (muttaḥidah) between asl and far'. Without a shared 'illah, qiyas is invalid.
Hanafi school requires the 'illah to be explicit or derived from the text.
5
Note that asl must have a textual basis
The asl must have a ruling from Quran, Sunnah, or ijma'. Qiyas cannot be performed from another qiyas.

The Four Pillars of Qiyas
Definition of the four pillars of qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and state the four pillars (arkān) of qiyas and explain why each is essential for a valid analogical argument.

Core Idea
Qiyas has four essential pillars: asl, far', 'illah, and hukm. Without any one, analogical reasoning fails.

This lesson lays the foundation for understanding qiyas, a key source of Islamic law. The text enumerates the four pillars that must be present for any valid analogical deduction. We will break down each pillar and its role.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the list
Commit to memory the four pillars: asl, far', 'illah, hukm.
This is the classical Hanafi enumeration.
2
Understand each term
Define each pillar: Asl is the original case with a known ruling; Far' is the new case; 'Illah is the common effective cause; Hukm is the ruling transferred from asl to far'.
The 'illah is the key component that justifies the extension.
3
Visualize the relationship
See how asl and far' share the 'illah, and the hukm moves from asl to far'.
This structure ensures a disciplined reasoning process.
4
Note the consequence
If any pillar is missing, the qiyas is invalid. The mujtahid must ensure all four are present.
This is critical for evaluating whether a qiyas argument is sound.

Qiyas Muttahid bi al-Naw': Definition and Ruling
Defines one type of qiyas and its ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qiyas muttahid bi al-naw' and state that it is not invalidated by differences (farq) between asl and far'.

Core Idea
Qiyas muttahid bi al-naw' remains valid despite superficial differences between the original and branch cases.

This lesson introduces a key classification of qiyas based on the type of ruling transferred. Understanding this classification helps you defend analogies against objections that focus on apparent differences. The Hanafi school holds that as long as the ruling type is the same, the qiyas is sound.

Guided Path
1
Define the term
Identify and memorize the Arabic definition: 'Al-muttahid bi al-naw'' means the extended ruling is of the same type as the original ruling.
2
Analyze the example
Study the example: father's guardianship over a minor boy is extended to a minor girl because both share the 'illah of minorhood. Note that the two differ in gender but the ruling type is the same.
The difference in mahall does not affect the validity of the qiyas.
3
State the ruling
Learn the ruling: 'la yubtilu bi al-farq' – it is not invalidated by pointing out differences between asl and far'.
This ruling protects many Hanafi analogies from being dismissed on minor grounds.

Qiyas: Conditions, Pillars, and Types in Hanafi Usul
Explanation of the conditions, pillars, and two types of qiyas based on the transmitted hukm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three conditions and four pillars of qiyas, and distinguish between mutahid bi al-naw' and mutahid bi al-jins using Hanafi definitions and examples.

Core Idea
Qiyas relies on a shared illa; its validity depends on the relationship between the asl and far' rulings — same type or same genus.

This passage from Talkhis al-Anwar lays out the foundational rules for qiyas in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It first enumerates the conditions that must be met for qiyas to be valid, then defines its four pillars, and finally classifies qiyas into two types based on how the ruling is transferred. Understanding these distinctions is essential for correctly applying analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions of valid qiyas
Read the passage to list the three conditions: (1) the ruling in the asl must be proven by a text, (2) the illa must be intelligible (ma'qul al-ma'na), (3) there must be no direct textual ruling on the far'.
These conditions ensure qiyas is used only when necessary and sound.
2
List the four pillars (arkan) of qiyas
The passage explicitly states the four pillars: asl (original), far' (branch), illa (cause), and hukm (ruling).
These are the essential components of any qiyas argument.
3
Understand the first type: mutahid bi al-naw'
In this type, the ruling transferred is of the same specific kind (naw') as the original ruling. The passage gives the example of a father's guardianship over a minor boy (asl) extended to a minor girl (far') via the illa of sifr (lack of discernment).
This type is not invalidated by pointing out differences between the cases, as long as the ruling is of the same type.
4
Understand the second type: mutahid bi al-jins
Here the ruling transferred is from the same genus (jins) as the original, but not identical. Example: the father's guardianship over property is removed when a daughter reaches puberty with intellect (asl), and the same cause removes guardianship over her person (far').
This type becomes invalid if the genus is not genuinely shared (tajnis fails).
5
Contrast the two types using the examples
Compare the examples: in mutahid bi al-naw', the exact same ruling (wilayat al-nikah) is transferred; in mutahid bi al-jins, the ruling (zawal al-wilaya) is the same in genus but not in every detail (property vs. person).
The key practical difference is that mutahid bi al-naw' tolerates differences, while mutahid bi al-jins requires genuine generic unity.

Commentary
This passage from Talkhis al-Anwar is an exposition on the conditions, pillars, and types of qiyas (analogical reasoning) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author first lists five conditions for the validity of qiyas: the original ruling must be proven by a textual source, the ruling must be based on a cause that is intelligible (ma'qul al-ma'na), the reasoning must be for a shar'i ruling not a linguistic one, and there must be no direct textual ruling on the branch. Then he states the four pillars of qiyas: asl (original case), far' (branch), illa (cause), and hukm (ruling). Next, he divides qiyas into two types based on the relationship between the ruling in the asl and the far': (1) Mutahid bi al-Naw' (same type): the ruling transferred is of the same kind as in the asl, like the father's guardianship over both a minor son and a minor daughter; this qiyas is not invalidated by mere differences between the cases. (2) Mutahid bi al-Jins (same genus): the ruling is from the same genus but not identical, like the removal of father's guardianship over a daughter's property and person when she attains puberty; this type of qiyas becomes invalid if the genus is not truly shared. The author emphasizes that the ruling in the far' must be derived from the same illa found in the asl.

Key Terms
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning: extending a ruling from an original case to a branch case due to a shared cause.
متحد با النوع (mutahid bi al-naw'): A type of qiyas where the ruling transferred to the branch is of the same specific type as the ruling in the original.
متحد بالجنس (mutahid bi al-jins): A type of qiyas where the ruling transferred is from the same general genus as the original ruling, but not identical.

Condition of Unity of Genus (Muttaḥid bi al-Jins) in Qiyas
Defines the condition of unity of genus in qiyas with an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the condition of unity of genus in qiyas, and distinguish valid from invalid analogies by verifying that the ruling in the branch belongs to the same genus as the ruling in the original.

Core Idea
Unity of genus between original and branch rulings is a condition for valid qiyas; without it, the analogy is invalid.

In Hanafi legal theory, qiyas (analogy) requires more than just a shared cause (ʿilla). The rulings themselves must belong to the same legal category, known as genus (jins). This lesson explores that condition through a classic example: the removal of a father's guardianship over his daughter's wealth and person. Understanding this condition prevents faulty analogies between rulings that are categorically different.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition
Read the definition: 'united in genus means that (the branch) must be of its (the original's) genus.' This establishes that for qiyas to be sound, the ruling in the branch must be of the same legal genus as the ruling in the original.
The term 'jins' here refers to the category of the ruling, not the subject matter.
2
Analyze the example
The example: A girl reaching puberty with intellect removes the father's guardianship over her wealth (original). The same cause (puberty with intellect) also removes guardianship over her person (branch). Note that both rulings are 'removal of guardianship' — the same genus.
The cause (ʿilla) is identical; the rulings are of the same genus.
3
Contrast with invalid qiyas
If the branch ruling were of a different genus (e.g., permissibility of marriage), the qiyas would be invalid. The passage states that without specification of genus (tajnīs), the qiyas is fāsid (invalid).
Tajnīs means identifying the common genus; its absence invalidates the analogy.
4
Apply the condition to a new scenario
Consider an original ruling: 'sale of food before possession is prohibited' (genus: prohibition). If the branch is 'sale of currency before possession' (also prohibition), the rulings share the genus 'prohibition' — qiyas is valid. If the branch were 'permissibility of exchange', genus differs — qiyas invalid.
Always check the genus of the ruling, not just the ʿilla.
5
Synthesize the rule
Formulate the condition: For a qiyas to be valid, the ruling in the farʿ must be of the same jins as the ruling in the aṣl. If they differ, the qiyas is invalid regardless of the ʿilla.
This condition is specific to Hanafi usul; other schools may not require it.

Commentary
This passage from Talkhīṣ al-Anwār explains the condition of 'muttaḥid bi al-jins' (united in genus) in analogical reasoning (qiyas). The author states that for qiyas to be sound, the ruling in the branch must belong to the same genus as the ruling in the original. The example given is the removal of a father's guardianship (wilāyat al-ab) over his daughter: when she reaches puberty with sound intellect, the guardianship over her wealth is removed. The same cause (ʿilla) — puberty with intellect — also removes guardianship over her person (her own affairs). Both rulings (over wealth and person) are of the same genus: removal of guardianship. If the rulings are not of the same genus, the qiyas is invalid (fāsid). The phrase 'فساده بمما نعية التجنيس' indicates that the invalidity arises from the absence of specifying the genus (tajnīs). In Hanafi usul, this condition ensures that the analogy is not between entirely different categories of rulings, preserving the integrity of the legal reasoning.
