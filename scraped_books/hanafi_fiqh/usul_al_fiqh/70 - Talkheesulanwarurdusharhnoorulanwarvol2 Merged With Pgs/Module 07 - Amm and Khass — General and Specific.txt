Definition and Types of Khass (Specific Word)
Defining khass and its three subtypes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-khass and classify its three subtypes (jins, naw', 'ayn) with examples.

Core Idea
Khass is a word coined exclusively for a known meaning — it can be specific by genus, species, or individual.

Words are divided into four types by form and lexicon: khass (specific), 'amm (general), mushtarak (shared), mu'awwal (interpreted). This lesson focuses on khass, which refers to a word whose meaning is known and exclusive. Understanding the levels of specificity within khass helps in interpreting how far a legal command extends.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of khass
Read the Arabic definition: 'kull lafz wudi'a li-ma'nan ma'lum 'ala al-infiraad'. A word coined exclusively for a known meaning. Note that exclusivity ('ala al-infiraad) is key.
In Urdu: 'خاص ہر وہ لفظ ہے جس کو معنی معلوم کے لئے وضع کیا گیا ہو علی الانفراد'.
2
Recognize the three subtypes
Khass is divided into: (1) khusus al-jins (specific by genus), (2) khusus al-naw' (specific by species), (3) khusus al-'ayn (specific by individual). Each differs in how many individuals the word applies to.
The subtypes are based on the level of generality within the meaning.
3
Apply each type to examples
For khusus al-jins: 'insan' (human) — applies to many individuals but is specific compared to 'animal'. For khusus al-naw': 'rajul' (man) — more specific than 'insan', still applies to many. For khusus al-'ayn: 'Zayd' — refers to one particular person.
Memorize the examples: insan, rajul, Zayd.
4
Distinguish between the types
Compare the scope: 'insan' covers all humans (genus), 'rajul' covers only adult males (species), 'Zayd' covers a single individual. The fewer individuals it applies to, the more specific.
This distinction is important in usul to determine how a word is interpreted in legal texts.

Khaas in Hanafi Usul: Definition, Types, and Legal Effect
Definition and classification of khaas with a dispute example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khaas, classify a given word into its three types (khusus al-jins, khusus al-naw', khusus al-'ayn), and explain why khaas is qat'i in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Khaas is a word that definitively indicates a single known meaning without possibility of interpretation.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, words are classified by their scope of meaning. Khaas (specific) is a fundamental category: it is a word placed for a single known meaning, used individually. This means that khaas texts are definitive (qat'i) and do not require further clarification. Understanding khaas is crucial for deriving rulings, as it determines whether a Quranic verse or hadith is absolutely binding on its specific subject. This lesson unpacks the definition, three types of khaas, and the practical example of ta'dil al-arkan to illustrate how khaas functions in juristic disputes.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of khaas
Khaas is every word placed for a known meaning individually. Note the key elements: wad' (placement for a meaning), ma'na ma'lum (known meaning), and ala al-infiraad (individually).
This definition excludes words that are not placed for a single meaning (e.g., mushtarak).
2
Identify the three types of khaas
Distinguish between khusus al-jins (genus-specific, e.g., insan), khusus al-naw' (species-specific, e.g., rajul), and khusus al-'ayn (individual-specific, e.g., Zayd).
The types differ in scope of meaning, but all are qat'i.
3
Apply the ruling of khaas: qat'i and does not admit bayan
Understand that khaas is definitive; its meaning is fixed and cannot be reinterpreted. This is why it takes priority over probable evidence.
This is a core Hanafi principle distinguishing it from other schools.
4
Analyze the dispute on ta'dil al-arkan
See how the Hanafi school uses the khaas principle: the Quranic command 'irka'u wa-sjudu' is khaas for the acts of bowing and prostration, so only those are fard from the Quran. The hadith on ta'dil, being ahad, cannot elevate the ruling to fard; it only makes it wajib.
This example shows the practical application of khaas in a cross-madhhab dispute.
5
Recognize that amr is a type of khaas
Note the benefit: commands and prohibitions fall under khaas, not a separate category. Begin to think of the imperative form as a specific demand.
This extends the concept of khaas beyond nouns to verbs.

The Ruling of Khaas (Specific Text)
Definition and illustrative example of khaas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khaas, explain its qat‘i nature, and apply the principle to distinguish fard from wajib in the example of ta‘dil al-arkan.

Core Idea
Khaas is definitive and needs no clarification; it determines fard, not wajib.

In this lesson, we explore the concept of khaas (specific text) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. A khaas text is inherently clear and applies directly to its subject without requiring further explanation. The passage then illustrates this principle with a concrete ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam al-Shafi‘i regarding the ruling of ‘adjusting the pillars’ (ta‘dil al-arkan) in prayer. By understanding the nature of khaas, you will see how the Hanafi school derives different levels of obligation (fard vs. wajib) from different types of evidence.

Guided Path
1
Define khaas
Read the source definition: khaas is a text that includes its specific subject definitively (qat‘i) and does not require further explanation because it is clear in itself.
2
Identify the definitive nature
Notice that khaas is described as 'la yahtamilu al-bayan'—it does not admit clarification. This means the ruling it conveys is immediate and cannot be extended or reduced without a clear exception (takhsis).
3
Apply to the example
Consider the Quranic command 'wa rka‘u wa sujdu'. According to the Hanafi view, this is khaas for the acts of ruku‘ and sujud themselves, not for their perfection. The command is decisive for the mere existence of these actions.
4
Distinguish fard and wajib
The Quranic command makes ruku‘ and sujud fard (because the dalil is qat‘i). The hadith of the Bedouin, which teaches additional details like adjusting the pillars, is zanni (speculative). Therefore the additional requirement is wajib, not fard.
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school; in the Shafi‘i school, fard and wajib are synonymous.
5
Observe the thamarah (practical consequence)
If a person omits ta‘dil al-arkan, according to the Hanafi school the prayer is valid but makruh (disliked). According to the Shafi‘i school, the prayer is invalid. This difference stems directly from the usul principle of khaas.

Thus, the actual bowing and prostrating are fard by the Quran, while perfecting them is wajib by the hadith. This example demonstrates how the usul principle of khaas affects the derivation of fiqh rulings.

واجب (wajib): An obligatory act established by a speculative (zanni) dalil, such as a khabar wahid.

Classification of Commands: Unconditional vs Time-Bound
Classifying amr into unconditional and time-bound, with focus on the first type (zarf) and its subcategories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a command as unconditional (mutlaq) or time-bound (muqayyad) and identify the four subcategories of time as a container (zarf) with their corresponding rulings on intention specification.

Core Idea
Time as a container makes intention specification a condition; time as a measure may not require intention.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, commands are categorized by their relationship with time. Some obligations never lapse, while others expire when their time passes. This lesson unpacks the first major division and the crucial role of time as a 'container' (zarf) for the five daily prayers, where specifying the intention is a condition for validity.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish mutlaq from muqayyad
Identify whether the commanded act remains obligatory after the time passes (mutlaq) or lapses with it (muqayyad). Example: zakah is mutlaq; the five daily prayers are muqayyad.
2
Recognize the first type of muqayyad: zarf
When time is a container (zarf), it is a condition for performance and a cause for obligation. This applies to the five daily prayers.
Time as container has four subcategories based on when the act is performed.
3
Map the four subcategories of zarf
If prayer is offered at the beginning (first part), later in valid time (connected part), after valid time but before qada (deficient part), or entirely missed (complete time for qada), each attributes the obligation to a different part of time.
The ruling of specifying intention applies to all, because the time is a container.

Four Types of Time-Bound Commands: Rulings on Intention
Classifying the four types of time-bound commands and their rulings on intention.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of time-bound commands and state the Hanafi ruling on intention for each type according to Imam Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
Not all time-bound acts require intention; Ramadan fasting is valid without intention according to Abu Hanifa.

Building on the classification of commands, this lesson focuses on the four distinct roles time can play in a time-bound command. Each type carries a different ruling on whether intention (niyyah) is a condition. Understanding these distinctions is essential for correct practice, especially for acts like fasting and making up missed prayers.

Guided Path
1
Recall the four types
The passage divides time-bound commands into four types: (1) zarf (container), (2) mi'yar (measure) and also cause, (3) mi'yar only (not cause), (4) mushtabah (ambiguous).
We have already covered type 1 in the previous lesson.
2
Analyze type 2: mi'yar as cause
Time is a measure (determining the extent of the act) and also a cause for obligation, but not a condition for performance. Example: the month of Ramadan is the cause for fasting to be obligatory, and it measures the number of fasts (30 days).
Intention is not a condition according to Imam Abu Hanifa.
3
Analyze type 3: mi'yar without cause
Time is a measure for the commanded act but not a cause for its obligation. Example: making up missed Ramadan fasts (qada) — the time (e.g., a day in Shawwal) measures the act but does not cause the obligation; the obligation comes from the missed Ramadan.
Intention is a condition here.
4
Analyze type 4: ambiguous time
Time is ambiguous, resembling both a container and a measure. Example: the time for a missed prayer whose exact time is unknown (e.g., a missed prayer from an unknown day).
An absolute intention suffices, but one cannot intend a nafl prayer; the act must be intended as the missed obligation.
5
Synthesize the rulings on intention
Summarize: Type 1 (zarf) → specification required; Type 2 (mi'yar + cause) → intention not required (Imam Abu Hanifa); Type 3 (mi'yar, no cause) → intention required; Type 4 (ambiguous) → absolute intention suffices, but not nafl.
Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) for type 2: if one wakes up in Ramadan without intention from the night, the fast is still valid according to Abu Hanifa.

Definition and Ruling of General (Aam)
Defines 'aam', its types, and its definitive ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'aam' (general term) according to Hanafi usul, distinguish its two types (specified and unspecified), and state its definitive ruling (qat'i) including the permissibility of abrogating a specific ruling.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, the general term (aam) is definitive (qat'i) in meaning until specification (takhsis) is proven.

One of the foundational concepts in usul al-fiqh is the classification of texts into general (ʿāmm) and specific (khāṣṣ). How a general term conveys its ruling has profound implications for legal derivation. This passage from Noor al-Anwar defines 'aam' and establishes its definitive nature in the Hanafi school, setting the stage for understanding specification (takhṣīṣ) and abrogation (naskh).

Guided Path
1
1. Grasp the definition of 'aam'
Read the Arabic and translation carefully. Note that 'aam' is defined as a word that comprehensively (bil-shumul) includes all individuals of a class whose limits (hudud) are agreed upon. This means the term applies to every member without exception.
The condition 'agreeing limits' distinguishes 'aam' from ambiguous terms (mujmal).
2
2. Distinguish the two types of 'aam'
The passage divides 'aam' into two: (a) general from which some individuals have been specified (takhṣīṣ) by evidence, and (b) general from which no specification has occurred. The first type still remains general for the remaining individuals.
Specification does not destroy generality; it merely excludes some individuals.
3
3. Understand the definitive ruling of 'aam'
The passage states that 'aam' imposes its ruling definitively (qatʿan) on all individuals it covers. In Hanafi usul, this means the ruling is certain and binding until a proof of specification arrives. This is a key difference from the Shafiʿi school, which views generality as probabilistic (ẓannī).
The definitive nature is why abrogation of a specific ruling by a general text is possible – the general is stronger.
4
4. Recognize the consequence: naskh of khass by aam
Because the general is definitive, it can abrogate a previously established specific ruling. This is a debated point across schools; the Hanafi position allows it under conditions.
This illustrates how understanding the nature of 'aam' affects higher-level legal operations.

Mu'awwal is a shared word whose one meaning is preferred by dominant opinion; acting upon it is obligatory but speculative.

In Hanafi usul, some words carry multiple meanings. When a shared word (mushtarak) has one meaning that becomes prevalent through tarjih (preference), it is called Mu'awwal. This lesson defines Mu'awwal, explains its legal status, and illustrates it with the Qur'anic term 'quru'.

Defines and classifies mutashabihat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mutashabih al-ma'na and mutashabih al-murad and classify Qur'anic examples accordingly.

Core Idea
Mutashabihat: meaning unknown (ma'na) or referent obscure (murad).

The Qur'an contains verses that are clear (muhkam) and others that are ambiguous (mutashabih). This lesson unpacks the two categories of mutashabihat as defined in Hanafi usul, clarifying their nature and the required response of the believer.

Guided Path
1
Define mutashabih
Identify the definition: a verse or word whose intended meaning is completely unreachable by human effort; belief in its truth is obligatory.
The term implies hope of comprehension is cut off.
2
Distinguish the two types
Differentiate mutashabih al-ma'na (even the lexical meaning is unknown, e.g., huruf muqatta'at) from mutashabih al-murad (the lexical meaning is known but the actual referent is known only to Allah, e.g., 'Hand of Allah').
The key is whether the basic meaning is known.

Understand the classification criteria

Haqiqah and Majaz: Literal and Figurative Language in Usul
Defines haqiqah and majaz as two categories of word usage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define haqiqah and majaz, distinguish between them, and apply the default rule that haqiqah is presumed in legal texts unless a qarinah indicates majaz.

Core Idea
Haqiqah is the default; a word is always taken in its original meaning unless there is evidence for figurative use.

This lesson introduces the foundational usul concept of haqiqah (literal) and majaz (figurative) word usage. Understanding this distinction is essential for interpreting the Quran and Sunnah. The Hanafi school presumes haqiqah unless a qarinah (contextual indicator) points to majaz. We will examine the definitions, rulings, and examples from the source text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The passage first lists four types of word usage (haqiqah, majaz, sarih, kinayah) but focuses on haqiqah and majaz. Recognize that haqiqah and majaz are the primary categories for interpreting texts.
Sarih and kinayah are defined separately in later lessons.
2
Define haqiqah
Haqiqah is defined as a word used for its original meaning (ma wudi'a lahu). Its ruling is that the original meaning is established, whether specific or general.
Example: 'salah' in Arabic originally means supplication; that is its haqiqah.
3
Define majaz
Majaz is using a word for a meaning other than its original due to a connection (munasabah) between the two meanings.
Example: using 'lion' for a brave man because of the connection of bravery.
4
Apply the default rule
In Hanafi usul, when encountering a word, assume haqiqah unless there is a qarinah (contextual indicator) for majaz. This presumption governs how we derive rulings from texts.
If a word has both a literal and a figurative meaning, the literal takes precedence without qarinah.
5
Work through the example
The passage uses 'salah' as an example. Its lexical haqiqah is supplication. In the Quran and Sunnah, it often refers to ritual prayer. This is either majaz (if derived from the original meaning) or haqiqah shar'iyyah (a new coinage by the Lawgiver). According to the passage, haqiqah refers strictly to lexical meaning.
Later usul works introduce haqiqah shar'iyyah as a separate category; the passage does not address that.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Not all concessions are true relief — some are metaphorical, and acting on the complete type is sinful.

In Hanafi usul, the term rukhsah (concession) is applied both to genuine alleviations and to rulings that resemble concessions only in form. Khabar Mashhur and Khabar Wahid: Definitions and Rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Khabar Mashhur and Khabar Wahid by their definitions and legal authority, and apply the Hanafi classification to determine the epistemic weight of a report.

Core Idea
Khabar Mashhur yields near-certainty (ilm al-tuma'nina); Khabar Wahid obligates action without certainty.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, reports (akhbar) are classified by their epistemic force, determining whether they demand belief, action, or both. This lesson focuses on two categories: Khabar Mashhur—originally solitary but later widespread—and Khabar Wahid—which never reached widespread transmission in the early generations. Understanding their definitions and rulings is crucial for correctly weighing evidence in legal derivation.

Guided Path

Contrast with mutawatir: mashhur does not achieve tawatur in the earliest generations.

It yields 'ilm al-tuma'nina (knowledge of tranquility), which is near-certainty. It is obligatory to believe in it; its denier is fasiq but not kafir. Compare its rank: above wahid but below mutawatir.
The Hanafi school uses 'ilm al-tuma'nina as a distinct epistemic category.

Define Khabar Wahid
Identify the definition: a report narrated by one or more narrators that never reaches tawatur or shuhrah (the level of mashhur) in the first three generations (Sahabah, Tabi'un, Tabi al-Tabi'in). Even if later narrators become numerous, it remains wahid.
The key condition is the state in the early generations, not later numbers.

It obligates action (wajib al-amal) but does not yield certain knowledge. It is not obligatory to believe in its content, only to act upon it if authentic.

The Hanafi school uses these categories to determine legal authority: Mutawatir yields certainty, Mashhur yields near-certainty, Wahid yields only action.

Key Terms
خبر متواتر (khabar mutawatir): Report transmitted by such a large number that collusion on lying is impossible; yields certainty.
خبر مشہور (khabar mashhur): Originally ahad then becomes widespread in later generations, transmitted by a number precluding collusion; yields 'ilm al-tuma'nina.
خبر واحد (khabar wahid): Report transmitted by one or more but not reaching tawatur or shuhrah in the early generations; demands action without certainty.
علم طمانیت ('ilm al-tuma'nina): Knowledge of tranquility: a level of conviction between certainty and dominant conjecture.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi usul, Khabar Mashhur yields near-certainty (ilm al-tuma'nina) and demands belief, while Khabar Wahid obligates action without certain knowledge.
