Silence as Permission to Prevent Deception: Hanafi vs Zufar
A usul dispute over silence as permission to prevent deception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that necessity to prevent public deception (daf' al-ghurur) can transform ambiguous silence into legal permission (idhn), and evaluate Zufar's counter-argument based on probability.

Core Idea
Silence becomes permission when necessary to prevent public harm, even if ambiguous.

In this passage, we explore a nuanced Hanafi usul dispute: can a master's silence when seeing his slave engage in trade be considered permission for the slave to continue trading? The Hanafi school answers yes, based on the obligation to prevent deception of the public. Imam Zufar dissents, arguing that silence is too ambiguous to ground a legal ruling. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind each position and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The core question: Does a master's silence upon seeing his slave buying and selling constitute permission for the slave to trade?
The dispute is over the legal effect of silence in a context where the public could be deceived.
2
State the Hanafi position and its basis
The Hanafi school (Imam Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad) holds that the silence is permission. Their basis is the necessity to prevent harm to the public (daf' al-ghurur) and the obligation to block deception (sadd al-dhara'i).
They argue that if the silence were not treated as permission, people would be misled into thinking the slave is authorized, leading to harm.
3
State Zufar's objection
Imam Zufar argues that silence could be due to consent or extreme anger. Since it is ambiguous, it cannot serve as definitive proof.
Zufar applies a stricter standard of legal proof, requiring explicit or unambiguous evidence.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response implicitly relies on the principle that preventing public harm outweighs the ambiguity. The most likely interpretation (consent) is adopted to avoid deception.
This reflects the Hanafi use of istihsan and sadd al-dhara'i in shaping rulings.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under the Hanafi view, contracts made by the slave are valid because he is considered permitted. Under Zufar's view, contracts are invalid unless explicit permission is established.
This difference affects the rights of third parties who trade with the slave.

Commentary
This passage discusses a third type of bayan through silence: bayan established by the necessity to prevent public deception (darurat daf' al-ghurur). The example given is of a master who sees his slave engaging in buying and selling but remains silent. According to the Hanafi school (Imam Abu Hanifa and his disciples), this silence is treated as explicit permission (idhn) for the slave to trade, because if it were not so, people interacting with the slave would be deceived into thinking the slave is authorized, leading to harm. Preventing such harm is obligatory. Imam Zufar disagreed, arguing that the master's silence is ambiguous—it could indicate consent or it could be due to extreme anger that prevents him from speaking. Since a mere probability cannot serve as legal proof, the silence should not be considered permission. The Hanafi position relies on the principle of sadd al-dhara'i (blocking means to harm) and the obligation to prevent deception, prioritizing public welfare over strict probability. This dispute illustrates a key difference in how the Hanafi school treats circumstantial evidence versus strict textual proof.

إِذْن (idhn): Permission, explicitly or implicitly given, for an action like trade.

Istishab al-Hal: A Dafi' Not a Mulzim
Explaining the evidentiary limit of istishab al-hal as a dafi' not mulzim.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that istishab al-hal only repels claims and does not establish obligations, using the examples of shuf'ah and mafqud.

Core Idea
Istishab al-hal defends the current state but cannot create new rights.

Istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) is a foundational usul tool, but the Hanafi school imposes a critical limit on its use. This passage draws a sharp line: istishab can protect you from a claim, but it cannot force someone else to do something. Through two concrete fiqh examples—shuf'ah and the missing person—we see how this boundary operates in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
State the Hanafi rule: Istishab al-hal is a proof (hujja) only for repelling (daf') not for imposing (ilzam). Understand the key terms: dafi' (repeller) and mulzim (imposer).
2
Analyze the shuf'ah example
Read the scenario: one partner sells his share, the other claims pre-emption. The buyer denies the claimant's ownership. The claimant relies on istishab of prior possession. The ruling: his istishab repels the buyer's denial but does not force the buyer to accept pre-emption. Understand why: for ilzam, clear evidence is needed.
Note the buyer's statement is accepted (his denial stands) because the claimant cannot prove ownership afresh.
3
Analyze the mafqud example
A missing person (mafqud al-khabar): we presume he is alive to repel claims against his wealth (e.g., not dividing it among heirs), but we do not allow him to inherit from others. The same principle: istishab protects his own rights but cannot create a right to inherit.
Contrast: other schools might allow him to inherit based on the same presumption.
4
Generalize the scope
The text says 'many other issues in fiqh' follow this pattern. Recognize that istishab is a defensive tool, not an offensive weapon. For example, a person in possession of an item can repel a claim of ownership but cannot use istishab to demand payment for it.
5
Distinguish from other madhahib
Unlike the Shafi'i school, which may use istishab both to repel and to impose duties, the Hanafi school restricts it to daf'. This difference leads to practical variations in rulings.
The thamarah al-khilaf: in the mafqud case, Shafi'is might allow him to inherit, Hanafis do not.

Commentary
This passage discusses the principle of istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity of a prior state) and its evidentiary weight in the Hanafi school. The author states that istishab is valid as a dafi' (a means to repel a claim) but not as a mulzim (a means to impose a duty). The text provides two examples. First, in shuf'ah (pre-emption): if a co-owner sells his share and the other co-owner claims pre-emption, the buyer may deny the claimant's ownership of the remaining share, claiming it is a loan. The claimant's proof is his prior possession (istishab). According to Hanafis, this istishab suffices to repel the buyer's denial (i.e., the claimant does not need to prove ownership) but it does not force the buyer to accept the pre-emption; for that, the claimant must provide clear evidence. Second, in the case of a missing person (mafqud al-khabar): he is considered alive for himself to prevent division of his wealth, but he cannot inherit from others because his life is only presumed to repel claims, not to establish new rights. This illustrates the limited scope of istishab as a proof.

مَفْقُودُ الْخَبَرِ (mafqūd al-khabar): A person whose whereabouts are unknown and whose life or death is uncertain.

Istihsan's Four Bases: When Qiyas is Overridden
Lists the four bases of istihsan and sets the stage for detailed examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four bases of istihsan and explain how each overrides qiyas jali in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Istihsan overrides clear qiyas only when based on one of four recognized proofs.

You already know that qiyas (analogy) is a principal method of deriving rulings. But what happens when a clear qiyas leads to a ruling that conflicts with an established tradition, consensus, necessity, or a stronger hidden analogy? The Hanafi school resolves this conflict through istihsan, a proof that takes precedence. This lesson defines istihsan and lists its four legitimate bases.

Guided Path
1
Define istihsan in Hanafi usul
Istihsan is a source of law that opposes clear qiyas. It is not arbitrary but based on specific proofs.
Istihsan literally means 'to deem something good'. In usul, it is a technical term for a proof that overrides qiyas.
2
List the four bases of istihsan
From the Arabic text, extract the four bases: athar (tradition), ijma' (consensus), darurah (necessity), and qiyas khafi (hidden analogy).
These are the only recognized bases for istihsan in the Hanafi school.
3
Explain how each base conflicts with qiyas jali
Clear qiyas would produce a certain ruling, but each of these four proofs indicates a different ruling. The mujtahid abandons the qiyas and adopts the ruling indicated by the overriding proof.
For example, a hadith (athar) might permit something that qiyas would prohibit.
4
Distinguish istihsan from arbitrary preference
Istihsan is not personal opinion; it is grounded in recognized shar'i proofs. Without such a proof, the qiyas jali remains.
This refutes the misconception that istihsan is a license for whimsical rulings.
5
Note cross-madhhab disagreement
The Shafi'i school generally rejects istihsan as an independent source, arguing that all rulings must be derived directly from Quran, Sunnah, ijma', or qiyas.
This difference leads to practical variations in furu' rulings.

The Hanafi Rule for Preferring Qiyas Jali over Qiyas Khafi by Strength of Effect
Discusses the rule for preferring qiyas jali over qiyas khafi (istihsan) based on strength of effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the strength of the effect (athar) determines whether qiyas jali or qiyas khafi (istihsan) is preferred, and evaluate examples accordingly.

Core Idea
The strength of the effect (athar) of the 'illa, not its apparentness, determines which qiyas is preferred.

In Hanafi usul, istihsan is often understood as a type of hidden analogy (qiyas khafi) that may override manifest analogy (qiyas jali). But when should one be preferred over the other? This lesson examines the rule that the decisive factor is the strength of the effect (quwwat al-athar) of the underlying cause ('illa), not whether it is apparent or hidden. The author of Nur al-Anwar illustrates this with three examples where istihsan overrides qiyas jali due to a stronger effect, and one example where qiyas jali is preferred.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
State the Hanafi rule for preferring one qiyas over another based on strength of effect.
The rule is: the criterion is strength/weakness of effect, not apparentness or hiddenness.
2
Understand the basis
Recognize that Hanafis define 'illa by its effect (athar), not by mere correspondence (tard).
This contrasts with the Shafi'i approach of tard.
3
Apply to examples
Analyze the three given examples where istihsan overrides qiyas jali and identify why the effect is stronger.
Examples: custom shoe order (ijma), vessel purification (necessity), birds' leftover (hidden beak purity).
4
Identify the exception
Note that if the hidden effect of qiyas jali is sound and the istihsan has hidden corruption, qiyas jali is preferred.
The prostration example shows this: bowing by qiyas is valid, but istihsan rejects it due to hidden corruption.
5
Compare with Shafi'i
Distinguish the Hanafi approach from the Shafi'i approach of tard.
Shafi'is reject istihsan; Hanafis treat it as qiyas khafi with a stronger effect.

Commentary
This passage from Nur al-Anwar with its Urdu commentary Ashraf al-Anwar discusses a critical usul rule: the basis for preferring qiyas jali (manifest analogy) over qiyas khafi (hidden analogy, which is a form of istihsan) or vice versa. The author first provides examples of istihsan that override qiyas jali: the permissibility of a custom-based order for a non-existent item (istihsan by ijma), the purification of vessels despite the difficulty of squeezing out impurity (istihsan by necessity), and the purity of birds' leftover water (istihsan by qiyas khafi). Then he clarifies that the first three types of istihsan (by text, by ijma, by necessity) are always preferred over qiyas. The confusion is only between qiyas jali and qiyas khafi. The rule is: the determining factor is the strength (quwwa) of the effect (athar) of the 'illa. If the effect of the qiyas khafi (istihsan) is stronger, it is preferred; if the effect of the qiyas jali is stronger, it is preferred. This is contrasted with the Shafi'i position of tard (simple concomitance). The Hanafi position is that the 'illa is considered 'illa by its effect (athar), not by mere correspondence. The passage defends Abu Hanifa against the criticism that istihsan goes outside the four proofs, arguing that istihsan is actually a stronger type of qiyas. Finally, it gives an example where qiyas jali is preferred: reciting a verse of prostration in prayer; by qiyas, one may bow instead of prostrating because both involve humility, but by istihsan, bowing does not suffice for the required prostration. This illustrates that when the hidden effect of qiyas jali is sound and the istihsan has a hidden corruption, qiyas jali takes precedence.

Key Terms
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): A method of legal derivation that abandons qiyas jali in favor of a stronger effect (athar) based on text, ijma, necessity, or hidden qiyas.
القياس الجلي (al-qiyas al-jali): A clear analogy where the effective cause ('illa) is apparent and easily discernible.
القياس الخفي (al-qiyas al-khafi): A hidden analogy that is not immediately apparent; often synonymous with istihsan.
الأثر (al-athar): The effect or consequence of the 'illa in establishing a ruling; its strength determines preference.

Istihsan in Action: Two Examples from Prayer and Sales
Explains preferring istihsan over qiyas jali through two fiqh examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi rule of istihsan, distinguish it from qiyas jali, and apply it to the given examples of sajdah tilawah and price disputes.

Core Idea
Istihsan overrides manifest analogy when a hidden analogy reveals a stronger legal effect.

This lesson examines how the Hanafi school employs istihsan (juristic preference) to set aside a manifest analogy (qiyas jali) in favor of a hidden analogy (qiyas khafi) that better reflects the law's purpose. Two concrete examples – the recitation of a sajdah verse during prayer and a dispute over sale price before delivery – illustrate how istihsan operates. By understanding these examples, you will grasp the disciplined nature of istihsan and its distinction from arbitrary preference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the manifest qiyas
For each example, determine what the manifest analogy (qiyas jali) would rule. For sajdah: ruku' and sajdah share humility, so ruku' could substitute. For the price dispute: only the buyer swears because the seller is not claimed against.
Qiyas jali relies on apparent similarity.
2
Identify the istihsan ruling
State what istihsan rules instead. For sajdah: ruku' does not suffice; actual prostration is required. For the price dispute: both seller and buyer swear, and the sale is rescinded.
Istihsan departs from the manifest analogy.
3
Locate the hidden analogy
Find the underlying analogy (qiyas khafi) that supports the istihsan. For sajdah: sajdah is ultimate veneration; in prayer, ruku' and sajdah are distinct and non-substitutable. For price dispute: each party is both claimant and denier, so both must swear.
The hidden analogy considers deeper legal purposes.
4
Analyze the effect comparison
Explain why the hidden analogy is considered stronger in effect. For sajdah: the distinct status of sajdah as ghayah al-ta'zim makes it non-substitutable. For price: mutual claims create a need for mutual oaths to achieve justice.
The effect (athar) of the hidden analogy outweighs the apparent similarity.
5
Generalize the rule
The text states that istihsan based on hidden qiyas can be extended to other cases, unlike istihsan based on text, consensus, or necessity, which are completely detached from qiyas.
This shows the systematic nature of istihsan in Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage is an expanded commentary on the principle that istihsan can be preferred over qiyas jali (manifest analogy) when the istihsan has a strong inner effect. The author, commenting on the matn of Nur al-Anwar, provides two fiqh examples to illustrate this. The first example concerns a person reciting an ayah of sajdah during prayer. By qiyas jali, since both ruku' and sajdah involve humility, one could substitute ruku' for the sajdah tilawah, and this is further supported by the Quranic verse 'wa kharra raaki'an wa anaab' where ruku' is used figuratively for prostration. However, the preferred istihsan holds that ruku' does not suffice because sajdah is the ultimate expression of veneration, and in prayer itself ruku' and sajdah are independent acts that cannot replace each other. Hence, the istihsan is based on a hidden analogy (qiyas khafi) that considers the distinct status of sajdah. The second example is a sales dispute before delivery: if buyer and seller disagree on the price, qiyas jali would require the seller to deliver without swearing, but istihsan requires both to swear because each is both claimant and denier, leading to rescission of the sale upon mutual oaths. The author concludes that istihsan based on hidden qiyas can be extended to other cases, unlike istihsan based on text, consensus, or necessity, which are completely detached from qiyas.

Key Terms
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Preferring a ruling that is stronger in effect over a manifest analogy (qiyas jali), often based on a hidden analogy (qiyas khafi) or other considerations.
القياس الجلي (al-qiyas al-jali): Manifest analogy where the illah is apparent and the similarity between asl and far is obvious.
القياس الخفي (al-qiyas al-khafi): Hidden analogy where the illah is not immediately apparent but is inferred from a deeper consideration of the law's purpose.
سجدة التلاوة (sajdat al-tilawah): Prostration required upon reciting or hearing a specific verse of the Quran.

When Qiyas is Overridden: Istihsan and Substituting Ruku for Sajdah Tilawah

Core Idea
Istihsan operates by preferring a hidden but sounder qiyas over a clear analogy when the latter leads to a flawed result.

This passage presents a classic Hanafi usul dispute about whether ruku can substitute for sajdah tilawah during prayer. The surface-level analogy (qiyas jali) says no, but a deeper investigation reveals a hidden analogy (qiyas khafi) that says yes. This exemplifies the mechanism of istihsan: it overrides a clear but flawed analogy with a sounder hidden one, based on the underlying purpose of the act.

Commentary
This passage presents a Hanafi usul discussion on whether ruku can substitute for sajdah tilawah during prayer. The author first states the original ruling: upon reciting a sajdah verse, one should immediately prostrate. Then he raises a scenario: if someone instead performs ruku with the joint intention of prayer ruku and sajdah tilawah, what is the ruling? Two approaches are given: qiyas jali (clear analogy) and istihsan (juristic preference). Qiyas jali deems it permissible because both ruku and sujud are forms of humility, citing the Quranic verse 'And he fell down bowing' to equate them. Istihsan, however, prohibits it because sujud is the ultimate act of glorification, higher than ruku; just as in prayer one cannot replace the other, so here. But the author then reveals a deeper istihsan: the apparent istihsan has a hidden flaw—sajdah tilawah is not an intrinsically intended worship but merely a display of humility. The ruku in prayer already fulfills that purpose, so actually the hidden qiyas (qiyas khafi) allows substitution. Thus the preferred Hanafi view (istihsan via qiyas khafi) permits ruku to stand in for sajdah tilawah, unlike the stricter qiyas jali. The authority weight is mufta bihi (the relied-upon position) in the Hanafi school.

قياس خفي (qiyas khafi): Hidden analogy where the 'illah is not immediately apparent but becomes clear upon deeper investigation; forms the basis of istihsan.

Extending Istihsan Based on Qiyas Khafi
States that rulings from istihsan by qiyas khafi can be extended, unlike other types of istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between extensible and non-extensible types of istihsan and apply the Hanafi rule to analogize a ruling from a qiyas khafi-based istihsan to a new case.

Core Idea
Only istihsan rooted in qiyas khafi is extensible; istihsan from athar, ijma, or darurah are non-extensible exceptions.

This passage from the Hanafi usul tradition establishes a key principle: the power of analogical extension (ta'diyah) depends on the source of the ruling. Istihsan based on hidden analogy (qiyas khafi) retains its analogical character and can be extended to similar cases. In contrast, istihsan based on tradition, consensus, or necessity is a deviation from qiyas and remains confined to its specific context. The author illustrates this with a classic fiqh example involving a price dispute before possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of istihsan
Read the passage carefully and list the types of istihsan: (1) based on qiyas khafi, (2) based on athar, (3) based on ijma, (4) based on darurah. Note which one the author says can be extended.
The passage mentions 'al-aqsām al-ukhar' meaning the other categories.
2
Understand the reasoning for extensibility
Analyze why istihsan by qiyas khafi is extensible: it is fundamentally a type of analogy (qiyas) – albeit hidden – and thus carries the capacity for analogical reasoning. The other types are 'ma'dūlah 'an al-qiyas min kulli wajh' – completely deviated from analogy.
3
Analyze the price dispute example
Break down the example: seller claims 2000, buyer claims 1000. By clear analogy (qiyas jali), the buyer is not claiming from the seller, so no oath. But istihsan (qiyas khafi) reveals that each is both claimant and denier, so both swear. After oaths, the judge rescinds the sale.
This shows how the hidden analogy (the mutual claim/denial) creates a different rule from the clear analogy.
4
Apply the rule to a new scenario
Consider a hypothetical: a ruling based on istihsan from a companion practice (athar) – can it be extended? According to the rule, no, because it is not based on qiyas. Test your understanding by proposing a new case where qiyas khafi might apply.

Commentary
This passage lays down a usul principle: a ruling established by istihsan derived from qiyas khafi (hidden analogy) can be extended to other similar cases through qiyas, because it is fundamentally a type of analogy. However, a ruling established by istihsan based on athar (tradition), ijma (consensus), or darurah (necessity) cannot be extended because these sources are completely external to qiyas. To illustrate, the author provides a fiqh example: before the buyer takes possession of a purchased item, if the seller and buyer disagree on the price (seller says 2000, buyer says 1000). But istihsan (based on qiyas khafi) requires both to swear because each is both a claimant and a denier: the buyer claims delivery, the seller claims extra payment. After both swear, the judge invalidates the sale. This rule is important for understanding the scope of analogical extension in Hanafi usul.

The Principle: Istihsan via Qiyas Khafi is Extendable
Principle of extendability of istihsan from qiyas khafi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that istihsan based on qiyas khafi is extendable to analogous cases, and distinguish it from non-extendable istihsan types.

Core Idea
Istihsan built on hidden analogy yields a rational ruling that can be extended; other istihsan types are confined to their case.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text establishes a critical distinction: not all istihsan (juristic preference) rulings can be applied to similar situations. Only those based on hidden analogy (qiyas khafi) produce rational rulings that analogize well to new contexts. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying Hanafi usul to novel cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule regarding rulings contrary to qiyas
The text begins: 'Whatever is established contrary to analogy is not extendable.' Understand this as the default principle: rulings that depart from manifest analogy (qiyas jali) are typically confined to their specific case.
Contrary to qiyas includes istihsan based on hadith, ijma, or necessity.
2
Recognize the exception: istihsan based on qiyas khafi
The text then says: 'the ruling preferred by hidden analogy is valid to extend.' Hidden analogy (qiyas khafi) is a rational process, so its resulting ruling shares the same rational basis and can be applied to analogous cases.
Qiyas khafi is a valid form of analogy in the Hanafi school.
3
Understand why other istihsan types are not extendable
Other forms of istihsan are based on non-rational sources (like a specific hadith, ijma, or darura). Their rulings are not rationally understood, so they cannot be analogically extended.
The hadith cited in the passage is described as 'ghayr ma'qul al-ma'na' (not rationally understood).
4
Apply the rule to the example of sale dispute
The ruling of mutual oaths and rescission in a sale before possession is derived from qiyas khafi. Since it is rational, it can be extended to similar cases like heirs and lease, as shown later.
The text uses this as the prime example.

Application: Extending the Mutual Oaths Ruling to Heirs and Lease
Application of extendable istihsan to heirs and lease with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which the mutual oaths ruling extends to heirs and lease, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf regarding extension after possession.

Core Idea
The rational ruling of mutual oaths in sale before possession extends to heirs and lease, but not after possession except according to Muhammad.

This passage applies the general principle of extendable istihsan to specific cases: if both parties in a sale die before possession, their heirs inherit the dispute and must take oaths; similarly, a lease dispute before the tenant takes possession is treated analogously. However, after possession, the nature of the dispute changes, and the non-rational hadith cannot be extended. This lesson explores the practical scope and the intra-Hanafi disagreement over extension after possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base case: dispute over price before possession
The starting point is a sale where buyer and seller disagree on the price before the buyer has taken possession. The Hanafi ruling, based on qiyas khafi, is that both must take oaths and the sale is rescinded.
This ruling is rational and thus extendable.
2
Observe the first extension: to heirs
If both seller and buyer die before possession, their heirs step into their positions. The same dispute over price leads to mutual oaths and rescission. This extension is valid because the illa (dispute before possession) remains.
This is explicitly stated in the text.
3
Observe the second extension: to lease
The text extends the same ruling to a lease contract: if the lessor and lessee dispute the rent before the tenant takes possession, they also take oaths and the lease is rescinded. The contract of lease admits rescission.
Lease is analogous to sale in terms of dispute before enjoyment.
4
Recognize the limit after possession
After the buyer takes possession, the analogy changes. The pure qiyas (jali) would hold that only the buyer takes an oath. The hadith, though absolute, is not rationally understood, so it does not override the qiyas jali and does not extend to this case. Hence, the mutual oaths ruling is confined to pre-possession.
The text says 'after possession, transitivity is not valid.'
5
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani holds that the hadith is general and applies even after possession, so he extends the mutual oaths to heirs after death and to lease after usufruct. The majority (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) restrict it to pre-possession.
The text says 'except according to Muhammad'.

Original and Substitute (Asl and Khalaf) in Legal Rulings
Explaining the principle of substitution when the original is unattainable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of asl and khalaf and explain the Hanafi position that the substitute fully assumes the legal effect of the original.

Core Idea
A substitute (khalaf) takes the place of the original (asl) when the original is impossible, and in Hanafi fiqh, the substitute is absolute, removing the same legal effects.

You have encountered the division of rights into original and substitute in many fiqh chapters. This lesson from usul al-fiqh systematizes that division and explains how substitutes function. The passage clarifies that when the original obligation becomes impossible, the substitute steps in fully, as in tayammum replacing wudu. We will examine the examples given and understand the Hanafi specificity that the substitute is complete, not merely a permission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
The text states that rights are divided into asl and khalaf. Understand that the substitute stands in place of the original when it is unattainable.
2
Analyze the first example: faith
The original is both inner attestation (tasdiq) and verbal confession (iqrar). In worldly rulings, verbal confession alone becomes a substitute. Trace how this applies in the case of coercion.
The compelled person's mere declaration suffices for legal iman.
3
Examine the second example: parent and minor
The parent's performance of iman for the minor substitutes for the minor's own performance. This makes the child Muslim by the parent's Islam for inheritance and funeral rulings.
4
Explore the third example: followership of the abode
For a captive child, the followership of the Muslim abode substitutes for parental followership, establishing the child as Muslim. Note the hierarchical arrangement (not a substitute for a substitute).
5
Apply to purification: water and tayammum
Water is original, tayammum is substitute. According to the Hanafi view, this substitute is absolute: it removes hadath entirely. Contrast with other views that limit its effect.
The text says: 'this substitute, according to us, is absolute, such that ritual impurity is removed by tayammum.'

Commentary
This passage belongs to the subject of usul al-fiqh, specifically the principle of asl (original) and khalaf (substitute) in legal obligations. The text explains that all rights (whether Allah's or servant's) are divided into an original and a substitute that steps in when the original is unattainable. The commentary gives multiple examples from the Hanafi school. First, faith (iman): its original is both inner attestation (tasdiq) and verbal confession (iqrar). But in the worldly realm, confession alone serves as a substitute, as seen with a person forced to convert: their mere verbal declaration makes them Muslim for legal purposes. Second, the father's (or parent's) performance of faith for a minor is a substitute for the minor's own performance – the child becomes Muslim through the parent. Third, the 'followership of the abode' (tabi'iyyat al-dar) is a substitute for parental followership; a captive child in a Muslim land is ruled Muslim. The text clarifies that these are not substitutes for substitutes but directly for the original, though some are arranged in degrees. Finally, the classic example: water purification (wudu) is the original, tayammum is its substitute. According to the Hanafi school, tayammum is a full substitute that removes ritual impurity and permits prayer until water is available. This differs from some other views that tayammum only permits prayer without removing impurity. The Hanafi position is that tayammum does lift the state of hadath (impurity) because it is a complete substitute.

Principles of Legal Substitution: Da'i and Dalil
Explores two types of substitution: da'i for mad'u and dalil for madlul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of legal substitution (da'i maqam mad'u and dalil maqam madlul) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh and apply them to derive rulings in new cases.

Core Idea
Legal substitution allows a cause or evidence to stand in for the actual effect to achieve precaution or prevent hardship.

This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar (a commentary on Nur al-Anwar) introduces two foundational principles of Hanafi usul: substituting the incentive (da'i) for the effect (mad'u), and substituting the evidence (dalil) for the evidenced (madlul). These principles underpin many rulings in Hanafi fiqh, often justified by istihsan or sadd al-dhara'i. By understanding these substitutions, you will see how the Hanafi school extends rulings to related situations to ensure precaution or relieve hardship.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar (a commentary on Nur al-Anwar) explains two key principles of Hanafi usul al-fiqh: (1) Iqamat al-Da'i Maqam al-Mad'u – substituting the incentive/cause for the effect, and (2) Iqamat al-Dalil Maqam al-Madlul – substituting the evidence for the evidenced. These principles operate in istihsan and sadd al-dhara'i, often for precaution or to prevent hardship.

The first example is istibra'. If a man buys a slave girl who is a virgin and purchased from a mahram, there is certainty she is not pregnant. Yet, this certainty is ignored, and istibra' (one menstrual cycle) is still required. That is because the very act of acquiring ownership (da'i) is treated as equivalent to the possibility of pregnancy (mad'u) to be cautious.

Next, valid seclusion (khulwa sahiha) is treated as actual intercourse for the obligation of full dower ('ubr/mahr) and waiting period ('idda), even if no intercourse occurred. Similarly, marriage is treated as intercourse for establishing lineage. Both seclusion and marriage are incentives (da'i) that lead to intercourse, so they are substituted for the actual act. This is iqamat al-da'i maqam al-mad'u.

Another application is the prohibition of incentives to zina: looking, kissing, and touching. These are placed in the position of actual intercourse in the rulings of istibra', prohibition of affinity (for the wife's relatives), ihram, zihar, and i'tikaf. This is for precaution (ihtiyat).

The second principle (iqamat al-dalil maqam al-madlul) is shown through travel and ritual purity. Travel is taken as equivalent to hardship for granting rukhsas (shortening prayer, breaking fast), even if the traveler experiences no hardship. The evidential link (dalil) between travel and hardship is substituted for the actual hardship. Similarly, purity after menstruation that is free from intercourse is taken as evidence of need for intercourse, and the entire pure period is treated as a time of need, allowing divorce to be effective. This prevents hardship because proving actual need is difficult.

The passage then distinguishes between darura (necessity) where reality cannot be known, and daf' al-haraj (preventing hardship) where reality can be known but with great difficulty. Finally, it distinguishes cause (sabab) from evidence (dalil): a cause always has an effect, but evidence may be absent. The example given is a conditional divorce based on love: the man says 'If you love me, you are divorced'; she says 'I love you'; she is divorced because the report (ikhbar) is taken as evidence of love, even though love is internal. This is iqamat al-dalil maqam al-madlul.

Key Terms
داعٍ (da'i): An incentive or cause that typically leads to an effect; here used for actions like seclusion or marriage that lead to intercourse.
مدعو (mad'u): The effect or thing called for; here, actual intercourse or possession.
دليل (dalil): An indicator or evidence that points to a fact; may be substituted for the fact to avoid hardship.
مدلول (madlul): The thing evidenced; the fact or reality indicated by the dalil.
ضرورة (darura): Necessity; a situation where the true reality cannot be ascertained at all.
حرج (haraj): Hardship; a situation where reality can be known but with great difficulty.
سبب (sabab): A cause that invariably produces an effect; unlike a dalil, it always has a direct influence.

Distinguishing Sabab from Daleel and Dharurah from Haraj
Clarificatory distinctions between key usul terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sabab and daleel, and between dharurah and wus' haraj, using the definitions and examples from the text.

Core Idea
A cause always produces its effect; a proof only provides knowledge. Necessity blocks knowledge; hardship allows it with difficulty.

This lesson focuses on two key distinctions that the author makes after explaining the three purposes. First, the difference between sabab (cause) and daleel (proof): a sabab is always effective, while a daleel may sometimes be devoid of effect. Second, the difference between dharurah (necessity) and wus' haraj (scope of hardship): in dharurah, reality is unknowable; in haraj, it is knowable but with difficulty. These distinctions refine the application of the principle.

Guided Path
1
Locate the two distinctions in the text
Find the Arabic phrases: 'وَالْفَرْقُ بَيْن الضرورة الخ' and 'وَالْفَرْق بين السبب والدليل'. Note the author's clear demarcation.
2
Analyze the difference between sabab and daleel
Sabab never lacks its effect (athar) in the musabbab; daleel may lack that effect. The benefit of daleel is merely knowledge of the madlool. Connect to previous examples: e.g., travel as daleel for hardship—the ruling applies even if no hardship, showing daleel is not a cause.
3
Analyze the difference between dharurah and wus' haraj
Dharurah: knowledge of reality is impossible (e.g., knowing water purity in darkness). Wus' haraj: knowledge is possible but entails great difficulty (e.g., traveling and knowing actual hardship). The text notes that in haraj, the ruling based on daleel is valid.
4
Apply the distinction to a new scenario
Given a case, determine if it falls under dharurah or haraj, and if the ruling is based on sabab or daleel.
This step solidifies understanding.

The Usul Rule on Sleep, Unconsciousness, and Istihsan
Explains the legal effects of sleep and unconsciousness on wudu, worship, and qada of prayers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on the effects of sleep and unconsciousness on wudu and worship, and apply the usul principle of istihsan to derive the rule on qada for unconsciousness.

Core Idea
Unconsciousness is like sleep but always breaks wudu; prolonged unconsciousness drops prayer obligation by istihsan.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi legal treatment of sleep and unconsciousness. The author from Nur al-Anwar distinguishes between these states and their effects on wudu, worship, and legal utterances. We will see how the Hanafi school uses istihsan to determine obligation of qada for missed prayers when unconsciousness is prolonged, based on the practice of the Companions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the States
Define sleep and unconsciousness as per the text. Sleep: loss of voluntary power due to fatigue. Unconsciousness: illness that weakens faculties without removing intellect.
Unconsciousness is more severe than sleep in loss of choice.
2
Determine Ruling on Wudu
Sleep only breaks wudu when lying down (on side or reclining with support). Unconsciousness always breaks wudu in any posture.
Contrast with Shafi'i position later.
3
Determine Effect on Worship
Sleep nullifies worship actions (recitation, movements) and utterances (divorce, etc.) due to lack of choice. Unconsciousness nullifies worship similarly, but with greater severity.
Sleeping person's prayer actions are invalid; laughing does not break wudu.
4
Analyze Rule for Qada
Short unconsciousness (less than a day/night) is like sleep—qada wajib. Prolonged unconsciousness (exceeding a day/night) is like insanity—performance obligation drops. Difference between Imam Muhammad (by number of prayers) and the two (by hours).
Based on companion actions and istihsan.
5
Evaluate Madhhab Dispute
Shafi'i holds no qada if unconsciousness covers a full prayer time. Hanafi uses istihsan based on companion distinction between short and long durations.
Tarjih: Hanafi position preferred due to explicit companion practice.

If a sleeping person recites in his prayer, his recitation is not valid, nor is his standing, bowing, and prostration counted because they occur without his choice. Similarly, if he speaks in prayer, his prayer is not invalidated because it is not real speech. And if he laughs (قهقهة) in prayer, it does not break wudu. Unconsciousness (إغماء) is a conjunction to what preceded. Since it resembles insanity, the author defined it: it is a kind of illness and loss of strength that weakens the faculties but does not remove intellect, unlike insanity which removes it. It is like sleep such that his worship is nullified, but it is more severe than sleep in the loss of choice. So it is an event (حدث) in every case, whether lying down, reclining, standing, sitting, riding, or prostrating, unlike sleep which only invalidates wudu when lying on one's side or reclining with support; if standing, sitting, or in bowing/sujood, it does not break wudu. Sleep may become extended, though the norm is non-extension. If it does not extend, it is like sleep regarding obligation of qada; if it extends, it is joined to insanity and performance obligation is dropped, as in prayer if it exceeds a day and a night according to Muhammad, or by hours according to the two (Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad). Sleep is not considered an excuse in rights of people; if someone destroys another's property by mistake, compensation is wajib. Sleep (النوم): it is when a person becomes incapable of using his power. The definition, ruling, and effect are given. The original definition: sleep is a languor that arises involuntarily due to fatigue. It delays address (خطاب) but does not prevent obligation (وجوب). So the obligation of performance is not established because address is not actualized; if he wakes up within the time, he performs, otherwise he makes qada. Sleep negates choice, so utterances of divorce, manumission, Islam, and apostasy are null. In prayer, sleep's recitation, speech, and laughing have no effect. Unconsciousness is like sleep but stronger; it breaks wudu in all states. If unconsciousness is not prolonged, it is joined to sleep in wajib of prayer; if prolonged (exceeding a day and a night by Muhammad's criterion or by hours according to the two), performance obligation drops. According to Shafi'i, if unconsciousness covers a complete prayer time, qada is not obligatory. But we (Hanafis) apply istihsan by distinguishing between prolonged and non-prolonged, because Ammar ibn Yasir was unconscious for a day and night and performed qada, while Ibn Umar was unconscious for more than a day and night and did not perform qada. Extension in fasting is rare, so it is not considered; even if unconscious for an entire month, he must make up the fasts.

Commentary
This passage from the sharh of Nur al-Anwar, a Hanafi usul work, discusses the legal effects of sleep (nawm) and unconsciousness (ighma') on acts of worship and legal utterances. The author first explains that a sleeping person's actions in prayer are not valid because they lack intent; similarly, speech does not invalidate prayer, and laughing does not break wudu. He then defines unconsciousness as a condition that weakens the senses but does not remove intellect, unlike insanity. Unconsciousness is compared to sleep but is stronger: it always breaks wudu, regardless of posture, whereas sleep only breaks wudu when lying down in a way that eliminates control. The passage also addresses prolonged unconsciousness: if it lasts less than a day and night, it is like sleep, requiring qada of missed prayers; if it exceeds that, it is like insanity, and the obligation of performance drops. There is a difference between Imam Muhammad (count by number of prayers) and the two shaykhs (count by hours). The author then cites the practice of the Companions to support the Hanafi division. The passage also notes that sleep negates choice for utterances like divorce and conversion. Finally, it mentions that for fasting, prolonged unconsciousness does not excuse making up fasts because it is rare.

Key Terms
النوم (an-nawm): A state where a person loses ability to use his power voluntarily; linguistically, a languor arising from fatigue.
الإغماء (al-ighmā'): A type of illness and loss of strength that weakens the faculties but does not remove intellect, unlike insanity.
الحدث (al-ḥadath): A state that invalidates wudu; here applied to unconsciousness and sleep (in certain postures).
القضاء (al-qaḍā'): Making up a missed obligatory act after its time has passed.
الأداء (al-adā'): Performance of an act within its prescribed time.
