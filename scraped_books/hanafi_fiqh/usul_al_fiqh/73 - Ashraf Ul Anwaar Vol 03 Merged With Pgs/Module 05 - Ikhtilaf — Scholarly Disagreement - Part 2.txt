Qisas: Inherited or Independent Right? Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi dispute on inheriting qisas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that qisas is not inheritable (ghayr mawruth) and distinguish its practical consequence from the Sahibayn's view in a case of absent heirs.

Core Idea
Qisas is indivisible, so each heir holds the full right independently — it is not inherited like property.

This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar dissects an intra-Hanafi dispute on the nature of the right to qisas (retaliation). The central question is whether this right is inherited from the deceased in shares (like property) or arises directly and independently for each heir. The answer determines how evidence is handled when some heirs are absent — a critical procedural difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central issue
Read the passage carefully to determine what is being disputed: is qisas inheritable (mawruth) or not?
2
Analyze Abu Hanifa’s position and dalil
Abu Hanifa holds that qisas is ghayr mawruth — established directly for each heir. His evidence: qisas is a single act intended to satisfy vengeance; it cannot be divided, so each heir must have the full right, analogous to the guardianship of marriage (wilayat al-nikah) among brothers.
The analogy (qiyas) to marriage guardianship is key.
3
Analyze the Sahibayn’s position and dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that qisas is inherited in proportion to each heir’s share of the estate. Their evidence: qisas is a right that transfers from the deceased like other inheritable rights.
4
Work through the practical consequence
The thamarah al-khilaf: if one heir is absent and another establishes evidence, must the absent heir re-establish evidence upon return? According to Abu Hanifa, yes — because each heir is independent. According to the Sahibayn, no — because one heir can represent the deceased.
This shows the real-world impact of the usul disagreement.
5
Note the exception: conversion to diyah
When qisas converts to diyah (blood money) through pardon or settlement, it becomes inheritable property, and the rules of inheritance (including representation by one heir) apply. This does not contradict the original rule because the substitute may differ from the original.
The analogy to tayammum vs. wudu illustrates this.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar discusses the Hanafi ruling on whether the right of qisas (retaliation) is inherited by heirs or established directly for them. The passage type is a madhhab dispute within the Hanafi school. The author first explains the basis: the cause (the killing) is connected to the deceased, but the right belongs to the heirs because the purpose is to satisfy their vengeance. Since qisas is a single, indivisible right, it is not split into shares like property; rather each heir has the full right independently. This is analogous to the guardianship of marriage where each brother can marry off the sister. Thus, if one heir (elder brother) executes qisas before the younger brother's maturity, it is valid. However, if two adult heirs are present and one is absent, the present one cannot execute alone because the absent one's possible waiver is likely. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that qisas is inherited in shares, like property. The practical difference arises when some heirs are absent: according to Abu Hanifa, the absent heir must re-establish evidence upon return because each heir is independent; according to the Sahibayn, once evidence is established by one heir, it suffices because one heir can represent the deceased. When qisas converts to money (diyah) through pardon or settlement, it becomes inheritable property, and the same rule applies as with other property: debts are paid, bequests executed, and one heir can represent the deceased. The author notes that the substitute (diyah) may differ from the original (qisas) in some rulings, just as tayammum differs from wudu. The commentary follows the author's order, explaining each point with Hanafi fiqh examples.

Key Terms
القصاص (al-qisas): Retaliation in kind for murder or injury, a right established for the heirs of the victim.
غير موروث (ghayr mawruth): Not inheritable; meaning the right is not transferred from the deceased but established directly for the heirs.
الدية (al-diyah): Blood money, a substitute for qisas when pardoned or settled, which becomes inheritable property.

The Nature of Qisas: Inherited or Initial Right?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas inheritance and transformation to diya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether qisas is inherited or an initial right for each heir, and explain the practical consequence regarding re-establishing evidence.

Core Idea
Qisas is a quality that does not admit division; it stands whole for each heir, not split by inheritance shares.

This passage examines a key intra-Hanafi dispute over the nature of qisas (retaliation). Abu Hanifa holds that qisas is established initially for each heir in full, like guardianship of marriage, while the Sahibayn treat it as inherited property divided in shares. This difference leads to a practical rule about re-proving evidence when an absent heir returns, which we will explore step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Disputed Issue
Determine whether qisas is established initially for each heir (Abu Hanifa) or inherited in proportion to shares (Sahibayn).
The core question is how the right to qisas vests in the heirs.
2
State Abu Hanifa's Position and His Proof
Abu Hanifa argues qisas is not inherited but an initial right for each heir in full, using qiyas (analogy) to guardianship of marriage (wilayat al-nikah), where each brother has complete authority.
The analogy rests on the quality of 'not admitting division'.
3
State the Sahibayn's Position
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that qisas is inherited like property, divided according to each heir's share.
For them, qisas is a financial right that can be split.
4
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilaf (Practical Consequence)
If some heirs are absent, according to Abu Hanifa the present heir cannot execute qisas until all are present; if he does, the absent heir must re-establish evidence upon return. According to the Sahibayn, once evidence is established, no re-proving is needed.
This difference arises from whether qisas is an independent right or a shared inheritance.
5
Apply to Transformation into Diya
When qisas is transformed into diya (blood money) via settlement or pardon, it becomes inheritable property. The ruling then follows property inheritance rules: debts and wills are settled, and once proof is established, it need not be repeated.
Diya is a deputy (na'ib) of qisas, sometimes differing in rulings, like tayammum differs from wudu.

And the pardon of the heir before the death of the wounded person: It is valid for the heirs to pardon before the death of the victim because the right belongs to the heirs in terms of the essence of obligation. Abu Hanifa said that qisas is not inherited, meaning it is not established in a way that shares are apportioned among heirs; rather, it is established initially as a right of the heirs. As we said, qisas is legislated for taking blood-vengeance, but since qisas is a quality that does not admit of division, it is established for each heir in its entirety, just as the guardianship of marriage for each brother is complete. Therefore, if the elder brother executed qisas before the younger brother reached majority, it is permissible. Unlike if the qualified claimants are two adults, and one is absent, then the present one may not execute qisas because the absent person is likely to pardon, and the possibility of the absent becoming mature and pardoning is rare, so he is not waited for. According to the Sahibayn, the right of qisas is established in inheritance shares, not initially as stated above. The fruit of the dispute appears: If some heirs are absent and the present heir provides proof, then according to Imam Abu Hanifa, when the absent returns, he must re-establish the evidence because each heir is independent in qisas, and the qisas verdict is not executed until all are present. According to the Sahibayn, since qisas is inherited from the deceased, upon the absent's return there is no need to re-establish evidence because any heir can claim on behalf of the deceased; once evidence is established, it need not be repeated. And when it is transformed (i.e., qisas becomes diya due to settlement or some heirs pardoning), it becomes inheritable, so its ruling becomes like that of other inherited property: the deceased's debt is paid from it, his wills are executed, and once a person claims on behalf of the deceased and provides proof, there is no need to re-prove because diya is the deputy of qisas, and sometimes the deputy differs from the original in some rulings, as tayammum differs from wudu in the condition of intention. And qisas is obligatory for the spouses as in the case of diya, so the woman may exact qisas from the husband and the husband from the woman. But according to him (Abu Hanifa) it is initial, and according to them (Sahibayn) it is by way of inheritance, just as the entitlement to diya is established by inheritance. Malik said: I do not include the husband and wife from the diya because its obligation occurs after death and the marital relationship ends with death. Our proof: The Prophet (peace be upon him) ordered that the wife of Ashim al-Babi be inherited from the blood-money of her husband. And for the deceased, the ruling of the living applies in matters of the hereafter because the grave is for the deceased like the covenant for the child. So what is owed to him from others or he owes to others of rights and claims, and what he receives of reward or punishment through acts of obedience and disobedience, just as the deceased experiences in the grave and perceives like the living.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a ruling regarding qisas (retaliation) and its inheritance. The author discusses two main issues: (1) whether heirs can pardon the offender before the victim dies, and (2) how the right to qisas is established—whether it is inherited in shares or initially given to each heir wholly. The passage presents the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). Abu Hanifa holds that qisas is not inherited; rather, it is established directly for each heir in full, similar to the guardianship of marriage. Hence, if one heir executes qisas before others become eligible, it is valid. However, if one heir is absent, the present heir cannot execute because the absent might pardon. The Sahibayn hold that qisas is inherited in proportion to each heir's share. The practical consequence (ثمرة) is regarding the need to re-establish evidence when an absent heir returns. According to Abu Hanifa, evidence must be presented again, while according to the Sahibayn, it is not necessary. The passage then discusses the transformation of qisas into diya (blood money) and its subsequent inheritance. Finally, it addresses the right of spouses to qisas. Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn agree that spouses can claim qisas against each other, but they differ on whether it is an initial right (Abu Hanifa) or inherited (Sahibayn). Imam Malik denies spouses the right to diya (and by extension qisas) because the marital bond ends at death. The Hanafi response cites a hadith where the Prophet ordered inheritance of blood-money for a wife, and they argue that the deceased is considered alive for certain rulings of the Hereafter, using an analogy that the grave is like a covenant for the child.

Key Terms
قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for murder or injury, a right of the victim or heirs.
دية (diya): Blood money paid as compensation for murder or injury.
وراثة (wiratha): Inheritance; transfer of rights from deceased to heirs.
وصف (wasf): Quality or attribute of a legal right.

Spouses' Right to Qisas and Diya: Hanafi vs. Maliki
Inter-madhhab dispute on spouses' entitlement to qisas and diya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that spouses inherit diya and qisas, rebut the Maliki objection using the hadith of Ashim al-Babi and the analogy of the grave, and identify the resulting ruling.

Core Idea
The deceased is like a living person for rights and obligations; spouses inherit diya and qisas as per the Prophet's command.

This passage addresses the inter-madhhab dispute about whether spouses can inherit diya and qisas. Imam Malik excludes spouses, arguing that marital bonds end at death. The Hanafi school counters with a hadith and a powerful analogy: the grave is for the deceased like a covenant for a child, so the deceased remains legally alive for certain rights. This lesson examines the proofs and the practical ruling.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi Position on Spouses and Qisas
The Hanafi school holds that spouses are entitled to qisas and diya from each other, just as they inherit other property. Abu Hanifa considers it an initial right, while the Sahibayn see it as inherited.
Both agree on entitlement; the difference is the mode of establishment.
2
Present the Maliki Objection
Imam Malik argues that diya and qisas are obligations arising after death, and since the marital relationship ends with death, spouses cannot inherit these rights.
Malik's reasoning is based on the termination of marriage at death.
3
Analyse the Hanafi Proof from Hadith
The Prophet (peace be upon him) ordered that the wife of Ashim al-Babi be included in the inheritance of the blood-money ('aql) of her husband. This explicit command demonstrates that spouses do inherit diya.
The hadith is a direct textual proof (dalil naqli) for inheritance of diya.
4
Explain the Supporting Analogy (Qiyas)
The Hanafi argue that the deceased is considered alive for rulings related to the Hereafter, such as reward and punishment. The grave is like a covenant (ahd) for the child, meaning the deceased still has rights and obligations.
This analogy justifies treating the deceased as having ongoing legal capacity for certain matters.
5
Derive the Ruling and Note the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The Hanafi ruling: spouses inherit diya and qisas (initially or via inheritance). The Maliki ruling: spouses do not inherit these. The practical consequence: in Hanafi law, a spouse can claim diya; in Maliki law, they cannot.
The thamarah is the inclusion or exclusion of spouses from blood money.

The author discusses two main issues: (1) whether heirs can pardon the offender before the victim dies, and (2) how the right to qisas is established—whether it is inherited in shares or initially given to each heir wholly. The passage presents the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). Abu Hanifa holds that qisas is not inherited; rather, it is established directly for each heir in full, similar to the guardianship of marriage. Hence, if one heir executes qisas before others become eligible, it is valid. However, if one heir is absent, the present heir cannot execute because the absent might pardon. The Sahibayn hold that qisas is inherited in proportion to each heir's share. The practical consequence (ثمرة) is regarding the need to re-establish evidence when an absent heir returns. According to Abu Hanifa, evidence must be presented again, while according to the Sahibayn, it is not necessary. The passage then discusses the transformation of qisas into diya (blood money) and its subsequent inheritance. Finally, it addresses the right of spouses to qisas. Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn agree that spouses can claim qisas against each other, but they differ on whether it is an initial right (Abu Hanifa) or inherited (Sahibayn). Imam Malik denies spouses the right to diya (and by extension qisas) because the marital bond ends at death. The Hanafi response cites a hadith where the Prophet ordered inheritance of blood-money for a wife, and they argue that the deceased is considered alive for certain rulings of the Hereafter, using an analogy that the grave is like a covenant for the child.

قصاص (qisas): Retaliation in kind for murder or injury, a right of the victim or heirs.
دية (diya): Blood money paid as compensation for murder or injury.
عقل (‘aql): Blood-money, synonym for diya or a specific type of compensation.
عهد (‘ahd): Covenant or pledge; used in analogy for the grave.

Core Idea
When parties collude on price, the original contract prevails if they later disagree; but if they agree to build on collusion, the stated price must be upheld to avoid invalidity.

This passage examines the effect of prior collusion (muwada'a) on a sale contract. It distinguishes between collusion regarding the existence of the sale itself and collusion regarding the price amount or type. The key dispute is whether the original contract or the collusive agreement is given weight. Collusion in the amount is more common: parties pretend a higher price than agreed.

This principle underlies all four scenarios.

Core Idea
In khul', the dower is subsidiary to divorce, so jest in the dower does not void the divorce according to the Sahibayn, but Imam Abu Hanifa conditions divorce on acceptance of the full dower.

This lesson explores a nuanced internal Hanafi dispute on whether jest (hazl) regarding the dower amount in a khul' contract invalidates the divorce. The Sahibayn hold that jest does not affect the divorce because the dower is subsidiary; Imam Abu Hanifa disagrees, arguing that if both parties built on the jest, the divorce is suspended until the wife accepts the full dower. Understanding this ikhtilaf sharpens your skill in applying the usul principle of subsidiary vs. principal in contractual rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contractual scenario
Recognize that the case involves khul' (divorce by mutual consent with a dower) where the dower amount is jokingly agreed upon (hazl). The parties either build on the jest, turn away from it, or differ.
2
State the Sahibayn's position and rationale
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that jest does not affect the contract; the divorce occurs and the full dower mentioned is due immediately. Their usul basis: the dower is subsidiary to divorce, so jest in a subsidiary does not affect the principal.
They argue using the principle that hazl is void in origin and ineffective in contracts affecting divorce.
3
State Imam Abu Hanifa's position and counter-argument
Imam holds that if both parties agree on the jest (i.e., build on it), the divorce is suspended on the wife's acceptance of the full dower. If they turn away from the jest, the divorce is valid immediately. He distinguishes khul' from marriage: in marriage, the dower is principal in establishment, so jest affects the dower, but in khul', the dower is subsidiary in establishment, so jest in the dower can invalidate the contract unless retracted.
Imam uses qiyas with marriage but arrives at a different result by highlighting the difference in the dower's role in thubut (establishment).
4
Analyze the role of qiyas and istihsan
Both parties use analogical reasoning (qiyas) but reach different conclusions. The Sahibayn apply a stricter analogy that jest is always ineffective; Imam uses a more nuanced analogy considering the nature of the dower. The passage also hints at istihsan in Imam's position because he gives weight to the seriousness of the contract when parties later claim turning away.
The debate illustrates how usul principles (subsidiary vs. principal, thubut, istihsan) shape fiqh rulings.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The practical consequence is clear: under the Sahibayn, the wife is immediately divorced and owes the full dower regardless of jest; under Imam, if both agreed on the jest, the wife can choose to accept the full dower to finalize the divorce, otherwise the divorce does not occur.
The text notes the Sahibayn's position as 'zahir' (apparent), suggesting it may be the relied-upon view in many later works.

Commentary
This passage discusses the effect of jest (hazl) on a divorce contract where the dower amount is agreed upon jokingly. The author presents the positions of Imam Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). The key issue is whether the divorce takes effect and whether the full dower is due. According to the Sahibayn, jest does not affect the validity of the contract; the divorce occurs and the dower is due as per the overt statement, even if they agreed on a jest. They argue that the dower is subsidiary to the divorce and jest cannot nullify the principal. Imam Abu Hanifa, however, holds that if both parties agree on a jest, the contract is void unless the wife accepts the full dower. He differentiates between khul' and marriage: in khul', the dower is subsidiary to divorce, so jest in the dower does not affect the divorce; in marriage, the dower is principal and can be affected by jest. The passage also covers scenarios of turning away from the jest and differing claims. The commentary emphasizes the Hanafi internal hierarchy and the reasoning behind each position.

Key Terms
هزل (مذاق) (hazl (mazāq)): Jest or joke in a legal contract that affects the validity of the contract according to some scholars.
مهر (مال) (mahr (māl)): The dower or payment given by the husband in exchange for divorce in khul'.
بناء على الهزل (binā' 'alā al-hazl): Both parties agreeing after the contract that they acted according to the jest.

Interdiction of the Prodigal (Safih): A Hanafi Usul Dispute
Usul dispute on hajr of safih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on hajr of the safih, apply their respective interpretations of Quran 4:6, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf in a given scenario.

Core Idea
A single Quranic verse yields three different rulings on interdiction—because usul principles determine how a text is read.

The legal status of a prodigal adult (safih) who wastes his wealth is contested among the early Hanafi masters. While they agree that his wealth should be withheld at a young age, they disagree on whether he should be interdicted from transactions after reaching maturity. This lesson dissects the usul dispute, examining how Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn derive rulings from the same Quranic text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The legal question is whether an adult safih (who is not a minor or insane) is interdicted from executing transactions, such as sale, lease, or gift.
2
State the agreed premise
All three imams agree that initially, at the onset of bulugh, if the safih is immature, his wealth should not be given to him. This is based on the Quranic verse.
The disagreement is on an additional matter: interdiction from transactions.
3
Compare the positions
Abu Hanifa: No interdiction at all. Sahibayn: Interdiction only for transactions that are voidable by jest (like sale, lease, gift); no interdiction for transactions like marriage and manumission.
4
Analyze the proof
Both sides cite Quran 4:6. The Sahibayn take the literal conditional: if no rushd, do not give wealth. Abu Hanifa uses life expectancy reasoning to set an age ceiling of 25, after which wealth must be given regardless of rushd.
The Sahibayn analogize the safih to a minor or insane person for transactions that waste wealth.
5
Determine the thamarah
Practical difference: A 20-year-old safih's sale of his property is valid according to Abu Hanifa, but invalid according to the Sahibayn.
6
Evaluate the tarjih
The mufta bihi is the Sahibayn's view, as it protects the safih's property and prevents him from becoming a burden on the community.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; both positions have strong usul foundations.

Commentary
This passage is a fiqh dispute regarding the legal interdiction (hajr) of a prodigal (safih) who is an adult but wasteful. The author first quotes the Quranic verse: 'If you perceive in them maturity, then give them their wealth' (4:6). This verse is used as a proof by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) to argue that wealth should not be handed over to a safih until he shows maturity. They rely on the apparent meaning of the verse. Abu Hanifa, however, sets an age limit: when the safih reaches twenty-five years, his wealth must be given to him even if he is still immature. His reasoning is based on a life expectancy calculation: if a person reaches puberty at twelve (minimum age for bulugh in the Hanafi school) and the minimum gestation period is six months, he becomes a father at around twelve and a half. Doubling that makes him a grandfather at twenty-five. After that age, it is futile to withhold wealth because he is unlikely to become mature. The text notes that all three imams agree that initially (at the onset of bulugh) the wealth should not be given if he is a safih. The disagreement is on the additional matter: whether the safih is interdicted from transactions (i.e., his contracts are invalid). Abu Hanifa says he is never interdicted because a free, sane adult cannot be restricted from transactions. The Sahibayn differentiate: for transactions that are not invalidated by jest (like marriage and manumission), he is not interdicted; but for transactions that are voided by jest (like sale, lease, gift), he is interdicted to protect his wealth, just like a minor or insane person. The reason is that if he is not interdicted, he will waste his wealth and become a burden on the public treasury. This dispute is from the Hanafi usul tradition, and the relied-upon opinion (mufta bihi) is that of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) in this matter, as it is the more cautious view for preserving wealth.

Key Terms
حجر (hajr): Legal interdiction preventing a person from executing transactions due to a deficiency in capacity or to protect his interests.
سفيه (safih): An adult who is wasteful and lacks prudent judgment in financial matters, but is not insane or a minor.
رشد (rushd): Maturity and sound judgment in financial affairs, enabling one to manage property prudently.

Legal Definition of Error (Khata) and Its Implications
Defines error linguistically and technically, lists its legal effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'al-khata' (error) linguistically and technically, and apply its legal effects to distinguish between divine rights (huquq Allah) and human rights (huquq al-ibad).

Core Idea
Error lifts divine punishment but not compensation — intention matters only for sin, not liability.

This lesson dissects the legal concept of error (khata) as defined in Hanafi usul. Error is a valid excuse for dropping divine rights, especially when it occurs after ijtihad, but it does not absolve one from financial liability toward other humans. Understanding this boundary is essential for correctly applying the famous hadith 'رُفِعَ عَنْ أُمَّتِي الْخَطَأُ وَالنِّسْيَانُ' and for resolving disputes over utterances like mistaken divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its definition
Read the linguistic and technical definitions of khata. Note that the technical definition adds 'contrary to what was intended' – this is the key for legal effects.
Linguistic: opposite of correctness. Technical: occurrence contrary to intention.
2
Distinguish divine vs. human rights
The passage states that error is an excuse for haqq Allah but not for haqq al-'abd. Identify examples: mistaken intercourse (no hadd), mistaken killing (no qisas but diyah wajib).
Haqq Allah: sin, hadd, qisas. Haqq al-ibad: compensation, diyah.
3
Analyze the examples of ijtihad and fatwa
A mujtahid who errs in a fatwa after exhausting effort is not sinful and deserves reward. This shows that error in ijtihad is entirely forgiven for divine rights.
Contrast with deliberate error: sinful.
4
Examine the ikhtilaf on mistaken divorce
The passage contrasts the Hanafi position (divorce takes effect) with the Shafi'i position (does not). Understand the proof: the mistaken person is a chooser (mukhtar) vs. analogy to the sleeping person.
Hanafi: speech is voluntary. Shafi'i: no intention, so like coerced.
5
Apply the hadith interpretation
The hadith is restricted to afterlife rulings, not worldly ones. Evidence: diyah and kaffarah are still obligatory for mistaken killing.
The same hadith is used to argue for both positions; Hanafi interpretation emphasizes worldly liability.

It is a valid excuse for dropping the right of Allah Almighty if it occurs after ijtihad. If a mujtahid errs in a fatwa after exerting his effort, he is not sinful; rather, he deserves a reward. No prescribed punishment or retaliation (applies) because if someone other than his wife is brought to him and he thinks she is his wife and has intercourse with her, he is not punished nor becomes sinful like the sin of adultery. And if he sees a figure from afar and thinks it is game, shoots it and kills it, but it was a human, he is not sinful with the sin of intent, and retaliation is not obligatory on him. But it is not made an excuse regarding the rights of servants such that he must pay compensation for transgression if he destroys someone's property by mistake, and the blood money becomes obligatory if he kills someone by mistake, because all these are rights of servants, and the replacement of property is the recompense for the act. And his divorce—i.e., the divorce of the mistaken person—is effective, as when one intends to say 'his mother, come sit' but his tongue slips and he says 'you are divorced,' divorce occurs according to us. According to al-Shafi'i, it does not occur, by analogy to the sleeping person who has no choice, while the mistaken person is a chooser who fell short. The intended meaning of the hadith is the removal of the judgment of the afterlife, not the judgment of this world, as evidenced by the obligation of blood money and expiation. And his sale—i.e., the sale of the mistaken person—must be concluded, as when someone intends to say 'al-hamdu lillah' but his tongue slips and he says 'I sell you such and such,' and the addressee says 'I accept.' This is the meaning of his statement 'if his opponent confirms him.' It is said that the meaning is that the opponent confirms that the issuance of the offer from you was a mistake, because if he does not confirm that, his ruling is the ruling of the deliberate, and his sale is like the sale of the coerced, i.e., it is concluded defectively, because the flow of speech on his tongue is volitional, so it is concluded, but it becomes invalid due to the lack of consent in it.

Commentary
This passage is a legal definition of 'al-khata' (error) and an exposition of its rulings. It begins by defining error linguistically as the opposite of correctness, and technically as the occurrence of something contrary to one's intention. Error is an excuse for dropping divine rights (huquq Allah) when it results from ijtihad (exertion of effort). For example, a mujtahid who errs in a fatwa is not sinful and still deserves a reward. Similarly, if someone mistakenly has intercourse with a woman he thinks is his wife, no hadd punishment applies, and if he mistakenly kills a person thinking it is game, no qisas (retaliation) applies. However, error is not an excuse regarding the rights of servants (huquq al-ibad); thus, compensation (daman) is due for property, and diyah (blood money) for killing. The passage then discusses the validity of the mistaken person's divorce: according to Hanafis, if one intends to say one thing but his tongue slips and he pronounces divorce, it takes effect, unlike the Shafi'i position which analogizes to the sleeping person. The hadith 'Error is lifted from my ummah' refers to the afterlife, not worldly rulings, as evidenced by the obligation of diyah and kaffarah. Finally, the sale of a mistaken person is concluded but invalid if the mistake is confirmed by the counterparty, because the speech is volitional but lacks consent.

Commentary
This passage discusses the first type of actions under coercion, where the coerced cannot be considered a mere tool for another. These actions include eating and sexual intercourse. The ruling is that the legal consequences fall solely on the coerced, not on the coercer. For eating, if a person is forced to eat his own food, if he is hungry, the coercer is not liable because the benefit goes to the coerced; if he is full, the coercer must pay the value. If forced to eat another's property, the coercer is liable for compensation. For intercourse, if with a non-wife, the coerced incurs the hadd penalty and sin, and the act does not transfer to the coercer. If with his wife during fasting, i'tikaf, ihram, or hayd, the coerced is sinful and must make up the fast etc., and the coercer does not share liability. The commentary also mentions that agreements like sales made under coercion are void due to lack of consent, but become valid if the coerced ratifies after coercion ends. Also, admissions under coercion are invalid because they are false statements made to avoid harm.

Key Terms
إكراه (ikrah): Coercion: forcing someone to act against their will under threat of harm. In this passage, it distinguishes between actions where the coerced can be a tool and those where he cannot.
مكره (mukrah): The coerced person: one who is compelled to act. In this passage, the mukrah bears responsibility for actions like eating and intercourse.
مكرِه (mukrih): The coercer: the person who compels another. In this passage, the mukrih is sometimes liable (e.g., for property damage) but not for actions like eating or intercourse.
آلة (ala): Tool or instrument. In coercion, refers to whether the coerced can be considered a mere instrument of the coercer.

Coercion to Kill: Who Bears Qisas?
Stating the second type of actions with the tool analogy and ensuing ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on qisas liability under coercion, identify the opposing madhhab positions, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) of each view.

Core Idea
The coercer is the killer in Hanafi law — intention through compulsion transfers agency.

This passage introduces a classic usul dispute: when a person is forced to kill, who bears criminal liability? The Hanafi school, represented by Abu Hanifa, treats the coerced as a mere tool, placing qisas on the coercer. Other scholars disagree, each offering a different allocation of liability. Understanding this debate sharpens our analysis of causation and agency in Islamic jurisprudence.

Guided Path

This category is distinct from actions where the coerced cannot be a tool, such as speech acts.

Abu Hanifa holds that qisas is on the coercer because he is the real killer; the coerced is like a knife. Understand this tool analogy deeply.

Commentary
This passage presents the second type of actions under coercion, where the coerced can function as a mere instrument, like in killing or destroying property. Abu Hanifa holds that in intentional killing with a sword, the coercer is the real killer and must be subject to qisas, while the coerced is like a knife. Muhammad and Zufar argue that the coerced is the actual doer and should face qisas. Al-Shafi'i says both are liable. Abu Yusuf says neither due to doubt. For accidental killing, the diyah is on the 'aqilah of the coerced, and kaffarah is on him. The passage shows the Hanafi position that the coerced is a tool, and the coercer bears the responsibility.

قاتل (qatil): Killer: the person responsible for the death. Dispute over whether the coercer or coerced is the qatil.

دية (diyah): Blood money: financial compensation for unintentional killing. In accidental killing, it is on the 'aqilah of the coerced.
