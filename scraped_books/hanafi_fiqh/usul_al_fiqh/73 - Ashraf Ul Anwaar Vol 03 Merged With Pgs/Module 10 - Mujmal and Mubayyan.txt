Ruling on Narration by Meaning for Mushkil, Mushtarak, and Mujmal
Prohibition of narrating by meaning for ambiguous texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories of ambiguous texts (mushkil, mushtarak, mujmal) and apply the Hanafi rule that narrating by meaning is impermissible for each, explaining the specific rationale for each category.

Core Idea
Narrating by meaning is forbidden when the text's intended meaning depends on the speaker's clarification or specific interpretation.

In hadith transmission, narrating by meaning (riwayat bil-ma'na) is often debated. This passage from a Hanafi usul text clarifies that for texts falling into the categories of mushkil (ambiguous), mushtarak (homonymous), or mujmal (concise), narrating by meaning is absolutely impermissible—even for a mujtahid. The rationale differs per category, but the core reason is that the intended meaning cannot be reliably determined without the original speaker's input or a specific interpretation that may not be authoritative for others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Define mushkil (text with ambiguity resolvable only by external evidence), mushtarak (homonym with multiple meanings), and mujmal (concise text requiring explanation). Understand that these are distinct from clear texts (zahir, nass).
These terms are key in Hanafi usul al-fiqh for classifying texts by clarity.
2
Understand the general prohibition
Note that the ruling applies to everyone: both mujtahid and non-mujtahid. The prohibition is absolute for these types; there is no exception for scholars who might think they can discern the meaning.
The passage explicitly says 'na jaiz' (impermissible) for all.
3
Analyze the exceptions and special cases
The author mentions jawami' al-kalim (comprehensive speech) as unique to the Prophet—no one can narrate it by meaning. Then, for mushkil and mushtarak, the reason is that their narration requires specific interpretation that may not be valid as proof for others. For mujmal, the meaning is unknown until the speaker clarifies.
This step distinguishes the rationale for each category.
4
Apply the reasoning to a hypothetical
Consider a hadith containing a mushtarak word like 'qadha' (can mean 'judgment' or 'creation'). If you paraphrase, you might choose the wrong meaning. The correct meaning is only known through context or explicit clarification from the Prophet. Hence, paraphrasing is invalid.
This demonstrates the practical consequence of the rule.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary explains the ruling on narrating hadith by meaning (riwayat bil-ma'na) for texts that are ambiguous (mushkil), homonymous (mushtarak), or abridged (mujmal). The author states that it is impermissible for anyone—mujtahid or non-mujtahid—to narrate such texts by meaning. The reason is that these types of texts require the original speaker's clarification or specific interpretation. For jawami' al-kalim (comprehensive speech), which was a unique gift to the Prophet, no one can reproduce the exact intended meaning. For mushkil and mushtarak, the meaning depends on a particular interpretation that may not be evident to others, so narrating by meaning would not be authoritative. For mujmal, the meaning is not understood until the speaker explains it; thus, one cannot assign a meaning independently. This is a standard Hanafi position emphasizing caution in transmitting problematic texts.

Key Terms
مشکل (mushkil): A text that is problematic due to ambiguity in meaning that can only be resolved by external evidence.
مشترک (mushtarak): A homonymous word that has multiple possible meanings, requiring context to determine which is intended.
مجمل (mujmal): An abridged or concise text whose meaning is not clear without explanation from the original speaker.

Types of Bayan: Taqrir, Tafsir, and Taghyeer
Classifying three types of bayan with examples and the mawsool/mafsool dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three types of bayan in Hanafi usul, give a Quranic example for each, and evaluate the Hanafi position on disconnected bayan.

Core Idea
Bayan removes ambiguity: taqrir emphasizes, tafsir explains, taghyeer alters — and Hanafis allow delay for taqrir and tafsir but not beyond the time of need.

In Hanafi usul, texts from the Quran and Sunnah often require clarification (bayan) to be properly understood and applied. The scholars identified three main types of bayan: confirmatory (taqrir), interpretive (tafsir), and alterative (taghyeer). Each serves a distinct purpose in resolving ambiguity. A key dispute arises over whether bayan must be delivered immediately with the original text or can be delayed. This lesson unpacks the definitions, examples, and the Hanafi ruling on disconnected bayan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of bayan
List the three types: bayan al-taqrir (confirmatory), bayan al-tafsir (interpretive), and bayan al-taghyeer (alterative). Note their distinct functions: taqrir emphasizes literal/general meaning, tafsir explains ambiguous terms, taghyeer changes a ruling via condition/exception.
These types are known by induction from the Quran and Sunnah.
2
Analyze the function using Quranic examples
For taqrir, examine the verse 'And no bird flies with its two wings' — the phrase 'flies with its two wings' removes the metaphorical possibility of 'bird' meaning speed. For tafsir, 'Establish prayer' is mujmal and the Sunnah clarifies details. For taghyeer, 'You are divorced if you enter the house' changes immediate divorce to conditional.
Each example illustrates how the bayan resolves a specific type of ambiguity.
3
Examine the dispute on mawsool vs mafsool for tafsir
Understand the mutakallim objection: if bayan is delayed, the mukallaf cannot act, leading to obligation of the impossible. The Hanafi response: delay from the time of address is valid as long as it is not beyond the time of need; the mukallaf holds a provisional belief in the literal meaning. The Quranic evidence is 'thumma inna alayna bayanahu' (Then upon Us is its clarification), where 'thumma' indicates delay.
This dispute centers on the category of tafsir; taqrir and tafsir can be mafsool, but taghyeer must be mawsul.
4
Apply the Hanafi distinction
Given a scenario where a mujmal text is clarified later by the Sunnah, determine whether it is valid according to Hanafi usul. Recognize that the delay is from the time of address, not beyond the time of need, so it is permissible. Contrast with taghyeer, which requires connection.
The practical consequence: the Hanafi position validates the gradual revelation of details (e.g., prayer rules) that came after the initial command.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the types of bayan (clarification) in usul al-fiqh. The author divides bayan into three main types: bayan al-taqrir (confirmatory clarification), bayan al-tafsir (interpretive clarification), and bayan al-taghyeer (alterative clarification). Bayan al-taqrir removes ambiguity regarding metaphor or specificity by emphasizing the literal or general meaning, as shown by the Quranic examples. Bayan al-tafsir explains what is mujmal (ambiguous) or mushtarak (homonymous), using the Sunnah to clarify the details of prayer and zakah, and the Prophet's statement to clarify the meaning of 'quru'. The author then addresses a dispute: some mutakallimin argue that bayan of mujmal and mushtarak must be connected to the original text, because delaying it would make action impossible. The Hanafi response distinguishes between delaying bayan beyond the time of need (invalid) and delaying it from the time of address (valid), citing the Quranic verse 'thumma inna alayna bayanahu' as evidence for permissible delay, though bayan al-tafsir is an exception (must be connected). Finally, bayan al-taghyeer changes a ruling from immediate to conditional, as in conditional divorces and exceptions in debts. For these, the condition or exception must be connected to the statement because they are not independent. The passage concludes with the rule that condition and exception are valid only when attached to the main clause.

Key Terms
بيان (bayan): Clarification or explanation of a text to remove ambiguity or add detail.
بيان تقرير (bayan al-taqrir): A confirming clarification that emphasizes the literal or general meaning by removing possibilities of metaphor or specificity.
بيان تفسير (bayan al-tafsir): An interpretive clarification that explains a mujmal (ambiguous) or mushtarak (homonymous) term so that its meaning is understood.
بيان تغيير (bayan al-taghyeer): An altering clarification that changes a ruling from one state to another, such as from immediate to conditional via a condition or exception.
موصول (mawsul): Connected – the bayan is attached to the original text in the same utterance.
مفصول (mafsool): Disconnected – the bayan is delivered separately from the original text, e.g., at a later time.

The Hanafi Position on Delayed Clarification of Mujmal and Mushtarak
Explains bayan tafsir and the dispute on mawsulan/mafsulan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on delaying clarification of mujmal and mushtarak, and apply the Quranic and rational proofs to justify it.

Core Idea
Delayed clarification of ambiguous texts is valid as a test of belief, not a burden on action.

In this passage, we learn about bayan al-tafsir—the clarification of ambiguous (mujmal) and homonymous (mushtarak) terms. The Hanafi school permits this clarification to be disconnected from the original speech, contrary to some Mutakallimun. We will unpack the arguments, examine the Quranic proof, and understand the rational basis for the Hanafi view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the types of bayan
Recognize that bayan is divided into tafsir (explanatory), taqrir (affirmative), and taghyir (transformative). This lesson focuses on bayan tafsir.
Later, bayan taghyir will be addressed separately.
2
Understand mujmal and mushtarak
Mujmal is an expression needing external clarification to be understood (e.g., 'establish prayer'); mushtarak is a word with multiple meanings (e.g., 'quru'). The text gives examples from the Qur'an and Hadith.
Both require bayan to specify intent.
3
Analyze the Hanafi-Mutakallim dispute
The Mutakallimun argue that clarification of mujmal/mushtarak must be immediate (mawsulan) because speech aims at action. Hanafis respond that delaying clarification serves as a test of belief, and action only becomes required when clarification arrives.
The Quranic verse (75:18-19) with 'thumma' (then) supports delay.
4
Apply the proof
The verse 'thumma inna alayna bayanahu' uses 'thumma' for delay, showing that clarification can be deferred. Hanafis use this as evidence that absolute bayan can be delayed, though they later exclude bayan tafsir from this generality for a special reason.
This is a dalil naqli (textual proof).

An example of mujmal is 'Establish prayer and give zakah' — the mujmal was clarified by both verbal and practical sunnah. An example of mushtarak is Allah's saying 'three quru' (monthly periods) because the word 'quru' is shared between purity and menstruation. According to some mutakallimun, the clarification of mujmal and mushtarak is only valid when connected, because the purpose of speech is to necessitate action, which depends on understanding the meaning, which in turn depends on clarification. If delaying clarification were permissible, it would lead to imposing the impossible. We (Hanafis) say: it benefits a test by believing in the truth at the time while awaiting clarification for action, and there is no harm in that because delaying clarification beyond the time of need is not valid, but delaying it from the (original) speech is valid. Possibly supporting us is Allah's saying: 'So when We have recited it, then follow its recitation. However, we exclude from it bayan tafsir for a reason to come, so bayan taqrir (affirmative clarification) and bayan taghyir (transformative clarification) remain on their original ruling: valid both connected and disconnected.

As for bayan taghyir, like suspension by a condition and exception: the condition mentioned later, such as one's saying 'You are divorced if you enter the house' is a clarification that transforms what preceded from immediate occurrence to suspension. For if he had not said 'if you enter the house', divorce would occur immediately, but with the condition after it, it becomes suspended. This is unlike a preceding condition in our view. Similarly, exception in his saying 'He has one thousand dirhams against me except one hundred' removes the obligation of one hundred from his liability; if he had not said 'except one hundred', the full thousand would be obligatory. And this is only valid when connected, because the condition and exception is itself dependent speech that conveys no meaning without what precedes it, so it must be connected to it. Also because the Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'Whoever takes an oath and then sees something better than it, let him expiate his oath and then do what is better' — he made expiation the release from the oath. If disconnected exception were valid, he would have made it a release as well, by saying now 'if Allah wills' and nullifying the oath. Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar discusses different types of bayan (clarification) in usul al-fiqh and the permissibility of withholding clarification from the original speech. The author first provides examples of bayan tafsir (explanatory clarification) for mujmal (ambiguous) and mushtarak (homonymous) terms. For mujmal, he cites the Qur'anic command to establish prayer and give zakah, which were clarified by the Prophet's words and actions. For mushtarak, he gives the word 'quru' which can mean either purity or menstruation; the hadith about the slave woman's 'iddah shows that in context it means menstruation for free women as well. The core issue is whether such clarification must be connected (mawsulan) to the original statement or can be disconnected (mafsulan); the Mutakallimun argue that because the purpose of speech is to prompt action, clarification must be immediate, or else it would be impossible to obey. The Hanafis counter that a temporary suspension is permissible: one believes the literal meaning and awaits clarification, which is not a burden beyond capacity. They support this with the verse from Surah al-Qiyama where 'thumma' indicates delay, showing that clarification can be deferred. However, they exclude bayan tafsir from this permissibility (detailed later). Then the passage discusses bayan taghyir (transformative clarification), such as conditional clauses and exceptions that change the apparent meaning. For example, 'You are divorced if you enter the house' transforms an immediate divorce into a conditional one. Similarly, 'I owe you one thousand except one hundred' reduces the obligation. These must be connected to the original statement because they are dependent clauses. The author proves this with the hadith about expiation and oath: the Prophet made expiation the only way to release an oath; if disconnected exception like 'if Allah wills' were valid after a delay, it would also release from the oath without expiation, but that is not the case. A reported opinion from Ibn Abbas allowing disconnected exception is rejected as inauthentic.

Key Terms
بيان التفسير (bayān al-tafsīr): Clarification that explains the meaning and referent of a mujmal (ambiguous) or mushtarak (homonymous) term, or similar unclear expressions.
مجمل (mujmal): An expression that is ambiguous in meaning and requires external clarification to be understood properly.
مشترك (mushtarak): A word that has multiple possible meanings (homonym), requiring clarification to determine which is intended.
موصولاً (mawṣūlan): The clarification is attached directly to the original speech in the same utterance.
مفصولاً (mafṣūlan): The clarification is separate from the original speech, whether delayed in time or in a different utterance.

Bayan al-Taghyir: Why Conditions and Exceptions Must Be Connected
Explains bayan taghyir and the requirement of connection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why bayan al-taghyir (e.g., conditions and exceptions) must be connected (mawsulan) and derive the ruling using the hadith on expiation and oath.

Core Idea
Transformative clarifications like conditions and exceptions must attached to the original statement because they are dependent and cannot stand alone.

The second part of our passage turns to bayan al-taghyir—clarification that transforms the original ruling, such as adding a condition or exception. Unlike bayan tafsir, this type must be connected (mawsulan) to the original speech. We will examine why, using examples from divorce and debt, and a powerful proof from the hadith on oath expiation.

Guided Path
1
Define bayan al-taghyir
This is clarification that changes the apparent meaning of a statement. Examples include a condition (e.g., 'if you enter the house') that turns an immediate divorce into a suspended one, or an exception (e.g., 'except one hundred') that reduces an obligation.
It transforms the ruling, unlike taqrir which confirms it.
2
Understand the examples given
Study the two examples: (1) 'You are divorced if you enter the house' – without the condition, divorce occurs immediately; with it, it is suspended. (2) 'I owe you one thousand except one hundred' – without the exception, the full thousand is owed; with it, only nine hundred.
The transformative effect is clear.
3
Analyze the requirement of connection
The passage states that such clarifications must be connected (mawsulan) because they are dependent speech (like condition and exception) that convey no meaning without what precedes them. Also, a hadith shows that expiation is the only way to release an oath, which would be contradicted if disconnected exception were allowed.
The hadith is from Bukhari/Muslim.
4
Evaluate the Ibn Abbas report
A report from Ibn Abbas suggests that disconnected exception (e.g., saying 'if Allah wills' a year later) is valid. The Hanafi author rejects this report as inauthentic, maintaining that only connected exception is valid.
This shows the importance of hadith criticism in usul.

Possibly supporting us is Allah's saying: 'So when We have recited it, then follow its recitation. Then indeed upon Us is its clarification' — 'thumma' indicates delay, and it shows that absolute clarification is permissible to be delayed. However, we exclude from it bayan tafsir for a reason to come, so bayan taqrir (affirmative clarification) and bayan taghyir (transformative clarification) remain on their original ruling: valid both connected and disconnected.

As for bayan taghyir, like suspension by a condition and exception: the condition mentioned later, such as one's saying 'You are divorced if you enter the house' is a clarification that transforms what preceded from immediate occurrence to suspension. For if he had not said 'if you enter the house', divorce would occur immediately, but with the condition after it, it becomes suspended. This is unlike a preceding condition in our view. Similarly, exception in his saying 'He has one thousand dirhams against me except one hundred' removes the obligation of one hundred from his liability; if he had not said 'except one hundred', the full thousand would be obligatory. And this is only valid when connected, because the condition and exception is itself dependent speech that conveys no meaning without what precedes it, so it must be connected to it. Also because the Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'Whoever takes an oath and then sees something better than it, let him expiate his oath and then do what is better' — he made expiation the release from the oath. If disconnected exception were valid, he would have made it a release as well, by saying now 'if Allah wills' and nullifying the oath. It is narrated from Ibn 'Abbas that it is valid disconnected as well, based on a report that he said 'I will certainly humiliate Quraysh' and then after a year said 'if Allah wills' — but this transmission is not authentic according to us. Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar discusses different types of bayan (clarification) in usul al-fiqh and the permissibility of withholding clarification from the original speech. The author first provides examples of bayan tafsir (explanatory clarification) for mujmal (ambiguous) and mushtarak (homonymous) terms. For mujmal, he cites the Qur'anic command to establish prayer and give zakah, which were clarified by the Prophet's words and actions. For mushtarak, he gives the word 'quru' which can mean either purity or menstruation; the hadith about the slave woman's 'iddah shows that in context it means menstruation for free women as well. The core issue is whether such clarification must be connected (mawsulan) to the original statement or can be disconnected (mafsulan); the Mutakallimun argue that because the purpose of speech is to prompt action, clarification must be immediate, or else it would be impossible to obey. The Hanafis counter that a temporary suspension is permissible: one believes the literal meaning and awaits clarification, which is not a burden beyond capacity. They support this with the verse from Surah al-Qiyama where 'thumma' indicates delay, showing that clarification can be deferred. However, they exclude bayan tafsir from this permissibility (detailed later). Then the passage discusses bayan taghyir (transformative clarification), such as conditional clauses and exceptions that change the apparent meaning. For example, 'You are divorced if you enter the house' transforms an immediate divorce into a conditional one. Similarly, 'I owe you one thousand except one hundred' reduces the obligation. These must be connected to the original statement because they are dependent clauses. The author proves this with the hadith about expiation and oath: the Prophet made expiation the only way to release an oath; if disconnected exception like 'if Allah wills' were valid after a delay, it would also release from the oath without expiation, but that is not the case. A reported opinion from Ibn Abbas allowing disconnected exception is rejected as inauthentic.

بيان التغيير (bayān al-taghyīr): Clarification that transforms the original ruling, such as a condition or exception that changes an immediate ruling into a suspended one or limits an obligation.
استثناء (istithnā'): An exception that excludes part of what is included in the general statement.
تعليق (ta'līq): Suspension of a ruling on a condition.

Bayan through Silence in Hanafi Usul
Explains how silence can function as bayan like spoken or implied speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify two types of bayan that can be derived from silence and explain how each functions as legal evidence in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, silence with the ability to object is a valid form of bayan indicating permissibility or consensus.

This passage introduces a key Hanafi usul principle: that silence in a context where objection is possible can serve as a form of legal clarification (bayan). The author distinguishes between bayan equivalent to spoken text (as in a Quranic verse that specifies a share) and bayan that arises from the state of the speaker (such as the Prophet's silence when observing transactions). Understanding this concept is essential for deriving rulings from the Sunnah and ijma.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two types of bayan
The passage states that bayan can be either in the ruling of spoken speech (bi hukm al-mantuq) or in the ruling of implied speech (bi hukm al-muqaddar). Identify the difference: the first has explicit words, the second relies on context and silence.
The second type is also called 'dalalat hal al-mutakallim' — indication from the speaker's state.
2

The verse 'And his parents inherit him, so for his mother is one-third' (Quran 4:11) first mentions parents jointly without specifying shares. Then it specifies the mother's share, thereby clarifying that the father gets the remainder. This is bayan through explicit speech (the specification).
This shows how an ambiguous (mujmal) statement can be clarified by subsequent specification.
3
Understand bayan through the state of the speaker
The speaker is 'silent in words but speaks through his state.' When the Prophet saw people engaging in transactions like mudaraba and did not object, his silence indicated permissibility. This is a form of bayan (clarification) without verbal utterance.
The Hanafi school accepts this as a valid source of law, provided the Prophet had the opportunity to object.
4
Apply the principle to the Companions' silence
The passage recounts Umar's ruling on a slave girl case: he returned the girl to her owner, ordered ransom for the children, but was silent about compensation for profits. This silence, in the presence of other Companions who could object, became ijma (consensus) that no penalty is due. Thus, silence can indicate a ruling.
The condition for such silence to be evidence is that the observer had the power to object and the doer is a Muslim.

Commentary
This passage explains bayan that is established by necessity of brevity or common usage. The example: if someone says 'he owes me one hundred and a dirham' (miah wa dirham), the conjunction 'wa' is considered bayan (clarification) that the hundred is also dirhams, because the speaker omitted repeating 'dirham' due to brevity or common usage. This applies to items that can be established in the dhimma (like measurable/weighable goods). However, if it is something like a garment (thawb) which is not generally in the dhimma except in salam, then the conjunction does not serve as bayan; instead, the interpretation returns to the speaker. Imam al-Shafi'i disagrees and says in all cases the interpretation of 'one hundred' is up to the speaker, so only one dirham is owed in the first example. The Hanafi position maintains a distinction based on the nature of the item.

Core Idea
Naskh does not change Allah's knowledge; it reveals the pre-eternal duration of a ruling.

This passage defines a key usul concept: bayan al-tabdil, which is naskh (abrogation). The Hanafi school holds that naskh is not a change in Allah's will but a clarification (bayan) of the temporal limit of a ruling that Allah always knew. The text uses Quranic verses, the example of wine, and an analogy of murder to explain this dual perspective, and refutes the Jewish objection that naskh implies divine ignorance.

The Nature of Oaths as Legal Causes: Hanafi, Shafi'i, and Zufar
Classifying oaths as real or metaphorical causes for expiation/penalty with three-madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi classification of oaths as metaphorical causes with resemblance to reality and evaluate the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) of this classification in the case of suspended divorce.

Core Idea
Oaths are metaphorically causes with a touch of reality — the Hanafi middle path avoids excess and deficiency.

The legal nature of oaths—whether they directly cause expiation or are merely metaphorical—has a profound impact on how later legal actions affect them. In this passage, the author presents the Hanafi position as an intermediate stance between Imam al-Shafi'i (who treats them as real causes) and Imam Zufar (who treats them as pure metaphor). By examining the reasoning and the fruit of the dispute, we will see how the classification determines whether a subsequent definitive divorce nullifies a suspended conditional divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Positions
Extract the views of Abu Hanifa (Hanafi), al-Shafi'i, and Zufar on whether oaths by Allah and suspended oaths are real causes (sabab haqiqi), metaphorical causes with resemblance (sabab majazi yushbihu al-haqiqah), or pure metaphor (majaz mahd).
Note that Zufar is a Hanafi scholar who differs intra-madhhab.
2
Analyze the Hanafi Reasoning
Understand why the Hanafis say oaths are not real causes: they are legislated to fulfill the matter; fulfillment prevents sin, so expiation is not directly linked to the oath itself. Yet they resemble real causes because violation can trigger expiation.
The key concept is 'shubhat al-haqiqah' (resemblance to reality).
3
Contrast Shafi'i and Zufar
Shafi'i: oaths are real causes but the ruling is delayed until violation. Zufar: oaths are pure metaphor with no resemblance. The Hanafi position bridges these two extremes.
Shafi'i's view leads to the ruling being present from the start, while Zufar's renders the oath completely disconnected from reality.
4
Examine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical consequence: if a man suspends divorce on a condition, then gives a definitive triple divorce, and later the condition occurs after remarriage, according to Hanafi the suspended divorce is void, but according to Zufar it takes effect.
This difference arises from whether the suspended divorce requires a locus (mahall) — the marriage — to subsist.
5
Understand Why the Hanafi Position is the Middle Way
The Hanafi classification as 'majaz yushbihu al-haqiqah' allows the metaphor to retain enough reality to require a locus, but not so much that it would be unaffected by the destruction of that locus.
Thus the ruling is both principled and practically balanced.

Commentary
This passage explains the classification of oaths by Allah (yamin billah) and suspended oaths (ta'liq bil talaq or 'itaq) as metaphorical causes (sabab majazi) for expiation (kaffarah) or penalty (jaza). It begins by stating that such oaths are metaphorical causes because they are legislated to fulfill the matter; fulfillment prevents sin and thus does not lead to expiation. However, because they carry the potential for expiation if violated, they are metaphorically called causes. The passage then presents the Hanafi position as intermediary between the excess of Imam al-Shafi'i (who considers them real causes) and the deficiency of Imam Zufar (who considers them pure metaphor). The Hanafi position is that they are metaphorical but have a resemblance to reality (shubah al-haqiqah). This distinction has practical consequences: if a man suspends divorce on a condition, then later gives a definitive triple divorce, and after remarriage the condition occurs, according to Hanafi the suspended divorce is invalidated because the definitive divorce removed the locus (the marriage), while according to Zufar the suspended divorce still takes effect because the suspension was pure metaphor and does not require a locus.

Key Terms
سبب مجازي (sabab majazi): A metaphorical cause: an action that is not literally a cause for a ruling but is treated as such due to resemblance.
شبهة الحقيقة (shubhat al-haqiqah): A semblance of reality: a characteristic that makes a metaphorical expression similar to a real one.

Definition and Legal Rulings of Forgetfulness (Nisyan) and Sleep (Nawm)
Explaining nisyan and nawm as excuses in worship and rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define nisyan and nawm according to Hanafi usul, explain their legal effects, and distinguish between predominant (ghalib) and rare forgetfulness in worship.

Core Idea
Forgetfulness does not remove obligation, but predominant forgetfulness is excused in worship; sleep postpones the address but does not lift the duty.

In this passage, the commentator presents Hanafi definitions of forgetfulness (nisyan) and sleep (nawm). These are not merely physical states but have precise legal consequences in obligations and rights. Understanding them helps you determine when a forgotten act must be repeated and when a slip is pardoned.

Guided Path
1
Define nisyan and its conditions
Extract the definition: an overwhelming necessity about previously known matters without affliction, with knowledge of other things. Identify what excludes insanity, sleep, blindness.
2
Identify the legal effect of nisyan on obligations to Allah
Understand that nisyan does not negate wujub; qada is required unless forgetfulness is predominant (ghalib).
3
Apply the concept of ghalabah to three cases
Examine why fasting, slaughter (tasmiya), and prayer (salam) are considered ghalib and therefore excused.
4
Contrast with rights of servants
State the rule: nisyan never excuses compensation (daman) in rights of servants.
5
Define nawm and its legal effects
Sleep postpones the address but does not remove obligation; acts of choice (divorce, etc.) are void.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary defines forgetfulness (nisyan) as an overwhelming necessity that affects a person regarding previously known matters without a pathological cause (like insanity), while the person still has knowledge of many other things. It distinguishes forgetfulness from insanity, sleep, and blindness by the condition of 'without affliction' and 'with knowledge'. The key legal point: forgetfulness does not negate the underlying obligation (wujub) in rights of Allah—if one forgets to pray or fast, they must make up (qada) later. However, when forgetfulness is predominant (ghalib), such as in fasting (due to natural inclination to eat/drink), in saying the tasmiya while slaughtering (due to awe), or in giving salam at the first tashahhud due to confusion with the second, these are excused and do not invalidate the act. The condition 'predominant' excludes cases where forgetfulness is unlikely, like speaking in prayer forgetfully, because the state of prayer itself serves as a reminder. This is the Hanafi position. Regarding rights of servants (huquq al-‘ibad), forgetfulness is never an excuse—if one destroys another's property forgetfully, compensation (daman) is obligatory. The passage then discusses sleep (nawm): it is a natural pause without choice that postpones the divine address (khitab) but does not remove the obligation. Thus, a sleeping person is obligated for the time-bound worship but is not required to perform it while asleep; if they wake up within time, they perform, otherwise they make up. Sleep also voidates voluntary choices: any divorce, manumission, Islam, or apostasy done in sleep has no legal effect. Similarly, recitation, speech, or laughter in sleep does not affect prayer. The commentator expands on the base matn, providing causal reasoning (ta'lil) and clarifying the Hanafi stance.
