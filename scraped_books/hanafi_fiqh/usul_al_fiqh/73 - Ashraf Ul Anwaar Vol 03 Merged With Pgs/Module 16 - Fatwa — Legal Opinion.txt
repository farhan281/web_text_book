Taqlid of a Tabi'i in the Hanafi School
Discussing the ruling on following a Tabi'i's fatwa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle regarding the authority of a Tabi'i's fatwa and identify the condition under which it may be followed.

Core Idea
A Tabi'i's fatwa is binding only if it became known and accepted in the time of the Companions.

The authority of a Tabi'i (Successor) in giving fatwa is a nuanced issue in the Hanafi school. While Imam Abu Hanifa famously said 'They are men and we are men,' other scholars consider a Tabi'i's ruling binding if it was well known and uncontested during the Companion era. This lesson unpacks the evidence and the preponderant view, training you to apply the correct usul principle when evaluating such secondary sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the different Hanafi views
First, isolate the two positions: some permit taqlid of a Tabi'i whose fatwa was known and accepted (e.g., Shuraih, Masruq), while Abu Hanifa generally rejects it. Note the condition attached to the permissive view.
2
Analyze the evidence for the permissive view
Examine the incident of Qadi Shuraih and the acceptance of his ruling by Ali (RA). Consider the significance of Ali's silence (sukut) and how it is interpreted as tacit approval (ijma suktuti).
This is a key usul principle: silence in the presence of a ruling can indicate acceptance.
3
Analyze the evidence from Masruq
Study how Masruq's opinion on vowing to sacrifice a child was accepted without objection from the Companions, becoming a form of ijma. Understand the qiyas he used from the story of Ismail.
4
Understand Abu Hanifa's objection
Abu Hanifa's position is based on the unique authority of Companions due to their direct companionship with the Prophet. Recognize that this objection does not deny the possibility of following a Tabi'i under specific conditions.
5
Apply the preferred view (Shams al-A'imma)
The chosen view is that a Tabi'i's fatwa is followed only if it became apparent in the Companion's time and was not rejected. Otherwise, the Tabi'i is treated like any other jurist, and his taqlid is not valid.
This is the operative rule for usul al-fiqh analysis.

As for the Tabi'i, if his fatwa became apparent during the time of the Companions, then in any case a Tabi'i, if his saying became well-known in the era of the Companions, like the status of Hadrat Shuraih, then such a Tabi'i's saying is in the view of some equal to the saying of a Companion, and its taqlid is wajib. This is the preponderant view. As narrated: Hadrat Ali (RA) filed a case in the court of Qadi Shuraih regarding his armor, saying 'This is my armor, I recognize it.' The case was filed against a Jew. Hadrat Shuraih asked the Jew, 'What do you say?' The Jew replied, 'The armor is mine, it is in my hand (I possess it).' Then Hadrat Shuraih asked Hadrat Ali for two witnesses. Hadrat Ali presented his son Hadrat Hasan (RA) and his slave Qanbar to testify in Shuraih's court. Hadrat Shuraih said, 'In any case, regarding your slave, I have accepted his testimony because he is freed. As for your son's testimony for you, I will not allow it.' It was from the madhhab of Ali that he considered valid the testimony of a son for his father, but Shuraih differed with him in that. Ali did not object to him, so he handed the armor to the Jew. Then the Jew said: 'O Commander of the Faithful, you went with me to your judge and he ruled against you, and you were satisfied with it. By Allah, I testify that it is your armor.' And the Jew became Muslim, and Ali gave the armor back to him and gifted him a horse, and he remained with Ali until he was martyred in the battle of Siffin. Similarly, Masruq was a Tabi'i who differed with Ibn Abbas on the issue of vowing to slaughter a child. Ibn Abbas said: 'Whoever vows to slaughter his child owes one hundred camels like blood money,' analogizing to the blood wit. Masruq said: 'No, rather he must slaughter a sheep,' and he reasoned with the ransom of Ismail (AS) when Ibrahim (AS) sacrificed a ram in place of Ismail (AS) by God's command. No one objected to Masruq's ruling, so it became consensus. It is narrated from Abu Hanifa: 'I do not do taqlid of the Tabi'i, because they are men and we are men.' The qawl of the Companion is accepted due to the possibility of having heard (from the Prophet) and the infallibility of their opinion through the blessing of companionship with the Prophet, which is absent in the Tabi'i. And this is the chosen view of Shams al-A'imma. All this applies when his fatwa was known in the time of the Companions. If his fatwa was not known and he did not oppose in opinion, he is like other jurists, and his taqlid is not valid. From the incident of Ali, it is understood that the testimony of a son for his father was permissible according to Ali, but Qadi Shuraih differed with him, yet Ali did not object (because he was both a Companion and the Commander of the Faithful). Therefore, Ali handed the armor to the Jew. Then the Jew said: 'I testify that by Allah it is your armor,' and he became Muslim. So Ali gave the armor back to him and gave him a horse, and the Jew stayed with him until he was martyred in Siffin. Similarly, Masruq was a Tabi'i who differed with Ibn Abbas on the issue of vowing to slaughter a child. Ibn Abbas said that one who vows to slaughter his son owes one hundred camels as blood money, analogizing to the value of a life. Masruq said it is not so; rather, he must slaughter a sheep, citing the ransom of Ismail (AS). No one rejected Masruq's ruling, so it became consensus. Imam Abu Hanifa is reported to have said: 'I will not do taqlid of the Tabi'i, because they are men and we are men.' The qawl of the Companion is accepted due to the likelihood of hearing from the Prophet and the correctness of their opinion through the blessing of companionship, which is not found in the Tabi'i. This is the chosen view of Shams al-A'imma. The difference is only when the Tabi'i's standing became apparent in the time of the Companions.

Commentary
This passage discusses the authority of a Tabi'i's (Successor's) opinion. The author explains that if a Tabi'i's fatwa was well-known during the era of the Companions and no objection was raised, some scholars consider it equivalent to a Companion's saying, making taqlid wajib. Two historical incidents illustrate this: (1) Qadi Shuraih, a Tabi'i, gave a ruling contrary to Ali's view regarding a son's testimony for his father, and Ali accepted it without objection, indicating acceptance. (2) Masruq, another Tabi'i, differed with Ibn Abbas on the penalty for vowing to sacrifice a child; Masruq's opinion was accepted without rejection, forming consensus. However, Imam Abu Hanifa is reported to have said he does not follow Tabi'un because they lack the direct companionship of the Prophet. The preferred view (mukhtar) of Shams al-A'imma is that a Tabi'i's qawl is followed only if it became manifest in the Companion's time; otherwise, he is like any other jurist and taqlid is not valid.

Key Terms
تابعی (tabi'i): A successor who met the Companions but not the Prophet.
فتوى (fatwa): A legal opinion given by a qualified jurist.
رايج مسلک (rajih maslak): The preponderant view in the madhhab.
سكوت (sukut): Silence; in usul, silence of a Companion in the presence of a ruling can indicate approval.
