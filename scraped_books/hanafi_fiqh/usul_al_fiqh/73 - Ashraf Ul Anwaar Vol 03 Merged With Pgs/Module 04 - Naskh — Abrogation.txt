Refutation of the Objection: Khabar al-Wahid and the Requirement of Knowledge
Usul dispute refuting objection to khabar al-wahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi response to the objection that khabar al-wahid cannot obligate action because it does not produce certain knowledge, and identify the specific linguistic argument (indefinite noun in negative context restricted by context) used to interpret the Quranic verse.

Core Idea
The verse 'Do not pursue what you have no knowledge of' is about false testimony, not khabar al-wahid — indefinite noun in negative context is restricted by context.

This passage addresses a fundamental objection to the authority of khabar al-wahid: if it does not yield certain knowledge (yaqeen), how can it obligate action? The objector cites the Quranic verse 'Do not pursue that of which you have no knowledge' to argue that action requires knowledge. The Hanafi response carefully distinguishes between the general wording and the specific context, showing that the verse targets false testimony, not all probabilistic reports. This lesson unpacks the logical structure of the objection and the linguistic finesse of the response.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
The objector argues that khabar al-wahid cannot obligate action because it does not produce certain knowledge, citing the verse as evidence.
2
Analyze the logical chain
The objection uses a necessary connection (luzum) between knowledge and action. The argument: if the report does not give knowledge, it cannot necessitate action; conversely, if it does give knowledge, it would necessitate action. This is a false dichotomy.
3
Identify the Hanafi response
The Hanafi scholar responds that the verse is specifically about false testimony (shahadat al-zur), not about accepting reports of uncertain knowledge. The linguistic clue is the indefinite noun 'ilm in a negative context, which normally implies generality, but the context (pursuing without knowledge) points to false testimony.
4
Apply the principle of contextual specification (takhsis)
Show how the indefinite in negative context is restricted by the context of the revelation.
5
Conclude the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafis accept khabar al-wahid as binding for action; the opposing view would require certainty for every action, which is impractical.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection to the authority of khabar al-wahid: since it does not produce yaqeen (certain knowledge), it cannot obligate action, based on the Quranic verse 'Do not pursue that of which you have no knowledge.' The argument plays on the necessary relationship (luzum) between knowledge and action. The response refutes this by explaining that the verse refers specifically to false testimony (shahadat al-zur), not to accepting khabar al-wahid. Thus, the verse does not negate acting upon probable knowledge from khabar al-wahid.

Naskh by Chronological Order: Pregnant Widow's 'Iddah
Uses chronological knowledge to abrogate a general verse with a later specific verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a naskh case based on known chronological order and apply the Hanafi principle that the later verse abrogates the earlier when the chronology is established, using the pregnant widow's 'iddah as an example.

Core Idea
When chronology is known, the later nāsikh abrogates the earlier mansūkh.

This passage demonstrates one of the most definitive methods of resolving textual contradictions in the Quran: abrogation (naskh) through established chronological order. The example of the pregnant widow's 'iddah illustrates how a later specific verse (Sūrat al-Ṭalāq 65:4) abrogates an earlier general verse (Sūrat al-Baqarah 2:234) for the overlapping case. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly deriving rulings when texts conflict, a key skill in uṣūl al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contradicting texts
Locate the two Quranic verses that appear to conflict: Sūrat al-Baqarah 2:234 (four months and ten days for widows) and Sūrat al-Ṭalāq 65:4 (pregnant women's term ends with childbirth). Both are general in scope, leading to a contradiction for the pregnant widow.
The conflict is only for the common case (pregnant widow). Each verse is independently clear.
2
Determine the chronological order
Identify which verse was revealed later. The passage cites Ibn Mas'ūd's firm assertion (mubāhalah) that Sūrat al-Ṭalāq (65:4) is later than Sūrat al-Baqarah (2:234). This chronological knowledge is the key to resolving the conflict through naskh.
The Hanafi school accepts such transmitted knowledge of chronology as proof for naskh.
3
Apply the naskh principle
Once chronology is known, the later verse (65:4) becomes the nāsikh (abrogator) and the earlier verse (2:234) is mansūkh (abrogated) for the overlapping case. Apply this to the pregnant widow: her 'iddah ends with childbirth, not after four months and ten days.
This principle requires that the two texts are genuinely irreconcilable (ta'āruḍ) and that the later text is operative.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that a pregnant widow's 'iddah ends at childbirth according to the Hanafi school, whereas 'Alī's precautionary opinion would take the longer period. This illustrates how a methodological choice (whether to rely on chronological naskh) produces a concrete difference in ruling.
The Hanafi position is supported by 'Umar and adopted by Abū Ḥanīfah and al-Shāfi'ī.

Commentary
This passage presents the second method of resolving textual contradiction: abrogation (naskh) when the chronological order is known. The example is the apparent conflict between two verses: Sūrat al-Baqarah (2:234) prescribes a four-month-ten-day waiting period for widows, while Sūrat al-Ṭalāq (65:4) prescribes that pregnant women's waiting period ends with childbirth. The common case is a pregnant widow. The author reports the opinion of 'Alī (taking the longer period) and the opinion of Ibn Mas'ūd (only childbirth), who argued vehemently that the later verse (Sūrat al-Ṭalāq) abrogates the earlier (Sūrat al-Baqarah) for pregnant widows. The passage then notes that 'Umar and both Abū Ḥanīfah and al-Shāfi'ī adopted Ibn Mas'ūd's view. This illustrates the usul principle that when the date of revelation is known, the later text abrogates the earlier, provided the two are genuinely contradictory. The Hanafi school applies this principle, but prefers reconciliation when possible.

Key Terms
الناسخ (al-nāsikh): The later revelation that abrogates a previous ruling.
المباهلة (al-mubāhalah): A mutual imprecation invoking curse on the liar; used by Ibn Mas'ūd to assert certainty about chronology.
العدة (al-'iddah): The waiting period a woman must observe after divorce or death of husband before remarriage.
العموم والخصوص من وجه (al-umūm wa al-khuṣūṣ min wajh): Partial generality and specificity: two texts each apply to a set of cases, overlapping only in some, making them partially contradictory.

Naskh by Chronology and the Principle of Hazard vs Mubah
Explains naskh by known chronology and hazr precedence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when naskh applies based on known chronology and apply the rule that a prohibitory text (hazr) takes precedence over a permissive text (mubah) in the Hanafi usul framework.

Core Idea
When dates are known, later abrogates earlier; when unknown, prohibition overrides permission.

This lesson examines two Hanafi usul tools for resolving apparent textual contradictions. The first relies on clear chronology: if the revelation dates of two conflicting verses are known, the later abrogates the earlier. The second applies when dates are unknown: prohibitory texts (hazr) are given priority over permissive ones (mubah) because permissibility is the original state. Both are demonstrated through the classical case of the pregnant widow's iddah, where a Companions' dispute is resolved by naskh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict
Locate the two Quranic verses (2:234 and 65:4) and note the area of overlap: both speak about iddah, but differ for a pregnant widow.
The common matter is the pregnant widow; 2:234 gives 4 months 10 days, 65:4 says deliver.
2
Apply naskh if chronology is known
Check if the revelation dates are known. Surah al-Talaq (65:4) is later than Surah al-Baqarah (2:234). Therefore, 65:4 abrogates 2:234 for the overlapping case.
This is the argument of Ibn Mas'ud and the Hanafi position.
3
Note the ikhtilaf among Companions
Observe that 'Ali preferred precaution (longer term) due to uncertainty. Ibn Mas'ud argued known chronology and challenged to mubahalah.
The dispute is not about the text but about whether the chronology is decisive.
4
Apply the hazr-mubah principle
If chronology is unknown, the prohibitory ruling (hazr) takes precedence over the permissive (mubah) because ibahah is the default, and any prohibition overrides it.
This is a separate method: hazr is considered later in indication even if not later in time.
5
Derive the Hanafi ruling
The Hanafi mufta bihi is that a pregnant widow's iddah ends with delivery, following naskh, supported by 'Umar, Ibn Mas'ud, and adopted by Abu Hanifa and al-Shafi'i.
The thamarah: she can remarry immediately after birth, even if husband's body is still present.

Commentary
This passage is a naskh analysis and a usul rule dealing with two ways to resolve apparent conflict between texts: first, by clear chronology (ikhtilaf al-zaman sarihan), and second, by the general principle that a prohibitory text (hazr) takes precedence over a permissive text (ibahah) when both apply to the same situation. The author begins with the first method: when the dates of revelation are known, the later text abrogates the earlier. The prime example is the two Quranic verses on iddah: Surah al-Baqarah 2:234 prescribes four months and ten days for widows, regardless of pregnancy. Surah al-Talaq 65:4 prescribes that the iddah of a pregnant woman ends with delivery. These verses conflict specifically for a pregnant widow. The Companions differed: 'Ali (ra) preferred precaution (the longer period), while Ibn Mas'ud (ra) argued that Surah al-Talaq was revealed later and thus abrogates the earlier verse for pregnant women. Ibn Mas'ud even challenged 'Ali to mubahalah (imprecation). The passage notes that 'Umar (ra) agreed with Ibn Mas'ud, and the Hanafi school (alongside al-Shafi'i) adopted this ruling: a pregnant widow's iddah ends with delivery, even if she gives birth while the funeral is still present. The second method is 'indication' (dalalah): when the time difference is not explicitly known, the prohibitory text is given precedence over the permissive text, because permissibility is the original state of things, so any prohibition takes priority. This is a standard Hanafi usul principle: when hazr (prohibition) and ibahah (permission) conflict, hazr is prioritized and considered later in indication.

Key Terms
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation: the later revealed ruling cancels the earlier ruling on the same subject.
حاظر (hazir): Prohibitory text or ruling.
مبيح (mubih): Permissive text or ruling.

Preferring the Prohibitor over the Permitter when Texts Conflict
Explains the rule and reasoning for preferring prohibitory texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that gives precedence to a prohibitory text over a permissive text when they conflict, and explain the reasoning from the principle of original permissibility.

Core Idea
When a prohibitor and permitter conflict, the prohibitor abrogates both original permissibility and the permitter.

In this lesson, we explore a foundational Hanafi usul rule: when two pieces of evidence conflict—one indicating prohibition and the other permissibility—the prohibitory evidence is given precedence. This rule is rooted in the principle that permissibility is the original state of things (al-ibāḥa al-aṣliyya). The author illustrates this with the classic example of the pregnant widow's waiting period from the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of evidence
The passage distinguishes between al-hāẓir (prohibitor) and al-mubīḥ (permitter). Understand each as textual evidence indicating prohibition or permissibility.
2
State the rule of preference
The rule: when they conflict, act upon the prohibitor and consider it as later (abrogating) in time.
This is a standing rule in Hanafi usul.
3
Understand the rationale
The basis is that permissibility is original; the prohibitor abrogates both the original permissibility and the permitter text.
If we acted on the permitter, it would necessitate double abrogation, which is unreasonable.
4
Apply to the example
Consider the Quranic verses on the pregnant widow's iddah: al-Baqarah 2:234 prescribes four months ten days, while al-Talaq 65:4 states the term ends with childbirth. The prohibitor (the shorter term indicated by the later verse) prevails.
Companion Ibn Mas'ud held that the later verse abrogates the earlier for pregnant widows.
5
Note the alternative views
Some scholars hold prohibition as the original rule, others suspend judgment. Recognize that this rule depends on accepting original permissibility.
The author references Tafsir al-Ahmadi for a fuller discussion.

Commentary
This passage presents a fundamental Hanafi usul rule: when two pieces of evidence conflict—one indicating prohibition and the other permissibility—the prohibitory evidence is given precedence over the permissive one. The reasoning is that permissibility is the original state of things; a prohibitory text is therefore considered to be later in time and thus abrogates both the original permissibility and the permissive text. The author provides the classic example of the two Quranic verses concerning the waiting period of a pregnant widow: Surah al-Baqarah (2:234) prescribes four months and ten days, while Surah al-Talaq (65:4) states that the term ends with childbirth. According to Ibn Mas'ud (and the Hanafis), the latter verse abrogates the former for pregnant widows, so the 'iddah is by childbirth. The author also discusses alternative views (some hold prohibition as original, others suspend judgment) and emphasizes that this rule is a major principle from which many furu' rulings are derived.

المبيح (al-mubīḥ): The textual evidence that indicates permissibility (ibāḥa).
الإباحة الأصلية (al-ibāḥa al-aṣliyya): The original permissibility of things before divine legislation.

Naskh as Bayan Tabdil: Definition and Proof
Defining naskh as a type of bayan and proving its legitimacy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of naskh, identify its Quranic proof, and explain why it does not imply divine ignorance.

Core Idea
Naskh is a divine clarification (bayan) of a preordained time limit, not a change in divine knowledge.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi definition of naskh (abrogation) as a type of bayan called bayan al-tabdil (clarification of change). The author explains that from Allah's perspective, naskh is merely a disclosure of the time limit He already decreed for a ruling, while from human perspective it appears as a substitution. We will examine the textual proof and rational analogy that refute objections to abrogation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of naskh
Read the passage where the author defines naskh: 'wa huwa bayan li muddat al-hukm al-mutlaq...' and note that it is a clarification of the duration of an absolute ruling that was known to Allah.
The definition emphasizes that naskh is bayan (clarification) for Allah but tabdil (change) for humans.
2
Examine the Quranic proof
Locate the two Quranic verses cited: 2:106 and 16:101. Understand that they explicitly mention abrogation and substitution, establishing its validity.
These verses are qati' al-thubut and qati' al-dalalah for the permissibility of naskh.
3
Analyze the analogy with the physician
The author uses the example of a physician changing treatments based on the patient's condition to argue that Allah's abrogation is wise, not foolish. This is a rational (aqli) defense against Jewish objections.
The analogy shows that change in ruling does not imply ignorance but adaptation to changing circumstances.
4
Identify the scope of naskh
The passage states that naskh only applies to rulings that admit existence and non-existence (mumkin 'amali), not to inherently obligatory beliefs (like iman) or inherently impossible acts (like kufr).
This sets the boundaries: naskh does not affect fundamental beliefs.
5
Refute the Jewish objection
The Jews argued that naskh implies safah (foolishness) and jahl (ignorance). The Hanafi response is that Allah is Hakim (Wise) and knows the interests of His servants, just as a physician knows when to change a prescription.
The thamarah al-khilaf is the rejection of naskh by Jews, leading to their belief in the eternity of Musa's Sharia.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary (sharh) on Nur al-Anwar defines naskh (abrogation) as a type of bayan (clarification) called bayan al-tabdil (clarification of change). The author first distinguishes between two types of bayan: one where the speaker's intent is sought for interpretation (discussed briefly at the start) and then bayan al-tabdil, which is naskh. The core definition: naskh is a clarification (bayan) of the time limit of an absolute ruling that was already known to Allah but stated without mentioning its duration, so humans thought it was permanent. When the new ruling comes, from Allah's perspective it is a clarification of His preordained time, but from humans' perspective it appears as a change. The example of wine (khamr) is given: initially permitted absolutely, then later prohibited. The text provides a powerful analogy with killing: when a person kills another, from Allah's perspective it is the realization of the predestined death, but from humans it is a change in what they expected. This analogy refutes the Jewish objection that abrogation implies divine ignorance. The Jews are refuted by arguing that Allah is like a wise physician who changes prescriptions according to changing circumstances. The passage also clarifies that naskh only applies to rulings that are intrinsically possible (not inherently obligatory like faith nor inherently impossible like disbelief) and that time-bound rulings are not subject to naskh before their time. A Hanafi note: the argument that naskh is permissible is based on the Quranic verses quoted (al-Baqarah 106 and an-Nahl 101), and the analogy with the physician is a rational proof (dalil aqli) to defend against the Jews. The ruling is from the core Hanafi usul position that naskh is valid and does not entail divine ignorance.

Key Terms
النَّسْخُ (al-naskh): A clarification (bayan) of the time limit of an absolute ruling that was known to Allah but revealed without specifying its duration, so it appears as a change (tabdil) to humans.
الْبَيَانُ (al-bayan): Disclosure or clarification of Allah's intent regarding a ruling, including both initial explanation and abrogation.
التَّبْدِيلُ (al-tabdil): Change or substitution; in the context of naskh, it is the replacement of one ruling with another from the perspective of humans.
مُمْكِنٌ عَمَلِيّ (mumkin 'amali): A practical matter that is possible to exist or not exist; rulings about such matters are subject to naskh.

Definition and Conditions of Naskh in Hanafi Usul
Defining the subject and conditions of naskh and refuting the Jewish rejection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define naskh according to the Hanafis, identify its subject and conditions, and explain why the Jewish objection is refuted.

Core Idea
Naskh does not imply change in Allah's knowledge; it is a statement of a pre-known period, applicable only to possible action-related rulings.

This lesson explores the Hanafi definition of naskh (abrogation) and its essential conditions. We will examine why naskh is permissible, what rulings can be abrogated, and how it differs from time-bound rulings. The passage also refutes the Jewish claim that naskh implies divine ignorance, using a powerful analogy from medicine.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of naskh
Locate the Hanafi definition: naskh is the lifting (raf') of a ruling by a later evidence from the Lawgiver. Understand that from Allah's perspective it is a statement (bayan) of what was always known, while from our perspective it is a change (tabdil).
The author compares naskh to killing: the killer causes a change in our perception, but the death was decreed.
2
Determine the subject (mahall) of naskh
Naskh applies only to rulings that admit existence and non-existence, are possible (mumkin), action-related, and not intrinsically obligatory (like iman) or impossible (like kufr). Also, the ruling must not be time-bound (muwaqqat).
Intrinsic obligations and prohibitions are never abrogated across religions.
3
Analyze the condition of time limitation (tawqit)
If a ruling is explicitly time-bound, it cannot be abrogated before the time ends, and after the time it is not called naskh—the ruling simply expires.
Examples from the Quran: 'فاعفوا واصفحوا حتى يأتي الله بأمره' shows temporary obligation.
4
Examine the dispute with the Jews
The Jews argue that naskh implies Allah is ignorant. The Hanafi response: Allah is wise and changes rulings based on changing human welfare, like a physician adjusting prescriptions daily.
The physician analogy refutes the claim of foolishness.
5
Distinguish naskh from news and stories
The author corrects examples given by others (e.g., the three days for Salih's people) which are merely news, not legislative rulings. Proper examples involve commands that are later superseded.
Focus on legislative verses, not historical accounts.

Commentary
This passage defines the Hanafi doctrine of naskh (abrogation) and its subject (mahall). The author begins by explaining that apparent changes in rulings (like the prohibition of something previously permitted) are from the perspective of the people a change (tabdil), but from Allah's perspective it is merely a statement (bayan) of what was already in His knowledge. This is likened to murder, where the killer causes a premature death, but the death was always decreed. Then the author asserts that naskh is permissible (ja'iz) by the Quranic text, contrary to the Jews who deny it. The Jewish objection is that naskh implies that Allah changes His mind out of ignorance. The Hanafi response is that Allah is wise and judges according to changing circumstances and human welfare, just as a physician changes prescriptions based on the patient's condition. The subject of naskh is a ruling that is possible (mumkin) and action-related, not intrinsically obligatory like iman or intrinsically prohibited like kufr – those are never abrogated. Furthermore, if a ruling is time-bound (muwaqqat), it cannot be called naskh when it ends; naskh only applies before the time ends. The author criticizes examples from other scholars (like the three days for Salih's people) which are merely reports, not legislative rulings. He gives better examples from the Quran: the command to pardon until Allah brings another command, and the command to keep sinful women in houses until death or another way. Thus, this passage is a comprehensive treatment of the definition and conditions of naskh in Hanafi usul.

Key Terms
النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation: lifting (raf') of a ruling by a later evidence from the Lawgiver, applicable to rulings that are possible and not time-bound.
التوقيت (al-tawqīt): Time limitation: attaching a specific time period to a ruling; such a ruling cannot be abrogated before the time ends and is not called naskh after it ends.

Conditions for Naskh and the Mu'tazila Dispute on Ability to Act
Explains conditions for naskh and refutes Mu'tazila requirement of ability to act

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi conditions for a ruling to be abrogated and apply the Hanafi principle that mental assent suffices, distinguishing it from the Mu'tazila position.

Core Idea
A ruling can be abrogated only if possible, not temporally limited, not perpetual, and mentally assented.

Naskh (abrogation) is a key mechanism in Islamic law by which Allah replaces one ruling with another. However, not every ruling can be abrogated; it must meet certain conditions. This passage from a Hanafi usul text outlines those conditions and then addresses a critical dispute with the Mu'tazila: does the mukallaf need to be able to physically perform the ruling before it can be abrogated, or is mental assent sufficient? The Hanafi position, supported by the famous event of the fifty prayers on the Night of Ascension, asserts that mental assent alone is enough.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general condition for a ruling to be abrogated
Read the opening sentence: the ruling must be possible in itself, i.e., not essentially obligatory (like faith) nor essentially impossible (like disbelief). It must also relate to action.
This excludes beliefs (i'tiqadiyyat) from the scope of naskh.
2
Recognize the barriers to naskh: tawqit and ta'bid
A ruling that has a fixed time limit (tawqit) cannot be abrogated before that time, and after it, naskh is irrelevant. A ruling declared perpetual (ta'bid) either explicitly (with the word 'abad') or by implication (like the finality of prophethood) is immune to naskh.
The author corrects some scholars' examples, stressing that only legal rulings (ahkam), not reports (akhbar), are relevant.
3
Understand the correct examples for tawqit and ta'bid
For tawqit, the author recommends verses like 'So pardon and forgive until Allah brings His command' (Surah Al-Baqarah 2:109) and the detention of adulteresses until death or a divine alternative. For ta'bid, the verse 'And never accept their testimony ever' (Surah An-Nur 24:4) regarding those flogged for slander is a proper example of a perpetual ruling.
Note that examples involving eternal punishment or reward are reports, not rulings, and thus not proper illustrations.
4
Analyze the dispute with the Mu'tazila over the condition of ability to act
The Mu'tazila require a period of time sufficient for the mukallaf to physically perform the ruling before naskh can occur. The Hanafis reject this, requiring only the ability to mentally assent to the ruling.
The practical difference: if a ruling is revealed and immediately abrogated, it is valid according to Hanafis but invalid according to the Mu'tazila.
5
Apply the Hanafi proof from the Mi'raj incident
The Prophet (peace be upon him) was commanded with fifty prayers on the Night of Ascension. Within a short time, the obligation was reduced to five. No one, including the Prophet, could have physically performed fifty prayers in that interval. Only mental assent (belief in the command) was possible. The Prophet's assent suffices for the entire ummah because he is their imam. Thus, naskh is valid without physical ability.
This event is considered a dalil naqli (textual proof) for the Hanafi position.

Commentary
This passage, from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary, explains the conditions that a ruling must meet to be subject to naskh (abrogation). The author opens by stating the general condition: the ruling must be possible in itself—it cannot be a necessary obligation (like faith) nor an impossible prohibition (like disbelief). Additionally, the ruling must not have an attached temporal limitation (tawqit) or a declaration of perpetuity (ta'bid) established by explicit text or by implication. The author then criticizes some scholars' examples for tawqit (the three days of enjoyment for Thamud, Joseph's seven years of cultivation), pointing out that these are reports/stories, not legal rulings. Better examples are verses from the Quran that have built-in temporal limits, such as the command to forgive until Allah gives another order, or the detention of adulteresses until death or a divine alternative. Similarly, for ta'bid, the author rejects using the verse about eternity in Paradise/Hell because those are akhbari (reports), not ahkam. The better example is the verse forbidding acceptance of testimony from those flogged for qadhf 'ever'—since it is a legal ruling with explicit perpetuity, it cannot be abrogated. Then the author discusses a key dispute with the Mu'tazila: for naskh to occur, is it required that the mukallaf be able to physically perform the ruling, or just mentally assent to it? The Hanafi position is that mental assent (tamakkun min 'aqd al-qalb) suffices. The proof is the incident of the fifty prayers on the Night of Ascension: the Prophet and the ummah could not possibly have performed fifty prayers in that short time, but they could mentally accept the obligation, and then it was abrogated to five. This shows that ability to act is not a condition.

Key Terms
تَوْقِيت (tawqīt): Attaching a specific time to a ruling such that it cannot be abrogated before that time and ceases to be a subject of naskh after it.
تَأْبِيد (ta'bīd): Declaring a ruling perpetual, either explicitly with the word 'abad' or implicitly by context (e.g., finality of prophethood). Such rulings are immune to naskh.
التَّمَكُّن مِنْ عَقْدِ الْقَلْب (al-tamakkun min 'aqd al-qalb): The ability of the mukallaf to mentally assent to and believe in a ruling before it is abrogated.

Ta'bid: Establishing Eternal Rulings by Nass or Dalalah
Explains ta'bid and its two modes of establishment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the two modes of establishing ta'bid (nass and dalalah) in Hanafi usul, and evaluate whether a given text qualifies as a ruling (hukm) for eternal application.

Core Idea
A ruling is eternally binding either by explicit text or clear indication, but only if it is a hukm, not a khabar.

This lesson delves into the concept of ta'bid—the eternal establishment of a ruling that renders it immune to abrogation (naskh). The passage you are about to study clarifies that ta'bid can be established in two ways: by explicit textual mention of eternity (nass) or by contextual indication (dalalah). More importantly, the author warns against a common mistake: using Quranic reports about eternal punishment as examples of ta'bid, when they are not rulings at all. This careful distinction is crucial for proper usul analysis.

Guided Path
1
Define ta'bid and its legal effect
Ta'bid means a ruling is permanently fixed and cannot be abrogated. First, understand that ta'bid applies only to rulings (ahkam), not to reports (akhbar) about eternal states.
The passage emphasizes that the khulud verse is a report, not a ruling.
2
Identify the first mode: nass (explicit text)
When the text explicitly uses the word 'abad' or its derivatives, the ruling is eternally binding. Look for the linguistic marker of eternity.
Example: the qadhf verse 'laa taqbaloo lahum shahaadatan abada' literally says 'never accept their testimony'.
3
Identify the second mode: dalalah (indication)
Even without the word 'abad', a ruling can be eternal if the Prophet (s) held it at his death and there is no evidence of abrogation. This is inferred from the fact that no prophet comes after him to change it.
The author refers to 'sharia rules that the Prophet passed away holding' as examples of dalalah.
4
Analyze the author's critique of the khulud example
Some scholars used the verse 'khaalideena feehaa abada' as a counterpart. The author refutes this: that verse is informational (khabar), not prescriptive (hukm). Abrogation applies only to rulings, so it is not a valid example of ta'bid.
The critique shows the importance of distinguishing between akhbar and ahkam in usul.
5
Apply the correct example: the qadhf verse
The author presents the qadhf verse (Surah al-Nur, 24:4) as a proper ta'bid example because it is a command (hukm) and explicitly uses 'abad'.
This verse is a ruling that remains unabrogated.

Condition for Naskh: Belief vs. Action
Dispute with Mu'tazila on the condition for naskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi condition for naskh (tamakkun min 'aqd al-qalb) and evaluate the Mu'tazilite counterargument using the Mi'raj incident.

Core Idea
For naskh, only ability to believe is required, not ability to act.

In this lesson, we explore a key usul dispute between the Hanafi school and the Mu'tazila regarding the conditions for abrogation (naskh). The Hanafis hold that a ruling can be abrogated as soon as the mukallaf has had the opportunity to mentally accept it, even if no time to act has passed. The Mu'tazila insist that there must be a sufficient period to actually perform the commanded act. The Hanafi proof is a powerful one: the command for fifty prayers on the night of Mi'raj, abrogated before anyone could pray even one.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the condition for naskh according to the Hanafis: tamakkun min 'aqd al-qalb (ability to mentally assent) only, not tamakkun min al-fi'l (ability to act).
2
Identify the Mu'tazilite position
Contrast with the Mu'tazila: they require tamakkun min al-fi'l before a ruling can be abrogated.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof (Mi'raj)
Examine the incident of the fifty prayers: commanded on Mi'raj, then reduced to five within the same night, before any prayer could be performed. This demonstrates that action is not required for valid naskh.
The Prophet's belief suffices for the entire ummah.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi view, abrogation can occur immediately after revelation; under Mu'tazilite view, it must be delayed until believers have had time to act.
5
Summarize the tarjih
The Hanafi position is preferred because it is supported by a clear historical precedent, while the Mu'tazilite condition would invalidate the agreed-upon naskh of the fifty prayers.

Core Idea
Qiyas cannot abrogate any proof because abrogation requires a textual source, not mere analogy.

In this lesson, we examine a fundamental Hanafi usul principle: qiyas (analogy) is not a valid abrogator (nasikh) for any of the four primary proofs. This ruling is based on the Companions' abandonment of opinion (ra'y) in favor of texts, as exemplified by the hadith of Ali. Ibn Shuraih allowed abrogation of Quran and Sunnah by opinion (qiyas); al-Anmati allowed abrogation of Quran by qiyas derived from the Quran itself. The Hanafi school rejects both views because qiyas is weaker than text.
The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafis never accept naskh by qiyas; some Shafi'is do in specific cases.

According to the majority, Ijma also cannot abrogate the Book or Sunnah. Fakhr al-Islam (Hanafi) suggested that Ijma can be abrogated by a later Ijma if the underlying maslaha changes, but this is not universally accepted.
This exception shows that Ijma, being based on consensus, may change, but qiyas never can.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Qiyas and ijma are based on opinion, so they cannot overturn a text; only a text can abrogate a text.

This lesson introduces a foundational rule of naskh in Hanafi usul: only textual proofs (Quran and Sunnah) can abrogate, not consensus or analogy. We will explore the reasoning behind this rule and a subtle exception regarding ijma abrogating ijma when the underlying benefit changes.

Guided Path

Identify the Rule
State clearly that qiyas and ijma are not valid as nasikh (abrogators) for any of the proofs (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas).

The passage gives the reason: qiyas and ijma are based on the gathering of opinions (ijtihad/ra'y), and the termination of a ruling's goodness (i.e., the time when a ruling's benefit ends) cannot be known through opinion alone.

The exception is limited: it only allows a later ijma to replace an earlier ijma when the benefit changes. It does not allow qiyas or ijma to abrogate Quran or Sunnah.
This preserves the principle that only texts can abrogate texts.

Why Ijma' Cannot Abrogate the Qur'an
Refutation of Mu'tazilah claim that ijma' can abrogate Qur'an

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school rejects abrogation of the Qur'an by ijma' and distinguish the two Hanafi responses to the Mu'tazilah claim.

Core Idea
Naskh requires revelation; ijma' cannot abrogate the Qur'an.

Some theologians have argued that if the entire community of scholars agrees on a ruling, that consensus can override a Quranic text. The Hanafi school firmly rejects this, because abrogation (naskh) is a legislative act that only revelation can perform. This lesson examines the famous example of the mu'allafat qulubuhum (those whose hearts are to be reconciled) and how the Hanafi response defends the principle that ijma' is not a source of naskh.

Guided Path
1
State the Mu'tazilah claim
The Mu'tazilah argue that ijma' can abrogate the Qur'an, citing the drop of mu'allafat qulubuhum from zakat recipients during Abu Bakr's caliphate.
This is a dalil for their position.
2
Identify the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response (qulna) offers two explanations: either the ruling terminated due to the end of its cause, or it was abrogated by a hadith that was later forgotten.
Both explanations avoid concluding that ijma' abrogated the Qur'an.
3
Analyze the first response
The ruling for mu'allafat was tied to a cause (ta'lif al-qulub). When the cause ended, the ruling ended naturally. This is not naskh but intiha' al-hukm.
This relies on the usul principle that ahkam follow 'illa.
4

Core Idea
Naskh is clarification of a ruling's duration, not contradiction; thus cross-type abrogation is valid.

The concept of naskh (abrogation) is essential for understanding how divine rulings evolve. Commentary
This passage presents a foundational usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the permissibility of cross-abrogation between the Qur'an and the Sunna. The author, a Hanafi commentator, first states the Hanafi position that naskh can occur in four forms: Qur'an by Qur'an, Sunna by Sunna, Qur'an by Sunna, and Sunna by Qur'an. Imam Shafi'i accepts only the first two. The passage then details Shafi'i's arguments: first, a rational argument that cross-abrogation would allow critics to accuse the Prophet of inconsistency with Allah's commands; second, hadith evidence commanding that any reported hadith must be compared with the Qur'an, implying that Qur'an is superior and cannot be abrogated by Sunna; third, the Qur'anic verse commanding the Prophet to explain what is revealed, which would be undermined if his own speech could abrogate what he was sent to explain. The Hanafi response refutes the rational argument by noting that similar disparagement could occur in intra-revelation abrogation (e.g., Qur'an by Qur'an) and that such criticisms come from ignorant people and hold no weight. The hadith is interpreted as a test for authenticity (as opposed to abrogation), and the verse about explanation is reconciled by defining naskh as clarification of duration, not contradiction. Supportive examples are given for each permissible form: Qur'an by Qur'an (verses of pardon abrogated by fighting verses), Sunna by Sunna (visiting graves), Sunna by Qur'an (change of qibla from Jerusalem to Mecca), and Qur'an by Sunna (prohibition of marrying more than nine wives abrogated by the Prophet's permission). The author concludes that many supposed cases of Qur'an abrogated by Sunna are actually Qur'an abrogating Qur'an when examined closely, as shown in his Tafsir al-Ahmadi.

The Three Categories of Mansukh (Abrogation)
Categorization of mansukh into three types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three types of mansukh (abrogation) in the Qur'an and explain the significance of each type for usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
A Qur'anic text may be abrogated in three ways: in both text and ruling, in ruling alone, or in text alone.

This passage presents a foundational categorization in the study of naskh: the types of mansukh (abrogated texts). Understanding these categories is crucial for anyone who works with the Qur'an, as it determines which verses are still operative and which have been superseded. The author distinguishes three types of abrogation, supporting each with examples from the Qur'an and from the practice of the Companions.

Guided Path
1

Read the passage and list the three types of mansukh as presented by the author: (1) abrogation of both recitation and ruling, (2) abrogation of ruling only, (3) abrogation of recitation only.
2
Examine the examples for each type
For each type, find the specific example given. For type 1: Surah al-Ahzab and Surah al-Talaq reduced in length. For type 2: verses of tolerance abrogated by verses of fighting. For type 3: the stoning verse and Ibn Mas'ud's reading.
Note that type 3 includes change of wording or description.
3
Understand the implications for tafsir and fiqh
Reflect on why this categorization matters: a verse may remain in the mus-haf but its hukm is no longer binding (type 2), or a hukm may remain operative even though the verse is no longer recited (type 3). This affects how scholars derive rulings.
Knowledge of mansukh is obligatory for mujtahids.

Commentary
This passage categorizes the abrogated (mansukh) into three types. First: abrogation of both the text (tilawah) and its ruling (hukm), like portions of Surah al-Ahzab which were revealed and later removed from the Qur'an entirely. Second: abrogation of the ruling only, while the text remains (e.g., verses calling for peace and tolerance abrogated by jihad verses). Third: abrogation of the text only, while the ruling remains (e.g., the stoning verse for married adulterers, which was abrogated from recitation but the ruling remains in effect). The author also mentions that the knowledge of mansukh is obligatory for those who work with the Qur'an. He notes the numbers: 70 or 120 verses of non-fighting abrogated, and about 20-40 verses abrogated in tilawah alone. He claims his detailed explanation in Tafsir al-Ahmadi surpasses even the books of Abu Hanifa and the Shafi'is in length.

حكم (hukm): Ruling: the legal prescription derived from the text.
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation: the replacement of a legal ruling by a later one, and/or removal of a text from recitation.

Types of Mansukh (Abrogated Verses)
Classifying the three types of abrogation in the Quran

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the three types of abrogated Quranic verses (mansukh) and apply the Hanafi position on abrogation by addition (ziyadah).

Core Idea
Abrogation can affect recitation, ruling, or both — knowing the type determines which evidence is binding.

After explaining the abrogator (nasikh), the author now categorizes the abrogated (mansukh) into three clear types. He also introduces a fourth subtle type — the abrogation of a description within a ruling — which leads to a key Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute over whether adding an exception to a Quranic command is abrogation or specification. Understanding these types is essential for correctly applying the Quran's rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three main types
Read the author's list: first (both recitation and ruling), second (ruling only), third (recitation only). Note the Quranic examples for each.
The fourth sub-type (abrogation of description) is treated under the third type but is distinct.
2
Understand the first type (both abrogated)
Verses that were originally part of the Quran but were abrogated in both wording and legal effect, like the longer versions of Surah al-Ahzab and at-Talaq.
This occurred through insa' (causing to forget) during the Prophet's lifetime.
3
Understand the second type (ruling abrogated, recitation remains)
Verses like 'For you is your religion...' that were abrogated by later verses of fighting (ayat al-qital). Their wording persists but the ruling is replaced.
The author notes varying counts of such verses; knowledge is obligatory (fard) for Quranic exegesis.
4
Understand the third type (recitation abrogated, ruling remains)
Examples include the stoning verse for elderly adulterers and additional wording in some qira'at (e.g., 'consecutive' for fasting). The ruling still applies though the text is not in the Mushaf.
This demonstrates that ijma' and sunnah can preserve a ruling even after its recitation is removed.
5
Analyze the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on ziyadah
The author treats the addition of wiping over socks as an abrogation of the absolute command to wash feet. The Hanafi school calls this naskh; the Shafi'i school calls it takhsis. The Hanafi requirement of mutawatir/mashhur evidence for such changes is tied to this classification.
The difference leads to practical consequences: Hanafi accepts wiping only with stronger evidence; Shafi'i allows it even with ahad hadith.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the types of abrogated (mansukh) verses in the Quran. The author, having previously discussed the abrogator (nasikh), now turns to the abrogated. He divides mansukh into three types: 1) Both recitation (tilawah) and ruling (hukm) are abrogated. This occurred during the Prophet's lifetime when certain verses were caused to be forgotten (insa'). Examples include Surah al-Ahzab and Surah at-Talaq, which were originally much longer but were abrogated in recitation, leaving only the current shorter versions. 2) Only the ruling is abrogated while the recitation remains. This includes verses about peaceful coexistence with disbelievers, such as 'For you is your religion...' which are abrogated by the later verses of fighting (ayat al-qital). The author notes differing opinions on the number of such verses (70, 120, etc.) and mentions his own detailed work in Tafsir al-Ahmadi. He emphasizes that knowing this distinction is obligatory (fard) for one who acts by the Quran to ensure they follow the abrogator and not the abrogated. 3) Only the recitation is abrogated but the ruling remains. Examples include the stoning verse for elderly adulterers, which is not found in the Mushaf but its ruling is preserved, and additional wording in Ibn Mas'ud's recitation (like 'consecutive' for fasting, or 'right hands' for cutting). Additionally, the author discusses a fourth type: abrogation of a description (wasf) within a ruling, where the generality or absoluteness is abrogated but the core ruling remains. An example is the obligation to wash feet in wudu being abrogated by the allowance to wipe over socks, per the hadith. He notes that according to the Hanafi school this is considered naskh (abrogation), whereas al-Shafi'i considers it takhsis (specification) or bayan (clarification). The author stresses that such abrogation by a solitary hadith is not valid in the Hanafi school; only mutawatir or mashhur reports can abrogate the Quran.

Key Terms
النَّسْخُ (an-naskh): Abrogation of a legal ruling or its recitation by a later divine text.
الْمَنْسُوخُ (al-mansūkh): The abrogated part of the Quran, whether in recitation, ruling, or both.
التِّلَاوَةُ (at-tilāwah): The recitation or wording of a Quranic verse.
الْحُكْمُ (al-ḥukm): The legal ruling derived from a verse.
الزِّيَادَةُ عَلَى النَّصِّ (az-ziyādatu 'alā an-naṣṣ): Addition to the text; a word or requirement not present in the Quran that changes a Quranic ruling.

The Four Types of Naskh in the Quran
Classification of naskh and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify each of the four types of naskh with clear Quranic examples.

Core Idea
Naskh can affect both recitation and ruling, only ruling, only recitation, or just the generality of a ruling.

Understanding the types of naskh is fundamental for distinguishing operative Quranic verses from abrogated ones. This lesson, drawn from a classical Hanafi commentary, presents four categories of abrogation, each with specific examples and implications for legal practice.

Apart from verses of jihad, according to the opinion of the author of al-Itqan, approximately [number] verses are abrogated in recitation, and in my view (the view of the author of Nur al-Anwar) they exceed that number and reach about forty or more. Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition on the types of naskh (abrogation) as discussed in Hanafi usul, specifically from the commentary on Nur al-Anwar. The author identifies four types: (1) naskh of both recitation and ruling, giving examples from Surah al-Ahzab and Surah al-Talaq which were longer but later abrogated in both text and ruling. (2) naskh of ruling only, where the verse remains in the mushaf but its legal effect is abrogated, as with verses of patience before jihad. (3) naskh of recitation only, where the wording is abrogated but the ruling remains, such as the verse of stoning for the married adulterer and the addition of 'consecutive' in Ibn Mas'ud's recitation. (4) naskh of the description (wasf) of the ruling, meaning the generality or condition is abrogated while the core ruling remains. The author specifically discusses ziyadah `alal-nass (addition to the text) as an example of this fourth type, using the case of wiping over khuffs as an addition that restricts the general command to wash the feet. He then contrasts the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions: Hanafis consider such an addition as naskh, requiring evidence from mutawatir or mashhur hadith, while Shafi'is consider it as takhsis (specification) that can be established by khabar wahid or qiyas. The author explains that the division into these four types applies only to the Quran, because the Quran has both recitation and ruling, and the ruling is attached to its meaning. The Sunnah, by contrast, does not have the same attachment to wording, so this division does not apply to it.

Key Terms
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a legal ruling by a later revelation; can affect recitation, ruling, or both.
زيادة على النص (ziyadah `alal-nass): An addition to the Quranic text that restricts its generality or adds a condition.
خبر واحد (khabar wahid): A hadith narrated by one or a few narrators that does not reach the level of tawatur.
مشہور (mashhur): A hadith narrated by at least three narrators in each generation but not reaching tawatur.
تخصيص (takhsis): Specification of a general text to a subset of its instances.

The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute on Ziyadah 'Alal-Nass
Comparative analysis of two usul positions on text additions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on ziyadah `alal-nass and articulate the key arguments and practical consequence against the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
Hanafis treat addition to Quranic text as naskh requiring strong evidence; Shafi'is treat it as takhsis allowing weaker evidence.

One of the major usul disputes between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools concerns whether an addition to the Quranic text constitutes abrogation (naskh) or specification (takhsis). This lesson examines the arguments on both sides, focusing on the example of wiping over khuffs and the addition of exile to flogging.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Ziyadah `alal-nass is naskh of the generality, requiring evidence of mutawatir or mashhur.
2
State the Shafi'i position
It is takhsis (specification) and can be established by any valid evidence including khabar wahid and qiyas.
3
Examine the Hanafi proof
The addition changes the ruling, so it is abrogation, not mere bayan. The example of wiping over khuffs shows a change from obligatory washing to conditional wiping.
4
Examine the Shafi'i proof
The addition clarifies and specifies the original, like other forms of bayan. They cite the hadith of adding exile to flogging as a precedent.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
Hanafis require mutawatir/mashhur for additions; Shafi'is accept khabar wahid and qiyas. Affects rulings like exile for fornication and adding condition of faith in expiations.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition on the types of naskh (abrogation) as discussed in Hanafi usul, specifically from the commentary on Nur al-Anwar. The author identifies four types: (1) naskh of both recitation and ruling, giving examples from Surah al-Ahzab and Surah al-Talaq which were longer but later abrogated in both text and ruling. (2) naskh of ruling only, where the verse remains in the mushaf but its legal effect is abrogated, as with verses of patience before jihad. (3) naskh of recitation only, where the wording is abrogated but the ruling remains, such as the verse of stoning for the married adulterer and the addition of 'consecutive' in Ibn Mas'ud's recitation. (4) naskh of the description (wasf) of the ruling, meaning the generality or condition is abrogated while the core ruling remains. The author specifically discusses ziyadah `alal-nass (addition to the text) as an example of this fourth type, using the case of wiping over khuffs as an addition that restricts the general command to wash the feet. He then contrasts the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions: Hanafis consider such an addition as naskh, requiring evidence from mutawatir or mashhur hadith, while Shafi'is consider it as takhsis (specification) that can be established by khabar wahid or qiyas. The author explains that the division into these four types applies only to the Quran, because the Quran has both recitation and ruling, and the ruling is attached to its meaning. The Sunnah, by contrast, does not have the same attachment to wording, so this division does not apply to it.

Key Terms
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a legal ruling by a later revelation; can affect recitation, ruling, or both.
زيادة على النص (ziyadah `alal-nass): An addition to the Quranic text that restricts its generality or adds a condition.
خبر واحد (khabar wahid): A hadith narrated by one or a few narrators that does not reach the level of tawatur.
مشہور (mashhur): A hadith narrated by at least three narrators in each generation but not reaching tawatur.
تخصيص (takhsis): Specification of a general text to a subset of its instances.

Increasing the Quranic Text via Khabar Wahid and Qiyas: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Explains the Hanafi vs Shafi'i dispute on allowing increase upon Quranic text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on ziyada ala al-nass via khabar wahid and qiyas, and identify the key examples and their thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
For Hanafis, increasing the Quran requires certainty; solitary reports or analogies cannot suffice.

The Quran established flogging for unmarried fornicators, but a hadith adds exile. Does this increase upon the Quran? Similarly, can we add the condition of faith to expiations by analogy? This passage presents the core usul dispute between the Hanafis and Shafi'is on whether khabar wahid and qiyas can increase the text of the Quran. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies the different epistemic thresholds each school demands when adding to divine revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested issue
The dispute is about ziyada ala al-nass: adding a new ruling or condition to the Quranic text via khabar wahid (solitary report) or qiyas (analogy). Does such an increase amount to naskh (abrogation) or bayan (explanation)?
This is an usul dispute affecting many furu' rulings.
2
State the Hanafi position
Hanafis do not allow increase upon the Quran through khabar wahid or qiyas. They require mutawatir or mashhur hadith because adding to the Quran demands qat'i proof. They consider such an increase as a form of naskh, which must be established with certainty.
Hanafis define naskh as the abrogation of a ruling, not mere explanation.
3
State the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i allows increase upon the Quran via khabar wahid and qiyas, regarding it as a valid form of bayan (elaboration) rather than naskh. The hadith 'al-bikr bi al-bikr jald mi'ah wa taghrib 'am' is cited as an example: it adds exile to the Quranic flogging.
Shafi'is consider this hadith as khabar wahid and treat it as a permissible increase.
4
Analyze the first example (ziyada in hadd)
The Quran (24:2) prescribes flogging for fornicators. The hadith adds exile. Shafi'is accept this as a valid increase; Hanafis argue that the hadith is actually mashhur (well-known) and thus acceptable even in their view, but the principle of rejecting khabar wahid for increase remains.
The thamarah al-khilaf: Shafi'is enforce exile; Hanafis do not.
5
Analyze the second example (qiyas in kaffarat)
The Quran in oath (5:89) and dhihar (58:3-4) says 'free a slave' without qualification. Shafi'is analogize to expiation for killing (4:92) which requires 'a believing slave,' thereby adding the condition of iman. Hanafis reject this analogy because it would increase the text of the Quran via qiyas, which they do not accept.
Hanafis argue that the ‘illah for the killing expiation is specific and cannot be extended.

Difference in Abrogation Between Quran and Sunnah
Explaining that the Quran allows independent abrogation of wording and ruling, while the Sunnah does not.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the types of abrogation applicable to the Quran and the Sunnah according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
In the Quran, wording and ruling can be abrogated independently; in the Sunnah, only the ruling is abrogated, not the wording.

This lesson explores a foundational distinction in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the difference between abrogation (naskh) in the Quran and the Sunnah. The Quran's wording is tied to recitation in prayer, while its ruling is tied to action. The Sunnah's ruling is not attached to the specific wording of a hadith. This difference allows for partial abrogation in the Quran but not in the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the attachments of wording and meaning in the Quran
The passage states that the Quran's wording (lafz) is tied to recitation and the ruling of prayer, while its meaning (ma'na) is tied to the obligation of action. So they have separate legal connections.
This is the basis for independent abrogation.
2
Recognize the possibilities of abrogation in the Quran
Because of separate attachments, it is possible that only the wording is abrogated (removed from recitation) while the ruling remains, or only the ruling is abrogated while wording remains, or both are abrogated.
Example: Verse of bequest (2:180) wording remains, ruling abrogated.
3
Contrast with the Sunnah
The passage explains that in the Sunnah, the ruling is not attached to the wording of the hadith. Therefore, the partial abrogation scenario does not apply. Instead, abrogation in Sunnah is by change of ruling via a later report.
The wording of a hadith is not recited in prayer, so its recitation status is irrelevant.
4
Understand the practical implication for usul
This principle affects how we handle abrogated Quranic verses (they may still be recited) and abrogated hadith (the wording remains as historical report but the ruling is no longer operative).
Also affects the concept of ziyada 'ala al-nass (increase over a text) which applies to Sunnah but differently.

Binding Nature of Previous Shariahs
Explaining when laws of previous prophets are binding on Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that previous shariahs (sharā'i' man qablanā) are binding only when narrated without disapproval, and distinguish binding from non-binding cases using Quranic examples.

Core Idea
A law of a previous prophet becomes our law only when Allah or His Messenger narrate it without disapproval.

We have learned about the Sunnah as a source of law. But what about the laws of prophets before Muhammad (peace be upon him)? The Hanafi school has a specific criterion: those laws become binding on Muslims only when they are mentioned in the Quran or Sunnah without any note of disapproval (nakīr). This passage from Ashraf al-Anwār explains the ikhtilaf, states the preferred Hanafi view, and provides clear examples from the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue and the two opposing views
Some scholars hold that previous shariahs are absolutely binding on us, while others deny any binding force. Recognize that the issue is whether they are a source of law.
The extreme positions are mentioned to clarify the middle ground.
2
State the Hanafi rule
The chosen view: previous shariahs bind us only when Allah and His Messenger narrate them without disapproval (بلا إنكار). Memorize this formulation.
This is attributed to Imam Abu Hanifah and is a great foundational principle.
3
Analyze the evidence: the Quranic examples
The passage gives examples of binding rulings (qiṣāṣ, water division by rotation, prohibition of sodomy) and non-binding ones (prohibitions on Jews for their transgression). For each, note the presence or absence of nakīr.
Binding examples are narrated without criticism; non-binding ones are mentioned as punishments.
4
Derive the underlying principle
When a previous ruling is narrated in our Book without disapproval, it becomes part of our religion. The verse 'Follow their guidance' (Quran 6:90) supports following the righteous predecessors, but only those rulings that are confirmed without nakīr.
The ruling is binding as a part of our shariah, not as a law of the previous prophet per se.

The Hanafi Rule on Laws of Previous Prophets (Shara'i' Man Qablana)
Explaining that previous prophetic laws bind us only when adopted in our Sharia.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on shara'i' man qablana and apply it to determine whether a previous prophetic law is binding on Muslims.

Core Idea
Previous prophetic laws bind us only when adopted in our Sharia — not as independent sources.

The Qur'an contains many references to laws that were revealed to earlier prophets. A common question is whether these laws automatically apply to Muslims. The Hanafi school provides a clear principle: such laws are binding only when they are confirmed in the Qur'an or Sunnah as part of our own Sharia. This lesson unpacks that rule using two Quranic examples of prohibitions that were specific to the Jews and not extended to the Muslim community.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Issue
Recognize that laws from previous prophets (shara'i' man qablana) are a potential source of Islamic law. The debate is whether they bind Muslims automatically or only under certain conditions.
This is a distinctively usul al-fiqh question, not a direct fiqh ruling.
2
Examine the Quranic Examples
Read the two verses cited: the prohibition of certain foods on the Jews as a punishment. Notice the cause (zulm) and the specific addressees (Jews). This shows the ruling was context-bound, not general.
3
Identify the Hanafi Principle
The author states the rule: 'They are binding upon us only on the basis that they are a law for our Messenger.' That is, previous laws become part of our Sharia only if they are explicitly or implicitly adopted by our Prophet or the Qur'an.
The key is the adoption by our Sharia, not the origin.
4
Apply the Principle
Use the rule to distinguish between cases: if a law is mentioned in the Qur'an without abrogation and not tied to a specific historical punishment, it is binding. If it is clearly a punishment for a previous community, it is not binding on us.
This principle prevents cherry-picking from earlier scriptures.

Inviolabilities Not Removed by Coercion
Rule statement with rationale and exceptions (usul rule)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that certain prohibitions are not removed by coercion and apply it to the examples of zina (distinguishing between male and female) and killing a Muslim.

Core Idea
Some prohibitions are absolute: a man coerced into zina remains sinful, but a woman is excused; killing a Muslim is never permitted even under threat.

Coercion (ikrah) often lifts liability in Islamic law, but not all prohibitions yield. This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar clarifies the Hanafi principle that certain inviolabilities—particularly those involving lineage, life, and limbs—remain binding even under duress. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the maxim of necessity (darurah) and avoiding a simplistic view that coercion always excuses.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Rule
Read the opening sentence: 'wa al-hurumatu anwa'... It states that some inviolabilities are not removed and no concession (rukhsah) enters them. Identify this as the foundational rule.
The term 'hurmat' here means a legal prohibition that is inherently inviolable.
2
Analyze the Example of Zina
The passage gives zina with a woman as the first example. Note that it distinguishes between a man and a woman under coercion. The man is never excused because of the corruption of lineage (fasād al-firāsh) and the child being lost. The woman is granted a concession because the child is still traced to her, so no 'killing of the lineage' occurs.
This distinction is specific to the Hanafi school and is based on the different effects on lineage.
3
Examine the Example of Killing a Muslim
The second example is murder: killing a Muslim is absolutely prohibited, even if the killer was coerced on pain of death. The rationale is that one's own life does not justify another's; coercion is treated as non-existent.
This also applies to severing a limb: life and limb are inviolable together.
4
Trace the Underlying Rationale (Illah)
The passage uses talil: zina corrupts lineage and destroys parentage; killing takes life. These are rights of Allah (ḥuqūq Allāh) and rights of humans (ḥuqūq al-ʿibād) that are too weighty to be overridden by personal duress.
Contrast with lesser prohibitions that can be lifted under necessity (e.g., eating carrion).
