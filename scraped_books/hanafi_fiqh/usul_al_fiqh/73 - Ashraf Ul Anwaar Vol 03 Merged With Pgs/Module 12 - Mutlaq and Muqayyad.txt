Mutlaq and Muqayyad in Separate Rulings
Applying absolute and restricted reports in distinct legal contexts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a mutlaq text is not restricted by a muqayyad text when the rulings are separate, and derive the corresponding fiqh ruling for a new scenario.

Core Idea
In separate rulings, the absolute stands free — both texts applied independently.

This lesson explains a key Hanafi usul principle: when a narrator reports a hadith in two versions — one absolute (mutlaq) and one restricted (muqayyad) — and the rulings are distinct, the absolute is not restricted by the restricted. Instead, both are applied in their respective contexts. We will explore this through the example of selling goods before possession, a classic case that illustrates how the school derives rulings from multiple narrations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two reports
Find the mutlaq and muqayyad versions of the hadith in the source text. One restricts the prohibition to food (muqayyad), the other is general (mutlaq).
The muqayyad is about food; the mutlaq is about 'what is not possessed'.
2
Determine if rulings are separate
Check whether the two reports address the same legal context or different ones. Here, one ruling is about food, the other about all goods — they are separate contexts.
The text states 'fi hukmayn' (in two rulings).
3
Apply the Hanafi rule
Since the rulings are separate, do not restrict the mutlaq with the muqayyad. Instead, apply each independently: the muqayyad applies to food, the mutlaq applies to all goods.
This is the madhhab position: 'al-mutlaq la yu'malu 'ala al-muqayyad fi hukmayn'.
4
Derive the fiqh ruling
From the mutlaq (general prohibition), deduce that selling any commodity before possession is impermissible. From the muqayyad, food is also covered. Thus, the Hanafi school rules that selling goods before possession is not allowed.
The text concludes: 'la yajuzu bay' al-'urud qabl al-qabd kama la yajuzu bay' al-ta'am qablahu'.
5
Note the significance for usul
This principle shows how conflicting narrations are reconciled by treating them as separate reports when the contexts differ, a distinctive Hanafi methodology.

Commentary
This passage addresses the scenario where a narrator reports a hadith in two different forms, one absolute (mutlaq) and one restricted (muqayyad) with a specific condition. According to the Hanafi school, when the rulings are separate (different legal contexts), the absolute is not restricted by the restricted; instead, both are applied independently. The example given: one hadith prohibits selling food before possession (restricted to food), while another prohibits selling anything not yet possessed (absolute, general). Since these are separate rulings (one about food, one general), the general prohibition applies to all goods, and the specific applies to food. Therefore, the Hanafi school holds that selling any commodity before possession is impermissible, just as selling food before possession is. The passage concludes by noting that the author (musannif) has finished discussing the common conflict between Quran and Sunnah and now begins to investigate the common bayan (clarification) – a transition to the next topic.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): An unrestricted text that applies without qualification
مقيد (muqayyad): A text that includes a qualifying condition or restriction
راو (rawi): Narrator of a hadith

Bayan al-Darurah: Clarification Through Silence
Definition and examples of bayan al-darurah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define bayan al-darurah, identify its two forms, and apply the conditions for Companion silence to a given scenario.

Core Idea
Silence of the Lawgiver or a Companion, under conditions, serves as a valid clarification (bayan) establishing permissibility.

In Hanafi usul, not all rulings are derived from explicit speech. Sometimes the absence of objection conveys permission. This lesson explores 'bayan al-darurah'—clarification obtained through silence—and its legal weight.

Guided Path
1
Define the concept
Understand that bayan al-darurah is clarification through silence, not originally intended for bayan, but needed for deriving rulings.
Silence is not speech; it becomes bayan by necessity.
2
Identify the two modes
First, silence that is in the ruling of spoken words (like implied shares in inheritance). Second, silence based on contextual indication (dalalat al-hal), e.g., Prophet's silence on transactions.
The Quranic verse 'for his mother is the third' implies the father's remainder.
3
Analyze the Prophet's silence
When the Prophet saw practices like mudarabah and remained silent, his silence indicated permissibility.
His silence is conditional on his being aware and able to speak.
4
Analyze a Companion's silence
Companion's silence is valid only if he can forbid and the doer is Muslim. Study the case of Umar and the slave girl.
Umar's silence on compensation for usufruct was consensus.
5
Apply the conditions
In a new scenario, check: is the Companion present and able to forbid? Is the doer Muslim? If yes, silence indicates permissibility.
If the Companion is unable to forbid, silence has no legal effect.

Or clarification by necessity (bayan al-darurah) is connected to his statement 'clarification of tafsir', meaning the clarification obtained through the method of necessity. It is a type of clarification that occurs through something not originally placed for it, i.e., silence. Because what is placed for clarification is speech, not silence. And it is either in the ruling of the spoken (mantuq) or it is in the ruling of the spoken, like the Qur'anic verse 'And his parents inherit from him, so for his mother is the third' – the beginning of the speech imposed an absolute share for both parents without specifying each's portion, then specifying the mother with the third became a clarification because the father gets the remainder; it was as if He said 'so for his mother is the third and for his father the remainder'. Or it is established by the contextual indication of the speaker (dalalat hal al-mutakallim) – that the state of the silent one, speaking through the language of condition (bilan al-hal) or the language of statement (bilan al-maqal), like the silence of the Lawgiver (the Prophet) when he sees an action they are performing and dealing with, such as mudarabat and partnerships, or sees something being sold in the market and does not forbid it; then it is known that it is permissible. So his silence stands in the place of a command of permissibility. Likewise, the silence of a Companion, under conditions of ability to forbid and the doer being Muslim, as narrated: a slave girl ran away and married a man and gave birth, then her master came and raised this case to Umar, who ruled for the master and ordered the father to pay for the children, taking them at a price, and was silent about compensation for the usufruct and benefits of her children. And that was in the presence of the Companions, so it was a consensus that the benefits of the child of a deceived party are not compensable for destruction. The Urdu commentary says: 'Or bayan al-darurah' – meaning clarification that is obtained by necessity. It is connected to 'bayan al-taghyir' earlier. This refers to a specific type of clarification that is obtained from something not originally placed for clarification, and that is silence, because speech is placed for clarification, not silence.

Commentary
This passage defines a type of clarification in Hanafi usul called 'bayan al-darurah' (clarification by necessity). It occurs through silence (sukun) of the Lawgiver (Prophet) or a Companion, which is not originally a means of clarification but becomes one by necessity. The text distinguishes two forms: first, when silence is in the ruling of spoken words, illustrated by the Quranic inheritance verse where specifying the mother's third implies the father's remainder, even though the father's share is not explicitly stated. Second, when the contextual situation (dalalat al-hal) indicates the meaning, as when the Prophet saw people engaging in transactions like mudarabah and partnerships and remained silent, thereby indicating permissibility. The silence of a Companion also carries the same weight under conditions: the Companion must have the power to forbid, and the doer must be Muslim. The example given is the case of a runaway slave girl who married and had children; Caliph Umar ruled that the master could take the children upon paying a price but was silent about compensation for the usufruct during the period of deception. The Companions' presence and silence constituted consensus (ijma) that the benefits of such a child are not subject to compensation. The Urdu commentary reinforces that this type of clarification is obtained through something not originally intended for bayan, i.e., silence.

Key Terms
بيان الضرورة (bayan al-ḍarūrah): Clarification obtained through necessity, specifically through silence of the Lawgiver or a Companion, which is not originally a tool of clarification but becomes one by necessity.
السكوت (al-sukūt): Silence of the Lawgiver or a Companion in the presence of an action, indicating approval.
دلالة الحال (dalālat al-ḥāl): Contextual indication that communicates meaning without explicit words.
