Tarjih as the Method to Resolve Mu'arada in Qiyas
Explains tarjih as the way out of mu'arada with rules and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for valid tarjih in Hanafi usul and distinguish tarjih by wasf from other forms of preference.

Core Idea
Tarjih resolves mu'arada by inherent strength (wasf) of evidence, not by adding a third proof.

When two or more qiyas conflict (mu'arada), the Hanafi method is not to bring a third evidence but to examine each for inherent descriptive qualities (wasf). This passage defines tarjih and lays out key rules: preference is based on attributes like justice of narrator, clarity of text, or strength of illa, not on numerical abundance or external support. Understanding this separates valid legal reasoning from flawed arguments.

Guided Path
1
Understand the context of mu'arada
Recognize that mu'arada is a conflict between two qiyas (or other evidences) that seem equally strong. The Hanafi approach is to resolve through tarjih, not by importing a third proof.
Mu'arada is distinct from contradiction (ta'arud) of primary texts; here it refers to conflicting analogical arguments.
2
Define tarjih and its fundamental condition
Tarjih means giving preference to one evidence over another based on a descriptive quality (wasf) inherent in the evidence itself. The condition is that the basis of preference cannot be an independent proof; it must be an attribute of the evidence (e.g., justice of narrator, clarity of text).
This condition prevents using a third evidence to tip scales, which would create an infinite regress.
3
Apply the rule to examples
Work through the examples: (1) Testimony of a just person is preferred over a sinful person – based on justice (wasf). (2) Testimony of four is not preferred over two – because quantity is not a wasf. (3) A qiyas is not preferred over another by bringing a third supporting qiyas, hadith, or verse – only by inherent strength.
The text explicitly rejects 'third evidence' tarjih for qiyas, hadith, and Quranic verses.
4
Identify the consequence of inability to tarjih
If the responder (mujib) cannot provide a tarjih for his evidence, he becomes munqati' (disconnected), meaning his argument fails. If he does provide tarjih, the questioner (sa'il) may counter with another tarjih.
This procedural rule ensures that tarjih remains within the realm of descriptive qualities.
5
Differentiate tarjih from other forms of preference
Distinguish tarjih by wasf from preference by external factors (e.g., later revelation, consensus). The passage focuses on tarjih within qiyas, noting that istihsan, well-known hadith, and muhkam Quran have inherent strength that may outweigh weaker evidences.
This aligns with Hanafi emphasis on internal qualities of evidence.

Commentary
This passage explains tarjih as the method to resolve mu'arada in qiyas. Tarjih means giving preponderance to one evidence over another based on a descriptive quality (wasf) inherent in the evidence itself, not by introducing an independent third proof. The author gives several rules: one cannot use a third qiyas, hadith, or Quranic verse to tip the scales; rather, preference is based on inherent strength (quwwa). Examples include preferring istihsan over qiyas jali, mashhur hadith over khabar wahid, and muhkam qat'i over zanni. The analogy of multiple wounds illustrates that quantity does not outweigh quality. Finally, if the responder cannot provide tarjih, he is considered munqati' (unable to defend his position).

Key Terms
الترجيح (tarjih): Preferring one evidence over another by a descriptive quality inherent in the evidence, not by an independent proof.
منقطع (munqati'): A responder who cannot provide a tarjih to defend his evidence is considered disconnected or incapable.
وصف (wasf): A descriptive attribute of the evidence itself used as the basis for tarjih, not an independent proof.

Sabab and Its Three Types in Hanafi Usul
Defining sabab and its three types in Hanafi usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define a real cause (sabab haqiqi) and distinguish it from the second type (cause with attributed illah) and the third type (metaphorical cause) according to Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
A real cause conveys to the ruling without attributing its obligation or existence; only when an intervening illah is attributed does liability arise.

In Hanafi legal theory, matters connected to rulings are classified into causes, signs, conditions, and more. The concept of 'cause' (sabab) is further divided into three types, each with distinct legal consequences. This passage from the Ashraf al-Anwar defines these types with illustrative examples, helping you grasp when a person is truly liable and when they are merely a conduit.

Guided Path
1
Set the context of the second division
Recognize that the passage begins by listing four types of matters related to rulings: cause, sign, condition, and sign (the repetition is likely a copyist error). Our focus is the first type: cause.
The second division refers to matters with which rulings are connected.
2
Grasp the definition of real cause (sabab haqiqi)
A real cause is a means that conveys to the ruling without the obligation of the ruling being attributed to it, nor its existence. It has no direct or indirect effect on the ruling. Example: informing a thief about a person's property does not make the informer liable for the theft because the thief has free will.
The key phrase: 'without the obligation of the ruling being attributed to it, nor its existence'.
3
Understand the second type: cause with attributed intervening illah
If the intervening illah (effective cause) is attributed to the initial cause (e.g., driving an animal that tramples someone), the initial cause becomes liable for compensation. The animal has no choice, so its action is attributed to the driver. This differs from a real cause where the intervening agent has choice.
The passage uses the example of driving and leading an animal.
4
Learn the third type: metaphorical cause (sabab majazi)
Oaths (by Allah, divorce, or manumission) are called causes metaphorically because they may lead to expiation or consequence when violated, though their original purpose is righteousness. They are not real causes but are treated as such due to their potential outcome.
An oath is a cause metaphorically because it can lead to the ruling when the preventer (righteousness) is removed.
5
Synthesize the three types
Compare the three types: real cause has no attribution; second type has attribution of the intervening illah; third type is figurative. Note when liability arises: only in the second type (and sometimes in the third by extension). The informer example shows the default rule, while the slanderer example shows a later fatwa that imposes liability for societal good, illustrating how the classification can be refined.
The later scholars' fatwa on the slanderer is an example of the second type applied to new circumstances.

Then the first type, cause, has four divisions: (a) real cause: that which is a means of reaching the ruling, i.e., conveying to the ruling in some way, unlike a sign, because a sign indicates the ruling but does not convey to it, without the obligation of the ruling being attributed to it, nor its existence being attributed to it, as the obligation is attributed to the effective cause and existence is attributed to the condition. And meanings of causation are not conceived in it such that it has any effect in the existence of the ruling, neither directly nor indirectly. If it had an effect, it would not be a real cause but rather a cause that resembles a cause or one that is in the meaning of a cause. However, a cause [ʿillah] intervenes between the cause and the ruling, which is not attributed to the cause. For example, informing someone about another person or his property so that he kills him or steals his property: this information is a real cause for theft or murder because it conveys to it, but it is not the cause of its occurrence nor has any effect in the act of theft. However, between the information and the act there is a causation that is not attributed to the information, which is the thief's will and action. It is not necessary that the informed person will certainly commit the act; Allah may grant him success to avoid it. Therefore, if the act occurs, the informer is not liable because he is merely a cause, not a cause in the sense of an effective cause. Based on this, it is appropriate that the person who slanders someone before a tyrant king and thereby extracts money from him is merely a cause, but later scholars gave a fatwa that such a slanderer should pay compensation because conveying falsehood to kings causes corruption in the times and increases the audacity of slanderers, so compensation should be taken from him since it cannot be taken from the king. As for the one in iḥrām who points out game, he guarantees its value because he abandoned the trust he undertook by his iḥrām through the act of pointing out, like the depositary who points out the deposit to a thief: he is liable for abandoning the required protection. If the intervening cause between the cause and the ruling is attributed to the cause, i.e., to the cause, the cause takes the ruling of causes regarding obligation of guarantee upon him, because then the ruling is attributed to the cause, and the cause is attributed to the cause, so the cause becomes the cause of the cause. This is the second type of cause. Therein lies the benefit of avoiding calling it a cause that is not attributed to the cause, like driving and leading an animal: each of them is a cause for the destruction of what is destroyed by trampling during driving and leading, and there intervenes between it and the destruction that which is a cause for it, namely the animal's action, but it is attributed to the driver and leader because the animal has no choice in its action, especially when someone is driving or leading it, and the cause is not suitable for the ruling, so the destruction is attributed to the cause of the cause regarding compensation of the place, i.e., blood money and value. As for what relates to the penalty of direct action, it is not attributed to it, so it does not bar inheritance, nor does expiation, retaliation, or oath become obligatory. And the oath by Allah Almighty, saying 'by Allah, I will do such-and-such' or 'I will not do such-and-such', or by divorce or manumission, saying 'if you enter the house, you are divorced' or 'you are free', is called a cause metaphorically due to frequency and consequence. This is the third type of cause. It is a metaphorical cause because the oath is legislated for righteousness, and righteousness is never a way to expiation in the oath by Allah, nor to consequence in the oath by other than Allah, because it is a preventer of violation; without violation, expiation does not become obligatory nor does consequence descend. But since it may lead to the ruling when the preventer is removed, it is called a metaphorical cause in consideration of what it leads to. In any case, the muḥrim who points out game is liable for its price because he abandoned the trust he undertook during iḥrām by the act of pointing out, just as the depositary, if he points out the deposit to a thief and the thief steals it, the depositary is liable for abandoning the protection he undertook while keeping the deposit.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of causes (asbaab) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author explains that the second division of matters related to rulings includes four types: cause, sign, condition, and another sign (though the text repeats 'sign', likely a copyist error). He then focuses on cause, dividing it into four. The first is the real cause (sabab haqiqi), which is a means that conveys to the ruling without the obligation of the ruling being attributed to it and without its existence being attributed to it. This distinguishes it from an effective cause (illah) where the ruling is attributed, and from a condition where existence is attributed. The real cause has no direct or indirect effect on the ruling; if it had an effect, it would become a cause resembling an illah. The author gives the example of informing someone about a person or property leading to murder or theft: the information is a real cause because it conveys to the act, but the obligation of the act is not attributed to the informer, nor is the existence of the act—the informer does not control the thief's choice. Therefore, the informer is not liable in the primary view. However, later scholars (mutakkhirin) ruled that slanderers who convey falsehoods to tyrants must pay compensation to prevent societal corruption. The author then discusses the second type of cause, where the intervening illah (the thief's action) is attributed to the cause (the informer) because the animal or thief has no choice, as in driving or leading an animal. In such cases, the cause becomes a 'cause of the cause' and the person is liable for compensation (diyah or value) but not for penalties like inheritance bar, expiation, retaliation, or oath. Finally, the third type is the metaphorical cause (sabab majazi), such as oaths. An oath is meant for righteousness but may lead to expiation or consequence when violated; since it potentially leads to the ruling when the preventer (righteousness) is removed, it is called a cause metaphorically.

Key Terms
سبب (sabab): A means that conveys to the ruling without the obligation or existence of the ruling being attributed to it.
علت (illah): The effective cause to which the obligation of the ruling is attributed.
علامت (ʿalāmat): A sign that indicates the ruling but does not convey to it.

Categories of Obstacles to Legal Capacity: Samawi and Muktasab
Listing and categorizing obstacles to legal capacity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the obstacles to legal capacity as samawi (heavenly) or muktasab (acquired) and recite the complete list of each category as found in the Hanafi usul tradition.

Core Idea
Legal capacity is hindered by eighteen obstacles: eleven beyond control (samawi) and seven from human choice (muktasab).

In Hanafi usul, legal capacity (ahliyyah) is not absolute; it is subject to obstacles that either remove or diminish it. The classical texts divide these obstacles into two broad categories: those that afflict a person without their choice (samawi) and those that result from human choice (muktasab). This lesson provides the complete list from the Ashraf al-Anwaar, serving as a foundational memorization anchor for understanding the limits of legal capacity.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the Two Categories
Identify the defining difference: samawi obstacles are beyond human control (e.g., sleep, death), while muktasab obstacles arise from human agency (e.g., ignorance, coercion).
This distinction determines whether the obstacle is an excuse or a fault in legal accountability.
2
Memorize the Samawi List
Commit the eleven heavenly obstacles to memory: childhood, insanity, imbecility, forgetfulness, sleep, blindness, slavery, illness, menstruation, postpartum bleeding, and death.
Use a mnemonic device if helpful; e.g., 'CIIF SBS I M P D'.
3
Memorize the Muktasab List
Learn the seven acquired obstacles: ignorance, intoxication, [jest?], travel, foolishness, mistake, and coercion.
Note the textual variation: the term 'المزل' may be an OCR error for 'الهزل' (jest) or 'السفر' (travel). Standard lists often include both.
4
Recognize the Scope and Effect
Understand that these obstacles affect either ahliyyat wujub (capacity for obligation) or ahliyyat ada (capacity for performance). Some obstacles fully remove capacity (e.g., death, insanity), while others only diminish it (e.g., illness, travel).
