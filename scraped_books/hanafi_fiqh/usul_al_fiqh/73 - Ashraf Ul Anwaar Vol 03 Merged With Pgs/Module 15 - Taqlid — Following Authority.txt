Taqlid of a Companion: Obligatory and Abandons Qiyas
States the rule on taqlid of a Companion with reasoning and internal ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on taqlid of a Companion, explain the two rationales for its precedence over qiyas, and distinguish the majority view from al-Karkhi's qualification.

Core Idea
Following a Companion's statement is wajib and overrides qiyas due to their direct witness of revelation.

In this passage, the author establishes a foundational principle of Hanafi usul: the opinion of a Companion (Sahabi) is not just a source of guidance but carries binding authority over analogical reasoning (qiyas). This lesson unpacks why a Companion's statement takes precedence, even if it appears to be their own ijtihad, and explores an internal Hanafi disagreement about the scope of this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
Read the first sentence of the passage. State the rule in your own words: taqlid of a Companion is wajib and causes qiyas to be abandoned.
The rule applies to qiyas of later scholars, not to the opinion of another Companion.
2
Understand the first rationale
The author gives a reason: there is a possibility that the Companion heard the ruling directly from the Prophet, even if not explicitly attributed. Recognize this as a presumption of direct transmission.
This presumption makes the Companion's statement akin to a marfu' hadith.
3
Understand the second rationale
Even if we concede the statement is the Companion's own ijtihad, it is still stronger than others' qiyas because they witnessed the circumstances of revelation and understood the deeper meanings of Shari'ah.
This argument highlights the Companions' epistemological privilege.
4
Examine al-Karkhi's qualification
Al-Karkhi limits the obligation to matters that are accessible by qiyas. Explain his reasoning: in non-qiyas matters, the Companion could be stating his own opinion and might have erred.
This is an internal Hanafi ikhtilaf on the scope of taqlid of a Companion.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The passage presents the majority view first and then al-Karkhi's. Based on the structure, the majority view is the relied-upon position. Note the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) between the two views.
For example, in purely rational matters, Karkhi would not consider it obligatory to follow a Companion's opinion.

Commentary
This passage discusses the Hanafi position on taqlid of a Companion. The author states that following a Companion's statement is obligatory and takes precedence over qiyas (analogical reasoning) made by later scholars. The reason is twofold: first, a Companion's statement may be based on direct hearing from the Prophet, even if not explicitly attributed; second, even if it is their own ijtihad, their reasoning is stronger because they witnessed the revelation and understood the spirit of the Shari'ah firsthand. Therefore, their opinion overrides the qiyas of later jurists. However, Imam al-Karkhi provides a qualification: this obligation only applies in matters that are accessible by qiyas (i.e., where legal reasoning is possible). In such cases, the presumption is that the Companion heard it from the Prophet. But in matters that are purely rational or not derivable by qiyas, the Companion's statement may simply be their own opinion and could be mistaken. Thus, taqlid of a Companion is not absolute; it is most authoritative when the Companion is likely transmitting a prophetic ruling. This is an internal Hanafi ikhtilaf (disagreement) on the scope of taqlid of Companions.

Key Terms
تقليد الصحابي (taqlid al-sahabi): Following the statement (opinion or narration) of a Companion as authoritative, even if it appears to be his ijtihad.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning from a primary source to derive a legal ruling.

Taqlid al-Sahabi: The Obligation and Limits of Following a Companion's Opinion
Debate on taqlid of Companions with Hanafi, Karkhi, and Shafi'i views

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle regarding taqlid of a Companion to distinguish when it is obligatory and when qiyas may be preferred.

Core Idea
Following a Companion's opinion is wajib in matters beyond reason, but in qiyasi matters, it is preferred, not binding.

In Hanafi usul, the opinions of the Companions carry significant weight. But does a mufti always have to follow a Companion's statement over his own qiyas? This lesson unpacks the nuanced Hanafi position, contrasting it with Al-Karkhi's restriction and Al-Shafi'i's rejection. Through concrete examples from Aisha and Ibn Umar, you will learn when taqlid of a Companion is obligatory and when ijtihad may still operate.

Guided Path
1
Map the three positions
Identify the Hanafi, Karkhi, and Shafi'i positions on taqlid al-sahabi as stated in the text.
Hanafi: wajib and overrides qiyas. Karkhi: only wajib in non-qiyasi matters. Shafi'i: not obligatory at all.
2
Examine the Hanafi justification
Understand why Companions' statements are given priority: possibility of direct hearing from the Prophet, and their superior understanding of Sharia due to witnessing revelation.
Even if it's their own ijtihad, it is stronger than a later scholar's opinion.
3
Analyze the examples
See how Aisha's statements on menstruation and the sale transaction are applied to override qiyas, and how these are non-qiyasi matters.
The minimum/maximum of menstruation cannot be known by reason; the sale transaction ruling is based on Aisha's threat of nullification.
4
Recognize intra-school nuance
Notice that in qiyasi matters (like salam capital disclosure), Abu Hanifa followed Ibn Umar while Sahibayn followed qiyas, showing flexibility.
This illustrates that in qiyasi matters, taqlid is not universally wajib; scholars may differ.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi usul dispute on taqlid al-Sahabi (following a Companion's opinion). The author first states the Hanafi position: following a Companion's opinion is wajib (obligatory) and overrides qiyas (analogical reasoning). The reasoning is twofold: (1) there is a strong possibility that the Companion heard the ruling directly from the Prophet, and (2) even if it is the Companion's own ijtihad, his close association with the Prophet and knowledge of revelation gives his opinion greater weight than the opinion of a later scholar. 

Al-Karkhi qualifies this: taqlid is only wajib in matters that cannot be reached by qiyas (ghayr ma'drak bil-qiyas), because in such matters the only source can be revelation, so the Companion's statement is presumed to be based on hearing. In matters reachable by qiyas, the Companion's statement could be his own ijtihad and possibly mistaken, so it is not binding on others. 

Al-Shafi'i rejects taqlid of Companions altogether, arguing that Companions disagreed with each other and no single Companion's opinion can be taken as authoritative over others. 

The Hanafis respond by citing two examples: (1) the minimum and maximum duration of menstruation, which cannot be known by reason, so Aisha's statement is accepted by all Hanafis; (2) a transaction where a woman sold an item for less than she bought it—qiyas would allow it, but Aisha declared it invalid and threatened Zaid ibn Arqam with nullification of his deeds unless he repented, so the Hanafis ruled it forbidden following Aisha. 

In matters reachable by qiyas, Hanafi scholars differed: Abu Hanifa followed Ibn Umar's opinion requiring disclosure of capital amount in salam, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad did not require it, acting by qiyas. This shows that for qiyasi matters, taqlid of a Companion is not universally wajib; some scholars may prefer qiyas. 

Thus, the Hanafi school's stance is nuanced: taqlid of a Companion is preferred and takes precedence over qiyas, especially in non-qiyasi matters, but in qiyasi matters, scholars may differ. Al-Karkhi restricts obligation to non-qiyasi matters. Al-Shafi'i's blanket rejection is contrasted with the Hanafi practice.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion combining a fiqh ruling on the liability of a common laborer (ajir mushtarak) and the usul principle of taqlid of a Sahabi's qawl. It begins by presenting the dispute between the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) and Abu Hanifa regarding a common worker like a washerman or tailor. When the item is lost under circumstances where precaution was possible (e.g., theft), the Sahibayn hold the worker liable, basing this on the practice of Ali ibn Abi Talib, who made a tailor pay compensation for a lost garment to protect people's property. Abu Hanifa, however, considers the worker an amanah (trustee) and thus not liable, drawing analogy to a private laborer (ajir khas) who is not liable for loss. Both agree that in cases beyond control (e.g., a general fire), there is no liability. This fiqh example leads into the broader usul issue: when is a Sahabi's statement binding? The author explains the conditions: if a Sahabi's saying does not reach another Sahabi, there is disagreement on whether it must be followed. If it reaches another Sahabi and he remains silent (indicating acceptance), it becomes ijma' (consensus) and is binding. If he disagrees, it becomes a difference of opinion among mujtahids, and a muqallid may choose either, but cannot create a third view. Finally, the text discusses the Tabi'i: if his fatwa became well-known in the time of the Sahaba, it carries weight and may be obligatory to follow, as illustrated by the story of Ali and the judge Shuraih. In that story, Shuraih accepted the testimony of Ali's freedman Qanbar but not his son al-Hasan, following his own ijtihad, though Ali himself held that a son's testimony for his father is acceptable. Ali did not overrule Shuraih, ultimately giving the armor to the Jew. This shows the respect for judicial ijtihad even when the judge is a Tabi'i.

A Tabi'i's fatwa is authoritative only if known among the Sahaba and unopposed.

While a Sahabi's opinion carries weight due to their direct contact with the Prophet, a Tabi'i (Successor) is generally not followed blindly. And it is narrated from Abu Hanifa: 'I do not do taqlid of the Tabi'i, because they are men and we are men.' Because the statement of a Sahabi is accepted only due to the possibility of hearing (from the Prophet) and the correctness of their opinion by the blessing of the companionship of the Prophet (peace be upon him), and that is absent in the Tabi'i. And that is the chosen view of Shams al-A'imma. And all this is (applicable) if his fatwa appeared in the time of the Companions. But if his fatwa did not appear and he did not stand out in opinion, then he is like other imams of fatwa; his taqlid is not valid.

Commentary
This passage discusses the ruling on taqlid (following) of a Tabi'i (a successor of the Companions) in Hanafi usul. The author begins by recounting a story of Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) accepting the judgment of Qadi Shuraih against him and then gifting the armor to the Jew who embraced Islam. This story illustrates that even a Sahabi and caliph can accept the ruling of a judge, and the Jew's conversion shows the beauty of Islam. Then the author gives an example of a difference between Ibn Abbas (a Sahabi) and Masruq (a Tabi'i) regarding the expiation of a vow to slaughter one's child. Ibn Abbas analogized to blood money (100 camels), while Masruq analogized to the ransom of Isma'il (a sheep). No Sahabi rejected Masruq's opinion, so it became consensus that a sheep suffices. This shows that a Tabi'i's opinion can become accepted if the Companions remain silent. Then the author narrates the position of Imam Abu Hanifa: he does not do taqlid of a Tabi'i because they are just men like us, unlike the Companions who have special status due to hearing directly from the Prophet and the blessing of companionship. This is the chosen view of Shams al-A'imma al-Halwani. However, the author qualifies: if the Tabi'i's fatwa appeared during the time of the Companions (and they did not reject it), it carries weight; but if it did not appear or he was not prominent in opinion, then his taqlid is not valid—he is like any other jurist.

Key Terms
تابعي (tābiʿī): A Muslim who met a Sahabi but did not meet the Prophet.
تقلید (taqlīd): Following a scholar's opinion without knowing the evidence.
إجماع سكوتي (ijmaʿ sukūtī): Tacit consensus: silence of the mujtahids on a given opinion indicates approval.

The Hanafi usul allows taqlid of a Tabi'i only when his fatwa emerged in the Sahaba era and was met with their silent approval, forming a sukuti ijma.
