Proving Khabar al-Wahid Obligates Action from Quran and Sunnah
Deriving the obligating force of khabar al-wahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive and explain the Hanafi ruling that khabar al-wahid obligates action without certain knowledge, using Quranic verse 9:122, verse 3:187, and Sunnah practices.

Core Idea
A single narrator's report must be acted upon, though it gives no certainty – proven by language and prophetic example.

This lesson walks through the classical Hanafi proof that a report transmitted by one or a few narrators (khabar al-wahid) is binding for practical action (amal) even though it does not produce certain knowledge (ilm al-yaqin). The author presents two Quranic arguments and several Sunnah-based proofs. You will examine the linguistic claim that the term 'ta'ifa' includes a single person, and see how the Prophet's own practice of sending individual messengers confirms the obligating force of such reports.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling and Its Basis
The ruling to be established is that khabar al-wahid obligates action, not certain knowledge. This is stated at the outset.
The proof begins with Quran, then Sunnah.
2
Analyze the Primary Quranic Dalil (9:122)
Focus on verse 9:122: 'Why not from every group a party go forth to gain understanding...' The word 'ta'ifa' (party) is argued to include one person. The command to warn obligates the party, and the group must accept the warning – thus accepting a single person's report.
The linguistic argument is crucial: ta'ifa = one or more.
3
Address the Alternative Interpretation
The author mentions that some scholars reverse the pronouns in the verse, which would break the proof. He rejects this interpretation, citing his own Tafsir Ahmadi for details.
This shows awareness of counterarguments.
4
Confirm with the Second Quranic Dalil (3:187)
Verse 3:187: 'Allah took a covenant from those given the Scripture: you surely explain it...' Obligating explanation implies acceptance of the explainer's report, which may be a single person.
This verse reinforces the same principle.
5
Support with Sunnah Proofs
Prophetic practice: the hadith of Barira (charity vs gift), hadith of Salman (gift), and sending of Ali, Mu'adh, and Dihyah al-Kalbi as single emissaries. These show that the Prophet acted upon reports from individuals.
The author notes that these ahad reports became like mashhur due to Ummah acceptance.

Commentary
This passage is an istinbat (derivation) of the ruling that khabar al-wahid (a report transmitted by one or a few narrators) obligates action (amal) but not certain knowledge (ilm al-yaqin). The author provides two Quranic proofs (dalil) and then Sunnah proofs. The first Quranic proof is verse 9:122, where Allah commands that a party (ta'ifa) go forth to gain understanding and then warn their people. The author interprets 'ta'ifa' as meaning one or two people, thus the verse obligates the group to accept and act upon the warning of even a single person. This establishes that khabar al-wahid must be acted upon. However, the author notes an alternative interpretation where the pronouns are reversed, which would break this proof. The second Quranic proof is from the covenant verse (3:187), which obligates those with knowledge to explain it, implying that their explanation (which could be khabar al-wahid) must be accepted. Then Sunnah proofs: the hadith of Barira where the Prophet distinguished between charity and gift based on her report; the hadith of Salman regarding a gift; and the Prophet's practice of sending single individuals as judges and messengers (Ali, Mu'adh, Dihyah). These practices show that khabar al-wahid was acted upon. Finally, the author notes that even though these reports are ahad, because the Ummah accepted them, they attain the status of mashhur (well-known) and thus the argument stands.

Key Terms
خبر الواحد (khabar al-wahid): A report transmitted by one or two narrators, not reaching the level of tawatur or mashhur, which obligates action but not certain knowledge.
موجب العمل (mujib al-'amal): That which necessitates that one act upon it, though it does not produce certain knowledge.
العلم اليقين (al-'ilm al-yaqin): Certain knowledge that admits no doubt, produced only by mutawatir or mashhur reports.
طائفة (ta'ifa): A party; here it is argued to include one person, thus making the report of a single person binding.

The Hanafi Definition of Islam: Tasdiq, Iqrar, and Iman Ijmali
Defining Islam through tasdiq and iqrar, with proofs from the Sunnah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi definition of Islam as inward affirmation and verbal declaration, and apply the principle that general belief (iman ijmali) suffices for valid iman, using the proofs from the Sunnah.

Core Idea
Islam is inward affirmation and verbal declaration; a general belief in all the Prophet brought suffices.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi definition of Islam. We will see that the school defines Islam (and iman) as a combination of inner conviction (tasdiq) and verbal confession (iqrar). More importantly, the Prophet's practice shows that a general, undetailed acceptance of the entire message is sufficient. This positions the Hanafi school against those who demand detailed doctrinal knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish tasdiq from iqrar
Understand that tasdiq is inner affirmation by choice, while iqrar is the verbal declaration that brings a person under the rulings of Islam.
The passage explicitly states 'Iqrar is a condition for the application of the rulings of Islam.'
2
Examine the Prophet's acceptance of general testimony
The hadith of the bedouin and the slave girl show that the Prophet accepted a simple shahada without requiring a detailed exposition of beliefs.
In both cases, the Prophet sufficed with a general answer; he did not probe for detailed understanding.
3
Formulate the principle of iman ijmal
General belief (iman ijmali) is sufficient for valid iman; it means accepting in totality all that the Prophet brought.
The passage says: 'The Prophet sufficed with general iman.'
4
Critique the opposing view
Some scholars insisted on detailed description (tafsil). The author rejects this as an unnecessary hardship, highlighting the Hanafi commitment to ease.
The author calls the opposing view 'a great difficulty, not hidden.'

Resolving Contradiction Between Two Qur'anic Verses
Rule for resolving verse contradictions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when two Quranic verses contradict and cannot be reconciled, both are set aside (tasaqata) and the ruling is sought from the Sunnah (hadith).

Core Idea
When two Quranic verses collide, both fall; the Sunnah resolves.

In the Hanafi usul hierarchy, when two proofs of the same rank—here, two Quranic verses—conflict and cannot be harmonized, the jurist does not pick one arbitrarily. Instead, both are abandoned, and the next lower source, the Sunnah, is consulted. This prevents subjective preference and maintains the epistemological order.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict
Locate two verses that seem to impose contradictory rulings on the same act. Example: verse commanding recitation in prayer (73:20) vs verse commanding silence (7:204).
2
Attempt reconciliation
If possible, reconcile using ta'wil (interpretation) or naskh (abrogation). If reconciliation fails, proceed to step 3. In the given example, reconciliation is impossible because both verses address the same act in prayer.
3
Apply tasaqut (both fall)
Declare both verses inoperative for this ruling because they are of equal strength (qat'i al-thubut) and cannot be preferred without resorting to quantity, which is prohibited.
Qur'anic verses are all qat'i al-thubut, so neither can outweigh the other by multiplicity.
4
Resort to the Sunnah
The next source in the Hanafi hierarchy is the Sunnah (hadith). Seek a hadith that addresses the same matter. The hadith 'Man kana lahu imamun...' provides the ruling: the follower does not recite behind the imam.
5
Verify the hadith's authority
Check the hadith's thubut (authenticity) and dalala (clarity). In this case, it is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but explicit (qati al-dalala), thus sufficient to resolve the conflict.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi method for resolving apparent contradiction between legal evidences of different ranks. When two Qur'anic verses appear to contradict, they both fall (tasaqata) and the jurist must resort to the Sunnah (hadith) because it is the next source after the Qur'an. One cannot resort to a third verse to tip the scale, as that would be giving preference based on quantity rather than quality, which is impermissible. The example given is the apparent conflict between the verse commanding recitation in prayer (faqra'u ma tayassara) and the verse commanding silent listening (wa idha quri'a al-qur'anu fastami'u lahu wa ansitu). The first is general and seems to obligate the follower to recite, while the second is specific to the context of listening. They contradict in the same locus (prayer) and time, so they fall. The ruling is then sought from the hadith: 'Whoever has an imam, the recitation of the imam is recitation for him,' which settles the matter that the follower does not recite behind the imam. Between two contradictory hadiths, the resort is to the sayings of the Companions (aqwal al-sahaba) or to qiyas (analogy). Fakhr al-Islam (Imam al-Bazdawi) expressed this with 'or' to indicate that neither is inherently prioritized in his view. Other scholars held different views: some prioritize the sayings of the Companions absolutely, others prioritize qiyas absolutely, and a third position (reconciliation) distinguishes between matters that can be known by reason (ma yudraku bi al-qiyas) and those that cannot. This passage demonstrates the Hanafi commitment to structured hierarchy in resolving conflicts.

Resolving Contradiction Between Two Hadiths: Companions' Sayings or Qiyas?
Internal Hanafi ikhtilaf on resolving hadith contradictions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three Hanafi positions on prioritizing Companions' sayings or qiyas when hadiths contradict, and apply the reconciliatory view.

Core Idea
When hadiths clash, fall back to Companions or qiyas — but which comes first is debated.

When two hadiths contradict and cannot be reconciled, the Hanafi school moves to the next tier of evidence: the sayings of the Companions (aqwal al-sahaba) or qiyas (analogy). However, there is internal disagreement on which takes precedence. Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi leaves it open with 'or', while others argue for absolute priority of one over the other. A middle view distinguishes based on whether the matter is within the scope of qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting hadiths
Locate two hadiths that cannot be reconciled by ta'wil or naskh. Example: a hadith commanding tayammum by striking hands once vs another commanding twice.
2
Check if a Companion's saying exists
See if there is a known statement from a Companion (or more) on the matter.
Companions' sayings are a source in Hanafi usul after the Sunnah.
3
Consider qiyas
If no Companion saying, resort to qiyas. If both exist, there is disagreement on priority.
Three views: (1) Bazdawi: no priority; (2) Companions always precede; (3) Qiyas always precedes; (4) Reconciliatory: Companions if not perceivable by qiyas, else qiyas.
4
Apply the correct view per context
In fatwa, the mufti must be aware of the ikhtilaf. Often the reconciliatory view is preferred.
5
Derive the ruling
From the chosen source, extract the ruling that resolves the hadith conflict.

This passage presents the Hanafi method for resolving apparent contradiction between legal evidences of different ranks. When two Qur'anic verses appear to contradict, they both fall (tasaqata) and the jurist must resort to the Sunnah (hadith) because it is the next source after the Qur'an. One cannot resort to a third verse to tip the scale, as that would be giving preference based on quantity rather than quality, which is impermissible. The example given is the apparent conflict between the verse commanding recitation in prayer (faqra'u ma tayassara) and the verse commanding silent listening (wa idha quri'a al-qur'anu fastami'u lahu wa ansitu). The first is general and seems to obligate the follower to recite, while the second is specific to the context of listening. They contradict in the same locus (prayer) and time, so they fall. The ruling is then sought from the hadith: 'Whoever has an imam, the recitation of the imam is recitation for him,' which settles the matter that the follower does not recite behind the imam. Between two contradictory hadiths, the resort is to the sayings of the Companions (aqwal al-sahaba) or to qiyas (analogy). Fakhr al-Islam (Imam al-Bazdawi) expressed this with 'or' to indicate that neither is inherently prioritized in his view. Other scholars held different views: some prioritize the sayings of the Companions absolutely, others prioritize qiyas absolutely, and a third position (reconciliation) distinguishes between matters that can be known by reason (ma yudraku bi al-qiyas) and those that cannot. This passage demonstrates the Hanafi commitment to structured hierarchy in resolving conflicts.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar is an extended commentary on the Nur al-Anwar, discussing the nature of prophetic revelation and the permissibility and mechanism of ijtihad by the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). The commentator begins by distinguishing between types of revelation: the first is 'ilham' (divine inspiration), which the Prophet receives directly in his heart, free from error, while the saints can err. The author notes that he did not include 'hatif ghaibi' (mysterious voices) or dreams, as these do not establish legal rulings. Then he discusses 'wahy batin' (interior revelation) which is attained through ijtihad – the Prophet's own reasoning based on textual evidence. Some scholars denied that the Prophet performed ijtihad, citing the verse 'Nor does he speak from desire; it is only a revelation revealed'. The commentary answers that the verse refers specifically to the Quran, not all his speech; his ijtihad-based rulings are also revelation, but of the interior type. The passage then explains that the Prophet is commanded to wait for revelation when a new incident occurs, up to three days or until urgency, then he may act on his opinion. If his opinion is correct, no revelation comes; if it is wrong, revelation corrects it. Importantly, the Prophet is protected from persisting on error – meaning that if he makes an error, Allah immediately reveals the correct ruling and does not leave him on the error. This is contrary to other mujtahids, who may remain on their erroneous ijtihad. The commentator illustrates this with the famous incident of the prisoners of Badr: The Prophet consulted his Companions; Abu Bakr advised ransom, Umar advised killing. The Prophet initially inclined to Abu Bakr's view, took ransom, but then revelation came and reproached them for not killing the prisoners, stating that it would have been better. The Prophet wept, acknowledging that if punishment had come, only Umar and Mu'adh would have been saved (the latter is a variant report). This example shows that the Prophet's ijtihad was subject to correction, and he was preserved from persisting on the error.

Key Terms
الْوَحْيُ الْبَاطِنُ (al-wahyu al-bāṭin): Interior revelation attained through ijtihad by the Prophet after contemplation on texts.
إلْهَام (ilhām): Divine inspiration cast into the heart, which for the Prophet is free from error; for saints it may err.
عِصْمَة (ʿiṣmah): Protection from persisting on error; the Prophet is infallible in that he is immediately corrected if he errs.

Shara'i' Man Qablana: When Previous Laws Become Our Shari'ah
Explains the Hanafi usul principle with proof and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of shara'i' man qablana to identify which previous prophetic laws are binding on Muslims and derive the ruling using the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
Previous prophets' laws bind us only if affirmed in the Qur'an without disapproval.

We often encounter stories of past prophets in the Qur'an. Are their laws automatically ours? The Hanafi school answers: only when the Qur'an mentions them without criticism. This lesson unpacks that rule, its proof from Surah al-An'am, and its practical application using examples from the text.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Restate the Hanafi principle: 'The laws of previous prophets are binding on us if mentioned in the Qur'an without disapproval.' This is the core of shara'i' man qablana.
2
Identify the proof
Locate the primary proof: Qur'an 6:90, 'Those are the ones whom Allah has guided, so follow their guidance.' The command to follow implies that their Shari'ah becomes ours when affirmed in our Book.
This is a qat'i al-thubut dalil but its dalalah is zanni, as it is an indirect inference.
3
Analyze the condition
Understand the crucial condition: 'without disapproval.' The passage contrasts prohibitions specific to Jews (punishments for rebellion) with universal moral laws. The former are not binding; the latter are.
The disapproval can be explicit (e.g., 'that is their recompense') showing it was a punishment.
4
Apply to examples
Work through the examples from the text: qisas (life for life) remains, division of water rights from Prophet Salih's story is binding, prohibition of sodomy from Prophet Lut's story is binding. Jewish dietary restrictions (suet, hoofed animals) are NOT binding because they are mentioned as punishments.
Each example shows how the condition is applied in practice.
5
Note cross-madhhab difference
Contrast with the Shafi'i position: Shafi'is generally require an explicit command to adopt a previous law; Hanafis rely on affirmation without disapproval. This affects which laws are considered binding.
This distinction is important for fatwa and comparative fiqh.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar is a commentary on the Hanafi usul principle of shara'i' man qablana (laws of previous prophets). The author first clarifies that the prohibitions specific to the Jews (like suet and hoofed animals) were punishments for their rebellion and are not binding on us. Then he states the rule: the laws of previous prophets only bind us if they are mentioned in our Qur'an without disapproval. When such laws are narrated in the Qur'an without criticism, they become part of our own Shari'ah because we are commanded to follow the guidance of those prophets (Qur'an 6:90). The Urdu commentary gives examples: the law of qisas (retaliation) from the Torah is retained in Islam, the division of water rights from the story of Prophet Salih shows the permissibility of rotating shares, and the prohibition of sodomy from the story of Prophet Lut remains. Conversely, the specific prohibitions on Jews (like suet) are not binding because they were punishments. Thus the principle distinguishes between laws that are universal moral teachings (which remain) and punitive restrictions (which do not). The phrase 'this is a great principle' refers to Imam Abu Hanifah's foundational rule that previous laws become part of our Shari'ah only through Quranic confirmation.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the textual (naqli) and rational (aqli) proofs for the authority of qiyas derived from the Quranic verse فَاعْتَبِرُوا and explain why circularity is avoided.

Core Idea
Qiyas is proven either by isharat al-nass or dalalat al-nass, never by qiyas itself.

The verse فَاعْتَبِرُوا يَا أُولِي الأَبْصَارِ is a key proof for qiyas. However, how exactly does it prove analogical reasoning? In this lesson, we follow the Hanafi usul tradition to examine two interpretive possibilities: one where the verse is taken generally, giving a textual proof through ishara, and another where it is seen as specific to punishments, yielding a rational proof through dalala. Both pathways avoid circularity and establish qiyas as a legitimate source.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source text and its literal meaning
The verse فَاعْتَبِرُوا يَا أُولِي الأَبْصَارِ commands those with insight to 'consider' or 'take a lesson'. Recognize that the word i'tibar is the root for qiyas.
In Hanafi usul, i'tibar is the act of analogical reasoning.
2
Understand the two interpretive approaches
If the verse is applied generally (umum), it directly commands i'tibar in all matters, so qiyas is proven textually via isharat al-nass. If it is specific to punishments, then the proof is rational via dalalat al-nass because the command implies that similar causes yield similar rulings.
The textual proof is stronger; the rational proof is still valid and avoids dependency on qiyas itself.
3
Analyze the avoidance of circularity (dawr)
The author states that neither approach uses qiyas to prove qiyas. In the textual approach, the proof comes directly from the text (ishara). In the rational approach, the proof uses implication (dalala), which is not qiyas but a separate method of extracting meaning.
Circularity would occur if the authority of qiyas were proven by qiyas itself.
4
Examine the linguistic analogy
The passage draws a parallel with metaphorical language: the word 'asad' (lion) is applied to a brave man due to shared bravery. Similarly, qiyas transfers a ruling due to a shared cause (illa). This illustrates the rational method.
This analogy shows how rational inference works in language and law.
5
Apply the distinction to a legal example
Consider the example given: enmity and denial of the Prophet caused punishment for past disbelievers. Any person with the same cause receives the same ruling. This is qiyas, but its proof comes from the verse's implication, not from a prior qiyas.
The example demonstrates the practical application of the rational proof.

The Lam of Consequence: Hanafi Justification for Paying Value in Zakat
Justification for paying cash value instead of sheep in zakat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the lam al-'aqibah as a textual indicator in Hanafi usul and apply it to justify paying cash value in zakat.

Core Idea
The lam in 'lil-fuqara' is not ownership but consequence — Allah gives the benefit, not the sheep.

This passage addresses a classical objection: if the hadith explicitly requires a sheep for zakat of grazing livestock, how can the Hanafi school allow paying its cash value? The response hinges on a subtle linguistic point — the lam of possession in the zakat verse (إِنَّمَا الصَّدَقَاتُ لِلْفُقَرَاءِ) is interpreted as the lam of ultimate consequence (لام العاقبة), not ownership. This lesson unpacks the textual reasoning, using Quranic verses and hadith to show that the obligation is to benefit the poor, not to transfer a specific sheep.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
The objector argues that the hadith ('In five camels, one sheep') specifies a sheep explicitly, so only a sheep fulfills the obligation. Paying its value violates this specification.
The opponent is likely a literalist or Shafi'i who insists on the specific animal.
2
Locate the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi scholar responds that the right of the poor is not to the specific sheep but to the value (benefit). The obligation is fulfilled when the poor receive the equivalent benefit.
The response is that the text itself (nas) indicates that the poor's right is to benefit, not to ownership of the animal.
3
Analyze the Key Dalil
The passage cites Surah Hud 11:6 ('There is no moving creature but its sustenance depends on Allah') to show that Allah provides for all, and the sheep is a means. It also cites the hadith that charity falls into Allah's hand before reaching the poor.
These texts establish that the sheep is taken by Allah symbolically; the poor receive the benefit.
4
Examine the Linguistic Argument
The lam in 'lil-fuqara' is argued to be the lam of consequence (لام العاقبة), not the lam of ownership (لام التمليك). This is because the sheep is first taken by Allah, then given to the poor from His own provision.
This interpretation is key: it shifts the obligation from transferring a specific item to ensuring the poor receive the benefit.
5
Derive the Ruling
Since the lam indicates consequence, the obligation is to benefit the poor. Paying cash value achieves the same consequence, so it is valid by textual indication (nas), not merely by analogy.
Thus, the Hanafi school allows qima in zakat as a valid fulfillment.

Dalalat al-Nass: Extending Zakat Sheep to Cash
Explaining permissibility of zakat cash substitution via dalalat al-nass

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the components of a dalalat al-nass argument and derive a ruling by applying the underlying purpose (illa) of the text.

Core Idea
Dalalat al-nass extends a ruling to unmentioned cases sharing the same underlying purpose.

This lesson explores the Hanafi principle of dalalat al-nass (textual indication) through a concrete example: the permissibility of giving cash instead of the obligatory sheep in zakat. The passage presents the reasoning, an objection, and a nuanced reply. Understanding this will strengthen your ability to derive rulings from the Shariah's purposes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit ruling and its wording
Locate the base ruling: the text permits the zakat payer to exchange the sheep for cash (gold or silver). Note that the explicit wording mentions 'sheep' as the obligatory item.
The base ruling is the permissibility of taking a sheep; the extension is to cash.
2
Extract the underlying purpose (illa)
Determine why the text mandated a sheep: to fulfill the poor's needs. The underlying purpose (illa) is need-fulfillment, not the specific animal.
Dalalat al-nass works by recognizing the illa behind the text.
3
Recognize the objection
The objection claims that the permission is unnecessary because the poor already receive other obligations (fitrana, 'ushr, kaffarah, khums). Hence, no extension is needed.
The objection questions the illa's applicability.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Anwār discusses the rule of 'istidlāl bi 'adam al-dalīl' – arguing from the absence of evidence. The author first distinguishes two situations: if the mujtahid claims that he personally does not have evidence for a ruling, that is acceptable because it is a statement about his own knowledge. However, if he claims that the ruling is not established in reality because he did not find evidence, then scholars differed. Some, citing the Prophet's argument in Sūrat al-An‘ām (6:145) where he says 'I do not find...', considered it permissible to use the lack of evidence as proof for a negative claim, but only in sharʿī matters, not in rational matters. The majority (jumhūr), however, hold that it is not a valid proof at all, whether for negation or affirmation, because Allah commanded the Prophet to demand proof from those who made claims (Qur’ān 2:111). The author concludes by saying he has explained this controversial point to the best of his ability. The text includes an Urdu commentary elaborating the positions.

Key Terms
الاستدلال بعدم الدليل (al-istidlāl bi 'adam al-dalīl): Arguing that a ruling is not valid or established simply because one has not found evidence for it.
الجمهور (al-jumhūr): The majority of scholars, who reject istidlāl bi 'adam al-dalīl entirely.
برهان (burhān): Proof, evidence; in the verse 2:111 it refers to a clear proof for a claim.

Qalb al-Wasf: Inverting the Shafi'i Proof on Intention in Ramadan
Debate on whether Ramadan fast requires specific intention due to its obligatory nature

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi technique of qalb al-wasf to refute an opposing school's qiyas and identify the thamarah al-khilaf in the dispute on intention in Ramadan fasting.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, obligation itself can prove that no extra intention is needed – that's qalb al-wasf.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute about whether the obligatory nature of Ramadan fasting requires a specific daily intention. The source text demonstrates the Hanafi use of qalb al-wasf (inversion of description) to turn the Shafi'i's own premise against them. By the end, you will understand how this technique functions in theological argumentation and why the Hanafi position holds that a single intention for Ramadan suffices.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Opponent's Argument
The Shafi'is argue that because Ramadan fast is fard (obligatory), it requires specific intention (ta'ayyun al-niyyah) each day, analogizing to qada' (make-up) fasts which are not valid without specific intention.
The Shafi'i basis: fard → need for intention, by qiyas to qada'.
2
Locate the Hanafi Inversion (Qalb al-Wasf)
Hanafis take the same characteristic (fard) and invert it: obligation from Allah means He has already specified the fast, so no extra intention by the servant is needed. They use the same premise to reach the opposite conclusion.
This is qalb al-wasf: turning the opponent's own description against them.
3
Trace the Evidence (Hadith)
Both sides rely on the hadith: 'When Sha'ban ends, there is no fast but Ramadan.' Hanafis interpret this as Allah's specification, making the fast already determinate. Shafi'is interpret it as a prohibition of other fasts but still requiring intention.
The same textual evidence yields different rulings due to different usul principles.
4
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Practical consequence: Hanafis hold that a single intention at the beginning of Ramadan covers all days; Shafi'is require intention for each day. This affects how the fast is begun and whether missed intentions invalidate the fast.
This is the real-world difference that the dispute produces.
5
Evaluate the Tarjih (Preponderance)
The Hanafi school (mufta bihi) adopts Imam Abu Hanifa's view that no daily intention is needed. The tarjih is based on the strength of the hadith and the principle that the Lawgiver's determination suffices.
In Hanafi usul, the position is considered zahir al-riwayah.

The Role of Intellect in Legal Capacity: Ash'ari, Mu'tazili, and Hanafi Views
Explaining the necessity of intellect for taklif and the dispute over its role

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the role of intellect in taklif, contrast it with the Ash'ari and Mu'tazili positions, and identify the dalil each school uses.

Core Idea
Intellect is necessary for taklif but not an independent source of obligation—Hanafis balance reason and revelation.

This passage from Nur al-Anwar addresses a core usul dispute: does the human intellect have an independent role in determining good and evil, or is it merely a passive receiver of revelation? The Hanafi school takes a middle path, asserting that intellect is an indispensable condition for legal responsibility but that specific rulings depend on revealed texts. We will explore the arguments of the Ash'aris (who deny any independent role for intellect) and the Mu'tazilis (who elevate it above revelation), and then see how the Hanafi position integrates both.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The dispute is about whether human intellect ('aql) alone can establish obligation (wujub) and prohibition (hurmah) or whether revelation (sam') is required. This affects the definition of a mukallaf (legally responsible person).
2
Locate each school's position
The Ash'aris say: 'لا عبرة للعقل دون السمع' (no consideration for intellect without revelation). The Mu'tazilis say intellect is a cause (illah) that obligates what it deems good and prohibits what it deems evil. The Hanafis, as implied in the Urdu commentary, consider intellect the first condition for taklif but not sufficient for all rulings.
3
Analyze the proofs (dalil) for each view
The Ash'aris cite Quran 17:15: 'وَمَا كُنَّا مُعَذِّبِينَ حَتَّى نَبْعَثَ رَسُوْلاً' (We do not punish until we send a messenger). The Mu'tazilis cite Prophet Ibrahim's reasoning before revelation (Quran 6:74). The Hanafis argue that without intellect, the address (khattab) cannot be understood, so intellect is presupposed.
The Hanafi proof is more philosophical: taklif requires understanding, which requires intellect.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The main consequence is the validity of faith in a sane person who has not received revelation. Ash'aris: invalid. Mu'tazilis: valid and obligatory. Hanafis: the person is accountable to use intellect to recognize the Creator, but specific Sharia obligations only apply after revelation reaches them.
This affects rulings on the status of non-Muslims before da'wah.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi middle ground
The Hanafi position avoids both extremes: it affirms the necessity of intellect for responsibility but does not make it an independent source of law. This aligns with the principle that the Shari'ah built upon reason but is not derived from it alone.
This is consistent with the Hanafi usul maxim: 'الشرع لا يرد العقل السليم' (sound reason is not contradicted by revelation).

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from Ashraf al-Anwar on a matn discussing the 'arid (obstructing matters) that affect legal rulings. It begins by addressing a specific fiqh issue: whether spouses inherit qisas (retaliation) and diyah (blood money) from each other. The author presents the Hanafi position that qisas is established for spouses just as diyah is, but with a difference: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, the right is ab initio (ibtida'an) for each spouse, meaning they can personally execute qisas; according to the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), it is by way of inheritance (irthen), meaning they are heirs to the right. Imam Malik's view is that spouses do not inherit diyah because marriage ends at death. The Hanafi proof is a hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) gave the wife of Ashyam al-Babi inheritance from her husband's 'aql (blood money). This establishes that the dead person retains certain rulings of the living for matters related to the Hereafter and rights. The commentator then transitions to the main topic: after discussing celestial (samawi) obstructing matters (like death, insanity, etc.), they now discuss acquired (muktasab) obstructing matters, which involve human choice. The first such 'arid is jahl (ignorance). Although ignorance is innate in humans, it is considered an 'arid because it is outside the essential nature of humanity, and because one can remove it by acquiring knowledge, so leaving knowledge is an acquired form of ignorance. The passage then classifies jahl into three types: (1) Jahl batil (false ignorance) — the ignorance of a disbeliever after clear proofs; this is no excuse in the Hereafter but may be an excuse in this world (e.g., to avoid capital punishment if they accept the covenant). (2) The ignorance of a follower of desire (sahib al-hawa) regarding Allah's attributes and the Hereafter, such as the Mu'tazila who deny divine attributes, vision of Allah, and intercession. This ignorance is blameworthy because the truth is available. (3) The ignorance of a rebel (baghi) who opposes the just imam based on a flawed interpretation. If such a rebel destroys property or kills someone while they have no military protection, they are liable for damages because they can be compelled by evidence and force. However, if they have protection (man'a), they are not held liable after repentance, similar to how combatants are not pursued for past acts after embracing Islam. Finally, the passage mentions the ignorance of a mujtahid who errs against the clear text of the Book, citing the example of Imam al-Shafi'i's opinion on a specific issue (likely divorce), indicating that such ignorance is not excusable.

Mawadi'a in Contracts: Rulings on Collusion in Sales, Non-Monetary Acts, and Marriage
Explains ruling on collusive agreements in sales and non-monetary acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on collusion (mawadi'a) to distinguish between valid and invalid contracts based on the nature of the subject matter (property vs. non-property) and the type of collusion (quantity vs. genus).

Core Idea
Collusion in non-property acts is binding; in sales, it depends on whether a valid price remains.

The concept of mawadi'a (collusion/pretence) arises when parties outwardly perform a transaction but secretly agree on a different reality. The Hanafi school distinguishes based on whether the transaction involves property or not, and further within sales based on whether the collusion concerns the quantity or the genus of the price. This passage from a Hanafi usul text explores these distinctions, providing the reasoning (ta'lil) behind each ruling and citing the prophetic hadith on the three acts where jest does not alter the legal effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The passage divides transactions into three categories: (a) sales (monetary), (b) non-monetary acts (divorce, emancipation, oath), and (c) marriage where property (dower) is incidental. The ruling on collusion varies for each.
2
Analyze collusion in sales: quantity vs. genus
In sales, if the collusion concerns the quantity of the price (e.g., show 2000 but intend 1000), the sale is valid because part of the named price remains (1000). If collusion concerns the genus (e.g., show dinars but intend dirhams), the named price becomes entirely different, leaving the contract without a valid price, thus invalid. Note the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn: Abu Hanifa holds both invalid; Sahibayn differentiate as above.
The text presents the Sahibayn's view as the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position.
3
Apply the hadith to non-monetary acts
For divorce, emancipation, and oaths, the hadith 'Three things, their seriousness is serious and their jest is serious' makes collusion ineffective; the act is binding even if done in jest. Collusion (pretence) is void.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its meaning (dalala) is clear and qat'i.
4
Evaluate collusion in marriage
In marriage, since the dower (mahr) is incidental, collusion about the essence of marriage (pretending to marry while not actually intending) renders the marriage valid but the dower void. Collusion about the amount of dower: if both agree to disregard the jest, the named dower stands; otherwise, the actual agreed amount applies.
This follows the principle that in marriage, jest does not affect the core contract but may affect the dower.
5
Synthesize the overarching principle
The ruling on collusion depends on whether the collusion voids an essential element of the transaction. In sales, the essential element is the price; in non-monetary acts, the essential element is the legal effect (e.g., divorce takes effect); in marriage, the essential element is the marriage itself, while the dower is dispensable.
This analytical framework reflects the Hanafi reliance on identifying the 'illa (effective cause) behind rulings.

Definition and Ruling of Safah (Foolishness)
Legal definition and effect of safah with Quranic proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define safah legally, explain its effect on ahliyyah, and identify the Quranic evidence and its preferred interpretation.

Core Idea
Safah restricts wealth control but does not remove legal capacity for obligations.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi definition of safah (foolishness) as a legal term. While the safih remains fully accountable for all Shariah duties, their wealth is placed under guardianship based on a careful reading of Quran 4:5. We will parse the definition, the two interpretations of the verse, and the resulting ruling.

Guided Path
1
Define Safah Linguistically and Technically
Start with the literal meaning: foolishness and lightness. Then move to the technical definition: acting contrary to the Shariah even if the act is originally permissible, specifically through israf (extravagance) and tabdhīr (waste).
The technical definition narrows the scope to financial mismanagement.
2
Identify the Effect on Ahliyyah
Note that safah does not create a defect in ahliyyah (legal capacity). The safih remains mukallaf – subject to rewards, punishments, and all Shariah rulings. The only impact is on financial disposition.
This distinguishes safah from insanity or minority.
3
Analyze the Quranic Dalīl (4:5)
Read the verse: 'And do not give the foolish your wealth...' Recognize two possible interpretations: (1) literal – addressing guardians not to give their own wealth to fools; (2) reinterpreted – 'your wealth' means 'their wealth' (the fools' wealth), with the pronoun referring to guardians as managers. The second interpretation is preferred for the issue at hand.
The preferred interpretation makes the verse serve as the basis for withholding the safih's wealth.
4
Derive the Ruling
From the preferred ta'wīl, the ruling is that upon reaching puberty, the safih's wealth is not given to him but placed under a guardian's management. This restriction applies only to wealth, not to other aspects of legal capacity.
The hukm is that the safih is hajr (interdicted) regarding his property.

And as for safah (foolishness) – it is an incidental attribute. Linguistically, it means foolishness and lightness. Technically, the author defines it as: 'It is acting contrary to the requirement of the Shariah, even if its origin is permissible, and it is extravagance and waste.' That is, exceeding the limits and squandering wealth. This does not cause a defect in legal capacity (ahliyyah) nor prevent any of the Shariah rulings, such that the safih is liable for all rulings and obligated to perform them all. But his wealth is withheld from him, i.e., the wealth of the safih is not given to him upon first reaching puberty due to the text: 'And do not give the foolish your wealth which Allah has made for you a means of support.' There are two interpretations of this verse: First, the literal meaning: 'O guardians, do not give the foolish among your spouses and children your wealth...' – this would not relate to the issue at hand. Second, that 'your wealth' means 'their wealth' (the fools' wealth), and the attribution to the guardians is because they are in charge of it, making the verse applicable to preventing the fool from receiving his own wealth.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'safah' (foolishness) legally. It begins with the linguistic meaning (lightness or foolishness) and then gives the technical definition as acting contrary to the Shariah's requirements even if the act itself is permissible in essence, specifically extravagance and waste. The key point is that safah does not nullify legal capacity (ahliyyah) – the safih remains fully responsible for all Shariah obligations (rewards, punishments, etc.). However, the safih is prevented from having control over his own wealth; it is withheld from him and placed under guardianship. This is based on the Quranic verse (4:5). The passage then analyzes the verse in two ways: the first interpretation takes the verse literally as addressing guardians about giving their own wealth to fools (unrelated to the safih's property), while the second interpretation (preferred) reinterprets 'your wealth' as 'their wealth' (the fools' wealth), making the verse a direct proof for preventing the safih from managing his own property upon reaching puberty.

Key Terms
السفه (as-safah): Acting contrary to the requirements of the Shariah, even if the act is originally permissible, specifically through extravagance and waste (israf and tabdhir).
