Prophet's Ijtihad and the Status of His Ilham: The Badr Incident as a Usul Principle
Textual analysis of the Badr incident to derive usul principles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two scenarios of nass and ijtihad—when the nass descends after the ijtihad and when it appears concurrently—and state the Hanafi principle that the earlier ijtihad is not nullified in the first case.

Core Idea
When revelation follows a valid ijtihad, the ijtihad stands; only contradictory pre-existing nass nullifies it.

The incident of the prisoners of war at Badr is a landmark for understanding the relationship between ijtihad and revealed text. The Prophet consulted his Companions, followed Abu Bakr's advice to accept ransom, and later a Quranic verse rebuked that decision. Yet the Prophet did not reverse the ruling; he permitted the consumption of the ransom. This passage unpacks the subtle usul principle that timing matters: a later nass that contradicts an ijtihad does not automatically undo the ijtihad if it was already acted upon. This is tied to the unique nature of prophetic ijtihad, which is a form of ilham that is a decisive proof for all. You will walk through the narrative and extract this critical distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the incident and the two opinions
Read the narrative: Abu Bakr recommended ransom; Umar recommended execution. The Prophet chose Abu Bakr's view. Understand the context of consultation (shura) and its role in ijtihad.
2
Observe the aftermath: revelation of the verse
After the ransom was taken, the verses of Surah al-Anfal (8:67-69) were revealed, rebuking the Prophet for taking captives before subduing the enemy. Note the tone: it is a rebuke but not an explicit nullification of the earlier action.
The verse ends with permission: 'So consume what you have taken of war booty as lawful and good.'
3
Trace the Prophet's response to the revelation
The Prophet and Companions wept, but he did not order the ransom to be returned or declared haram. He allowed its consumption. This implies the earlier ijtihad stands despite the revelation.
The author explicitly says the ruling on ransom was executed and its consumption permitted.
4
Extract the usul principle from the passage
The author states: 'This is the difference between the descent of a text contrary to an opinion and its appearance contrary to it: in the first, the opinion is not nullified by the text; in the second, it is nullified.' Identify this as the key principle.
The principle applies to prophetic ijtihad; for other mujtahids, the same logic holds but with the caveat that their ijtihad is not infallible.
5
Apply the distinction between prophetic and saintly ilham
The passage analogizes the difference between prophetic ijtihad and that of other mujtahids to the difference between prophetic ilham and saintly ilham. Prophetic ilham is a subset of wahy and is binding on all; saintly ilham is only a proof for the saint personally if it agrees with Shariah.
This highlights the elevated authority of prophetic reasoning.

The Prophet (peace be upon him) consulted his Companions, and each gave his opinion. Abu Bakr said: O Messenger of Allah, these captives are your people and family; take ransom from them, which will benefit us, and set them free; perhaps after this they will be guided to Islam. Umar said: O Messenger of Allah, take responsibility for killing al-Abbas, make Ali responsible for killing Aqil, and assign me to kill so-and-so, so that each of us kills his close relative. The Prophet said: Allah makes some hearts soft like water and some hard like stone. Your example, O Abu Bakr, is like Ibrahim who said: Whoever follows me is of me, and whoever disobeys me, You are Forgiving, Merciful. Your example, O Umar, is like Nuh who said: My Lord, leave not upon the earth any inhabitant of the disbelievers. Then the Prophet's opinion settled on Abu Bakr's view, so he ordered the taking of ransom and foretold: 'You will be martyred in Uhud corresponding to their number.' The Companions accepted willingly. When the ransom was taken, this verse was revealed: 'It is not for a prophet to have captives until he has made slaughter in the land. You desire the goods of this world, while Allah desires the Hereafter. And Allah is Exalted in Might and Wise. If not for a decree from Allah that preceded, you would have been struck for what you took with a great punishment. So consume what you have taken of war booty as lawful and good, and fear Allah. Indeed, Allah is Forgiving and Merciful.' Upon hearing this rebuke, the Prophet and all the Companions wept. The Prophet said: 'If punishment had descended, none of us would have been saved except Umar and Mu'adh ibn Sa'd' (both had the same opinion). So it became evident that the truth was Umar's opinion, and that the Prophet erred when he acted upon Abu Bakr's opinion, but he did not persist in error; rather, he was made aware by the revelation of verses, and he executed the ruling on ransom, permitted its consumption, and did not order its return or prohibition. This is the difference between the descent of a text contrary to an opinion and its appearance contrary to it: in the first, the opinion is not nullified by the text; in the second, it is nullified. This is like inspiration: the difference between the ijtihad of the Prophet and other mujtahids is like the difference between the inspiration of the Prophet and that of the awliya. For his inspiration is a decisive proof for him, even if it is not so for others in terms of quality. The inspiration of the Prophet is a type of revelation that is a binding proof applicable to all creation, while the inspiration of the awliya is a proof only for themselves, if it agrees with the Shariah, and does not extend to others, except when we adopt their statement by way of etiquette.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of the Battle of Badr incident to derive a key usul principle regarding the relationship between ijtihad (independent reasoning) and nass (revealed text). The author first narrates the consultation between the Prophet and his Companions about the prisoners of war. Abu Bakr recommended taking ransom, while Umar recommended killing them. The Prophet followed Abu Bakr's opinion, accepted ransom, and later a Quranic verse was revealed rebuking that decision. The author then extracts a critical distinction: if a nass descends after a ruling based on ijtihad, but the revelation shows a different preference without explicitly nullifying the earlier ruling, the earlier ruling remains valid (as here, the Prophet allowed the ransom and did not order its return). However, if the nass appears concurrently or before the ijtihad, and the ijtihad contradicts it, then the ijtihad is nullified. This distinction is grounded in the Prophet's unique status: his ijtihad is considered a form of ilham (inspiration) that constitutes a decisive proof (hujjah qati'ah) for him and the entire ummah because it is a subset of wahy (revelation) that is binding on all. In contrast, the ilham of awliya (saints) is only a proof for themselves personally, provided it aligns with the Shariah, and does not obligate others—except when adopted as a matter of spiritual etiquette (adab). The author thus emphasizes the elevated nature of prophetic authority in legal reasoning.

Key Terms
إلهام (ilham): Inspiration or divine insight placed by Allah into the heart of a prophet or saint, which for the Prophet is a type of revelation and a decisive proof for all, while for awliya it is only a proof for themselves if it agrees with Shariah.
اجتهاد (ijtihad): The effort of a qualified scholar to derive a legal ruling from the sources. Here specifically applied to the Prophet's own reasoning, which partakes of the nature of revelation and is thus infallible in its binding force.

The Nature and Authority of Prophetic Ijtihad as Ilham
Defines the rule of prophetic ijtihad as ilham

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that prophetic ijtihad is a form of ilham and constitutes a conclusive proof (hujja qatia) for the Ummah, and differentiate it from the inspiration of saints.

Core Idea
Prophetic ijtihad is protected revelation (ilham) — a conclusive proof for all, unlike saintly inspiration which binds only the individual.

This passage clarifies a foundational Hanafi usul principle: the ijtihad of the Prophet ﷺ is not ordinary reasoning but a form of divine inspiration (ilham) that is part of revelation. It is a conclusive proof (hujja qatia) for the entire Ummah, whereas the inspiration of saints is only binding upon themselves, provided it aligns with Shariah. This distinction has direct implications for how we understand the authority of Prophetic rulings and their permanence in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key distinction
The passage begins by distinguishing between a ruling made before the revelation of a contradictory text and one made after. In the first case, the earlier opinion-based decision is not invalidated; in the second, it is invalid. This sets up the analogy to ilham.
The key is timing relative to revelation.
2
Understand the analogy to ilham
The author compares the difference between Prophetic ijtihad and others' ijtihad to the difference between Prophetic ilham and saintly ilham. The point of similarity is that both the Prophet's ijtihad and his ilham are conclusive proofs (hujja qatia) for him and transitive to the Ummah.
The analogy is structural, not literal.
3
Define the nature of prophetic ilham
Prophetic ilham is described as a type of revelation (wahy) that is a transitive proof (hujja muta'addiya). It is conclusive for the Prophet and extends to all people.
This makes Prophetic ijtihad part of the revealed Shariah.
4
Define the nature of saintly ilham
Saintly ilham is only a proof for the saint himself, provided it agrees with the Shariah. It does not bind others, though one may voluntarily follow it out of etiquette.
The condition of agreement with Shariah is crucial.
5
Apply the rule to the Badr example
The Urdu commentary illustrates with the ransom of captives at Badr: the Prophet acted on Abu Bakr's opinion before revelation. When revelation came, it did not invalidate the earlier decision because it was made before the text. This mirrors the rule that Prophetic ijtihad (like ilham) is authoritative and not undone by later revelation.
This example demonstrates the practical application of the principle.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul al-fiqh discussion on the nature and conditions of ijtihad. The author, commenting on the matn, begins by reinforcing that qiyas is the essence of ijtihad and foundation of fiqh. He then clarifies the ruling (hukm) of ijtihad: it is correctness by dominant opinion (zann ghalib), not certainty. This means a mujtahid may be correct or may err, and the truth in matters of ikhtilaf is one single ruling in Allah's knowledge, but we do not know it with certainty. Hence, we affirm the correctness of the four madhahib (in practice). Next, the author lists the conditions of ijtihad: (1) mastery of the Quran's meanings and its technical categories like 'amm, khass, etc. (2) knowledge of the Sunnah, specifically ahkam-related hadith (approx. 3000). (3) knowledge of qiyas and its methods. Importantly, ijma is not a condition for ijtihad itself, but is needed to know ijma rulings. Then he provides a proof that ijtihad can be wrong: the case of al-mufawwada, where Ibn Mas'ud said, 'I will exert my ijtihad; if correct from Allah, if wrong from me and Satan.' The Companions present did not object, forming tacit consensus (ijma sukti) that ijtihad is fallible. The Mu'tazila are refuted: they claim every mujtahid is correct and truth is multiple. This is impossible because contradictory rulings (e.g., halal and haram) cannot both be true in reality. The view wrongly attributed to Abu Hanifa is clarified: he meant all are correct in practice (for the practitioner's accountability), not in absolute reality. Finally, the passage distinguishes ijtihad in fiqhi matters (where error is possible) from creedal matters (where error leads to kufr or misguidance). The difference between Ash'ari and Maturidi is not in fundamentals, so neither calls the other misguided.

Key Terms
الاجتهاد (al-ijtihad): The exertion of one's utmost effort to arrive at a ruling by dominant opinion (ghalib al-zann) from the sources.
غالب الرأي (ghalib al-ra'y): Dominant opinion, a strong probability that is less than certainty.
المسألة المفوضة (al-mas'ala al-mufawwada): A woman whose husband dies before consummation and without fixing a dowry.

The Mujtahid: Error and Reward in Ijtihad
Debate on mujtahid correctness and error with Mu'tazila and within Hanafi school

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the reward of a mistaken mujtahid and apply the distinction between correctness in premises and correctness in outcome.

Core Idea
A mistaken mujtahid is rewarded for his effort, even if his final ruling is wrong.

Is every mujtahid correct? The Mu'tazila say yes; the Hanafis say no. But both schools agree that sincere ijtihad is rewarded. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi proof from the Companions and the Quran, and shows why the chosen view holds that a mujtahid who errs is correct in his method but mistaken in his conclusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed claim
The Mu'tazila claim that every mujtahid is correct (musib). The Hanafi school rejects this and asserts that ijtihad is susceptible to error.
This is a key usul dispute about the nature of ijtihad.
2
Examine the Companions' ijma'
Hazrat Abdullah ibn Mas'ud's statement and the silent agreement of the Companions establish that ijtihad can be wrong.
The story of the mufawwada woman is the evidence.
3
Analyze the Quranic proof
The story of Dawood and Sulayman (21:79) shows that while both were correct in the initial stage, only Sulayman was right in the final ruling.
The verse 'We made Solomon understand it' is key.
4
Apply the distinction between beginning and end
The chosen view (mukhtar) holds that a mujtahid who errs is correct (musib) in arranging his premises but wrong (mukhti') in the outcome.
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school.
5
Derive the consequences
The mistaken mujtahid gets one reward; the correct one gets two. Also, this doctrine supports the impermissibility of takhsis al-illa.
Takhsis al-illa would imply every mujtahid is correct, which is rejected.

The woman intended by 'mufawwada' is one whose marriage has taken place and her husband died before consummation and her mahr was not fixed. Abdullah ibn Mas'ud was asked about her ruling. He said: I will perform ijtihad in this matter with my opinion. If I hit correctness, it is from Allah. If I err, it is from me. So the error is attributed to me or to Satan. My opinion is that she should be given mahr al-mithl, neither less nor more. This answer was given in the presence of the Companions; none objected. Thus it became ijma' that ijtihad is susceptible to error. The Mu'tazila said: Every mujtahid is correct, and in fields of disagreement, truth is multiple in Allah's knowledge. This is false because some mujtahid deem a thing prohibited and others permissible; how can two truths coexist in reality? Contradiction would be necessary. And this (that every mujtahid is correct) has been narrated from Abu Hanifa. For this reason some have attributed to him Mu'tazili inclination, but he is innocent. His intent is that all are correct in action, not in reality. This difference is in transmitted matters, not rational; only in particular fiqh rulings, not in creed. In creed, truth is one. In some books it is mentioned that this difference with the Mu'tazila is only in ijtihad matters. Then: The mujtahid when he errs, some say he is mistaken from beginning to end, i.e., in arrangement of premises and extraction of result. This is the inclination of Sheikh Abu Mansur and others. The chosen view is that he is correct in the beginning and mistaken in the end, because he did what he was tasked with in arranging premises and exerted his effort; thus he is correct therein, even if he erred in the final outcome. He is excused, rather rewarded, for the mistaken one has one reward and the correct one has two. There occurred in the time of David and Solomon an incident of a flock grazing on people's crops. David judged in one way and erred; Solomon judged in another way and was correct. Allah said: 'We made Solomon understand it, and to each We gave judgment and knowledge.' It indicates that the mujtahid errs and is correct. From 'to each We gave' we learn they were correct in arranging premises, though David erred in the end. The story is in books. Because the mujtahid errs and is correct, we say: specification of the 'illa is not permissible. That is, one cannot say: my 'illa is effective but the ruling is absent due to a preventer, because that would lead to making every mujtahid correct, as no mujtahid would be unable to say this. This is contrary to some, like the Iraqi scholars and Karkhi, who allow specification of the derived 'illa because the 'illa is an indicator and can be made an indicator in some places and not others.

Commentary
This passage is a usul discussion on the nature of ijtihad and its relation to error and correctness. It opens with the famous story of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (ra) regarding a woman whose husband died before consummation and without a fixed mahr. Ibn Mas'ud's statement 'I will exert my own opinion' shows his ijtihad, and he explicitly acknowledges the possibility of error. The fact that no companion objected to his statement established an ijma' that ijtihad can indeed be erroneous. The passage then contrasts this with the Mu'tazila position that every mujtahid is correct and that truth is multiple in matters of disagreement. The Hanafi author rejects this as false, arguing that contradictory rulings cannot both be true in reality. He then addresses a controversial narration from Abu Hanifa that seems to support the Mu'tazila view, clarifying that Abu Hanifa meant correctness in action (practice) not in ultimate truth. The author further clarifies that this disagreement is limited to transmitted (naqli) matters of fiqh, not rational (aqli) matters of creed, where truth is singular. The discussion then moves to the precise status of a mujtahid who errs. Some scholars, like Sheikh Abu Mansur al-Maturidi, hold that he is mistaken both at the beginning (arranging premises) and at the end (conclusion). But the chosen (mukhtar) view is that he is correct in his effort of arranging the premises, as he fulfilled his obligation, but mistaken in the final outcome. He is excused and even rewarded (one reward for the mistaken, two for the correct). This is supported by the Quranic story of Dawood and Sulayman (peace be upon them) in the matter of the sheep grazing on crops. Dawood's judgment was mistaken, Sulayman's was correct, yet Allah says 'And to each We gave judgment and knowledge', indicating their correctness in the initial stage. The passage concludes with a related usul principle: the impermissibility of specifying (takhsis) the derived cause ('illa). The author argues that allowing a mujtahid to claim that his 'illa is effective but the ruling is absent due to a preventer would lead to every mujtahid being considered correct, which is not acceptable. This is in contrast to some Hanafi scholars like Karkhi who allow such specification because the 'illa is merely an indicator.

Key Terms
مُجْتَهِد (mujtahid): A scholar who exerts his full capacity to derive legal rulings from the sources.
خَطَأ (khata'): Error in ijtihad, i.e., the ruling derived does not correspond to what Allah actually intended.
إِصَابَة (isabah): Correctness in ijtihad, either in method or in reaching the actual divine ruling.
تَخْصِيص الْعِلَّة (takhsis al-illa): Claiming that a valid 'illa (cause) does not produce its ruling in a particular case due to a preventer.

The Mujtahid's Error: Process vs. Outcome
Debate on mujtahid error and its connection to specifying causes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on whether a mujtahid who errs is considered correct in process or mistaken entirely, and apply it to the Quranic story of Dawud and Sulaiman.

Core Idea
A mujtahid is correct in effort but may err in conclusion — rewarded nonetheless.

Is a mujtahid who reaches a wrong conclusion completely mistaken, or is his effort still valid? The passage opens with this debate, clarifying that the chosen Hanafi view holds the mujtahid correct in the process of ijtihad (arranging premises) even if the final ruling is wrong. The Quranic incident of Dawud and Sulaiman (AS) serves as the proof. In this lesson, we unpack the two positions and the evidence.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute in usul al-fiqh about the status of a mujtahid who errs (mukhti') and the issue of specifying a derived cause (takhsis al-illah al-mustanbata). The author presents the chosen (mukhtar) position: a mujtahid is correct (musib) in the initial arrangement of premises (i.e., effort in ijtihad) but may err in the final conclusion. He is excused and even rewarded—one reward for the mistaken, two for the correct. The story of Dawud and Sulaiman (AS) is cited as a Quranic proof: both were given judgment and knowledge, but only Sulaiman was made to understand the correct verdict at the end. This shows that a mujtahid can be correct in process yet err in outcome. The passage then links this to the prohibition of takhsis al-illah (specifying a derived cause by claiming a preventer). Allowing such specification would imply every mujtahid is correct in his derivation, which is rejected. The exception is the Iraqi scholars (like al-Karkhi) who permit it because the cause is merely a sign. However, the chosen Hanafi position restricts this: for a textual cause (mansusa), specification may be allowed (e.g., zina is cause for lashing but lashing is not applied in some cases due to a preventer). For a derived cause (mustanbata), specification leads to too many correct judgments. The Hanafi position is that the ruling is absent because the cause as a whole is absent in that locus, not that the cause is present but the ruling is prevented.

Key Terms
مصيب (musib): Correct in ijtihad, arriving at the intended ruling (or process thereof).
مخطئ (mukhti'): One who errs in ijtihad, especially in final conclusion.
اجتهاد (ijtihad): Exertion of scholarly effort to derive a ruling from the sources.

Takhsis al-Illah: Specifying a Derived Cause is Impermissible
Prohibition of specifying a derived cause and its justification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school prohibits specifying a derived cause (takhsis al-illah al-mustanbata) and distinguish it from specifying a textual cause.

Core Idea
Takhsis of derived cause leads to every mujtahid being correct – untenable.

Can a scholar claim that his derived cause is valid but a preventer blocked its effect in a particular case? This lesson examines the Hanafi prohibition of specifying a derived cause (takhsis al-illah al-mustanbata). The passage argues that allowing such specification would logically lead to deeming every mujtahid correct in his derivation, which contradicts the earlier conclusion that a mujtahid can err. The Iraqi scholars (e.g., al-Karkhi) permit it, but the chosen Hanafi view restricts takhsis to textual causes only.

Guided Path
1
Define takhsis al-illah
Takhsis al-illah means a scholar claims his derived cause was true and effective, but the ruling was absent due to a preventer (mani'). Understand the two types of cause: textual (mansusa) and derived (mustanbata).
Textual causes are explicitly stated in revelation, while derived causes are extracted through ijtihad.
2
State the Hanafi ruling and its rationale
The Hanafi school considers takhsis of derived cause impermissible because it would lead to taswib (deeming every mujtahid correct) – no mujtahid would be unable to claim a preventer.
This connects back to the previous lesson: a mujtahid can err, so his derived cause may be wrong.
3
Present the opposing view (Iraqi scholars)
The Iraqi scholars (e.g., al-Karkhi) permit takhsis of derived cause because they view the cause as a mere sign (amara) that can be absent in some places.
They argue that just as a textual cause (e.g., zina for lashing) can be prevented by circumstances, so can a derived cause.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that a textual cause can be specified due to an external preventer that indicates the cause is not present in that locus. For a derived cause, the very claim of a preventer shows the cause is absent entirely; thus the cause itself is not established.
The passage distinguishes: in textual cause, the ruling is absent despite the cause; in derived cause, absence of ruling proves the cause is absent.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: In fiqh, a scholar cannot invoke a preventer to save his derived cause; he must accept that his derivation was incorrect.
This upholds the possibility of error in ijtihad.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute in usul al-fiqh about the status of a mujtahid who errs (mukhti') and the issue of specifying a derived cause (takhsis al-illah al-mustanbata). The author presents the chosen (mukhtar) position: a mujtahid is correct (musib) in the initial arrangement of premises (i.e., effort in ijtihad) but may err in the final conclusion. He is excused and even rewarded—one reward for the mistaken, two for the correct. The story of Dawud and Sulaiman (AS) is cited as a Quranic proof: both were given judgment and knowledge, but only Sulaiman was made to understand the correct verdict at the end. This shows that a mujtahid can be correct in process yet err in outcome. The passage then links this to the prohibition of takhsis al-illah (specifying a derived cause by claiming a preventer). Allowing such specification would imply every mujtahid is correct in his derivation, which is rejected. The exception is the Iraqi scholars (like al-Karkhi) who permit it because the cause is merely a sign. However, the chosen Hanafi position restricts this: for a textual cause (mansusa), specification may be allowed (e.g., zina is cause for lashing but lashing is not applied in some cases due to a preventer). For a derived cause (mustanbata), specification leads to too many correct judgments. The Hanafi position is that the ruling is absent because the cause as a whole is absent in that locus, not that the cause is present but the ruling is prevented.

Key Terms
تخصيص العلة (takhsis al-illah): Specifying a cause by claiming that a preventer blocks its effect in some cases.
العلة المستنبطة (al-illah al-mustanbata): A cause derived through ijtihad from the text, not explicitly stated.
العلة المنصوصة (al-illah al-mansusa): A cause explicitly stated in the Quran or hadith.
مانع (mani'): A preventer that blocks the application of a cause.

Introduction to Mu'aradah and Qalb in Usul
Explaining the two types of mu'aradah and the concept of qalb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mu'aradah, distinguish its two types, and explain the concept of qalb in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Mu'aradah is presenting a contrary proof; qalb is when the opponent's own proof is turned against them.

In usul al-fiqh, after defending a legal cause from counterexamples (naqd), the next challenge is mu'aradah—opposition through a proof. This lesson introduces the two types of mu'aradah, with special emphasis on the first type, qalb, where the opponent's own argument becomes the weapon against them.

Guided Path
1
Define mu'aradah
State the definition from the text: presenting a proof against what the opponent's proof established.
2
Identify the two types
First type: qalb (same proof); second type: different proof.
Qalb is the first type and is specific to usul and debate.
3
Analyze qalb
Qalb is both mu'aradah (opposition) and munaqadah (contradiction) because the opponent's proof becomes evidence for the opponent, indicating a defect.
4
Understand the primacy of mu'aradah
Mu'aradah is primary; munaqadah is secondary. The passage emphasizes this hierarchy.
5
Distinguish mu'aradah from naqd
Naqd is a counterexample to the cause, while mu'aradah is a counter-proof. The passage is structured sequentially: naqd first, then mu'aradah.
This distinction is crucial to avoid confusion.

Commentary
This passage introduces the concept of mu'aradah (opposition) in usul al-fiqh. After completing the defense against the naqd (counterexample), the author turns to mu'aradah which is defined as presenting a proof against what the opponent's proof established. It has two types: the first is when the same proof that the opponent used is turned against him (this is called qalb, inversion); the second is when a different proof is used. However, the term mu'aradah is primary. Defines two categories of jahl and their legal effect

Commentary
This passage from Nur al-Anwar discusses the legal categories of jahl (ignorance) and their effect on liability. The text distinguishes between two types of jahl in the context of ijtihad. The first type is ignorance that leads a mujtahid to oppose clear texts from the Quran and mutawatir sunnah. Such ignorance is not excusable, as it contradicts definitive evidence. Examples given include the fatwa of Dawud al-Isfahani permitting the sale of ummahat al-awlad (mother-slaves who have borne children to their master) based on a hadith of Jabir, which contradicts the famous hadith that declares such a slave is free upon the master's death. Another example is Imam al-Shafi'i's permissibility of judging by one witness and an oath, which contradicts the famous hadith 'The burden of proof is on the claimant, and the oath is on the denier.' The author notes he is merely reporting the statements of earlier scholars and does not presume to criticise such imams, as there may be valid interpretations. The second type is ignorance in areas of valid ijtihad (where there is no clear text) or in matters of shubha (doubt). This type can serve as a valid excuse, and the resulting shubha can avert the application of hadd (fixed punishments) and kaffara (expiation). An example is a fasting person who undergoes cupping and then intentionally breaks his fast under the impression that cupping breaks the fast. According to al-Awza'i, cupping does break the fast based on a hadith, so such a person is considered to have acted in a place of correct ijtihad, and thus no kaffara is due. However, if the person did not consult a jurist or was not aware of the hadith, then kaffara would be necessary because his assumption was unfounded. Another example is a person who commits zina with his father's slave girl, thinking she is lawful for him. Since there is a connection of property between father and son creating a shubha, the hadd is not applied. But if he knows she is unlawful, hadd applies. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that shubha repels hadd.

Key Terms
جَهْل (jahl): Lack of knowledge regarding a Shariah ruling; categorized as either excusable or inexcusable based on context.
مَوْضِع الاجْتِهَاد الصَّحِيح (mawdi' al-ijtihad al-sahih): An area where no clear text exists or where valid juristic reasoning can lead to multiple plausible rulings.
شُبْهَة (shubha): A doubt or semblance of permissibility that arises from conflicting evidence or ambiguous circumstances, sufficient to avert hadd.
