Deriving the 'Illah of Riba from the Hadith of Six Commodities
Deriving 'illah via isharat al-nass

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of istinbat to identify that the effective cause ('illah) for riba in same-genus sales is measure (qadr) and genus (jins), and evaluate the objection regarding quality using isharat al-nass.

Core Idea
Riba's cause is measure and genus, not quality – established by textual indication.

In this lesson, we examine how the Hanafi school derives the effective cause ('illah) of riba from the famous hadith of six commodities. The author of Nur al-Anwar guides us through a meticulous istinbat: from the obligation of equality to the requirement of similarity (mumathala), then to the two components — measure (qadr) and genus (jins). We also see how a potential objection about quality is resolved by the text itself. This is a classic example of Hanafi usul at work, demonstrating that the 'illah is not a product of mere reason but is extracted from the very phrasing of the nass.

Guided Path
1
Identify the textual command
The hadith uses the imperative 'bi'u' (sell). The author clarifies that it does not obligate selling, but obligates equality when selling. This is based on the usul principle that al-amr li al-wujub, but here context restricts the obligation to the condition of sale.
The amr is for wujub, but the hal (circumstantial qualifier) restricts it — a key Hanafi concept.
2
Analyze the requirement of mumathala
The phrase 'mithlan bi mithlin' requires exact similarity. The author argues that such similarity requires both formal similarity (by measure/weight) and conceptual similarity (by genus). The parallel narration 'kaylan bi kayl, waznan bi wazn' confirms that measure is central.
Mumathala is defined as equality in form (surah) and meaning (ma'na).
3
Locate the indication of genus and measure
Which part of the hadith indicates which? 'Al-hintatu bi al-hintati' indicates genus (jins); 'mithlan bi mithlin' indicates measure (qadr). This is an example of isharat al-nass – the text implicitly points to the 'illah without explicit statement.
Hanafis accept isharat al-nass as a valid source of 'illah; other schools may require explicit expression.
4
Apply the condition: if either is missing
If genus differs (wheat vs barley) or measure is absent (countable items like eggs or fruits sold by number), equality is not required and riba does not arise. This confirms that the 'illah is indeed qadr and jins.
This is a test of the 'illah: its absence should remove the ruling.
5
Address the quality objection
Objection: similarity should also require equality in quality (good vs bad). The answer is that the text itself drops quality via 'jiduha wa radiyuha sawa' (its good and its bad are equal). This shows the 'illah is anchored in the nass, not mere rational analogy.
This rebuttal prevents the need for a separate qiyas on quality.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed istinbat (derivation) of the effective cause ('illah) for riba from the famous hadith of six commodities. The author, commenting on the matn of Nur al-Anwar, first presents the hadith text with its variant readings, clarifying linguistic points: 'mithlan bi mithlin' is a hal (circumstantial qualifier) implying the condition of equality, and the command (amr) indicates obligation (wujub). However, the hadith does not make the sale obligatory; it only obligates equality when such a sale occurs. The author then establishes that the 'illah for the obligation of equality is two things: al-qadr (measure/weight) and al-jins (genus). This is derived by isharat al-nass (textual indication) from the requirement of mumathala (similarity) in the hadith: the command for equality in measure implies that the items must be exact similars, and similarity is achieved only through formal similarity (by measure) and conceptual similarity (by genus). The text 'al-hintatu bi al-hintati' indicates genus, and 'mithlan bi mithlin' indicates measure. If either is missing—different genus (e.g., wheat for barley) or different measure (countable items)—equality is not required and riba does not occur. The author then addresses an objection: why is quality (jooda/radaa) not considered? The response is that the value of quality is dropped by the nass itself: the hadith says 'its good and its bad are equal.' This is a key Hanafi usul point: the 'illah is not merely rational but established by textual indication. The passage concludes by distinguishing between the ruling itself (obligation of equality) and the ruling about the 'illah (that the cause is measure and genus), which together are the full import of the nass.

Key Terms
علة (illah): The effective cause (ratio legis) for a ruling, derived from textual indication (isharat al-nass) rather than mere ra'y.
مماثلة (mumathala): Complete similarity between two items in form (by measure/weight) and meaning (by genus).
قدر (qadr): The standardized measure or weight (kayl/wazn) that determines formal similarity.
جنس (jins): The genus or species of the commodity (e.g., wheat, barley, gold).

Proving the Condition of Witnesses in Marriage (Usul Analysis)
Textual analysis of a hadith used to prove a shart

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of proving a shart from a hadith (dalil naqli) and evaluate the opposing Maliki position.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school proves the condition of witnesses in marriage from the explicit text 'La nikaha illa bi-shahidayn'.

In Islamic legal theory, some conditions (shurut) are established by explicit textual evidence rather than rational analogy. This lesson explores the Hanafi proof for the requirement of witnesses in marriage and the ikhtilaf with the Maliki school. Understanding this derivation strengthens your ability to handle textual evidence in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith and its wording
Locate the exact hadith cited: 'La nikaha illa bi-shahidayn' – There is no marriage except with two witnesses.
The hadith is a single-transmission (khabar wahid) but accepted by the Hanafi school.
2
Analyze the linguistic indicator (sighah)
The phrase 'la nikaha' is a negation (nafi) that, according to Hanafi usul, indicates absolute negation of validity – i.e., the contract is non-existent without the condition.
In usul, 'la' in this context functions as 'la li-nafi al-jins' which makes the predicate general.
3
Derive the ruling (hukm)
From the negation, the Hanafi school concludes that witnesses are a necessary condition (shart) for the validity of the marriage contract. Without them, the marriage is invalid (batil).
This is a direct derivation from the zahir of the text – no qiyas is needed.
4
Contrast with the Maliki position
Imam Malik uses the hadith 'A'linu al-nikah' (announce the marriage) to argue that publicity, not witnesses, is the condition. The Hanafi response is that the two hadiths are not contradictory; publicity is recommended but does not replace the condition of witnesses.
The thamarah al-khilaf: In Hanafi, marriage without witnesses is invalid; in Maliki, it is valid if publicized.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the rulings of travel (safar). It begins by defining safar as a long journey away from one's place of residence, with a minimum distance of three days' travel. The key point is that travel does not remove one's legal capacity (ahliyyah); the traveler remains fully obligated to perform acts of worship, but concessions are granted due to the inherent hardship of travel. Unlike illness, which varies in its effect, travel is treated as a cause of hardship as a legal fiction (hukman), so concessions apply regardless of whether actual hardship is felt. Specifically, travel affects the shortening of four-rak'ah prayers and allows postponing the fasting of Ramadan. However, a critical ruling is mentioned: if a person begins the day as a fasting resident or traveler and then travels, he may not break his fast because the obligation was already established and the travel is voluntary. This is a concession to benefit all travelers.

Rukhsah for Travel Established by Sunnah Despite Incomplete Cause
Explains why travel concessions begin upon leaving the city even before completing three days' journey

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi principle that travel concessions are established by Sunnah immediately upon leaving, and apply this to distinguish when Sunnah overrides qiyas in rukhsah.

Core Idea
Sunnah grants travel concessions immediately; qiyas would require three days' journey.

This passage from a Hanafi commentary tackles a key usul issue: when does a traveler's concession (rukhsah) begin? While strict qiyas suggests the full cause (three days' travel) must be complete, the well-known Sunnah of the Prophet (peace be upon him) shows that concessions begin as soon as one leaves the built-up area. This illustrates how Sunnah can establish a ruling even when the complete legal cause is not yet present.

Guided Path
1
Identify the complete cause (illa tammah) for travel according to qiyas
In Hanafi usul, the complete cause that triggers travel concessions is a journey of three days. Qiyas would suggest that no concession applies before this cause is fulfilled.
The complete cause is defined as three days with ease (bi al-maysarah).
2
Examine the Sunnah evidence
The Prophet (peace be upon him) consistently granted concessions to travelers as soon as they left the inhabited area (umran al-misr). This practice is reported as a well-known Sunnah.
The key phrase: 'حِينَ يَخْرُجُ مِنْ عُمْرَانِ الْمِصْرِ' (when he leaves the inhabited area).
3
Apply the Hanafi usul principle: Sunnah overrides qiyas for rukhsah
The Hanafi school holds that when a clear Sunnah practice establishes a concession, it takes precedence over analogical reasoning. This is based on the principle of taysir (facilitation) and the fact that delaying the concession until the complete cause would defeat its purpose for many travelers.
This is a form of istihsan—juristic preference for ease.
4
Distinguish travel from sickness regarding breaking fast
Travel is volitional (mukhtar), so a traveler who begins fasting cannot break unless compelled. Sickness is involuntary (samawi), so a sick person may break after commencement. This distinction supports the general principle that rukhsah attaches differently to volitional vs. involuntary causes.
The passage uses this comparison to highlight that travel concessions are granted even without completion of the cause.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with usul reasoning from a Hanafi fiqh commentary. It distinguishes between travel and sickness as causes for breaking the fast after commencement. For a traveler who was already traveling or a resident who travels after beginning the fast, breaking is not allowed because travel is voluntary and does not compel breaking. For a sick person, however, breaking is allowed because sickness is beyond human control and provides a legitimate excuse. The passage then addresses expiation (kaffarah): if a traveler breaks in these cases, the presence of travel creates a doubt (shubhah) that prevents expiation. For a resident who travels after breaking, expiation remains because the travel was avoidable; but if one becomes sick after breaking, expiation is dropped because sickness is heavenly. Finally, it discusses an usul point: the travel concessions (like breaking fast or shortening prayer) become effective immediately upon leaving the city, based on the Sunnah, even though the full cause of travel (three days) is not yet complete. Qiyas would demand the cause be complete, but the Sunnah establishes the concession earlier to benefit all travelers. This illustrates the Hanafi principle of istihsan (juristic preference) in applying rukhsah.
