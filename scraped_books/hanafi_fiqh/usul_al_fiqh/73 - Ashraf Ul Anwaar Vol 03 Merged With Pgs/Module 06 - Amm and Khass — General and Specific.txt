Majhul Narrator: Classification and the Case of Fatima bint Qays
Classification of majhul narrators and example of Fatima bint Qays

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the fourth and fifth types of majhul narrators in Hanafi usul and apply the rule to the hadith of Fatima bint Qays.

Core Idea
A narrator unknown among the Salaf whose hadith was only rejected is unacceptable; if neither accepted nor rejected, it is permissible but not obligatory.

In Hanafi usul al-hadith, classifying narrators whose reliability is unknown (majhul) requires examining the reaction of the Salaf (early scholars). This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar distinguishes two key types: those whose hadith was met only with rejection, and those whose hadith was unknown altogether. The famous case of Fatima bint Qays illustrates how Umar's rejection with Sahaba consensus invalidates her narration, while a narration with no Salaf reaction may still be acted upon under conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of majhul narrators
Locate the descriptions of the fourth and fifth types: the fourth is when the Salaf only rejected the hadith; the fifth is when the hadith neither appeared nor was rejected or accepted.
The fourth type is explicitly linked to the Fatima bint Qays example.
2
Understand the condition for the fifth type
For the fifth type, acting upon the hadith is permissible but not obligatory, provided it does not contradict qiyas (analogy).
The phrase 'بشرط إن لم يكن مخالفا للقياس' is key.
3
Examine the Fatima bint Qays example
See how Umar's rejection and the Sahaba's silence (consensus) made the hadith unacceptable (munkar).
Umar explicitly stated they would not leave the Quran and Sunnah for her word.
4
Analyze the Hanafi position using the dalil
The Hanafi response relies on Quranic verses (65:1, 2:241) and the sunnah as narrated by Umar, which contradict Fatima's hadith.
This shows that rejection by Salaf plus stronger evidence makes a narrator unacceptable.
5
Distinguish permissibility from obligation
For the fifth type, acting is permissible (ja'iz) but not wajib. This distinguishes it from a sahih hadith which must be followed.
This nuance is important for fatwa and ijtihad.

Commentary
This passage discusses two types of unknown (majhul) narrators in Hanafi usul al-hadith. The fourth type is a narrator about whom nothing appears from the Salaf except rejection; such a narrator's report is not accepted. The example given is the hadith of Fatima bint Qays, which was rejected by Umar and the majority of Sahaba. The fifth type is a narrator unknown among the Salaf, whose narration was neither accepted nor rejected; acting upon it is permissible but not obligatory, provided it does not contradict analogy. The text also compares the positions of Ali and the Hanafi scholars regarding the hadith of Ma'qil ibn Sinan, emphasizing the preference for hadith over analogy. The author concludes that attributing the ruling to the hadith strengthens its authority against opponents.

Key Terms
المجهول (al-majhul): A narrator whose reliability is unknown because he is not well-known among scholars.
وإن لم يظهر (wa in lam yazhar): A condition for a majhul narrator: if his hadith did not appear among the Salaf (either accepted or rejected), then it is permissible to act upon it but not obligatory.
الرد (ar-radd): Rejection of a narrator's hadith by the Salaf, which makes the narrator unacceptable.

The Four Subtypes of Internal Defect: Contradiction with Stronger Evidence
Explains internal defect due to hadith contradicting stronger sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of internal defects (khalal bil-'arad) in hadith according to Hanafi usul and apply the rule that such hadith are rejected or reinterpreted.

Core Idea
A hadith contradicting Quran, well-known sunnah, established practice, or early consensus is defective and must be rejected.

Not every authentic-looking hadith is acceptable. Hanafi usul teaches that a hadith may be internally defective if it contradicts a stronger source. This lesson unpacks the four subcategories of such contradiction, using concrete examples from the text. You will learn to recognise when a hadith's content, not its chain, renders it mardud.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defect type
Recognise that the passage discusses 'khalal bil-'arad'—a defect arising from the hadith contradicting a stronger evidence.
This is the second major category of internal defect; the first was defect in the sanad.
2
List the four subcategories
The author lists four situations: contradiction with (1) the Quran, (2) a well-known sunnah, (3) a famous incident, and (4) what early imams disregarded.
Each subcategory represents a stronger source that overrides the hadith.
3
Analyse the Hanafi criterion
Understand that the Quran and well-known sunnah are qat'i (decisive), so ahad hadith cannot oppose them.
This is a key principle: zanni cannot override qat'i.
4
Apply the criterion to examples
Work through each example: Fatiha hadith vs Quranic generality, witness/oath hadith vs well-known sunnah, Bismillah hadith vs continuous practice, and zakat on orphan's wealth vs early disregard.
Notice the pattern: the stronger source is always more widely transmitted or established.
5
State the outcome
The passage concludes that such hadith are 'mardud' (rejected) and 'munqati' (disconnected).
Rejection is not always absolute; interpretation (ta'wil) may resolve the contradiction.

Commentary
This passage details the second type of internal defect: a hadith contradicting a stronger proof (دليل فائق). The author lists four subcategories: (1) contradiction with the Quran, e.g., the hadith requiring Fatiha for prayer opposes the general command to recite from Quran; (2) contradiction with a well-known sunnah, e.g., the hadith allowing judgment with one witness and oath opposes the well-known principle that proof is on the claimant; (3) contradiction with a famous incident, e.g., the hadith that Prophet said Bismillah aloud contradicts the continuous practice witnessed by thousands; (4) when early imams (Companions) disregard the hadith, as they did with zakat on orphan's wealth despite a hadith about it. In each case, the hadith is rejected (mardud) and considered disconnected (munqati'). The Hanafi position is clear: such opposition indicates the hadith is not authentic, or it is interpreted away.

Key Terms
خلل بالعرض (khalal bil-'arad): A defect arising from the hadith contradicting a stronger evidence, not from the narrator itself.
عموم (umum): Generality of a text that includes all individuals; here referring to Quranic command to recite what is easy.
سنة معروفة (sunnah ma'rufah): A well-known and widely accepted sunnah, often transmitted by many companions.
حديث مردود (hadith mardud): A rejected hadith due to internal defect such as contradiction with stronger evidence.

Narrator's Selective Practice Does Not Bind Other Mujtahids
Explains the non-binding nature of narrator's selective practice

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that a narrator's personal practice of one valid interpretation does not invalidate other interpretations of the same hadith.

Core Idea
A narrator's ijtihad is not a proof against other valid interpretations.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi usul principle: the narrator of a hadith may act on one of its multiple possible meanings, but that does not bar other mujtahids from adopting a different meaning. The example of Ibn Umar and the khiyar al-majlis hadith illustrates how the Hanafi school maintains flexibility in understanding textual possibilities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its scope
The rule states: 'The narrator's practice of some of its possibilities does not prevent acting [on another].' This applies when a hadith has multiple equally valid linguistic interpretations (muhtamalat).
The rule protects the independence of ijtihad from being overridden by a narrator's personal choice.
2
Analyze the given example
The hadith: 'The two parties in a sale have the option as long as they have not separated (mā lam yatafarraqā).' The word tafarruq can mean physical separation (majāz or haqīqa?) or separation by words. Ibn Umar (the narrator) practiced physical separation, but the Hanafi school adopted separation by words.
The example demonstrates that the narrator's own practice does not make other interpretations invalid.
3
Derive the usul principle
The narrator's action is his own ijtihad, not an additional transmitted proof (dalil). Therefore, it does not restrict the meaning of the hadith to one possibility.
This principle supports the Hanafi view that a hadith may carry multiple legal rulings depending on ijtihad.
4
Contrast with potential opposing views
Some scholars (e.g., Shafi'is) may give weight to the narrator's practice as an indication of the intended meaning, but Hanafis argue it is not binding unless it amounts to tafsīr or consensus.
The difference affects how much authority a narrator's personal action carries in interpretation.

Commentary
This passage discusses the principle that a narrator's own practice of one interpretation of a hadith that carries multiple valid possibilities does not preclude others from acting on a different interpretation. The example given is the well-known hadith of khiyar al-majlis (option of the session) narrated by Ibn Umar. The hadith states that the buyer and seller have the option until they separate (mā lam yatafarraqā). The word tafarruq can mean either physical separation (tafarruq al-abdān) or separation by words (tafarruq al-aqwāl). Ibn Umar himself practiced the interpretation of physical separation, but the Hanafi school adopts the interpretation of separation by words (i.e., one of them saying 'I have sold' or 'I have bought'). Ibn Umar's personal practice does not invalidate the alternative interpretation. This is because the narrator's action is not a proof (ḥujja) that nullifies other possibilities; it is simply his own ijtihad. The passage is clarifying that a narrator's selective practice does not constitute criticism (ṭaʿn) of the hadith or prevent acting on other valid understandings.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of ta'āruḍ (contradiction) between legal evidences from a Hanafi perspective. The author begins by clarifying that apparent contradiction between proofs arises only due to our ignorance of abrogation; in reality, no contradiction exists in divine speech. The core definition (rukn) of ta'āruḍ is that two evidences are perfectly equal, with no preference (maziyyah) in either essence (dhat) or attribute (sifah). Therefore, no real contradiction exists between texts of different levels of clarity, such as mufassar and muhkam, or between explicit meaning ('ibarah) and implied meaning (isharah), because one is stronger in attribute. Similarly, no contradiction exists between a mashhur hadith and an ahad hadith, nor between a specific (khass) text and a general ('amm) text that has been partially specified, because one is stronger in essence. True ta'āruḍ requires three conditions: (1) unity of the locus (mahall) — the same subject; (2) unity of time (waqt); and (3) opposition of rulings (tadadd al-hukm) — e.g., one says permissible, the other says forbidden. If any condition is missing, it is not ta'āruḍ. Examples: marriage makes the wife permissible and the mother-in-law forbidden—different loci, so no contradiction. Wine was permissible initially then forbidden—different times, so no contradiction. Also, if rulings are not opposite (e.g., both recommendatory), there is no contradiction. Some scholars also add unity of relation (nisbah), as permissibility of a wife is relative to her husband, while prohibition is relative to others. This passage serves as a foundational usul definition for understanding how Hanafi jurists handle conflicting evidences.

Three Methods of Resolving Apparent Contradiction in Evidences
Explaining the three ways to resolve ta'arud between two proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three Hanafi methods of resolving apparent contradiction (ta'arud) between two textual evidences (tarjih, tatbiq by ruling type, tatbiq by condition) and apply each method to given examples.

Core Idea
When two evidences appear contradictory, a Hanafi usuli first seeks reconciliation (tatbiq) before abrogation (naskh).

In this passage, the author explains the three 'deliverances' (mukhlis) from apparent contradiction between two proofs. Understanding these methods is essential for Hanafi jurists to harmonize texts and derive rulings without recourse to abrogation unless necessary. The passage illustrates each method with concrete Quranic examples, showing how apparent conflicts are resolved by considering preference, type of ruling, or factual condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two seemingly contradictory texts
Locate the two evidences that appear to conflict. Note that they are both authoritative (e.g., both are Quranic verses).
In this lesson, the examples are two verses about oaths (2:225 and 5:89) and two recitations of 2:222.
2
Determine if the texts are equal in strength
If one text is superior in transmission (e.g., nass vs zahir, mutawatir vs ahad), apply tarjih (preference) and follow the stronger. Only if they are equal proceed to the next step.
Tarjih is the first method mentioned. The other methods assume equality.
3
Consider reconciliation by type of ruling
If the texts can be interpreted as addressing different domains (e.g., worldly vs otherworldly accountability), carry each to its appropriate domain. This resolves the contradiction without abrogation.
The oath verses example: one verse is carried on worldly expiation, the other on otherworldly sin.
4
Consider reconciliation by condition
If the texts can be applied to different factual circumstances, carry each to its specific condition. The apparent contradiction disappears when the contexts are distinguished.
The menstruation recitation example: one recitation applies when menses ends after ten days, the other when it ends before ten.
5
Derive the fiqh ruling
Apply the reconciled understanding to determine the legal ruling in concrete cases, respecting the domain or condition specified.
For the false oath: sin but no expiation; for menstruation: ghusl is required only if bleeding stops before ten days.

Commentary
This passage explains the principle of resolving apparent contradictions between two textual evidences (ta'arud al-adillah) from a Hanafi usul perspective. The author presents three methods of reconciliation, termed 'mukhlis' (deliverance).

1. **Preference (tarjih)**: When two evidences are not equal in strength—one being more widely transmitted (mashhur) or from solitary reports (khabar wahid) while the other is a clear text (nass) or apparent (zahir)—the stronger is preferred over the weaker. This is a standard Hanafi method of resolving conflicts.

2. **By type of ruling (hukm)**: The two texts are interpreted to refer to different types of rulings—one concerning worldly legal consequences and the other concerning otherworldly accountability. The example given is the two verses about oaths: Surah al-Baqarah (2:225) states accountability for what the hearts have earned, implying expiation for a deliberate oath. Surah al-Ma'idah (5:89) mentions accountability only for deliberate oaths, excluding the false oath (ghamus). The apparent contradiction is resolved by understanding the al-Baqarah verse as referring to the sin (otherworldly) and the al-Ma'idah verse as referring to the expiation (worldly). Thus, a false oath incurs sin but not expiation. This is the Hanafi position, as opposed to Imam Shafi'i who considers intent necessary for accountability.

3. **By condition (hal)**: The two texts are applied to different factual circumstances. The example is the two recitations of 'hatta yathurna' in the verse prohibiting intercourse with menstruating women (Quran 2:222). One recitation (takhfif) means 'until they become pure' (i.e., cessation of blood), and the other (tashdid) means 'until they bathe (i.e., perform ghusl)'. The Hanafi reconciliation: If menstruation ends after ten days (maximum duration of menses), cessation alone suffices; if it ends in less than ten days, the woman must bathe or wait for a complete prayer time to pass to ensure purity. This illustrates the principle of applying each text to different situations.

Key Terms
المخلص (al-mukhlis): That which delivers from the contradiction between two evidences, i.e., a method of reconciliation.
الغموس (al-ghamus): A false oath sworn intentionally, knowing it is false; it is a major sin.
التطبيق (al-tatbiq): Reconciliation between two apparent contradictory texts by assigning them to different contexts or rulings.
المعارضة (al-mu'arada): Apparent contradiction between two textual evidences.
الترجيح (al-tarjih): Preferring one evidence over another due to its greater strength (e.g., nass over zahir).

Resolving Ta'āruḍ by Carrying to Different States (al-Ṭaṭbīq)
Explains the method of taṭbīq for reconciling contradictory Quranic texts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of al-ṭaṭbīq to resolve apparent contradictions between two Quranic texts or variant readings by identifying the distinct situations each applies to.

Core Idea
When two texts contradict, carry each to a different situation — reconciliation is better than abrogation.

In Hanafi usul, when two textual proofs appear contradictory, the preferred method is to reconcile them by assigning each to a distinct context or state (ḥāl). This lesson unpacks that method through two vivid examples from the Quran: the treatment of the false oath (yamīn ghamūs) and the variant readings of the menstruation verse. You will learn how to identify the operative difference that resolves the apparent conflict.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent contradiction
Look at the two texts or readings. What exactly seems to conflict? In the ghamūs example, one verse (2:225) seems to indicate punishment, the other (5:89) indicates expiation—do both apply? In the qirā'āt example, one reading says purification by cessation of blood, the other says by washing—which is required?
Contradiction must be real, not merely apparent.
2
Determine the possibility of taṭbīq
Ask: can each text be assigned to a different state without arbitrariness? For ghamūs: one verse is for worldly punishment (expiation), the other for otherworldly punishment (sin). For qirā'āt: the lightened reading applies when menstruation lasts ten days; the emphasized applies when it lasts less than ten days.
If taṭbīq is possible, it is obligatory (wājib) over naskh or tarjīḥ.
3
Apply the principle to the given examples
For ghamūs: carry verse 2:225 to otherworldly sin, and verse 5:89 to worldly expiation → no expiation in this world, sin in the next. For qirā'āt: if menstruation stops after ten days (maximum), mere cessation permits intercourse; if less than ten days, ghusl is required before intercourse.
The justification relies on the nature of menstruation and the possibility of return of blood.
4
Address the objection (iʿtirāḍ)
Objection: the later verse 'fa-idhā taṭahharna' (2:222) uses the emphatic form, suggesting ghusl is always required. The author offers two responses: either it indicates recommendation (istihbāb), not obligation; or the emphatic form is interpreted like the lightened form as a linguistic variant.
The Hanafi response preserves the taṭbīq without rejecting the emphatic reading.
5
Conclude the rule
Al-ṭaṭbīq is a preferred Hanafi method; when possible, it allows both proofs to remain operative without discarding one. This reflects the principle that sharīʿah rulings are situation-dependent.
Impossibility of taṭbīq leads to naskh or tarjīḥ.

Preference for the Prohibiting Text over the Permitting Text
Explaining why a prohibiting text takes precedence over a permitting text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for conflicting mubih and muhrim texts and explain why the prohibiting text takes precedence using the principle of avoiding repeated naskh.

Core Idea
When mubih and muhrim conflict, act on muhrim to avoid repeated naskh.

How do we resolve a conflict between a text that permits an action and a text that prohibits it? The Hanafi school has a clear rule: give precedence to the prohibiting text. This lesson unpacks the logical reasoning behind this preference, based on the foundational principle that the original state of things is permissibility (ibaha aṣliyya), and the need to avoid unnecessary multiple abrogations. You will learn the argument and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting texts
Recognize a text that indicates permissibility (mubih) and a text that indicates prohibition (muhrim). Assume both are authentic and apply to the same action.
If one is earlier and one later, timing may indicate naskh; but here we consider a scenario where sequence is uncertain or both are equally authoritative.
2
Recall the Hanafi premise: original permissibility
According to the majority Hanafi view, the original ruling for all things is permissibility (ibaha) unless evidence shows otherwise. This is based on Quranic verses like 'Khalaqa lakum ma fi al-ard jami'an' (2:29).
Alternative views exist: some hold prohibition or suspension as original; this rule depends on the chosen premise.
3
Analyze the two possible orders
Consider what happens if we act on the mubih first versus the muhrim first. Acting on mubih leads to multiple abrogations (naskh): the muhrim abrogates original ibaha, then mubih would need to abrogate muhrim again. Acting on muhrim avoids this: mubih aligns with original ibaha, and muhrim abrogates both permissions in one step.
Multiple abrogations are considered unreasonable (ghayr ma'qul).
4
Conclude: precedence of muhrim
Therefore, to avoid unreasonable multiple abrogations, we must give priority to the prohibiting text. This rule is a major principle (asl kabir) with many applications.
This conclusion holds under the presumption of original permissibility.
5
Acknowledge alternative foundational views
Note that scholars differ on the original state: some say prohibition, others suspension. The rule changes if one adopts those views. But the practical outcome in many cases remains similar because prohibition texts often abrogate permission texts.
The author mentions these alternatives but affirms the Hanafi position.

Commentary
This passage presents a Hanafi usul rule for dealing with two conflicting texts: one permitting (mubih) and one prohibiting (muhrim). The author argues that when both texts are present and cannot both be applied literally, we must act on the prohibiting text. The reasoning is based on avoiding unnecessary multiple abrogations (naskh). The original state of things according to most Hanafis is permissibility (ibaha). If we act on the prohibiting text, the permitting text aligns with original ibaha, so we have two indicators of permissibility (original and textual). Then the prohibiting text abrogates both simultaneously, which is a single understandable abrogation. If we act on the permitting text first, then the prohibiting text abrogates the original ibaha, and then to give effect to the permitting text, it would have to abrogate the prohibiting text again, leading to two abrogations, which is unreasonable. Therefore, the rule is to act on the muhrim. The author notes that this rule is based on the view that ibaha is the original principle, and acknowledges alternative views (prohibition as original, or suspension).

Key Terms
المُبِيح (al-mubīḥ): A text (from Quran or Sunnah) that indicates permissibility (ibaha) of an action.
المُحْرِم (al-muḥrim): A text that indicates prohibition (hurma) of an action.
النَّسْخ (al-naskh): Abrogation of a ruling by a later revelation or evidence.
الإباحة الأصلية (al-ibāḥa al-aṣliyya): The presumptive ruling of permissibility for all things in the absence of a prohibiting text.

Refuting Delayed Takhsis: Cow and Nuh Stories
Hanafi defense of the rule that takhsis cannot be delayed.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi argument against delayed takhsis by distinguishing takhsis from taqyid al-mutlaq and bayan, and apply this distinction to Quranic counterexamples.

Core Idea
Delayed specification of a general term is invalid; apparent counterexamples are actually taqyid or bayan.

In this lesson, we defend the Hanafi principle that specification of a general term (takhsis al-'amm) cannot occur after a delay. Two Quranic objections—the cow of Bani Israel and the son of Nuh—are examined and reinterpreted. The Hanafi response demonstrates that these cases are not delayed takhsis but rather taqyid al-mutlaq (restriction of an absolute) or bayan (clarification of meaning). By mastering this analysis, you internalize the Hanafi usul framework for reconciling apparently conflicting texts.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi rule against delayed takhsis
Recall that 'amm is qat'i in its application, so any specification must be immediate. Delayed specification is invalid.
This is a core Hanafi usul position opposed by Shafi'is.
2
Analyze the first objection: the cow story
The initial command 'slaughter a cow' is a nakira in an affirmative context—it is mutlaq (absolute) not 'amm (general). Later details restrict attributes, which is taqyid, not takhsis.
Taqyid of mutlaq is permissible with delay, similar to naskh.
3
Analyze the second objection: Nuh's family
The word 'ahl' (family) in the command is interpreted by Hanafis as those who follow in religion, not blood relatives. Thus the son was never included; the later verse clarifies the intended meaning (bayan), not a delayed specification.
The exception 'except those against whom the word has preceded' is a confirmatory bayan.
4
Address the counter-objection about the exception
An opponent argues that if 'ahl' did not include the son, why the exception? The Hanafi response: Nuh's ignorance due to affection led him to ask; Allah's reply clarifies that the son was never part of 'ahl' religiously.
This shows the exception is for clarification, not specification.
5
Conclude why the Hanafi position remains intact
Both objections are resolved: one is taqyid, the other is bayan. No valid counterexample to the rule against delayed takhsis exists.
The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafis require immediate application of specifications; Shafi'is allow delay.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi defense of the usul principle that specification of a general term (takhsis al-'amm) cannot be delayed. The matn states the rule; here the commentator addresses two counterexamples from the Quran.

First objection: The story of the cow (Baqara 67-71). The command was initially general ('a cow'), then details were given later. Hanafi response: This is not takhsis but taqyid al-mutlaq (restricting an absolute). The word 'cow' is a nakira in an affirmative context, which is specific for one individual but absolute in attributes. The later details restrict the absolute, not specify a general. This is like naskh (abrogation), which is allowed with delay.

Second objection: Prophet Nuh's command to take his family (al-Hud 40-46). 'Family' is general, and later his son is excluded. Hanafi response: The word 'ahl' (family) never included the son in its linguistic meaning here—family means those who follow in religion, not blood relatives. So the later verse is not a delayed specification but a clarification of the true meaning of 'ahl'. However, an interlocutor might object: if 'ahl' didn't include the son, why did Allah say 'except those against whom the word has preceded'? That implies the son was included but excepted. The commentator notes that Nuh's ignorance due to fatherly love led him to ask, and Allah corrected him, showing the son was never part of 'ahl'. So the exception was for clarification, not specification.

النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation of a previous ruling by a later one; in the passage, the cow story is compared to naskh because naskh must be delayed.

Commentary
This passage is an extended commentary (sharh) on a matn discussing the usul principle of istithna (exception). The author first clarifies a previous point about the word 'ma' and the exclusion of prophets from its generality, then transitions to the main topic: the function of istithna. The author states the Hanafi position: istithna prevents the speech from covering the excepted part from the very beginning. In other words, when a speaker says 'I have one thousand dirhams except one hundred,' he is considered to have said 'I have nine hundred dirhams'; the excepted amount is never part of the speech's scope. This is based on the idea that exception is a specification (takhsis) of the general word. The author then presents the Shafi'i view: istithna operates as a counteraction (mu'arada). The excepted item is initially included in the ruling (e.g., the thousand is owed), but then the exception contradicts that ruling, causing both to fall away (tasqut). A practical difference emerges when the exception is of a different category (e.g., a garment instead of dirhams). According to the Hanafis, such an exception is invalid because it cannot serve as a correct specification; the entire amount remains owed. According to the Shafi'is, it is valid, and the value of the garment is deducted from the total. The author then critiques the Shafi'i view using two arguments. First, the consensus of linguists: exception from negation is affirmation, and from affirmation is negation. This shows that exception directly operates on the ruling, not as a counteraction. Second, the example of 'La ilaha illa Allah' is cited. If exception were mere specification (as Hanafis hold), the phrase would only negate other gods, not affirm Allah. But if it is counteraction, as the Shafi'is claim, then the phrase simultaneously negates and affirms, achieving the intended tawhid. However, the author notes that this reasoning has a flaw ('khada shah'). Then the Hanafis present their own proof from the Quran: the story of Noah. Allah says 'He lived among them a thousand years except fifty years.' If we interpret this as counteraction, it would mean both the thousand and the fifty are invalidated, resulting in a lie. Therefore, the correct interpretation is that the exception subtracts from the thousand, leaving nine hundred fifty. This proves that exception operates as specification (takhsis), not counteraction. The passage concludes by asserting the Hanafi position.

Key Terms
استثناء (istithna): A linguistic device that excludes a part of a general statement, e.g., 'A thousand except one hundred' means nine hundred. In usul, it is a form of takhsis (specification) that prevents the general word from covering the excepted part from the outset.
معارضة (mu'arada): Counteraction or opposition between two conflicting pieces of evidence or statements. In the context of istithna, it means the initial ruling and the exception contradict each other and nullify both.

The Legal Nature of Istithna: Extraction vs. Opposition
Dispute over the legal effect of istithna

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that istithna operates by extraction (ikhraj) and contrast it with the Shafi'i position of opposition (mu'aradah), using Quranic and linguistic evidence.

Core Idea
Istithna extracts the excepted item from the ruling; it does not create opposition that nullifies the statement.

This lesson examines a foundational dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools of legal theory (usul al-fiqh): what is the legal effect of an exception (istithna)? Is it merely extracting the excepted item from the general ruling, leaving the remainder intact? Or does it create a mutual opposition (mu'aradah) that nullifies both parts? The answer determines how we interpret key texts like the Shahada and contractual statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two competing theories
Read the passage and note that the Hanafi school views istithna as extraction (istikhraj), while the Shafi'i school views it as opposition (mu'aradah). Understand what each theory implies for the legal ruling.
Extraction: the excepted item is removed, remainder stays. Opposition: both parts cancel out.
2
Examine the Hanafi proof from the Quranic verse
Analyze the verse about Prophet Nuh (29:14): 'a thousand years except fifty.' Work through how interpreting this as opposition would make the statement false, supporting extraction.
If istithna were opposition, the verse would claim both 'a thousand years' and 'not a thousand years' simultaneously.
3
Analyze the linguistic evidence and Hanafi reconciliation
The linguists say istithna is extraction, but also that from negation it affirms and from affirmation it negates. The Hanafis reconcile by treating the remainder as expressed (ibarah) and the excepted as implied (isarrah).
This reconciliation is key to understanding how 'la ilaha illa Allah' works without mu'aradah.
4
Apply the theories to the specific case of different genus exception
Consider a statement: 'I owe a thousand dirhams except a garment.' Note the practical consequence: Hanafis invalidate the exception; Shafi'is validate it and deduct the garment's value.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).
5
Understand the types of istithna: muttasil and munfasil
Distinguish between connected exception (muttasil, same genus) and disconnected exception (munfasil, different genus). Hanafis treat munfasil as metaphorical, not true istithna.
The verse of Ibrahim (enemy to me except the Lord) is an example of munqati' (disconnected) exception.

Commentary
This passage discusses the nature of istithna (exception) in usul al-fiqh, focusing on whether it operates by way of mu'aradah (opposition) or ikhraj (extraction). The Hanafi position, following Imam Abu Hanifa, holds that istithna is a form of extraction or exclusion, meaning the excepted item is removed from the ruling, leaving the remainder in force. The Shafi'i view, attributed to Imam al-Shafi'i, treats istithna as a form of mu'aradah, causing the entire sentence to be contradictory and thus both parts to fall away. The author begins by noting the difference regarding the phrase 'la ilaha illa Allah'. If understood as mu'aradah, it would mean 'there is no god except Allah, for He exists' – which is strained. The Hanafi proof from the Quranic verse about Prophet Nuh (a thousand years except fifty) shows that understanding istithna as mu'aradah would lead to falsehood in historical reports. Since istithna is used in reports, it cannot be mu'aradah because that would render the statement false. The Shafi'i view only applies to obligations (ijab), not to reports (akhbar). Thus, we learn that istithna is extraction, not opposition. The author then discusses a specific case: if someone says 'I owe a thousand dirhams except a garment', Hanafis consider this invalid because the genus differs, while Shafi'is validate it and estimate the garment's value – this is a fruit of the dispute. The linguistic argument is presented: people of language say istithna is extraction, and from negation it yields affirmation, from affirmation negation. These two aspects seem contradictory, but Hanafis reconcile by saying that istithna is 'ibarah (expression) for the remainder and 'isarrah (implication) for the excepted item; thus the remainder is expressed directly, while the excepted item is implied. The example of tawhid is addressed: in 'la ilaha illa Allah', the negation of other gods is the objective, but the existence of Allah is already acknowledged by polytheists, so the istithna affirms it; thus extraction works. Finally, the author categorizes istithna into muttasil (connected, where the excepted item is of the same genus and extractable from the beginning) and munfasil (disconnected, where the excepted item is of a different genus, considered a new independent statement). The Quranic verse of Ibrahim ('enemy to me except the Lord of the worlds') is given as an example of munqati‘ istithna. The passage reaffirms Hanafi methodology and contrasts with Shafi'i.

Key Terms
استثناء (istithna): A grammatical construction using 'illa' or similar to exclude something from the general rule.
معارضة (mu'aradah): Mutual opposition that causes both parts to fall.
استخراج (istikhraj): Extraction; the excepted item is taken out of the ruling.
متصل (muttasil): Connected exception: the excepted item is of the same genus and could be part of the general term.
منفصل / منقطع (munfasil / munqati'): Disconnected exception: the excepted item is of a different genus and cannot be extracted; considered a new beginning.

Istithna (Exception): Definition, Types, and Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Explains istithna, its types, and the Hanafi-Shafi'i interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of istithna, explain the Hanafi position that istithna primarily expresses the remainder, and apply the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute to a practical example.

Core Idea
Istithna primarily indicates the remainder; affirmation/negation is secondary.

In this lesson, we delve into the linguistic device of istithna (exception) as used in Arabic speech and its application in usul al-fiqh. The Hanafi school holds a nuanced view: istithna is fundamentally an expression of the remainder after exclusion, while the resulting affirmation or negation is only an implied meaning. This distinguishes them from the Shafi'i school, which sees istithna as directly affirming or negating the predicate. We will explore the linguistic evidence, the analogy to ghaya (limit), and the practical consequences of this difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two linguistic definitions
Linguists give two definitions: (1) istithna is extraction and speaking of the remainder; (2) istithna from negation is affirmation, from affirmation is negation. Recognise the apparent contradiction.
The first definition focuses on the remainder; the second focuses on the effect of the exception on the predicate.
2
Apply the Hanafi reconciliation
The Hanafis reconcile by assigning the first definition as the primary meaning (ibarah) and the second as an implied meaning (isharah). Understand why the reverse is not possible, using the analogy of ghaya (limit).
Istithna, like ghaya, marks what is not intended from the beginning; thus, speaking of the remainder is the intended sense.
3
Distinguish muttasil and munfasil istithna
Muttasil (connected) is the original form where the excepted item is of the same genus and can be extracted logically. Munfasil (disconnected) occurs when the excepted item is of a different genus, making it a new independent statement; applying the term istithna to it is metaphorical.
Example of munfasil: Quran 26:77 'Except the Lord of the worlds' where Allah is not part of the idols.
4
Analyse the practical dispute
Consider the statement: 'Zayd owes 1000, Amr owes 1000, Bakr owes 1000, except 100.' According to the Hanafi view, the exception applies to the collective (total 3000 minus 100 = 2900) or distributes differently, while the Shafi'i view applies the exception to each individual (each 1000 minus 100 = 900).
The dispute rests on whether the primary function is to express the remainder (Hanafi) or to negate/affirm (Shafi'i).

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition on the nature and types of istithna (exception) in Arabic speech, specifically as applied in usul al-fiqh. The author (commentator of Ashraf al-Anwar on Nur al-Anwar) explains that istithna is fundamentally the act of speaking about the remainder after excluding something from the general statement. The text begins by addressing a counterargument: if one interprets the Quranic verse about Nuh staying a thousand years minus fifty as a counter (i.e., that istithna functions like a conditional counter), it would lead to falsehood because the actual stay was not exactly a thousand years. Therefore, the Hanafi position holds that istithna does not negate or affirm but simply expresses the remainder. The passage then presents the linguistic definitions: one group says istithna is extraction and speaking of the remainder; another group says istithna from negation is affirmation and from affirmation is negation. The Hanafis reconcile these by stating that the primary function (ibarah) is to speak of the remainder, while the affirmation/negation is only an indication (isharah) derived from it. The analogy with ghaya (limit) is used: just as a limit marks the end of what is intended, istithna marks what is excluded. The text then distinguishes two types of istithna: muttasil (connected) where the excepted item is of the same genus as the general statement, and munfasil (disconnected) where the excepted item is of a different genus, making it a new independent statement (as in Ibrahim's speech: 'They are enemies to me, except the Lord of the worlds'). The passage concludes with a specific fiqh application: when exceptions follow multiple conjunctive statements (e.g., 'Zayd owes 1000, Amr owes 1000, Bakr owes 1000, except 100'), the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools differ on whether the exception applies to each item individually or to the total sum. The Hanafi position (as per the earlier reconciliation) considers the exception to apply to the collective or distribute depending on context, while Shafi'i applies it to each. Key Terms
الاستثناء (al-istithna): To extract something from a general statement and to speak about the remainder.
عبارة (ibarah): Explicit expression; the literal meaning of the words.
إشارة (isharah): Implicit indication; a derived meaning from context.

منفصل (munfasil): Disconnected exception: the excepted item is of a different type, making it a new independent statement.

Exception removes generality; it cannot govern all clauses—only the nearest suffices.

This lesson examines a foundational usul dispute: when a speaker says multiple conjoined statements and then adds an exception, does the exception affect all the statements or only the last one? Core Idea
When two items in a debt phrase share a common genus, the conjunction clarifies; otherwise interpretation is needed.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi usul principle: how the necessity of brevity (darurat kathrat al-kalam) allows the conjunction 'wa' to serve as bayan (clarification) in common commercial phrases. Hanafi and Shafi'i Interpretation of the Istithna' in the Riba Hadith
Analyzing the ikhtilaf in interpreting the istithna' in the hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi and Shafi'i interpretations of the istithna' in the hadith 'لا تبيعوا الطعام بالطعام إلا سواء بسواء' and apply the Hanafi usul principle of ta'wil to determine that small quantities of food are not covered by the prohibition of riba al-fadl.

Core Idea
The exception in the hadith is interpreted by Hanafis as a circumstance, not an object, so small amounts remain permissible.

This lesson examines a classical usul al-fiqh dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over the interpretation of the Prophet's hadith prohibiting the sale of food for food except like for like. The core issue is whether the exception 'illa sawa'an bi sawa'in' (except equal for equal) applies to all quantities or only to customary large amounts. The Hanafi school, using ta'wil (interpretive reasoning) on the excepted-from (mustathna minhu), argues that small quantities (qalil) are not included in the prohibition, while the Shafi'i school interprets the exception as applying to the object itself, making any unequal exchange haram. Understanding this ikhtilaf reveals how usul principles—especially the treatment of exception clauses and the role of custom—shape fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith and the grammatical challenge
Locate the hadith 'لا تبيعوا الطعام بالطعام إلا سواء بسواء'. Recognize that the excepted term 'سواء بسواء' is a verbal noun (masdar) indicating a state, while the excepted-from 'الطعام' is a concrete noun. Grammatically, the mustathna and mustathna minhu must be of the same type for the exception to be straightforward. Analyze why this mismatch necessitates ta'wil (figurative interpretation) in one of the two terms.
This grammatical issue triggers the usul dispute; the rule 'al-mustathna minhu wa al-mustathna yajibu an yatthida fi al-jins' is key.
2
State the Shafi'i interpretation
Imam al-Shafi'i performs ta'wil on the mustathna (the exception). He interprets 'سواء بسواء' as 'طعامًا مساويًا بطعام مساوٍ' (equal food for equal food), converting the verbal noun into a concrete noun. This makes the exception apply to a specific object (equal food), so all other unequal exchanges—including small amounts—remain under the general prohibition (hurma). Understand that this interpretation leads to the ruling that selling a handful of dates for two handfuls is haram.
The Shafi'i position is consistent with the principle that the original ruling in transactions is prohibition until proven otherwise.
3
State the Hanafi interpretation
The Hanafi school performs ta'wil on the mustathna minhu (the excepted-from). They interpret 'الطعام بالطعام' as referring to circumstances (ahwal) rather than the physical food itself. Thus the hadith means 'Do not sell food for food in any circumstances except in the circumstance of equality.' The circumstances applicable are those of customary large amounts (inequality, guesswork, equality). Small quantities (qalil) are not a recognized circumstance in custom, so they remain outside the scope of the prohibition and are permissible by the original principle of ibaha.
This ta'wil uses the rule that the mustathna minhu can be interpreted broadly to include states (ahwal) when the literal reading is problematic.
4
Apply the interpretation to determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider a concrete scenario: selling a handful (hafnah) of dates for two handfuls. According to the Hanafi interpretation, since the handful is a small amount not customarily treated as subject to riba, the transaction is permissible. According to the Shafi'i interpretation, any unequal exchange is haram, so it is prohibited. Identify the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) as the permissibility or prohibition of small-quantity exchanges.
The ikhtilaf is not about the text itself but about how to resolve the grammatical tension between the general and the exception.

Commentary
This passage presents a classic usul al-fiqh dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the interpretation of the hadith 'Do not sell food for food except like for like' (la tabi'u al-ta'am bi al-ta'am illa sawa'an bi sawa'in). Small amounts are not included in the prohibition because they are not customarily considered substantial. This dispute showcases the Hanafi use of ta'wil (interpretive reasoning) and the role of custom ('urf) in limiting the scope of a general text.

Hanafi Interpretation of the Exception in Riba al-Fadl Hadith
Reconciling the exception 'illa sawaan' with small quantities in riba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on the interpretation of the exception in the riba hadith and explain the Hanafi response based on custom and textual indication.

Core Idea
The exception in the riba hadith applies to states of transaction, not food items, making small quantities permissible.

This lesson examines a fundamental usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over the interpretation of the exception (استثناء) in the hadith prohibiting riba al-fadl. The Hanafi school argues that the exception modifies the state (حال) of the transaction rather than the commodity itself, and that only large quantities are covered by the prohibition. This interpretation relies on custom (عرف) and textual indication (دلالة النص), allowing small quantities to remain on original permissibility.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a legal cause ('illah) according to the Hanafi usul categories of description (wasf), name (ism), and ruling (hukm), and identify its subdivisions including clear/hidden and singular/plural.

Core Idea
The 'illah is fundamentally a description (wasf) that can be a name or a ruling, and it divides into clear/hidden and singular/plural.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the classification of the 'illah. Understanding these categories is essential for applying qiyas correctly and for recognizing disputes between madhahib. We will work through the types of 'illah and their subdivisions, using examples from the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three possible forms of 'illah
The passage states that the 'illah can be a description (wasf), a name (ism), or a ruling (hukm). Recognize that all three are valid.
Description is the most general; name and ruling are subcategories.
2
Distinguish the subdivisions of wasf
Wasf includes: singular/plural (fard/'adad), necessary/accidental (lazim/'arid), clear/hidden (jali/khafi). Understand each with examples.
The text provides examples: fard (measure alone), 'adad (measure+genus), lazim (intrinsic), 'arid (accidental), jali (cat hadith), khafi (riba).
3
Recognize the broad terminology of wasf
In Hanafi usul, the term 'wasf' is used comprehensively to include all forms of 'illah: whether it is a description, a name, or a ruling.
The passage states: 'the comprehensive meaning is called description absolutely.'
4
Apply classification to examples
Practice categorizing given examples: 'blood' as ism, obligation as hukm, cat's movement as jali wasf, riba cause as khafi wasf.
The Hajj analogy shows a ruling (obligation) serving as 'illah for another ruling.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the classification of the 'illah (legal cause) in qiyas. The commentator expands on the matn’s statements about the 'illah being a description (wasf), name (ism), or ruling (hukm), and its subdivisions. He begins by affirming that the categories of 'singular' (fard) and 'plural' ('adad) are also divisions of the description. The singular description is like the cause being merely measure or merely genus for the prohibition of certain women (e.g., combining two sisters or a woman and her aunt in marriage). The plural description is like measure combined with genus for the prohibition of excess in riba. Then he explains that the 'illah can be a name: for example, the word 'blood' (dam) in the hadith 'Indeed it is blood of a large vein' — if we consider the word itself, it is a name; if we consider the flowing, it is an accidental description. He then discusses clear (jali) vs. hidden (khafi), giving the cat’s leftover water as an example of a clear 'illah (the cat’s frequent movement avoids hardship), and the causes for riba (measure/genus for Hanafis, edibility for Shafi‘is, storage for Malikis) as hidden. He then treats the 'illah as a ruling (hukm): the Prophet’s analogy of Hajj on behalf of another to paying a debt, where the common cause is the obligation (wujub) derived from debt. Finally, he summarizes that all these classifications are divisions of the description, and the comprehensive term ‘description’ in usul terminology includes wasf, ism, and hukm. The discussion follows the order of the matn and clarifies ambiguities.

Key Terms
العِلَّة (al-‘illah): The legal cause in qiyas that necessitates the ruling.
الوَصْف (al-wasf): A description of the original case that serves as the 'illah.
الجَلِيّ (al-jaliyy): A clear 'illah that is easily understood by all.
الخَفِيّ (al-khafiyy): A hidden 'illah that only experts can identify.

When Qiyas Yields to Nass: The Case of Mutual Oaths
Textual analysis of a hadith limiting qiyas extension

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a hadith that overrides qiyas is not extended analogically to other cases, and identify the Hanafi principle that governs such exceptions.

Core Idea
A hadith that contradicts qiyas is confined to its wording; it is not subject to analogical extension (ta'diyah).

The Hanafi school holds that qiyas (analogical reasoning) is a valid source, but when a clear textual ruling (nass) contradicts the outcome of qiyas, the text prevails. However, such a ruling is often treated as an exception and is not extended to similar cases by analogy. This lesson examines a hadith on mutual oaths in sales to illustrate this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contradiction between qiyas and hadith
According to qiyas, only the buyer should swear because he denies the extra price, while the seller has no claim. The hadith, however, requires both parties to swear and rescind the contract.
The hadith is: إِذَا اخْتَلَفَ الْمُتَبَايِعَانِ وَالسِّلْعَةُ قَائِمَةٌ بِعَيْنَهَا تَحَالْفَا وَتُرَادَا.
2
Recognize the hadith as an exception overriding qiyas
The ruling of mutual oath contradicts both qiyas and 'reason' (ʿaql), so it is accepted solely from the text and not subject to analogical reasoning.
3
Apply the principle of non-extension (ta'diyah mahzura)
Because the ruling is an exception, it cannot be extended to other cases, such as disputes between heirs after the parties' death, or to hire contracts.
Most Hanafi scholars (except Imam Muhammad) hold that the ruling does not extend to heirs.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi disagreement
Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani held that the ruling does extend to heirs, but the majority (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) confine it to the original parties.
The mufta bihi is the majority view (non-extension).

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of a hadith regarding oaths when buyer and seller dispute after possession. This demonstrates the limitations of qiyas when a clear textual ruling (nass) contradicts it, and the Hanafi principle of restricting a ruling to its precise wording.

The Principle of 'Aks (Reverse) in Hanafi Usul
Defines 'aks as the absence of ruling with absence of description, with example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'aks and apply it to identify a valid 'illa in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A valid 'illa must be both consistent and reversible; if the description is absent, the ruling must also be absent.

In Hanafi usul, not every description that accompanies a ruling qualifies as an effective cause ('illa). The description must consistently produce the ruling (iṭṭirād) and also, when absent, the ruling must be absent (in'ikās). This principle, known as 'aks, distinguishes Hanafi methodology from other schools.

Guided Path
1
Define iṭṭirād and in'ikās
Explain that iṭṭirād means the ruling exists when the description exists; in'ikās means the ruling is absent when the description is absent.
2
Understand the example of wiping the head
The description 'head' is both mutṭarid and mun'akis: for any head, wiping does not require repetition; for non-head limbs, repetition is Sunnah. This satisfies 'aks.
3
Contrast with Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that wiping is a pillar, so repetition is Sunnah, and the reverse (non-pillar items not having repetition) fails because rinsing and sniffing are not pillars yet have repetition.
This illustrates that the Hanafi condition of 'aks is not accepted by Shafi'is.
4
Apply the concept to a new case
Given a description that is only consistent but not reversible, it is not a valid 'illa. For example, 'water is purifying' – water always purifies, but absence of water does not mean purification is absent (e.g., tayammum). So 'water' is not a valid 'illa for purification.

Definition of Shart and Its First Two Types
Defines shart and explains its first two types with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define shart in Hanafi usul and distinguish between shart mahd and shart fi hukm al-'illah.

Core Idea
Shart is that on which existence depends, not obligation; it is external to the essence.

This passage introduces the Hanafi definition of shart (condition) as distinct from 'illah (cause). It then classifies shart into five types by induction, detailing the first two: pure condition (shart mahd) and condition that is treated like a cause (shart fi hukm al-'illah). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly analyzing legal rulings that depend on conditions.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition of shart
From the first sentence, shart is defined as that upon which existence (wujud) depends, not obligation (wujub). The author adds that it must be external to the essence to exclude parts.
The definition is key to separating shart from 'illah.
2
Understand the first type: shart mahd
Shart mahd has no effect on the ruling itself; the ruling simply depends on its occurrence. The example: 'If you enter the house, you are divorced' — entering does not cause divorce but triggers it.
3
Analyze the second type: shart fi hukm al-'illah
This type is a condition but is treated like a cause for legal consequences. Example: digging a well in a public path — the actual cause of loss is the weight of the falling object, but the earth was a preventer (manee'). Digging removes the preventer, so the condition (digging) is elevated to the status of a cause for liability via istihsan.
Note the distinction: the digger is liable only if digging in a public place; if in his own property or if someone jumps deliberately, no liability.
4
Explore the second example: tearing a water-skin
Tearing a water-skin (shaq al-ziq) is a condition for the liquid to flow. The skin is a preventer; its removal enables the flow. The one who tears is liable for loss, analogously to the well case.
This illustrates the same principle: a condition that removes a preventer is treated like a cause.
5
Identify the underlying principle
The shart fi hukm al-'illah is an application of istihsan (juristic preference). A condition that removes a preventer is upgraded to function like a cause for legal effect, even though it is not the true cause.
This shows the flexibility of Hanafi usul in handling conditions.

The Fourth and Fifth Types of Shart and the Rule Distinguishing Condition from Description
Textual analysis: types of shart and the rule for distinguishing condition from description.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fourth and fifth types of shart in a ta'liq statement, apply the Hanafi rule that the last condition is operative, and distinguish between a condition expressed by explicit sighah and one implied by a description.

Core Idea
The last condition in a series triggers the ruling; earlier conditions are merely nominal. A description in an indefinite noun functions as an implicit condition.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, conditions (shurut) that suspend rulings are carefully classified. This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar explains two further types: the nominal condition (shart isman) that appears when multiple conditions are stated, and the condition that serves as a mere sign (shart ka al-alamah). Then it provides a crucial rule to distinguish between an explicit conditional sentence and a description that implies a condition. This lesson will walk you through the classifications and the rule, enabling you to analyze ta'liq statements correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conditions in the example
In the example 'If you enter the house, and this house, then you are divorced,' there are two conditions: entering the first house and entering the second house. Note that the second condition is introduced after the first.
The second condition is the one that actually triggers the divorce according to Hanafis.
2
Determine which condition is operative
According to the Hanafi rule, the last condition (the second one) is the operative condition. The first is merely nominal (isman la hukman). The suspension of the consequence is on the last condition.
This is the fourth type of shart.
3
Apply the rule to various scenarios
If both conditions occur while the wife is in the marriage, divorce occurs. If only the first occurs in the marriage, no divorce. If only the second occurs in the marriage (even if the first occurred outside marriage), divorce occurs because only the last condition matters and milk is only required at ta'liq and nuzul.
This contrasts with Zufar's view that both must occur in the marriage.
4
Contrast with Imam Zufar's qiyas
Imam Zufar analogizes the second condition to the first, requiring both to occur in the marriage. His reasoning is that if the first condition is ineffective without marriage, the second should be treated similarly.
The Hanafi response: the ruling is tied to the last condition; the first is just nominal.
5
Apply the rule distinguishing condition from description
Learn the dabita: An explicit condition uses conditional particles (in, idha). A description (wasf) in an indefinite noun (e.g., 'a woman I marry') implies a condition. But if the description is in a definite noun (e.g., 'this woman'), it does not imply a condition because isharah (pointing) is stronger.
This is the fifth type: shart ka al-alamah, like ihsan for stoning.

Core Idea
An explicit condition uses 'if'; an implicit condition hides in an indefinite noun — both bind the ruling to a future event.

In usul al-fiqh, conditions shape legal obligations. But not every condition is spelled out. The Hanafi school recognizes that a condition can be implied through an indefinite description (nakira) in a future-oriented statement. This lesson unpacks the rule (dabita) that distinguishes an explicit shart from ma'na al-shart, using divorce examples to illustrate the difference. Mastering this distinction is crucial for correctly deriving rulings from speech.

الإعتاق (al-i'taq): The act of emancipating; for Sahibayn it is establishment of 'itq (indivisible), for Abu Hanifa it is removal of ownership (divisible).
مُتَجَزِّئ (mutajazzi): Divisible; capable of being split into parts, each producing a legal effect separately.

Hazl and talji'ah do not negate legal capacity but invalidate the contract's effect.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar explains the rulings on contracts made in jest (hazl) or under compulsion (talji'ah) in Hanafi fiqh. The text first distinguishes hazl from the option of stipulation (khiyar al-shart) by noting that in a jest sale, the parties intend to deceive outsiders into thinking a sale occurred, whereas in a stipulated option, the intention is to explicitly inform that the sale is conditional. Then it clarifies that talji'ah, where a person is forced by circumstances to simulate a sale, carries the same legal effect as hazl: it does not negate legal capacity (ahliyyah) but the contract itself is not valid in transferring ownership because the inner intent contradicts the outward appearance. The author then details four possible scenarios after the parties have secretly agreed to a fake sale: (1) If they later agree that they were merely building on the original jest, the sale is invalid (fasid) and does not transfer ownership even if possession is taken, analogous to a sale with a perpetual option condition. (2) If they agree that they have abandoned the jest and now transact seriously, the sale becomes valid. (3) If they agree that they had no prior understanding at the time of contracting (their minds were empty of the jest), the sale is valid because the default in contracts is validity. (4) If they disagree about their intention (one claims it was a jest, the other claims it was serious), the relied-upon position of Imam Abu Hanifa is that the contract is valid, based on the principle that the default in contracts is validity unless there is evidence to the contrary. This follows the Hanafi usul that the apparent action is given weight in the absence of clear proof of inner intent.

Key Terms
الهَزْل (al-hazl): A contract made in jest where the parties secretly agree not to intend the legal consequences, but outwardly perform the contract.
التَّلْجِيَة (al-talji'ah): A contract made under compulsion (idtirar) where the outward appearance contradicts the inner intention, similar to hazl.
الأصل في العقود الصحة (al-asl fi al-uqud al-sihhah): The default principle that contracts are considered valid unless there is evidence to the contrary.

Effect of Jest (Hazl) in Marriage and Divorce

Jest only nullifies marriage/divorce if both parties agree to abandon it; otherwise the apparent contract is binding.

The side of seriousness, and he did not mention regarding if they both agree on abandoning (i'rad) or differ in it, because the ruling of the first is apparent by a higher priority, and the ruling of the second is that the statement is of the one who claims i'rad. As for according to him (Abu Hanifa), due to what preceded, and as for according to them (the two companions), due to its nullity; thus it is said. If it is in the same genus, by their agreeing to mention in the contract one hundred dinars while the actual exchange between them is one hundred dirhams, the named (musamma) is obligatory according to them in every case, whether they agreed on i'rad, or on building (bina'), or that nothing occurred to them, or they differed. Due to nullity of the jest in the essence and the property is obligatory as a subsidiary. And according to him, if they agreed on i'rad, the named is obligatory due to nullity of the jest by i'rad. If they agreed on building (bina'), divorce is conditional upon her acceptance of the named because it is the condition in the contract. If they agreed that nothing occurred to them, the named is obligatory and divorce occurs due to preponderance of the side of seriousness. If they differed, the statement is of the one claiming i'rad because it is the principle. It may not be said: How can property be subsidiary in jest? ... And according to him, divorce must be suspended on her choice. If they agreed that nothing occurred to them... And this is all regarding compositions. If that (jest) is in confession (iqrar) regarding what admits of revocation like sale, by their agreeing that he confesses to sale in front of people while in reality there was no confession, and regarding what does not admit of it like marriage and divorce, by their agreeing that they confess to marriage and divorce in front of the common people while there was no confession between them, then jest nullifies it because confession is susceptible to truth and falsehood, and the thing confessed about when it is false, how can the report of it become true? Jest in apostasy is disbelief; that is, if he utters words of disbelief in jest, he becomes a disbeliever. It may be objected: How can he be a disbeliever when he does not believe it? He answered: His disbelief is not due to a word he slipped without belief, but due to the jest itself because it is jest.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary (Ashraf al-Anwar) explains the rulings of jest (hazl) in marriage and divorce contracts according to the Hanafi school. The author distinguishes between two types: when the parties agree to a mock marriage where the mahr is exaggerated but they secretly intend a different amount, and when the jest pertains to the genus of the dower (e.g., saying dinars but intending dirhams). The opinions of Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) are contrasted. A key point is that when both parties agree to abandon the jest (i'rad), the named mahr is obligatory according to all, but the companions hold it valid regardless, while Abu Hanifa requires the wife's acceptance for divorce to occur if they built on the jest. The passage also extends to confessions (iqrar) and apostasy (ridda): a jest confession does not create a legal obligation because confession can be false; however, jest in uttering words of disbelief is actual disbelief, even without belief, because the act of jesting itself shows disrespect. The commentary uses reasoning (talil) to explain the differences, emphasizing the principle that seriousness is the origin and jest is exceptional.

Key Terms
الهزل (al-hazl): Jest or mockery; saying something without intending its legal consequences.
الإعراض (al-i'rad): Abandonment of the jest; both parties agree to disregard the mock agreement and treat the contract seriously.
البناء (al-bina'): Building upon the jest; the parties continue with the mock agreement without abandoning it.

According to Imam Abu Hanifa, what happens if both parties agree to jest in the genus of the mahr (e.g., saying 100 dinars but intending 100 dirhams) and then agree to build upon the jest (bina')?
A) The named mahr (100 dinars) is obligatory, and divorce occurs immediately.
B) The intended mahr (100 dirhams) is obligatory, and divorce occurs.
C) The named mahr is obligatory, but divorce is conditional upon the wife's acceptance of it.
D) The contract is void and no mahr or divorce occurs.

Key Takeaway
The effect of jest depends on the mutual agreement of the parties: if they both intend jest and agree to abandon it (i'rad), the contract is void; otherwise, the apparent contract is binding.
