The Effect of Duress on Verbal Statements
Explaining the classification of verbal statements under duress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify verbal statements into those that are and are not affected by duress, and apply the Hanafi ruling to each type.

Core Idea
Divorce under duress is valid; sale under duress is void but ratifiable.

This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar explains a key Hanafi rule: duress does not affect all statements equally. Some verbal acts, like divorce, are effective even under compulsion because they do not require consent. Others, like sales, become void but can be later ratified. Understanding this classification is essential for distinguishing between valid and void transactions under duress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
The passage divides statements into those that do not depend on consent and those that do. Name each category and list examples from the text.
First category: divorce, marriage, manumission, etc. Second category: sale and similar contracts.
2
Apply the rule to the first category
For statements like divorce, explain why duress does not invalidate them, citing the condition that they do not require rida.
These acts are purely verbal and their validity is not contingent on the doer's willingness.
3
Apply the rule to the second category
For statements like sale, explain why they become void (fasid) and how ratification works.
The contract is concluded as void due to lack of consent, but later ratification makes it valid.
4
Examine the special case of confessions
Confessions under duress are universally invalid because the speaker is not testifying to truth but acting to avoid harm.
Duress provides evidence of falsehood, so the confession is rejected.
5
Distinguish between 'not invalidated' and 'valid'
For the first category, the act is valid from the start; for the second, the act is void but can become valid through ratification.
The passage uses 'لم تبطل' (not invalidated) for the first and 'يفسد' (becomes void) for the second.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the matn ruling regarding the effect of duress (ikrah) on verbal statements. The author begins by stating that in statements, the coerced person cannot be a mere instrument for another, as speech is inherently personal. Then the passage classifies statements into two types: those that do not admit of being subject to duress and are not dependent on consent, such as divorce, marriage, manumission, etc.; these are not invalidated by duress. The reason is that these acts are purely verbal and their validity does not depend on the consent of the doer; hence even if coerced, they take effect. The second type are those that do admit duress and are dependent on consent, like sales and similar contracts. In these, the effect of the statement is confined to the coerced person, but the contract becomes void due to lack of consent; however, if the coerced person ratifies it after the duress is lifted, it becomes valid because the cause of voidness is removed. The passage then addresses confessions (iqrar): they are not valid under duress because the validity of a confession depends on the truthfulness of the thing confessed, and duress indicates falsehood, as the person is speaking to avoid harm, not to state the truth. Moreover, the confession cannot be treated as a metaphor because the presence of duress proves falsehood.

Key Terms
إكراه (ikrah): Compelling a person by threat of harm to do something they would not do willingly.
مكره (mukrah): The person being coerced.
رضاء (rida): Consent; the willingness of the person to perform the act.

Four Types of Prohibitions Under Compulsion in Hanafi Usul
Classifying prohibitions according to removability under compulsion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four types of prohibitions under compulsion (ikrah) according to Hanafi usul, and apply the classification to new cases.

Core Idea
Prohibitions under compulsion are not all equal: some never lift, some fully lift, some remain sinful but allowed, some require compensation.

In the study of compulsion (ikrah) in Hanafi usul, a critical distinction is how different prohibitions behave. This passage presents a fourfold classification: absolute prohibitions that never lose their unlawfulness, prohibitions that are completely lifted by necessity, prohibitions that remain inherently evil but are permitted under duress, and prohibitions that could be lifted but are not removed by compulsion alone, requiring compensation. Understanding these categories is essential for correctly applying rulings on compelled actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
Read the passage and list the four types of hurmah: (1) never removed, no concession; (2) removed entirely under compulsion; (3) not removed but concession allowed; (4) bears removal but not removed by ikrah, with concession.
The text explicitly enumerates these with examples.
2
Examine the evidence for type 2
Note the Quranic verse (6:119) cited as proof that prohibitions like wine, carrion, and pork are lifted under necessity.
This verse is a key dalil for the principle of necessity overriding textual prohibitions.
3
Distinguish between types 3 and 4
Type 3 (utterance of kufr) does not have its prohibition removed, but action is allowed. Type 4 (taking property) has a prohibition that could be removed (by owner's permission) but is not removed by ikrah; compensation is required.
The difference lies in the nature of the harm and the possibility of reparation.
4
Analyze the consequence of patience
The text notes that enduring in types 3 and 4 until death leads to martyrdom, highlighting the ethical weight of refusing even when action is permissible.
This underscores the moral ideal of patience for the sake of Allah's religion.

The author previously mentioned four types of actions based on compulsion: obligatory, prohibited, permissible, and concession. Now he intends to explain the four types of prohibitions for which one is compelled. The result is the same. And a prohibition that does not bear removal but bears concession, like uttering the word of disbelief. And a prohibition that bears removal but is not removed by the excuse of compulsion, although it also bears concession, like taking the property of another. Commentary
This passage is a classification of prohibitions (hurmah) in the context of compulsion (ikrah) according to the Hanafi school. The author, commenting on a prior classification, now details four types of prohibited acts based on how their prohibition behaves under duress. First, prohibitions that are absolute and never lifted, such as adultery and murder of a Muslim, because the harm (loss of lineage, sanctity of life) cannot be justified by compulsion. Second, prohibitions that are lifted entirely under compulsion, like consuming wine, carrion, or pork, supported by the Quranic exception in Surah al-An'am (6:119). These are purely textual prohibitions that cease in necessity. Third, prohibitions that remain inherently prohibited but allow a concession (rukhsah) in action, such as uttering words of disbelief under threat; the act is still evil but permitted to save life. Fourth, prohibitions that could be removed by permission but not by compulsion alone, like taking another's property; it remains unlawful but a concession is given to ward off greater harm, with the obligation to compensate later. The author concludes that patience in the third and fourth types leads to martyrdom. The entire discussion grounds the fiqh of ikrah in usul principles.

Key Terms
حرمة (hurmah): The legal prohibition attached to an act or thing; its attribute of being haram.
رخصة (rukhsah): A concession or dispensation that allows a normally prohibited act under specific circumstances like compulsion.
إكراه (ikrah): Compulsion that forces a person to act against his will, typically through threat of death or severe harm.

The Third Type of Prohibition: Rukhsa Under Coercion
Expansion of a usul rule on rukhsa under coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a prohibition under severe coercion is not nullified but a license (rukhsa) is granted, with compensation remaining obligatory.

Core Idea
Coercion grants a license, not nullification—the prohibition's iṣmah remains, requiring compensation.

In this lesson, we examine a key distinction in Hanafi uṣūl: how some prohibitions (ḥarām) can be temporarily lifted under coercion without being truly nullified. The classic example is eating another's wealth under duress. This passage clarifies that the underlying prohibition remains intact—what changes is the ruling from ḥarām to permissible via rukhṣa, with compensation due afterward. Understanding this prevents confusion between a license and a true change in the legal status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the third type of prohibition
Locate the passage's description of the third division of ḥarām—those whose prohibition can be lifted in some states but not absolutely. Note that the prohibition itself is not removed; rather, a license (rukhṣa) is granted under coercion.
This type contrasts with prohibitions that are absolutely removed, like eating carrion to save life.
2
Analyze the example: eating another's wealth
The text uses the case of taking another's property. Even under severe coercion, the act is permissible only as a license—the original ḥurma remains, so compensation (ḍamān) is obligatory after coercion ends.
The sanctity (ʿiṣmah) of property is not nullified by coercion.
3
Distinguish between rukhṣa and ibāḥa
The author notes that ibāḥa (original permissibility) is not an independent category here; it merges into fard or rukhṣa. Rukhṣa is a temporary dispensation that does not change the fundamental ḥukm.
This prevents the misconception that coercion creates a new mubāḥ category.
4
Note the condition of severity (ikrāh mulji')
The license applies only when coercion is severe and compelling (ikrāh mulji'), such as threat of death. Lesser coercion may not permit the act.
Hanafi fiqh distinguishes between mulji' (compelling) and ghayr mulji'.
5
Recognize the virtue of patience
If one bears the coercion and is killed, they are considered a martyr (shahīd) for preserving the law. This underscores that the preferred legal choice is to avoid the prohibited act even under duress.
The license is a concession, not an obligation.
