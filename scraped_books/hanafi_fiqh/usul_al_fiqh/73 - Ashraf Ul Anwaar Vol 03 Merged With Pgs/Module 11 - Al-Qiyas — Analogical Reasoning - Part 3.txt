Rejecting Ghalabat al-Ashbah: Brother vs. Paternal Cousin
Hanafi refutation of Shafi'i ghalabat al-ashbah method

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi critique of ghalabat al-ashbah and apply the principle that preferring one qiyas over another by another qiyas is invalid.

Core Idea
Ghalabat al-ashbah is invalid because it reduces to preferring one qiyas by another qiyas.

The Shafi'i school sometimes uses ghalabat al-ashbah (preponderance of similarities) to resolve conflicts between analogies. In this passage, the Hanafi commentator refutes this method using a concrete example: brother vs. paternal cousin. Understanding this critique is essential for mastering the Hanafi approach to qiyas and tarjih.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Shafi'i argument
The Shafi'is claim that a brother shares only one attribute (mahramiyya) with father/son, but shares multiple attributes (zakah, marriage of ex-wife, testimony) with a paternal cousin. Thus, the cousin is more deserving of legal attachment.
This is the key claim that uses ghalabat al-ashbah.
2
Understand the method of ghalabat al-ashbah
Ghalabat al-ashbah means preferring one analogy over another by counting the number of shared attributes. The Shafi'is argue that more similarities indicate stronger legal resemblance.
This method is accepted by some schools but rejected by the Hanafis.
3
Recognize the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafi scholar states that this is equivalent to preferring one qiyas over another by another qiyas, which is circular and invalid. The method lacks a sound basis.
The author says 'you have already known its falsehood' from earlier discussions.
4
Connect to the broader principle of tarjih
In Hanafi usul, valid tarjih must be based on textual evidence or clear rational preference, not on mere counting of similarities. Ghalabat al-ashbah leads to infinite regress and is rejected.
This principle protects the integrity of qiyas as a source of law.

Umum al-Wasf in Riba: Shafi'i vs. Hanafi
Shafi'i preference for tu'm as a cause for riba due to generality, refuted by Hanafis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Shafi'i argument for tu'm as a cause for riba and explain the Hanafi response using the principles of 'illa qasira and specific nass overriding general.

Core Idea
Generality does not give preference to a cause if the opponent allows restricted causes; specific text overrides general.

This passage presents a classic usul al-fiqh dispute: should the 'illa for riba be the general attribute of being edible (tu'm) or the specific combination of measurement and genus? The Shafi'is argue for generality; the Hanafis counter with two methodological points that strike at the heart of cause prioritization.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
Determine which description (wasf) of ribawi items is the effective cause for prohibiting riba: the general 'tu'm' or the specific 'qadr and jins'.
The Shafi'is propose tu'm; Hanafis insist on qadr and jins.
2
State the Shafi'i argument
The Shafi'is claim tu'm is better because it covers both small amounts (a handful) and large amounts (measured), while measurement covers only large quantities.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf—what each school prohibits.
3
Explain the Hanafi first response
Since the Shafi'is themselves allow ta'lil with a 'illa qasira (a cause that does not extend to all cases), generality cannot give a preference; if a restricted cause is acceptable, then a general cause has no inherent superiority.
This undercuts the Shafi'i claim that tu'm's generality makes it better.
4
Explain the Hanafi second response
The specific wasf (qadr and jins) is like a nass (text), and according to the Shafi'is themselves, a specific nass overrides a general one. Therefore, the specific description should be preferred over the general one.
This uses the opponent's own rule against them.
5
Conclude with the Hanafi position
The Hanafis uphold that the effective cause is qadr and jins, not tu'm alone. The Shafi'i preference for generality is rejected on both counts.
The mufta bihi position remains the Hanafi one.

Commentary
This passage addresses the dispute over the effective cause ('illa) for riba. The Shafi'is argue that tu'm (being edible) is a more suitable cause than the combination of qadr (measurement) and jins (genus) because tu'm applies to both small amounts (like a handful) and large amounts (measured), whereas measurement only applies to large quantities. Second, a description (wasf) is like a text (nass), and in the case of texts, the specific overrides the general; thus, the specific description (qadr and jins) should be preferred over the general one (tu'm).

Qillat al-Awsaf: Shafi'i Argument Refuted by Hanafis
Shafi'i argument that a single description is preferable due to its fewness, refuted by Hanafis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafis reject qillat al-awsaf as a basis for tarjih in qiyas and identify the correct criterion (ta'thir).

Core Idea
Preference in qiyas is based on the strength of effect, not the number of components.

The Shafi'i school argues that a cause with fewer descriptions should be preferred over a composite cause because it is simpler and clearer. The Hanafi school firmly disagrees, stating that the basis for preference is the effectiveness of the cause (ta'thir), not its simplicity. In this lesson, we examine the arguments from both sides and the Hanafi rebuttal, which upholds that a composite cause may be stronger in effect and thus more appropriate for generating a ruling.

Guided Path
1
State the Shafi'i position
Identify the claim: that a single description (e.g., tu'm alone or thamaniyya alone) is 'few' and therefore preferable over a composite cause (qadr and jins combined).
2
Identify the Hanafi response
The Hanafis reject this outright: preference is for ta'thir (effect), not for fewness or abundance.
3
Analyze the Hanafi dalil
The Hanafi proof is a rational argument: a cause with two parts can have a stronger effect than a cause with one part. Thus, the number of components is irrelevant.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: in identifying the 'illa for riba, Shafi'is may accept a single description (tu'm), while Hanafis require both qadr and jins as a stronger composite cause.
5
Understand the usul principle
This dispute illustrates that in Hanafi usul, ta'thir is the primary criterion for tarjih among causes, not quantitative simplicity.

A cause with two parts can be more effective than a cause with one part. For example, a composite cause may have a stronger impact in generating the ruling.

Intiqal (Transition) After Refutation of Causes: The First Division
Introduction to intiqal and its first division

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions that trigger intiqal and apply the first type of transition (from one cause to another) to a given argument in usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
When a cause is refuted, the arguer may transition to a stronger cause to salvage the original ruling.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, when a mujtahid proposes a cause ('illa) for a ruling, the opponent may raise objections that refute that cause. If the cause is successfully refuted, the proponent may resort to intiqal (transition) — moving to a different argument. This lesson focuses on the first of four types of intiqal: moving from one cause to another cause to prove the original cause. The classic example involves a child entrusted with property: the initial cause is authorization to consume, and when that is challenged, the proponent transitions to the absolute nature of that authorization.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Concept of Intiqal
Intiqal is the transition from a refuted cause or argument to a new one. In usul, it is a legitimate move to strengthen one's case after a successful objection (def' al-'illa).
Intiqal is not a fallacy; it is a reasoned response to criticism.
2
Recognize the Trigger Condition
The trigger for intiqal is the establishment of the refutation of the cause (thubut daf' al-'illa). The text mentions that this applies to both 'illa tardiyya (coextensive cause) and 'illa mu'aththira (effective cause).
Some scholars limit intiqal to cases where only the tardiyya cause is refuted.
3
Analyze the First Division of Intiqal
The first division is 'intiqal min 'illa ila 'illa ukhrā li-ithbāt al-ūlā' — transition from one cause to another to prove the first cause. The new cause substantiates the original ruling via a different angle.
The example in the text illustrates this precisely.
4
Work Through the Example
Examine the case: a child (ṣabī) is given a deposit (wadī'a). If he takes possession, does he guarantee its loss? The mu'allil initially argues: he does not guarantee because he is authorized to consume (musallaṭ 'alā al-istihlāk). The opponent objects: he is only authorized to preserve (hifẓ), not consume. The mu'allil then transitions to a new cause: the authorization to consume is absolute (al-taslīṭ 'alā al-istihlāk al-batt), which implies that the child's possession is akin to ownership, thus justifying non-liability.
This transition does not abandon the original conclusion; it strengthens the evidentiary basis.

Commentary
This passage introduces the concept of intiqal (transition) in argumentation. After a mu'allil's proposed cause is refuted through objections (def' al-illal), the mu'allil may resort to transitioning to a different argument. The text identifies four types of transition. The first type is when the mu'allil moves from one cause to another cause to establish the original cause. This new cause reinforces the original cause. The Urdu commentary clarifies that the transition occurs when the mu'allil is forced to change his argument due to the refutation.

عِلَّة مُؤَثِّرَة ('illa mu'aththira): A cause that has a real effect on the ruling; it is stronger and harder to refute.
مُعَلِّل (mu'allil): The person who posits a cause ('illa) to justify a ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the four types of intiqal in usul al-fiqh debates, and distinguish them from other argumentative moves.

Core Idea
Intiqal is a valid dialectical shift — moving to a new cause or ruling to strengthen one's argument.

In Hanafi usul, when a mu'allil's cause ('illah) is challenged, he may resort to intiqal — a structured transition. This lesson classifies four types, illustrating each with examples from fiqh disputes.

1
Recognize the purpose of intiqal
Intiqal is a response when the initial 'illah is contested. It is not a retreat but a strategic shift to maintain the argument.
Think of intiqal as a tool for defense in munazara.
2
Identify the four types from the text
The text lists four types: (1) from cause to cause to prove the first, (2) from ruling to ruling with the same cause, (3) to a new ruling and new cause, and (4) implied as transition to the same ruling with a different cause (not elaborated).
The fourth type is mentioned in the enumeration but not exemplified; later works fill this.

Work through the minor's trust example (type 1) and the mukatab expiation example (types 2 and 3). See how the mu'allil shifts his argument.
Pay attention to the opponent's objection and how the transition responds.
4
Note the Hanafi stance on effective cause
Hanafis emphasize strength of effect (quwwat al-ta'thir) over generality or quantity of attributes. This underlies the rejection of Shafi'i arguments about 'umum and 'illah qasirah.
This principle shapes when and how intiqal is used.

Commentary
This passage from Ashraf al-Anwar, a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary, discusses two preliminary examples before classifying the types of 'intiqal' (transition) in 'illah argumentation. The author first refutes Shafi'i arguments about preferring a specific attribute (like taste) over a combined cause (genus + quantity) in riba rulings. He then refutes the claim that paucity of attributes makes a cause better. After establishing that the effectiveness of a cause is proven by refutation or removal, the author introduces the main subject: the 'mualil' (the one who presents the cause) has four ways to transition when his 'illah is challenged. The four types are: (1) Transition from one cause to another to prove the first cause — exemplified by a minor's destruction of a trust. (2) Transition from one ruling to another using the same first cause — exemplified by the mukatab's emancipation for expiation. (3) Transition to another ruling and another cause — demonstrated in the same mukatab example when the opponent objects differently. The fourth type is implied in the listing but only three are explicitly elaborated; the fourth is typically 'transition to the same ruling with a different cause' but the text here enumerates four categories. The explanation follows the Hanafi usul perspective throughout, emphasizing that preponderance depends on effective strength (quwwat al-ta'thir), not on generality or quantity.

Key Terms
الانتقال (al-intiqal): Transition; moving from one 'illah, ruling, or argument to another in dialectical debate to defend a position.
المعلل (al-mu'allil): The person who presents an 'illah to support a ruling; the respondent in a debate.
العلة القاصرة (al-'illah al-qasirah): A deficient cause that does not extend to all relevant branches; considered ineffective by Hanafis.

The Rule of Intiqal (Transferring Arguments) in Hanafi Usul
Resolves an objection against transfer of argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on intiqal, explain the exception for infinite regress, and respond to the objection using the Ibrahim (AS) narrative.

Core Idea
Transfer of argument is valid except when it leads to infinite regress—truth must be concluded.

In Hanafi usul, debate is a means to discover truth. The principle of intiqal (transferring to another argument) is generally allowed to facilitate conclusion. However, a critical exception exists: if transfer creates an infinite chain of proofs, no ruling can ever be established, defeating the purpose of debate. This passage addresses an objection from the story of Prophet Ibrahim (AS) with Nimrod, showing why his transfer does not contradict the rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its exception
The passage states that all modes of transfer (intiqal) are valid except the fourth. Determine what the fourth mode is: transferring to another cause to prove the first ruling, leading to infinite regress.
Focus on the 'except the fourth' clause.
2
Understand the rationale for the exception
Why does infinite regress invalidate the transfer? Because debate aims to reach a conclusion; endless shifting prevents any ruling from being established.
The text explicitly says: 'an infinite regress occurs and no ruling can be established.'
3
Examine the objection
The opponent cites Prophet Ibrahim's (AS) transfer from the life/death argument to the sun argument against Nimrod. How does this seem to contradict the rule?
Ibrahim's transfer appears to be a valid intiqal, so why is it not the fourth mode?
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The author replies that Ibrahim's transfer was not because his first argument was invalid, but because Nimrod's literalist mindset could not grasp the deeper meaning. He transferred to a clearer, empirical proof to conclude the debate. This does not lead to infinite regress.
Ibrahim's case is a special pedagogical necessity, not a general license.
5
Derive the operative rule
Based on the text, intiqal is permissible when it serves to clarify or conclude the debate without opening an endless chain. The rule: permissible except when it leads to infinite regress.
This is the mufta bihi position.

Attribution of Intervening Cause in Liability
Explaining how an intervening cause transfers liability from the direct actor to the originating cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of attributing an intervening cause to the originating cause to determine liability for damages in cases of indirect causation.

Core Idea
An intervening cause does not break liability if the intermediate agent lacks choice.

This lesson examines a foundational usul principle: when a person sets in motion a chain of events that leads to damage, and an intervening cause (like an animal's trampling) occurs, the liability may be traced back to the originator. The key is whether the intervening actor has free will. In Hanafi law, if the animal has no choice, its actions are attributed to the driver or leader, making them liable for compensation but not for criminal penalties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the causal chain
Analyze the sequence: originator (driver/leader) → intervening cause (animal's trampling) → damage. Note which elements are within human control.
The animal's action is the 'illa (intermediate cause).
2
Assess the intervening actor's choice
Determine whether the intermediate actor (animal) has the capacity to choose. If not, its act is attributed to the originator.
An animal is deemed to lack choice (ikhtiyar) in Hanafi usul.
3
Apply the attribution principle
If the intervening actor lacks choice, the ruling (hukm) of compensation is attributed to the originator as if they directly caused the damage.
This transforms the sabab into a 'cause of the cause' (sabab al-illa).
4
Distinguish compensation from penalties
Compensation (damages) is transferred to the originator, but criminal penalties (qisas, kaffarah, inheritance deprivation) remain with the direct actor only when directly committed.
Penalties require direct commission (mubasharah), not just causation (tasabbub).
5
Test with the example
Apply the principle to the sa'iq (driver from behind) or qa'id (leader from front): the driving/leading is the sabab, the trampling is the illa, and the driver/leader is liable for the property damage but not for homicide penalties if the animal kills someone.
This illustrates the second type of cause mentioned in the text.

Commentary
This passage discusses a usul principle regarding the attribution of an intervening cause in liability. It begins by stating that if an intervening cause is attributed to the original cause, then that cause takes the ruling of the cause, and liability for compensation falls on the original actor. The example given is that of a person driving an animal from behind (sa'iq) or leading it from the front (qa'id). Both actions are the cause of damage that occurs by the animal's trampling, even though the animal's action (trampling) is an intermediate cause. Because the animal lacks choice, its action is attributed to the driver or leader. The ruling of compensation (damages for the destroyed property) is then attributed to the driver or leader as if they directly caused the damage. However, penalties such as inheritance deprivation, expiation, or retaliation are not imposed because those are directly related to the immediate act of killing, which is not attributed to the original cause. This illustrates the Hanafi distinction between causes and the attribution of liability.

علة (illa): The intermediate cause between the originary cause and the effect (e.g., animal's trampling).
سبب (sabab): The originary cause (e.g., driving or leading) that, through the intervening 'illa, leads to damage.
سائق (sa'iq): The person driving the animal from behind.
قائد (qa'id): The person leading the animal from the front.
اختيار (ikhtiyar): Free choice or volition; animals are deemed to lack ikhtiyar in legal attribution.

Three Types of Sabab and Seven Types of 'Illa in Hanafi Usul
Classification of sabab and 'illa with examples from divorce and analogical reasoning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of sabab (real, in meaning of 'illa, metaphorical) and enumerate the seven types of 'illa based on the presence or absence of the three attributes (ism, ma'na, hukm).

Core Idea
Sabab is a sign without impact; 'illa is the effective cause — seven types from three attributes.

In Hanafi uṣūl al-fiqh, understanding the precise meaning of sabab (cause) and 'illa (effective cause) is crucial for correct analogical reasoning. This passage from the commentary on Nūr al-Anwār introduces a classification that some Hanafis adopt: three types of sabab and seven types of 'illa based on three attributes. We will unpack each category and see how they function in legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Define sabab and 'illa
First, grasp the Hanafi distinction: sabab is an apparent indicator that entails a ruling but does not have effective impact; 'illa is that to which the ruling is attributed primarily and which has effective impact.
This distinction is central to Hanafi usul; other schools may use the terms interchangeably.
2
Identify the three types of sabab
Some Hanafi scholars divide sabab into: (1) real sabab (sabab ḥaqīqī), e.g., sale as a cause for ownership; (2) sabab in the meaning of 'illa (sabab fī maʿnā al-ʿilla), e.g., conditional divorce; (3) metaphorical sabab (sabab majāzī), e.g., adding a time to a pronouncement.
The second and third types blur the line between sabab and 'illa, leading to overlap with the seven types of 'illa.
3
Understand the three attributes of 'illa
A 'illa is classified by three attributes: (a) name (ism) – being placed for the ruling; (b) meaning (ma'na) – being effective in the ruling; (c) ruling (hukm) – the ruling follows immediately without delay. A complete 'illa has all three.
These attributes are logical and used to categorize 'illa into seven combinations.
4
Enumerate the seven types of 'illa
Based on presence/absence of the three attributes, there are seven possible types: (1) has all three; (2) only ism; (3) only ma'na; (4) only hukm; (5) ism and ma'na; (6) ism and hukm; (7) ma'na and hukm. The author notes that the matn replaced two types (pure ma'na and pure hukm) with other categories.
This exhaustive classification helps scholars precisely identify which 'illa is operative in analogical reasoning.

An 'illah's completeness depends on whether it combines name, meaning, and immediate ruling effect.

In Hanafi usul, the 'illah (cause) is the bedrock of qiyas. Not all causes are equal; they vary in strength based on three attributes: being a name (coined for the ruling), having meaning (being effective), and producing the ruling immediately. Commentary
This passage is a detailed classification of 'illah (cause) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, specifically the division based on three attributes: being a name (ism), having meaning (ma'na), and producing a ruling (hukm). The author begins by defining these three attributes: an 'illah is a name when it is coined for a ruling and the ruling is primarily attributed to it; it is a meaning when it is effective in causing the ruling; and it is a ruling when the ruling is established immediately upon its existence without delay. If all three are present, the 'illah is complete; otherwise it is deficient. Considering the presence or absence of these three attributes, there are seven possible types. The author then lists the seven types: (1) all three present (complete), (2) name only, (3) meaning only, (4) ruling only, (5) name and meaning, (6) name and ruling, (7) meaning and ruling. However, the author of the matn (the original text) did not mention types (3) and (4) (meaning only and ruling only) but instead mentioned 'illah fi khayr al-asbab and a description having shubhat al-'ilal. The commentary then proceeds to explain the types as given by the author of the matn. The first type is a complete 'illah like an absolute sale that transfers ownership immediately without any option. The second type is like a suspended obligation, e.g., 'You are divorced if you enter the house' — the pronouncement of divorce is a name for divorce but not effective as a ruling until the condition is met, and it has no meaning before that. Similarly, an oath by Allah for expiation is like this. The third type is like a sale with an option (khiyar al-shart) — it is a name and has meaning because it is effective in establishing ownership, but the ruling is delayed until the option is dropped. Also a suspended sale (bay' al-mawquf) is similar. Another example is selling someone else's property without permission — it is a name and meaning for ownership but the ruling is delayed until the owner's permission. Pronouncing divorce with a future time (e.g., 'tomorrow') is also name and meaning but not ruling immediately. The nisab of zakah before the passing of the year is another example. This classification helps in understanding the different types of 'illah in Hanafi usul and how they relate to the attributes of cause and effect.

Key Terms
علة ('illah): The cause to which the obligation of a ruling is primarily attributed in usul al-fiqh.
اسم (ism): An attribute of 'illah meaning it is coined for the ruling and the ruling is attributed to it.
معنى (ma'na): An attribute of 'illah meaning it is effective in causing the ruling.
حكم (hukm): An attribute of 'illah meaning the ruling is established immediately upon its existence without delay.

The Fourth Type of 'Illah: Cause Resembling a Sabab
Defining and exemplifying the fourth type of 'illah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify an 'illah that resembles a sabab, distinguish it from direct causes, and apply the concept to new cases using Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
An 'illah resembling a sabab operates through an intermediary—it is a cause of a cause.

This lesson introduces the fourth category of 'illah in Hanafi usul: a cause that is not direct but acts through an intermediate. The author provides three illustrative examples: purchasing a relative, terminal illness, and the acquittal of witnesses (tazkiyah). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correct qiyas and for analyzing madhhab disputes, as seen in the differing views of Abu Hanifah and the Sahibayn on liability after retraction.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition
Read the Arabic definition: 'عِلَّةٌ فِي شَبَهِ الأَسْبَابِ' meaning a cause that resembles a sabab (direct cause) but is not itself a direct cause. It requires an intermediary to produce the legal effect.
This is a subclass of 'illah; the intermediary is itself a sabab.
2
Examine example 1: purchase of a relative
Purchase (shira') is a cause of ownership (milk), and ownership is a cause of emancipation ('itq). Thus purchase is a 'cause of a cause' ('illat al-'illah). It only leads to emancipation via the intermediary of ownership.
The purchase itself does not free the slave; ownership does.
3
Examine example 2: terminal illness (marad al-mawt)
Terminal illness is a cause for legal interdiction (hajr) in meaning but not in ruling. The actual interdiction is established by death; the illness is a precursor or indirect cause.
The illness restricts transactions in effect, but the formal hajr only applies after death.
4
Analyze example 3: tazkiyah (acquittal of witnesses) and the dispute
Tazkiyah is a cause for accepting the testimony of the original witnesses. If the original witnesses retract, Abu Hanifah holds the acquitters liable because their tazkiyah is an 'illah in shabah al-asbab—they are a cause of a cause. The Sahibayn disagree, viewing tazkiyah as mere testimony about character.
This example shows how the classification affects fiqh rulings (daman vs. no daman).
5
Summarize the thamarah (practical outcome)
The practical effect: Abu Hanifah holds acquitters liable for half the blood-money if they retract after judgment, while the Sahibayn exempt them.
Dispute arises from different views on causation chains.

Commentary
This passage defines the fourth type of 'illah in Hanafi usul: a cause that resembles a sabab (cause) but is not a pure cause. It operates through an intermediary. The author gives three examples. First: buying a relative who is a slave (e.g., one's father or son) causes ownership, and ownership causes emancipation; thus the purchase is a cause of a cause. Second: terminal illness (marad al-mawt) is a cause for the legal restriction (hajr) on the sick person's property in terms of meaning, but not formally, because the restriction is only fully realized upon death. Third: tazkiyah (acquittal of witnesses) according to Abu Hanifah. Here, the acquitters testify to the good character of the original witnesses; if the original witnesses retract, Abu Hanifah holds the acquitters liable for damages, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) do not. The Sahibayn argue that the acquitters merely describe the witnesses' good qualities and are not directly involved in the judgment, so they are like people who praise a defendant and then retract.

Key Terms
علَّة ('illah): A cause that produces a legal effect; in Hanafi usul, 'illah is the effective attribute upon which a ruling is based.
سبب (sabab): An apparent cause that regularly produces its effect, often without the need for an intermediary.

The Fifth, Sixth, and Seventh Types of 'Illah
Classification of the fifth, sixth, and seventh 'illal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the fifth, sixth, and seventh types of 'illah in Hanafi usul, apply each type to classical examples, and explain why the classification matters for analogical reasoning (qiyas).

Core Idea
An 'illah may be only part of a composite, or effective in meaning but not named, or named in law but hiding a true cause.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, legal causes ('illal) are classified into seven types. The first four were covered earlier. This lesson completes the classification by examining the remaining three: a descriptive attribute that resembles a cause because it is one component of a composite cause (type 5), a cause that is effective in meaning and ruling but is not the named basis (type 6), and a cause that is named and carries the ruling but whose effective reality is a hidden attribute (type 7). Understanding these finer distinctions sharpens your ability to evaluate analogical arguments and to appreciate how the Hanafi school articulates its legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fifth type
Look for a description that is only one part of a composite cause. The passage gives the example of quantity (qadr) or genus (jins) for riba: each alone resembles a cause but is not a 'pure cause' – the full cause is the composite.
Some jurists hold that the last component (e.g., genus) is the actual cause.
2
Identify the sixth type
Find a cause that is effective in meaning and ruling but is not the name given to the ruling. In emancipation, if ownership is the last component, it is the effective part; if kinship is the last, it is effective.
The ruling is attributed to the whole composite, but the real effect comes from one part.

Recognize a cause that is the name for the ruling and legally attached to it, but the true effective cause is a hidden attribute. The passage gives travel for the license of shortening prayer (true cause is hardship) and sleep for hadath (true cause is emission of filth).

Three Types of 'Illa: Shibh al-'Illa, 'Illa Ma'nan, 'Illa Hukman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the fifth, sixth, and seventh types of 'illa in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, apply them to examples, and explain their role in legal reasoning.

Core Idea
A 'illa may be composite, with the last part being the true cause, or nominal, where the ruling attaches to the name.

The Hanafi usul classification of 'illa (cause) into seven types is a cornerstone of analogical reasoning (qiyas). This lesson covers the fifth, sixth, and seventh types, which deal with partial causes, the true influential part of a composite cause, and nominal causes that serve as proxies for hidden real causes. Mastering these distinctions sharpens your ability to evaluate qiyas arguments and understand how rulings are attached to descriptions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the fifth type (shibh al-'illa)
The fifth type is a description that resembles a cause but is not fully influential. It is typically one part of a two-part composite cause. For example, in the prohibition of riba, the full cause is quantity and genus together; each alone is only a shibh al-'illa.
The text notes that some scholars consider this type a cause in meaning only.
2
Analyze the sixth type (cause in meaning and ruling, not name)
The sixth type is the last part of a composite cause that actually influences the ruling. The ruling exists when this part is present, but the law formally attaches the ruling to the composite. Example: in emancipation, the cause is kinship and ownership together, but whichever occurs last is the true influent.
The first part then becomes a cause in meaning only (shibh al-'illa).

These are pragmatic where the real cause is hidden or difficult to ascertain.
4
Compare the three types
The fifth type is a partial cause with resemblance; the sixth is the actual influential part of a composite; the seventh is a proxy cause. All three show that not every description that triggers a ruling is the true underlying cause.
The Hanafi school uses this to justify rulings based on apparent descriptions.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A condition can substitute for an absent cause; a sign cannot — this determines liability.

This passage explores a foundational distinction in Hanafi legal theory: the difference between a condition (shart), a cause (illah), and a sign (alama). The discussion uses the example of ihsan (legal marital status) in the punishment for zina. The classification directly affects whether witnesses who retract their testimony are liable for blood money. Understanding this distinction is crucial for analyzing how rulings are attributed to their effective causes.

First, note the three terms: shart (condition), illah (cause), and alama (sign). The text explains that a condition is something on which the existence of the ruling depends, a cause is that to which the ruling is directly attributed, and a sign is merely indicative with no causal or conditional link.
The distinctions are from Hanafi usul, not Arabic grammar.
2

Distinguishing Rights: Yad vs Raqabah in Hanafi Usul
Refutation of a potential contradiction by distinguishing legal rights

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a pledgor's freeing of a pledged slave is effective immediately while freeing in death illness is not, using the Hanafi distinction between right over yad (possession) and raqabah (essence).

Core Idea
The pledgee's right is over possession, not ownership — freeing requires full ownership of the essence (raqabah).

This passage addresses an apparent contradiction in Hanafi fiqh: if freeing a slave in death illness is restricted when it affects creditors' or heirs' rights, why is freeing a pledged slave effective immediately despite the pledgee's right? The answer lies in the nature of the right – the pledgee holds a claim over possession (yad) only, whereas creditors and heirs have a claim over the very essence (raqabah) of the property. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correct application of rulings on freeing slaves and, by analogy, other property disposals during illness or pledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hypothetical Objection
Recognize the objection: 'If freeing in death illness is ineffective when it harms creditors/heirs, why is freeing a pledged slave effective despite the pledgee's right?' This sets up the need for a distinction.
2
State the Hanafi Answer
The answer: the pledgee's right is only over yad (possession/use), while the pledgor retains full ownership of the raqabah (essence). Freeing requires ownership of the raqabah, which the pledgor has.
In death illness, the right of creditors/heirs attaches to the raqabah itself, so freeing can be restricted.
3
Apply the Distinction to Both Cases
Map the rights: In death illness, the right of creditors/heirs is over the asset itself (raqabah) because it forms part of the estate. In a pledge, the pledgee's right is limited to possession; the pledgor still owns the essence.
4
Extract the Usul Principle
The underlying principle: 'The validity of freeing depends on ownership of the raqabah.' Possessory rights do not prevent disposal of the essence. This is an example of istihsan overriding strict qiyas.
Qiyas would suggest both cases are similar (rights of others), but istihsan distinguishes based on the nature of the right.
5
Note the Cross-Madhhab Difference
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that any right over the slave (even possession) prevents freeing, as it impairs full control. The Hanafi school argues that only rights over the essence matter.
This difference leads to different practical rulings.

Commentary
This passage details the two categories of rights of people (huquq al-'ibad) upon death. First: rights owed by the deceased. These are of two sub-types: a) rights attached to specific property (ayn), such as a mortgaged item, rented property, sold item, or deposit. These rights remain attached to the property even after death; the rightful owner can take it, and it does not become part of the general estate. b) Unsecured debts (dayn): these are not enforceable solely on the basis of the deceased's personal liability (dhimma) after death unless there is property or a guarantor (kafil) from his lifetime. If none, the debt is not pursued in this world but is deferred to the Hereafter. Abu Hanifa rules that a guarantee (kafalah) for an insolvent deceased is invalid because the deceased's dhimma is no longer valid. However, if the deceased had property or a guarantor, the guarantee is valid. Voluntary payment of the debt by someone is accepted. In contrast, a bankrupt slave (al-'abd al-mahjur) who acknowledges a debt can have a valid guarantee because his dhimma is intact due to his life and reason. Second: rights owed to the deceased. These are prioritized as: 1) preparation (tajhiz - funeral expenses), 2) debts (to clear the deceased's liability), 3) bequests (up to one-third), and 4) inheritance for heirs. This order considers the strength of need.

Priority of Rights Owed to the Deceased: Tajhiz, Debts, Wasiyyah, Inheritance
Details the priority order of rights owed to the deceased.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the order of priority for claims against the deceased's estate and explain the rationale for each step based on the strength of need.

Core Idea
The needs of the deceased determine priority: first funeral, then debts, then bequests, then inheritance.

The second part of the passage lists the rights owed to the deceased in order of priority. This order is not arbitrary but based on the strength of the deceased's need. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for proper estate distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories of rights owed to the deceased
The passage lists: 1) Tajhiz (funeral preparation), 2) Debts (due from the deceased), 3) Bequests (wasiyyah) up to one-third, 4) Inheritance (for heirs).
2
Understand the rationale for prioritizing tajhiz
Funeral preparation is the strongest need because the deceased requires it immediately for proper burial.
3
Explain why debts come second
Clearing the deceased's debts is necessary to free his soul from liability, which is a stronger need than voluntary bequests.
Debts are obligatory; bequests are voluntary.
4
Explain why bequests come before inheritance
A bequest up to one-third is given priority over the heirs' rights because the need to fulfill the deceased's wishes is stronger than the heirs' claim to the full estate.
5
Describe the nature of inheritance
Inheritance is considered a form of caliphate (succession) from the deceased, benefiting his soul indirectly through the heirs' prayers and charity.

Liability for Intentional Killing Under Coercion: Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Ikhtilaf on qisas for coerced killing

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa, Muhammad, Zufar, Abu Yusuf, and Shafi'i on liability for intentional killing under coercion, and evaluate the Hanafi tarjih.

Core Idea
For coerced killing, Abu Hanifa holds the coercer liable as the real killer; Muhammad and Zufar hold the coerced; Abu Yusuf holds neither due to doubt; Shafi'i holds both.

This passage extends the classification of coerced acts to homicide, revealing a rich ikhtilaf among the early imams. Understanding each position and its underlying reasoning is critical for applying the correct ruling in cases of duress causing death.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
The act in question is intentional killing with a sword under coercion. Note that it falls under the instrumental category.
The passage specifies 'qatl 'amadan bi al-sayf'.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that qisas is obligatory upon the coercer (mukrih) because the coerced is merely a tool like a knife.
This is the relied-upon (mufta bihi) position in the Hanafi school.
3
State Muhammad and Zufar's position
Muhammad and Zufar hold that qisas is upon the coerced because he is the actual doer, even though ordered.
They view the coerced as the direct agent.
4
State Abu Yusuf's position
Abu Yusuf holds that qisas is obligatory upon neither due to the doubt (shubha) that surrounds both parties.
Shubha here refers to the doubt that the coercion creates regarding intention.
5
State Shafi'i's position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that qisas is obligatory upon both: the coercer because he commanded, and the coerced because he performed the act.
This is a cross-madhhab position.
6
Determine the tarjih
The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is Abu Hanifa's view. The tool analogy is considered strongest because the coercer is the true cause.
Diyat and kaffara follow similar rules as noted in the passage.
