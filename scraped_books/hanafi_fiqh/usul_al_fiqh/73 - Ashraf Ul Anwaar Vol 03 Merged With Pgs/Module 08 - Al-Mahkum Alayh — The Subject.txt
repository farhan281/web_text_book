Sufficiency of Summary Belief (Iman Ajmali) for Entering Islam
Stating that summary belief suffices for Islam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that summary belief (iman ajmali) is sufficient for entering Islam, identify the prophetic dalil supporting this, and explain the school's response to the opposing view.

Core Idea
Summary belief in all the Prophet brought suffices for Islam; detailed knowledge is not required.

The Hanafi school holds that a person enters Islam simply by affirming a general belief in everything the Prophet (peace be upon him) brought, without needing to articulate or understand every ruling. This lesson examines the basis for this position, drawing on the Prophet's practice with a Bedouin and a slave girl. We will also consider an opposing view that demands detailed knowledge and the Hanafi response that it leads to undue hardship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
What is the legal ruling being asserted? The passage states that for entering Islam, a summary (ijmali) statement of faith is sufficient. Determine the exact content of that statement.
2
Locate the dalil
What proof does the author cite? Two incidents: the Bedouin testifying about Ramadan, and the slave girl asked about Allah and the Prophet. Notice how the Prophet accepted their simple answers without requiring elaboration.
The hadith are transmitted via ahad chains, thus zanni al-thubut.
3
Analyze the wajh al-dalalah
How does this prove the ruling? The Prophet's acceptance shows that a general affirmation of tawhid and risalah is enough. He did not ask for a list of beliefs or rulings.
The direct application (tatbiq) of the dalil to the ruling is clear.
4
Note the opposing view
Some mashayikh require a detailed (tafsili) statement, such that a person who cannot explain Islam after puberty is considered an apostate. Identify their basis: the gravity of apostasy demands clarity.
This appears to be a minority position within or outside the Hanafi school.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The author rejects the opposing view because it leads to great difficulty (haraj), which the Sharia avoids. Understand the principle that the law is based on ease.
This is a classic application of the maqasid principle of raf' al-haraj.

Introduction to Ahliyyah and the Role of Aql
Definition of legal capacity and intellect in Hanafi usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ahliyyah (legal capacity) and explain the role of 'aql (intellect) as a necessary condition for legal responsibility (taklif) according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Legal capacity requires intellect; without understanding the divine address, obligation is impossible.

The author transitions from discussing the correlates of rulings to the subject of rulings—the mukallaf. He begins by emphasizing that legal capacity (ahliyyah) cannot exist without intellect ('aql), because the divine address (khitab) must be understood; addressing one who does not understand would be repugnant. This foundational principle sets the stage for understanding who is legally responsible in Hanafi jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the new topic
Recognize that the author shifts from muta'alliqat al-ahkam to al-mahkum 'alayhi (the subject of rulings), i.e., the mukallaf.
2
Define ahliyyah
Legal capacity is the capability to be addressed by divine law and to incur rights and obligations.
Hanafi usul distinguishes between capacity for rights and capacity for actions.
3
Recognize the role of 'aql
Intellect is considered (mu'tabar) for establishing ahliyyah; it is a precondition.
4
Explain the rationale
Without 'aql, the khitab cannot be understood, and addressing one who does not understand is repugnant (qabih).
5
Note the nature of 'aql
The passage references that 'aql is a creation (khalq) from Allah, as explained in the Sunnah.
This theological stance differentiates Hanafi usul from other schools.

Ahliyya al-Ada' and Its Two Types: Deficient and Complete Capacity
Defining ahliyya al-ada' and dividing it into qasira and kamila.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ahliyya al-ada', distinguish between its deficient and complete types based on the powers of intellect and body, and apply each type to determine whether an act is valid or obligatory.

Core Idea
Ahliyya al-Ada' rests on two powers: intellect (understanding address) and body (action). Deficiency in either gives deficient capacity—acts valid but not obligatory; full capacity makes acts obligatory.

In Hanafi usul, capacity to perform legal acts (ahliyya al-ada') is not uniform—it depends on intellectual and physical abilities. The source passage defines the two types and their legal consequences. This lesson will guide you through the definition, examples, and application of these concepts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two components of ahliyya al-ada'
Ahliyya al-ada' depends on two powers: the power to understand the Lawgiver's address (qudrat fahm al-khitab), which resides in the intellect ('aql), and the power to act upon it (qudrat al-'amal bihi), which resides in the body (badan). Recognize that both are necessary for full capacity.
These two powers are the basis for the entire classification.
2
Distinguish between deficient (qasira) and complete (kamila) capacity
Deficient capacity arises when either intellect or body is deficient. Complete capacity requires both intellect and body to be fully capable. The text gives examples: a sane minor has deficient body but capable intellect; an adult lunatic has deficient intellect but capable body.
Deficiency can be in one or both components.
3
Apply the legal effect of deficient capacity
For a person with deficient capacity, if they perform an act, it is valid (sahih) but not obligatory (not wajib). Understand that validity does not require obligation; the act is recognized but accountability is suspended.
E.g., a child's prayer is valid but not a duty.
4
Apply the legal effect of complete capacity
Complete capacity triggers obligation (wujub) and the direct address (tawajjuh al-khitab) of the Lawgiver. The person becomes fully mukallaf. The reason given is that obligating before perfection would cause hardship (haraj), which is negated.
Puberty is the practical criterion for complete capacity.
5
Understand the rationale for puberty as the criterion
Since true perfection of intellect is not knowable without great experience, the Lawgiver established puberty (bulugh) as a proxy, because at that age intellect generally becomes balanced. This is a facilitation (taysir).
This is a key example of the Lawgiver's consideration of actual and typical conditions.

Commentary
This passage defines ahliyya al-ada' (capacity for performance) and divides it into two types based on the capacities of intellect and body. The deficient capacity (qasira) arises from deficiency in either intellect or body. A Minor's Acts of Worship: Valid but Not Obligatory
Explains the ruling on a minor's worship acts that oscillate between good and bad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on a minor's acts that are dair bayn al-amrayn and explain the rationale of habituation.

Core Idea
A minor's worship is valid but never obligatory—its purpose is habituation, not liability.

Hanafi usul al-fiqh classifies a minor's legal acts based on their benefit or harm. The third category—acts that oscillate between good and bad, like worship—carries a unique ruling: they are valid when performed by an intelligent minor, but no obligation to complete or make them up. This principle reflects the school's nuanced understanding of deficient legal capacity (ada' qasirah). In this lesson, we will unpack the ruling and its underlying wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Category
Recognize that 'dair bayn al-amrayn' refers to acts that can be seen as both good and bad, such as acts of worship (e.g., prayer). This is the third subtype of a minor's deficient performance (ada' qasirah).
The first two categories are not detailed in this passage but are acts of pure benefit (e.g., accepting gifts) and pure harm (e.g., torts).
2
Extract the Ruling
From the text: if an intelligent minor (sabi 'aqil) performs such an act, it is valid (sahih). However, he is not required to complete it if started, nor to make it up (qada) if broken.
The ruling explicitly separates validity from obligation—a key Hanafi distinction.
3
Understand the Rationale (Ta'lil)
The text states the benefit is purely habituation: by practicing worship, the minor becomes accustomed so that after puberty, it is not difficult. Thus, the act is valid for reward and training, not for fulfilling a duty.
This ta'lil explains why no qada is required—the act is not wajib in the first place.
4
Apply to a Specific Case
Consider a minor who starts the fajr prayer but stops. The prayer is valid for the parts performed, but he has no obligation to complete or repeat it. This contrasts with an adult, who must complete or make up.
The same applies to fasting, Hajj, etc.—all bodily acts of worship.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the term 'marad' (illness) from the matn. It begins by defining illness linguistically and legally: it is a physical condition that disrupts the body's natural equilibrium. The author then clarifies a key point: illness does not negate a person's legal capacity (ahliyyat al-hukm wa al-ibadah). The sick person retains the ability to be addressed by divine commands and to perform valid legal acts such as marriage, divorce, and contracts. However, illness is also a cause of death, which introduces limitations. Since death is pure incapacity, illness as its precursor becomes a cause of incapacity in worship—hence the concessions of sitting and lying down in prayer. Furthermore, death transfers rights to creditors and heirs; thus, illness becomes a cause for the attachment of these rights to the sick person's property. This attachment restricts the sick person's transactions to protect these rights, resulting in a form of interdiction (hajr) limited to the amount of debt and two-thirds of the estate (the share of heirs). This hajr is not immediate but is retroactively applied from the onset of the illness if death occurs. The passage then distinguishes between two types of transactions: those that admit revocation (like gifts and sales below market value) are initially valid but can be overturned if necessary; those that do not admit revocation (like tadbir or emancipation) are treated as suspended upon death and are only valid to the extent that they do not harm creditors' or heirs' rights. This is a classic Hanafi treatment of the rights of the terminally ill.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the term 'marad' (illness) from the matn. It begins by defining illness linguistically and legally: it is a physical condition that disrupts the body's natural equilibrium. The author then clarifies a key point: illness does not negate a person's legal capacity (ahliyyat al-hukm wa al-ibadah). The sick person retains the ability to be addressed by divine commands and to perform valid legal acts such as marriage, divorce, and contracts. However, illness is also a cause of death, which introduces limitations. Since death is pure incapacity, illness as its precursor becomes a cause of incapacity in worship—hence the concessions of sitting and lying down in prayer. Furthermore, death transfers rights to creditors and heirs; thus, illness becomes a cause for the attachment of these rights to the sick person's property. This attachment restricts the sick person's transactions to protect these rights, resulting in a form of interdiction (hajr) limited to the amount of debt and two-thirds of the estate (the share of heirs). This hajr is not immediate but is retroactively applied from the onset of the illness if death occurs. The passage then distinguishes between two types of transactions: those that admit revocation (like gifts and sales below market value) are initially valid but can be overturned if necessary; those that do not admit revocation (like tadbir or emancipation) are treated as suspended upon death and are only valid to the extent that they do not harm creditors' or heirs' rights. This is a classic Hanafi treatment of the rights of the terminally ill.

Ignorance as an Excuse for Agents and Slaves
Ruling on ignorance of appointment or dismissal for agents and slaves

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule distinguishing excusable ignorance of fact from non-excusable ignorance of law for agents and authorized slaves.

Core Idea
Ignorance of a fact excuses; ignorance of an accessible law does not.

Does ignorance always excuse a person in Islamic law? Not according to the Hanafi school. This lesson examines a crucial usul rule: when an agent or authorized slave acts without knowledge of their appointment or dismissal, their ignorance excuses their actions. However, when they know the facts but are ignorant of the legal ruling (and could have learned it), that ignorance is no excuse. We will unpack this distinction through two clear scenarios involving agents and slaves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios for agents/slaves
The passage distinguishes when the agent or slave is ignorant of the authorization (appointment) itself versus ignorant of the revocation. In each case, the consequence differs.
First scenario: they transact without knowing they were ever authorized. Second: they transact without knowing they were dismissed.
2
Apply the principle: ignorance of fact vs. law
Note that in the first scenario, ignorance is of the fact of authorization (a fact), so it excused. In the second scenario, they know the authorization but not the dismissal (another fact), so the transaction is valid because their ignorance of the dismissal is also a fact. The passage contrasts this with the earlier discussion of minors: ignorance of the legal option (khiyar) is ignorance of law, which is excused only if knowledge is inaccessible.
The key criterion: whether knowledge of the matter was accessible in the abode of Islam.
3
Understand the rationale (talil)
The reasoning: the agent/slave could not have known the change in status without the news reaching them. Hence, their ignorance of the factual event (appointment or dismissal) is excusable. The same logic applies to the minor who never heard of the marriage contract.
This is a 'talil' (reason-oriented) argument: the effective cause is the inability to access knowledge of the fact.

This passage presents two scenarios where ignorance serves as an excuse in legal transactions. Jest (Hazl): Definition, Condition, and Distinction from Khiyar
Defines jest, distinguishes from seriousness, and states condition for nullifying legal effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define hazl in Hanafi usul, identify its condition for affecting contract validity, and distinguish it from khiyar al-shart.

Core Idea
Jest invalidates a contract only if explicitly stated beforehand; implicit jest has no legal effect.

In Hanafi legal theory, the intention behind contractual words determines their effect. This lesson examines hazl (jest)—speech where the speaker does not seriously intend the legal consequences. We will see how the Hanafi school defines hazl, contrasts it with seriousness, and sets a strict condition for it to nullify a contract. Understanding this clarifies when contractual consent is valid and when it is not.

Guided Path
1
Define hazl and jidd
Read the opening definition. Hazl is using a word to mean something other than its literal or figurative sense; jidd is the opposite—using a word as intended.
The text describes hazl as 'pure play' (la'ab mahd).
2
Identify the legal effect of hazl
Hazl indicates the speaker consents to the act (uttering the words) but not to the ruling (the legal consequence). This lack of consent to the ruling makes the contract invalid.
The text says 'yunafi ikhtiyar al-hukm wa al-rida bihi'.
3
Compare hazl with khiyar al-shart
Both involve limited consent, but hazl invalidates the contract entirely, while khiyar al-shart does not. In khiyar, the party consents to the ruling conditionally.
Key difference: 'al-mazl yufsid al-bay', wa khiyar al-shart la yufsiduhu.
4
State the condition for hazl to have legal effect
The condition is that the parties explicitly state before the contract that they are jesting. It cannot be inferred from circumstances or implied by conduct.
The text: 'shartuhu an yakuna sarihan mashrutan bil-lisan'.

It is that the intended meaning of an expression is not what the word is placed for, nor what is appropriate for it via metaphor; that is, the expression is not taken in its literal or metaphorical meaning, but is pure play. It is the opposite of seriousness (al-jidd), which is that the intended meaning is what the word is placed for or what is appropriate for it via metaphor. And it (jest) contradicts the choice of the ruling and consent to it, but does not contradict consent to the direct action. That is, the jester does not choose the ruling nor is content with it, but he is content with performing the cause. Because the utterance is from valid consent and choice, but he does not intend or consent to the ruling. So the jest is like a conditional option (khiyar al-shart) indefinitely in sale, due to lack of consent to the ruling of sale, not due to lack of consent to the sale itself. Commentary
This passage provides a definition and legal analysis of hazl (jest) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It first defines hazl as using a word to mean something other than its literal or metaphorical meaning, essentially as play. It distinguishes hazl from seriousness (jidd). The key legal point is that hazl indicates lack of true consent to the legal ruling (hukm), though the person consents to the act itself. This makes hazl analogous to a conditional option (khiyar al-shart) in that both involve lack of full consent. However, the texts stress a crucial difference: hazl invalidates the contract (e.g., sale) while khiyar al-shart does not. The condition for hazl to have legal effect is that it must be explicitly stated before the contract (e.g., 'we are jesting'), and it cannot be inferred from mere circumstances. It is not required to be mentioned within the contract itself. This distinguishes it from khiyar al-shart, which must be stipulated within the contract. Key Terms
هزل (hazl): The use of contractual language in jest, meaning the speaker does not intend the legal consequences.
جد (jidd): Seriousness, the opposite of hazl; intended meaning matches the word's literal or metaphorical sense.
خيار الشرط (khiyar al-shart): An option stipulated in a contract to cancel it within a specified period.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi usul, jest (hazl) invalidates a contract only if the parties explicitly state their jest before the contract; implicit jest does not affect validity.
