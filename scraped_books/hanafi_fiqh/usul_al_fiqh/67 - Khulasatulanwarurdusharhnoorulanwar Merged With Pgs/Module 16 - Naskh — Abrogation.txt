Three Types of Qada' in Hanafi Usul
Classifying qada' into three types with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the three categories of qada' (making up missed acts) in Hanafi usul, distinguishing each by its basis in reason or revelation.

Core Idea
Qada' can replicate the original (rational), substitute by revelation (irrational), or partially resemble performance.

In Hanafi usul, qada' (making up a missed obligation) is not monolithic. The classical scholars classified it into three types based on how the compensatory act relates to the original. This passage from Nur al-Anwar explains each type with examples: fasting for fasting, fidyah for fasting, and missed takbirat performed in ruku. Understanding these categories clarifies how the Law fulfills obligations when exact repetition is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of qada'
List the three types mentioned in the passage: qada bi-mithl al-ma'qul, qada bi-mithl ghayr al-ma'qul, and qada mushabah bil-ada'.
2
Analyze the first type: qada bi-mithl al-ma'qul
This is the rational substitute. Example: making up a fast by fasting. Reason can see the similarity: both involve abstention.
Also called qada bi-mithlin ma'qul.
3
Analyze the second type: qada bi-mithl ghayr al-ma'qul
The substitute is not rationally similar. Example: fidyah for fasting. Reason sees no similarity—fasting is hunger, fidyah is feeding. Only Sharia commands it.
The Quranic basis is Surah al-Baqarah 2:184.
4
Analyze the third type: qada mushabah bil-ada'
This hybrid resembles performance. Example: missed Eid takbirat performed in ruku. It is qada because the time (standing) passed, but resembles ada' because ruku includes partial standing and catching the rak'ah.
Precautionary ruling based on the principle of catching the imam in ruku.

Commentary
This passage expands on the types of qada (making up a missed obligation). The author identifies three categories: First, qada bi-mithl al-ma'qul: where the replacement resembles the original in a way understandable by reason, such as making up a missed fast by fasting again. Second, qada bi-mithl ghayr al-ma'qul: where the similarity is established only by Sharia, not reason, like making up a fast with fidyah (monetary compensation) because fasting involves abstention and fidyah involves feeding, which are opposites in purpose. The Quranic basis is cited (Surah al-Baqarah 2:184). Third, qada mushabah bil-ada': when the compensatory act resembles the original performance, such as performing missed takbirat of Eid prayer while in ruku. This is considered qada because the proper time (standing) has passed, but it resembles ada' because ruku still involves partial standing and the person catches the rak'ah. This classification is significant in Hanafi usul for understanding how obligations are fulfilled when the original act cannot be repeated exactly.

قضاء بمثل غیر معقول (qada' bi-mithl ghayr al-ma'qul): Making up by something whose similarity to the original is known only through revelation
قضاء مشابہ بالا داء (qada' mushabah bil-ada'): Making up that resembles performance in that the compensatory act retains some element of the original act's timing or nature

Effect of Abrogation on Permissibility
Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on permissibility after abrogation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the effect of abrogation on permissibility and contrast it with the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
Abrogation of obligation removes permissibility entirely; a new proof is needed for any remaining recommendation.

This lesson examines a classic dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the fate of an act whose obligation has been abrogated. Does the act retain its permissibility? The Hanafi school argues that abrogation removes the entire command, while the Shafi'i school maintains that permissibility may survive. Understanding this dispute illuminates how each school reads the relationship between command and permissibility in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
What exactly is being disputed? The core question: when the obligation of a commanded act is abrogated, does its permissibility remain?
2
Identify the Hanafi position and its dalil
The Hanafi school holds that permissibility does not remain. Their evidence: the example from Bani Israel where a ruling (cutting limbs) was abrogated by consensus, so its permissibility was also abrogated.
3
Identify the Shafi'i position and its dalil
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that permissibility remains. His evidence: the fast of 'Ashura was initially obligatory, then abrogated, but its recommendation remains.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Hanafis respond that the recommendation of 'Ashura was established by a separate proof, not by survival of the original permissibility. Abrogation removes the entire command, so any remaining permissibility must be proven anew.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if someone performs an act whose obligation has been abrogated, for Hanafis it is not valid as a religious observance, while Shafi'is may consider it valid as nafl if performed with proper intention.

Abrogation of Wajib and the Fate of Permissibility
Hanafi vs Shafi'i dispute on naskh and jawaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on whether permissibility (jawaz) remains after wajib is abrogated, and evaluate the Shafi'i argument using the case of fasting on Ashura.

Core Idea
Abrogation of wajib does not leave permissibility; permissibility requires its own text.

In this passage, we encounter a classic usul dispute between the Hanafis and Imam al-Shafi'i. The question is: when an act that was wajib is abrogated, does its permissibility (jawaz) automatically remain? The Hanafis say no, while the Shafi'is say yes. Understanding this dispute requires us to examine how each school uses textual evidence and analogy. We'll break down the arguments and see the practical consequence in the case of kaffarah yamin.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested issue
State the specific legal question: does the attribute of permissibility (jawaz) persist after the attribute of obligation (wujub) is removed through naskh?
2
Contrast the two positions
The Hanafi position (from the matn) is that permissibility does not remain. Imam al-Shafi'i holds that it does remain. Identify who says what.
3
Analyze the supporting proofs
Shafi'i cites the fast of Ashura (originally fard, now mustahabb) as evidence that permissibility survives. Hanafis respond with an analogy from Bani Israel (amputation of limbs abrogated, so permissibility also ceased) and argue that Ashura's recommendation comes from a separate text.
Note that the Hanafi proof is based on qiyas with a previous shariah.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The disagreement affects kaffarah yamin: after the obligation to give kaffarah before swearing is abrogated, Shafi'is still permit it, while Hanafis do not.
This shows that the dispute is not merely theoretical.

Arguing that a general can abrogate a specific ruling

Core Idea
A general text can abrogate a specific one if irreconcilable and the general is later.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi usul principle: the permissibility of abrogating a specific ruling by a general text. Through the example of the urine hadith, you will see how Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf used a general command to override a specific permission, and how the objection regarding joint abrogation was answered.

Response to Objection on Sā'ima Camels and Zakat
Refutation of an objection using naskh distinction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between carrying mutlaq to muqayyad (haml) and abrogation (naskh) in Hanafi usul, and apply this distinction to the case of zakat on camels.

Core Idea
Hanafis did not carry mutlaq to muqayyad; a separate hadith abrogated the absoluteness, preserving the principle.

In this lesson, we examine a common objection against the Hanafi school: that they violate their own principle when they restrict the general hadith on zakat for camels. The objection claims they carried the mutlaq to the muqayyad against the rule. The Hanafi response demonstrates a sophisticated usul operation: not haml (carrying), but naskh (abrogation) by a separate, specific hadith. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the mutlaq-muqayyad rule.

Guided Path
1
State the Objection
The objector claims that Hanafis violate their principle that when mutlaq and muqayyad share the same cause, the mutlaq remains absolute. In zakat on camels, they carried the mutlaq hadith to the muqayyad.
The cause is the obligation of zakat on camels.
2
Identify the Hanafi Principle
The Hanafi rule: if mutlaq and muqayyad appear in the same cause, the mutlaq is kept absolute. It is not automatically restricted.
This is to preserve the generality of the command until a specific proof justifies restriction.
3
Analyze the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response denies that they carried the mutlaq to the muqayyad. Instead, they present a separate hadith that explicitly negates zakat on non-sā'ima camels. This hadith acts as an abrogator (nāsikh) of the absoluteness of the first mutlaq hadith.
The separate hadith is: 'Laysa fī al-ʿawāmil wa al-ḥawāmil wa al-ʿalūfa ṣadaqa.'
4
Differentiate Haml from Naskh
Haml (carrying mutlaq to muqayyad) would impose a condition on the same ruling. Naskh (abrogation) uses a new, independent proof to override the earlier ruling. Here, the new hadith does not merely restrict; it removes the ruling for non-sā'ima camels entirely.
This preserves the Hanafi principle because the mutlaq is not being restricted by the muqayyad; it is being abrogated by a separate text.
5
Evaluate the Evidence
The muqayyad hadith ('fī khams mina al-ibil al-sā'ima shāh') remains in effect for sā'ima camels. The mutlaq hadith is abrogated for non-sā'ima by the third hadith. Thus, the Hanafi position is consistent.
The practical result: zakat is only due on sā'ima camels, but the usul operation is naskh, not haml.

Narrator Acting Contrary to Hadith After Narration Invalidates the Hadith
Explains the second form of hadith criticism: narrator's contrary action after narration indicates abrogation or fabrication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a narrator's contrary action indicates abrogation or fabrication, and apply the Hanafi principle that such contradiction renders the hadith saqit al-'amal.

Core Idea
A narrator's contradiction after narration must be due to abrogation or fabrication — never mere negligence.

This passage explores a critical tool in Hanafi hadith criticism: the narrator's own practice. If a trustworthy narrator acts contrary to a hadith he himself transmitted, the hadith cannot be arbitrarily abandoned; his action must be grounded in definitive evidence of abrogation or fabrication. Understanding this principle helps you evaluate hadith applicability and safeguard against hasty dismissal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two possible reasons for narrator contradiction
List the two legitimate explanations given in the text for a narrator acting contrary to a hadith he narrated: abrogation (naskh) or fabrication (wad'). Note that these are the only acceptable reasons; otherwise the narrator's reliability is impugned.
The text states it cannot be imagined that a muhaddith acts arbitrarily.
2
Examine the example of Abu Hurayra
Understand why Abu Hurayra's practice of washing three times instead of seven is justified. The earlier ruling of seven washings was abrogated by a later relaxation, and Abu Hurayra had definitive knowledge of this.
The example illustrates naskh as a valid reason for contradiction.
3
Apply the condition 'with certainty'
The text adds a crucial condition: the contradiction must be certain (yaqin), not merely a difference in interpretation. If the narrator's action is based on a different plausible meaning of the hadith, the hadith remains operative.
This distinguishes between abrogation and ta'wil (interpretive difference).
4
Derive the ruling for the hadith
If the contradiction is due to naskh, the hadith is saqit al-'amal (inoperative) but its transmission remains authentic. If due to wad', the hadith is fabricated and not a valid transmission. If due to negligence, the narrator's 'adala is impugned.
The ruling impacts both the hadith's applicability and the narrator's reliability.

Preserving Hadith Authority Despite Narrator's Uncertain Contradiction
Stating rulings for two exceptional scenarios to preserve hadith authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a narrator's contradictory action before narration or with unknown date does not invalidate the hadith, and justify it using the principle 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk'.

Core Idea
A narrator's contradiction before narration or with unknown date preserves the hadith; certainty is not removed by doubt.

We have already covered the ruling when a narrator's contrary action occurs after narration — that invalidates the hadith. But what if the contrary action happened before the narrator even heard the hadith? Or if we simply don't know when it happened? This passage resolves those two scenarios, reinforcing that doubt cannot override the original certainty of a transmitted hadith. The Hanafi tradition applies a core legal maxim here that you'll find throughout fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two new scenarios
The text presents two possibilities: the narrator's contrary action occurred (1) before hearing the narration, or (2) at an unknown time (could be before or after). Recognize these as distinct from the already-ruled post-narration scenario.
These are the 'remaining cases' after covering post-narration contradiction.
2
Understand the proof for scenario 1 (pre-narration)
If the action was before narration, the narrator simply had not encountered the hadith yet; he followed his own ijtihad until the evidence reached him. Upon hearing the hadith, he would abandon his prior opinion and follow the text. This does not impugn the hadith's authority.
The narrator is a mujtahid who changes when presented with stronger evidence.
3
Understand the proof for scenario 2 (unknown date)
When the timing is unknown, we apply the maxim 'certainty is not removed by doubt.' The hadith is a certain proof (yaqin) until proven otherwise. The possibility that the action occurred after narration creates doubt, but doubt cannot overturn certainty. Thus the hadith remains valid.
This is a rational (aqli) proof based on epistemological priority.
4
Contrast with the post-narration scenario
If the contrary action occurs after narration, the narrator's change indicates he rejected the hadith, which invalidates it. The distinction hinges on the timing relative to awareness of the narration.
The ruling changes because the narrator's action after knowledge contradicts acceptance.
5
Apply the legal maxim
The maxim 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk' (certainty is not removed by doubt) is central. For the unknown-date case, the original certainty of the hadith as hujja remains; the uncertain timing does not weaken it.
This maxim is universally applied in Hanafi usul and fiqh.

Narrator's Refraining from Action Invalidates the Hadith as Proof
Explaining the rule: narrator's inaction invalidates the hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a narrator's refraining from acting on his own narration invalidates the hadith as evidence, and explain the example of Ibn Umar and raf' al-yadayn.

Core Idea
A narrator's refusal to act on his own narration invalidates the hadith; it signals abrogation.

We have seen that a narrator contradicting his own narration renders the hadith unreliable. The author now adds another scenario: even if the narrator does not contradict but merely refrains from acting upon it, the same consequence applies. The Hanafi school argues that such inaction is evidence of abrogation (naskh) or defect. This is a key principle in evaluating hadith evidence, especially when the narrator is a Companion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its scope
The rule: if a narrator refrains from acting on his own narration, that hadith cannot be used as evidence. It is treated like direct contradiction. Scope: applies to any hadith where the narrator's action contradicts his transmission.
The rule is based on the principle that a Companion's practice is a source of legal knowledge, and their inaction indicates abrogation.
2
Analyze the example: Ibn Umar and raf' al-yadayn
Ibn Umar narrated that the Prophet (peace be upon him) used to raise his hands at the time of bowing and after raising from bowing. However, his own practice, as testified by his student Mujahid, was to only raise his hands at the opening takbeer. This is a clear case of refraining from action.
The hadith is well-known: 'He (the Prophet) used to raise his hands when he went into bowing and when he raised his head from bowing.' (Reported by Bukhari and Muslim).
3
Apply the rule to the example
Since Ibn Umar, the narrator, refrained from acting on his own narration, the Hanafi school considers the hadith to be defective (saqit) and not authoritative. Therefore, it cannot be used as evidence for the permissibility of raising hands at those points.
The practical consequence: the Hanafi position on raf' al-yadayn is that it is only performed at the opening takbeer.
4
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The reasoning is that if the hadith were still operative and not abrogated, the Companion would have acted upon it. His refraining is evidence that the ruling has been abrogated (naskh) or that the hadith is weak. The text says: 'ترك العمل به دلیل علی انتساخه' (abandoning action upon it is evidence of its abrogation).
This reasoning is based on the Hanafi usul principle that the actions of a Companion can qualify or abrogate a hadith they themselves transmitted.
5
Note the inter-madhhab dispute
The Shafi'i school holds that the hadith is authentic and that Ibn Umar may have forgotten or had a different reason for not practicing it, but not abrogation. They argue that the hadith is still operative. This leads to a practical difference: Hanafis do not raise hands except at the opening takbeer, while Shafi'is raise hands at the beginning of prayer, before bowing, and after rising from bowing.
The dispute highlights how a Companion's practice can be interpreted differently in usul al-fiqh.

Conditions for True Conflict (Ta'arud) in Hanafi Usul
Explaining the cause and condition of ta'arud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the cause of apparent conflict between evidences and apply the Hanafi condition of equality to determine whether true ta'arud exists.

Core Idea
True ta'arud requires equality; otherwise, the superior evidence prevails and no conflict exists.

The Lawgiver's speech is free from real contradiction, yet scholars discuss apparent conflict (ta'arud) to resolve interpretive difficulties. This lesson clarifies that ta'arud arises only when we are ignorant of abrogation (naskh) and when the two evidences are equal in rank and attributes. We also see a sample objection about placement and the response that follows al-Bazdawi's arrangement.

Guided Path
1
Define ta'arud and its essence
Identify that ta'arud is an apparent conflict between two shari evidences, not a real contradiction. The passage stresses that it 'seems' (lagta hai) that one evidence contradicts another.
Apparent, not real – the speech of the Lawgiver is free from inconsistency.
2
Understand the objection and response
Note the objection: why place ta'arud here instead of in the chapter of tarjih? The answer: the matan author followed al-Bazdawi. This shows that arrangement is a matter of authorial choice, not substance.
The objection is meta-commentary; it does not affect the ruling.
3
Identify the cause of apparent conflict
The passage states that conflict appears only when it is unknown whether one evidence is abrogated (naskh). If naskh is known, the conflict disappears because the abrogated evidence is no longer operative.
Naskh is a key tool to resolve apparent contradictions.
4
State the condition of equality (musawat)
The first condition for true ta'arud is that both evidences must be equal in rank (martabah) and attributes. If one is superior in essence (dhat) or attribute (sifah), the superior one is operative and the weaker is disregarded.
Superiority can be in clarity, strength, or other uṣūlī attributes.
5
Apply to the example: mufassar vs muhkam
The passage gives an example: conflict cannot occur between mufassar (clearly detailed) and muhkam (firm). In Hanafi usul, muhkam is superior in clarity, so the muhkam prevails and no ta'arud exists.
This illustrates the practical application of the equality condition.

Resolving Conflict by Naskh: The Pregnant Widow's Iddah
Applying naskh to resolve contradiction between two verses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of naskh based on chronological priority (ikhtilaf al-zaman) to resolve apparent contradictions between Quranic verses, using the iddah of a pregnant widow as a case study.

Core Idea
A later verse abrogates an earlier one — the pregnant widow's iddah ends with delivery, not four months and ten days.

How do we resolve when two Quranic verses seem to command different things? The Hanafi school employs the principle of naskh (abrogation) grounded in known chronological order. In this lesson, you will study a classic case: the iddah of a pregnant widow. Surah al-Baqarah (2:234) prescribes four months and ten days for widows generally; Surah al-Talaq (65:4) sets the iddah of pregnant women as delivery. Since 65:4 was revealed later, it abrogates the earlier general rule for pregnant widows. Master this reasoning to handle similar conflicts in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent contradiction
State the two rulings: 2:234 gives a general iddah of four months and ten days for widows (including pregnant ones); 65:4 says pregnant women's iddah is until delivery. Note how they seem to conflict for a pregnant widow.
The conflict is between a general ruling (amm) and a specific ruling (khass) that appears to differ.
2
Establish the chronology of revelation
Determine which verse was revealed earlier and which later. Historical reports (e.g., the ijtihad of Caliph Umar) indicate that Surah al-Talaq was revealed after Surah al-Baqarah. This is explicit difference in time (ikhtilaf al-zaman sarihan).
If chronology were unknown, naskh might not apply; other reconciling methods would be used.
3
Apply the principle of naskh
The earlier ruling (2:234) is abrogated (mansukh) for the subset of pregnant widows because the later ruling (65:4) is naskh. The later verse takes precedence and renders the earlier ruling as if non-existent for this case.
Hanafi usul holds that a later Quranic verse can abrogate an earlier one (naskh al-Qur'an bi al-Qur'an).
4
Derive the operative ruling
Conclude that the iddah of a pregnant widow is until delivery, even if she gives birth moments after the husband's death. This is the mufta bihi Hanafi position.
The ruling directly follows from the naskh; no further reconciliation is needed.
5
Note the ikhtilaf
Some early scholars (e.g., Ibn Mas'ud) held that both verses remain in force, requiring the pregnant widow to observe the longer period. Understand the Hanafi response: the chronology is established and naskh is operative.
This ikhtilaf is legitimate; the Hanafi tarjih is based on clear evidence of chronology and the later verse's specificity.

Naskh by Difference in Time: The Pregnant Widow's Iddah
Explains naskh via chronological order with pregnant widow example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of naskh by chronological order to resolve apparent contradictions between Quranic verses, and explain the practical difference between the positions of Ali (ra) and Ibn Mas'ud (ra).

Core Idea
When two verses contradict, the later abrogates the earlier — chronology is key.

This passage explains a fundamental usul al-fiqh principle: how an explicit difference in time of revelation resolves contradiction between two nusus. The pregnant widow's iddah serves as a concrete example where two Quranic verses appear to conflict. By establishing which verse came later, the Hanafi school applies naskh and derives a clear ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two apparently conflicting texts
Locate the two Quranic verses: Surah al-Baqarah 2:234 (four months ten days for widows) and Surah al-Talaq 65:4 (pregnant women until delivery). Note their general applicability.
The conflict arises when a woman is both a widow and pregnant.
2
Determine the relationship between the texts
Analyse the relationship as 'umum wa khusus min wajh': they overlap in the case of a pregnant widow (common aspect) and are exclusive in other cases (widow non-pregnant, pregnant non-widow).
This relationship is key to resolving the contradiction.
3
Establish chronological priority
Use historical knowledge to determine which verse was revealed later. The passage states that Surah al-Talaq was revealed after Surah al-Baqarah.
Certainty of chronology is essential for naskh.
4
Apply naskh in the common aspect
Since the later verse (al-Talaq) specifically addresses pregnant women, it abrogates the earlier general ruling (al-Baqarah) for the common case (pregnant widow). Thus the iddah ends with delivery.
The earlier ruling becomes mansukh and is treated as non-existent for that case.
5
Evaluate the Companion disagreement
Consider the view of Ali (ra): due to uncertainty about chronology, he prefers taking the longer period. Contrast with Ibn Mas'ud (ra) and the Hanafi position: with known chronology, naskh applies.
The thamarah al-khilaf is whether the widow can remarry immediately after delivery.

(3) Second exclusive aspect: a woman who is a widow but not pregnant. In the exclusive aspects there is no difficulty; so the waiting period of a widow (only) is four months and ten days according to the verse of al-Baqarah, and a pregnant woman (only) has her waiting period as delivery according to the verse of al-Talaq. However, difficulty arises in the common aspect because this woman has two identities: should we consider her a widow or a pregnant woman? The commentator (may Allah have mercy on him) has transmitted here the disagreement of the jurist Companions, Sayyiduna Ali and Sayyiduna Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (may Allah be pleased with them). The madhhab of Sayyiduna Ali (may Allah be pleased with him): The fourth Caliph, Sayyiduna Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) says that such a woman should observe the longer of the two terms: meaning one looks whether delivery occurs before four months and ten days or after. If earlier, then she waits for four months and ten days; if later, then even after four months and ten days pass, she remains seated until she gives birth. The madhhab of Sayyiduna Abdullah ibn Mas'ud: The waiting period of such a woman is only delivery. The excuse of Sayyiduna Ali (may Allah be pleased with him) is that he says: 'We do not know whether the verse of Surah al-Baqarah is earlier or that of Surah al-Talaq. Therefore, due to the unknown chronology, we cannot declare one as abrogated and the other as abrogator, and the way of reconciliation is that she observes the longer of the two terms as her waiting period.'

Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the usul rule that a clear chronological difference between two contradictory texts (nass) leads to naskh (abrogation) of the earlier by the later. The author first divides time difference into two types: 'صراحة' (explicit, where historical evidence shows which is earlier) and 'دلالة' (implied, not elaborated here). The example given is the famous case of a pregnant widow's iddah (waiting period after husband's death). Two Quranic verses appear contradictory: Surah al-Baqarah (2:234) prescribes four months and ten days for widows in general, and Surah al-Talaq (65:4) prescribes until delivery for pregnant women. When a woman is both a widow and pregnant, which rule applies? The author states that since Surah al-Talaq is later (revealed after al-Baqarah), it abrogates the earlier ruling for pregnant widows; thus her iddah ends with delivery. This is the relied-upon Hanafi position. The passage then cites Mulla Jiyun's analysis of the relationship between the two verses as 'عموم خصوص من وجہ' (general and specific from one aspect), meaning they overlap in some cases (the common aspect) and differ in others (the exclusive aspects). For a widow who is not pregnant, the Baqarah verse applies; for a pregnant woman whose husband has not died, the Talaq verse applies; but for a pregnant widow (common aspect), the later verse (Talaq) abrogates the earlier (Baqarah). The author also reports a disagreement between two prominent Companions: Sayyiduna Ali (ra) held that in the common aspect, the woman should observe the longer of the two terms (four months ten days or delivery) because he was uncertain which verse was revealed later. Sayyiduna Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (ra) held that only delivery matters, taking the later verse as decisive. The Hanafi madhhab follows Ibn Mas'ud's view, as the chronological order is known. This passage illustrates both the rule of naskh by time and the importance of knowing the chronological order of revelation.

منسوخ (mansukh): The earlier ruling that is abrogated and becomes ineffective.

Abrogation of the Widow's 'Iddah: The View of Ibn Mas'ud and the Hanafi School

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The verse on the 'iddah of pregnant widows is abrogated because the revelation chronology establishes the later verse supersedes the earlier.

The duration of the 'iddah for a widow who is pregnant has been a point of scholarly discussion. This lesson examines the position of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud, supported by the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools and the majority of the Salaf, that the verse in Surah al-Talaq abrogates the general waiting period of four months and ten days prescribed in Surah al-Baqarah. Understanding this case clarifies how the principle of naskh (abrogation) is applied based on the known order of revelation.

Guided Path

Naskh requires established chronology and mutual exclusivity of rulings.

Bayan Tabdil: Naskh as Exposition and the Shafi'i Dispute
Defines bayan tabdil as naskh and discusses the forms of abrogation with ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the concept of bayan tabdil as naskh, distinguish the Hanafi position on permissible forms of naskh from the Shafi'i position, and apply the underlying usul principle to given scenarios.

Core Idea
Naskh is bayan tabdil: from Allah's perspective, it is mere exposition of an eternal decree, not a change in knowledge.

This passage introduces an important usul concept: bayan tabdil, which is equated with naskh (abrogation). The text emphasizes that from the Lawgiver's perspective, naskh is not a true change but merely a manifestation of a pre-eternal divine decree. It then outlines the Hanafi position on the forms of abrogation and contrasts it with the Shafi'i view, setting the stage for understanding how differences in usul principles lead to divergent fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
Define bayan tabdil and its relationship to naskh
Identify that bayan tabdil is a type of bayan (exposition) that consists of naskh. Understand that from Allah's knowledge, the ruling was always planned; it only appears as a change to us.
The passage uses the Qur'anic verse 'ما ننسخ من آية أو ننسها' to establish this.
2
Recognize the Hanafi view on the forms of naskh
List the four permissible forms: Book by Book, Book by Sunnah, Sunnah by Sunnah, Sunnah by Book. Note that qiyas and ijma' cannot abrogate the Book or Sunnah.
This is the Hanafi position; it allows cross-type naskh.
3
Identify the Shafi'i restriction
Imam al-Shafi'i only allows naskh within the same type: Book by Book and Sunnah by Sunnah. He argues that Sunnah explains the Book and cannot override it.
This is the key ikhtilaf in this passage.
4
Analyze the epistemological basis
Understand why the Hanafis view naskh as bayan: Allah's knowledge is eternal, so the later ruling was always known. Thus, naskh is merely revealing what was always intended.
This contrasts with the perception of change from the human perspective.
5
Apply the dispute to a concrete example
Consider the abrogation of the previous qibla (Bayt al-Maqdis) by the Ka'ba. In Hanafi, this is considered naskh of a Qur'anic ruling by a later Qur'anic verse (or by Sunnah according to some). Examine how the Shafi'i position would treat the same example.
The change of qibla is a classic case of naskh.

The Hanafi Definition of Naskh: Clarification, Not Change
Definition and foundational rules of naskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define naskh from the Hanafi perspective, explain its dual nature as bayan from Allah and change from our perspective, and identify the groups that reject it and why.

Core Idea
Naskh is Allah’s clarification of a ruling's duration—only a change from our limited human perspective.

Naskh is a foundational concept in usul al-fiqh. The Hanafi school defines it not as an alteration in divine will but as a disclosure of the finite term a ruling was always meant to have. This lesson unpacks that definition and the groups that deny it.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic and technical definition
Note that naskh is equated with tabdīl (substitution) in the source. The Quranic verses 'When We substitute one verse for another' and 'Whatever verse We abrogate…' link the two terms. Technically, naskh is the lifting of a ruling by a later divine text.
Linguistically, naskh means removal or copying; here it means removal (rafʻ).
2
Grasp the bayan perspective
From the Lawgiver's side, naskh is pure bayān (explanation) because Allah knew the ruling was temporary. The change only appears so to us. This is key to the Hanafi concept that naskh does not imply a change in divine knowledge.
This refutes the charge that naskh implies badā’ (change in divine will).
3
Identify the rejectors
The text lists Jews, Rāfiḍa, Khawārij, and Muʻtazila as those who do not accept naskh. Understand their motivations – often based on a misunderstanding of divine immutability.
Each group’s reasoning differs; the Hanafi position refutes them collectively.
4
Grasp the condition for abrogation
Only the Quran and Sunnah can abrogate. Qiyas and ijma‘ lack the authority to repeal divine texts. This establishes the hierarchy: the nasikh must come from a source equal or superior in authority.
This sets up the four-form analysis in the next lesson.

(5) Explanation of Tabdil. The shaikh also calls it, as Allah Almighty said: 'When We substitute one verse for another' and in another place: 'Whatever verse We abrogate or cause to be forgotten, We bring a better one or the like.' So naskh and tabdil are the same thing, and it is from one perspective a bayan (clarification) and from another perspective naskh and tabdil, i.e., according to us it is a change in the previous ruling, while in Allah's knowledge it was already known that this ruling would have a certain duration and then another ruling would come. However, it is permissible to abrogate the Book with the Sunnah and the Sunnah with the Book, resulting in four forms according to variation and preservation: (1) Abrogation of the Book by the Book, (2) Abrogation of the Book by the Sunnah, (3) Abrogation of the Sunnah by the Sunnah, (4) Abrogation of the Sunnah by the Book. Explanation of the types of mansūkh through examples: (1) Mansūkh al-Tilāwa wa Mansūkh al-Hukm: meaning its ruling is abrogated and it is not present in the Quranic manuscripts. For example, it is narrated that Sūrat al-Ahzāb was like Sūrat al-Baqarah in length and had two to three hundred verses; now only seventy-three verses remain; the ruling of the remaining verses was abrogated and their recitation as well. (2) Mansūkh al-Hukm dūn al-Tilāwa: for example, Allah's command 'For you your religion and for me my religion' has its ruling abrogated by the verse of jihad and fighting, but the recitation remains. There are dozens of such verses (according to differing opinions of scholars). (3) Mansūkh al-Tilāwa dūn al-Hukm: for example, if a married man and a married woman commit fornication, they are to be stoned; this ruling remains, but its recitation ('The elder and the elder woman, when they commit fornication, stone them as a punishment from Allah, and Allah is Mighty, Wise') is abrogated. Similarly, the reading of Ibn Mas‘ūd, 'and whoever does not find, then fasting three consecutive days' and His saying 'cut off their hands' instead of 'their hands'. (3) Mansūkh Wasf al-Hukm: meaning not the entire ruling is abrogated, but rather a description of the ruling, such as its generality or absolute nature, is abrogated, while the original ruling and its recitation remain. Example: Allah's command to wash the feet in wudu was general, whether one is wearing socks or not, but the famous hadith which commands wiping over the feet for the wearer of khuffs abrogated that generality. Difference: We call this naskh, and Imām al-Shāfi‘ī calls it takhsīs.

Forms of Naskh and the Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Listing and explaining the forms of naskh and types of mansukh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four Hanafi forms of naskh between Quran and Sunnah, explain the Shafi'i objection, and classify the three types of mansukh with examples.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school allows all four cross-abrogations; Shafi'i restricts to same-source, affecting rulings like stoning and wiping over khuffs.

Having established the definition, the text now details the operational types of naskh: four combinations between Quran and Sunnah. Imam Shafi'i disagrees and limits abrogation to within the same source. The passage then illustrates the three categories of abrogated rulings, including the controversial 'mansūkh wasf al-hukm' which Hanafis call naskh and Shafi'is call takhsīs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four forms
The text lists: (1) Book by Book, (2) Book by Sunnah, (3) Sunnah by Sunnah, (4) Sunnah by Book. Understand each as a logical possibility given that both are divine revelation.
Form (2) and (4) are the points of disagreement with Shafi'i.
2
Analyze the Hanafi rationale
Hanafis argue that since both Quran and Sunnah are wahi, abrogation can occur across them. They cite the stoning verse as evidence: the recitation was abrogated but the ruling from Sunnah remains.
This is tied to the concept that naskh is a bayan from Allah.
3
Understand the Shafi'i objection
Shafi'i holds that the Quran is qat'i and cannot be abrogated by zanni Sunnah, and vice versa. He allows only Quran by Quran and Sunnah by Sunnah.
Shafi'i's position is based on epistemological hierarchy.
4
Classify the three categories of mansukh
Work through each: (1) Mansūkh al-tilāwa wa al-hukm (lost verses of Ahzab), (2) Mansūkh al-hukm dūn al-tilāwa (tolerance verses), (3) Mansūkh al-tilāwa dūn al-hukm (stoning verse). Note that the text actually adds a fourth: mansūkh wasf al-hukm.
The fourth category (wasf) is distinct because only a description (generality/absoluteness) is abrogated.
5
Examine the example of wudu feet
The general command to wash feet is abrogated for khuff wearers by the hadith of wiping. Hanafis call this naskh; Shafi'is call it takhsīs. Understand the practical difference: for Hanafis, the original ruling is repealed for that group; for Shafi'is, the original ruling never applied to khuff wearers because it was specified.
This affects how one derives rulings from general texts.

The author presents three scholarly positions: (1) absolute obligation, (2) absolute non-obligation, and (3) the majority view that distinguishes between rulings that have been disapproved (nakir) in the Quran or Sunnah and those that have been merely mentioned without disapproval. The third view is adopted as the relied-upon position. To illustrate, rulings like qisas (retribution), appointment of turns (from the story of Salih's she-camel), and the prohibition of sodomy are mentioned positively in the Quran without negation, so they remain obligatory. Conversely, the prohibition of certain animals for Jews is explicitly mentioned as a punishment particular to them, thus not binding. The passage concludes by addressing an objection: since previous shariah are abrogated, how can some rulings be binding? The answer is that those rulings are binding not because they are from previous shariah but because they have been incorporated into Islamic law through being affirmed in the Quran and Sunnah without censure.

Key Terms
شرائع سابقہ (shara'i' sabiqah): Previous shariah laws revealed to earlier prophets before Islam.
نکیر (nakir): Disapproval or censure expressed in Quran or Sunnah against a previous ruling.
