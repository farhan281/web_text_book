The Four Divisions of Khabar by Certainty
Classifying khabar into four categories based on certainty of truth or falsehood

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the four divisions of khabar based on certainty of truth/falsehood and classify any given khabar into the appropriate division.

Core Idea
Khabar is divided by certainty: certain truth, certain falsehood, equal probability, and preponderant probability.

The science of usul al-fiqh classifies reports (akhbar) not only by chain of transmission but also by the strength of their truthfulness. This lesson introduces a fourfold division based on whether truth or falsehood is certain, equally possible, or preponderant. Understanding these categories helps the jurist evaluate the epistemological weight of a report.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two certain categories
The first division is when the truth of the report is certain (khabar sadiq yaqini). The second is when its falsehood is certain (khabar kazib yaqini). These are based on incontrovertible evidence.
Certain truth corresponds to mutawatir reports; certain falsehood to fabricated (mawdu) reports.
2
Identify the two uncertain categories
When neither truth nor falsehood is certain, the report falls into the third or fourth division. The third division has no preponderant probability (both sides equally likely). The fourth has one preponderant probability (taghlub al-zann).
The fourth division is the domain of wahhab al-zann, which can be sufficient for some rulings.
3
Distinguish between certainty and preponderance
Certainty (yaqin) allows no doubt. Preponderant probability (zann ghalib) is strongest opinion but admits theoretical possibility of error. The third division (equal probability) is called shakk or doubt, which carries no legal weight.
In Hanafi usul, only yaqin and zann ghalib are considered valid bases for obligation.
4
Apply the classification to examples
Evaluate reports by asking: Is truth certain? Is falsehood certain? If not, is there a preponderant probability? Classify accordingly.
This process trains the student in epistemological ranking of evidence.

Categories of Hadith Transmission: 'Azimah and Rukhsah
Classification of hadith transmission methods into 'azimah and rukhsah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'azimah and rukhsah methods of hadith transmission, list the four 'azimah categories, and explain the conditions for validity of the secondary methods.

Core Idea
Hadith transmission has four primary methods requiring direct hearing and three secondary methods with conditions.

In this lesson, we explore the classification of hadith transmission methods into primary ('azimah) and secondary (rukhsah) categories. This distinction is fundamental in Hanafi usul al-hadith for evaluating the reliability of chains. The commentator explains that only four methods are considered primary; the rest are valid only under specific conditions such as explicit permission and verification of the shaykh's intent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two broad categories
First, recognize that hadith transmission methods are divided into 'azimah (primary, obligatory) and rukhsah (concessionary, secondary). The first four methods are 'azimah; the rest are rukhsah.
'Azimah methods involve direct hearing; rukhsah methods rely on permission or familiarity.
2
List the four 'azimah methods
The text names: qira'ah (reading to shaykh), sama' (hearing from shaykh), mukatabah (written correspondence), and wajh (sending a messenger). Understand that mukatabah and wajh require proof that the writing or messenger comes from the shaykh.
Mukatabah includes a prescribed format for the letter.
3
List the three rukhsah methods
The secondary methods are ijazah (general permission), munawalah (handing over), and mujazalah (permission for a book already studied). These do not require direct hearing but have conditions.
Mujazalah specifically requires the recipient to be knowledgeable of the book's contents.
4
Analyze the conditions for rukhsah methods
For any rukhsah method, the basic condition is the shaykh's explicit permission. For mukatabah, the letter must be proven to be the shaykh's; for wajh, the messenger must be verified. For mujazalah, the recipient must be 'alim (knowledgeable) of the book.
These conditions ensure the chain's reliability despite the lack of direct encounter.

Core Idea
The hadd is Allah's fixed punishment – no ruler can change it. Exile is discretionary policy, not part of the hadd.

This passage defends the action of 'Umar ibn al-Khattab (RA) by clarifying a fundamental usul rule: the hadd punishments prescribed by Allah are immutable. It also answers a key objection: how can one claim exile is not part of the hadd when the hadith seems to include it? The answer distinguishes between the fixed legal penalty and the ruler's discretionary authority (siyasah) to take additional measures for the public good.

The Benefit of 'If the Hadith is Apparent' – Why a Companion's Non-Action Does Not Invalidate
Explains why a Companion's non-action does not invalidate a sound hadith if it is zāhir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a Companion's non-action on a hadith does not necessarily invalidate the hadith and apply this principle using the Hanafi usul condition that the hadith must be apparent.

Core Idea
A Companion's ignorance due to rarity does not weaken a sound hadith if it is zāhir.

You may have encountered the argument that if a Companion did not act on a hadith, the hadith must be weak. This lesson delves into a key Hanafi usul principle that clarifies why such a conclusion is not automatic. The condition 'if the hadith is apparent' (zāhir) protects sound hadiths from being rejected simply because a Companion was unaware of them. Through the example of Abu Musa al-Ash'ari and the hadith about laughing in prayer, we explore the reasoning behind this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key condition
The passage begins with 'the benefit of the condition that if the hadith is apparent.' This condition is the focus: it protects a hadith from being rejected if it is clear and apparent, even if some Companions did not act on it.
2
Understand the rationale
The author explains that the possibility of a hadith being hidden from a Companion is not a cause for criticism because Companions are not omniscient. Rare events may not reach them.
3
Examine the example
The hadith from Zayd ibn Khalid al-Juhani says loud laughter in prayer requires repeating wudu. Abu Musa al-Ash'ari did not act on it. The passage argues that this non-action does not invalidate the hadith because the event (loud laughter in prayer) is rare.
The verse 'You have been given but little knowledge' supports the idea that even a Companion may be unaware.
4
Apply the principle
You should now be able to state that a Companion's non-action on a sound hadith that is zāhir is not a valid criticism if the hadith could plausibly have been unknown to them. The rarity of the incident justifies the possibility of ignorance.
This principle prevents unnecessary rejection of authentic hadith.

Conditions for Genuine Conflict (Ta'arud) Between Evidences
Commentary on conditions for ta'arud

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two conditions for genuine conflict (ta'arud) between legal evidences and apply them to distinguish true conflict from apparent contradiction in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Ta'arud requires equality of rank and unity of place, time, and contradictory ruling.

You have probably encountered situations where two Quranic verses or hadiths seem to contradict each other. In Hanafi usul, not every apparent contradiction is a genuine conflict (ta'arud). This lesson, drawn from a classical commentary, explains the precise conditions under which two evidences can be considered in genuine conflict, and how many apparent contradictions are resolved by abrogation or by recognizing that one evidence is superior to the other. Understanding these conditions is crucial for correct istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the context of ta'arud
Understand that apparent conflict between evidences is only a result of our ignorance of which evidence is abrogated; if abrogation is known, there is no conflict.
The passage begins by addressing an objection about placement and explaining the role of naskh.
2
Identify the first condition: equality of rank
The two evidences must be equal in rank, both in essence and attributes. If one is superior, there is no conflict and the weaker evidence is disregarded.
Examples: muhkam vs mufassar (attribute), khabar wahid vs khabar mashhur (essence).
3
Distinguish superiority by essence vs. attribute
Superiority can be in essence (e.g., khabar mashhur over khabar wahid) or in attribute (e.g., muhkam over mufassar, 'ibarat al-nass over isharat al-nass).
Both types prevent genuine ta'arud.
4
Identify the second condition: unity of place and time with contradictory rulings
The two evidences must address the same subject (mahall) at the same time (waqt) and give contradictory rulings (e.g., one halal, the other haram).
If the place or time differs, there is no genuine conflict.
5
Apply the conditions to examples
Test given examples: wife vs. mother-in-law (different place), wine in early vs. later Islam (different time). Conclude they are not ta'arud.
Only when all conditions are met does genuine ta'arud occur, requiring resolution via naskh or tarjih.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a matan statement about the occurrence of conflict (ta'arud) between legal evidences. It begins by addressing an objection: why did the matan author place the discussion of conflict here instead of in the chapter of preference (tarjih) as the author of Tawdih did? The answer is that the matan author followed the method of 'Allama Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi. Then the commentary explains that apparent conflict between evidences is only a result of our ignorance of which evidence is abrogated; if abrogation is known, there is no conflict. The main part of the passage lays out the conditions for genuine conflict. The first condition is equality: the two evidences must be equal in rank, whether in essence (like khabar wahid vs khabar mashhur) or in attribute (like muhkam vs mufassar, or 'ibarat al-nass vs isharat al-nass). If one is superior, the weaker is disregarded. The second condition, derived from the matan phrase 'unity of place and time with contradictory ruling,' requires that both evidences address the same locus (mahall) at the same time with contradictory rulings (e.g., halal vs haram). Two examples illustrate negative cases: (1) different loci (wife and mother-in-law) do not create conflict because the place is not unified; (2) different times (wine initially halal then haram) do not create conflict because the time is not unified. The commentary thus clarifies that true conflict is a specific technical situation, and most apparent conflicts are resolved by identifying abrogation, superiority, or lack of unity.

مساوات (musāwāt): Equality of two evidences in rank (essence and attributes) such that neither is superior.

Taqrir al-Usul: Preserving the Original State When Proofs Conflict
Explaining the principle of taqrir al-usul with the donkey example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define taqrir al-usul, state when it is applied, and apply it to the example of the donkey's leftover water.

Core Idea
When all proofs conflict equally, preserve the original state—taqrir al-usul.

In legal reasoning, there are times when all available evidence points in opposite directions with equal strength. How does a jurist proceed? This lesson introduces the Hanafi principle of taqrir al-usul, which instructs us to keep things as they originally were until a clear preference emerges. We will explore this principle through the classic example of the donkey's leftover water (su'r).

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Problem of Conflicting Proofs
Identify when hadiths, qiyas, and Companion sayings all conflict with equal strength, leaving no method of preference (tarjeeh). This is the condition that triggers taqrir al-usul.
Without this deadlock, tarjeeh principles would be used instead.
2
Define Taqrir al-Usul
State the definition: 'taqrir kulli shay'in 'ala aslihi' — leaving everything on its original ruling. The original state of things is assumed to continue unless overridden by strong evidence.
The original state of a donkey is purity (tahara), and its meat is permissible until proven otherwise.
3
Apply to the Donkey Example—Conflicting Hadiths
Analyze the two hadiths: one forbids donkey meat (indicating impurity), another permits it (indicating purity). Since both are equally authentic and directly conflicting, no tarjeeh is possible.
The conflict in meat ruling extends to saliva because saliva derives from meat.
4
Include Conflicting Companion Sayings
Note that Companion reports also conflict: some permit using water left by a donkey for wudu, while others consider it impure. This further confirms the deadlock.
5
Arrive at the Ruling via Taqrir al-Usul
Since all proofs are in equilibrium, apply taqrir al-usul: preserve the original state. The original state of the donkey is purity, therefore its leftover water is pure (tahir) and permissible for use in wudu.
This is the Hanafi fatwa on the matter.

Commentary
This passage introduces the principle of taqrir al-usul (preserving the original state) in usul al-fiqh. When all methods of tarjeeh (preference) fail because proofs are equally strong and conflicting, and no further evidence is available, the ruling is to keep everything as it was originally (e.g., permissible things remain permissible, forbidden things remain forbidden). The example given is the leftover (su'r) of a domestic donkey after it drinks from a vessel.  There are conflicting hadiths: one prohibits donkey meat (indicating impurity) and another permits it (indicating purity). Since the proofs are in equilibrium, taqrir al-usul is applied, meaning we look to the original state of the donkey (which is a pure animal) and rule its leftover as pure. Because the leftover involves saliva, and the ruling of saliva follows the ruling of the meat (since it is derived from it). The conflict extends to the actions of the Companions: some allowed ablution with the donkey's leftover water, while others considered it impure. In Hanafi fiqh, the donkey's leftover is considered pure (tahir) according to taqrir al-usul, as the original state of animals is purity.

Mashkūk: Definition and Legal Consequence
Clarifying the meaning of mashkūk and its ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mashkūk and apply its legal consequence (wudu and tayammum) to a case of doubtful impurity.

Core Idea
Mashkūk means the ruling is known but evidence is unclear; it demands caution: both wudu and tayammum.

This passage addresses an objection regarding the term 'mashkūk' used for donkey urine. The author clarifies that mashkūk does not imply ignorance of the ruling; rather, the ruling is established, and the term indicates a state of doubt in the evidence, necessitating a cautious combination of wudu and tayammum.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its context
Locate the word 'mashkūk' in the passage and note that it is applied to the urine of a donkey (sūr himār). The objection claims the Sharia is clear, so mashkūk is inappropriate.
The term is being defended against a charge of ambiguity.
2
Extract the definition of mashkūk
From the response: mashkūk means the ruling is known (not majhūl), but the evidence (or status) is unclear. The maker of the text and commentary call it mashkūk for this reason.
Mashkūk is distinct from majhūl (unknown ruling).
3
Determine the legal consequence (hukm)
The passage states the known ruling is 'wujūb al-wuḍū' wa ḍamm al-tayammum' – obligation of wudu and joining tayammum to it. This is the precautionary (ihtiyāt) ruling.
The hukm is not purity or impurity, but a combined purification act.
4
Apply to the case of doubtful impurity
For any matter classified as mashkūk (doubtful impurity), one must perform both wudu (to remove the impurity) and tayammum (as a substitute due to doubt). This ensures caution.
In Hanafi usul, mashkūk items require both acts to be safe.
5
Distinguish from other categories
Contrast mashkūk with: (a) najis muhaqqaq (certain impurity) – only wudu, (b) tahir (pure) – no purification needed, (c) majhūl – no known ruling.
The key is the combination of wudu and tayammum.

Explaining the principle of preferring the prohibitive over the permissive when texts conflict.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When texts conflict, prefer the prohibitive to avoid repetition of abrogation.

This lesson presents a key Hanafi rule for resolving textual conflict (ta'arud). When two texts appear contradictory—one permitting, one prohibiting—and we cannot determine which came first, the Hanafi method gives precedence to the prohibitive. The reasoning hinges on the foundational principle that the original state of things is permissibility (ibaha). Acting on the prohibitive avoids a logical problem known as 'repetition of abrogation' (takrar al-naskh). This rule is a practical tool for the mufti and mujtahid when faced with seemingly contradictory evidence.

Guided Path
1

Recognize that two texts (one mubih, one muharram) are in apparent contradiction, and we do not know the chronological order of revelation.

2

Know that the original state of things is permissibility. This is the default assumption before any textual ruling.
This principle is widely accepted across schools.
3
Analyze the Two Possibilities
Consider what happens if we act on the mubih vs. the muharram. Acting on muharram means permissibility was abrogated once. Acting on mubih would require permissibility → prohibition → permissibility, i.e., two abrogations.
4
Apply the Rule of Tarjih
Since the latter scenario leads to takrar al-naskh (repetition of abrogation), which is generally avoided in usul, we prefer the muharram.
This avoids logical inconsistency and respects the principle of asal al-ibahah.
5

The prohibitive text is given precedence (muharram muqaddam). This is the operative rule for cases of textual conflict with unknown chronology.
This rule is specific to Hanafi usul and is a form of tarjih.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Bayan clarifies divine communication: through reinforcement, explanation, conditional modification, or necessary implication from context.

Understanding how the Quran and Sunnah clarify meaning is essential for proper istinbat (derivation of rulings). The Hanafi school classifies Bayan into four types, each serving a distinct function in removing ambiguity. This lesson unpacks each type with textual examples from the Quran and hadith.

Chapter: Types of Bayan. From the Book and Sunnah, each has five types based on Bayan. A brief explanation, as needed, is given. The share of the father is not mentioned, but it is in the status of the spoken because the heirs are two; when one gets one-third, the remainder goes to the father. As if it were said: 'so for his mother is one-third, and for his father is the remainder.' (2) Established by contextual indication of the speaker: Many acts were done in the presence of the Prophet (peace be upon him) which he saw and said nothing. (3) Established by the necessity of removing harm (gharar) from people.

Commentary
This passage is a classification of the types of Bayan (clarification) in the Quran and Sunnah according to Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author lists four (the text mentions 'five' but only four are detailed; perhaps a lacuna or the fifth is subsumed). Bayan al-Taqrir reinforces the meaning and removes ambiguity of majaz (figurative language) or takhsis (specification) by adding clarifying words. Similarly, 'all together' removes the possibility that only some angels prostrated. Bayan al-Tafsil explains mujmal (ambiguous), mushtarak (homonym), khafi (hidden), and mushkil (difficult) texts. The command 'establish prayer' is mujmal; the Prophet ﷺ detailed its manner. The word 'quru'' is mushtarak between menstruation and purity; a hadith specifies that a slave woman's 'iddah is two menstruations, thus clarifying the meaning. Examples include: (a) inheritance verse where the father's share is understood from context (in hukm al-mantuq); (b) the Prophet's silent approval of certain acts (taqriri hadith); (c) necessity to remove deception (gharar) from people, which allows rulings derived from maslahah. This classification is fundamental in Hanafi usul for understanding how divine communication is clarified.

The condition 'if you enter the house' is the alteration.
4
Generalize the concept
Bayan taghyir can occur in other contexts, e.g., adding a time condition to a contract.
5
Distinguish from other types of bayan
Bayan taghyir differs from bayan tabyin (clarification) and bayan tabdil (replacement).

The Closing Prayer: Humility and Gratitude in Hanafi Scholarship
Closing doxology and prayer of the commentator

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Scholarly works end with gratitude, not pride.

A commentary on a foundational text of Hanafi usul al-fiqh closes not with a boast of achievement, but with a prayer acknowledging difficulty and dependence on Allah. This brief doxology encapsulates the adab (etiquette) of the Hanafi scholarly tradition: humility before knowledge, gratitude for completion, and blessings upon the Prophet.
