The Three Restrictions in the Hanafi Definition of Sunnah
Defining Sunnah by listing what it excludes and clarifying its usage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three restrictions in the Hanafi definition of Sunnah and explain how each restriction excludes a specific category (nafl, fard, wajib) and apply this definition to distinguish Sunnah Mu'akkadah from other acts.

Core Idea
Sunnah is defined by three exclusions: not nafl, not fard, not wajib.

The Hanafi definition of Sunnah is built on what it is not. By understanding the three restrictions, you will see how the school precisely isolates the category of emphasized Sunnah (Sunnah Mu'akkadah). This passage also introduces an important dispute: can the term 'Sunnah' be used for practices of the Companions? We'll examine both positions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three restrictions
List the three conditions (quyūd) in the definition: 'an yutlab' (it is demanded), 'min ghayri iftirāḍ' (without obligation), 'wa lā wujūb' (and no obligation).
These are called 'fawā'id al-quyūd' (benefits of the restrictions).
2
Understand what each excludes
First restriction excludes nafl (supererogatory) because nafl is not demanded in the same way. Second excludes farḍ (definitive obligation). Third excludes wājib (probable obligation).
In Hanafi usul, farḍ and wājib are distinct; farḍ is qat'i, wājib is zanni.
3
See the definition as jāmi' and māni'
The three restrictions make the definition comprehensive (jāmi') for all Sunnah instances and exclusive (māni') against non-Sunnah acts.
4
Note the commentator's clarification
The commentator states that this definition applies specifically to Sunnah Mu'akkadah, not to Sunnah Zawā'id (non-emphasized). The latter is covered separately.
Do not confuse the definition's scope: it is for the emphasized category only.
5
Explore the madhhab dispute
Hanafis permit using 'Sunnah' for non-prophetic practices based on the hadith 'man sanna sunnah hasanah'. Shāfi'īs require explicit attribution, citing Sa'īd ibn al-Musayyib's usage.
This is a legitimate ikhtilāf with practical implications for fatwā.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the definition of Sunnah, explaining the benefits of the three restrictions in the definition. The first restriction 'that it is demanded' excludes nāfilah (supererogatory acts) because they are not demanded in the same way; they are optional. The second restriction 'without obligation' excludes farḍ (obligatory acts) because farḍ carries a definitive obligation. The third restriction 'and no obligation' excludes wājib (necessary acts) because wājib in the Hanafi school is a category distinct from farḍ but still carries a level of obligation. Thus the definition applies precisely to Sunnah. The commentator then notes that this definition refers specifically to Sunnah Mu'akkadah (emphasized Sunnah), while the subsequent division will also cover Sunnah Zawā'id (non-emphasized Sunnah). Then an essential note is added regarding the usage of the term 'Sunnah' for non-prophetic practices. The Hanafis permit calling the practices of righteous caliphs or scholars 'Sunnah' based on the hadith 'Whoever establishes a good practice...'. The Shāfi'īs, however, require explicit attribution to avoid confusion, citing Sa'īd ibn al-Musayyib's statement where 'Sunnah' refers to the ruling of Zayd ibn Thābit, not the Prophet. This shows the difference in terminological scope.

نفل (nafl): Supererogatory act; excluded from Sunnah by first restriction.
فرض (fard): Obligatory act with definitive evidence; excluded from Sunnah by second restriction.
واجب (wajib): Necessary act with probable evidence; excluded from Sunnah by third restriction.

Defining and exemplifying the two categories of Sunnah

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sunnah Mu'akkadah demands emulation with blame for omission; Sunnah Ghayr Mu'akkadah is commendable but not required.

This lesson introduces the foundational Hanafi division of Sunnah into two categories: the emphasized (mu'akkadah) and the non-emphasized (ghayr mu'akkadah). Understanding this distinction is essential for classifying the Prophet's actions and determining the legal weight of following them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Recognize that Sunnah is divided into Sunan al-Hādī (Sunnah Mu'akkadah) and Sunan Zawā'id (Sunnah Ghayr Mu'akkadah).
2
Analyze the defining feature of Sunan al-Hādī
Note that it is demanded in a way that the abandoner deserves blame and reprimand.
This blame is not sin; it indicates strong recommendation.
3
Analyze the defining feature of Sunan Zawā'id
Note that the abandoner does not deserve blame or reprimand; these are habitual actions of the Prophet.
4
Recall examples
Memorize examples: Mu'akkadah includes congregation (jama'ah), adhān, iqāmah. Ghayr Mu'akkadah includes dress, sitting, standing.
Examples help anchor the definitions.

Commentary
This passage presents the first division of Sunnah according to the Hanafi school. The first category is Sunnah al-Hādī (also called Sunnah Mu'akkadah or emphasized Sunnah). Its defining characteristic is that it is demanded to the extent that abandoning it incurs blame and reprimand, though not sin. Examples include performing prayer in congregation, the adhān and iqāmah. These are highly recommended and the community should maintain them. The second category is Sunnah al-Zawā'id (also called Sunnah Ghayr Mu'akkadah or non-emphasized Sunnah). Abandoning it does not incur blame or reprimand. This category covers the habitual actions of the Prophet (peace be upon him) such as his manner of dress, sitting, standing, and other personal habits. Following these is meritorious but not required.

The Division of Rukhsah into Real and Metaphorical
Explaining the reason for not defining rukhsah directly, then dividing it into haqiqi and majazi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why rukhsah is not defined directly and state its division into haqiqi and majazi types, including their subtypes.

Core Idea
Rukhsah lacks universal meaning; it is divided first, then defined by type.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, rukhsah (license) is a commanded thing that is an exception to the general ruling (azimah). The author of this text does not give a single definition upfront because rukhsah does not have a single shared meaning across all its instances. Instead, he first divides it into categories and then defines each. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind that approach and the resulting division.

Guided Path
1
Understand why rukhsah cannot be defined directly
Examine the text's claim: definition is needed only for terms that have a universal shared meaning. Rukhsah lacks this, so direct definition is impossible. Instead, the author divides it first.
This is a key methodological point in Hanafi usul: when a term is used for multiple distinct realities, it must be broken down before definition.
2
Identify the two main divisions: haqiqi and majazi
Rukhsah is first divided into real (haqiqiyyah) and metaphorical (majaziyyah). In real rukhsah, the azimah remains practiced; in metaphorical, the azimah is lost.
The text says 'first, rukhsah has two types: (1) Real rukhsah and (2) Metaphorical rukhsah'.
3
Recognize the subtypes within each division
Each main type has two subtypes: one where the rukhsah is equal to azimah in all respects, and another where it differs. Similarly for metaphorical: perfect (azimah lost entirely) and imperfect (azimah partially remains).
Thus there are four types in total, though the text originally says 'three' due to a scribal error.
4
Grasp the author's method: division as a tool for definition
By dividing rukhsah into these categories, the author avoids a vague single definition and provides precise meanings for each subtype, making the concept clearer.
This method is characteristic of Hanafi usul literature (e.g., al-Nasafi, al-Bazdawi).

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul text deals with the concept of rukhsah (license or relief) as a type of commanded thing (ma'mur bih). The author explains why he does not give a single definition of rukhsah: because rukhsah lacks a universal shared meaning applicable to all its instances; it is a term with multiple distinct realities. Therefore, he divides it first. Rukhsah is divided into two main categories: haqiqiyyah (real) and majaziyyah (metaphorical). Each of these is further subdivided. Real rukhsah is where the azimah (the original, stricter ruling) remains practiced but a license is given in some circumstances; it may be equal to azimah in some respects or different. Metaphorical rukhsah is where the azimah is actually not present, so it is called rukhsah metaphorically; it is actually azimah but due to loss of the original strict ruling, it is termed rukhsah. The passage then discusses the ruling of one type of real rukhsah: it is permissible to take the rukhsah, but acting on azimah is more virtuous. The examples include various situations of compulsion or necessity, such as uttering kufr under duress while maintaining faith in the heart, breaking a fast under coercion, destroying property, etc. The note at the end is incomplete but suggests contemplation on the examples. This is a standard Hanafi usul analysis of the concept of rukhsah and its hierarchy compared to azimah.

عزیمت (azimah): The original, stricter ruling that is the norm; the determination to follow the primary obligation.
حقیقیہ (haqiqiyyah): Real; said of a rukhsah where the azimah still remains although a license is given.
مجازیه (majaziyyah): Metaphorical; said of a rukhsah where the azimah is actually lost, so it is called rukhsah only metaphorically.

The Ruling of Real Rukhsah and Its Examples
Stating the ruling of real rukhsah and providing examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the ruling of real rukhsah to new scenarios and distinguish it from metaphorical rukhsah using the provided examples.

Core Idea
In real rukhsah, the prohibition remains; only punishment is withheld – azimah is superior.

After dividing rukhsah into types, the author turns to the ruling of real rukhsah: it is permissible to take the license, but acting upon the original ruling (azimah) is more virtuous. This is because the prohibition (haram) remains in itself, even though the person who commits the act under compulsion is not punished. To illustrate, the text gives six examples from different areas of fiqh, involving compulsion (ikrah) and necessity (darurah).

1
Recall the ruling of real rukhsah
The text states that for real rukhsah, acting on azimah is superior (awla), but acting on rukhsah is permissible (jaiz). The prohibition remains; only punishment is waived.
This is a key distinction: rukhsah does not make the act halal in itself.
2
Understand the reason for the permissibility
Although the evidence of prohibition (dalil al-hurmah) is still established, the committer is treated like one who does something permissible in terms of accountability. This is a concession, not a change in the legal nature of the act.
The text says: 'In the aspect of rukhsah, the prohibited and its cause remain... the committer is treated like one who does something permissible.'
3
Examine the six examples
Each example involves a situation of compulsion or necessity where the original prohibition would normally apply. The rukhsah allows the act, but the azimah (avoiding it) is more virtuous.
Examples include uttering disbelief under coercion, breaking a fast, destroying property, etc.
4
Apply the condition: the excuse must be genuine
In example 1, the heart remains firm in faith (mutmain bil iman) – this is a condition for the rukhsah to apply. If the compulsion is not genuine, or the person internally consents, the ruling changes.
This condition is implied in the text and is common in Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul text deals with the concept of rukhsah (license or relief) as a type of commanded thing (ma'mur bih). The author explains why he does not give a single definition of rukhsah: because rukhsah lacks a universal shared meaning applicable to all its instances; it is a term with multiple distinct realities. Therefore, he divides it first. Rukhsah is divided into two main categories: haqiqiyyah (real) and majaziyyah (metaphorical). Each of these is further subdivided. Real rukhsah is where the azimah (the original, stricter ruling) remains practiced but a license is given in some circumstances; it may be equal to azimah in some respects or different. Metaphorical rukhsah is where the azimah is actually not present, so it is called rukhsah metaphorically; it is actually azimah but due to loss of the original strict ruling, it is termed rukhsah. The passage then discusses the ruling of one type of real rukhsah: it is permissible to take the rukhsah, but acting on azimah is more virtuous. The examples include various situations of compulsion or necessity, such as uttering kufr under duress while maintaining faith in the heart, breaking a fast under coercion, destroying property, etc. The note at the end is incomplete but suggests contemplation on the examples. This is a standard Hanafi usul analysis of the concept of rukhsah and its hierarchy compared to azimah.

Key Terms
رخصت (rukhsah): A license or relief; an exception to the general ruling (azimah) due to a valid excuse.
عزیمت (azimah): The original, stricter ruling that is the norm; the determination to follow the primary obligation.
حقیقیہ (haqiqiyyah): Real; said of a rukhsah where the azimah still remains although a license is given.
مجازیه (majaziyyah): Metaphorical; said of a rukhsah where the azimah is actually lost, so it is called rukhsah only metaphorically.

Nature of Rukhsah: Hanafi vs Shafi'i on Concession
Explaining the Hanafi view that rukhsah cancels the original obligation, with examples and refutation of Shafi'i position

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi and Shafi'i views on the nature of rukhsah and apply the Hanafi principle of isqat to practical cases.

Core Idea
Rukhsah cancels the original obligation, making the stricter act impermissible.

This passage tackles a foundational disagreement in usul al-fiqh: does a concession (rukhsah) merely permit an easier option, or does it actually cancel the original ruling? The Hanafis firmly hold the latter—rukhsah is isqat (cancellation). The Shafi'is see it as a superior dispensation that does not remove the original obligation. We'll walk through the proofs, the counter-arguments, and a vivid example involving an oath to illustrate the practical consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State the Hanafi view: rukhsah is isqat (cancellation) of the original obligation, so performing the 'azimah is not permissible. Then state the Shafi'i view: rukhsah is af'dal (superior), and completing the 'azimah is better.
The distinction is not merely theoretical; it directly affects permissibility.
2
Analyze the Shafi'i proof
Examine Quran 4:101 'there is no sin upon you in shortening the prayer' and the hadith 'accept the charity' as cited by the Shafi'is. Understand how they infer obligation or preference to accept the rukhsah.
The Shafi'is interpret negation of sin as an indication of permissibility and even preferability.
3
Understand the Hanafi response
See how the Hanafis counter: the negation of sin only refutes the misconception that shortening might be sinful, analogously to the verse on Safa and Marwah. It does not imply superiority or obligation to accept the rukhsah.
This is a key methodological difference in interpreting Quranic negations of sin.
4
Apply the second example and the thamarah
Consider the case of wine and carrion in necessity. According to Hanafis, the prohibition is completely lifted (isqat), so refusing and dying would be sinful. According to Shafi'is (and Abu Yusuf), only accountability is removed. Trace the oath consequence: if one swears not to eat haram, in necessity, Hanafis say oath not broken (prohibition gone), Shafi'is say broken (prohibition remains).
This is the thamarah al-khilaf: the practical difference that matters in fatwa.
5
Note the third example
Wiping over khuffayn (masah) cancels the obligation to wash the feet. This illustrates another instance where rukhsah is isqat.
Consistent with the Hanafi principle across different chapters of fiqh.

Commentary
This passage discusses the nature of rukhsah (concession) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, contrasting it with the Shafi'i view. The author first states the Hanafi position: rukhsah is isqat (cancellation) of the original obligation, meaning performing the 'azimah (the original stricter ruling) is not permissible. For example, when traveling, one must shorten the prayer; completing it would be invalid. The Shafi'is hold that rukhsah is afdal (superior) and that completing the prayer is better, though shortening is permissible. The Shafi'i proof is the Quranic verse negating sin for shortening prayer, which they take to imply permissibility and even preference. The Hanafi response clarifies that the negation of sin only refutes the misconception that shortening might be sinful, analogous to the verse about Sa'f and Marwah. The passage then gives a second example: the permissibility of wine and carrion in necessity. According to Hanafis, the prohibition is completely lifted, so refusing to eat or drink and dying would be sinful. Imam Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i hold that only accountability is removed, not the prohibition itself, citing Allah's forgiveness. The outcome of the difference is illustrated with an oath: if one swears not to eat haram and then eats in necessity, Hanafis say the oath is not broken because the prohibition is gone; Shafi'is say it is broken because the essence of prohibition remains. A third example is wiping over khuffayn, which removes the obligation to wash the feet; the original obligation (washing) is the 'azimah.

Key Terms
رخصت (rukhsah): A concession that lifts the original obligation or makes permissible what was forbidden due to necessity.
عزیمت (azimah): The original, stricter ruling that applies in normal circumstances.
اسقاط (isqat): Cancellation or removal of a legal obligation or prohibition.
ارفع (af'dal): Superior or better.

Types of Amr and Nahi in Hanafi Usul
Classification of commands and prohibitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a command (amr) as muwaqqat (time-bound) or mutlaq (absolute), and a prohibition (nahi) as qabih li-aynihi (evil in itself) or qabih li-ghayrihi (evil due to externals), and link these types to their asbab.

Core Idea
Commands and prohibitions have types that affect scope, bindingness, and cause of ruling.

In this lesson, we study the Hanafi classification of amr and nahi. Understanding these types is essential for accurate istinbat. The text first establishes that all types of amr and nahi have causes, then details the axes of classification: temporal scope (muwaqqat/mutlaq) and practical range (wasi/dayyiq) for amr, and domain (shar'i/hissi) and moral basis (li-aynihi/li-ghayrihi) for nahi. We will explore how these categories feed into cause-based rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two axes of amr classification
The text divides amr by time (muwaqqat vs mutlaq) and by scope (wasi' vs dayyiq). Note: muwaqqat is a command bound to a specific time (e.g., prayer times), while mutlaq is not time-restricted (e.g., seeking knowledge).
This affects performance and qada rules.
2
Identify the two axes of nahi classification
Nahi is divided by domain: shar'i (e.g., prohibition of drinking wine) and hissi (sensory, e.g., prohibition of touching fire). Also by evil: qabih li-aynihi (inherently evil, like zina) and qabih li-ghayrihi (evil due to externals, like sale during Friday prayer).
Qabih li-ghayrihi may become valid when cause disappears.
3
Link types to asbab
The text asserts that all these types have asbab. For example, the obligation of prayer (an amr muwaqqat) has asbab: entry of time (as mentioned later in the table). For nahi, the sabab is the presence of the evil attribute or external factor.
4
Apply to an example from the table
Consider zakat: its obligation is an amr mutlaq (not bound to a specific day) but conditional (wasi' because it applies to various types of wealth). The sabab is ownership of nisab and passage of a year.

Asbab (Causes) of Shar'i Rulings and the General Principle
Listing asbab and general rule for causation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the sabab (cause) for each of the listed shar'i rulings from the text, apply the general principle of attributability (idafah) to identify the cause of any ruling, and explain how the principle aids in legal derivation.

Core Idea
Every shar'i ruling is attributed to a cause; know the cause to understand the ruling's conditions and scope.

This lesson delves into the second part of the passage: the specific asbab (causes) for key ahkam (faith, prayer, zakat, etc.) and the general principle that every ruling is attributed to something (idafah). This principle is a powerful tool for the faqih to deduce causes without memorizing every detail. We will work through the table and then apply the principle to new cases.

Guided Path
1
Review the table of ahkam and their asbab
The source lists thirteen rulings with their causes. For example, obligation of prayer has cause 'entry of time'; zakat has cause 'ownership of nisab + year'. Note that the real cause for prayer is Allah's command, but time is its representative.
Table includes worship, penalties, expiations.
2
Understand the general principle
The commentator states that every ruling is attributed (mansub) to something (by idafah or otherwise). The existence of the ruling is dependent on that attributed thing. This means we can identify the cause by looking at what the ruling is linked to.
Example: 'Theft' is attributed to the act of stealing; thus the hadd cause is the act itself.
3
Apply the principle to deduce causes
Using the principle, if you know a ruling, ask: 'What is this ruling attributed to?' The answer is its cause. For instance, the ruling 'fasting is obligatory' is attributed to 'sighting of Ramadan'.
4
Test the principle on a new example
Consider the ruling that 'giving zakat is obligatory'. Using the principle, it is attributed to 'ownership of nisab wealth' (as per the table). So that is its cause.
This method works for most positive rulings ('awamir).

General Principle for Identifying Causes of Rulings
General rule for finding causes; objection about conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi general principle of attribution (idāfah) to identify the cause (sabab) of any Sharʿī ruling, and distinguish between literal and metaphorical attribution.

Core Idea
Every ruling is attributed to a cause; its existence depends on that cause.

In this passage, the commentator (shāriḥ) presents a powerful shortcut: instead of memorizing every cause individually, you can identify the cause of any ruling by noticing to what it is attributed (mansūb ilayhi). This principle, passed down from the senior Hanafi masters, saves the student from endless enumeration. However, Hanafis sometimes attribute rulings to conditions metaphorically, which raises an objection—the text addresses and resolves this.

Guided Path
1
Review the examples of causes
The passage gives examples for purification (prayer causes purification), penalties (attribution to the act), and expiations (cause is something between prohibition and permission). Notice each ruling is tied to its attributed entity.
The examples are illustrative; focus on the pattern of attribution.
2
Extract the general principle
From the examples, distill the rule: 'Every ruling is attributed to something, and its existence depends on that attributed thing.' This is the key to identifying any cause without exhaustive memorization.
Write the principle in your own words.
3
Apply the principle to a new ruling
Take a ruling like 'zakat is obligatory on nisab-level wealth'. The ruling is attributed to 'wealth reaching nisab', so the cause is ownership of nisab, not the passage of a year (which is a condition). Practice applying to other examples.
Ensure you separate cause from condition.
4
Analyze the objection
The objector says: Hanafis sometimes attribute rulings to conditions (shart) not causes, e.g., sadaqat al-fitr attributed to the head rather than fitr. Understand why this seems problematic: conditions do not generate rulings; causes do.
The objector assumes attribution must be literal.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response: such attribution is metaphorical (majāz), not literal. Hanafis do not believe the condition is the real cause; they use idāfah figuratively. This resolves the objection because metaphorical attribution does not equate shart with sabab.
Memorize the key phrase: 'shay kī idāfat sabab kī taraf majāzan hotī hai'.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary discusses the ruling on ambiguous disparagement (ta'n mubham) in hadith criticism. The author states that a vague claim that a hadith is defective or munkar is insufficient to reject it; the critic must specify the defect in a way that is unanimously accepted as valid jarh. This is because general accusations without explanation do not constitute proof (hujja). However, if the critic provides a clear interpretation that no hadith authority disputes, then the disparagement is accepted and the hadith is abandoned. Additionally, the critic must be a reliable, precise person who is not harsh or biased. Disparagement from biased or extreme individuals is rejected because their prejudice may influence their judgment. The commentator warns that such people have historically harmed the religion by distorting rulings, making halal haram and vice versa.
