Why 'Nazm' Matters: Wording and Meaning in Hanafi Usul
Explaining the semantic preference for 'nazm' and the scope of Quranic divisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the matn author chose 'nazm' over 'lafz' for the Quran and identify the dual form 'aqsamihima' as indicating divisions of both wording and meaning.

Core Idea
The Quran's wording is called 'nazm' to honour it; its divisions apply to both the verbal form and the eternal meaning.

Have you ever wondered why Hanafi usul texts use the word 'nazm' instead of the more common 'lafz' for the Quranic expression? This lesson unpacks that choice, revealing a deep respect for the divine text and a crucial methodological point: that understanding Sharia rulings requires knowledge of how both the wording and the meaning are categorized. We will follow the commentator's reasoning and see how even a single word selection carries significant implications for legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
The passage begins with a question: why did the matn author use the word 'nazm' for the Quranic words rather than 'lafz'? Recognizing the question helps focus on the author's terminological choice.
2
Analyze the Answer: Respect and Precision
The commentator explains that 'lafz' literally means 'to throw', which carries a hint of disrespect. In contrast, 'nazm' means 'to string pearls' – it conveys careful arrangement, which is more befitting the Quran's majesty.
This shows that word choice in usul is not arbitrary; it reflects underlying values.
3
Understand the Implication: Verbal vs. Eternal
'Nazm' refers to the created verbal speech (kalam lafzi), while 'ma'na' refers to the eternal internal speech (kalam nafsi). This distinction is foundational in Hanafi theology and usul.
The text notes that the Quran consists of Allah's attributes, commands, and prohibitions – all eternal.
4
Recognize the Dual Form 'Aqsamihima'
The phrase 'aqsamihima' (their two divisions) uses the dual form to emphasize that the divisions apply to both nazm (wording) and ma'na (meaning). This is a key point: legal rulings come from understanding both dimensions.
The commentator clarifies that 'aqsam' (types) alone would be insufficient; the dual form precisely indicates both aspects.

The Four Levels of Clear Words in Hanafi Usul
Explaining the four levels of clarity in words and their distinguishing criteria.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four levels of clear words (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) and apply the criteria of interpretation (ta'wil) and specification (takhsis) to classify a given textual expression.

Core Idea
The four clear words form a hierarchy based on openness to interpretation and specification.

In Hanafi usul, words are classified not only by specificity (general/specific) but also by clarity of meaning. The second major division examines whether a word's meaning is clear or obscure, and if clear, how firmly it is established. This hierarchy determines how much interpretive flexibility remains for the jurist. Understanding these levels is essential for correctly deriving rulings from primary texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify clarity or obscurity
First, determine if the word's meaning is apparent or hidden. If obscure, it belongs to one of the obscure types (khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih). If clear, proceed.
This passage focuses on the clear types; obscure types are only mentioned briefly.
2
Check openness to interpretation (ta'wil)
If the meaning is clear, ask: is it still open to interpretation? If yes, go to step 3. If no, go to step 4.
3
Determine source of clarity
If open to interpretation, is the clarity due to the word's own form (nafs al-sigha) or due to an external factor (arid)? If the former, it is zahir (apparent); if the latter, it is nass (explicit).
Both zahir and nass are open to interpretation, but nass has a stronger indication.
4
Check possibility of specification (takhsis)
If not open to interpretation, can the word be specified by other evidence? If yes, it is mufassar (explained). If no, it is muhkam (clear).
Mufassar can be abrogated but not interpreted away; muhkam is the strongest and cannot be abrogated or specified.
5
Apply the hierarchy
Note that these four types are hierarchical: every nass is also zahir, every mufassar is also nass and zahir, etc. When classifying, use the lowest applicable category.
This differs from the first division where types were mutually exclusive.

Commentary
This passage introduces the second major division of words in Hanafi usul, based on the clarity or obscurity of meaning. The author explains that words are classified into four levels of clarity: ظاهر (apparent), نص (explicit), مفسر (explained), and محكم (clear). These are distinguished by two criteria: first, whether the word is open to interpretation (تأویل); if it is, then whether its clarity is from the wording itself (ظاهر) or from an external factor (نص). If not open to interpretation, then whether it can be specified (تخصیص) — if yes, it is مفسر; if no, it is محكم. The author notes a key difference from the first division: the first division's types were mutually exclusive, while these four are hierarchical (each lower type is included in the higher). Therefore, each has an opposite counterpart in obscurity. The passage begins to outline the four levels of obscurity: خفی (hidden), which is due to an external cause; then it mentions that if the obscurity is not due to an external cause, we check if it can be removed by reflection — implying the other three levels (مشکل, مجمل, متشابه) which are not named here but are standard in Hanafi texts. This classification is crucial for understanding how texts are interpreted and which rulings they can support.

Key Terms
ظاهر (zahir): A word whose meaning is clear from its own wording, yet still open to interpretation (تأویل).
نص (nass): A word whose meaning is clear not from its own wording but due to an external factor (e.g., context), and is open to interpretation.
مفسر (mufassar): A word whose meaning is clear and not open to interpretation, but can be specified (تخصیص) by evidence.
محكم (muhkam): A word whose meaning is clear, not open to interpretation, and cannot be specified or abrogated.
خفی (khafi): A word whose meaning is hidden due to an external cause (عارض), but can be clarified with effort.

Second Division: Classification by Clarity and Hiddenness
Explains the second division classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four levels of clarity (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) and their four counterparts of hiddenness (khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih) according to Hanafi usul, and apply the criteria for categorization.

Core Idea
Words are classified by clarity into four hierarchical levels, each with a corresponding hidden counterpart.

The second division of word classification examines how clear or hidden a word's meaning is. Unlike the first division (based on generality), this division is hierarchical: the higher levels of clarity include the lower ones. Understanding these categories is essential for determining the strength of evidence in legal texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two axes
Note that the division considers both clarity (zuhur) and hiddenness (khafa') of meaning. Each axis has four types.
The clarity types and hiddenness types are counterparts.
2
Analyze the clarity types
For clarity, check: is the meaning open to interpretation (ta'wil)? If yes, is the clarity due to the wording itself (zahir) or an external cause (nass)? If not open to interpretation, does it accept deferral (ta'khir)? If yes, mufassar; if no, muhkam.
Zahir < Nass < Mufassar < Muhkam in strength.
3
Analyze the hiddenness types
For hiddenness, check: is the hiddenness due to an external cause (khafi) or the wording itself? If due to wording, can reflection remove it? Yes = mushkil, No = does the Lawgiver promise explanation? Yes = mujmal, No = mutashabih.
Khafi < Mushkil < Mujmal < Mutashabih in obscurity.
4
Note the hierarchy nuance
Unlike the first division (mutually exclusive), these types are hierarchical: the higher includes the lower. For example, a muhkam word is also mufassar, nass, and zahir, but not vice versa.
This affects how we weigh conflicting texts.

Third Division: Classification by Usage
Explains the third division classification.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish haqiqah, majaz, sarih, and kinayah according to Hanafi usul, and apply the criteria to classify words based on their usage and disclosure.

Core Idea
Words are classified by usage into literal (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz), each potentially used with disclosure (sarih) or concealment (kinayah).

The third division shifts focus from inherent clarity to how a word is used. It asks: is the word employed in its original meaning or a transferred one? And is its intended meaning disclosed openly or concealed? This classification helps the jurist determine whether a text is straightforward or requires interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Determine the usage: haqiqah vs majaz
Check if the word is used in its original, literal meaning (haqiqah) or a metaphorical meaning (majaz). This is the primary distinction.
Majaz requires a link (qarīnah) that prevents the literal meaning.
2
Determine the mode: sarih vs kinayah
Regardless of haqiqah or majaz, check if the meaning is disclosed openly (sarih) or concealed and requires inference (kinayah).
Sarih and kinayah are not independent categories but are superimposed on haqiqah and majaz.
3
Recognize the two methods of the usuliyun
Some scholars (like al-Bazdawi) separate the division into two parts: isti'mal (haqiqah/majaz) and jaryan fi bab al-bayan (sarih/kinayah). Others (like author of al-Tawdih) start with two primary divisions.
This explains why the text says 'they are four' but the relationship is cross-cutting.
4
Apply to a text
Given a phrase, first decide if it is haqiqah or majaz, then whether it is sarih or kinayah. This determines the evidentiary weight.
A sarih haqiqah is the strongest; a kinayah majaz is the weakest.

The Hanafi Proof that Amr Does Not Imply Repetition
Hanafi proof on non-repetition of amr and objection about worship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to reconstruct the Hanafi proof that the imperative (amr) does not imply repetition, using the derivation from the masdar, and critically evaluate the objection regarding repeated acts of worship.

Core Idea
The imperative is derived from the singular masdar; singularity in origin precludes repetition by default.

The passage presents the core Hanafi usul argument: the imperative form (amr) is a concise version of the masdar (verbal noun), which is singular. Since the masdar indicates a single instance, the command derived from it also indicates a single instance. This proof is then challenged by the fact that many acts of worship (e.g., Hajj, prayer) are repeated. Understanding this proof and its response is central to the Hanafi position on the scope of commands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi claim
The Hanafi claim is that the imperative form does not require repetition (takrar). The proof is based on the linguistic relationship between the imperative and the masdar.
The claim is a rule of usul: al-amr la yufidu al-takrar.
2

The imperative is 'summarized from requesting the action using the masdar' (mukhtasar min talab al-fi'l bi al-masdar). For example, 'idrib' is derived from 'I request striking from you' using the masdar 'darb'.
The word 'masdar' itself is singular in form.
3
Apply the principle of singularity
In singular words (alfaz al-wahdan), the meaning of oneness (ma'na al-tawahhud) is observed. The masdar is a singular word, so it indicates a single instance. Since the imperative is even more concise, it also indicates a single instance.
The passage says: 'معنى التوحد مرعي في الفاظ الوحدان'.
4
State the conclusion
Therefore, the command does not demand repetition; it is fulfilled by doing the commanded act once. Repetition would require a separate evidence (dalil) external to the command.
The proof is aqli (rational) based on linguistic analysis.
5
Evaluate the objection from worship
The objector asks: if commands do not repeat, why are acts of worship (like Hajj) repeated yearly? The passage ends, but the Hanafi response is that such repetition is established by a separate dalil (e.g., the verse 'wa atimmu al-hajja wa al-‘umrata lillah' indicating obligation each year), not by the inherent nature of the command.
This objection shows that the rule has exceptions only when supported by additional evidence.

Nass: Definition and Distinction from Zahir
Defines Nass and explains its distinction from Zahir with a dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Nass according to the Hanafi school, distinguish it from Zahir by applying the condition of sawq al-kalam, and identify the ruling and possibility of ta'wil.

Core Idea
Nass is clearer than Zahir due to the speaker's intent, but still allows interpretation.

In Hanafi usul, texts are classified by levels of clarity. You already know Zahir (clear by wording). Now we encounter the next level: Nass. This lesson defines Nass, explains what makes it stronger than Zahir, and clarifies a key dispute about whether the speaker's specific intent is a condition. Understanding Nass is critical because it determines how binding a ruling is and when interpretation is allowed.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of Nass
Nass is speech that has greater apparentness than Zahir, but the extra clarity comes from the speaker's intention (ma'na min al-mutakallim), not from the wording itself (la fi nafs al-sigha). This means the wording alone might be ambiguous, but because the speaker brought the speech specifically for that meaning, it becomes stronger.
Compare to Zahir: Zahir is clear from wording alone, regardless of speaker's intent.
2
Analyze the condition of sawq al-kalam
There is a dispute: the famous view (mashhur) holds that sawq al-kalam (intent) is a condition for Nass, and its absence is a condition for Zahir. The correct view (sahih) is that sawq is a condition only for Nass; Zahir does not require either presence or absence of intent.
The correct view is more nuanced: Zahir's clarity is independent of intent.
3
Apply the example of polygyny verse
The verse فَانكِحُوا مَا طَابَ لَكُمْ مِنَ النِّسَاءِ is Nass for polygyny because it was revealed in response to the Companions' question about the permissible number of wives. The speaker (Allah) brought this speech specifically to answer that question.
Without the context of the question, the verse might be considered Zahir.
4
Understand the ruling and ta'wil
The ruling of Nass is that acting upon its clear meaning is obligatory (wajib), but it is still open to interpretation (ta'wil). If the text is general ('amm), it may be specified (takhsis); if it is literal (haqiqa), it may be metaphorical (majaz).
Nass is not absolutely definitive (qat'i); it can be overridden by stronger evidence.

Core Idea
Nas is clear but not sealed: acting upon it is obligatory, yet ta'wil remains possible.

In this lesson, we explore the concept of nas (explicit text) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. Define nas and state its ruling
Nas is a text whose meaning is clear (zahir) but still admits the possibility of ta'wil (interpretive departure). The ruling is that it is wajib (obligatory) to act upon its clear meaning; this obligation cannot be delayed or ignored.

In the Hanafi usul hierarchy, texts vary in clarity. The highest level is muhkam – a text whose meaning is so clear that no alteration or abrogation is possible. This lesson unpacks its definition, two types, and its decisive ruling. We will also see how earlier levels (zahir, nas, mufassar) relate to muhkam through Quranic examples.

Guided Path

Read the passage's definition: muhkam is a text whose intended meaning is protected from the possibility of naskh (abrogation) and tabdil (alteration). Note that this protection can be inherent or external.
Muhkam is not just clear; it is permanently fixed.
2
Distinguish the two types of muhkam
Identify muhkam li-aynihi (inherently muhkam, e.g., attributes of tawhid) and muhkam li-ghayrihi (externally muhkam, due to the Prophet's death ending naskh). Understand that both types share the same ruling.
3
Understand the ruling of muhkam
The passage states that acting upon muhkam is wajib and qat'i, with no possibility of ta'wil, takhsis, or naskh. Denying it leads to kufr. This elevated status demands absolute acceptance.
In contrast, texts below muhkam (zahir, nas, mufassar) may allow ta'wil or takhsis under certain conditions.

Commentary
This passage defines 'muhkam' (perfectly clear text) – the highest level in the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity. Muhkam is immune to naskh (abrogation) and tabdil (alteration). Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of the fourth category in the hierarchy of textual clarity: al-muhkam. The commentary expands on the matn's definition. Muhkam is a text whose meaning is protected from abrogation and alteration either inherently (like verses on tawhid) or contextually (because the Prophet's death ended the possibility of abrogation). Acting upon it is wajib and qat'i; rejecting it renders one a disbeliever. The matn gives Quranic examples for zahir, nass, mufassar, and muhkam. The commentator explains each: the trade/usury verse is both zahir (explicit in permissibility/prohibition) and nass (distinguishing between them). The marriage verse is zahir for permissibility and nass for number. The mufassar example is the angels' prostration verse, where the words 'all' and 'together' eliminate probabilities of specification and interpretation. The commentator then addresses three objections to this example. First, that there remains probability about how they prostrated (in circles or rows). The answer: such detail does not affect the purpose of glorification, and the verse is not claimed to be clear from absolutely all aspects, only those relevant. Second, that the exception of Iblis is like specification. The commentator refutes two ways: exception is not with independent speech, and here it is a disconnected exception (munqati') which does not affect the general statement. Third, that the verse seems to have no probability of abrogation, so it should be muhkam. The answer: originally it had such probability (according to some scholars), so it qualifies as mufassar. The author of al-Tawdih suggests better examples: for mufassar, 'Fight the polytheists all together' (from rulings); for muhkam, 'Jihad continues until the Day of Resurrection' (also from rulings) rather than the belief verse. The Hanafi hierarchy is thus maintained, with muhkam as the highest level, followed by mufassar, nass, and zahir.

Key Terms
محكم (muhkam): A text whose meaning is protected from the probability of abrogation and alteration, either inherently or contextually.
مفسر (mufassar): A text that has no probability of specification (takhsis) or interpretation (tawil), though it may have probability of abrogation.
ظاهر (zahir): A text that indicates a meaning with probability of another interpretation; it is explicit in one sense but not exclusively.
نص (nass): A text that indicates a primary meaning and eliminates the possibility of other interpretations for that specific aspect, though it may still be subject to specification.

Definition of Khafi in Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Khafi: wording is clear, but meaning is hidden by an external factor—only reached through deliberate search.

In this lesson, we explore a key category in the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity: Khafi (hidden). Unlike previous categories where obscurity lies in the wording itself, Khafi texts are clear in expression but their intended application is concealed due to something external. Understanding Khafi is essential for accurately deriving rulings from the Quran and Sunnah when context or circumstances create ambiguity. We will examine its definition, ruling, and a classic example from the verse on theft.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining characteristic of Khafi
Read the definition: Khafi is a text whose intended meaning is hidden due to an external factor (a'rid) other than the wording itself. Contrast this with categories like Mushkil or Mujmal where obscurity is in the wording.
The external factor may be circumstantial, contextual, or practical.
2
Analyze the ruling (hukm) of Khafi
The text states that one must reflect (nazar) on the Khafi text to determine whether its hiddenness indicates a merit (maziyyah) or a deficiency (taqsan) in application. This investigation is wajib to uncover the intended meaning.
Not all hiddenness is negative; sometimes it points to a deeper ruling.

This demonstrates Khafi: the wording is clear, but the external factor (the thief's method) hides the intended application.
4
Distinguish Khafi from similar categories
Compare Khafi with Mushkil (obscure wording) and Mujmal (ambiguous wording). Use the criterion: Is the hiding factor within the wording or external? If external, it is Khafi.

Khafi: Definition, Ruling, and Application to Theft
Defining khafi and illustrating with theft ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khafi in Hanafi usul and apply its two-category rule (excess vs deficiency) to determine whether a textual ruling extends to a new case.

Core Idea
Khafi is hidden due to excess or deficiency in meaning—the ruling applies differently based on which type.

A text may appear clear (zahir) but still have a hidden aspect when applied to new cases. In Hanafi usul, this is called khafi—hiddenness that requires effort to uncover. The resolution depends on whether the hiddenness comes from an excess of meaning (the text covers more than usual) or a deficiency (it lacks an essential element). The famous example of theft and its punishment illustrates how the Hanafi school uses this distinction to include pickpockets but exclude grave robbers from the hadd penalty.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of khafi
Khafi is a text whose intended meaning is not immediately apparent but requires investigation. The hiddenness arises either because the word's meaning is more comprehensive than usual (excess) or because it lacks some element that would bring it under the ruling (deficiency).
Khafi is one level below zahir in the Hanafi clarity hierarchy: zahir, khafi, mushkil, mujmal.
2
Distinguish the two causes of hiddenness
If the hiddenness is due to an excess of meaning (the term covers extra instances not normally included), the original ruling does NOT apply to those extra instances. If due to deficiency (the term lacks a necessary attribute), the original ruling also does NOT apply. In both cases, the ruling is restricted.
The author says: 'If hiddenness is due to increase, original ruling not applied; if due to deficiency, original ruling not applied.'
3
Apply to the theft verse
The Quranic verse (5:38) commands hand-cutting for male and female thieves. The word 'sariq' (thief) is zahir in meaning, but becomes khafi when applied to pickpockets and grave robbers. Compare their acts to the Shariah definition of theft: taking protected wealth secretly to nisab. A pickpocket takes openly (excess) so the verse applies. A grave robber takes from an unprotected place (deficiency) so the verse does not apply.
The definition of theft is: 'akhdh mal muhtaram muhraz khufyatan' (taking protected wealth secretly).
4
Examine the dispute on the grave robber
Even if the grave is secure, the dominant Hanafi position (Imam Abu Hanifa) holds that hand-cutting is still not applied because the lack of protection is inherent in the situation (the dead cannot guard). A rare exception (secure grave) is not given weight. Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam al-Shafi'i hold that cutting applies based on a hadith: 'Whoever robs a grave, we cut.' The Hanafi response interprets that hadith as a discretionary punishment (siyasa), not the prescribed hadd.
The hadith 'la qat'a 'ala al-mukhtafi' (no cutting on the grave robber) and the practice of the Prophet and caliphs support the Hanafi view.

Haqiqah and Majaz: The Rule of Precedence
Definition of signs to distinguish literal from metaphorical; rule that haqiqah takes precedence over majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between haqiqah and majaz using the Hanafi sign of deniability and apply the rule that haqiqah takes precedence over majaz when interpreting a legal text.

Core Idea
Haqiqah is inseparable from its referent; majaz is detachable — and when both are possible, the literal meaning wins.

How do we know whether a word in a sacred text is meant literally or figuratively? The Hanafi usul al-fiqh provides a clear sign: if the meaning can be truthfully denied of the referent, it is majaz; otherwise, it is haqiqah. And crucially, when both interpretations are possible, the literal must be chosen. This lesson unpacks that rule through a Quranic example on oaths.

Guided Path
1
Define haqiqah and majaz
Extract the definitions from the text: haqiqah is the literal meaning that cannot be detached from its referent; majaz is a metaphorical meaning that can be denied of the referent.

2
Identify the distinguishing sign
The sign is whether you can truthfully say 'it is not X' about the referent. If yes, it's majaz; if no, it's haqiqah.
This 'deniability test' is the operative criteria in this passage.
3
State the rule of precedence
The principle: whenever acting upon haqiqah is possible, majaz is dropped. Majaz is a substitute (musta'ar) for haqiqah and does not compete with the original.
This is a core Hanafi rule in interpreting ambiguous texts.

In metaphorical usage (majaz), the meaning can be detached: a grandfather may be called 'father' metaphorically, but it is correct to say 'he is not a father' in the literal sense. The core rule follows: when interpreting a text, the literal meaning takes precedence over the metaphorical whenever the literal is possible. This is because majaz is merely a stand-in (na'ib, khalifah, musta'ar) for haqiqah, and the borrowed item does not compete with the original. The author then applies this principle to Quranic interpretation. The verse '..بما عقدتم الأيمان' contains the word 'عقدتم' from 'عقد'. Here, 'عقد' has a literal meaning (the actual binding of an oath) and a metaphorical meaning (mere resolve or intention). Since it is possible to apply the literal meaning (the actual oath-taking), the metaphorical reading is dropped, and the verse refers to intentionally bound oaths, not merely resolved ones. The text then notes that this explanation opens onto the classification of oaths into three types, which is the subject of further detail in subsequent portions of the book.

Key Terms
حقیقت (ḥaqīqah): Literal meaning of a word, which cannot be denied of its referent (e.g., a real father).
مجاز (majāz): Metaphorical meaning that can be detached from its referent; e.g., calling a grandfather 'father'.
مسمی (musammā): The named thing or referent of a word.
مستعار (mustaʿār): That which is borrowed; metaphor is considered a borrowed usage from the literal.

Definition and Explanation of Isharat al-Nass
Defining isharat al-nass and explaining its conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of isharat al-nass and distinguish it from ibarat al-nass and iqtida al-nass by identifying its conditions.

Core Idea
Isharat al-nass: a linguistically implied ruling not intended by the speaker, weaker than ibarat but stronger than iqtida.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, textual indications are categorized by strength. Among them, isharat al-nass holds a middle position: it is derived from the wording but not as the primary intent. This lesson unpacks its formal definition clause by clause, showing how each condition excludes other types of textual inference. Mastering these distinctions is essential for accurate derivation of rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of textual indication
Recall that Hanafi usul recognizes ibarat al-nass (direct meaning), isharat al-nass (implied meaning), and iqtida al-nass (necessary meaning). Note that isharat al-nass occupies the middle ground.
This lesson focuses solely on isharat al-nass.
2
Parse the definition clause by clause
The definition states: 'acting upon what is established by its wording linguistically, but it is not intended nor was the text brought for it, and it is not apparent from every aspect.' Break down each part: (a) established by wording, (b) linguistically, (c) not intended, (d) not the purpose of the text, (e) not apparent from every aspect.
Each clause serves to exclude something else.
3
Apply each condition to exclude other types
The condition 'linguistically' excludes iqtida al-nass (which is rational). The condition 'not intended nor was the text brought for it' excludes ibarat al-nass (which is the intended meaning). Verify that the definition now only covers isharat.
The phrase 'not apparent from every aspect' is not a separate condition but emphasizes lower clarity.
4
Distinguish isharat from iqtida
Isharat relies purely on language (e.g., the implication of a phrase). Iqtida relies on rational necessity (the text would be invalid without a missing element). Practice by thinking of examples: the verse on foster mothers implies foster sisters via isharat; a statement 'the butcher sells meat' implies he sells it as food via iqtida.
This distinction is crucial for correct derivation.

Commentary
This passage formally defines isharat al-nass (indication of the text), which is the second type of dalalat al-nass. Unlike ibarat al-nass, the ruling derived via isharat al-nass is not the primary objective of the text; it is a secondary implication that is linguistically understood. The definition has three key conditions: (1) the ruling must be established by the wording according to linguistic usage, (2) it is not what the speaker intended to convey, and (3) the text was not brought specifically for that ruling. The commentator then explains the function of each condition: the word 'binazmihi' includes both isharat and ibarat, so other conditions are needed to differentiate. The condition 'lughatan' excludes iqtida al-nass (which relies on rational necessity). The condition 'ghayru maqsud' excludes ibarat al-nass (which is the intended meaning). Finally, the phrase 'not apparent from every aspect' is not a separate condition but emphasizes that isharat al-nass is less clear than ibarat al-nass; it is called 'isharat' because it merely indicates rather than being explicit.

Key Terms

اقتضاء النص (iqtida al-nass): A meaning that is necessary for the text to be valid but is omitted from the wording; derived by rational necessity.

Distinguishing 'Ibarat al-Nass and Isharat al-Nass: Explicit vs. Implicit Textual Indication
Defining and distinguishing two types of textual indication with conflict resolution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'ibarrah and isharah in a given text, apply the Hanafi rule that 'ibarrah takes precedence in conflict, and identify the practical consequence (thamarah) in the menses example.

Core Idea
Explicit meaning ('ibarrah) always outweighs implicit meaning (isharah) when they conflict, because primary intent is stronger.

This lesson introduces two key concepts in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: 'Ibarat al-Nass and Isharat al-Nass. Using a sensory analogy and a concrete fiqh example, the text explains how both yield definitive rulings individually, but when they conflict, the explicit meaning takes precedence. The menses dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is illustrates the practical outcome.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definitions via sensory analogy
The text compares intentional focus (direct gaze) to 'ibarrah and peripheral vision to isharah. This helps grasp that 'ibarrah is the directly intended meaning, while isharah is an implication not primarily intended.
Keep the analogy in mind when analyzing texts.
2
Identify each type in a concrete example
Read the Quranic verse (al-Baqarah 2:233) and see how the lam in 'al-mawludi lahu' indicates lineage from father—this is isharah. Then read the hadith on women's deficiency: the explicit purpose is to show religious deficiency, but the word 'shatr' implies a ruling on menses duration.
Always ask: Is this meaning directly intended or merely implied?
3
Apply the conflict resolution rule
When a single text yields two possible rulings—one via 'ibarrah and one via isharah—the ruling from 'ibarrah takes precedence because it is more primary (awla) and more indicative (adallu).
This is a fixed Hanafi principle; it does not depend on personal preference.
4
Work through the menses example
The hadith on women's deficiency is explicit about religious deficiency but implicitly suggests menses maximum of 15 days (via 'shatr'). Another hadith (al-Daraqutni) explicitly states maximum 10 days. Since the second hadith is explicit ('ibarrah) on the menses duration, it overrides the implicit indication from the first hadith. Thus Hanafis rule 10 days; Shafi'is, relying on the isharah, rule 15.
Note the practical difference: a woman bleeding beyond 10 days is considered istihadah in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage from Khulasat al-Anwar is an expansion on the distinction between two types of textual indication: 'Ibarat al-Nass (explicit meaning) and Isharat al-Nass (implicit meaning). The author begins with a sensory analogy: when you intentionally look at someone, you also see things peripherally; the intentional focus is like 'ibarrah, and the peripheral is like isharah. Then an example of isharah is given from the Quranic verse (al-Baqarah 2:233), where the lam in 'al-mawludi lahu' indicates that lineage is attributed to the father, not the mother—an implied meaning. Both types yield definitive rulings when alone, but when they conflict within the same text, the explicit meaning ('ibarrah) takes precedence because it is more direct and stronger as a textual indicator. The commentator illustrates this with the hadith on women's deficiency: the explicit purpose is to explain religious deficiency (not praying/fasting during menses), but the word 'shatr' (half) implies that the maximum menses is fifteen days. That hadith is from clear, explicit wording ('ibarrah), so it overrides the isharah of the first hadith. Thus, the Hanafi ruling is that the maximum menses is ten days. Finally, the author notes that isharah can also carry generality ('umum) like 'ibarrah, meaning both can indicate general rules. This lesson is foundational for understanding how Hanafi usul prioritizes textual indications.

Conflict between Dalalat al-Nass and Isharat al-Nass on Kaffarah for Intentional Murder
Analyzing the conflict between two textual indications regarding kaffarah for intentional murder

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of tarjih to resolve a conflict between dalalat al-nass and isharat al-nass, and state the ruling on kaffarah for intentional murder.

Core Idea
When texts conflict, isharat al-nass can override dalalat al-nass if the former gives a complete punishment.

This lesson examines a key usul dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding whether intentional homicide requires kaffarah (expiation). The conflict arises from two different types of textual indication: dalalat al-nass (implied meaning by necessary concomitant) from the verse on accidental killing, and isharat al-nass (contextual indication) from the verse threatening eternal Hell for the intentional killer. We will see how the Hanafis resolve this conflict by preferring isharat al-nass.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting indications
Locate the dalalat al-nass from verse 4:92 (expiation for accidental killing implies a fortiori expiation for intentional) and the isharat al-nass from verse 4:93 (Hell as complete punishment for intentional killer implies no expiation).
Dalalat al-nass is a mafhum muwafaqa; isharat al-nass is a contextual inference.
2
Understand the Shafi'i argument
Imam al-Shafi'i relies on dalalat al-nass: since accidental killing requires expiation, intentional killing more so requires it.
This is a straightforward a fortiori reasoning (mafhum muwafaqa).
3
Understand the Hanafi counter-argument
The Hanafis point to isharat al-nass from 4:93: 'his recompense is Hell, abiding therein forever' – this indicates a complete punishment that leaves no room for expiation.
Isharat al-nass is considered a valid indicator for preference.
4
Apply the Hanafi tarjih principle
When dalalat al-nass and isharat al-nass conflict, the Hanafi school gives preference to isharat al-nass because it directly addresses the intentional killer and specifies a complete punishment, thus overriding the weaker indication.
Tarjih is based on the completeness and directness of the punishment.
5
State the practical outcome
The thamarah al-khilaf: in Hanafi fiqh, intentional murder requires only qisas or diya, not kaffarah; in Shafi'i fiqh, kaffarah is also obligatory.
This difference affects actual fatwa practice.

Thus, both punishments can apply simultaneously because they address different aspects of the crime. The passage demonstrates the Hanafi methodology in handling conflicting textual indications and the use of different types of nass (ibarah, dalalah, isharah).

إشارة النص (isharat al-nass): An indication from the text that is not explicit but inferred from the context or purpose.

Hudud and Kaffarat: Established by Dalalat al-Nass, Not Qiyas
Explaining the principle that hudud and kaffarat are proven by dalalat al-nass, not qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why hudud and kaffarat are established by dalalat al-nass and not by qiyas, and apply this principle to determine which method of textual extension is valid in these cases.

Core Idea
Hudud require certainty; only the definitive implication of a text suffices, not speculative qiyas.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, fixed penalties (hudud) and expiations (kaffarat) carry severe consequences and thus demand the highest level of legal certainty. This lesson examines the epistemological distinction between dalalat al-nass (the definitive implied meaning of a text) and qiyas (speculative analogy). The Hanafi school holds that hudud and kaffarat can only be proven through the former, because the latter introduces doubt. Through the examples of the companion Ma'iz and the Quranic verse on muharibun, we will see how dalalat al-nass extends rulings from a specific text to all cases sharing the same effective cause ('illah) without resorting to independent analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
State the core rule: hudud and kaffarat are proven by dalalat al-nass, not by qiyas.
2
Understand the Reasoning
Recognize that dalalat al-nass is considered qat'i (definitive) because the effective cause is directly indicated by the text itself, whereas qiyas involves speculative identification of the cause.
Hudud require certainty, so zanni (speculative) evidence is insufficient.
3
Analyze the First Example (Ma'iz)
The Prophet (PBUH) applied the hadd of rajm on Ma'iz based on his confession (ibarat al-nass). The cause is the act of zina, not Ma'iz's identity. By dalalat al-nass, the same hadd applies to all fornicators.
The extension is not based on qiyas because the text itself indicates the cause.
4
Analyze the Second Example (Muharibun)
The Quranic verse (5:33) prescribes a hadd for those who wage war against Allah and His Messenger. The cause is waging war and spreading corruption. Supporters share this cause, so by dalalat al-nass, the same hadd applies to them.
The extension relies on the definitive indication of the text, not independent analogy.
5
Distinguish from Qiyas
In qiyas, the mujtahid independently identifies the cause, which introduces speculative doubt. In dalalat al-nass, the cause is explicitly or implicitly indicated by the text itself, providing certainty.
This distinction is critical for understanding Hanafi usul.

قياس (qiyas): Analogical deduction that extends a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a shared effective cause.
