Dalaalat al-Nas: Certain Implication vs. Qiyas and Conflict with Ishaarah
Defining dalaalat al-nas, distinguishing from qiyas, and resolving conflict with ishaarah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish dalaalat al-nas from qiyas, derive a ruling using dalaalat al-nas from a Quranic text, and apply the Hanafi rule that ishaarah takes precedence over dalaalah in case of textual conflict.

Core Idea
Dalaalat al-nas yields certain rulings through a fortiori implication, distinct from speculative qiyas.

In Hanafi usul, it is crucial to distinguish between different types of textual indications. This lesson introduces dalaalat al-nas—the implied yet certain meaning derived from the text by necessary implication. You will learn why it is not qiyas, how to derive a ruling from the 'uff' verse, and how to resolve conflicts when dalaalah opposes ishaarah, using the case of expiation for intentional killing as an example.

Guided Path
1
Recognize dalaalat al-nas as a textual implication
Understand that dalaalat al-nas goes beyond the literal wording (ibarah) to a necessary meaning. For example, the prohibition of 'uff' implies prohibition of any greater harm.
This is not qiyas because the implication is from the language itself, not a constructed analogy.
2
Distinguish dalaalat al-nas from qiyas
Identify two key differences: (1) dalaalat al-nas gives certain (qati) rulings, while qiyas gives speculative (zanni) rulings; (2) dalaalat al-nas has no rejecters, while qiyas does.
3
Apply dalaalat al-nas to the 'uff' verse
From Quran 17:23's prohibition of 'uff', extract the 'illah (causing pain) and extend to hitting and cursing by a fortiori reasoning.
This is an example of qiyas awla, but it is still dalaalat al-nas because the implication is inherent in the text.
4
Understand the conflict between dalaalah and ishaarah
Learn that both normally give certain rulings, but when they conflict, ishaarah is given precedence. The Quran 4:92 (expiation for accidental killing) by dalaalah seems to require expiation for intentional killing, but Quran 4:93 (eternal Hell for intentional killing) by ishaarah implies no expiation is needed.
5
Resolve the conflict in the intentional killing case
Apply the Hanafi rule: ishaarah overrides dalaalah. Therefore, no expiation for intentional killing; only qisas/diyah. This resolves the apparent contradiction and harmonizes the texts.
The verse 4:93 addresses the spiritual punishment, while another text (e.g., Surah al-Baqarah) addresses worldly punishment.

Imam Razi's claim and its refutation:
Imam Razi and some other scholars consider qiyas and dalālat al-naṣṣ to be the same thing. This is false, because what is known through qiyas is speculative, whereas what is established through dalālat al-naṣṣ is certain. The second difference is that there are rejecters of qiyas, but dalālat al-naṣṣ has no rejecter. The example of dalālat al-naṣṣ given by the author:
Allah says: 'Do not say to them 'uff' nor repel them' – meaning do not even say 'uff' to parents. The author says that the prohibition of 'uff' is dependent on the prohibition of hitting. This is lenient; rather, he intends that from the prohibition of 'uff' the prohibition of hitting is inferred. The detail: The meaning of Allah's command is a prohibition of harming parents, which is derived as 'ibārat al-naṣṣ'. Its necessary meaning is to cause parents pain and mental distress, and from that same meaning of prohibition, the prohibition of hitting parents is also established. Similarly, cursing them, etc. This is by way of dalālat al-naṣṣ: when the lowest level of pain and hurt – tā'fīf (saying 'uff') – is forbidden, then its highest level – hitting and abusing – is even more forbidden.

'And what is established by it is like what is established by ishārah, except when there is conflict.'
Dalālat al-naṣṣ, like 'ibārah and other types, gives certain rulings. However, in case of conflict, ishārah prevails over it. The commentators presented this example: 'Whoever kills a believer by mistake, then (upon him is) the freeing of a believing slave.' – i.e., if a person accidentally kills a Muslim, freeing a believing slave is obligatory as expiation.

Explanation of the conflict:
From 'ibārat al-naṣṣ, the expiation for accidental killing is established. And by way of dalālat al-naṣṣ, it is also understood that whoever intentionally kills a Muslim should also have to give expiation, because his crime is greater than the mistaken one. Since expiation is given for the lesser, it is even more appropriate for the greater. Using this reasoning, Imam Shafi'i held that expiation is also required for intentional killing. However, according to the Hanafis, for intentional killing there is only qisas or diyah, not expiation. The reason is that this ruling (of dalālat al-naṣṣ) conflicts with another verse. In that verse, Allah says: 'But whoever kills a believer intentionally, his recompense is Hell, abiding therein.' From this, as ishārat al-naṣṣ, it is understood that in case of intentional killing, expiation is not obligatory, because this is the complete punishment for the intentional killer. Therefore, to resolve the conflict, we apply the principle of preference, giving preference to ishārat al-naṣṣ (non-obligation of expiation) over dalālat al-naṣṣ (obligation of expiation). Hence, expiation is not required for the intentional killer.

Objection: If Hell is the complete punishment, then the intentional killer should not receive qisas/diyah either.
Answer: In killing, there are two crimes: (1) destruction of life, (2) commission of a forbidden act. In this verse, the punishment for the commission of the forbidden act, i.e., Hell, is mentioned. As for the destruction of life, its punishment is not mentioned here; rather, in Surah al-Baqarah it is: 'And We prescribed for them therein: a life for a life, an eye for an eye, a nose for a nose...' – so the punishment of qisas or diyah is carried out based on another text. (2) Scholastic answer: According to this text, Hell is the complete punishment. We have not added anything to it. However, as for qisas, it is established from another text (mentioned above).

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a matn (likely Noor al-Anwar) discussing the usul concept of dalaalat al-nas (indication of the text). The author begins by explaining that this chapter deals with dalaalat al-nas, while the topics of iqtida al-nass and mahdhuf (implied elements) are excluded. He then refutes Imam Razi's claim that qiyas and dalaalat al-nas are identical. Two arguments are given: (1) dalaalat al-nas yields certain rulings (qati), while qiyas yields speculative (zanni); (2) dalaalat al-nas is universally accepted, while qiyas has rejecters. This establishes dalaalat al-nas as a stronger proof.

The author then provides the matn's example: the Quranic verse 'Do not say to them 'uff'' (17:23). The literal prohibition of 'uff' (a minimal expression of annoyance) implies by dalaalat al-nas that any greater harm (e.g., hitting) is also prohibited. This is a fortiori (qiyas awla) reasoning, but the Hanafi usul distinguishes it from qiyas because it is based on the language itself, not a constructed analogy.

Next, the passage discusses the relationship between dalaalat al-nas and ishaarat al-nas (indication by allusion). Normally, both give certain rulings. However, when they conflict, ishaarah is given precedence. The example is the verses on killing: Verse 4:92 'ibarah establishes expiation for accidental killing. By dalaalat al-nas (a fortiori), expiation would seem obligatory for intentional killing. But verse 4:93 threatens the intentional killer with eternal Hell, which by ishaarah implies that no further expiation is due. The Hanafis resolve the conflict by preferring ishaarah over dalaalah, thus not requiring expiation for intentional killing.

The passage then addresses a potential objection: if Hell is the complete punishment, why do intentional killers still face qisas/diyah? The answer distinguishes two aspects of the crime: (1) the spiritual offense (violation of a divine prohibition) punished by Hell, and (2) the worldly harm (taking a life) punished by qisas/diyah from another text. This shows the Hanafi commitment to harmonizing all textual evidence without contradiction.

In summary, this lesson clarifies the nature of dalaalat al-nas, its evidential weight, and how it is prioritized relative to other textual indications.

Key Terms
دلالة النص (dalālat al-naṣṣ): The indication of the text that goes beyond its literal wording but is necessarily implied, such that if the lesser is prohibited, the greater is also prohibited by a fortiori reasoning.
اشارة النص (ishārat al-naṣṣ): The indication of the text by allusion, where the text points to a ruling without explicitly stating it, e.g., the mention of a complete punishment implying the absence of another.
عبارة النص (ʻibārat al-naṣṣ): The explicit wording of the text from which the ruling is directly understood.
قیاس (qiyās): Analogical reasoning where a ruling is extended from a original case to a new case based on a common effective cause.

Intending Multiple Divorces with Informative Phrases
Usul al-Fiqh dispute on the effect of intention in khabar divorce phrases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafis reject the intention of three divorces when using phrases 'anti tāliq' or 'ṭallaqtuk', and evaluate the Shafi'i counterargument based on linguistic principles.

Core Idea
Informative divorce phrases imply only one prior divorce; generality in the implied is not accepted.

In Hanafi usul, the classification of speech as informative (khabar) or performative (inshā') has profound legal consequences. This lesson examines a classic dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is over the validity of intending three divorces when using phrases like 'anti tāliq'. Understanding the linguistic reasoning behind each school's position is essential for grasping how usul al-fiqh governs real-world marital cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Speech Type
Recognize that 'anti tāliq' and 'ṭallaqtuk' are considered informative (khabar) by Hanafis, meaning they report an event that must have occurred before the statement. Per Shafi'is, they are performative (inshā'), creating the legal effect directly.
The classification determines the need for a prior event.
2
Apply the Principle of Iqtiḍā'
For a khabar to be meaningful, the informed-of must exist before the statement. Since no actual prior divorce occurred, we assume one by necessity (iqtiḍā') to avoid rendering the speech meaningless.
This is a linguistic necessity, not a factual assumption.
3
Determine the Scope of the Implied
The implied element (muqtaḍā) is the assumed prior divorce. Hanafis hold that the muqtaḍā is specific and does not carry generality (ʿumūm). Thus, only one divorce is implied, not multiple.
This principle is key to rejecting the intention of three.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i View
Shafi'is treat these phrases as direct declarations (inshā'), so no prior event is needed. The speaker can intend any number of divorces, and the intention is effective.
The dispute is rooted in different linguistic classifications.
5
Derive the Practical Outcome (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
If a husband says 'anti tāliq' intending three, according to Hanafis only one divorce occurs; according to Shafi'is, three occur if intended.
This difference has significant real-world implications for marriage continuity.

Commentary
This passage discusses a key dispute between Hanafis and Shafi‘is regarding the effect of intention in pronouncements of divorce that are phrased as informative statements (khabar) rather than performative ones (inshā’). The Hanafi position is based on the principle that the words 'anti ṭāliq' (you are divorced) and ‘ṭallaqtuk’ (I have divorced you) are news (khabar) about an event that must have already occurred. To make sense of the statement, one must assume (bi-ṭarīq al-iqtiḍā’) that a prior divorce took place, even if it did not in reality. However, since we are assuming only one occurrence by implication, intending three divorces is not valid because the implied element does not have generality (lā naqūlu bi ʿumūm al-muqtaḍā). The Shafi‘is, on the other hand, treat these phrases as direct declarations of divorce (inshā’), so the speaker can intend multiple divorces. This dispute highlights the importance of linguistic and legal principles in determining the outcome of marital dissolution.

Key Terms
خبر (khabar): An informative statement that reports an event; requires the event to have occurred before the statement.
إنشاء (inshā'): A performative utterance that creates a legal effect; not a report of a prior event.
اقتضا (iqtiḍā'): Implication; the necessity of assuming an omitted element for the statement to be coherent.
مقتضی (muqtaḍā): The implied element; here, the prior divorce assumed to make the khabar meaningful.
