Understanding the Prophet's Great Character and Its Exclusivity
Commentary on a phrase from the matn.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three interpretations of 'khuluq 'azim' and evaluate the objection regarding exclusivity using principles of textual interpretation (ta'wil).

Core Idea
The Prophet's great character is uniquely his, yet the Quranic verse lacks explicit exclusivity — a classic usul tension.

This commentary passage unpacks why the author used an allusion rather than the Prophet's name, defines 'great character,' and presents three interpretations. It also raises an objection: the phrase implies exclusivity, but the Quranic verse does not. Analyzing this helps us see how commentators navigate between literal wording and intended meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's rhetorical choice
Recognize that the author said 'who is distinguished with great character' instead of naming the Prophet. This implies the attribute is so uniquely associated with him that it suffices.
This is a literary device (kinayah) that implies exclusivity (ikhtisas).
2
Define 'khuluq 'azim'
The commentator defines it as a moral faculty enabling easy action. This sets the conceptual foundation.
This definition is adapted to the prophetic context.
3
Analyze the three interpretations
List each opinion: (1) the Quran became his nature (from Aisha), (2) generosity in both worlds, (3) three specific hadith-based attributes. Note which is considered strongest.
The first is strongest; the third is 'gharib' (rare) in practice, not weak.
4
Examine the objection
The objection is that the author's phrase implies exclusivity (ikhtisas), but the Quranic verse 'And indeed you are of a great character' does not specify exclusivity. Why would the author base his phrase on that verse?
This is a question of talmih (allusion) vs. direct correspondence.
5
Evaluate the response
The passage presents the objection but leaves it open. This indicates that the commentator sees it as a valid point for discussion, not a fatal flaw. It invites further usul analysis.
One might answer that the exclusivity is understood from context, or that the verse itself implies it through the Prophet's unique station.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the praise phrase 'who is distinguished with great character' from the matn. The commentator first explains why the author did not explicitly name the Prophet: the attribute of great character is so uniquely associated with him that it suffices. He then defines 'khuluq 'azim' as a faculty for easy action. Three interpretations are presented: (1) the character of the Prophet was the Quran itself (from Aisha's hadith); (2) it means his extra ordinary generosity in this world and the Hereafter; (3) it consists of three specific prophetic commands: maintaining ties with those who sever, forgiving oppressors, and repaying evil with good. The commentator notes that the first opinion is strongest. Shaykh Ahmad considers the third interpretation as 'gharib' (rare) in practice, meaning it is difficult to implement in modern times, not that it is weak. Finally, an objection is raised: the author's phrase implies exclusivity (ikhtisas) while the Quranic verse 'And indeed you are of a great character' does not specify exclusivity, so how can it be considered a talmih? This objection is left open, indicating a point of discussion.

Key Terms
خلق عظیم (khuluq 'azīm): A moral faculty enabling easy performance of actions; defined specifically for the Prophet as his Quranic nature, generosity, and three prophetic attributes.
اختصاص (ikhtiṣāṣ): Exclusivity or distinction of a quality to a particular person.
تلمیح (talmīḥ): An allusion to a Quranic verse or hadith without quoting it directly.

Definition of the Quran: Linguistic Origin and Essential Attributes
Examines the linguistic derivation of 'Quran' and its definitional attributes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic derivation of 'Quran', identify its essential attributes as per the Hanafi definition, and apply the definition to exclude abrogated verses.

Core Idea
The Quran is the gradually revealed word of Allah, written in the mus-hafs of the seven reciters; its precise definition ensures no other book is confused with it.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the definition of the Quran. He examines whether 'Quran' is derived from 'qara'a' or 'qarana', showing that it is not a mere verbal noun but a specific name. He then unpacks each attribute: that it is revealed, that it is revealed to Prophet Muhammad, and that it is written in the mus-hafs. Along the way, he addresses objections about the nature of writing and the scope of the masahif, providing a rigorous usul analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic derivations of 'Quran'
Determine whether 'Quran' is a masdar from 'qara'a' (to read) or 'qarana' (to join). Note that in both cases, if it were a masdar, it would be a generic noun covering all books, not specific to the Quran.
This step establishes that 'Quran' is not a verbal noun but a proper name (ism 'alam).
2
Analyze the first definitional attribute 'al-munazzal'
The word 'al-munazzal' (revealed) excludes all books not from heaven. The addition of 'ala al-rasul' specifies it is revealed to Prophet Muhammad, excluding earlier scriptures.
The alif-lam in 'al-rasul' is for covenant, referring specifically to the Prophet.
3
Examine the nuance of tashdid vs. takhfif in 'munazzal'
With tashdid, it means gradual revelation; with takhfif, instantaneous. The passage provides two explanations for instantaneous: the annual Ramadan descent or Gabriel's final recitation.
The gradual revelation aligns with the Quran's actual descent over 23 years.
4
Understand the second attribute 'al-maktub fi al-masahif'
This attribute restricts the definition to what is written in the standard mus-hafs. The passage addresses an objection that writing is only of inscriptions, not words/meanings, and clarifies that writing establishes both essentially.
This attribute excludes abrogated verses and non-canonical recitations.
5
Determine the function of the alif-lam in 'al-masahif'
If it is for genus, the definition includes any mus-haf; if covenant, it refers to the seven reciters' mus-hafs. The covenantal reading is preferred for a comprehensive and restrictive definition.
The passage also explains why the mus-hafs themselves are not defined: they are well-known and to avoid undignified sale.

Firstly, mention an unfamiliar name and then clarify it by mentioning the known name. For example, someone said: (layth) you do not understand, so he said (asad). Similarly, (ghayth) followed by (matar), (ghudanfar) followed by (asad), etc. These are famous examples of definition by word (ta'rif lafzi). The second opinion is that it is a verbal noun (masdar). Then there is disagreement about which verb form it comes from. If it is from the verb 'qara'a' (nasara yansuru), then the masdar is in the meaning of the passive participle (maf'ul) and its meaning is 'that which is recited' and the reason for naming is clear. If it is from the verb 'qarana' (daraba yadribu), then also the masdar is in the meaning of the passive participle and its meaning is 'that whose verses and stories have mutual connection and relationship' and this matter is not hidden from anyone, as said by Imam al-Razi (may Allah have mercy on him). In both cases, this would not be a definition by word (ta'rif lafzi) but rather a generic noun (ism jins) and would include every book, because every book is recited and its contents and meanings have mutual connection. His statement: 'al-munazzal 'ala al-rasul 'alayhi al-salam': In the first opinion, this is the beginning of the real definition; in the second opinion, it is the first restriction. With (al-munazzal), all books that did not descend from heaven but were written by people are excluded. However, the definition includes the heavenly books (Torah, Psalms, Gospel, and the scriptures of previous prophets). Therefore, the restriction 'ala al-rasul 'alayhi al-salam' is added. The alif and lam of (al-rasul) are for the covenant (al-'ahd) and the meaning is the Holy Prophet (peace be upon him). Thus, the definition becomes specific to the Quran. The vocalization of (al-munazzal)... It is permissible to read it with tashdid and with takhfif. If with tashdid, then it is a passive participle form from the verb form of taf'il and its meaning is 'revealed gradually'. This is true for the Quran because the Holy Quran was revealed little by little over approximately thirty years in accordance with the welfare of the servants and circumstances. In the second reading, it is a passive participle from the verb form of if'al and the meaning of munzal is 'revealed at once'. The scholars gave two explanations for this: First, that in Ramadan every year the entire Quran would descend at once upon the Holy Prophet (peace be upon him). This explanation is not in accordance with analogy (qiyas). A better explanation is that in the year of his death, the Archangel Gabriel (peace be upon him) recited the entire Quran to the Holy Prophet (peace be upon him). And Allah knows best what is correct. His statement: 'al-maktub fi al-masahif'... This is the second part of the definition and the second attribute of the Holy Quran. Objection: Calling 'al-maktub' an attribute of the Holy Quran is not correct because writing is of inscriptions, not of words and meanings which the Quran comprises? Answer: Writing (kitabah) is derived from 'thabata' (to be firm) and is an ism maf'ul, and there is no doubt that words and meanings are established. The Phrase 'Mā Yalī Ibtidā' al-Shurū'' and Its Application to Asr Prayer
Textual analysis of an author's phrase and its application to Asr prayer timing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling on performing a current Asr prayer in partial time versus a missed Asr prayer (qadā'), and apply the concept of 'cause of obligation' (sabab al-wujūb) to determine the required time completeness for prayer validity.

Core Idea
The cause of obligation determines whether a prayer must be performed in complete time; today's Asr may be performed in partial time, yesterday's qadā' may not.

This passage from the Noor al-Anwar commentary clarifies the author's expression 'mā yalī ibtidā' al-shurū'' (what is adjacent to the beginning of the start) and then applies it to an important fiqh question: can a missed Asr prayer (qadā') be performed in the partial time at the end of the day, while the current Asr prayer can? The answer hinges on the cause of obligation (sabab al-wujūb) being the complete time for a missed prayer and the partial time for the current prayer. Let us walk through the analytical steps to understand this legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the phrase 'mā yalī ibtidā' al-shurū''
The author uses this expression to refer to the earliest part of the prayer time, which includes both the first complete part (juz' awwal) and the partial time (juz' nāqis) even though they are distinct. The first part is mentioned because the majority consider it important and recommend praying in it; the partial time is mentioned because Imam Zufar had a disagreement regarding that portion.
Note: The expression itself is a technical term in Hanafi usul to denote the very beginning of the time.
2
Identify the two types of prayer times: complete and partial
A complete time (waqt kāmil) is the full period from the entry of the prayer time to the next prayer entry (or sunrise/sunset). A partial time (juz' nāqis) is a portion of that period, especially the end part just before the time expires. The passage focuses on Asr prayer which has a partial time at the end before sunset.
For Asr, the partial time is from when the shadow of an object becomes twice its length until sunset.
3
Analyze the ruling for yesterday's missed Asr (qadā')
The cause of obligation (sabab al-wujūb) for a missed prayer is the entire time of that prayer. Since the cause was the complete time of yesterday, the qadā' must compensate with a perfect prayer performed in a complete time. Therefore, performing it in the partial time of today is not valid – it does not fulfill the obligation.
The logic: the cause (complete time) requires a complete performance.
4
Analyze the ruling for today's current Asr (adā')
The cause of obligation for today's Asr is the partial time itself (the time that has already entered). Since the cause is partial, the prayer can be performed in that partial time. Thus, if one delays until the partial time, the performance is valid as adā'.
This is the basis for the permissibility of delaying Asr to the partial time (though it is makruh tanzīhan).
5
Resolve the objection about prolonging prayer into partial time
An objection arises: if a person starts Asr at the beginning of its time (complete time) but prolongs it until sunset (partial time), the prayer was obligatory in complete time but performed partially in partial time. The answer: such a person is acting upon the 'azīmat (the more stringent ruling) of performing the prayer in the complete time. The slight dislike (karāhah) from ending in the partial time is forgiven because it is unavoidable when one follows the 'azīmat of completing the prayer with proper recitation and slow performance.
This shows that the 'azīmat is the preferred path, and minor drawbacks are excused.

In Hanafi usul, the interpretation of legal language begins with the literal meaning (haqiqah). However, when the literal meaning is either impossible to apply without undue hardship (muta'dhirah) or has been abandoned by custom (mahjurah), the principle directs us to resort to the figurative meaning (majaz). Analyzes waw as circumstantial in a legal example

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Waw in 'ad li alfan wa anta hurr' is haal not 'atf—making emancipation conditional on actual payment, not just the obligation.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, understanding the precise function of particles like waw is critical for deriving correct rulings. A seemingly small grammatical nuance can change the legal effect entirely. This passage examines a classic example: a master's statement to his slave 'Pay a thousand and you are free.' The waw here is not a mere conjunction but a circumstantial clause, with significant implications for when emancipation takes effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the grammatical structure
Locate the two clauses connected by waw in 'Ad li alfan wa anta hurr'. The first is an imperative (amr/insha), the second is a nominal sentence (khabar).

3
Identify the haal interpretation
Since 'atf is impossible, the waw must be haaliyyah, introducing a circumstantial clause describing the state of the agent. The clause 'wa anta hurr' describes the condition under which the command is to be fulfilled.
4
Derive the legal effect
From the haal interpretation, the slave's freedom is contingent upon actual payment of the thousand. The emancipation is not immediate but conditional on the act of paying.
The text says: 'he is not freed except with payment.'
5
Analyze the objection and response
Consider the objection that haal should refer to the obligation to pay rather than actual payment. Examine the two responses: (1) qalb (inversion) reverses the logical order, and (2) the haal clause substitutes for the answer of the imperative, making it a conditional result.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When a speaker says 'inda, he affirms custody, not ownership — the default is deposit, not debt.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, precise linguistic analysis of particles like 'inda carries significant legal weight. This lesson unpacks a key principle: the word 'inda (عند) literally denotes presence (حضرة), which implies safekeeping (حفظ) rather than obligation (لزوم). Therefore, when someone says, 'You have a thousand dirhams with me' (لك عندي ألف درهم), the default interpretation is that the money is a deposit (وديعة), not a debt. This ruling shapes how Hanafi jurists interpret financial declarations and distinguishes between trust and liability.

Presence implies custody (حفظ) without obligation (لزوم). The speaker acknowledges physical holding, but not a binding commitment to return or pay.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
In Arabic legal statements, the grammatical case of 'ghayr' determines whether it describes or excludes, changing liability.

This lesson examines how a single Arabic particle can alter legal obligations. The word 'ghayr' can function as an adjective or an exception, distinguished only by its grammatical case (i'rab). Derive the legal ruling
If adjective, the full amount is owed (e.g., a full dirham). If exception, the owed amount is reduced by the excluded part (e.g., a dirham minus a daniq).
This ruling is based on the literal meaning of the speaker's words; no external evidence overrides the i'rab.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Noor al-Anwar is a classic example of how Hanafi usul scholars defined and differentiated technical terms. The author of the matn had used a phrase that could be interpreted in two ways, and the commentator resolves it by offering two tawjih (interpretations). The first interpretation is that the author was defining the muqtada itself (the thing demanded by the text to make the ruling valid), not the resulting ruling. The second interpretation is that the author was defining the ruling derived from the muqtada. The commentator also clarifies a key distinction between muqtada and mahdhoof: if you explicitly mention the muqtada (the thing implied by the text), the sentence remains grammatically and semantically coherent; but if you bring out the mahdhoof (the omitted word), the sentence changes both in structure and meaning. The example given for muqtada is the conditional oath: 'If I eat, my slave is free' — the muqtada is 'food' (since eating requires an object), and making it explicit does not alter the ruling. For mahdhoof, the Qur'anic verse 'Ask the village' (12:82) is explained: the original phrase 'Ask the people of the village' changes the object from the village to its inhabitants, showing a clear semantic shift.

Key Terms
اقتضاء (iqtida'): The act of demanding or requiring; a verbal noun used in the sense of requiring something for the correctness of the text.
مقتضی (muqtada): The thing that is demanded (by the text) to validate the mentioned ruling; the implied element that does not change the text when made explicit.
محذوف (mahdhoof): An omitted word that, if made explicit, changes the grammatical structure and/or meaning of the text.

Alternative Expression of the Difference on Conditional Sentences
Quoting Sheikh Ahmad Jeevan on alternative expression of the dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on conditional sentences (jumla shartiyya) and evaluate the attribution of a view to Arabic linguists.

Core Idea
In a conditional sentence, the ruling applies to both condition and result, not just the result.

Previously, the difference between Hanafis and Shafi'is regarding conditional sentences was discussed. Here, Sheikh Ahmad Jeevan presents an alternative way of expressing that difference and refutes a false attribution to Arabic linguists. By analyzing this passage, you will understand the precise points of contention and the linguistic foundations of the disagreement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
The Shafi'i position according to the alternative expression: the ruling is only in the result (jaza). The Hanafi position: the ruling applies to both condition and result.
2
Understand the role of the condition
In the Shafi'i view as presented, the condition is like an adverb or state; in the Hanafi view, the condition is an integral part of the conditional sentence.
3
Examine the attribution
Sheikh Ahmad Jeevan denies that Arabic linguists hold the Shafi'i view; he says this is a fabrication and attributes it only to the author of al-Miftah.
4
Determine the Hanafi basis
The Hanafis rely on the fact that the conditional sentence (jumla shartiyya) is a composite of condition and result, so the ruling covers both.

The Rule of Lengthening Speech for Brevity
Explanation of a linguistic rule for shortening speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the Hanafi usul principle of kathrat al-kalām li-ikhtiṣārihi using the given example.

Core Idea
In Arabic usul, lengthening an expression can actually shorten the meaning.

This lesson introduces a unique linguistic principle used in Hanafi usul: sometimes a longer phrase conveys a shorter intended meaning. The example given shows that saying 'one hundred and a dirham' is more concise than 'one hundred dirhams and one dirham' for the total of one hundred and one. Understanding this rule helps in interpreting legal texts where such constructions appear.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Identify the rule: 'The necessity of verbosity (or lengthening speech) for brevity.' This means that adding extra words can actually make the intended meaning more concise.
2
Parse the example
Examine the example: 'He owes me one hundred and a dirham' (له على مائة و درهم). What is the intended meaning? The longer expression 'one hundred dirhams and one dirham' is actually longer than 'one hundred and a dirham'.
In Arabic, the phrase 'مائة و درهم' literally means 'one hundred and a dirham', but it is understood as 'one hundred and one dirhams'.
3
Recognize the mechanism
The lengthening (adding the waw 'and' and the word 'dirham') allows the speaker to avoid saying the full 'one hundred dirhams and one dirham'. The apparent verbosity is a concise expression for the intended number.
This is a stylistic device recognized in Arabic usul for efficient communication.
