Complete Power (Qudrah Kamilah) and its Condition for Continuity
Defining complete power and its role in sustaining obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qudrah kamilah and explain why its continuity is a condition for ongoing obligations.

Core Idea
Complete power must persist for the obligation to persist.

In Hanafi usul, not all power is equal. While minimal power (qudrah mutlaqah) may suffice for a one-off act, a higher degree of power—qudrah kamilah—is required for obligations that extend over time. This lesson anchors that distinction and its legal consequence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its definition
The passage defines qudrah kamilah as 'the power that facilitates performance'. Note that it is more than mere ability.
2
Locate the legal condition
The passage states that the continuity of this power is a condition for the continuity of the obligation. Understand that this applies to ongoing acts.
3
Apply to a concrete example
For an obligation like fasting the entire day, one must have the power to fast the whole day, not just the beginning. If power ceases (e.g., becomes ill), the obligation may be excused.
4
Distinguish from qudrah mutlaqah
Qudrah mutlaqah is the bare minimum to initiate an act; qudrah kamilah is sustained ability to complete a continuing act.

قوله : وكامل وهو القدرة الميسرة للاداء ودوام هذه القدرة شرط لدوام الواجب .

Commentary
This brief passage introduces the second type of qudrah: qudrah kamilah (complete power). While qudrah mutlaqah is the minimal power sufficient for performing a single act, qudrah kamilah is a higher degree that makes performance easy. The key ruling is that the continuity (dawam) of this complete power is a condition for the continuity of the obligation (dawam al-wajib). In other words, if an obligation is ongoing (e.g., maintaining a state of purity for an extended prayer, or fasting a whole day), one must have the ability to sustain that act throughout its duration. If the power ceases, the obligation may be lifted or become excused. This is particularly relevant for Hanafi rulings on obligations that extend over time, such as the ability to fast the entire day or to perform Hajj with continued health and means.

Key Terms
قدرة كاملة (qudrah kamilah): The power that facilitates performance (al-muyassira lil-ada'), beyond the minimal ability.

Key Takeaway

Address of Disbelievers with Acts of Worship
Discusses two types of worship and which address disbelievers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of worship according to Hanafis and explain why disbelievers are not addressed with furu'.

Core Idea
Disbelievers are obligated to believe but not to perform acts that can lapse due to incapacity.

The Hanafi school distinguishes between acts of worship that can fall away due to excuses and those that never lapse. This distinction determines whether disbelievers are addressed with these acts. In this lesson, we will unpack this classification and understand the Hanafi position on the address (khitab) of disbelievers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Categories
Note the two types of worship: those that can lapse (e.g., salah, sawm) and those that never lapse (e.g., iman).
The Arabic term for falling away is suqut.
2
Understand 'Saqit'
An obligation is saqit when it drops due to inability or excuse. Only acts that are not prerequisite for others can lapse.
Iman never lapses because it is foundational.
3
State the Hanafi Position
Disbelievers are not obligated to perform the first type because they lack the capacity (iman) required for validity. They are obligated only for the second type.
They are mukhattab with usul but not furu'.
4
Contrast with Shafi'i View
The Shafi'i school holds that disbelievers are addressed with both usul and furu'. Understand the thamarah (practical difference).
The difference affects what a disbeliever is punished for.

Source Text

بیماری کی وجہ سے ساقط ہو جاتی ہے۔ (۲) وہ عبادات ہیں جو کسی صورت میں ساقط ہونے کا احتمال نہیں رکھتی ہیں جیسے ایمان پس احناف کے نزدیک کفار پہلی قسم کی عبادت کے مخاطب نہیں لیکن دوسری قسم کے مخاطب ہیں یعنی وہ نماز ، روزہ وغیرہ کی ادائیگی کے مخاطب نہیں لیکن تو حید و رسالت پر ایمان لانا ان پر بھی لازم ہے اور اگر وہ ایمان نہیں لاتے تو ان سے مواخذہ ہوگا۔
(1) Those [acts of worship] that fall away due to illness. (2) Those acts of worship that cannot possibly fall away in any circumstance, like iman. So according to the Hanafis, disbelievers are not addressed with the first type of worship, but they are addressed with the second type. That is, they are not addressed with performing prayers, fasting, etc., but it is obligatory upon them to have iman in tawhid and risala, and if they do not have iman, they will be held accountable.
Source pages p. 115

Commentary
This passage explains a key principle in usul al-fiqh regarding the address (khitab) of disbelievers with the branches of sharia (furu'). The author divides acts of worship into two types: those that can lapse due to inability (like prayer, fasting—which drop due to illness, travel, etc.) and those that never lapse, such as iman (faith). The Hanafi position is that disbelievers are not obligated to perform the first type (e.g., salah, sawm) because such acts are contingent on capacity and they lack the prerequisite of iman. However, they are obligated to believe in the oneness of God and prophethood; if they disbelieve, they are punished for that. This reflects the Hanafi view that kuffar are mukhattab (addressed) with the usul (fundamentals) but not with the furu' (branches), unlike the Shafi'i view that they are addressed with both.

Key Terms
مخاطب (mukhattab): One who is addressed by a legal command or prohibition
ساقط (saqit): An obligation that falls away due to an excuse or inability
