The Effect of Halala on the Number of Remaining Divorces: A Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'i on number of divorces after halala

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principles of isharat al-nass and istihsan to resolve a dispute between the apparent meaning of a Quranic verse and a hadith, and derive the ruling on the number of divorces after halala.

Core Idea
After halala, the Hanafi husband regains full three divorces using the hadith's implicit indication (isharat al-nass) over the verse's apparent meaning.

This lesson examines a classic usul al-fiqh dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools on whether halala restores the first husband's right to three divorces even if he had given fewer before. The Hanafi position, held by Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, that the husband gets three divorces, relies on the hadith of Umm Rifa'a and the principles of isharat al-nass and istihsan. By the end of this lesson, you will understand how the Hanafi school derives a ruling that may appear to contradict the apparent meaning of the Quranic verse, using a stronger textual indication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
State the factual scenario: a man divorces his wife with less than three pronouncements, she undergoes halala with another man and then remarries the first husband. What is the dispute? Hanafi (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf): husband gets three divorces. Shafi'i (and Imam Muhammad): only remaining number.
Consensus exists when three divorces were given originally.
2
Examine the opposing school's primary proof
Imam Shafi'i cites Quran 2:230: 'hatta tankaha' meaning 'until she marries another husband'. He argues the verse only removes the prohibition, not creating a fresh halal with new divorces. Therefore, only the remaining number of divorces apply.
The apparent meaning (zahir) of the verse is that the prohibition ends, but no new rights are granted.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response using the hadith
Imam Abu Hanifa does not rely on the verse for the fresh legality. Instead, he uses the hadith of Umm Rifa'a where the Prophet said 'an ta'udi' (if you wish to return). 'Return' implies going back to the original state, which included the husband's authority over three divorces.
The hadith is ahad but accepted by both schools for the condition of a guardian.
4
Apply the usul tool: isharat al-nass
The Hanafi school extracts from the word 'return' an implicit indication (isharat al-nass) that the original marriage is restored in full, including the right to three divorces. This is because 'aud' means returning to the previous state, which had that right.
This is a method of deriving rulings from the text's implication, not its explicit statement.
5
Understand the role of istihsan
The Hanafi school uses istihsan (juristic preference) to prefer the hadith's implicit indication over the apparent meaning of the Quranic verse, because the hadith provides a stronger evidence for the fresh halal.
Istihsan allows departing from a weak analogy or apparent meaning in favor of a stronger textual indication.

The issue is that if a person gives his wife three divorces and she, after performing halala, remarries the first husband, then by consensus he will own three divorces. But if the first husband gave one or two divorces and after halala remarriage occurs, then according to Imam Muhammad and Imam Shafi'i, from the three divorces only those that remain, the first husband will have authority over only them. And according to Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf, he will have authority over three divorces. Because the second husband made the woman lawful anew for the first husband, i.e., made her lawful with a new legality. So the divorces that occurred in the first marriage will have no effect. After introductory words, now we come to the actual objection: Imam Shafi'i objected that regarding halala, the verse says: 'If he divorces her, she is not lawful for him thereafter until she marries another husband.' In this, the word 'hatta' is specific with the meaning of limit and end, i.e., from this it is understood that with marriage to a second husband, the previous prohibition ends. But from this it does not occur to me that the woman will be lawful for the first husband with a new legality. So when the limited case (mugayya) occurs, i.e., three divorces, the second husband cannot be a hallal by consensus for the first husband. So where the limited case does not occur, how can he be a hallal? The Hanafi response to this objection: The author gave this response from Imam Abu Hanifa: We do not prove the second husband being a hallal from 'hatta tankaha' but we prove it from the hadith of 'Usaila'. This hadith is famous, and Imam Shafi'i also proves from it the condition of a guardian for halala. So we said that the indication of this hadith proves from it the condition of a guardian, similarly from its 'isharat al-nass' it proves that the second husband is a hallal, because the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) did not say to the wife of Rifa'a: 'If you wish, your prohibition from the first husband ends', but rather 'if you wish to return (an ta'udi)', i.e., if you wish to go back to the first husband. And the meaning of 'aud' is returning to the previous state, and in the previous state the first husband had the authority of three divorces, so now also it will be proved. In short, when from this text the permissibility is proved in the case where no permissibility exists (because Rifa'a gave his wife three divorces, and after irrevocable divorce no permissibility remains), then permissibility will be established a fortiori in the case where permissibility exists in an imperfect way, because here full three divorces did not occur.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of a fiqh dispute about the effect of halala on the number of divorces remaining to the first husband. The text first states the agreed ruling: if a man divorces his wife with three divorces, and she marries another man and then separates, upon remarrying the first husband, he regains control of three divorces by consensus. The dispute arises when the first divorce was less than three. Imam Muhammad and Imam Shafi'i hold that only the remaining divorces are valid, so if he had given one divorce, he only has two left. Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf hold that he gains full control over three divorces because the second marriage created a fresh lawful relationship. Imam Shafi'i argues from the Quranic verse using the word 'hatta' which indicates only the removal of the prohibition, not a new complete permissibility. The Hanafi response, attributed to Imam Abu Hanifa, is that they derive the ruling not from the verse but from the hadith of Umm Rifa'a. In that hadith, the Prophet did not say to her 'your prohibition ends' but rather 'if you wish to return', implying a full return to the original marriage state, which included the authority over three divorces. This is an example of istihsan (juristic preference) over the apparent meaning of the Quran, and illustrates the Hanafi use of ahad hadith with a stronger indication.

Key Terms
حلالہ (halala): The act of a divorced woman marrying another man and then separating so that she becomes lawful for her first husband.
استحسان (istihsan): Juristic preference that allows departing from the apparent meaning of a text in favor of a stronger evidence or maslaha.
اشارۃ النص (isharat al-nass): The implicit indication of a text that leads to a ruling not explicitly stated.

Dispute on Obligation from Prophet's Action
Presenting the Hanafi view that the Prophet's actions do not imply obligation, and responding to Shafi'i evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on fi'l and wujub, state the evidence, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
The Prophet's actions do not automatically imply obligation; only accompanied commands or specific context do.

The Hanafi school holds that the actions of the Prophet (fi'l) are not inherently obligating. This lesson examines the dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i, who argues that actions are like commands. Through two incidents, the Hanafis demonstrate that mere action is not wajib.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Recall that Hanafis say fi'l does not indicate wujub unless accompanied by a command (amr).
2
Identify the Hanafi evidences
List the two incidents: the Prophet forbidding continuous fasting (wisal) and his explanation for removing sandals in prayer.
Both show that the Prophet prevented imitation, proving his actions are not obligatory.
3
Identify the Shafi'i evidence
Imam al-Shafi'i uses the hadith 'Pray as you see me praying' and the missed prayers at Khandaq.
4
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
Consider a practical scenario: if a person sees the Prophet eating with his right hand, is that wajib? Hanafis say no (unless there is a command); Shafi'is say yes.
The difference affects daily fiqh: following prophetic actions counts as mustahabb for Hanafis, but wajib for Shafi'is.

Naskh (Abrogation) in the Hadith on Congregational Prayer
Applying naskh to reconcile hadith on jama'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the usul principle of naskh to reconcile two apparently contradictory hadiths on the minimum number for congregational prayer and state the resulting Hanafi ruling.

Core Idea
The later hadith 'two and above constitute a congregation' abrogates the earlier 'one is a devil, two are devils.'

In the early period of Islam, travel restrictions made it difficult to gather for prayer, so the Prophet ﷺ discouraged small congregations. Later, when Islam strengthened, these restrictions were lifted, and the Prophet ﷺ explicitly stated that two persons form a valid jama'ah. The author of Nur al-Anwar uses this historical shift to resolve an apparent contradiction. This lesson examines the usul mechanism of naskh (abrogation) as applied to these texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting hadiths
First, locate the two Prophetic statements in the passage: (1) 'One is a devil, two are devils' – a prohibition of two-person congregation; (2) 'Two and above constitute a congregation' – a permission. Recognize these as the texts that need reconciliation.
The prohibition hadith is mentioned indirectly; the permission hadith is quoted in Arabic: الاثنان فما فوقها جماعة.
2
Establish the chronological order
The passage states that the prohibition belonged to the early period of Islam when travel of two kos was not permitted. Later, when Islam became strong, permission was granted. Thus, the prohibition came first, and the permission later. This temporal sequence is essential for naskh.
The concept of 'travel of two kos' indicates a short distance – the early community was weak and vulnerable.
3
Apply the principle of naskh
According to Hanafi usul, a later, clear text (nasikh) abrogates an earlier, conflicting text (mansukh) if reconciliation is impossible. Here, the earlier prohibition categorically forbids two-person jama'ah, and the later hadith explicitly permits it. Since both cannot be applied simultaneously, the later hadith abrogates the earlier.
Naskh requires certainty that the later text is indeed later and that the earlier is not a general rule later specified (takhsis). The historical context confirms naskh.
4
Derive the operative ruling
With the abrogation established, the operative ruling is that two persons (including the imam) constitute a valid congregation for all prayers except Jumu'ah, for which the consensus-based condition of three men besides the imam (total four) remains. Note the intra-Hanafi difference from Imam Abu Yusuf who requires only three total.
The passage mentions that for Jumu'ah, three besides the imam are necessary, but Abu Yusuf holds three including the imam suffice.
5
Recognize the usul lesson
This case illustrates how naskh resolves apparent contradictions in the Sunnah. It also shows that the ability to identify nasikh and mansukh depends on knowledge of history (asbab al-nuzul/wurud) and careful textual analysis.
Hanafi usul gives special attention to the timing of hadith and the conditions of their utterance.

Definition and Components of Sunnah in Hanafi Usul
Definition of Sunnah with its four components and brief preview of four divisions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of Sunnah, identify its four components (qawl, fi'l, sifah, taqrir), and explain why taqrir is a valid proof.

Core Idea
Sunnah is more than sayings: it includes actions, attributes, and silent approvals, all binding proofs.

The author now moves from the Quran to the Sunnah, the second primary source. In this passage, he defines Sunnah technically, distinguishes its general and specific meanings, and outlines the four components that constitute Prophetic tradition. Understanding these foundational elements is essential for evaluating hadith and deriving rulings in Hanafi fiqh.

Commentary
This passage introduces the concept of Sunnah in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author begins by defining Sunnah both linguistically and terminologically. He clarifies that in the Shariah, Sunnah primarily refers to the Prophet's sayings, actions, and tacit approvals (taqrir). He then gives a broader definition used by some, which includes the practices of the Companions and Followers, citing a hadith about clinging to the Sunnah of the Prophet and the Rightly Guided Caliphs. However, the commentator notes that the author intends the specific Prophetic meaning. Subsequently, the passage presents the definition of Sunnah by Hafiz Ibn Hajar, breaking it into four components: qawl (saying), fi'l (action), sifah (attribute, e.g., physical and moral traits), and taqrir (approval). تقرير (taqrir): The Prophet's tacit approval of a companion's action done in his presence or knowledge, indicating permissibility.
اتصال (ittisal): In the first division: the quality of the chain of transmission being connected (e.g., muttasil hadith).

Definition of Sunnah and Its Four Divisions
Defining Sunnah as qawl, fi'l, taqrir and introducing four divisions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Sunnah as qawl, fi'l, and taqrir, and list its four divisions according to Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Sunnah is the Prophet's words, actions, and approvals — and it has four divisions distinct from hadith terminology.

This lesson introduces the foundational concept of Sunnah in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. You will learn that Sunnah manifests in three forms: the Prophet's statements (qawl), his actions (fi'l), and his tacit approvals (taqrir). Importantly, taqrir is a valid legal proof because the Prophet's silence on an action indicates its permissibility. The passage then outlines four divisions of Sunnah that are specific to the methodology of fiqh, not hadith terminology, setting the stage for deeper analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components of Sunnah
Read the passage and extract the three categories: qawl (sayings), fi'l (actions), and taqrir (tacit approvals). Understand each definition.
Taqrir is especially important as a proof in Hanafi usul.
2
Understand why taqrir is a shar'i proof
The author argues that it is impossible for the Lawgiver (the Prophet) to remain silent upon seeing an impermissible act. Thus, his silence indicates permissibility (or at least non-prohibition). This is the logical basis (talil) for accepting taqrir as evidence.
This argument is specific to Hanafi usul; other schools may have different criteria.
3
Distinguish from hadith terminology
The passage emphasizes that the four divisions are according to usul al-fiqh, not mustalah al-hadith. Note that terms like khass, 'amm, amr, nahy, etc., apply to both Quran and Sunnah, but here only divisions unique to Sunnah are discussed.
This distinction prevents confusion when studying later chapters.
4
List the four divisions
From the text, identify: 1) mode of connection (ittisal), 2) mode of discontinuity (inqita'), 3) locus of the report (mahall al-khabar), and 4) the report itself (nafs al-khabar). These subdivisions will be elaborated in subsequent lessons.
Each division governs how the Sunnah is transmitted or understood.

The Three Types of Khabar: Mutawatir, Mashhur, Wahid
Explaining the threefold division of khabar based on quality of connection with wajh al-hasr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three types of khabar (mutawatir, mashhur, wahid) and apply the Hanafi criterion of quality of connection (wajh al-hasr) to classify any given hadith report.

Core Idea
Perfect connection yields mutawatir; deficiency in form only yields mashhur; deficiency in both form and meaning yields wahid.

This lesson unpacks the first division of the Sunnah according to Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It focuses on the quality of a hadith's connection (ittisal) to the Prophet (peace be upon him) as the basis for classifying reports into three categories. Understanding this classification is essential for determining the epistemological weight and legal authority of hadith proofs.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of connection (ittisal)
Recognize that every hadith is transmitted through a chain of narrators. The quality of this chain from the Prophet to us is what we analyze. A perfect connection means a large, uncountable number of narrators in every generation.
This is the standard for mutawatir.
2
Apply wajh al-hasr: perfect vs. imperfect
The author uses wajh al-hasr (logical limitation) to argue that any hadith's connection is either perfect (mutawatir) or imperfect. If imperfect, it must be deficient either in form alone or in both form and meaning.
Form refers to the number of narrators in the first generation (Companions); meaning refers to fame (shuhrah) across the first three generations.
3
Distinguish deficiency in form vs. meaning
If the hadith is not mutawatir in the Companion generation but becomes widely narrated later, it is deficient in form only → mashhur. If it did not become well-known even in the later generations (Companions, Successors, Followers), it is deficient in both form and meaning → wahid.
This is the precise Hanafi definition of wahid, which is narrower than the general 'ahad' category.
4
Identify the three categories and definitions
Mutawatir: perfect connection, yields certain knowledge. Mashhur: deficient in form only (first generation), yields near-certainty. Wahid: deficient in both form and meaning across three generations, yields zann.
Memorize these definitions with their respective epistemological weights.
5
Apply the classification to example hadith
Practice by taking a known hadith, counting narrators in each generation, and classifying it according to the criteria. This step solidifies the abstract rules.
Use the hadith 'Actions are judged by intentions' for mashhur, or 'The rewards of deeds depend upon intentions' for mutawatir (if applicable).

The Threefold Division of Khabar and Definition of Mutawatir
Explaining the classification of ahadith based on continuity of transmission and defining mutawatir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a transmitted report (khabar) as mutawatir, mashhur, or ahad based on the quality of its chain (ittisal), and state the definition and conditions of khabar mutawatir.

Core Idea
A report is mutawatir if its narrators are so numerous that collusion on a lie is impossible, yielding certain knowledge.

The strength of a hadith in Hanafi usul depends on the continuity of its transmission from the Prophet ﷺ. This lesson introduces the foundational classification of reports into three categories: mutawatir, mashhur, and ahad. You will learn how to distinguish them based on the degree of connection and understand what makes a report mutawatir, including the conditions that guarantee its truthfulness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the basis of classification
Understand that the division is based on the completeness of the chain (ittisal) from us to the Prophet. The chain is either complete (mutawatir) or incomplete. If incomplete, the doubt is either only in form (first generation) or in both form and meaning (first three generations).
2
Differentiate between the three types
Use the two criteria: (a) complete transmission? If yes, mutawatir. If no, (b) is the doubt only in form (i.e., the report was not numerous among Companions but later became widespread)? If yes, mashhur. If the report was not widespread in any of the first three generations, it is ahad.
3
Define khabar mutawatir
Memorize the definition: a report narrated by a group so large that collusion on a lie is impossible. This is the key feature that gives certain (qat'i) knowledge.
4
List the conditions for mutawatir
From the text: the narrators must be numerous, from diverse locations, upright (adalah), and free from accusation. These conditions ensure that collusion is impossible.
5
Note the ikhtilaf with muhaddithin
The general muhaddithin do not require diversity of location or uprightness for tawatur; they consider a large number sufficient. This affects which reports are considered mutawatir.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed explanation of the first division of khabar (report/transmission) in usul al-hadith, which pertains to the quality of its connection (ittisāl) to the Prophet (ﷺ). The author begins by stating that this division examines whether a hadith is connected, and if so, to what degree. He then enumerates the three categories: mutawātir (successive), mashhūr (well-known), and wāḥid (solitary).

The reasoning for this specific tripartite classification (wajh al-ḥaṣr) is as follows: The transmission chain is either completely continuous or not. If it is complete, meaning that at every generation from the Companions down to us there was a large number of narrators, then it is mutawātir. If it is not completely continuous, then the deficiency is either in form (ṣūratan) only—meaning that in the first generation (the Companions) it was not reported by a large number, even if later generations had many narrators—or in both form and meaning (ṣūratan wa maʿnan), meaning that in all three early generations (Companions, Followers, and Followers of the Followers) it did not achieve widespread transmission. The former (doubt in form only) is called mashhūr, and the latter (doubt in both) is called wāḥid.

The passage then focuses on khabar mutawātir, providing its definition: a report narrated by a group so numerous that their collusion upon a lie is inconceivable. The commentator expands on the conditions that preclude collusion: the narrators must be many, from different regions, and upright (ʿādil). Uprightness is defined as avoiding major sins, abstaining from minor sins and acts contrary to chivalry (muruwwa), such as eating while walking or bathing naked. However, the standard of muruwwa changes with time; in later periods, bathing naked in a washroom with privacy does not affect integrity.

The passage then highlights an ikhtilāf (disagreement) between the general muhaddithīn and Mulla Jion. The muhaddithīn do not require the last two conditions (diverse locations and uprightness); they hold that if a large number narrate a report, even if they are all from one area or are sinful, it is still mutawātir. Mulla Jion, however, insists that these conditions are necessary but does not fix a specific minimum number; any number that makes collusion impossible suffices.

In summary, this passage establishes the foundational classification of transmitted reports in Hanafi usul and clarifies the definition and conditions of mutawātir transmission, which yields certain knowledge.

Khabar Mash'hur: Definition, Ruling, and Status
Defines Khabar Mash'hur, its epistemic status, and the ruling on its denier.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi category of Khabar Mash'hur, explain its epistemic effect ('ilm al-tuma'nina), and distinguish the majority view on its denier from the stricter view of Imam al-Jassas.

Core Idea
Khabar Mash'hur is a middle category of hadith that produces tranquility of heart; its denier is a transgressor but not an unbeliever.

This lesson examines the Hanafi usul concept of Khabar Mash'hur (well-known report). You will learn how it fits into the threefold classification of reports, what legal effect it has, and the differing opinions on the status of one who rejects it. Understanding this category is crucial for grasping how Hanafi jurists derive rulings from the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of khabar in Hanafi usul
Recall that Hanafi usul divides reports into Mutawatir (mass-transmitted), Mash'hur (well-known), and Ahad (solitary). This lesson focuses on the middle category.
The Shafi'i school does not recognize Mash'hur as a separate category; they classify all non-mutawatir as ahad.
2
Understand the epistemic effect of Khabar Mash'hur
The passage states that Khabar Mash'hur obligates 'ilm al-tuma'nina — knowledge accompanied by tranquility. This is a level of certainty below Mutawatir but above Ahad, due to the prevalence of truthfulness in its transmission.
This means that a Khabar Mash'hur produces confidence for action but not absolute certainty required for creedal matters.
3
Compare the status of Khabar Mash'hur to other categories
The passage explicitly states that Khabar Mash'hur is lower than Mutawatir and higher than Khabar Wahid. This hierarchy determines how each category is used in legal derivation.
For example, Mutawatir can abrogate the Quran, while Khabar Mash'hur cannot; it can only add to the Quran in terms of explanation.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on the denier of Khabar Mash'hur
The majority (including the classical Hanafi scholars) hold that denying Khabar Mash'hur makes one a fasiq and misguided but not a kafir. However, Imam al-Jassas equates its denial with that of Mutawatir, leading to excommunication. This ikhtilaf affects how severely the rejection is treated.
The difference stems from whether Khabar Mash'hur is considered to produce certain knowledge (qat'i) or only predominant speculation (ghalabat al-zann).

The Authority of Khabar Wahid: Four Sunnah Examples and the Objection Answered
Establishing the authority of khabar wahid using four examples from the Sunnah and answering an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the four examples from the Sunnah establish the authority of khabar wahid and evaluate the Hanafi response to the circularity objection using the concept of Ummah's acceptance.

Core Idea
The Ummah's acceptance of ahad reports elevates them to certainty, answering the objection that proving ahad by ahad is circular.

The Hanafi school holds that khabar wahid (single-narrator report) is a binding proof in Islamic law. In this passage, the commentator provides four concrete examples from the life of the Prophet ﷺ where he acted upon a single report. But a key objection arises: these examples themselves are ahad reports, so how can they prove the principle? The commentator's answer, rooted in the Ummah's collective acceptance, is a distinctive feature of Hanafi usul. This lesson will walk you through the examples, the objection, and the Hanafi rebuttal, sharpening your ability to derive usul principles from prophetic practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four examples
List each of the four incidents: Barirah’s report about charity meat, Salman’s report about medicine, sending individual companions as judges, and sending Dihyah al-Kalbi as a single messenger. For each, identify how the Prophet accepted a single report.
The examples are not quoted verbatim but summarized by the commentator.
2
Extract the method of istinbat
For each example, ask: what action of the Prophet indicates acceptance of a single report? For Barirah, he accepted her statement that the meat was charity. For the judges, he made their rulings binding on the community. This shows that khabar wahid is actionable.
Focus on the Prophet's practice (fi'l) as the dalil.
3
Formulate the objection
State the objection clearly: All the hadiths used as proofs are themselves ahad (single reports). Therefore, proving the authority of khabar wahid using ahad reports is circular.
The objection challenges the epistemological foundation of the proof.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The commentator admits the reports are ahad but argues that the Ummah has accepted them with full approval. This widespread acceptance transforms them into effectively mutawatir (mass-transmitted) in terms of acceptance, breaking the circularity.
This is a unique Hanafi argument: ijma' on the acceptance of these reports gives them certainty.
5
Evaluate the epistemological weight
Consider: does the Ummah's acceptance truly remove the circularity? Hanafis say yes, because the collective acceptance creates certainty (qat'). Other schools may require tawatur in transmission itself. Note the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference): Hanafis accept khabar wahid in matters of belief and practice; others restrict its use.
This step links the usul dispute to real-world fiqh applications.

Hujjiyyah of Khabar Wahid: The Ijma Proof and Refutation of the Counter-Argument
Explaining the proofs for khabar wahid's authority and refuting a counter-argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that khabar wahid necessitates action (yujibu al-'amal) by identifying the ijma proof and refuting the opposing argument based on the verse 'wa la taqfu'.

Core Idea
Khabar wahid is authoritative for action due to ijma, even though it does not produce certain knowledge.

This lesson examines a key usul dispute: does a single-narrator report obligate action? The Hanafi school, following the jumhur, holds that it does, based on ijma of the ummah. We will analyze the proofs and the counter-opinion that demands certainty, culminating in the Hanafi refutation that preserves the practical authority of khabar wahid.

Guided Path
1
Understand the nature of khabar wahid
Define khabar wahid as a report transmitted by one or a few narrators not reaching tawatur. Note its epistemological status: it produces zann (preponderant conjecture), not yaqin (certainty).
This distinction is crucial for the dispute.
2
Identify the primary Hanafi proof: ijma
Recognize that the main proof for the authority of khabar wahid is the consensus (ijma) of the Companions and the ummah. The commentator provides examples to demonstrate this ijma.
Ijma is a binding source in Hanafi usul.
3
Examine the examples of ijma
Work through the three examples: (a) Companions accepting a single hadith to settle disputes, (b) Abu Bakr's hadith at Saqifah Bani Sa'idah accepted by all, (c) universal practice on water purity and qiblah direction.
The first two are specific to the Companions, the third is a general ongoing ijma.
4
Analyze the counter-opinion
Understand the opposing view that khabar wahid does not obligate action because it lacks 'ilm (certain knowledge). They cite the Quranic verse 'wa la taqfu ma laysa laka bihi 'ilm' (17:36) and argue that action (malzum) requires knowledge (lazim).
This is a logical argument, but it ignores context.
5
Apply the Hanafi refutation
The Hanafi response: the verse is about false testimony, not about accepting reports from reliable narrators. Moreover, the ijma of the ummah overrides this analogy. Khabar wahid creates sufficient zann for obligation in practical matters.
Ijma is a stronger proof than a general logical principle.

Core Idea
The narrator's status decides whether his khabar wahid overrides qiyas — only jurist-narrators' reports are automatically preferred.

In Hanafi usul, not all khabar wahid is treated equally. The narrator's reliability and legal acumen are decisive. This lesson introduces the three-tier classification of narrators and the rule that narrations from those known for both fiqh and justice take precedence over qiyas—a principle that distinguishes the Hanafi school from Imam Malik's approach.

If the narrator is ma'ruf bil-fiqh wal-'adalah, his khabar wahid is given precedence over qiyas. If only ma'ruf bil-'adalah, the narration must be presented to qiyas; it is rejected if contradictory.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four categories of the Prophet's actions and apply the Hanafi rule that actions of unknown intention default to mubah.

Core Idea
The Prophet's actions must be followed per his intention; when unknown, default to mubah.

This passage presents the core Hanafi principle for understanding the sunnah fi'liyyah: the Prophet's actions are not all equal in obligation. They range from fard to mubah depending on the intention (jihah) with which he performed them. When that intention is unknown, we assign the lowest level—mubah—because it is impossible that he did something disliked or forbidden. The author asserts that we must follow the Prophet's actions with the same intention he had. This means the ruling of an action for us is tied to the evident purpose (jihah) behind the action.

When the intention is not known, the default is mubah. The proof: it is impossible the Prophet acted on makruh or haram. Therefore, the action must be permissible until proven otherwise.

Types of Sunnah Fi'liyyah and the Rule of Following
Classification and rule for following Prophetic actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four legal categories of the Prophet's actions, apply the rule of following according to his intention (jihah), and evaluate the Hanafi position on actions of unknown jihah.

Core Idea
Follow the Prophet's actions with his intention; if intention is unknown, treat them as permissible.

The Prophet's actions carry legal weight, but not all are obligatory. This passage clarifies that we must follow each action according to the Prophet's own intention—whether he performed it as obligatory, recommended, or merely permissible. When the intention is unknown, the Hanafi school defaults to permissibility, a principle that balances emulation and ease.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
List the four types of Sunnah Fi'liyyah from the passage: Mubah, Mustahab, Wajib, Fard. Understand that these correspond to levels of legal obligation.
These categories are based on the ruling (hukm) of the act, not on its form.
2
Understand the jihah principle
The passage states that we must follow the Prophet's actions with the same intention (jihah) he performed them. For example, if he performed an act as Wajib, we must do it as Wajib; if as Mustahab, as Mustahab.
Jihah refers to the legal categorization intended by the Prophet.
3
Apply the default rule for unknown jihah
If the jihah is not known, the passage says we place the action on the lowest level—permissibility (ibahah). This is because it is impossible that the Prophet performed a Makruh or Haram action.
The rational evidence is that the Prophet's actions are immune from impermissible acts.
4
Analyze the scholarly disagreement
The passage presents three opinions on actions done forgetfully or naturally (e.g., sleeping posture): (1) tawaqquf (stop and not follow), (2) follow unless prohibited, (3) follow as permissible. The Hanafi preferred view (Imam al-Karkhi and the author) is permissibility.
The thamarah al-khilaf: practical difference in whether one can do an action like lying on the right side without considering it Sunnah.

Chapter: Explanation of the types of Sunnah Fi'liyyah. Sunnah Fi'liyyah, i.e., the blessed actions of the Prophet (peace be upon him), according to legal ruling are of four types, which are as follows: (1) Permissible (Mubah), (2) Recommended (Mustahab), (3) Obligatory (Wajib), (4) Compulsory (Fard). Ruling regarding the mentioned types: The author, may Allah have mercy on him, states: The rulings upon which the Prophet (peace be upon him) acted from a particular direction (jihah), we are also bound to act upon them from that same direction. And regarding actions whose direction is not known, we will act upon them by placing them on the lowest level (permissibility). Evidence: Because it is impossible that the Prophet (peace be upon him) acted upon something disliked or prohibited. A brief overview of scholars' disagreement: Actions that occurred from the Prophet (peace be upon him) forgetfully or naturally, and there is no clear evidence that they were specific to him regarding their attainment, there are different opinions among scholars. First opinion: Until their following is known, to stop (tawaqquf) is obligatory. Second opinion: Until evidence of prohibition arrives, following them is obligatory. Third opinion: According to Imam al-Karkhi and the author, may Allah have mercy on them, they are to be acted upon by way of permissibility (ibahah).

Commentary
This passage introduces the types of Sunnah Fi'liyyah (actions of the Prophet) and the ruling on following them. The author first categorizes prophetic actions into four legal categories: Mubah (permissible), Mustahab (recommended), Wajib (obligatory), and Fard (compulsory). Then he explains the rule: we must follow the Prophet's actions with the same intention (jihah) he performed them. For example, if he performed an action as Wajib, we must do it as Wajib; if as Mustahab, we do it as Mustahab. If we do not know the intended category, we default to the lowest level, which is Mubah. The evidence is rational: the Prophet would never do something Makruh (disliked) or Haram (prohibited), so his actions must fall within the permissible or higher categories. The passage then discusses disagreement among scholars about actions that were done forgetfully or by nature (like sleeping posture) with no clear ruling. Three opinions are presented: (1) stop and do not follow until clarity, (2) follow unless proven prohibited, (3) follow as permissible. The preferred Hanafi position (Imam al-Karkhi and the author) is that such actions are to be followed as permissible, not as obligatory or recommended, because the original ruling of all actions is permissibility until evidence indicates otherwise.

Key Terms
مباح (mubah): An action that is neither rewarded nor punished if done or left, and the Prophet did it without special intention
مستحب (mustahab): An action that is recommended, doing it brings reward but leaving it does not bring punishment
واجب (wajib): An action that is obligatory but of slightly lower emphasis than Fard, according to Hanafi terminology
فرض (fard): An action that is compulsory, requiring definitive evidence; reward for doing, punishment for leaving
