Abandoned by Shariah Treated Like Abandoned by Custom: Examples
Extending the rule of mahjurah to include what is abandoned by Shariah, with examples of agency and oath.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that what is abandoned by Shariah is interpreted figuratively, using the examples of agency and oath.

Core Idea
Shariah's disapproval triggers figurative interpretation, just as custom does.

In Hanafi usul, the principle that what is abandoned by custom (mahjur 'adatan) leads to figurative interpretation is extended to what is abandoned by Shariah (mahjur shar'an). This ensures that legal language is understood in a way that avoids conflict with Shariah values. This lesson explores two concrete applications: agency for litigation and an oath concerning a child.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Identify the maxim: 'Al-mahjur shar'an ka al-mahjur 'adatan' – what is abandoned by Shariah is like what is abandoned by custom. It means when the literal meaning of a word conflicts with Shariah disapproval, we resort to figurative (majaz) interpretation.
2
Understand the trigger for majaz
The literal meaning must be something that Shariah disapproves of (even if not directly prohibited). The disapproval can come from a clear text, like the hadith on mercy to children. Disapproval makes the literal meaning 'abandoned' (mahjur), so it is not taken at face value.
3
Apply to agency for litigation
Consider an agency (wakalah) for 'khusumah' (litigation). The literal meaning of khusumah is denial (inkar), which is condemned in Shariah (false denial is haram). Therefore, the agency is interpreted figuratively as 'answering the opponent' (jawaab).
The Hanafi school uses this to avoid Shariah violation.
4
Apply to an oath not to speak to a boy
An oath 'I will not speak to this boy' uses the literal term 'sabi' (child). Shariah discourages cutting ties with children (as per the hadith). Hence, the oath is interpreted figuratively as referring to the person (dhat) for all time, not restricted to childhood.
The indefinite form 'a boy' (nakirah) remains literal.
5
Analyze the objection and answer
An objection claims that figurative interpretation leads to multiple sins (not showing mercy, not respecting elders, abandoning a Muslim). The answer: these sins occur only inadvertently, not by intention; the oath's figurative meaning is valid.
This reinforces that Shariah-abandoned acts are avoided through reinterpretation, not by committing sins.

When Haqiqah Is Abandoned: Five Indicators in Hanafi Usul
Listing cases where haqiqah is abandoned

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the five indicators (dalalat) that can override the literal meaning (haqiqah) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Haqiqah is not absolute; custom, word limitation, context, speaker's intent, and speech setting can override it.

In Hanafi usul, words are generally taken in their literal sense (haqiqah), but this principle has exceptions. This lesson examines five distinct indicators that cause the abandonment of haqiqah. These indicators — custom, the word's own limitation, syntactic context, speaker's meaning, and the place of speech — are crucial for accurate legal interpretation. Mastering them allows us to correctly derive rulings from texts and oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first indicator: custom (al-adah)
Look at the first example: a vow (nadhr) regarding prayer and hajj. Custom overrides the literal meaning because the common understanding of 'prayer' and 'hajj' refers to the customary acts, not every possible literal interpretation.
This is based on the maxim 'al-adah muhakkamah' (custom is authoritative).
2
Identify the second indicator: word's own limitation (dalalah al-lafz fi nafsih)
In the oath 'I will not eat meat', the word 'lahm' (meat) in common usage does not include fish. Similarly, 'every slave of mine is free' excludes the mukatab because he is not fully a slave. The word itself limits its scope.
This relies on the customary usage (urf) of the word.
3
Identify the third indicator: context of composition (siyaq al-nazm)
The phrase 'divorce my wife if you are a man' is interpreted as giving authority (tawkil) rather than a conditional divorce. The structure of the sentence and circumstances show the speaker's intention to delegate, not to condition.
Context overrides the literal conditional meaning.
4
Identify the fourth and fifth indicators: speaker's meaning (ma'na yarji' ila al-mutakallim) and place of speech (mahall al-kalam)
The fourth indicator involves the speaker's intention, as in certain oaths (yamin al-qard? — uncertain text). The fifth is the physical or situational context (mahall al-kalam). Both provide clues that override literal meaning.
These are less commonly invoked but recognized in Hanafi usul.
5
Apply the indicators to a new scenario
Given a statement like 'I will give charity to the poor', does 'poor' include a mukatab? Using the second indicator, 'poor' in custom may include or exclude depending on usage. Use the passage's methodology to determine which indicator applies.
Practice mapping specific cases to the correct indicator.

Types of Qara'in for Preferring Majaz over Haqiqah
Lists and explains indicators that necessitate acting upon majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five types of qara'in (indicators) that necessitate preferring majaz over haqiqah according to Hanafi usul and apply them to interpret oaths and vows.

Core Idea
Haqiqah is not always intended; strong qara'in—custom, derivation, context—shift meaning to majaz.

In this lesson, we examine a key passage from Nur al-Anwar that outlines the qara'in which require abandoning the literal meaning for the figurative. Understanding these indicators is crucial for correctly interpreting legal speech, especially in oaths and vows. The author lists five qara'in, and the Urdu commentary elaborates on the first two, including a famous dispute with Imam Malik regarding the word 'lahm'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the First Qarina: 'Urf (Custom)
Understand how custom transfers meaning from literal to conventional. In the example of salat and hajj, the literal meanings (dua, intent) are abandoned in favor of the conventional rituals due to widespread custom.
Note that 'urf must be general and established among the people.
2
Identify the Second Qarina: Dalalah fi Nafsihi (Word's Own Indication)
Recognize how etymology (ishtiqaq) points away from literal meaning. The word 'lahm' derives from strength requiring blood, so it excludes fish. Similarly, 'mamluk' excludes the mukatab because his ownership is weak.
This qarina relies on the root meaning and the absence of a key attribute in the referent.
3
Identify the Third Qarina: Siyaq al-Nathm (Context of the Sentence)
Analyze how sentence structure indicates majaz. The example 'divorce my wife if you are a man' shows that the conditional is not a delegation but a threat, so the literal meaning of 'being a man' is not intended.
Context includes the syntactic structure and the implied speaker intent.
4
Identify the Fourth Qarina: Dalalah al-Ma'na al-Raji' ila al-Mutakallim (Meaning Referring Back to Speaker)
Consider indicators that show the speaker's intended meaning, such as in the oath of retraction (yamin al-qarar radd). The precise meaning is derived from what the speaker is understood to intend.
This qarina requires knowledge of the speaker's context and prior statements.
5
Identify the Fifth Qarina: Dalalah Mahall al-Kalam (Subject of Speech)
Use the topic of discourse to interpret figuratively. The Prophet's hadith 'innama al-a'malu bi al-niyyat' and 'rafi'a 'an ummati al-khata' wa al-nisyan' are interpreted by the subject of actions and forgetfulness, not literal negation.
This qarina emphasizes that the overall subject shapes the meaning of individual words.

Silence as Permission (Idhn) in Transactions
Ruling on silence as permission when seeing a slave transacting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that silence can constitute idhn in transactions when the observer is aware and able to object.

Core Idea
Silence with knowledge and ability to object is permission.

This lesson presents a fundamental Hanafi usul principle: silence in the face of an action that would normally require objection is deemed permission. The example of a master watching his slave transact illustrates the rule. Imam Zufar's dissent is also noted, highlighting a valid ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and parties
Recognize the situation: a master sees his slave (authorized to trade) buying and selling in the market. The master is aware and capable of objecting.
The slave here is 'abd majur' – a servant with permission to trade.
2
Recognize the key condition
The master knows of the slave's actions and does not object. If the master were unaware or unable to object, silence would not be consent.
Knowledge and ability to object are essential for silence to convey permission.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule
In Hanafi usul, such silence is treated as implicit permission (idhn). The slave's transactions are valid without explicit verbal consent.
This is based on the maxim that silence in the presence of a transaction requiring objection indicates consent.
4
Contrast with Imam Zufar's view
Imam Zufar (may Allah have mercy on him) holds that silence does not stand in place of explicit permission. He requires a clear verbal or behavioral indication.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school itself.

Determine the practical consequence

Commentary
This passage presents a Hanafi usul rule: silence in the presence of an action that would normally require explicit permission is deemed permission when the observer is in a position to object. The example given is a master seeing his slave (who is authorized to trade) engaging in buying and selling; the master's silence is treated as consent. Imam Zufar disagrees, requiring explicit verbal permission. The Hanafi position is that silence as a qarinah (contextual indicator) suffices.

Key Terms
اذان (idhn): Permission explicitly or implicitly granted
سكوت (sukūt): Silence in the face of an action that requires objection if disapproved
