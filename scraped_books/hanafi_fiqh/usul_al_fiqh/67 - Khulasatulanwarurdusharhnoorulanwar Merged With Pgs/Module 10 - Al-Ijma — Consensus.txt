Example of Qiyas from Ijma: Prohibition of Zina's Relatives
Extends ijma on slave-girl's mother prohibition to zina via same 'illa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of qiyas from ijma by identifying the effective cause (juz'iyya) in a consensus-based case and extending it to new situations such as zina.

Core Idea
The child being a part (juz') of both parents creates prohibition; this cause applies equally in zina.

This passage illustrates how the Hanafi school uses qiyas (analogy) grounded in ijma (consensus) to derive rulings. The consensus on the prohibition of a slave-girl's mother after legitimate intercourse provides the basis. By extracting the effective cause (juz'iyya), the same prohibition extends to cases of zina, demonstrating the school's systematic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original case and its ruling
State the ijma case: a man who has legitimate intercourse with his slave-girl – her mother becomes forbidden. Note that this is a ruling established by consensus.
This is the asl (original case) for the qiyas.
2
Extract the 'illa (effective cause)
The cause is juz'iyya: the child born is part of both the man and woman. This creates a relationship that triggers prohibition of each other's ascendants and descendants.
This extraction is takhrij al-manat from the consensus.
3
Verify the 'illa in the new case (far')
In zina, if a child is born, it is also part of both the adulterer and adulteress. Therefore, the same 'illa is present.
Hanafis accept this 'illa even though lineage is not legally established; the effect (partiality) remains.
4
Apply the ruling via qiyas
Just as the lawful wife's mother is forbidden, the adulteress's mother becomes forbidden to the adulterer. The prohibition transfers from the child to the parents.
This is the derived hukm.
5
Note the opposing school's view
Shafi'is reject this analogy because they hold that zina does not create lineage or mahram relations. Thus, they do not consider the adulteress's mother prohibited.
The dispute is based on differing usul principles about the effect of zina.

Example of analogy extracted from consensus (ijma): There is consensus among jurists that if a person has intercourse (wali, i.e., legitimate) with his slave-girl, then her mother becomes forbidden to him. The cause is that the child born from the intercourse is a part of both the man and the woman; therefore, for the man and the woman, each other's ascending and descending relatives become forbidden. The detail: The child is the source of prohibitions; if it is a son, then the woman's mother and daughters are forbidden to him; similarly, if it is a daughter, then the man's father and sons are forbidden to her. Then this prohibition transfers from the child to the man and woman, so for each, the other's ascending and descending relatives become forbidden. In short, just as there is consensus upon the prohibition of the original and branch relatives due to the cause of partiality (juz'iyya), this same cause of partiality is also found in the case of zina. Therefore, according to the same detail, for the adulterer, the mother of the adulteress (with whom he committed adultery) becomes forbidden, just as it is forbidden for him to marry his own wife's mother (mother-in-law). (Understand this point with special attention).

Commentary
This passage shows qiyas based on ijma. The jurists agree that legitimate intercourse with a slave-girl makes her mother permanently prohibited to the man. The 'illa is juz'iyya: the child is a part of both parents, creating a relationship that forbids each other's ascendants and descendants. This 'illa is also present in zina, so through qiyas, the same prohibition applies: the adulterer cannot marry the adulteress's mother, just as he cannot marry his wife's mother. This is a clear application of extending a ruling from a consensus-based original case to a new case via shared effective cause.

اجماع (ijma): Consensus of jurists on the prohibition.

Taqrir al-Asl: Preserving Original Rulings When Evidences Conflict
Explains taqrir al-asl with the su'r al-himar example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of taqrir al-asl and apply it to the classic example of donkey's leftover water to derive the ruling.

Core Idea
When no preference can be made between conflicting evidences, each element remains on its original ruling.

In Hanafi usul, when multiple evidences conflict and no method of tarjih (preference) can resolve the contradiction, a final principle is invoked: taqrir al-asl, meaning to preserve each subject on its original legal state. This lesson explores that principle through the well-known example of water left by a domestic donkey (su'r al-himar). Understanding this principle helps clarify how Hanafi scholars handle deadlocks in evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict
Recognize that two hadiths and two Companion statements give opposite rulings on donkey meat and leftover water, creating a ta'arud (conflict).
One hadith prohibits donkey meat; another permits it. One athar allows wudu with su'r; another declares the donkey impure.
2
Recognize the failure of tarjih
Observe that all standard methods of preference (tarjih) are exhausted: the hadiths cannot be reconciled, the athar contradict, and analogies (qiyas) fail due to differences in need (hajah).
The author lists three failed qiyas attempts.
3
Invoke taqrir al-asl
Apply the principle: each entity is kept on its original ruling. Water is originally pure (Quran 25:48), and a person in hadath is originally in a state of minor impurity.
Original states are determined by clear proofs before the conflict.
4
Derive the combined ruling
Since water is pure but doubt exists due to conflict, wudu is performed with the water. Since the hadath state is unresolved, tayammum is also required.
The result is both wudu and tayammum are obligatory.

If it is not possible to resort to any of the aforementioned matters for removing conflict, then taqrir al-asl (preserving the original state) becomes obligatory, meaning: establish every thing on its original basis and keep what was as it was (act by keeping each evidence on its original state). Example: The issue of su'r al-himar (donkey's leftover water) is an example of taqrir al-asl. In this regard, all evidences are conflicting. For example, two hadith are reported: (1) The Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade eating the meat of domestic donkeys on the day of Khaybar, and the Companions overturned the pots in which this meat was cooking. This proves prohibition. (2) Ghalib ibn Fihr (may Allah be pleased with him) complained to the Prophet (peace be upon him) of his poverty, saying, 'I have nothing left but small donkeys.' The Prophet (peace be upon him) said, 'Slaughter fat, healthy donkeys from your wealth and eat them.' This is proof of permissibility. Question: The hadiths conflict regarding the ruling of meat; how do you make this an example of conflict regarding saliva? Answer: Saliva is generated from meat, and the ruling of meat will be the ruling of saliva. So if meat is prohibited, saliva is prohibited; if meat is permissible, saliva is permissible. And su'r (leftover water) is related to saliva because when the animal puts its mouth on the vessel, its saliva touches the vessel. Statements and actions of the Companions are also conflicting in this regard: (1) In one narration, permission is reported to perform wudu with water leftover by a donkey, while (2) the narration of Anas (may Allah be pleased with him) is contrary, as he said, 'The Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade domestic donkeys and said they are impure.' This proves the impurity of su'r al-himar, while the first athar proves purity. In this regard, analogies are also conflicting: (1) For purity, su'r cannot be analogized to the sweat of a donkey because need is greater for sweat and less for su'r. (2) Similarly, for impurity, because su'r and milk are both generated from meat, but su'r al-himar cannot be attached to donkey's milk because there is need in su'r and not in milk. (3) It is also not possible to make su'r al-himar subject to the su'r of a dog and declare it prohibited, because there is not the same need in a dog as in a donkey, since a donkey is a domestic animal. Likewise, su'r al-himar cannot be made subject to the su'r of a cat and declared pure, because the need for a cat is not as great as for a donkey. Summary: When all avenues of preference are blocked, taqrir al-asl becomes obligatory. The conclusion is that the person in hadath (impurity) is kept on his original state, and water is kept on its original state. So water, due to its original state, is pure (And We sent down from the sky pure water), it does not become impure by the donkey putting its mouth in it. Rather, it remains pure according to its original state. And a person, according to his original state, is in hadath (minor impurity), so he remains in hadath, and for purification, tayammum becomes obligatory upon him. Therefore, if no water is available except the water left by a donkey, he should perform both wudu and tayammum. Objection: When water is originally pure and purifying, and not impure, wudu should be sufficient. Why is tayammum added? Answer: The requirement of taqrir al-asl is that each of the two things (the person in hadath and water) be kept on its original state. If wudu alone were deemed sufficient, water would remain on its original state, but the person (in hadath) would not remain on his original state.

Resolving Ta'arud: The Failed Qiyas and the Necessity of Taqrir al-Asl
Analyses the conflicting qiyas and the necessity of taqrir al-asl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the types of conflicting evidences in the donkey's leftover water example and explain why each attempted qiyas fails, leading to the application of taqrir al-asl.

Core Idea
When hadiths, athar, and qiyas all contradict without possibility of tarjih, only taqrir al-asl saves the ruling.

The previous lesson introduced the principle of taqrir al-asl; here we examine the detailed conflict that necessitates it. The passage walks through conflicting hadiths, Companion statements, and three attempted analogies, each failing due to differences in need (hajah). Understanding this walkthrough shows how Hanafi usul handles deadlock and why the principle of taqrir al-asl becomes the final resort.

List the conflicting hadiths on donkey meat
Identify the two hadiths: one prohibiting donkey meat (Khaybar) and one permitting it (Ghalib ibn Fihr). Note that the ruling on saliva is derived from meat.
Saliva is generated from meat, so the conflict extends to su'r.

Identify the conflicting athar on purity

The conflict is between permissibility and impurity.
3
Examine the three failed qiyas attempts

The three attempts: (1) analogizing to sweat (fails because sweat is more needed), (2) analogizing to milk (fails because milk has less need), (3) analogizing to dog/cat (fails because need differs).
4
Conclude that taqrir al-asl is the only way
Since all tarjih methods (including qiyas) are exhausted, the principle of taqrir al-asl is invoked: water remains pure, person remains in hadath.
This leads to the ruling: wudu + tayammum.

If it is not possible to resort to any of the aforementioned matters for removing conflict, then taqrir al-asl (preserving the original state) becomes obligatory, meaning: establish every thing on its original basis and keep what was as it was (act by keeping each evidence on its original state). Example: The issue of su'r al-himar (donkey's leftover water) is an example of taqrir al-asl. In this regard, all evidences are conflicting. For example, two hadith are reported: (1) The Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade eating the meat of domestic donkeys on the day of Khaybar, and the Companions overturned the pots in which this meat was cooking. This proves prohibition. (2) Ghalib ibn Fihr (may Allah be pleased with him) complained to the Prophet (peace be upon him) of his poverty, saying, 'I have nothing left but small donkeys.' The Prophet (peace be upon him) said, 'Slaughter fat, healthy donkeys from your wealth and eat them.' This is proof of permissibility. Question: The hadiths conflict regarding the ruling of meat; how do you make this an example of conflict regarding saliva? Answer: Saliva is generated from meat, and the ruling of meat will be the ruling of saliva. So if meat is prohibited, saliva is prohibited; if meat is permissible, saliva is permissible. And su'r (leftover water) is related to saliva because when the animal puts its mouth on the vessel, its saliva touches the vessel. Statements and actions of the Companions are also conflicting in this regard: (1) In one narration, permission is reported to perform wudu with water leftover by a donkey, while (2) the narration of Anas (may Allah be pleased with him) is contrary, as he said, 'The Prophet (peace be upon him) forbade domestic donkeys and said they are impure.' This proves the impurity of su'r al-himar, while the first athar proves purity. In this regard, analogies are also conflicting: (1) For purity, su'r cannot be analogized to the sweat of a donkey because need is greater for sweat and less for su'r. (2) Similarly, for impurity, because su'r and milk are both generated from meat, but su'r al-himar cannot be attached to donkey's milk because there is need in su'r and not in milk. (3) It is also not possible to make su'r al-himar subject to the su'r of a dog and declare it prohibited, because there is not the same need in a dog as in a donkey, since a donkey is a domestic animal. Likewise, su'r al-himar cannot be made subject to the su'r of a cat and declared pure, because the need for a cat is not as great as for a donkey. Summary: When all avenues of preference are blocked, taqrir al-asl becomes obligatory. The conclusion is that the person in hadath (impurity) is kept on his original state, and water is kept on its original state. So water, due to its original state, is pure (And We sent down from the sky pure water), it does not become impure by the donkey putting its mouth in it. Rather, it remains pure according to its original state. And a person, according to his original state, is in hadath (minor impurity), so he remains in hadath, and for purification, tayammum becomes obligatory upon him. Therefore, if no water is available except the water left by a donkey, he should perform both wudu and tayammum. Objection: When water is originally pure and purifying, and not impure, wudu should be sufficient. Why is tayammum added? Answer: The requirement of taqrir al-asl is that each of the two things (the person in hadath and water) be kept on its original state. If wudu alone were deemed sufficient, water would remain on its original state, but the person (in hadath) would not remain on his original state.

Commentary
This passage from the Hanafi usul work explains the principle of taqrir al-asl (preserving the original state) when evidences are irreconcilably conflicting. The author illustrates this with the classic Hanafi example of the ruling on water leftover by a donkey (su'r al-himar). After demonstrating that all methods of preference (tarjih) among conflicting hadiths, companion statements, and analogies fail, the principle of taqrir al-asl is invoked. This principle dictates that each entity is kept in its original legal state: water remains pure (as per its original ruling), and a person in a state of minor impurity (hadath) remains in that state. Consequently, if only donkey-leftover water is available, one must perform both wudu (using the water, as it is pure) and tayammum (because the person's hadath state is unresolved due to doubt). The commentary follows the author's order: first stating the principle, then presenting the conflicting evidences, the resolution via taqrir al-asl, and finally addressing an objection about combining wudu and tayammum. This example is a standard Hanafi teaching to show how usul principles operate when other methods of conflict resolution fail.

Definition and Types of Ijma' (Consensus)
Definition and classification of ijma' with conditions and cross-madhhab notes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ijma', distinguish its two types (azimat and rukhsat), and identify the conditions for a valid mujtahid in ijma'.

Core Idea
Ijma' is the unanimous agreement of qualified mujtahids of an era; tacit acceptance (sukuti) is binding in the Hanafi school.

In this lesson, we examine the first source after the Qur'an and Sunnah: Ijma' (consensus). The text defines ijma' both linguistically and technically, then divides it into two types and clarifies who qualifies as a mujtahid in this context. Pay careful attention to the Hanafi position on tacit consensus and the conditions that make ijma' binding.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic and technical definition
Note that ijma' linguistically means agreement or unity. Technically, it is the agreement of righteous mujtahids of the Ummah in one era on a religious matter, whether verbal or practical.
The definition emphasizes that the mujtahids must be righteous (salihun) and truly knowledgeable.
2
Differentiate between azimat and rukhsat (explicit and tacit consensus)
Azimat (explicit) is when all mujtahids openly agree by statement or action. Rukhsat (concession, actually sukuti) is when some express agreement and others remain silent, neither agreeing nor objecting. This is called ijma' sukuti.
Hanafis accept sukuti as binding; Shafi'is do not.
3
Identify the conditions for a valid mujtahid in ijma'
The mujtahid must be pious and not a slave of desires. It is not required that he be a Companion, from Ahl al-Bayt, from Madinah, nor that the generation must pass away (inkirad al-asr).
These conditions make ijma' flexible and not tied to a specific group or time.
4
Note the cross-madhhab differences
Imam Shafi'i does not accept ijma' sukuti. Imam Malik only accepts the consensus of the people of Madinah. The Hanafi school maintains its own position as described.
Understanding these differences helps appreciate the diversity of usul.

Definition, Types, Conditions, and Proofs of Ijma'
Defines ijma', divides into types, states conditions, ruling, and proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ijma' linguistically and terminologically, distinguish its two types (azimat and rukhsat), list the conditions for its validity, and identify the Quranic proofs for its authority.

Core Idea
Ijma' is the unanimous agreement of all mujtahids in one era; it is a certain proof, and its denial is kufr.

This passage from Nur al-Anwar introduces ijma', the third primary source of Islamic law after Quran and Sunnah. Ijma' is a definitive proof (qati) that establishes rulings with certainty. The Hanafi school has a unique position on tacit consensus (sukti ijma') and conditions of validity, which are crucial for any student of usul. We will unpack the definition, types, conditions, and proofs from the text.

Guided Path
1
Define ijma' linguistically and terminologically
Identify the literal meaning (agreement, union) and the technical definition: agreement of all qualified mujtahids of the Ummah in one era upon a shar'i ruling (verbal or practical). Note that it is a qati' proof.
The definition explicitly excludes non-mujtahids.
2
Distinguish the two types: azimat and rukhsat
Azimat is explicit consensus (all verbally or practically agree). Rukhsat is tacit consensus (sukti) where some remain silent. The Hanafi school accepts both.
Sukti ijma' is also called rukhsat in this text.
3
List conditions for the validity of ijma'
The mujtahids must be upright and independent. It is not required to be a Sahabi, from Ahl al-Bayt, from Madinah, nor that the era expire. Earlier disagreement does not necessarily prevent later consensus (except according to a specific view of Abu Hanifa on the umm walad case). Unanimity is required, not majority.
Imam Malik's condition of Madinans is rejected.
4
Understand the Hanafi position on sukti ijma'
The Hanafi school considers sukti ijma' valid and binding; Imam Shafi'i disagrees. The Hanafi proof: silence in the presence of a ruling indicates consent when dissent is possible.
This is a key intra-madhhab point for fatwa.
5
Analyze the Quranic proofs
Three verses are cited: (1) 2:143 – Ummah as middle/witnesses; (2) 3:110 – best Ummah; (3) 4:115 – threat for opposing believers' way. Each is used to derive the authority of ijma'.
The first verse is the primary proof; the others support.

Chapter of Ijma' - Discussion of Ijma'. Linguistic meaning: agreement, union. Terminological definition: agreement of the qualified mujtahids of the Ummah of Muhammad (peace be upon him) in one era upon a matter, whether verbal or practical. Ijma' has two types: it is described as the pillar of Ijma'. One is 'azimat (strong determination) and the other is rukhsat (concession). Definition of 'azimat: all mujtahids agree on the issue, which is known from their statements—for example, they all say 'ajma'na 'ala hadha'—or proven by their actions. Definition of rukhsat: some reliable ones agree and other mujtahids neither agree nor object. This is called sukti ijma' (tacit consensus), which is considered valid among us; Imam Shafi'i (may Allah's mercy be upon him) does not believe in it. Statement of the conditions for the validity of Ijma': the [mujtahid] must be reliable, upright, and clear-sighted, not a slave of his desires. However, it is not necessary for a mujtahid to be a Companion, nor a member of Ahl al-Bayt (may Allah be pleased with them), nor that he be from the people of Madinah, nor that the generation passes away. Imam Malik (may Allah's mercy be upon him) considers only the consensus of the people of Madinah as valid. Also, it is not necessary that there was no earlier disagreement on the issue, as some people have attributed this condition to Imam Abu Hanifa (may Allah's mercy be upon him), which is false. For example, the sale of umm walad was prohibited by Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) and permitted by Ali (may Allah be pleased with him). But now, according to Imam Muhammad (may Allah's mercy be upon him), it is not permissible in any case, even if the judge has ruled, because consensus on the prohibition of selling umm walad had occurred in the era of the Companions. However, according to Imam Abu Hanifa (may Allah's mercy be upon him), due to the earlier disagreement, this consensus is not considered binding, and the judge's ruling will be given weight. Condition for the formation of Ijma': for the formation of Ijma', it is necessary that all [mujtahids] agree. If even one differs, this consensus is not formed. Majority agreement is not sufficient, as the Mu'tazila believe. Ruling of Ijma': whatever is agreed upon confers certainty and certitude; therefore, one who denies a consensual ruling is a disbeliever, unless there is an obstacle preventing certainty and certitude. However, the denier of the [occurrence of] consensus will not be declared a disbeliever. Proofs for the authority of Ijma': (1) 'And thus We have made you a middle nation to be witnesses over the people' – 'middle' means just, and the testimony of the just produces certain knowledge. (2) 'You are the best nation brought forth for mankind' – the consensus of the best nation will also be a proof. (3) 'And whoever opposes the Messenger after the guidance has become clear to him and follows other than the way of the believers, We shall turn him to what he has turned and burn him in Hell' – here warning of Hell is for opposing the way of the believers, and Ijma' is also the way of the believers, so opposing it is not permissible.

Commentary
This passage provides a comprehensive definition of Ijma' (consensus) as a source of Islamic law. It first gives the linguistic meaning: agreement or union. Conditions for Formation of Ijma and Its Binding Certainty
States the condition for ijma and its binding ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi condition for the formation of ijma (unanimous agreement) and distinguish between denial of a certain ijma ruling (kufr) and denial of the occurrence of ijma (not kufr).

Core Idea
Ijma requires unanimous agreement; once formed, it gives definite certainty, and its denial is kufr.

In this passage, the author lays out the fundamental condition for the formation of ijma (consensus) in Hanafi usul: it must be unanimous. Majority opinion, as advocated by the Mu'tazila, is insufficient. Additionally, the passage clarifies the legal status of ijma: once it is established, it yields certainty, making denial of that certain ruling an act of disbelief, with specific exceptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main claims
The passage contains two distinct parts: (1) the condition for the formation of ijma, and (2) the ruling of ijma (its epistemological and legal effect). Read carefully to separate them.
2
Analyse the condition: unanimous agreement
The condition states that 'all of the original assembly' must agree. This means every qualified mujtahid in that generation. The text explicitly rejects majority opinion as insufficient, citing the Mu'tazila as the opposing view.
Note the term 'اصل اجتماع' (original assembly) – it refers to all mujtahids of the age, not just a select group.
3
Analyse the ruling: definitive certainty
Ijma yields 'قطیعت اور یقین' (definitiveness and certainty). This means the ruling becomes qat'i (certain) and binding on all Muslims.
4
Determine the consequence of denial
Denying a ruling established by definitive ijma is 'كفر' (disbelief). However, there is an exception: if there is a 'عارض' (obstacle) that prevents certainty, denial may not be kufr. Also, denying the occurrence of ijma (the assembly) is not kufr, only denying the certain ruling itself.
The distinction between 'منکر اجماع' (denier of ijma ruling) and 'منکر اجتماع' (denier of the assembly) is crucial.
5
Synthesise the complete rule
Form a clear statement: For ijma to be valid, all mujtahids must agree. Once valid ijma exists, the ruling is definitive; anyone who denies it after knowing it is a disbeliever, unless a legitimate obstacle (like a conflicting strong dalil) undermines the certainty. Denying that the ijma occurred is not disbelief.

Three Quranic verses establish ijma as a binding proof: the Ummah is just, the best, and its path is protected.

In this lesson, we examine the primary Quranic evidences for hujjiyyat al-ijma (authority of consensus). The Hanafi school relies on these verses to argue that the consensus of the Ummah yields certainty and must be followed. We will analyze each verse's linguistic and legal implications.

Core Idea
When scholars differ, their differing views become a binding consensus; no new opinion is allowed.

You might think consensus requires unanimity, but the Hanafi school introduces a sophisticated concept: when qualified scholars disagree on a matter, their differing opinions collectively form a binding consensus (ijma murakkab). Commentary
This passage is a discussion of a usul al-fiqh rule concerning ijma (consensus) when scholars hold differing opinions on a matter. The text first notes that the consensus of post-Companion generations on issues where the Companions differed is treated like khabar wahid (isolated report) — it does not produce certainty but is given precedence over qiyas (analogy). Then it introduces the main issue: if the ummah (community) has differing opinions on a matter, can these differences collectively be considered a form of ijma? There are two positions. The first (preferred opinion) holds that each of the differing opinions attains the rank of consensus, meaning it is impermissible to adopt a new opinion outside these. An example is the differing opinions of the four Imams on a single issue, or the case of a pregnant woman whose husband dies (a specific fiqh example). The second opinion restricts this to the Companions only. The commentator (al-Sharh) inclines to the first, stating it is general for all eras of mujtahids, and calls it 'ijma murakkab' (composite consensus). He explains that the invalidity of a fifth school after the four established schools is based on this concept: since the four Imams differed, their collective opinions constitute a consensus that a fifth opinion cannot be introduced. Then an objection is raised: if ijma murakkab requires contemporaneity, then the Shafi'i and Hanbali schools would be invalid because they came after Abu Hanifa and Malik, and their differences would have been prohibited. But they are valid, so the condition cannot be simultaneity. If simultaneity is not required, why is the consensus of Shafi'i and Ahmad considered valid while others are not? The commentator concedes the answer is difficult. The passage ends with the completion of the book.
