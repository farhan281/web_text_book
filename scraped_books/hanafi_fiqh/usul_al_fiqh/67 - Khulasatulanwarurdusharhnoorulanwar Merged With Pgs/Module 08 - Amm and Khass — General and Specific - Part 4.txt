Hanafi Rule on Mutlaq and Muqayyad: Proof, Dispute, and Five Scenarios
Explains Hanafi rule on mutlaq and muqayyad with proof, Shafi'i dispute, and five scenarios.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for carrying mutlaq to muqayyad, identify the exception, and apply the rule to examples like expiation of oath and sadaqat al-fitr.

Core Idea
Mutlaq is not carried to muqayyad except in a single ruling to avoid contradiction.

This lesson examines the Hanafi usul principle governing the relationship between absolute (mutlaq) and restricted (muqayyad) texts. You will learn the core rule, its rational proof, the exception when both belong to a single ruling, and how the Hanafis respond to the Shafi'i objection using the avoidance of contradiction. Finally, a summary of the five possible scenarios of mutlaq and muqayyad occurrences is presented.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi Rule
Identify the baseline rule: the mutlaq text is not carried to the muqayyad text, even if both occur in the same incident. This is the default position.
The rule applies unless an exception is proven.
2
Identify the Exception
Note the exception: when both mutlaq and muqayyad appear within a single legal ruling (hukm wahid), the muqayyad prevails. The restricted text is like a speaker, the absolute like one silent; the speaker takes priority.
This exception is limited to cases where acting on both simultaneously would cause contradiction.
3
Analyze the Hanafi Proof
Understand the proof: mutlaq and muqayyad are two independent proofs. Both must be applied as far as possible. Only when they conflict in a single ruling is one given precedence.
The proof relies on the principle of avoiding ijtima' al-diddayn (conjunction of opposites).
4
Examine the Shafi'i Objection
Read the objection: the Shafi'is argue that in the case of sadaqat al-fitr, since the ruling and incident are the same, the Hanafis should have carried the mutlaq to the muqayyad (from the hadith 'from among the Muslims').
The Shafi'i position generally carries mutlaq to muqayyad when both address the same subject.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi Response
Consider the response: there is no contradiction because the mutlaq text (general charity) and the muqayyad text (only Muslims) relate to different causes. Hence, both can be applied without conflict.
This response preserves the independence of both texts while respecting the rule of avoiding contradiction.
6
Review the Five Scenarios
The passage concludes with a diagram of five possible scenarios of mutlaq and muqayyad occurrences based on unity or difference of cause and ruling. Familiarize yourself with the summary table in the source.
These scenarios help determine when the rule or the exception applies.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the Hanafi rule regarding the relationship between mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (restricted) texts in the Quran and sunnah. The author begins by stating the Hanafi position: the absolute is not carried to the restricted, regardless of whether they appear in the same incident (e.g., expiation of zihar) or in different incidents (e.g., expiation of murder and oath). The rule applies generally: the absolute remains operative on its own. The example given is that in the expiation of zihar, one may feed the poor unconditionally; having intercourse before completing the expiation does not incur sin (unlike some other schools). Similarly, in expiation of oath, freeing a disbelieving slave suffices, whereas in expiation of murder only a believing slave suffices (due to a separate restricted text).

Then the Hanafi proof is presented: both the absolute and restricted are independent proofs, so both should be acted upon as much as possible. The exception (istithna') is when both address a single legal ruling (hukm wahid). In that case, acting on both simultaneously is impossible, so the restricted is given precedence because it is like a speaker (natiq) while the absolute is silent (sakit), and the speaker takes priority.

An example is given from the recitation of Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (non-mutawatir) which adds the condition of consecutiveness (tawatur) for the fasts in expiation of oath, while the mutawatir recitation does not mention it. If both were taken as equally authoritative, one would have to both require consecutiveness and not require it, which is a contradiction. Thus, the Hanafi rule avoids this by not carrying the absolute to the restricted except when they are in a single ruling (and here they are not, since the recitations are considered different sources).

The commentary then addresses that Imam al-Shafi'i here departs from his usual principle: he does not carry the absolute to the restricted in this case because he considers non-mutawatir recitations (whether solitary or famous) as non-authoritative. Then an objection from the Shafi'is is raised: according to the Hanafi principle, when the incident and ruling coincide, the absolute should be carried to the restricted. In the case of sadaqat al-fitr (charity at the end of Ramadan), there is a hadith that says 'Pay on behalf of the free and the slave among the Muslims', which is restricted to Muslims. So the Hanafis should not have said that sadaqat al-fitr is obligatory on behalf of a disbelieving slave. The Hanafi response: their principle was to avoid contradiction (ijtima' al-diddayn), but in this case there is no contradiction because the mutlaq and muqayyad texts relate to different causes (asbab). The absolute text about feeding the poor and the restricted text about Muslims relate to different issues, so both can be acted upon without conflict.

Finally, the passage concludes by introducing the five possible scenarios (suwar khamsa) of the occurrence of mutlaq and muqayyad texts, which are summarized in a diagram. The author indicates that the commentator points to these scenarios, showing the positions of the Hanafis and Shafi'is.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A text that is unrestricted in its application, without any qualifying condition.
مقید (muqayyad): A text that includes a restriction or condition (e.g., 'believing' slave, 'consecutive' days).
اجتماع ضدین (ijtima' al-diddayn): The conjunction of two opposites, which is logically impossible; the Hanafi principle avoids this by not carrying mutlaq to muqayyad when it would cause contradiction.

Hanafi Rulings for the Five Scenarios of Mutlaq and Muqayyad
Usul dispute: Hanafi rules for mutlaq and muqayyad scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five scenarios of mutlaq and muqayyad and state the Hanafi ruling for each, including areas of consensus and dispute with the Shafi'i school.

Core Idea
Mutlaq is carried to muqayyad only when both incident and ruling are identical.

This lesson presents the Hanafi framework for handling texts that appear in absolute (mutlaq) and restricted (muqayyad) forms. By examining five possible scenarios—determined by the unity or difference of the cause, incident, and ruling—you will learn when the Hanafi school applies the muqayyad to restrict the mutlaq and when it maintains the mutlaq's absoluteness. The passage also highlights points of consensus and disagreement with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1

Learning Objective

1
State the objection

The verse commands verification (tabayyun) when a fasiq brings news. The implication (mafhum) is that if a fasiq's news is unreliable, his testimony is also unreliable. Therefore, a witness must be 'adil.

The practical difference: In Hanafi fiqh, witnesses must be 'adil in all cases; in Shafi'i, for financial transactions, apparent justice may suffice because the requirement is limited to the raj'ah context.
The difference stems from different usul approaches to the same texts.

Learning Objective

This passage explains the fourth invalid cause from the Hanafi perspective – the Maliki claim that joining two statements with waw (و) forces them to share the same ruling. Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a general expression in the apodosis of a conditional is restricted to its specific cause, and distinguish it from independent general statements.

Core Idea
A general expression in a conditional apodosis is restricted to its specific cause, not universally binding.

This lesson examines the fifth invalid reason (wajh fasid) concerning general expressions (sīgha ʻāmm). The Hanafi school holds that when a general expression appears in the result clause of a conditional sentence or in a response occasioned by a specific event, it does not retain its full generality but is restricted to that occasion. This principle is crucial for understanding how the apparent generality of texts can be limited by their context. We will explore the rule, its rationale, and examples from the classical sources.

Guided Path

Locate the word or phrase that is 'amm' (e.g., 'my slave' in 'my slave is free'). Then determine whether it appears in an independent statement or in the apodosis of a conditional or response.
Focus on the clause structure: if it follows 'if' or 'when' as the result, it is in the apodosis.

Apply the Hanafi rule for general expressions in apodoses
If the general expression is in the apodosis, it is restricted (mukhassaṣ) to the specific cause of the speech (sabab). It does not apply to all individuals, only to the context at hand.
This is the established Hanafi position, even if the text appears general on its face.
3
Distinguish from independent general statements
If the general expression is an independent statement (not part of a conditional or response), it retains its full generality and applies to all individuals of its category.
The context of the speech determines whether the expression is restricted.

The final part of the passage reinforces that the restriction can be known through multiple proof methods.

The Principle of Dalālat al-Ḥāl in Oaths: A Hanafi–Shāfi‘ī Dispute
Dispute about contextual specification in an oath.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on contextual specification in oaths and explain why dalālat al-ḥāl does not restrict a general expression according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Context alone does not restrict a general expression in an oath; the literal meaning prevails until a clear specifier appears.

This lesson examines a classic dispute (ikhtilāf) between the Hanafi school and other schools regarding the role of context (dalālat al-ḥāl) in limiting a general expression (al-‘āmm). The example is an oath uttered in response to an invitation: 'If I eat today, my slave is free.' The Hanafi position is that the general expression 'eating' remains general; the opponent schools (Mālikī, Shāfi‘ī, and Zufar from within the Hanafi school) argue that the context of the invitation restricts the oath to eating with the host. Understanding the principles behind this disagreement is key to mastering how Hanafi usul approaches textual interpretation.

Guided Path
1
State the factual scenario
The oath-taker says to his host, 'If I eat today, my slave is free.' The cause (sabab) is the invitation. The question: does the context of the invitation restrict the general expression 'eat' to eating with the host?
The scenario is hypothetical but illustrates a real usul principle.
2
Identify the Hanafi ruling
The general expression remains on its generality; the mention of 'today' is considered superfluous (laghw). Thus eating anywhere that day breaks the oath.
The expression 'eat' is ‘āmm and is not restricted.
3
Understand the Hanafi proof
‘Āmm is authoritative unless a clear specifier (mukhassiṣ) exists. Context alone (dalālat al-ḥāl) is not a valid specifier because it is not a textual or analogical evidence.
The proof rests on the principle that ‘āmm yufīdu al-‘umūm.
4
State the opposing school's position
Mālik, Shāfi‘ī, and Zufar hold that dalālat al-ḥāl limits the speech to the specific occasion (the invitation). So the oath only applies to eating with the host.
They give weight to contextual cues as specifiers.
5
Analyse the thamarah al-khilāf
Practical consequence: if the oath-taker eats elsewhere, Hanafī rules he has broken his oath; the others rule he has not.
This difference affects fatwa in similar cases.

Applying 'Amm to Prove Zakat on Jewelry
Refuting the denial of zakat on jewelry using the principle of 'amm

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A general command remains binding on all instances unless a clear qarina proves exclusion.

In this passage, the Hanafi school defends the obligation of zakat on jewelry by invoking the usul principle that a general (عام) text remains operative on its generality until a contextual indication (قرینہ) restricts it. The opponent's claim that the word 'kanz' in the verse refers only to buried treasure is rejected because the accompanying phrase 'wa la yunfiqunaha' indicates that the verse is about spending in Allah's way, i.e., paying zakat, not about hiding treasure. This lesson will walk you through the Hanafi reasoning and show how to apply the principle of 'amm in istinbat.

State the Hanafi Principle on 'Amm
According to Hanafi usul, a general expression ('amm) is qat'i (definitive) in its application to all individuals of its category, and it remains binding until a valid qarina (contextual indication) restricts it.

Since the general verse includes all wealth, and there is no qarina that excludes jewelry (e.g., a hadith specifying that jewelry is exempt), the ruling of zakat applies to jewelry as well.

The opponent says 'kanz' means buried treasure, excluding jewelry. But the phrase 'wa la yunfiqunaha' (and they do not spend it) shows the verse is about withholding zakat, not about hiding wealth. Thus the general remains general.
The absence of a qarina to restrict the general means the general must be upheld.

Based on the principle of 'amm, zakat is obligatory on jewelry, including gold and silver used for adornment, unless there is a specific exclusion (takhsis) from another proof.
This is the Hanafi position; other schools may differ.

A narrator's adherence to a plausible meaning of a hadith is valid, not a contradiction — even if it differs from the apparent meaning.

This passage addresses a subtle point in hadith criticism: what happens when a narrator reports a hadith that has multiple possible meanings but then acts on a meaning different from the most apparent one? Does this contradict the hadith and weaken it? The text clarifies that as long as the meaning is linguistically plausible, the narrator's practice is valid and does not harm the hadith's credibility. This principle is key to understanding how Hanafis handle textual ambiguity in the Sunnah.

Resolving Conflict Between Two Quranic Verses: Resort to Sunnah
Explains how to resolve ta'āruḍ between two Quranic verses by resorting to Sunnah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when two Quranic verses conflict, both are set aside and the ruling is sought from the Sunnah.

Core Idea
Conflicting Quranic verses drop; the answer lies in Sunnah, not in a third verse.

When two proofs appear to contradict, the Hanafi school provides a clear hierarchy for resolution. The first level is conflict between two Quranic verses. In this lesson, you will learn why such verses cannot be reconciled by another verse and why we must turn to the prophetic Sunnah. The classic example of recitation behind the imam illustrates this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting verses
Locate the two Quranic verses that appear to contradict each other on the same issue. In the example, one verse commands general recitation (al-Muzzammil 73:20) and the other commands silent listening (al-A'raf 7:204).
Ensure the conflict is genuine and not resolvable by takhṣīṣ.
2
Apply the rule 'idhā ta'āradā tasāqaṭā'
State that when two verses conflict, they both drop (tasāqaṭā) because neither can be preferred over the other by another verse. The principle of kathrat al-ulā (preferring the more frequent) does not apply here.
This is a specific Hanafi rule for equal conflicting texts.
3
Resort to the Sunnah
Since the verses drop, the mujtahid must look to the Sunnah (hadith) for the ruling. In the example, the hadith 'Man kāna lahu imām...' is cited.
The Sunnah is the second primary source.
4
Derive the fiqh ruling from the hadith
From the hadith, the ruling is that the imam's recitation suffices for the follower; hence recitation behind the imam is not required (or is prohibited).
This ruling becomes the operative law.

Ruling on contradiction: In its ruling there is detail; it will be seen which evidences are in contradiction, i.e., what is the status of the evidences. If first the contradiction occurs between two verses, its solution will be sought in the Sunnah of the Messenger. Because when two verses contradict each other, they both drop (idha ta'āradā tasāqaṭā). Now preference cannot be given by means of a third verse because here the principle of preferring the more frequent (kathrat ulā) cannot apply; inevitably one turns to hadith and the Sunnah of the Messenger. Example: In the Qur'an regarding recitation there is one ruling: 'So recite what is easy of the Qur'an' and another ruling: 'And when the Qur'an is recited, listen to it and be silent.' The generality (umum) of the first verse demands that recitation be performed even behind the imam, while the specificity (khusus) of the second verse prevents that. Therefore, concerning recitation behind the imam, we turn to the Sunnah of the Messenger, and the saying of the Prophet ﷺ: 'Whoever has an imam, the recitation of the imam is his recitation.' Thus recitation behind the imam is prohibited. If contradiction occurs between two sunnahs, then one turns to the sayings and reports of the Companions (رضي الله عنهم). After that, contradiction is removed through qiyas. The text of the matn is: 'al-masīru ilā aqwāl al-ṣaḥābah radiyallāhu anhum aw al-qiyās.' The letter 'أ' comes for uncertainty and option, meaning it is optional to remove the contradiction occurring in the sunnah through the sayings of the Companions or through qiyas. Here also the author (matn) followed 'Allāmah Fakhr al-Islām al-Bazdawī. Its rationale is that whichever of the sayings of the Companions and qiyas is prevalent (rā'ij) will be given precedence. In this regard, differing opinions are listed. First opinion: The prevalent will be considered, i.e., in the matter where this contradiction occurs, it will be seen whether the sayings of the Companions are prevalent or qiyas; whichever is prevalent, the contradiction will be removed through it. Second opinion: The sayings of the Companions are always and in every matter given precedence over qiyas; in the presence of the sayings of the Companions, one does not turn to qiyas. Third opinion: Some have reconciled, saying that one looks at whether this matter is subject to qiyas (mudrak bil-qiyās) or not. If it is subject to qiyas, then qiyas prevails; if not subject to qiyas, then the sayings of the Companions are considered preponderant, because inevitably the Companions have heard from the Lawgiver (peace be upon him) regarding this matter, otherwise they cannot decide from themselves. Example: Regarding the number of ruku' in the eclipse prayer, two hadiths are contradictory: (1) 'A'ishah (رضي الله تعالى عنها) reported that the Prophet prayed the eclipse prayer with four ruku' and four prostrations. (2) The Prophet prayed the eclipse prayer as two rak'ahs, each rak'ah with one ruku' and two prostrations. The first hadith requires that in the eclipse prayer there are four ruku' and four prostrations, while the second hadith conveys the meaning like ordinary prayers. Therefore, we turn to qiyas, and since the eclipse prayer is also a prayer, we analogize it to ordinary prayers, and in ordinary prayers within two rak'ahs there are two ruku' and four prostrations. So in the eclipse prayer it will also be the same. Fifth opinion: Qiyas is preferred over the sayings of the Companions, whenever and wherever.

Commentary
This passage presents a fundamental rule in Hanafi uṣūl al-fiqh: how to resolve contradictions (ta'āruḍ) between legal evidences. The author begins by stating that the ruling depends on the nature of the conflicting evidences. If two Quranic verses conflict, they both drop (tasāqaṭā) and cannot be reconciled by a third verse, because the principle of preferring the more frequent (kathrat al-ulā) does not apply here. Instead, one must turn to the Sunnah. An example is given of recitation behind the imam: the general command to recite and the specific command to listen conflict, so we resort to the hadith 'the imam's recitation suffices for the follower.' This illustrates the hierarchy: Quran → Sunnah. Next, if two hadiths conflict, one looks to the sayings and practices of the Companions (aqwāl al-ṣaḥābah). If not resolved there, then qiyas (analogy) is used. The author then cites the matn: 'The resort is to the sayings of the Companions or qiyas,' indicating a choice. However, the author clarifies, following al-Bazdawī, that the prevalent (rā'ij) among the two in the particular issue should be given precedence. Three opinions are then discussed: (1) prefer whichever is prevalent; (2) always prefer the Companions' sayings; (3) a middle view that if the issue is subject to qiyas, then qiyas prevails; otherwise, the Companions' sayings prevail. An example of the eclipse prayer shows how qiyas is applied when the Companions' sayings are not clear. The fifth opinion mentioned at the end seems to reverse the preference, but it is not elaborated. The passage thus provides a structured approach to handling conflicting evidences, emphasizing the role of the Companions and qiyas in the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
تعارض (ta'āruḍ): Conflict or contradiction between two legal evidences that cannot be reconciled.
سنت (sunnah): The prophetic tradition, including sayings, actions, and approvals, used as the second primary source after Quran.
اقوال صحابہ (aqwāl al-ṣaḥābah): The statements and legal opinions of the Companions of the Prophet, considered a source when no clear text from Quran or Sunnah exists.
قیاس (qiyās): Analogical reasoning to extend a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a common effective cause (ʿillah).

This passage presents a fundamental rule in Hanafi uṣūl al-fiqh: how to resolve contradictions (ta'āruḍ) between legal evidences. The author begins by stating that the ruling depends on the nature of the conflicting evidences. If two Quranic verses conflict, they both drop (tasāqaṭā) and cannot be reconciled by a third verse, because the principle of preferring the more frequent (kathrat al-ulā) does not apply here. This illustrates the hierarchy: Quran → Sunnah. Next, if two hadiths conflict, one looks to the sayings and practices of the Companions (aqwāl al-ṣaḥābah). If not resolved there, then qiyas (analogy) is used. The author then cites the matn: 'The resort is to the sayings of the Companions or qiyas,' indicating a choice. However, the author clarifies, following al-Bazdawī, that the prevalent (rā'ij) among the two in the particular issue should be given precedence. Three opinions are then discussed: (1) prefer whichever is prevalent; (2) always prefer the Companions' sayings; (3) a middle view that if the issue is subject to qiyas, then qiyas prevails; otherwise, the Companions' sayings prevail. An example of the eclipse prayer shows how qiyas is applied when the Companions' sayings are not clear. The fifth opinion mentioned at the end seems to reverse the preference, but it is not elaborated. The passage thus provides a structured approach to handling conflicting evidences, emphasizing the role of the Companions and qiyas in the Hanafi school.

The Four Methods of Resolving Textual Conflict (Mukhallis 'an al-Mu'āraḍa)
Explaining the four categories of resolving textual conflict with elaboration on the first two.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four methods of resolving textual conflict in Hanafi usul and apply the first two: preference by proof and preference by ruling type.

Core Idea
Textual conflict is resolved not by rejecting evidence but by preference or contextual allocation.

In Hanafi usul, when two evidences appear contradictory, we do not automatically reject one. Instead, we apply a systematic method to resolve the conflict (mukhallis 'an al-mu'āraḍa). This lesson introduces the four categories and explains the first two in detail, including the classical example of yamīn ghamūs from the Qur'an.

Guided Path
1
List the Four Categories
Identify the four ways to resolve conflict: (1) by the strength of the proof (ḥujja), (2) by the type of ruling (ḥukm) – akhirah vs. dunya, (3) by the state (ḥāl) of the case, and (4) by difference in time (ikhtilāf al-zamān).
These are the standard Hanafi categories from the matn.
2
Explain Preference by Proof
When one evidence is stronger in transmission or clarity (e.g., mutawātir over aḥad, or naṣṣ over ẓāhir), we give it preference. This is straightforward and follows the hierarchy of proofs.
No conflict in meaning; the weaker is simply set aside.
3
Explain Preference by Ruling Type
When two strong proofs conflict, we may assign one to worldly matters (dunyā) and the other to otherworldly matters (ākhirah). Both are valid but apply to different domains.
This method acknowledges that a ruling can have multiple dimensions.
4
Apply to the Yamīn Ghamūs Example
Analyze the two Qur'anic verses: Surah Baqarah (2:225) and Surah Mā'idah (5:89). One seems to hold the swearer accountable, the other exempts thoughtless oaths. The Hanafi resolution: the Baqarah verse refers to otherworldly accountability (sin), the Mā'idah verse to worldly accountability (kaffāra). Thus no true conflict.
This illustrates the second method in action.

Reconciling Variant Readings: Hanafi Tawil of Quran 2:222
Explanation of tawil for variant Quranic readings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of takhsis to reconcile two variant Quranic readings and identify the resulting rulings.

Core Idea
Two Quranic readings can both be valid when each is specified to a different factual scenario.

This lesson explores how Hanafi usul reconciles what appears to be a contradiction between two canonical readings of the same Quranic verse. Rather than discarding one reading, the Hanafi method assigns each to a distinct circumstance, preserving both as equally valid revelations. This illustrates the principle of takhsis (specification) applied to variant readings (qira'at).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Readings
Recognize that the word 'yat-hurn' in Quran 2:222 has two valid recitations: with takhfif (light) and tashdid (emphatic). Each implies a different condition for permissible intercourse.
Takhfif: purification by blood cessation alone; Tashdid: purification through ghusl.
2
Understand the Implied Rulings
The light reading suggests ghusl is not necessary; the emphatic reading suggests ghusl is obligatory. This appears contradictory unless reconciled.
3
Apply Takhsis by Circumstance
Each reading is specified to a different factual scenario: completion of menstrual days (takhfif applies) vs. early cessation before ten days (tashdid applies, requiring ghusl to ensure purity).
The key is the principle of 'min qabl al-hal' – contextual specification.
4
Analyze the Objection and Response
The objection cites the following phrase 'fa-idha tatahharna' (with tashdid only) as contradicting the tawil. Two Hanafi responses: (1) it indicates istihbab (recommendation) not wujub; (2) 'tatahharna' can mean the same as 'tathurna' – a known linguistic phenomenon.
This shows how Hanafi usul defends a tawil through multiple valid arguments.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of Quran 2:222 regarding menstruation. The author explains a tawil (interpretation) based on the principle of 'min qabl al-hal' (specifying by circumstances). Two canonical variant readings of the word 'yat-hurn' (with tashfeed/lightening or tashdeed/emphasis) are reconciled by assigning each to a distinct factual scenario. The lightened reading applies when the menstrual period ends after its normal duration, so ghusl is not required before intercourse. The emphasized reading applies when bleeding stops early (before ten days), requiring ghusl to ensure purity. An objection is raised that the subsequent phrase 'fa-idha tatahharna' (with emphasis) contradicts this tawil. Two answers are given: (1) the subsequent phrase indicates istihbab (recommendation) rather than wujub (obligation), so it does not conflict; (2) the emphasized form can be interpreted as equivalent in meaning to the lightened form, a known linguistic phenomenon (e.g., tabayyana = bana). This demonstrates how Hanafi usul reconciles apparent textual contradictions by considering contextual circumstances and linguistic possibilities.

Three Categories of Negation in Hanafi Usul
Explains the three categories of negation and their legal weight

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories of negation (nafy) in Hanafi usul and determine when a negation is equivalent to affirmation (ithbat) based on its evidentiary basis.

Core Idea
Negation equals affirmation only when based on apparent evidence, not on presumption of continuity.

In Hanafi legal theory, not all negations carry the same weight. The founding imams differed on how to treat a statement that denies something exists. This lesson unpacks a key rule from the matn: negation is like affirmation in two specific cases but not in the third. Understanding these distinctions resolves many apparent conflicts between positive and negative evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
The passage lists three categories of negation: (1) from the genus of what is known by its proof, (2) from the doubtful category, and (3) not from either. Each has a different evidentiary basis.
Type 1 is based on clear evidence; type 2 involves uncertainty about the evidence; type 3 is based solely on istishab.
2
Determine the evidentiary basis
For each type, ascertain whether the negation rests on a clear proof (dalil), on a combination of possibilities, or on istishab (presumption of continuity).
Hanafis do not consider istishab as a binding proof, unlike Shafi'is.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule
For types 1 and 2, the negation is treated like affirmation. For type 3, it is not equivalent; the positive evidence is preferred.
This rule resolves conflicts between positive and negative evidence.
4
Understand conflict resolution
When both parties present evidence (type 2), resort to tarjih based on the narrator's status (Isa ibn Aban). When the negation is type 3, give priority to the positive evidence (Karkhi).
Tarjih is a key Hanafi tool for resolving contradictory evidence.

This exemplifies the Hanafi usul principle that istishab is not a binding proof, and that negation with evidence is treated like affirmation.

Applying the Rule of Negation: The Hadith of Burairah
Applies the rule of negation to the Burairah hadith, illustrating the Hanafi-Shafi'i difference

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the hadith of Burairah and contrast it with the Shafi'i position, applying the rule of negation to determine which evidence is preferred.

Core Idea
In the Burairah story, the negation that Mughith was still a slave is based on istishab, so it does not override the positive evidence of freedom.

The hadith of Burairah is a classic example where the rule of negation is applied. The dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is over whether the freed slave woman has an option to separate from her husband when he is free or only when he is a slave hinges on the strength of the evidence for his status at the time of choice. This lesson walks through the application of the rule.

Guided Path
1
Understand the facts of the Burairah hadith
Aisha (ra) bought and freed Burairah, whose husband Mughith was a slave. The Prophet (saw) gave her the choice to stay or separate. The dispute is whether at the time of choice Mughith was still a slave or had become free.
This happened before the revelation of the verse about freed slave women's option.
2
Identify the two positions
Imam Shafi'i held that Mughith remained a slave (negation of freedom), so the option applies only if the husband is a slave. Hanafis held that he was free (affirmation) based on evidence, so the option applies regardless.
The negation in Shafi'i's view is based on the apparent state of affairs.
3
Classify the negation used by Shafi'is
Apply the rule: the negation 'he did not become free' is not based on a clear proof but on istishab (presumption of continued slavery). Therefore, it falls into the third type (not equivalent to affirmation).
Hanafis do not consider istishab as a binding proof.
4
Apply the Hanafi analysis
Since the negation is from the third type, the positive evidence (that he was freed) is preferred. Thus, Burairah had the option even if her husband was free.
This follows Imam al-Karkhi's principle of giving precedence to positive evidence over negative based on istishab.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: According to Shafi'i, the option is conditional; according to Hanafis, it is unconditional. This affects many cases where a slave woman is freed and then marries a free man.
The ruling has implications for marriage contracts and options.

Preferring the Positive Report Over the Negative in Conflicting Narrations
Explains rule of preferring positive over negative report with three examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for preferring the positive report (muthbit) over the negative (nafi), apply it to the three examples given (Mughith, Maymunah, water/food), and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-position.

Core Idea
A positive report (muthbit) is preferred because it is based on evidence; the negative (nafi) only restates the original state.

When two narrations conflict—one affirming an additional matter (muthbit) and one denying it (nafi)—Hanafi usul gives priority to the positive, provided it rests on evidence. This lesson unpacks that rule through three classic examples: the status of Mughith, the Prophet's marriage to Maymunah, and the purity of water and food. You'll learn the rationale, compare with the Shafi'i view, and see how the commentator refines the matn's classification.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish muthbit from nafi
Identify which report affirms an additional matter (muthbit) and which merely denies change (nafi). In each example, determine the original state and which report breaks from it.

2
Grasp the rule and its rationale
The positive report is preferred because the reporter must have evidence for the change; the negative reporter relies only on the original state (istishab). Hanafis hold that a claim of change requires proof, while mere negation adds nothing new.
This is a principle of tarjih by external factor (dalil).
3
Apply to Mughith and Maymunah
Work through each example: identify the reports, classify them as muthbit or nafi, and apply the preference rule. Note the Shafi'i objection and the Hanafi response, especially narrator superiority in the Maymunah case.

4
Analyze water/food example
Here the matn classified both as 'known by evidence', but the commentator argues they belong to 'ambiguous matters'. Understand why: both reports (purity vs impurity) could potentially be evidence-based, leading to true conflict, and then the original state is acted upon until evidence resolves.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary from a Hanafi usul text, explaining the principle of preference (tarjih) when a positive report (muthbit) and a negative report (nafi) conflict. The commentator presents three examples to illustrate the rule that the positive report is given priority because it is based on evidence, while the negative report only affirms the original state.

The first example concerns the freedom or slavery of Sayyidina Mughith. The principle is that freedom is the original state in Dar al-Islam, and slavery is incidental. Narrators agree he was originally a slave, but a report states he is free. The Shafi'is argue the negative report (he is a slave) is based on the original state and thus preferred. However, Hanafis prefer the positive report (he is free) because the reporter must have had evidence for this change from the original state. The negative report merely relies on the original state and does not contradict the positive report with evidence.

The second example is the Prophet's marriage to Maymunah while in ihram. There is conflict: one report says he married while muhrim (negative regarding permissibility for him), another says he married while halal (positive). All agree he was in ihram before marriage. The Shafi'is argue that since a muhrim cannot marry, the Prophet must have broken his ihram, so the negative report (he was muhrim at marriage) is incorrect. Hanafis hold that marriage in ihram is permissible for the Prophet, so he remained muhrim. The Hanafi preference is based on the superiority of the narrator (Ibn Abbas) over the other (Yazid), and the principle that the positive report (he was muhrim) is supported by evidence, while the negative (he was halal) is a negation.

The third example deals with water purity and food permissibility. The matn classified them as 'what is known by its evidence' (ma yu'rafu bi-dalilihi). The commentator considers this lenient; it should be 'what is ambiguous' (mamma yashtabihu haluhu). The original state is purity/permissibility. A report of impurity/prohibition is positive (claiming a change), so it is preferred if based on evidence. If both reports are based on evidence, then conflict arises, and the original state is acted upon until evidence resolves it.

Overall, the commentator emphasizes that in Hanafi usul, when a positive report conflicts with a negative, we prefer the positive because it relies on evidence beyond the original state, whereas the negative merely restates the original state.

نافی (nafi): A report that merely denies the additional matter and affirms the original state (e.g., slavery, being in ihram, purity of water).
تعارض (ta'arud): Conflict between two reports that are equal in strength and cannot be reconciled.

Bayan al-Taqrir: Confirmatory Clarification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Bayan al-Taqrir, identify its linguistic function, and apply it to Quranic examples to show how it removes the possibility of metaphor and specification.

Core Idea
Bayan al-Taqrir uses additional words to block metaphorical or specific interpretations from the original text.

When a Quranic or Sunnah text is clear but could be misinterpreted as metaphorical or specific, Allah or the Prophet sometimes adds words to confirm the literal meaning. This type of clarification, called Bayan al-Taqrir, is crucial for ensuring the intended meaning is understood. In this lesson, we will analyze its definition and see it in action through two Quranic examples.

The key is that it adds words, not just repeats.

Example: The saying of Allah Most High: 'And no bird flies with its wings' (6:38). Here there was possibility of metaphor in 'bird' that anything fast-moving is a bird, but 'flies with its wings' removes the possibility of metaphor. Example: The saying of Allah Most High: 'Then all the angels prostrated together' (15:30). Here there was possibility of specification, which 'all together' removed. (2) Bayan al-Tafsir: Explaining the meaning and application of mujmal, mushtarak, khafi, and mushkil. Example: 'And establish prayer' (2:43) is mujmal, whose explanation came from hadiths that prayer has such-and-such pillars and how to pray, and likewise 'and give zakat'. Example: 'And divorced women should wait for three quru'' (2:228) where quru' is a mushtarak word between menstruation and purity. Its detail became known from 'the divorce of a slave woman is two divorces and her waiting period is two menstruations'.

Bayan al-Tafsir: Explanatory Clarification
Definition and examples of Bayan al-Tafsir from Quran and Sunnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Bayan al-Tafsir, list the types of ambiguous texts it clarifies (mujmal, mushtarak, khafi, mushkil), and apply it to examples from Quran and Sunnah.

Core Idea
Bayan al-Tafsir explains the meaning of texts that are ambiguous or multivalent, making them actionable.

Some Quranic and prophetic statements are brief, ambiguous, or contain words with multiple meanings. Bayan al-Tafsir is the type of clarification that unpacks these texts, detailing their application. This lesson explores its definition and two key examples: the general command to pray (mujmal) and the homonym 'quru'' (mushtarak).

Guided Path
1
Define Bayan al-Tafsir
Read the Arabic definition: it is explaining the meaning and application (mafhūm and miṣdāq) of mujmal, mushtarak, khafi, and mushkil texts.
It covers four types of ambiguous texts.
2
Understand what 'mujmal' means
A mujmal text is brief and requires external explanation. The example 'wa aqīmū aṣ-ṣalāh' is mujmal; the Sunnah explains its pillars and details.
Mujmal cannot be acted upon without bayan.
3
Analyze the first example
The verse 'wa aqīmū aṣ-ṣalāh' orders prayer but doesn't specify how. The Prophet's hadiths (bayan) detail the actions, recitations, and conditions. This is Bayan al-Tafsir.
The bayan comes from the Sunnah, showing the role of hadith.
4
Analyze the second example
In verse 2:228, 'quru'' is a mushtarak (homonym) meaning both menstruation and purity. The Sunnah clarifies it means menstruation for a free woman, but for a slave woman it means two menstruations (from other hadith).
The example 'ṭalāq al-amah ṭalqatān wa ‘iddatuhā ḥayḍatān' specifies the meaning.
