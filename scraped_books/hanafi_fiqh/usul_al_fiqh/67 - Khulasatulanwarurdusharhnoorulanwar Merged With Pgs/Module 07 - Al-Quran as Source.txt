Context of Praise Creates Specificity: Interpreting 'Khuluq 'Azim'
Analyzing the Quranic phrase 'khuluq 'azim' with three scholarly opinions and an usul objection-response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a quality mentioned in a context of praise becomes specific to the praised person (al-madh yufid al-ikhtisas) and identify when such specificity is implied in Quranic and hadith texts.

Core Idea
Praise context turns a general attribute into an exclusive one.

This passage analyzes the Quranic phrase 'khuluq 'azim' (great character) from Surah Al-Qalam. Three scholarly opinions are given, followed by an objection: the author's statement seems to imply exclusivity, but the verse itself does not specify exclusivity. The response provides a key usul principle: when a quality is mentioned in praise of someone, it becomes specific to that person. This lesson unpacks that principle and its application in textual interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three interpretations
Read the passage and note the three opinions on the meaning of 'khuluq 'azim'. The second opinion defines it as generosity and devotion; the third defines it as maintaining ties, forgiving, and doing good (from a hadith).
The first opinion is not fully quoted but referenced.
2
Understand the objection and its basis
The objection claims that the author's wording implies exclusivity (ikhtisas) of the attribute to the Prophet, but the verse itself (Quran 68:4) does not state exclusivity. This is a challenge to the suitability (munasaba) of the reference.
The objection assumes that the text is general ('amm) and cannot be restricted without evidence.
3
Analyze the response: the principle of praise context
The response states that when a quality is mentioned in a context of praise (maqam al-madh), it automatically becomes specific to the one being praised. This is an established principle in Hanafi usul: al-madh yufid al-ikhtisas.
This principle overrides the default generality ('umum) of the expression.
4
Extract the usul rule
The rule: 'If a quality is cited in praise of someone, it is understood as exclusive to that person.' This applies even if the wording is general, because the context of praise provides a qarina (contextual indicator) that restricts the meaning.
This is a form of takhsis bi al-siyaq (specification by context).
5
Apply the principle to a new example
Consider the statement: 'Zayd is a person who stays awake at night.' In a context of praise, this means that staying awake at night is a distinctive trait of Zayd, not that everyone who stays awake is praised. Apply this to other Quranic verses where a quality is mentioned in praise.
The principle helps resolve apparent conflicts between general wording and specific reference.

Meaning of 'Aal' in Salawat and Its Inclusivity
Explaining the referent of 'aal' in the phrase 'wa 'ala aalihi' with three scholarly views

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify three scholarly views on the referent of 'aal' in the salawat and apply the Hanafi cautious view to determine inclusion of Banu Hashim.

Core Idea
The term 'aal' in salawat is interpretively flexible — it may include wives, children, or all pious believers, but the Hanafi cautious view includes all of Banu Hashim.

This passage examines a seemingly minor phrase 'wa 'ala aalihi' that carries significant implications for the salawat we recite daily. The Hanafi school has a nuanced position that balances textual evidence with practical caution. Understanding this debate refines our practice of sending blessings upon the Prophet (peace be upon him).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Interpretations
Locate the three statements (aqwal) about the referent of 'aal' in the passage: wives, children, and all pious believers.
The first two are specific (khass), the third is general (amm).
2
Distinguish Specific from General
Note that the commentator considers the third view 'more appropriate' due to its generality. This reflects the usul principle that a general term covers all instances unless specified.
The usul maxim: al-amm yufidu al-istiqhraq.
3
Examine the Hanafi Cautious View
The text adds a cautious position that includes all descendants of Abbas and Abdul Muttalib (Banu Hashim). The dalil is the hadith prohibiting zakat for Ahl al-Bayt, which defines them practically.
This view is based on the zakat hadith, not just a linguistic argument.
4
Evaluate the Objection and Answer
Read the objection that the author should have mentioned the Companions. Then see the response: the author's Hanafi orthodoxy is not in question; the omission is due to the focus on Ahl al-Bayt.
This illustrates adab in scholarly discourse: defending intention, not attacking.
5
Apply the Tarjih
Determine the mufta bihi: the cautious view including Banu Hashim is relied upon. This affects how we recite salawat and understand the scope of blessings.
The third (general) view is considered more appropriate, but the cautious view is the operative Hanafi position.

The Three Sources of Shariah and the Scope of al-Kitab
Enumeration of the three primary sources and clarification of the first.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to enumerate the three primary sources of Shariah according to the Hanafi school and explain why only the ayat al-ahkam are considered the source for legal rulings.

Core Idea
Not every Quranic verse is a source of law; only about 500 ayat al-ahkam are directly used for legal rulings.

This lesson establishes the foundational sources of Islamic law. The passage clarifies that the principles (usul) of Shariah are three: the Quran, the Sunnah, and Ijma. It then delves into the first source, al-Kitab, explaining that legal rulings are derived from only a specific subset of Quranic verses—the ayat al-ahkam—and not the entire Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three sources
List the three primary sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) as stated in the passage. Note that in Hanafi usul, Qiyas is often added as a fourth source.
The passage only mentions three, but the full Hanafi list includes Qiyas.
2
Understand 'usul al-shar'' vs 'usul al-fiqh'
Consider why the author used 'shar'' instead of 'fiqh'. The answer: these principles apply to both fiqh and kalam (theology).
3
Define al-Kitab in this context
Al-Kitab refers to the Quran, but specifically the verses that contain legal rulings (ayat al-ahkam), which number about 500.
Other verses address stories, warnings, and theological points.
4
Apply the distinction
When deriving a ruling, first determine if the Quranic verse in question is among the ayat al-ahkam. If not, look to other sources (Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas).
For example, the verse on inheritance (4:11) is an ayah al-ahkam; the verse on the creation of the heavens (2:164) is not.

The Rational Enumeration of the Four Usul
Logical enumeration of the four usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi division of usul al-shariah and classify any given source of evidence into one of the four usul.

Core Idea
All legal evidence divides into revelation or non-revelation; revelation into Quran or Sunnah; non-revelation into ijma or qiyas.

This passage lays out the foundational Hanafi taxonomy of the primary sources of Islamic law. Understanding this rational division is essential for correctly applying the rules of interpretation (usul al-fiqh) and for recognising how other commonly cited sources—such as istihsan or customary practice—are subsumed under the four usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching dichotomy
The text divides all legal evidence (dalil) into two primary categories: revelation (wahy) and non-revelation. Memorise this as the first level.
2
Subdivide revelation
Revelation is further divided into recited (wahy matlu = Quran) and non-recited (ghayr matlu = Sunnah). Note that both are wahy, but only the Quran is recited in worship.
3
Subdivide non-revelation
Non-revelation sources are divided based on consensus: if it is the statement of the whole (i.e., the entire ummah), it is ijma; if it is the statement of a part, it is qiyas (analogy).
4
Understand the objection and response
The objection claims other sources (previous laws, urf, istihsan) are missing. The response shows they are subsumed: previous laws are confirmed by Quran/Sunnah, urf is akin to ijma (action of the whole), istihsan is a type of qiyas (hidden analogy).

The Second and Third Attributes Defining the Quran

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Quran is defined as the mutawatir transmission without doubt, preserved in the codices of the seven reciters.

Thief's Liability: Natural vs Deliberate Destruction – Hanafi & Shafi'i Views

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on a thief's liability for stolen property that is destroyed, distinguish between halak and ihlak, and identify the usul principles underlying the dispute with Imam Shafi'i.

Core Idea
A thief is liable only if he deliberately destroys the stolen property; natural destruction nullifies the owner's inviolability ('ismat) before liability arises.

In this passage, we encounter a nuanced dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Shafi'i regarding a thief's obligation to compensate for stolen property that has been destroyed. The key is understanding the concept of 'ismat (inviolability) and how it transfers at the moment of theft. The Hanafi school makes a critical distinction between natural destruction (halak) and deliberate destruction (ihlak). This lesson unpacks the arguments and their practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Note that the passage distinguishes between natural destruction (هلاک, halak) and deliberate destruction by the thief (إهلاك, ihlak). The dispute centers on whether liability arises in each case.
Halak includes acts of God; ihlak is the thief's own action.
2
Understand the concept of 'ismat
The Hanafi argument relies on the idea that property has two attributes: 'ismat (inviolability) and milk (ownership). At the moment of theft, 'ismat is voided from the owner and transferred to the thief only for deliberate destruction. Natural destruction does not create liability because the 'ismat is already lifted from the owner and not yet attached to the thief in that sense.
This is a key usul principle: the transfer of 'ismat affects the legal attribution of responsibility.
3

Imam Shafi'i holds that the thief is liable regardless of how the property perished, citing the Quranic verse 'جزاء بما كسبا' (recompense for what they have earned). He argues that the thief's unlawful possession triggers full responsibility for the property.
The opposing dalil is a general implication from the Quranic text.
4
Examine the Hanafi response
Imam Abu Hanifa responds by differentiating between natural destruction (no liability) and deliberate destruction (liability). This is based on a narration from Hasan ibn Ziyad that Imam Abu Hanifa held deliberate destruction creates liability. The distinction is rooted in the transfer of 'ismat.
The Hanafi position is more nuanced, using a combination of textual interpretation and legal reasoning (qiyas).
5
Apply to a practical example
Consider a stolen sheep that dies naturally before recovery. Under Hanafi, no compensation; under Shafi'i, the thief must pay its value. This illustrates the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The Hanafi proof for 'isma transfer rests on 'jaza', not 'faqta'u'.

In this passage, you will encounter a classic usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over the interpretation of the theft verse (Quran 5:38). The dispute centers on whether the cutting of the hand also removes the need for financial compensation (daman) when the stolen property is destroyed. The Hanafi argument hinges on a single word: 'jaza'. Let's walk through the reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central issue
The Shafi'i school holds that if stolen property is destroyed, the thief must pay compensation. The Hanafi school holds that no compensation is due because the property's inviolability ('isma) transfers to Allah at the moment of theft. The dispute is whether the Quranic verse (5:38) supports this transfer.
2
Locate the Shafi'i objection
The Shafi'i objection states that the word 'faqta'u' (cut off) does not imply any transfer of 'isma. Therefore, claiming that 'isma transfers is an addition to the Quranic text.
Imam Shafi'i's objection is textualist: the command to cut says nothing about 'isma.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that they do not rely on 'faqta'u' for the transfer of 'isma. Instead, they rely on the word 'jaza' (recompense) in the same verse. The absolute form of 'jaza' in the context of punishments indicates a punishment due to Allah's right, which implies violation of Allah's 'isma.
The Hanafi argument uses three linguistic points: (1) 'jaza' as a masdar li al-ta'lil, (2) the absolute form implies the perfect recompense, (3) the root meaning 'to suffice'.
4

The Hanafis apply the usul principle that an absolute term (mutlaq) is carried to its perfect individual (al-fard al-kamil). Since 'jaza' is absolute, it means that the amputation itself is the complete recompense, leaving no need for additional compensation.
This principle is a key tool in Hanafi derivation from the Quran.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if the stolen property is destroyed, the Hanafi school does not require the thief to pay its value, while the Shafi'i school does. However, if the property still exists, both schools agree it must be returned to the owner because ownership (milk) remains.
This shows how a difference in usul (linguistic interpretation) leads to a different fiqh ruling.

The Cause of Qada: Hanafi vs. Shafi'i
Comparison of Hanafi and Shafi'i views on the cause of qada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the cause of qada and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the i'tikaf example.

Core Idea
For Hanafis, the same command obligates both ada and qada; Shafi'is require a new cause for qada.

This passage presents a fundamental usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: what makes qada (making up missed worship) obligatory? The Hanafi majority holds that the same divine command that obligates the original act also obligates its qada, while the Shafi'i school argues that each act of qada requires its own specific textual cause. Understanding this dispute is key to grasping how each school approaches the extension of rulings to new cases.

Time as Measure: No Specific Intention Needed
Ruling on time as measure in usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when time is a measure (miyar), intention of specification is not required, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Time as measure: the very act fills the time, so no need to specify—the time determines it.

In this passage, we examine the second category of time-bound commands in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: when time acts as a measure (miyar) and cause (sabab) for the obligation. The key example is fasting in Ramadan. Understanding this principle clarifies why the Hanafi school does not require a specific intention for the Ramadan fast and how it differs from prayer. We will also explore the dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i and an intra-Hanafi disagreement regarding travelers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the second type of time-bound command
Locate the phrase 'au yakunu miyaran lahu wa sababan li wujubihi' and understand that time here is both a measure (determining the extent of the act) and a cause (making the act obligatory).
Contrast with the first type where time is a container (zarf).
2
Understand the consequence of time as measure
When time is a measure, no other act can be performed in that time slot. The hadith 'idha insalakha al-sha'banu fala sawma illa min ramadan' proves that only the Ramadan fast is valid in Ramadan.
This negates the possibility of supererogatory or make-up fasts during Ramadan.
3
Derive the ruling on intention
Because time itself determines the obligation, intention of specification (niyyat al-ta'yin) is not required. Even if one mistakenly intends a nafl or qada fast, it counts as the Ramadan fast.
The phrase 'yusabu bi-mutlaq al-ism wa ma'a al-khata' fi al-wasf' encapsulates this.
4
Analyze the inter-madhhab dispute
Imam al-Shafi'i requires specification of intention, analogizing to prayer. The Hanafi response distinguishes prayer (time as container) from fasting (time as measure).
The thamarah al-khilaf: if one intends nafl in Ramadan, Shafi'i says it does not count as Ramadan; Hanafi says it does.
5
Examine the intra-Hanafi traveler exception
According to Abu Hanifa, a traveler may intend another wajib fast (e.g., qada) because Ramadan obligation is dropped. The Two Companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, holding that the traveler should take the concession not to fast.
This shows further usul application: the cause (sabab) being lifted affects the ruling.

Core Idea

Hanafi Classification of Oaths: Haqiqah and Dalil
A usul dispute on interpreting Quranic oaths through the lens of haqiqah and majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of haqiqah to distinguish between yamin ghamus and yamin mun'aqida and derive the differing rulings on kaffarah.

Core Idea
The word 'aqd' in the Quran is haqiqah for deliberate oaths, limiting expiation to yamin mun'aqida.

In this lesson, we examine a classic usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over the interpretation of two Quranic verses on oaths. At stake is whether a deliberate false oath (yamin ghamus) requires expiation (kaffarah) or only incurs sin. The Hanafi position rests on the principle that the word 'aqd' (as in 'categorized oaths') is used in its literal sense (haqiqah) for intentional oaths, thus restricting the ruling of Surah al-Ma'idah to mun'aqida only.

3
Analyze the linguistic operation

The Hanafi response hinges on this usul principle.
4

The Shafi'is interpret both verses as applying to all deliberate oaths. What is their basis?

(2) Yamin ghamus: swearing a deliberate false oath about a past matter to deceive someone. Its ruling: there is sin but no expiation. (3) Yamin mun'aqida: swearing with intention to do or not do something, then doing the opposite. Its ruling: both sin and expiation. The origin is that Allah mentioned the issue of oaths in two places in the Quran: first in Surah al-Baqarah: 'But He will take you to account for what your hearts have earned,' then in Surah al-Ma'idah: 'He will take you to account for your deliberate oaths.' Commentary
This passage presents a Hanafi usul dispute with Shafi'i on two Quranic interpretation issues. First, regarding oaths: the Prophet's school divides oaths into three types (laghw, ghamus, mun'aqida). The Shafi'i school equates all deliberate oaths requiring expiation, but Hanafis distinguish: the word 'aqd' in Surah al-Ma'idah is haqiqah for intentional (mun'aqida) oaths only, while 'kasb' in Surah al-Baqarah is broader and refers to sin, not expiation. This shows the usul principle that a word's haqiqah (literal usage) is primary, and general expressions ('amm) are interpreted in light of more specific ones. Second, for the marriage verse, Hanafis interpret 'nikah' as 'guardian' (wali) based on its linguistic meaning 'union'—the wali effects the union. Shafi'is take 'nikah' as the contract itself. The consequence: in Hanafi law, if a valid marriage (by a lawful wali) or an invalid one (by an unlawful wali) occurs, the prohibition of affinity (mahramiyyah) is established; Shafi'is limit it to the valid contract. This exemplifies how usul disagreements on haqiqah vs majaz produce different fiqh rulings.

Key Terms
یمین لغو (yamin laghw): An oath taken on a matter believed true or out of habit, without deliberate falsehood or intention.
یمین غموس (yamin ghamus): A deliberate false oath about a past event to deceive.
یمین منعقدہ (yamin mun'aqida): An oath to do or not do something, with deliberate intention, then broken.
حقیقت (haqiqah): Literal meaning of a word as originally placed.

The Meaning of Nikah: Haqiqah, Majaz, and Legal Consequences
A usul dispute on the meaning of 'nikah' in Quran 4:22: haqiqah vs. majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi interpretation of 'nikah' in Quran 4:22 as the guardian (wali) and derive the resulting ruling on the prohibition of affinity (musaharat).

Core Idea
In marriage law, 'nikah' is haqiqah for union through the guardian, not the contract.

This lesson continues our usul exploration with a second Quranic dispute: the meaning of 'nikah' in the verse prohibiting marrying wives of one's fathers (Surah al-Nisa 4:22). The Hanafi school interprets 'nikah' literally as 'union' effected by the guardian (wali), while the Shafi'i school takes it as the marriage contract itself. The difference has direct consequences for when the prohibition of affinity (musaharat) is established.

Guided Path
1
Understand the dispute
Identify the contested verse: 'Do not marry what your fathers married' (4:22). What exactly is prohibited? The key term is 'nikah'.
The word 'nikah' is polysemous; the usul question is which meaning is haqiqah.
2
Analyze the Hanafi interpretation
Hanafis take 'nikah' in its literal linguistic sense of 'union' (ضم). The union of spouses is effected through the guardian (wali). Therefore, the verse addresses the wali, prohibiting him from marrying a woman previously married by his father.
The wali is the subject of the prohibition.
3
Examine the Shafi'i alternative
Shafi'is interpret 'nikah' as the marriage contract itself. Thus the verse prohibits the contracts of the fathers, and the prohibition of affinity follows only from valid marriages.
This leads to a narrower scope of prohibited relationships.
4
Trace the usul basis
The Hanafi position relies on the principle that the linguistic haqiqah of a word takes precedence over a technical (shar'i) meaning, unless there is a clear qarinah (contextual clue). Here, no such clue exists.
Contrast: Shafi'is hold that the shar'i meaning (contract) becomes the haqiqah due to frequent usage.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: under Hanafi law, if a man marries a woman previously married (even invalidly) by his father, the prohibition of affinity (musaharat) is established because the wali's act of union occurred. Shafi'is require a valid contract for musaharat.
This affects mahram relationships in rulings like nikah and mahramiyyah.

Hanafi Interpretation of 'Aw' in the Ayah of Muharibeen
Explaining the Hanafi interpretation of أو in the punishment verse for bandits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that the particle 'aw' can denote specification (ta'dil) rather than choice, using Q. 5:33 and the supporting hadith, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf with the Maliki school.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, 'أو' does not always mean choice; context and Sunnah can redefine it as specification.

The verse (Q. 5:33) lists four punishments for highway robbers connected by 'أو'. The apparent meaning is that the ruler may choose any punishment. However, the Hanafi school argues that 'أو' here means 'بل' (but rather), indicating that each punishment is graded to a specific crime. This lesson explores the textual analysis and the proof from the hadith of Jibril that supports this interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic issue
The particle 'aw' in Arabic can have multiple meanings: ibaha (permission), takhyeer (choice), ta'dil (specification), etc. In Q.5:33, four punishments are listed with 'aw'. The apparent meaning is choice, but Hanafis argue for specification based on context and Sunnah.
Hanafi usul recognizes that particles can be repurposed when literal meaning leads to contradiction with stronger evidence.
2
Locate the Hanafi proof from Sunnah
The hadith of Jibril provides a detailed gradation: for killing+robbery = crucifixion; for killing only = killing; for robbery only = amputation; for intimidation only = exile. This shows each punishment is tied to a specific crime, not left to choice.
This hadith is cited in the source text as the key dalil for the Hanafi position.
3
Apply the usul principle
In Hanafi usul, when a particle's literal meaning leads to an absurdity or is contradicted by stronger evidence, it is reinterpreted. Here, the literal meaning of choice would imply all punishments are interchangeable, but the hadith shows gradation. Therefore, 'aw' is interpreted as ta'dil (specification).
This is an application of the principle that al-haqiqah tukhraj bi al-dalil (the literal meaning is abandoned for a contextual one with evidence).
4
Compare with Maliki position
Malik holds 'aw' as takhyeer (choice), giving the ruler discretion among the four punishments. The thamarah al-khilaf is that Hanafis require applying the specific punishment matching the crime, while Malikis permit choosing any punishment for any crime of hirabah.
Understanding the practical consequence (thamarah) is crucial for distinguishing the two schools.

The Authority of Textual Implication in Hanafi Usul

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Muqtada carries the same authority as explicit text unless they conflict; then explicit text prevails.

In Hanafi usul, not every ruling is explicitly stated in the text; some are necessarily implied (muqtada). These implied rulings are considered as binding as explicit ones, unless there is a conflict. This lesson unpacks this principle using the Quranic example of freeing a slave in expiation, where the general word 'raqabah' is restricted by muqtada to a believing owned slave.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
The passage states: 'what is established by muqtada is like what is established by dalalah al-nass, except when there is conflicting evidence.' This is the core rule: muqtada has the same authority as explicit text, but explicit text takes precedence in case of conflict.
Muqtada = textual implication; dalalah al-nass = explicit textual indication.
2
Locate the example
The author cites Quran 58:3: 'fa-tahriru raqabah' (then freeing a slave). This verse is the basis for the example of muqtada.
This verse is about expiation for zihar (a form of divorce).
3
Analyze the general term
The word 'raqabah' (slave) is general ('amm) in Arabic, encompassing any slave, regardless of ownership or faith. Literally, it would include slaves owned by others or non-Muslims.
In usul, 'amm indicates generality unless specified otherwise.
4

Shariah and reason dictate that freeing a slave for expiation requires: (a) ownership of the slave (one cannot free another's slave), and (b) the slave must be a believer (since expiation for a sin requires purification of faith). These conditions are not stated in the text but are necessarily implied (muqtada) for the act to be valid.
The muqtada is derived from the nature of the act and the purpose of expiation.
5

The restricted meaning—'a believing owned slave'—is considered as if it were explicitly stated. Thus, the ruling that expiation requires a believing owned slave has the same authority as an explicit textual command.
This illustrates the principle that muqtada is equivalent to dalalah al-nass except in cases of conflict.

Core Idea
Wāw does not force shared rulings; Hanafīs reject the inference that child prayer exemption implies zakat exemption.

In this lesson, we examine a fourth argument that the Hanafī author considers invalid but which is accepted by the Mālikī school. The argument hinges on the Arabic conjunction wāw: when two commands are joined by wāw, the Mālikīs claim they share the same legal ruling. They apply this to the famous verse commanding prayer and zakat. Your goal is to understand both the reasoning and the Hanafī rejection, which is based on a different reading of the wāw's function.

The issue is about the function of wāw in legal interpretation.

The Mālikīs argue that wāw indicates jam' (conjunction), which requires sharing of the ruling. They cite the verse 'Aqīmū al-ṣalāta wa ātū al-zakāta' as evidence. Since prayer is not obligatory on children by consensus, zakat on their wealth must also not be obligatory.
The proof relies on the assumption that wāw necessarily transmits the ruling.

The Hanafīs do not accept this inference. They hold that wāw does not imply shared ruling; each command is interpreted independently based on its own evidence. The author labels the Mālikī argument as 'fāsid' (invalid).
The Hanafīs have their own basis for making zakat obligatory on a child's wealth, such as istihsān.

The practical difference is: for the Mālikīs, zakat is not obligatory on a child's wealth; for the Hanafīs, it is obligatory (though the guardian pays).
This is a concrete example of how a subtle usul disagreement leads to different fiqh rulings.
5
Analyze the Weakness in the Mālikī Argument
The Hanafīs argue that wāw is a general conjunction and does not by itself enforce legal sharing. Counterexamples exist in the Quran where wāw joins commands with different rulings. The Mālikī deduction is therefore insufficient.

Types of Revelation (Wahi) in Hanafi Usul: Apparent and Hidden
Defining two main categories of revelation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Wahi Zahir and Wahi Batin, and state the Hanafi position that the Prophet's ijtihad is a form of revelation with divine approval.

Core Idea
Revelation in Islam includes both angelic transmission and prophetic ijtihad, the latter validated by divine correction.

In Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh, the concept of revelation (wahi) is broader than merely the Quran. This passage from Khulasat al-Anwar explains the two primary categories: apparent revelation through angels and hidden revelation through the Prophet's ijtihad. Understanding this classification is essential for grasping the Hanafi theory of legal sources and the role of ijtihad in the prophetic era.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
The passage divides wahi into Wahi Zahir (apparent) and Wahi Batin (hidden). Note the defining feature of each.
Wahi Zahir involves external means; Wahi Batin involves internal reasoning.
2
Break down Wahi Zahir
Wahi Zahir has three subcategories: (1) through angel's speech, (2) through angel's indication without speech, (3) direct inspiration (ilham) into the prophetic heart.
The first is the most common, e.g., Jibril bringing Quran.
3
Define Wahi Batin
Wahi Batin refers to rulings the Prophet deduced via ijtihad after reflecting on revealed commands. It is considered revelation because Allah corrects any error.
This shows the integration of reason and revelation.
4
Recognize the Hanafi justification
The Prophet was authorized to use ijtihad after the revelation period. If wrong, Allah sent Wahi Zahir to correct him; if correct, it is implicitly approved.
Thus, Wahi Batin is binding on the Prophet and the Ummah.
5
Appreciate the ikhtilaf
Some scholars deny Wahi Batin as revelation. The Hanafi school defends it based on the principle of divine correction.
This distinction affects how we understand the Prophet's actions as sources of law.

Commentary
This passage defines the types of Wahi (revelation) from the perspective of Hanafi usul. It begins by dividing revelation into two major categories: Wahi Zahir (apparent revelation) and Wahi Batin (hidden revelation). Apparent revelation comes through external means like an angel. Three subcategories are given: first, speech from an angel (like Jibril bringing Quran and Sunnah); second, an angel's indication without words, such as the Holy Spirit (Ruh al-Qudus) insinuating meaning into the Prophet's heart; third, direct inspiration (ilham) from Allah into the prophetic heart. Hidden revelation refers to the Prophet's own ijtihad after the revelation period ended. Some deny that ijtihad counts as revelation, but the Hanafis maintain that it does, with divine approval. If the Prophet's ijtihad is wrong, Allah corrects it through apparent revelation. Thus, hidden revelation is considered a subset of the general category of revelation.

Key Terms
وحی ظاہر (wahi zahir): Revelation that comes through external means like an angel or direct inspiration
وحی باطن (wahi batin): Revelation through ijtihad and inner inspiration, confirmed by divine approval
