The Four Aspects of Quranic Nazm: Methodology and Objections
Defining four aspects of nazm and addressing objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four aspects of Quranic nazm and explain why the majority of usul scholars omitted them, citing the Hanafi response.

Core Idea
The four aspects of nazm are a division of the names of Quranic types, not the Quran itself.

This passage introduces a distinctive Hanafi analytical framework for studying the textual divisions (قسام) of the Quran. It details four aspects (مواضع, معانی, ترتیب, أحکام) that can be applied to each type and then addresses objections from the majority of usul scholars who omitted this division. The passage also notes that the author of the text partially followed Imam Bazdawi but did not fully implement all four aspects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four aspects
Read the description of each aspect: positions (مواضع), meanings (معانی), order (ترتیب), and rulings (أحکام). Understand what each aspect covers: lexical material, technical definitions, conflict resolution, and legal outcomes.
The rulings aspect includes three categories: قطعي (definitive), ظني (speculative), and واجب التوقف (obligatory suspension).
2
Understand the calculation
The author multiplies 20 base types (from previous lessons) by 4 aspects to get 80 sub-types. Recognize that this is a combinatorial expansion for thorough analysis.
The 20 base types come from five divisions each with four types (e.g., عام، خاص, etc.).
3
Analyze the first objection
The majority of scholars did not mention this division. The author's response: it is a division of the names (اسامي) of the types, not the Quran itself, so the majority omitted it as extraneous to core usul.
This clarifies that the framework is meta-linguistic, not a direct Quranic division.
4
Analyze the second objection
Why did the author not follow the majority? Answer: he follows Imam Bazdawi in usul. Then a critique: the author himself did not fully apply all four aspects—he omitted 'positions' entirely and applied 'order' inconsistently.
This shows that even within the Hanafi school, there is divergence in implementing Bazdawi's method.

Validity of Divorce After Khula': Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Presenting Hanafi proof that divorce can occur after khula' using Quranic verses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of the letter 'fa' indicating sequence to derive the ruling that divorce after khula' is valid, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
The 'fa' in 'فإن طلقها' proves sequence – khula' is a divorce, not an annulment.

This passage delves into a classic usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. At issue is the legal nature of khula' (divorce initiated by the wife in exchange for compensation). The Hanafis argue that khula' is a type of divorce, so a subsequent divorce is valid. Their proof comes from the Quranic structure in Surah Al-Baqarah, 2:229-230, where the conjunction 'fa' in 'فإن طلقها' indicates that the third divorce occurs after the mention of khula'. This lesson guides you through the textual evidence, the opposing interpretation, and the Hanafi rebuttal.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the core disagreement
State the Hanafi and Shafi'i definitions of khula'. Recognize that the practical difference is whether a subsequent divorce is possible.
2
2. Locate the Hanafi Quranic proof
Read Quran 2:229-230: 'Divorce is twice...' then the khula' verse, then 'فإن طلقها...'. Note the order of revelation.
The Hanafis argue that the sequence implies a temporal order.
3
3. Understand the Shafi'i objection
The Shafi'is claim that the khula' verse is a parenthetical clause (جملة معترضة) and the third divorce clause relates back to 'Divorce is twice'.
This would mean that the third divorce is not after khula' but after the two divorces mentioned initially.
4
4. Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal using usul
Focus on the letter 'fa' (فا) which in Arabic grammar indicates sequence (تعقيب). The Hanafis argue that this linguistic feature overrides the parenthetical claim.
This is a key usul principle: the apparent meaning (zahir) of the text is given weight unless a stronger contextual indicator changes it.
5
5. Resolve the objection about four divorces
The Shafi'is object that if khula' is a divorce and then a third divorce follows, that makes three, plus the revocable ones? Actually the objection is it would allow four. The Hanafis answer that khula' is not an additional independent divorce; it is counted within the two revocable divorces mentioned in 'الطلاق مرتان'.
Thus no extra divorce is created.

Commentary
This passage presents the fifth subsidiary issue (tafri'ah) regarding the validity of divorce after khula'. The Hanafi position is that khula' is a type of divorce, not a dissolution of marriage (faskh), so a subsequent divorce is valid. The Shafi'i position is that khula' annuls the marriage, so divorce cannot occur afterward. The Hanafi proof comes from the Quranic verses in Surah Al-Baqarah (2:229-230): The verse 'Divorce is twice' (الطلاق مرتان) establishes that revocable divorces are limited to two. Then the khula' verse (فإن خفتم ألا يقيما حدود الله) appears, and then the third divorce is mentioned (فإن طلقها فلا تحل له). The Hanafis argue that the sequence (indicated by the conjunction 'fa') shows that the third divorce occurs after khula', proving that khula' does not prevent subsequent divorce. They also use the letter 'fa' to indicate sequence (تعقيب). The Shafi'i counter that the khula' mention is a parenthetical clause (جملة معترضة) and the third divorce clause relates back to 'Divorce is twice'. The Hanafi response emphasizes that the 'fa' indicates a sequential relationship, so the third divorce is after khula'. Finally, an objection that this would allow four divorces is answered: khula' is not an additional independent divorce but is subsumed within the two revocable divorces (i.e., khula' itself counts as one of the two). The passage reflects Hanafi usul in interpreting Quranic textual structure and conjunctions.

Key Terms
خلع (Khul‘): Divorce at the wife's request in exchange for a ransom (fidyah); considered a type of divorce in Hanafi fiqh.
فا (Fā’): The Arabic letter 'fa' used for sequence (تعقيب) and causation; here it shows that the third divorce follows khula'.
جملة معترضة (Jumlat Mu‘taridah): A parenthetical clause that interrupts the main flow of the sentence and is not necessarily sequential.

Definition and Ruling of Muawwal
Defines muawwal and its ruling, addresses classification objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define muawwal, explain the process of tarjih from mushtarak, and state its hukm as zanni with the consequence that its denier is not a disbeliever.

Core Idea
Muawwal is a zanni text meaning derived by ijtihad from a mushtarak word; acting upon it is obligatory but its denier is not a disbeliever.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi usul al-fiqh concept of muawwal—a meaning obtained when a mujtahid gives preference to one interpretation of a multi-sense word (mushtarak) based on predominant opinion. The passage defines muawwal, clarifies its process (ta'wil), and addresses a key objection about its classification within types of texts. It then explains its ruling (hukm) and why it is considered zanni (presumptive) rather than qat'i (definitive).

Guided Path
1
Define mushtarak and muawwal
Identify that muawwal is a meaning derived from a mushtarak (word with multiple senses) after giving preference to one aspect using predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'i).
Mushtarak is the raw material; muawwal is the result of ijtihad.
2
Understand the process of tarjih
Note that tarjih (preference) can occur via various means: khabar wahid, qiyas, deep reflection on the word, or context. The act of tarjih is called ta'wil, and the resulting meaning is muawwal.
Ta'wil is the human action; muawwal is the attributed meaning.
3
Analyze the objection and answer
Examine the objection: if muawwal comes from ta'wil (a human act), why is it counted among types of text? The answer: after ta'wil, the ruling is attributed to the text itself, so it is part of the text divisions.
This clarifies that muawwal retains textual authority even though derived through ijtihad.
4
Recognize the hukm and its zanni nature
The ruling of muawwal is that acting upon it is obligatory, but it is zanni because it involves possibility of error. Evidence: the hadith that a mujtahid is rewarded even if wrong.
The zanni nature has practical benefit: deniers are not declared disbelievers.
5
Apply to a fiqh example
Consider the mushtarak word 'qur'' (which can mean purity or menstruation). A mujtahid uses tarjih to prefer one meaning, producing a muawwal. The resulting ruling (e.g., when 'iddah ends) is obligatory to follow but still zanni.
This shows how muawwal operates in real legal derivation.

In this lesson, we explore the concept of mujmal (ambiguous expression) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. 1
State the definition of mujmal
Read the source text's definition: 'Mujmal is that in which meanings are crowded and the intended meaning is confused such that it cannot be grasped from the expression alone.' Internalise that ijmal arises from either crowding of meanings or unfamiliarity of the word.
The definition emphasises that the ambiguity is inherent in the expression, not in the listener's ignorance.
2
Identify the ruling of mujmal
The ruling (hukm) has two parts: (1) believe that the intended meaning is the truth, and (2) suspend judgment (tawaqquf) until clarification (bayan) arrives. This prevents both denial and premature interpretation.
Tawaqquf does not mean rejecting the text; it means awaiting authoritative explanation.
3
Analyse the example of salah
Salah lexically means 'supplication', but in the Shari'ah it refers to a specific set of acts. The Prophet ﷺ provided comprehensive clarification by demonstrating the prayer from start to finish. Then, by seeking and reflection, the scholars identified the fard and wajib components.
This shows that bayan can be by action, not just words.
4
Analyse the example of zakah
Zakah lexically means 'growth', but the Prophet ﷺ clarified it by saying 'Take from your wealth…' and by specifying the nisab and the passing of a year. This illustrates that clarification may also identify conditions and causes.
The text mentions that seeking reveals the 'illa (cause) and shart (condition).
5
Distinguish between complete and partial clarification
For salah and zakah, the clarification is sufficient (kafi) – it fully specifies the intended meaning. For riba, the Prophet ﷺ gave a hadith about equal exchange, but the 'illa (e.g., weight, foodstuff) remained disputed among scholars. Thus clarification can be incomplete, requiring further ijtihad.
This distinction affects how later jurists derive rulings from these texts.

Definition and Ruling of Mutashabih and the Dispute on Its Knowledge
Defines mutashabih, states its ruling, and presents the dispute over who knows its interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mutashabih, state its ruling, and explain the Hanafi position on whether the rasikhun know its ta'wil, citing Quran 3:7.

Core Idea
Mutashabih is a term whose intended meaning is hopeless; its ruling is to believe it is true without claiming interpretation.

In this lesson, we examine the usul term 'mutashabih'—Quranic verses whose intended meaning is beyond human access. The Hanafi school holds that we must affirm their truth but refrain from claiming certain knowledge of their interpretation. This position is derived from Quran 3:7 and is contrasted with the view of Imam al-Shafi'i and the Mu'tazila. We also explore the two types of mutashabih and the reasoning behind the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition and ruling of mutashabih
Read the opening definition: mutashabih is a word for which hope of knowing its intended meaning is cut off. Its ruling is to believe it is true before the afterlife.
Example: the huruf muqatta'at (disconnected letters) at the start of some surahs.
2
Understand the two types of mutashabih
The passage divides mutashabih into mutashabih al-ma'na (meaning itself unknown) and mutashabih al-murad (meaning known but divine intent unknown). Note the distinction.
Mutashabih al-ma'na: e.g., huruf muqatta'at. Mutashabih al-murad: e.g., verses about Allah's attributes.
3
Analyze the Hanafi reading of Quran 3:7
The Hanafi position stops at 'except Allah' (waqf), making 'and those firmly grounded in knowledge' the start of a new sentence describing their response. This is supported by a variant recitation without the conjunction 'wa'.
Thus the rasikhun do not know the ta'wil; they only affirm it.
4
Compare with the Shafi'i/Mu'tazila reading
Imam al-Shafi'i and the Mu'tazila read the verse with a conjunction, making the rasikhun included in those who know the ta'wil. They believe scholars can interpret mutashabihat definitively.
The practical consequence (thamarah): for Hanafis, definitive interpretation is impermissible; for Shafi'is, it is permissible for qualified scholars.
5
Evaluate the 'verbal dispute' claim
Some scholars call this a verbal dispute because both sides agree the rasikhun have knowledge—but Hanafis say it is non-certain (zanni) while Shafi'is say it is certain (qat'i). Reflect on whether this distinction is substantive.
The author of the text implicitly sides with the Hanafi view by asserting the denial of certain knowledge.

Commentary
This passage defines the usul term 'mutashabih' and explains its ruling and the classical dispute about whether the rasikhun (those firmly grounded in knowledge) know its ta'wil (interpretation). The author follows the Hanafi position. First, the definition: mutashabih is a linguistic term for something whose intended meaning is inaccessible to human reason; the example given is the huruf muqatta'at at the start of some Quranic chapters. The basis of the dispute is the verse: 'And none knows its interpretation except Allah, and those firmly grounded in knowledge say: We believe in it' (Quran 3:7). Definition and Ruling of Sarih (Explicit) Words
Defining sarih as words with clear meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a sarih word and explain its legal effect without requiring intention, using the Hanafi usul framework.

Core Idea
A sarih word binds by its clarity—intention does not matter.

This lesson introduces the usul term 'sarih' (explicit). In Hanafi usul, when a word is so clear that its meaning is unmistakable—whether literal or metaphorical—the shari' ruling attaches to the utterance itself, regardless of the speaker's inner intention. The passage illustrates this with examples from divorce and manumission, and demonstrates the practical consequence: even a mistaken utterance of a sarih word produces its legal effect.

Guided Path
1
Define sarih
Identify the core definition: a word whose meaning appears clearly (zahara al-murad bihi zuhuran bayyinan).
Note that 'zuhur' here means manifestness, not mere possibility.
2
Distinguish haqiqah and majaz within sarih
Recognize that sarih includes both literal (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz) uses, as long as the meaning is clear.
The clarity itself, not the type of usage, is the criterion.
3
Identify the examples
The passage gives 'You are free' (anta hurr) and 'You are divorced' (anti taliq) as prototypical sarih words.
These are sarih in dissolving slavery and marriage respectively.
4
State the ruling (hukm) of sarih
The legal effect attaches to 'ayn al-kalam (the very words) and stands in place of the meaning, so no intention is required.
This is the key Hanafi position: istighna 'an al-niyyah.
5
Apply to the counterfactual example
If someone intended to say 'Subhanallah' but uttered 'You are divorced', divorce occurs because the words are sarih in talaq.
This demonstrates the principle: the objective meaning prevails over subjective intent.

Bayan Darurat: Four Types of Silence-Based Exposition
Defines bayan darurat and lists its four subtypes with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four subtypes of bayan darurat and apply them to new examples from Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Silence can serve as legal exposition in four distinct ways in Hanafi usul.

In this lesson, we explore a unique concept: bayan darurat, or exposition through silence. Unlike explicit speech, silence can convey legal rulings when context demands it. The text outlines four scenarios where silence is treated as an effective means of communication in Shari'ah, each with its own basis and examples.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the concept of bayan darurat
Understand that bayan darurat is a type of exposition (bayan) that relies on silence (sukut) rather than explicit speech. It is established through something other than its original subject (speech).
Silence here is not mere absence of speech but carries legal weight.
2
Identify the four subtypes
List the four subtypes: (1) in the status of what is spoken, (2) established through indication of the speaker's state, (3) established due to necessity of removing deception from people, (4) established due to necessity of excessive speech.
Each subtype has a different basis and application.
3
Examine the example for each subtype
For each subtype, relate the example from the text: (1) inheritance verse implying father's share, (2) Prophet's silence on actions, (3) master's silence on slave's trading, (4) elliptical speech like 'one hundred and dirham'.
Understand how silence or omission conveys the intended ruling.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi exception
Imam Zufar disagreed on the third subtype; he held that silence does not stand in place of explicit permission. This is a legitimate difference within the Hanafi school.
Recognize that not all Hanafis accept all subtypes uniformly.
5
Apply the concept to a new scenario
Given a situation where the Lawgiver remains silent on a novel action, determine whether it falls under bayan darurat and which subtype applies.
Practice extends beyond memorization to application.

Bayan Darurat: It is a type of bayan that occurs through that which is not put for it, i.e., silence. That is, it is a type that is established through something other than its original subject (speech). Sometimes it is in the status of what is spoken, sometimes it is established through the indication of the speaker's state, and sometimes it is to protect people from deception, and sometimes due to the necessity of excessive speech. See examples of the four types: (1) Sometimes it is in the status of what is spoken: Allah's saying regarding inheritance 'and his parents are his heirs, for his mother one third.' The father's share is not mentioned but it is in the status of what is spoken because there are only two heirs; when the mother gets one third, the remainder goes to the father. (2) Established through indication of the speaker's state: Many actions were done in front of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and he remained silent. This silence is also in the status of permission because the Lawgiver cannot remain silent on an impermissible matter. This is considered a hadith. (3) Established due to necessity of removing deception from people: Look at the example of a slave who is buying and selling in the market while he is a mudabbar (whose freedom is deferred) and yet the master is silent, then this will be considered permission. According to Imam Zufar, silence does not stand in place of explicit permission. (4) Established due to necessity of excessive speech: To shorten speech by saying 'he owes me one hundred and dirham' meaning 'he owes me one hundred dirhams and one dirham.'

Commentary
This passage defines bayan darurat, a type of exposition that relies on silence (sukut) rather than explicit speech. The text explains that silence can have legal effect in four ways: (1) when the meaning is deduced from the context as if spoken (e.g., inheritance shares), (2) when the Prophet's silence on an action indicates its permissibility, (3) when a master's silence in the face of a slave's trading implies permission (with a Hanafi ikhtilaf: Imam Zufar disagrees), and (4) when omission of words for brevity still conveys the intended meaning (e.g., 'one hundred and dirham' means 'one hundred dirhams and one dirham'). This concept is crucial in Hanafi usul for deriving rulings from non-verbal indicators.

Key Terms
بیان ضرورت (bayan darurat): A type of bayan that is established through silence (sukut) rather than explicit speech, with four subtypes.
سکوت (sukut): Silence that serves as an indicator of a legal ruling, especially in contexts where speech is expected.
