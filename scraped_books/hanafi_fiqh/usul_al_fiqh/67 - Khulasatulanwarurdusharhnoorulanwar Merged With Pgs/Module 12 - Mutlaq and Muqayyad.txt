Quran is Both Words and Meanings
Critical refinement of Quran definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of the Quran as the combination of Arabic words and meanings, and identify and refute two common misconceptions about this definition.

Core Idea
The Quran is both the Arabic words and the meanings — not one without the other.

When scholars define the Quran, they often mention its revelation in Arabic and its transmission. But does that mean the Quran is only the words? Or could it be only the meanings? In this passage, the commentator of Noor al-Anwar clarifies the correct Hanafi position: the Quran is the combination of both lafz and ma'na. He also refutes two misconceptions that arise from the matn and from an attributed fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition given by the author
The author (matn) states that 'Quran' is a name for both the arrangement (nazm) and the meanings (ma'na) together. This is the core definition.
The word 'nazm' here refers to the specific Arabic wording of the Quran.
2
Recognize the misconception from the matn
The matn defines the Book of Allah by mentioning revelation (inzal), writing (kitabah), and transmission (naql) — all attributes of words. This could lead to the misunderstanding that the Quran is only words.
The commentator alerts us that the matn's own phrasing might cause this false inference.
3
Recognize the misconception from the attributed fatwa
A fatwa attributed to Imam Abu Hanifa reportedly allowed prayer in translation for someone able to recite Arabic. This could imply that the Quran is only meanings, not the specific Arabic words.
The commentator denies the attribution and says it does not reflect Imam Abu Hanifa's actual doctrine.
4
Understand the commentator's response
The commentator affirms the definition: Quran is both words and meanings. He refutes both misconceptions by clarifying the author's intent and denying the fatwa's authenticity.
This refinement preserves the integrity of the Quran as divine speech in its original Arabic.

Defining Husn Li-Ghayrihi and Its Three Divisions
Defines and divides husn li-ghayrihi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define husn li-ghayrihi and distinguish its three divisions based on the relationship with the commanded act.

Core Idea
Husn li-ghayrihi: goodness from an external factor, not the act itself.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, commanded acts are categorized by the source of their goodness. If goodness is intrinsic, it is husn li-aynihi; if derived from an external factor, it is husn li-ghayrihi. This lesson explores the concept and its three divisions as outlined in the text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition
Find the sentence that defines husn li-ghayrihi. What makes an act 'good for other than itself'?
Focus on the phrase 'حسن غير'.
2
Break down the three divisions
List the three types mentioned. How does each relate to the commanded act or another act?
Look for the words 'لا يتأدى', 'يتأدى', and 'في شرطه'.
3
Understand the examples
The text gives examples: wudu, jihad, and ability (qudra). Explain why each is considered husn li-ghayrihi.
Wudu enables prayer, jihad defends faith, ability enables obligation.
4
Connect to the Quranic proof
Note that the third division is supported by Quran 2:286. How does ability relate to obligation?
The verse states Allah does not burden beyond capacity.

Commentary
This passage defines husn li-ghayrihi (goodness for other than itself) and its subdivisions. The author explains that in husn li-ghayrihi, the goodness of the commanded act comes from an external factor, not from the act itself. He gives examples: wudu (ablution) is good because it enables prayer, jihad is good for defending faith, and ability (qudra) is good because it enables fulfilling obligations. Then he divides husn li-ghayrihi into three types: (1) The goodness is not attained by performing the other commanded act itself — meaning the act's goodness is independent of performing another act. (2) The goodness is attained by performing the other commanded act — for example, wudu's goodness is realized when one prays with it. (3) The goodness arises from a condition (shart) being good, and the condition is ability (qudra). This is based on the Quranic verse 'Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity' (2:286). The author uses a Qur'anic proof (dalil naqli) to support the third division.

Key Terms
حسن لغيره (ḥusn li-ghayrihī): Goodness that comes from something other than the act itself, like its purpose or condition.
شرط (sharṭ): Condition; here specifically ability (qudra) that enables performance of obligations.

Absolute and Time-Bound Commands in Hanafi Usul
Defines two types of commands and discusses delayed performance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define an absolute command (muṭlaq ʿan al-waqt), explain the Hanafi position on its delayed performance, and identify the dispute with Imam al-Karkhi.

Core Idea
Absolute commands allow delay until death; immediate performance is not required.

This passage introduces a foundational usul classification: commands are either absolute (muṭlaq) or time-bound (muqayyad). Understanding this distinction directly impacts rulings on obligations like zakat and prayer. The text also addresses a critical intra-madhhab question—can an absolute command be delayed? The majority Hanafi position permits delay, but Imam al-Karkhi holds a different view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of commands
The text states that command (amr) is of two types: absolute of time (muṭlaq ʿan al-waqt) and time-bound (muqayyad bi al-waqt). Note the examples: zakat and sadaqat al-fitr for absolute, and prayer time for time-bound.
This primary classification is essential for deriving rulings.
2
Define the absolute command
An absolute command is not nullified by the passage of time. Its obligation remains until it is performed. The text clarifies that sin only occurs when death approaches without action.
Examples: zakat, sadaqat al-fitr.
3
Analyze the Hanafi position on delay
The Hanafi school allows delay for absolute commands. The reasoning (talil) is: if immediate performance were required, the command would annul its own purpose (i.e., the command's subject could become impossible).
This contrasts with al-Karkhi's view.
4
Examine the time-bound command
A time-bound command has time as an integral element: it may be a container (zarf), a condition (shart), or a cause (sabab) for the obligation. Example: prayer times. When the time expires, the command is either void or becomes qada.
The text mentions four subdivisions but the discussion is cut off.
5
Apply the distinction to a real case
Consider zakat: it is absolute. Therefore, you may delay payment, but if you die without paying, you have sinned and the estate must still pay. This affects practical fatwa.
Compare with prayer, which is time-bound and requires performance within its window.

Commentary
This passage is cut off, but it presents the Shafi'i argument for carrying absolute (mutlaq) to restricted (muqayyad) in expiations. The Shafi'i principle is that if a ruling is given in absolute terms in one context and restricted in another, both rulings should be unified where the underlying cause (illa) is the same. Imam Shafi'i argues that since expiation for murder requires a believing slave, all expiations (for zihar, broken oath, etc.) should likewise require a believing slave because all are 'kaffarah' and share the same nature. He also uses the principle 'absence of condition or description leads to absence of ruling' to argue that if the condition (iman) is missing from one text, it still applies via qiyas. The Hanafi response is not given in the surviving text; typically they reject this analogy due to different causes and contexts.

The Hanafi Rule: Mutlaq is Not Carried to Muqayyad
States the Hanafi position on carrying mutlaq to muqayyad with proofs and objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on mutlaq and muqayyad, identify the exception when they are in a single ruling, and apply the rule to the kaffarah examples.

Core Idea
Mutlaq is not carried to muqayyad unless both share a single legal ruling.

In this passage, the author explains the Hanafi position on a key usul principle: when an absolute (mutlaq) text and a restricted (muqayyad) text appear, they are treated as independent proofs. The Hanafis do not automatically carry the mutlaq to the muqayyad, except when both texts govern the same legal ruling. This principle has important consequences for how we reconcile apparently conflicting texts in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
Understand that the dispute concerns whether a mutlaq text (e.g., 'free a slave' in kaffarah yamin) is automatically restricted by a muqayyad text from a different ruling (e.g., 'a believing slave' in kaffarah qatl).
Hanafis treat them as independent unless they share a single ruling.
2
State the Hanafi rule and its basis
The mutlaq and muqayyad are two independent proofs; both should be acted upon if possible. When they conflict within the same ruling, the muqayyad takes precedence because it is like a speaker (natiq) while the mutlaq is like a silent one (sakit).
This is derived from the principle that the explicit overrides the implicit.
3
Examine the Shafi'i objection and Hanafi response
The Shafi'is argue by analogy that since all kaffarat are expiations, the restriction in one (believing slave) should apply to all. The Hanafis respond that the gravity of the crime differs, so justice requires different rules.
The Hanafis also point out that carrying mutlaq to muqayyad across rulings leads to inconsistency (e.g., feeding in yamin but not in qatl).
4
Understand the exception
If both the mutlaq and muqayyad occur in the same legal ruling (hukm wahid), then the muqayyad restricts the mutlaq. This is because it is impossible to act on both without contradiction.
Example: Ibn Mas'ud's reading includes 'consecutive days' for kaffarah yamin, which restricts the mutlaq text.
5
Apply the rule to the kaffarah examples
In kaffarah yamin and kaffarah qatl (different rulings), a disbelieving slave suffices for yamin because the mutlaq is not restricted. In kaffarah zihar (same ruling as its own mutlaq? Actually, within zihar, the mutlaq and muqayyad are in one ruling, so the muqayyad applies.)
The practical consequence is that a believing slave is not required for kaffarah yamin according to the Hanafis.

The author says that whether the absolute and restricted are in one incident (like expiation of zihar) or in different incidents (like the example of expiation of murder and oath), in any case, according to the Hanafis, the absolute will be acted upon its absolute, and it will not be carried to the restricted. Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi position on the principle of carrying mutlaq (absolute) to muqayyad (restricted) in the context of expiations (kaffarat). The text begins by addressing a Shafi'i objection that the Shafi'is analogically equated expiation of oath with expiation of murder, requiring a believing slave in both. The Hanafis respond that the restriction to a believing slave in murder is because of the gravity of the crime; for lesser crimes, such restriction would be unjust. The passage then presents another objection: if the Shafi'is carry the restriction from oath to murder in terms of freeing a believing slave, they should also carry the requirement of feeding from murder to oath. The Hanafis reply that in oath, feeding is explicitly mentioned (mansus), while in murder it is not; thus the existence of a ruling in one case does not necessitate its absence in another. The main rule is stated: According to the Hanafis, the absolute (mutlaq) is not carried to the restricted (muqayyad) even if they occur in the same incident, let alone different incidents. The only exception is when both appear in a single legal ruling (hukm wahid), where the restricted takes precedence because silence (mutlaq) yields to speech (muqayyad). An example is given: the reading of Ibn Mas'ud mutlaq in fasting kaffarah includes sequentiality, while the mutawatir reading does not; to avoid contradiction, the absolute must be interpreted according to the restricted.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A text or ruling that is unrestricted, without any qualifying condition.
مقید (muqayyad): A text or ruling with a qualifying condition or restriction.
کفارہ (kaffarah): An expiation or atonement for a sin or violation, such as breaking an oath or unintentional manslaughter.
حکم واحد (hukm wahid): A single legal ruling; when both mutlaq and muqayyad pertain to the same subject matter and ruling.
