Resolving the Objection on Sacrifice: The Role of Asl and Khalaf
Resolving an objection using two-probability analysis with asl and khalaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi concepts of asl and khalaf to derive the ruling for a vowed sacrifice when the days of sacrifice pass, and explain the two-probability analysis used in this dispute.

Core Idea
When a substitute (khalaf) is limited to a specific time, the original rule (asl) revives once that time ends.

This passage addresses an objection that sacrifice is irrational because it destroys animals, so no compensation should be required. The Hanafi response uses a two-probability analysis: either sacrifice itself is the original rule, or charity is the original and sacrifice a substitute. Understanding this reasoning is crucial for grasping how Hanafi usul handles time-bound acts of worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
The objector claims that sacrificing animals is unreasonable because it wastes life, so if the animal dies, no qada' should be required. Recognize this as a challenge to the Hanafi ruling that requires compensation.
2
Recognize the Two Probabilities
The response posits two possibilities: (1) sacrificing is the original ruling (asl) during the days, or (2) giving charity is the original and sacrificing is a substitute (khalaf). Understand that the law must choose one.
3
Determine the Asl During the Days
Since the days of sacrifice are for Allah's hospitality, and hospitality is best with meat, sacrificing is designated as the original during this period.
4
Determine the Asl After the Days
When the days end, the special hospitality context disappears, so we revert to the original rule: giving charity. The substitute (sacrifice) no longer applies.
5
Derive the Final Ruling
If the animal dies after the days, the person must give the animal or its value in charity. If the next Eid comes, we do not revert to sacrificing because the substitute was specific to the original time.
Note that even if the next year's Eid arrives, we do not revert to the substitute; the original charity remains.

Commentary
This passage addresses an objection against the Hanafi ruling that if a person vowed to sacrifice an animal but the animal died, he must give the animal itself or its value in charity. The objector argues that sacrifice is irrational as it destroys animals, so qada' should not be required. The response employs a two-probability analysis: (1) During the days of sacrifice, the act of sacrificing is itself the primary ruling (asl) because those days are designated for Allah's hospitality with meat. (2) The original ruling might have been simply giving charity, and sacrificing is a substitute (khalaf) that applies only during those days. Therefore, when the sacrificial days end, we revert to the original ruling of giving charity. This demonstrates the Hanafi use of probability (ihtimal) and the concept of 'asl and khalaf in deriving rulings for time-bound acts of worship.

Key Terms
اصل (asl): The original or primary ruling from which a substitute (khalaf) may be derived.
خلف (khalaf): A substitute ruling that replaces the original in certain circumstances.

Two Types of Isti'ara in Legal Declarations
Explains two types of ittisal governing isti'ara in legal declarations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of ittisal (connection) in Hanafi usul and apply them to determine the permissibility of metaphorical expressions in legal declarations.

Core Idea
The direction of legal metaphor depends on the type of causal link: reciprocal for causal-effect, one-way for effect-cause.

In Hanafi legal theory, not every metaphorical usage of a term is permissible. The permissibility depends on the type of connection (ittisal) between the term's original meaning and its intended meaning. This passage from Noor al-Anwar explains two distinct types of such connections: one where metaphor is bidirectional, and another where it is only one-way. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly interpreting ambiguous legal declarations and for resolving disputes between madhahib.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of ittisal
Read the passage to identify the first type (ittisal al-ma'lul bi al-'illa) where the effect is connected to the cause, and the second type (ittisal al-musabbab bi al-sabab) where the effect is connected to the cause in a different sense.
In the first type, 'illa means efficient cause; in the second, sabab does not mean 'illa.
2
Apply the first type to the example of purchase and ownership
Analyze the example: 'If I buy a slave, he is free' intending ownership. Understand why metaphor is allowed both ways and why the declaration is believed inwardly but not judicially.
The leniency principle: if he buys half, the half becomes free in purchase context but not in ownership context.
3
Apply the second type to the example of emancipation and divorce
Analyze the example: 'You are free' intending divorce. Understand why metaphor is only allowed from cause to effect (free → divorced) and not vice versa.
The exception: when the effect is specific to the cause, metaphor from effect to cause is permissible, as in the Quranic verse about wine and grapes.
4
Analyze the Shafi'i dispute and Hanafi response
Examine Imam al-Shafi'i's argument that divorce and emancipation share attributes of pervasiveness and permanence, and the Hanafi response that their essential purposes differ (removing a restriction vs establishing strength).
This illustrates how Hanafi usul prioritizes underlying legal purpose over shared attributes.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Noor al-Anwar discusses the two types of 'ittisal' (connection) that govern the permissibility of isti'ara (metaphorical usage) in legal declarations. The author first defines the first type: the connection of the effect (ma'lul) to the cause ('illa). In this type, metaphor is permissible in both directions: one can say the cause and mean the effect, or say the effect and mean the cause. This is because each depends on the other—the effect on the cause for existence, the cause on the effect for being legislated. The principle in metaphor is to mention the dependent-upon and intend the dependent. The author illustrates with an example: a man says 'If I buy a slave, he is free' intending ownership by 'purchase'. Here he is believed inwardly (dinan) but not judicially (qada'an) because of leniency—if he buys half, the half becomes free in purchase but not in ownership (since ownership of half does not entail full emancipation). Conversely, if he says 'If I own a slave, he is free' intending purchase, he is believed both inwardly and judicially, because there is no leniency (ownership implies full ownership). An objection is raised that even here there is leniency because ownership can also come through marriage, bequest, or inheritance, but the author points out that the text only discusses inward belief, not judicial, so the objection is irrelevant.

The second type is the connection of the effect (musabbab/musabbab) to the cause (sabab). In this type, cause does not have the meaning of 'illa (efficient cause) but is merely one side of the ruling. The example given: if a man says to his female slave 'You are free' (anta hurra), this removes ownership of the person (milkiyya raqaba), and as a consequence the right of sexual enjoyment (milkiyya mut'a) is also removed. Here 'emancipation' (which removes ownership of the person) is the cause, and 'removal of right to enjoyment' is the effect. In this type, metaphor is only permissible from cause to effect: one can say 'You are free' intending divorce (i.e., removal of the right to enjoyment), but one cannot say 'You are divorced' intending emancipation. The reason: the effect depends on the cause for its existence, but the cause does not depend on the effect for being legislated. Emancipation is legislated for removing ownership of the person; the removal of the right to enjoyment happens incidentally. However, if the effect is specific to the cause—like in the Quranic verse where 'wine' (khamr) is mentioned but grapes are intended—then metaphor from effect to cause is valid because grapes are the specific material for wine. The author then presents the Shafi'i view: Imam al-Shafi'i argues that between divorce and emancipation there is a conceptual connection (ittisal ma'nawi) based on both having the characteristics of pervasiveness (saraya) and permanence (luzum), so metaphor in both directions is permitted. The Hanafi response rejects this: divorce is for removing a restriction (qayd), while emancipation is for establishing strength (quwwa); they are fundamentally different and cannot be used metaphorically for each other. This illustrates the Hanafi method of distinguishing legal categories based on their essential purposes (maqasid).

Key Terms
اتصال (ittisal): Connection between cause and effect that permits metaphorical usage in legal declarations.
استعارہ (isti'ara): Metaphorical usage of a legal term to mean another term due to conceptual connection.
سبب (sabab): The cause or trigger in a legal context, but in the second type of ittisal, it does not carry the meaning of 'illa (efficient cause).
مسبب (musabbab): The effect that results from a cause, but is not itself necessary for the existence of the cause.

Deriving Ghusl Obligation from Iqsal: Hanafi Istinbat
Hanafi istinbat using dhalalah and istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi concept of dhalalah to derive the ruling that ghusl is wajib due to iqsal, and explain the role of istihsan in this derivation.

Core Idea
Iqsal is a dhalalah for semen exit, so ghusl becomes wajib by istihsan even without felt exit.

This passage illustrates how the Hanafi school uses dhalalah (indication) and istihsan to extend the ruling of ghusl from actual semen exit to include iqsal (meeting of circumcised parts). The author addresses an objection: if ghusl depends on semen exit, how can iqsal trigger it? The response classifies semen exit into actual and by-dhalalah, and uses precaution (ihtiyat) to obligate ghusl.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Read the objection posed to the Hanafi position: if all instances of ghusl depend on the actual exit of semen (mani), how can iqsal be considered such an instance? Note that iqsal does not involve felt exit.
2
Understand the two categories of semen exit
The Hanafi response divides semen exit into two types: 'iyanan' (actual, apparent) and 'dhalalatan' (by indication). The latter is inferred from the meeting of circumcised parts, which is strong evidence of semen exit.
This division is key to the istinbat.
3
Explain the legal reasoning (istihsan)
The cause (sabab) of ghusl is the exit of semen. Since iqsal is a reliable indicator (dhalalah) of that cause, the Hanafis treat iqsal as equivalent to actual exit via istihsan (juristic preference) for the sake of precaution.
Istihsan here is based on the hidden nature of small semen exit.
4
Trace the derivation chain

The Eighth Invalid Proof: Command Does Not Necessitate Prohibition of Its Opposite
Presenting three schools on whether commanding something implies prohibition of its opposite.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three schools regarding the relationship between a command and its opposite, and apply the Hanafi position that command implies karahah, not haram.

Core Idea
A command does not automatically forbid its opposite; only karahah unless the commanded act is missed.

A central debate in usul al-fiqh concerns whether the command for an act (al-amr) entails a ruling about its opposite (al-didd). Does commanding salah make its opposite, like sleeping or talking, prohibited? This passage examines three schools: two that assert varying degrees of prohibition and the Hanafi school that limits it to dislike (karahah) unless the commanded act is entirely lost. Understanding this nuance is crucial for deriving correct rulings without over-extending the command's implication.

Guided Path
1

Identify what the Hanafis say about the implication of a command: command of a thing entails karahah (dislike) for its opposite, not prohibition.

2

Two other schools: (1) Imam al-Haramayn and al-Ghazali hold that command necessitates prohibition of the opposite; (2) Some usulis hold that command necessitates the opposite being haram (stronger than prohibition).
The difference between 'prohibition' and 'haram' is subtle; the second group sees a direct obligation.

Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: For the Hanafis, engaging in the opposite is makruh (disliked), not haram, unless it causes missing the commanded act. For the others, it is inherently prohibited.
Example: Prolonged sitting in the first rak'ah that misses the standing in the second rak'ah becomes haram only if the standing is lost.

Taqrir al-Usul and Conflicting Qiyas: Su'r al-Himar
Principle of preserving original ruling when qiyas conflict, applied to donkey's leftover water

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of taqrir al-usul when analogies conflict and derive the composite ruling for donkey's leftover water.

Core Idea
When analogies conflict and no preference possible, preserve the original rulings of both water and person.

This passage explores a challenging usul scenario where multiple analogies (qiyas) about the purity of donkey's leftover water (su'r al-himar) are attempted but none can be preferred. The Hanafi solution is taqrir al-usul: keeping each element (water and the person) on its original ruling, resulting in a composite obligation of both wudu and tayammum. Understanding this principle is crucial for deriving rulings when proofs seem to cancel out.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and attempted analogies
Note the specific case: water left by a donkey. List the analogies tested: sweat (for purity), cat's leftover (impurity), donkey's milk, dog's leftover (impurity), cat's leftover (purity).
All analogies fail due to differences in need (haja).
2
Understand why each analogy fails
Examine each attempted qiyas: the effective cause (illa) of need (haja) differs between the original and the analogy, making the analogy invalid.
Key concept: the degree of necessity changes the ruling.
3
Define taqrir al-usul
When all paths of preference are blocked, the original rulings of each element (water's purity, person's hadath) are preserved. This is not a proof (hujja) but a necessity-based approach.
Contrast with istishab (presumption of continuity); taqrir al-usul applies when no tarjih is possible.
4
Apply the principle to derive the ruling
Water remains pure (its original ruling) and the person remains in hadath (his original ruling). Hence, both wudu (with the water) and tayammum are required.
The ruling is called mashkuk because the evidence is conflicting, not because the ruling is unknown.
5
Distinguish mashkuk from majhul
Mashkuk means the ruling is known (wudu + tayammum) but the evidence is conflicting. Majhul means the ruling is unknown. This distinction answers the objection that the Shariah is clear.
This protects against the claim that declaring a ruling doubtful implies ignorance.
6
Compare Hanafi and Shafi'i approaches
Hanafis hold that a mujtahid cannot have two contradictory sayings at once; he must choose based on inner inclination. Shafi'is do not require inner inclination and may narrate multiple positions simultaneously.
This explains why the Hanafis do not simply drop both analogies.

Preferring the Prohibitory Over the Permissive When Chronology is Unknown
Explains the rule for preferring prohibitory texts when chronology is unknown.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when a prohibitory text (muharram) conflicts with a permissive text (mubah) and the chronological order is unknown, the prohibitory text is given precedence by using the principle of original permissibility (asl al-ibaha) to infer its later date.

Core Idea
When texts conflict, assume the prohibitory came later because permissibility is the default.

How do we resolve a conflict between a text that says something is allowed and another that says it is forbidden, when we don't know which was revealed first? The Hanafis have a clear rule based on the foundational maxim 'asl al-ibaha' — the original state of things is permissibility. In this lesson, you will understand and apply this rule, which avoids logical contradictions and upholds the integrity of abrogation.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Principle asl al-ibaha
Begin by internalizing the Hanafi maxim: 'The original state of all things is permissibility.' This means that unless there is a clear prohibition, an act is considered allowed. This principle is the foundation for the rule.
This maxim is considered qat'i (certain) in Hanafi usul.
2
Identify the Conflict
When you have two texts: one indicating prohibition (muharram) and one indicating permissibility (mubah), and you cannot determine which was revealed first temporally, a conflict arises. You need a method to decide which text governs.
The conflict is not about strength of evidence but about chronology.
3
Apply the Logical Inference – Avoid Takrar
Assume the permissive text is earlier. If you then prefer the permissive, you would have: permissibility (original state) → prohibition (later text) → permissibility (if you still follow the earlier permissive). This sequence is an unnecessary repetition (takrar) of permissibility after prohibition, which is logically problematic. To avoid this, you must deem the prohibitory text as later, thus it abrogates the permissive.
This reasoning is a form of qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
4
Derive the Ruling
The ruling becomes: In case of unknown chronology, the prohibitory text is given precedence and is considered the abrogator. The permissive text is abrogated.
This rule is specific to Hanafi methodology; other schools may have different mechanisms.

The Principle of Negation (Nafy) in Hanafi Usul
Explanation of the principle that negation can be equivalent to affirmation in Hanafi usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a negation (nafy) into one of three categories and determine whether it equates to affirmation (ithbat) in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A negation based on evidence or ambiguous knowledge equals affirmation; a negation based on mere presumption does not.

The passage addresses a fundamental disagreement about the original state of things (ashya') — whether it is permissibility, prohibition, or suspension. This disagreement leads to conflict when a negative statement (nafy) opposes a positive one (ithbat). The Hanafi master provides a rule: nafy can be as strong as ithbat if it stems from clear evidence or even ambiguous knowledge, but not if it is merely based on istishab (presumption of continuity). This rule resolves many disputes in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three opinions
State the three views on the original state of things: original permissibility (ibaha), original prohibition (hurmah), and suspension (wuquf) until evidence arrives.
These opinions are the background for the need of the nafy principle.
2
Understand the problem
Recognize that when a nafy and an ithbat oppose each other, the ruling depends on which carries more weight. Without a principle, conflict is unresolved.
The matn provides a solution by categorizing nafy.
3
Learn the matn's rule
The matn states: nafy is like ithbat if it belongs to the genus of 'what is known by its evidence' (ma yu'rafu bi-dalilihi) or 'what is ambiguous in state' (mimma yushtabahu haluhu). Otherwise, it is not like ithbat.
The third category (neither) corresponds to nafy based on istishab.
4
Apply the categories
Given a nafy statement, determine which category it falls into by looking at its evidentiary basis. If it mentions a clear evidence, it is ma yu'raf. If the narrator relies on knowledge but with some ambiguity, it is mimma yushtabah. If it simply assumes the previous state continues, it is istishab.
Only the first two categories give nafy the strength of ithbat.
5
Recognize the role of istishab
Note that istishab is explicitly excluded: it is not a valid proof (hujja) in Hanafi usul, so nafy based on it does not equal ithbat.
This is a key difference from some other schools that accept istishab as a proof.

This passage discusses a usul principle regarding the legal weight of negative statements (nafy). In both cases, the nafy can counterbalance the ithbat, leading to a conflict that needs further resolution. This principle is crucial in evaluating conflicting reports and arguments.
