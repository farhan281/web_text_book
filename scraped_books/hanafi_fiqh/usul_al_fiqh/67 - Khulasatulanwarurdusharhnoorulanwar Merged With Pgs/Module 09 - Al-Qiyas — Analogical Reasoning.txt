Example of Qiyas from the Quran: Prohibition of Sodomy
Demonstrates qiyas from Quran

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the prohibition of sodomy via qiyas from the Quranic prohibition of menstrual intercourse by identifying the 'illah (cause) of harm and applying it to the new case.

Core Idea
An explicitly stated cause in the Quran extends to all cases sharing that cause.

This lesson walks through the first example in the text: deriving the prohibition of sodomy from the Quranic prohibition of intercourse during menstruation. The key is the explicitly stated cause: harm (adha). The passage also addresses the objection that the ruling is already found elsewhere in the Quran, demonstrating that multiple proofs can support the same ruling.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the original ruling (asl) and its evidence
The original ruling is the prohibition of intercourse with a menstruating wife, established by Quran 2:222: 'So keep away from women during menstruation and do not approach them until they become pure.'
2
2. Extract the 'illah from the text
Allah explicitly states the cause: 'Say: It is harm' (Quran 2:222). Thus the 'illah is harm (adha).
This is a clear case of ta'lil from the nass itself.
3
3. Verify the 'illah in the new case (far')
Sodomy (liwata) also involves harm, both physical and spiritual. Therefore the same 'illah is present.
4
4. Derive the ruling via qiyas
Since the cause is the same, the ruling of prohibition is transferred from the original case to sodomy.
This is qiyas jaliy due to the explicit 'illah.
5
5. Address the objection
Some argue that sodomy is already prohibited explicitly in Quran 4:16, so qiyas is unnecessary. The response: a ruling can be established by multiple proofs; here both text and qiyas support the prohibition.
This shows that qiyas is valid even when a direct text exists—it reinforces the ruling.

Commentary
This passage is a practical demonstration of qiyas (analogical deduction) applied to derive rulings from the three primary sources: Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma'. It begins by affirming the four sources of Islamic law (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas) and notes that qiyas must be derived from the three primary sources, not from mere rational analogy or speculative reasoning. The author then provides three concrete examples. First, from the Quran: the prohibition of intercourse during menstruation is established by the verse (2:222), and the cause ('illah) given is 'harm' (adha). This same cause is found in sodomy (liwata), so sodomy is also prohibited by qiyas. A note advises avoiding the term 'liwata' out of respect for Prophet Lut. An objection is raised that sodomy is already explicitly prohibited in the Quran (4:16), but the response clarifies that a ruling can be established by multiple proofs; here both text and qiyas support it. Second, from the Sunnah: the hadith on riba in six commodities (wheat, barley, dates, salt, gold, silver) prohibits excess and credit. The Hanafis identify the cause as quantity (measure or weight) along with genus. This cause applies to building lime, and similarly to lentils (unclear), thus their exchange with excess or credit is also prohibited. Third, from Ijma': it is agreed that if a man has intercourse with his slave woman, her mother becomes prohibited to him. The cause is that the resulting child is a part of both parties, so each becomes related to the other's ascendants and descendants. The prohibition is explained in detail: the child's existence creates prohibition between the man and the woman's family, and vice versa. This lesson illustrates how qiyas extends rulings from established sources to new cases based on shared causes.

Key Terms
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical deduction from a primary source to a new case based on a shared cause.
العلة (al-'illah): The effective cause that is common to the original case and the new case, justifying the transfer of the ruling.
الأصل (al-asl): The original case with a known ruling from a primary source.
الفرع (al-far'): The new case whose ruling is being sought via qiyas.

Example of Qiyas from the Sunnah: Prohibition of Riba in Building Lime
Demonstrates qiyas from Sunnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to extend the prohibition of riba to commodities not mentioned in the hadith by identifying the 'illah of quantity (measure/weight) along with genus.

Core Idea
The 'illah for riba in the six commodities is quantity with genus; anything matching this cause is riba.

The second example demonstrates qiyas from the Sunnah. The Prophet (peace be upon him) prohibited excess and credit in six specific commodities. Hanafi jurists extracted the cause: quantity (measure or weight) plus genus. This cause applies to building lime, so trading it with excess or on credit is also prohibited. This lesson shows how a Sunnah-based ruling can be extended to new items.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the original ruling from the Sunnah
The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'Wheat for wheat, barley for barley, dates for dates, salt for salt, gold for gold, silver for silver, like for like, hand to hand, and excess is riba.'
The ruling: it is prohibited to exchange these items with excess or on credit.
2
2. Extract the 'illah from the hadith
Hanafi jurists derived the 'illah as quantity (measure or weight) along with genus. The items share this characteristic: some are measured by volume (wheat, barley, dates, salt) and some by weight (gold, silver).
The 'illah is not the items themselves but the common attribute.
3
3. Verify the 'illah in the new case (far')
Building lime is also sold by measure or weight and belongs to the same genus? Actually it is a different genus but the 'illah is quantity with genus only? Wait, the text says 'same cause' in building lime, meaning building lime is also a commodity sold by measure/weight, so the 'illah of quantity applies. But careful: the text says 'this same cause is found in building lime' (یہیں علت عمارتی چونے میں بھی پائی جاتی ہے). So building lime is treated as ribawi because it is measured or weighed.
The 'illah is general: any staple commodity that is measured or weighed. Building lime is such a commodity.
4
4. Derive the ruling via qiyas
Since building lime shares the 'illah (quantity with genus-like classification), the prohibition of excess and credit applies to it. Hence, selling building lime with excess or on credit is riba.
This is a classic example of ta'lil bi al-wasf al-munasi.
5
5. Note the extent of the analogy
The text also mentions lentils and other similar items, showing that the analogy extends to all commodities that are measured or weighed and are staple foods or currency-like.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the prohibition of marrying certain relatives of a slave woman after intercourse by using qiyas based on consensus (ijma') and the cause of lineage through the child.

Ijma' establishes the original ruling; the cause is the child being part of both parties, creating prohibited relationships.

Qiyas from Sunnah: Extending Riba Prohibition to Grains
Derives prohibition of excess in grains via qiyas on riba hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of istinbat by identifying the 'illa from the riba hadith and extending the ruling to new cases via qiyas jali.

Core Idea
The prohibition of excess in weighable food items of the same genus derives from the riba hadith via qiyas on the ʿilla of qadr and jins.

This lesson demonstrates how Hanafi jurists use qiyas jali (clear analogy) to extend a textual ruling to new cases. The Prophet's hadith explicitly prohibits riba in six items. By analyzing the text, the jurists extract the effective cause (ʿilla) and apply it to other items sharing that cause, such as stone lime. Understanding this process is essential for mastering Hanafi istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six items and the ruling
Locate the six items mentioned in the hadith and note that the ruling is prohibition of excess (riba fadl) and credit (riba nasi'ah). This is the original case (asl).
The six items are gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, and salt.
2
Extract the ʿilla from the text
Hanafi usul scholars derive the ʿilla as the combination of being measurable by weight or volume (qadr) and belonging to the same genus (jins). This is based on the wording and consensus of the madhhab.
The ʿilla is not explicitly stated; it is inferred by induction and suitability.
3
Verify the ʿilla in the new case
Check whether the new item (farʿ), e.g., stone lime, shares both attributes: it is weighed and has a genus. Since it does, the ʿilla is present.
Stone lime is a construction material sold by weight and considered a genus.
4
Apply qiyas to transfer the ruling
Because the ʿilla is present, the ruling from the asl (prohibition of excess and credit) applies to the farʿ. This is qiyas jali: clear analogy.
The transfer is valid according to Hanafi usul because the ʿilla is jaliyyah (clear).
5
Note the epistemological weight
The hadith is qatiʿ al-thubut (mutawatir in meaning) but zanni al-dalala for the ʿilla, which is derived by ijtihad. The resulting ruling is authoritative but admits of ikhtilaf with other schools.
Some schools use different ʿillas, e.g., edibility.

Qiyas as the Fourth Source: Example and Defense
Defends qiyas as fourth source with example and responses to objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi defense of qiyas as the fourth source, apply qiyas to the case of zina, and evaluate objections regarding its ranking and validity.

Core Idea
Qiyas is the fourth source, generally zanni, but grounded in definitive texts; its separate mention refutes deniers.

This passage from Khulasat al-Anwar defends the inclusion of qiyas as a fourth source of Islamic law, distinct from Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma. It illustrates qiyas with a concrete example (prohibition of marrying the mother of a woman one committed zina with) and addresses several objections: why not simply say 'four sources'? Why mention qiyas separately? And why use the term 'asl' for all four? The Hanafi responses clarify the epistemological difference between definitive and presumptive sources and the deliberate emphasis to refute deniers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the qiyas example
Break down the example: original case (asl) is the prohibition of marrying one's wife's mother (by ijma), its ruling (hukm) is haram, and the effective cause (illa) is juz'iyya (conjugal relations). The new case (far') is the mother of the woman with whom one committed zina. Since the same illa exists, the ruling is extended: haram.
This is a classic qiyas jali (clear analogy).
2
Understand the first objection
Objection: If qiyas is an independent principle, why did the author not simply state 'the sources are four'? The first answer: the first three sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) are qat'i al-thubut (definitive in transmission), while qiyas is generally zanni (presumptive). Thus, they are not equal in rank.
Some qiyas based on qat'i texts can also be qat'i.
3
Examine the second objection
Another objection: What is the benefit of mentioning 'al-rabi' (the fourth) separately when the refutation of deniers was already implied? Answer: Explicitly mentioning it provides a clear, intentional refutation, not just an implied one.
This also explains why 'al-asl' is used for qiyas but not for the first three.
4
Address the terminology objection
Objection: Calling all four 'usul' is inaccurate since qiyas is a branch (far') of the first three. Answer: Something can be an asl relative to one thing and a far' relative to another. Example: a father is an asl for his children but a far' for his ancestors.
Thus, qiyas is an asl for deriving new rulings while being a far' from the primary sources.

Qiyas Awla: A Fortiori Analogy in Halal After Divorce
Argument establishing halal via qiyas awla from hadith of Rifa'a

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of qiyas awla and apply it to derive a ruling from the hadith of Rifa'a.

Core Idea
If a ruling is proven even where the impediment is strongest, it applies a fortiori where the impediment is weaker.

In this passage, the Hanafi school uses a powerful form of analogical reasoning called qiyas awla (a fortiori analogy). The argument centers on the hadith of Rifa'a, which established that after a triple divorce, the wife can only become halal for the first husband through a valid second marriage with consummation. The author then extends this ruling with 'greater reason' to cases of incomplete divorce, demonstrating how the strength of the 'illa determines the scope of the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original ruling (asl)
The hadith of Rifa'a provides the base case: a woman divorced three times cannot return to her first husband without tahlil (a valid second marriage with consummation). The ruling is that halal is established only after tahlil.
The asl is the case explicitly mentioned in the text.
2
Extract the effective cause ('illa)
The 'illa is the complete absence of halal after triple divorce. This is the strongest impediment to re-establishing halal.
The 'illa is derived from the context: after triple divorce, no halal remains.
3
Identify the new case (far')
The far' is a case of incomplete divorce (less than three), where some halal still exists imperfectly.
This case is not explicitly mentioned in the hadith but is implied by the reasoning.
4
Apply qiyas awla
Since the ruling (halal after tahlil) applies in the asl despite the strongest impediment (complete absence of halal), it applies with greater reason (awla) in the far' where the impediment is weaker (some halal remains).
Qiyas awla is valid because the 'illa in the far' is less severe, making the case more deserving of the ruling.
5
State the derived ruling
Therefore, halal is established after tahlil even in cases of incomplete divorce. The first husband can remarry his wife after she goes through a valid tahlil.
This ruling is a direct application of qiyas awla within Hanafi usul.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A ruling contrary to qiyas can be extended if a general cause common to both cases exists.

This passage tackles a common methodological objection in usul: if a ruling is established as an exception to qiyas (khilaf al-qiyas), can it be used as a basis for analogy? The objector says no, but the Hanafi response reveals a more nuanced approach. Instead of direct qiyas, the Hanafis identify a general cause ('illah 'ammah) that applies to both the original and new case. Here, the cause is incapacity ('ajz), which links fidyah for fasting and for prayer. This lesson dissects the objection and the Hanafi refutation.

1

The objector asserts a principle: a ruling that is proven contrary to qiyas cannot be used to analogize another issue. They claim fidyah for fasting is such a ruling, so extending it to prayer is invalid.
This is a common strict qiyas position.
2

The Hanafi response denies that they are using direct qiyas from fasting. Instead, they argue that the original ruling might be due to a general cause, not specificity to fasting.
The response shifts from qiyas to ta'lil (reasoning via cause).

The Hanafis propose that the ruling for fasting fidyah could be because of incapacity, a cause that also applies to prayer. This general cause makes the extension valid even if the original ruling is an exception.
This is a key Hanafi usul tool: seeking a broader 'illah.

Incapacity applies to both fasting (unable to fast due to death) and prayer (unable to pray). Thus, the same 'illah supports fidyah for prayer as a precautionary measure.

The General Cause (Sabab 'Amm) in Qiyas
Objection and answer regarding the cause of emancipation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a general cause (sabab 'amm) is sufficient for qiyas, even when the specific mode of acquisition differs, and identify the objection that fails due to focusing on incidental details.

Core Idea
A cause (sabab) general in nature suffices for qiyas; exact identity of means is not required.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, qiyas requires a common cause ('illah) between the original case (asl) and the new case (far'). This passage presents an objection that pushes back on an analogy between emancipation of a slave (milk yamin) and dissolution of marriage (nikah) because the ownership rights are acquired differently. The Hanafi response clarifies that the cause is general: emancipation removes ownership regardless of how it was acquired. This lesson breaks down the argument and its underlying usul principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
The objector claims that emancipation only removes ownership (milk al-mut'ah) acquired through milk yamin (slave ownership), not through marriage. Therefore, there is no commonality between the two cases, and qiyas fails.
Milk al-mut'ah is the right of possession or usufruct in general.
2
Analyze the Basis of the Objection
The objection rests on the assumption that the cause must be exactly identical in every aspect—including the mode of acquisition. The objector treats the difference in acquisition as destroying the commonality.
3
State the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response is that it suffices for the cause to be general (fi al-jumlah). Emancipation is, in general terms, a cause that removes ownership; whether that ownership was acquired through milk yamin or nikah is incidental.
The principle: 'يكفي السبب في الجملة' (the cause is sufficient in general).
4
Implication for Qiyas
By accepting a general cause, the ruling of emancipation is extended to all cases where ownership is removed by this cause, regardless of the specific method of acquisition. This makes the analogy valid.

The Hanafi Usul Principle of 'Thumma': Delay and Its Fiqh Consequences
Usul dispute on 'thumma' with fiqh application in conditional divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to: explain the Hanafi usul principle that 'thumma' (then) indicates a break in speech (تراخى), apply it to conditional divorce scenarios, and evaluate the disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad).

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, 'thumma' is like a pause; Abu Hanifa holds it delays both teaching and ruling, while the Sahibayn delay only ruling.

This lesson explores the usul principle surrounding the particle 'thumma' (ثم) and its implications for sequential statements in fiqh. The key dispute is whether 'thumma' severs the speech into independent units, delaying both the content and the legal effect, or merely delays the legal effect while keeping the speech continuous. This linguistic distinction leads to divergent rulings in conditional divorce cases, illustrating how usul principles directly shape fiqh outcomes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested linguistic property
'Thumma' is understood to indicate delay (تراخى). The disagreement is whether this delay severs the speech into separate, independent statements or merely postpones the legal effect of the later statement while keeping all statements as part of one continuous utterance.
Abu Hanifa likens 'thumma' to a pause and then a new beginning.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that 'thumma' delays both teaching and ruling (تعليما وحكما). The first statement is finalized upon utterance, and the second statement is an entirely new, independent declaration. Thus, in a series, the first takes effect immediately (if unconditional) and the subsequent ones are void because the subject matter (mahall) is already exhausted.
This is based on the literal meaning of 'thumma' implying a restart.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that 'thumma' delays only the ruling, not the teaching. All statements remain linked within a single speech act. Therefore, when a condition is present, all subsequent statements are attached to it and take effect sequentially upon fulfillment of the condition.
The Sahibayn view gives more weight to the continuity of the speaker's intent.
4
Apply to conditional divorce scenarios
Work through the example from the text: a man says to his non-consummated wife 'أنت طالق ثم طالق ثم طالق إن دخلت الدار'. According to Abu Hanifa, the first divorce occurs immediately (since it is unconditional), and the subsequent ones are void. If the condition is advanced ('إن دخلت الدار فأنت طالق ثم طالق ثم طالق'), the first is suspended on the condition, the second occurs immediately (since after 'then' it is a new statement), and the third is void. Compare with the Sahibayn's view where all three are suspended.
The thamarah al-khilaf is evident in the number of divorces that occur.
5
Determine the practical consequence and tarjih
The difference leads to distinct fiqh rulings: Abu Hanifa's position often results in fewer divorces taking effect, while the Sahibayn's results in all divorces occurring in sequence. The text presents both views but implies that Abu Hanifa's is more linguistically grounded, though the Sahibayn's is also considered valid.
In practice, the mufta bihi (operative position) may vary depending on the context, but Abu Hanifa's view is often preferred in classical Hanafi fatwa.

Dalalat al-Nass: A Definitive Proof for Hudud and Kaffarat
Explains the principle that hudud and kaffarat are proven by textual indication, not analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of dalalat al-nass to distinguish it from qiyas and derive rulings for hudud and kaffarat.

Core Idea
Dalalat al-nass derives a definitive ruling from the text's underlying cause, not speculative analogy.

In Hanafi usul, not all legal deductions carry the same weight. Some rulings require certainty, especially those involving fixed punishments (hudud) and expiations (kaffarat). This passage introduces dalalat al-nass as a proof that bridges explicit text and implied cause, offering a stronger basis than qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Locate the statement that hudud and kaffarat are proven by dalalat al-nass and not qiyas. Note the justification: dalalat al-nass is definitive (qat'i), while qiyas is speculative (zanni).
2
Examine the Examples
For each example (stoning of Ma'iz, punishment for highway robbers' supporters, expiation for breaking the fast), identify the explicit ruling and the underlying cause ('illa) that allows extension via dalalat al-nass.
The cause is not the specific person but the underlying act or state.
3
Understand the Shafi'i Objection
Imam al-Shafi'i restricts the expiation ruling to intercourse alone. Understand his evidence (textual specificity) and the Hanafi response (the ruling is based on the cause, not the specific act).
4
Grasp the Takhsis Restriction
Note that rulings established by dalalat al-nass do not accept particularization (takhsis) because they lack generality ('umum). Only texts with explicit generality can be particularized.
5
Distinguish from Qiyas
Contrast dalalat al-nass with qiyas: in dalalat al-nass, the cause is evident from the text itself; in qiyas, the cause is extracted through ijtihad. This makes dalalat al-nass a stronger proof.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion on the rule that hudud (fixed punishments) and kaffarat (expiations) are established by dalalat al-nass (textual indication) and not by qiyas (analogy). (3) The expiation for intentionally breaking the fast in Ramadan by intercourse—the cause is intentionally invalidating the fast, so by textual indication it applies also to one who eats or drinks intentionally, and to the spouse as well. The Hanafi response is that the ruling is based on the cause ('illa'), not the specific act. Finally, the passage explains that rulings proven by dalalat al-nass do not admit takhsis (particularization) because they lack 'umum (generality); only rulings with generality can be particularized. This is a standard Hanafi usul position.

Two Categories of Sahaba Narrators and Precedence Over Qiyas
Definition of narrator categories and dispute with Imam Malik

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two categories of Sahaba narrators, state the Hanafi rule on precedence over qiyas for first-category narrators, and evaluate the opposing view of Imam Malik.

Core Idea
Reports from jurist Sahaba override qiyas; reports from non-jurist Sahaba are overridden by qiyas.

This passage from Khulasat al-Anwar defines the key term 'those known in fiqh and ijtihad' and explains how this classification affects the authority of a Sahaba's khabar wahid when it conflicts with qiyas. The Hanafi school holds that reports from such elevated narrators take precedence, while Imam Malik argues for qiyas to prevail. Understanding this distinction is crucial for properly applying usul al-fiqh in weighing evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first category
The passage lists examples of first-category Sahaba (e.g., the four caliphs, Ibn Abbas, Ibn Umar, Aisha). Note the two requirements: they must be known for fiqh and ijtihad AND have precedence (tqaddum) and priority (awaliyya).
The category is called 'معروف بالفقه والاجتهاد'.
2
Understand the rule for first-category reports
According to the Jumhur (majority, including Hanafis), a khabar wahid from a first-category Sahabi takes precedence over qiyas. This is an established rule.
The Hanafi position is that this type of report is stronger than analogical reasoning.
3
Contrast with Imam Malik's view
Imam Malik holds that even first-category reports should be compared to qiyas; if they contradict, qiyas is preferred. His proof uses the incident of Abu Hurairah's hadith and Ibn Abbas's qiyas.
Imam Malik's position is that all ahad reports are subject to qiyas scrutiny.
4

The Hanafis respond that Abu Hurairah is not considered a faqih (first category) by the majority, so the example does not apply to first-category narrators. Thus, for first-category, the rule stands; for second-category, qiyas prevails by agreement.
This nuance is the key to the entire dispute.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: For first-category reports, Hanafis act on the hadith even against qiyas; Malikis act on qiyas. For second-category reports, both agree on qiyas.
The classification determines the outcome.

Key Terms
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning; deriving a ruling for a new case based on an existing ruling due to shared effective cause (illa).
قول صحابی (qawl al-sahabi): A statement or opinion of a Companion of the Prophet Muhammad.
اجیر مشترک (ajir mushtarak): A common hired worker who works for multiple people, e.g., a washerman (qassar), porter, etc.
غیر مدرک بالقیاس (ghayr mudrak bil-qiyas): Matters that cannot be accessed or determined through analogical reasoning (usually because they are purely devotional or have no known illa).

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi school uses a case-by-case approach in non-analogical matters, sometimes preferring qawl al-sahabi and sometimes qiyas, based on the strength of the evidence in that specific context.
