Historical Survey of Early Usul al-Fiqh Works (2nd-7th Centuries)
Chronological list of major Usul al-Fiqh works from 2nd to 7th century

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major Usul al-Fiqh works from the 2nd to 7th Islamic centuries, state their authors and approximate dates, and distinguish the madhhab affiliation of each work.

Core Idea
Al-Shafi'i's Risalah is the first systematic Usul work, but Hanafis like al-Maturidi and al-Jassas wrote foundational texts soon after.

This lesson traces the chronological development of Usul al-Fiqh as a discipline. You will learn about the key works that shaped the field, from al-Shafi'i's groundbreaking Risalah to the sophisticated treatises of later centuries. Understanding this timeline helps you locate the origins of fundamental usul concepts and see how different madhhabs contributed to the science.

Guided Path
1
Start with the first work
Identify al-Shafi'i's Risalah (d. 204 AH) as the first systematic composition in Usul al-Fiqh. Note that it was originally the introduction to his fiqh work al-Umm.
The Risalah discusses commands, prohibitions, and bayan (clarification).
2
Move century by century
For each century (3rd to 7th), list the works mentioned. Associate each with its author and approximate death date.
Focus on the madhhab affiliation: al-Maturidi (Hanafi), al-Jassas (Hanafi), al-Tusi (Hanafi), al-Razi (Shafi'i/Ash'ari), etc.
3
Note the distinctive features
Observe that some works specialize in specific topics, e.g., al-Anwar on Qiyas, and that some authors are known for multiple works (al-Razi's Muhassal and Mahsul).
Also note the variety of madhhabs: Hanafi, Shafi'i, Maliki (Ibn al-Hajib).
4
Create a mental timeline
Memorize the sequence of centuries and the key works for each. This will help you situate later usul discussions within their historical context.
Use the passage as a reference; the timeline is linear but cross-madhhab.
5
Reflect on the significance
Consider why this historical survey matters: it shows that Usul al-Fiqh developed gradually with contributions from multiple schools, and that al-Shafi'i is credited with the first systematic treatment.
Contrast this with the misconception that Usul began only with al-Shafi'i; earlier works existed but his was the first systematic.

Commentary
This passage presents a historical survey of major works in Usul al-Fiqh from the 2nd to 7th Islamic centuries. It begins with Imam al-Shafi'i's Risalah, which is widely regarded as the first systematic work in Usul al-Fiqh, originally an introduction to his fiqh work al-Umm. The author notes that the Risalah discussed topics such as commands (awamir), prohibitions (nawahi), and Bayan (clarification). Following al-Shafi'i, scholars from various schools contributed to the field. In the early 3rd century, Abu Mansur al-Maturidi (a Hanafi theologian) wrote two works: Kitab al-Jadal (on dialectic) and Ma'khidh al-Shar' (on the sources of law). In the 4th century, Hanafi scholar Ibn Burhan al-Farisi wrote al-Dhakhirah, and the Hanafi jurist Abu Bakr al-Jassas (known for his tafsir and usul) wrote Kitab al-Usul. The 5th century saw Qadi Abu Zayd al-Tusi's al-Anwar, which extensively discussed Qiyas (analogy). In the 6th century, works include al-Usul by Qadi al-Qudah al-Hanafi (a Hanafi authority), al-Awsat by Ibn al-Burhan al-Shafi'i, al-Mughni by an author whose name is unclear, and Muhassal and Mahsul by Fakhr al-Din al-Razi (Ash'ari theologian). The 7th century lists Ahkam al-Ahkam by Sayf al-Din al-Amidi (Shafi'i), al-Samanthi by Ibn al-Hajib (Maliki grammarian and usuli), al-Hasil by Qadi Ta' al-Din, and al-Safwah by Abu al-Raja Mukhtar. This overview highlights the rich scholarly tradition across madhhabs, especially the Hanafi and Shafi'i contributions, and shows the development of usul al-fiqh as a disciplined science.

Biography of Hafiz al-Din al-Nasafi and Introduction to Manar
Biographical introduction to the author of Manar and his works

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the author of Manar, state his key works, and explain the relationship between Manar and the usul works of al-Bazdawi and al-Sarakhsi.

Core Idea
Manar is the abridged usul of al-Bazdawi and al-Sarakhsi, authored by a Hanafi master whose works shaped centuries of curriculum.

This lesson introduces you to the author of one of the most widely studied texts in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: al-Manar. Understanding the background of its author, Hafiz al-Din al-Nasafi, helps you appreciate the text's place in the tradition and its relationship to earlier usul works. We will also clarify a common confusion between two scholars bearing the same nisbah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the author's full profile
Extract his name (Abdullah ibn Ahmad ibn Mahmud), kunyah (Abu al-Barakat), laqab (Hafiz al-Din), and nisbah (al-Nasafi). Note that he was an Imam of his time with ijtihad-level mastery in fiqh and usul.
2
List his notable teachers and scholarly credentials
Identify his teachers: Muhammad ibn al-Sattar, Hamid al-Din al-Darir, Badr al-Din Khawahir Zadah. Recognize his expertise also in hadith.
These teachers are themselves significant figures in the Hanafi tradition.
3
Catalog his major works
Besides Manar, list his other works: Mudarik al-Tanzil (Quran commentary), Kanz al-Daqa'iq (fiqh), al-Wafi and its commentary al-Kafi, and a work on creed. Note the enduring popularity of these works in curricula.
Kanz al-Daqa'iq is a well-known Hanafi fiqh text.
4
Understand the textual relationship of Manar
Manar is an abridgment of the usul works of Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi and Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi, with greater adherence to al-Bazdawi's arrangement and terminology. The author also wrote a commentary on his own text, Kashf al-Asrar.
This shows Manar is not an original work but a summary, which is common in the tradition.
5
Distinguish between the two Nasafis
Note that the author of Manar (Hafiz al-Din, d. 710 AH) is not the same as Abu Hafs al-Nasafi (d. 537 AH), author of 'Aqa'id al-Nasafi. The similarity in nisbah often causes confusion.
The two scholars lived about two centuries apart.

Name and lineage

Conditions of the author of Manar

The name of the author of Manar is Abdullah ibn Ahmad ibn Mahmud, his patronym is Abu al-Barakat, and his title is Hafiz al-Din. He is from Nasaf, which is a place in the outskirts of Turkestan, and he is called Nasafi in relation to it.
Abu al-Barakat al-Nasafi was an Imam of his time and counted among the peerless scholars. He possessed an ijtihad-level mastery in fiqh and usul al-fiqh, and also had complete command over hadith and its related disciplines.
Famous teachers and shuyukh
Among his teachers, Muhammad ibn al-Sattar [al-Kurdi?], Hamid al-Din al-Darir, and Badr al-Din Khawahir Zadah are especially notable.
Scholarly achievements
Besides the text of Manar, he has other highly authoritative and reliable works in various sciences, among which (Mudarik al-Tanzil wa Haqa'iq al-Ta'wil), (Kanz al-Daqa'iq), (al-Wafi) and its commentary (al-Kafi), and [Muhammad?] and 'Aqidah Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah are more famous and well-known. The popularity of his works can be gauged from the fact that most of his books have been part of the curricula in Arab seminaries for centuries.

Introduction to the text of Manar

The concise text of usul al-fiqh (Manar) is essentially an abridgment of Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi's (Usul) and Shams al-A'imma al-Sarakhsi's (Usul), in which the arrangement and expression of al-Bazdawi's usul is more closely followed. The author himself has also written an extensive commentary on this text, named (Kashf al-Asrar fi Sharh al-Manar), which is very comprehensive and evidence-based. From biographical works, his birth year is not known; however, his death occurred in 710 AH in Baghdad.
May Allah have mercy on him and benefit us with his knowledge. Ameen.
Clarification of a point
Let it be clear that the author of 'Aqa'id al-Nasafi is someone else, whose name is Abu Hafs Umar ibn Ahmad al-Nasafi (birth 461 AH, death 537 AH). The author of 'Aqa'id al-Nasafi passed away approximately two centuries before the author of Manar. Because of the common attribution to Nasaf, students often get confused, so this clarification has been made.

Conditions of the author of Nur al-Anwar

Name and Lineage
His name is Ahmad, his father's name is Abu Sa'id, he is famous as Mulla Jiyun. The lineage is as follows: Ahmad ibn Shaykh Abu Sa'id ibn 'Abd Allah ibn Shaykh 'Abd al-Razzaq ibn Shah Makhdum (Makhdum Khasah). In the end, his noble lineage connects to Hadrat Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, may Allah be pleased with him.

Birth and Residence
His grandfather 'Abd al-Dah's great-grandfather Makhdum Khasah, who was from the descendants of Shaykh Salah al-Din Dihlawi, was among the famous saints of the town of Amethi. They moved from Delhi and settled in Amethi. Mulla Jiyun was born in this town of Amethi. The date of birth is approximately 1048 AH.

Acquisition of Knowledge
At the age of seven, he memorized the Holy Quran. Then he engaged in the acquisition of sciences and arts, and living in various towns of Europe, he benefited from the scholars of his age. He read most of the textbooks under Shaykh Muhammad Sadiq Turki. Finally, he obtained a certificate of completion from Mulla Lutfullah al-Gurdizi al-Jahanabadi. Regarding his scholarly depth, the words of Maulana Azad Bilgrami are: 'He acquired the divine word and the first of intellectual and transmitted sciences, an ocean without shore.' After completing his studies, he adorned the seat of teaching and continued teaching in his homeland.

Memory Power and Simplicity of Temper
He was extremely simple, of a poor nature, humble in temperament, completely detached from formal relationships, and unique in memory power. He would memorize entire pages and sheets of textbook expressions, and long odes would be memorized by hearing them once.

Emperor 'Alamgir before Mulla Sahib
At the age of forty, he arrived in Delhi via Ajmer Sharif, and resided there for a considerable period, continuing the activity of teaching and instructing. The pull of fortune brought him to Emperor Shah Jahan. Shah Jahan appointed him and the narrow-minded 'Alamgir for education, and 'Alamgir bent the knee of discipleship before him, and then continued to honor and respect him excessively throughout his life. Similarly, Shah 'Alam, the successor of 'Alamgir, showed him respect, and Shah Farrukh Siyar also greatly valued and respected him.

Departure from the World
He passed away in 1130 AH, after fifty days his blessed body was taken from Delhi to Amethi and buried in his madrasa.

Scholarly Achievements
He devoted his entire life to teaching and writing. In usul al-fiqh, Nur al-Anwar, a commentary on al-Manar, is his lasting legacy, which is well known to every child in the world. He wrote this book in only two months during his stay in Medina.

Commentary
This passage provides a biographical introduction to Mulla Jiyun (Ahmad), the author of Nur al-Anwar, a celebrated commentary on al-Manar in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It covers his lineage, tracing back to Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, his birth around 1048 AH in Amethi, his education under various teachers including a Turkish scholar, his extraordinary memory, his role as tutor to Emperor Aurangzeb ('Alamgir) and other Mughal princes, his death in 1130 AH, and his most famous work, Nur al-Anwar, which he wrote in just two months during a stay in Medina. This work remains a key text in the Dars-i Nizami curriculum for Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The biography highlights his scholarly dedication and the esteem in which he was held by the Mughal court.
