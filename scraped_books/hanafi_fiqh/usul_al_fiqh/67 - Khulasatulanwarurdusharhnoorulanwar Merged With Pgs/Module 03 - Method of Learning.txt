History of the Compilation of Usul al-Fiqh
Historical account of the compilation of usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why usul al-fiqh was not compiled during the time of the Companions and identify the early Hanafi contributions to its compilation.

Core Idea
Usul al-fiqh was compiled after the Companions' era because divine clarity sufficed; Imam Abu Hanifa laid its foundation even though his writings are lost.

This passage traces the early history of usul al-fiqh, explaining why it was not needed by the Companions and how it gradually developed as a formal discipline. It highlights the foundational role of Imam Abu Hanifa and his students in laying the groundwork for what later became the systematic study of usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Understand the need for principles
Recognize that human actions are infinite, yet each requires a ruling. General principles are necessary to derive rulings systematically.
This is the rationale behind usul al-fiqh.
2
Analyze the Companions' dispensation
The Companions did not need formal usul because of their direct spiritual clarity and proximity to the Prophet (ﷺ). Sciences had not yet become formalized.
This shows that usul is a subsequent development.
3
Trace the shift after the Companions
After the Companions' era, each science took the form of a craft (sina'ah). Usul al-fiqh was compiled in this context.
This marks the beginning of formal compilation.
4
Identify Imam Abu Hanifa's role
Imam Abu Hanifa is recognized as the first compiler of fiqh, and in doing so, he laid the foundation for usul al-fiqh even though he did not author a separate work on it.
His methodology is implicit in his fiqh.
5
Note the lost works of his students
Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad are reported to have written books on usul, but these are not extant.
This indicates that the early Hanafi school engaged with usul from the beginning.

Commentary
This passage is a historical overview. It begins by explaining the necessity of compiling usul al-fiqh: since actions are infinite but each has a ruling, general principles are needed for derivation. It notes that the Companions did not need formal usul because of their direct spiritual clarity and proximity to the Prophet. After them, sciences became formalized, and usul al-fiqh was codified. The second century saw jurists like Imam Abu Hanifa, who, while being the first to compile fiqh (probably referring to his organization of fiqh topics), also laid foundations for usul al-fiqh even though no written work of his remains. Allama Khudri mentions that Imam Abu Yusuf and Imam Muhammad authored works on usul, but these are lost. This explains the early development of the discipline within the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
اصول فقہ (usul al-fiqh): The principles and methodology for deriving Islamic legal rulings from the primary sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas).
تدوین (tadwin): The process of compiling or codifying knowledge into a systematic written form.
صناعت (sina'at): A craft or formal discipline; here meaning that knowledge became organized as a distinct field of study.

History of the Compilation of Usul al-Fiqh
Historical narrative of usul al-fiqh compilation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key figures and works in the compilation of usul al-fiqh from the second to fifth centuries and explain why the discipline emerged after the Companions' era.

Core Idea
Usul al-fiqh was compiled after the Companions, with Imam Shafi'i's Risalah as the first authored work.

The science of usul al-fiqh provides the rules for deriving legal rulings from the Quran and Sunnah. This lesson traces its historical development from the second to the fifth centuries, highlighting why it was necessary, why the Companions did not compile it, and how early scholars like Imam Shafi'i, al-Maturidi, and others shaped its formalization.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem necessitating usul al-fiqh
The passage states that human actions are infinite, and stating a ruling for each separately is difficult or impossible. Thus, general principles (qawa'id) were needed to derive rulings systematically.
This mirrors the purpose of usul: to provide a methodology for istinbat.
2
Explain why the Companions did not compile usul al-fiqh
The Companions, due to their direct association with the Prophet and purity of heart, were independent of formal usul. Sciences had not yet taken a disciplinary form.
This highlights that usul is a later scholarly construct, not a revealed science.
3
Trace the early implicit principles and first written work
Imam Abu Hanifah practiced ijtihad with implicit principles, but no written usul from him is known. According to the passage, the first authored work is Imam Shafi'i's Risalah (d. 204 AH), which discussed commands, prohibitions, and naskh.
Shafi'i's Risalah is considered the foundation of the discipline.
4
Outline the contributions of the third and fourth centuries
In the third century, Abu Mansur al-Maturidi wrote Kitab al-Jadal and Ma'khadh al-Shar'. In the fourth century, Ibn Burhan al-Farsi wrote Kitab al-Dhakhirah and al-Jassas wrote Kitab al-Usul.
These works refined and expanded the discipline.
5
Describe the fifth-century consolidation
Qadi Abu Zayd al-Tusi's al-Anwar is described as the best among earlier works, with extensive discussions on qiyas, strengthening the foundations of usul al-fiqh.
al-Anwar represents the culmination of early Hanafi usul writings.

After that, Islamic scholars wrote lengthy and concise books in usul al-fiqh with great refinement and research, and by bringing this art to perfection, they provided evidence of excellent services.

Third Century
By the end of the second century, the work of systematic composition and authorship on usul al-fiqh had already begun. Thus, at the end of the second century or the beginning of the third century, Shaykh Abu Mansur Muhammad ibn Muhammad al-Maturidi (d. 322 AH) wrote two excellent books on usul al-fiqh: one is Kitab al-Jadal and the other is Ma'khadh al-Shar'. Both books are extremely precise and flawless.

Fourth Century
In the fourth century, Shaykh Ahmad ibn Husayn, known as Ibn Burhan al-Farsi (d. 350 AH) composed Kitab al-Dhakhirah, and Imam Abu Bakr al-Jassas Ahmad ibn Ali (d. 370 AH) composed Kitab al-Usul. Both these books are among the best books of that era in usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage provides a historical overview of the compilation of usul al-fiqh, following the author's chronological narrative. The author begins by explaining the necessity of compiling principles (qawa'id) to derive rulings, given the infinite nature of human actions. He notes that the Companions were self-sufficient due to their direct prophetic training, but after their era, sciences were formalized, including usul al-fiqh. In the second century, while early jurists like Imam Abu Hanifah practiced ijtihad with implicit principles, no written works on usul al-fiqh are known from him; later claims about writings by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani are unconfirmed. The first authored work is Imam Shafi'i's al-Risalah (d. 204 AH), which discussed commands, prohibitions, bayan (clarification), and naskh (abrogation). In the third century, Abu Mansur al-Maturidi authored Kitab al-Jadal and Ma'khadh al-Shar', focusing on dialectics and sources. The fourth century saw Kitab al-Dhakhirah by Ibn Burhan al-Farsi and Kitab al-Usul by al-Jassas. Finally, in the fifth century, Qadi Abu Zayd al-Tusi's al-Anwar emerged as a comprehensive work on qiyas (analogy), strengthening the discipline's foundations.

Key Terms
اصول فقہ (usul al-fiqh): The science of principles of Islamic jurisprudence, which provides rules for deriving legal rulings from primary sources.
تدوین (tadwin): The systematic compilation and codification of a science into a formal discipline.
اجتہاد (ijtihad): Independent legal reasoning by a qualified scholar to derive rulings from the sources.

Definition, Purpose, and Subject of Usul al-Fiqh
Defining Usul al-Fiqh, its goal, and subject matter with objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definitions of Usul and Fiqh from the Hanafi perspective, identify the purpose of Usul al-Fiqh, and explain how the subject matter of evidences and rulings forms one unified science by distinguishing between establisher and established.

Core Idea
Usul al-Fiqh is the science of deriving rulings from evidences; its subject is one: evidences as cause, rulings as effect.

This lesson introduces the foundational definitions of Usul al-Fiqh according to the Hanafi school. You will learn what 'Usul' and 'Fiqh' mean, the purpose of this science, and a key conceptual clarification that resolves the apparent duality of its subject matter.

Guided Path
1
Define 'Asl' linguistically
Identify the meaning of 'Asl' as the foundation upon which something rests, whether sensory or intellectual.
This is the linguistic basis for 'Usul'.
2
Define 'Fiqh' technically
State the technical definition of Fiqh as knowledge of legal rulings from their detailed evidences.
Note the requirement of detailed (tafsili) evidences.
3
State the purpose of Usul al-Fiqh
Recognize that the purpose is to know legal rulings from evidences, leading to happiness in both worlds.
4
Identify the subject matter and the objection
The subject includes both evidences and rulings. The objection is that this implies two sciences.
The objection is a common confusion.
5
Analyze the answer
Understand the answer: evidences are the establisher (muthbit) and rulings are the established (thabit), forming one unified subject as cause and effect.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary explains the classification of hadith transmission methods into 'azimat (resolution) and rukhsat (concession) within the category of samā' (hearing). The author states that the first three methods—qirā'ah 'alā al-shaykh (reading to the shaykh), samā' min al-shaykh (hearing from the shaykh), and mukātabah (correspondence)—are considered 'azimat because they involve direct or near-direct connection. The remaining three—ijāzah (general permission), munāwalah (handing over), and mujāzalah (conditional permission for a knowledgeable person)—are rukhsat as they lack the direct hearing element. A detailed example of mujāzalah is provided: a shaykh may permit a student to narrate a book like Mishkat al-Masabih on the shaykh's authority only if the student already knows the book independently. This condition ensures the student can accurately transmit it. If the student only hopes to learn later, the permission is merely for blessings, not for formal transmission. The Hanafi approach prioritizes precision in isnad continuity.

Key Terms
عزیمت (azimat): The resolute or primary method of hadith transmission requiring direct hearing or its equivalent.
رخصت (rukhsat): A concessionary method that does not require direct hearing but allows transmission through authorization.
مجازلہ (mujazalah): A type of ijazah given to someone already knowledgeable of the book, enabling narration on the giver's chain.

The Four Ways to Resolve Apparent Contradiction Between Evidences (Mukhallas 'an al-Mu'arada)
Classifies four methods to resolve conflict between conflicting shari' evidences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the four Hanafi methods of resolving apparent contradiction between shari' evidences without resorting to abrogation.

Core Idea
An apparent contradiction between two valid evidences can be resolved by contextualizing each to its own domain, condition, or time.

This passage introduces the concept of mukhallas 'an al-mu'arada, a key tool in Hanafi usul for harmonizing conflicting texts. You will learn the four categories and see how classical scholars reconciled verses that seem to contradict, such as those on oaths and the menstruation verse with different qira'at.

Guided Path
1
Define the concept of mukhallas 'an al-mu'arada
Identify what mukhallas means: a way out of apparent contradiction between two shari' evidences. Understand its place in Hanafi usul as a preferred alternative to tarjih or naskh.
This is the overarching idea of the lesson.
2
Classify the four types
List them: (1) min qibal al-hujja, (2) min qibal al-hukm, (3) min qibal al-hal, (4) min qibal ikhtilaf al-zaman. Note that the fourth is mentioned but not elaborated extensively.
The passage focuses on the first three with examples.
3
Analyze the first type: min qibal al-hujja
How does prioritizing stronger evidence resolve contradiction? If one evidence is stronger in authenticity or clarity (e.g., mutawatir over ahad, nass over zahir), the stronger is preferred while the weaker is not outright rejected.
This is similar to tarjih but framed as mukhallas.

Analyze the second type: min qibal al-hukm
Understand the distinction between worldly (dunya) and otherworldly (akhira) accountability. Work through the oath of ghamus example: Surah al-Baqarah addresses divine accountability, Surah al-Ma'idah addresses expiation.
Both verses remain operative, each in its own domain.
5

This example also illustrates the fourth type (time difference) implicitly.

The thing that delivers from contradiction (mukhallas 'an al-mu'arada) has the following four types: (1) from the side of the evidence, (2) from the side of the ruling, (3) from the side of the state/condition, (4) from the side of difference of time, whether the difference of time is explicit or implicit. Clarification of each of the four types: (1) From the side of the evidence: It means that from two shari' proofs, one proof is superior and the other is inferior, for example one is khabar wahid and the other is khabar mashhur or mutawatir, or one is zahir and the other is nass, so the superior will be given preference over the inferior, as has passed before. (2) From the side of the ruling: The form of deliverance from the side of the ruling is that the ruling of one pertains to otherworldly matters and the ruling of the other pertains to worldly matters. Example: Regarding the oath of ghamus (deliberate false oath), the verse of Surah al-Baqarah: 'Allah does not hold you accountable for your useless oaths, but He holds you accountable for what your hearts have earned (the verse).' From the generality of 'what your hearts have earned', accountability is understood because just like in a binding oath, in the oath of ghamus the intention of the swearer is included. While in the verse of Surah al-Ma'idah: 'Allah does not hold you accountable for your useless oaths, but He holds you accountable for what you have contracted of oaths (the verse),' from the specification of 'what you have contracted' it is understood that this accountability is attached to a useless oath. Now this contradiction is resolved thus: In Surah al-Baqarah, accountability means otherworldly accountability, so on the oath of ghamus there is accountability before Allah, while in Surah al-Ma'idah, accountability means worldly accountability (expiation). Therefore, expiation will only be due on a binding oath, not on a useless oath or the oath of ghamus. (3) From the side of the state/condition: Its form is that one is specified with one condition and the other with another condition. Example: Allah Almighty says: 'They ask you about menstruation. Say: It is harmful, so keep away from women during menstruation and do not approach them until they are pure.' In this, regarding the word 'yutahharna' (they become pure), two recitations are transmitted. One with takhfif (without shaddah), the meaning of which is that when those menstruating women become pure due to the cessation of blood, intercourse with them becomes permissible whether they perform ghusl or not, i.e., ghusl is not necessary. While the other recitation with tashdid (with shaddah) means that until they perform ghusl, do not have intercourse with them. Here the recitation with takhfif is specified with the condition when menstruation stops after the completion of its days. Then ghusl is not necessary because there is no fear of blood returning. And the recitation with tashdid is specified with the condition when blood stops before the days (ten days). In this case, ghusl is deemed necessary so that the non-return of blood becomes certain. Otherwise, due to the remaining days, menstrual blood may come at any time. By performing ghusl or the passing of one prayer time in a state of purity, the non-return of blood is confirmed and certain. Objection: This interpretation of yours does not work here because immediately thereafter comes another part: 'faidha tatahharna fa'tuhunna' (and when they have purified themselves, then come to them). Here only the recitation with tashdid is found, which indicates that until menstruating women become pure by performing ghusl, they should not be approached. Answer: The first answer is that this relates not to obligation but to desirability (istihbab), meaning that it is recommended in any case that intercourse with one's wife be done only after purification.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of 'mukhallas 'an al-mu'arada' — the way out of an apparent contradiction between two shari' evidences. The author classifies it into four categories: from the side of the evidence itself, from the side of the ruling's domain (worldly vs. otherworldly), from the side of the specific condition or state, and from the side of a difference in time. For each, he provides a Quranic example. The first type is straightforward: if one evidence is stronger in its authenticity or clarity, it takes precedence. The second type is illustrated with the oath of ghamus: one Quranic verse implies accountability for intentional oaths in the Hereafter, while another implies worldly expiation only for binding oaths — thus no contradiction, as each addresses a different realm. The third type is elaborated with the different qira'at (recitations) of the verse on menstruation: one recitation (takhfif) makes permissibility conditional on cessation of blood without requiring ghusl, while the other (tashdid) requires ghusl. The commentator reconciles them by assigning each recitation to a different situation: if the menses ends after its full term (10 days), ghusl is not needed; if it ends earlier, ghusl is required to ensure purity. An objection is raised that the adjacent verse 'faidha tatahharna' (with tashdid) seems universal, but the answer given is that the command there is not for obligation but for recommendation (istihbab). This demonstrates the Hanafi usul principle of reconciling conflicting texts through contextual specification.

Key Terms
مخلص عن المعارضة (mukhallas 'an al-mu'arada): A technique to remove an apparent contradiction between two shari' evidences by showing they apply to different aspects, domains, conditions, or times.
من قبل الحجة (min qibal al-hujja): Resolving contradiction by giving precedence to the stronger evidence (e.g., mutawatir over ahad, nass over zahir).
من قبل الحكم (min qibal al-hukm): Resolving contradiction by showing that one evidence pertains to otherworldly matters (ukum al-akhira) and the other to worldly matters (ukum al-dunya).
من قبل الحال (min qibal al-hal): Resolving contradiction by specifying each evidence to a different state or condition (hal).

Types of Revelation: Zahir and Batin
Classifies revelation into two types with subcategories.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify revelation into apparent and inner types and explain the Hanafi position on inner revelation as an authoritative source.

Core Idea
Revelation includes both external (angelic speech) and inner (ilham/ijtihad corrected by wahy); the Hanafi school accepts both as authoritative.

In Hanafi usul, understanding the nature of revelation is essential for identifying the sources of law. This lesson explores the classification of wahy into two broad categories: zahir (apparent) and batin (inner). By learning their subdivisions, you will see how the Prophet's ijtihad is also considered a form of revelation.

Commentary
This passage from Khulasat al-Anwar explains the classification of wahi (revelation) as understood in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author divides revelation into two primary categories: zahir (apparent/conveyed through external means) and batin (inner/inspirational). Under zahir wahi, three subcategories are enumerated: first, direct speech from an angel where the identity of the angel is known definitively, exemplified by the revelation brought by Jibril; second, an angelic indication without words, as in the hadith of inspiration about rizq; third, ilham (inspiration) directly into the heart. Regarding batin wahi, the author clarifies that it refers to rulings derived by the Prophet (saw) through ijtihad on matters not explicitly revealed. Notably, the Hanafi school affirms batin wahi, though some scholars reject it. The passage further explains that if the Prophet's ijtihad was mistaken, Allah would correct him through wahy, so ultimately it is also authoritative. This classification is foundational for understanding the sources of Shari'ah.
