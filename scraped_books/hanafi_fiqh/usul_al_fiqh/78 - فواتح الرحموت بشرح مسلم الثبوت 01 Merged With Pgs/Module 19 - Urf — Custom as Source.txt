Abandoned Literal Meaning and Its Effect on Oaths
Usul rule on hijr al-haqiqah with oath example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of hijr al-haqiqah to determine the legal effect of oaths when the literal meaning is abandoned by custom.

Core Idea
When literal meaning is abandoned by custom, the metaphorical becomes the default in Hanafi usul.

This passage examines a key Hanafi usul principle: how to interpret oaths when the literal meaning ('placing one's foot') is no longer used customarily. The rule determines that the abandoned literal is disregarded and the metaphorical (absolute entry) prevails. We will walk through the objection, the response, and the nuances of intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath phrase and its literal meaning
The phrase in question is 'I will not place my foot in this house.' The literal meaning (haqiqah) is contacting the floor with one's foot. But customarily, 'placing one's foot' is used to mean simply 'entering.'
The literal meaning is not entirely gone, but it is abandoned in common usage.
2
Determine if the literal meaning is abandoned (hijr)
Check if the literal meaning is no longer the usual intent when the phrase is uttered. The passage indicates that 'placing one's foot' is abandoned in favor of 'entering' in common parlance.
Abandonment must be widespread ('urf) – here it is.
3
Apply the rule: disregard abandoned literal, prefer metaphorical
When a literal meaning is abandoned by custom, it is left (tutrak) and the metaphorical (majaz) is preferred. Thus, the oath 'I will not place my foot' is interpreted as 'I will not enter.'
This is the core Hanafi position.
4
Apply to the scenario: entering riding vs. barefoot
Entering riding or wearing shoes does not literally involve placing the foot, but metaphorically it is entry. Because the metaphorical meaning is absolute entry, such entry breaks the oath. Entering barefoot literally involves placing the foot, but since the literal is abandoned, it also breaks the oath – no contradiction.
The objector's paradox is resolved by the hijr principle.
5
Consider the effect of specific intention
If the swearer explicitly intended the literal meaning (e.g., 'I intend placing my foot only while walking'), then he is believed in court and the oath is not broken by non-literal acts. However, the default is the metaphorical meaning unless proven otherwise.
Intention qualifies the general rule but does not negate it.

Commentary
This passage is a usul rule discussion about how an oath phrased metaphorically is interpreted when the literal meaning has fallen out of use. The objector argues that an oath 'not to place his foot' in a house is broken by entering riding or wearing shoes, even though those actions are not literally 'placing the foot'—only metaphorically. Yet entering barefoot (literal) also breaks the oath, leading to contradiction. The Hanafi answer is that 'placing the foot' is no longer used literally for only the foot; customarily it means simply 'to enter.' Thus the abandoned literal meaning is ignored and the metaphorical ('absolute entry') becomes the default. This is further supported by the example from Fatawa Qadikhan: if one intends the literal 'walking entry' and enters riding, he is not breaking the oath because he intended a non-abandoned literal meaning. However, the preferred answer is that the contextual evidence (the oath's purpose of avoiding the house) shows that the swearer intended to prevent any entry, not just foot placement. If one specifically intends the literal 'placing foot' he is believed in court, but the default is the broader meaning. This illustrates the Hanafi principle that when a haqiqah is abandoned (hijr), it is disregarded and majaz becomes operative.

Key Terms

المجاز (al-majaz): Metaphorical usage that becomes preferred when the literal is abandoned.

Possessive in Oaths: Owned vs. Rented House
Resolving the objection about oath regarding one's house when living in a rented house.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of 'idafa li al-ikhtisas' to interpret possessive constructions in oaths, distinguishing between ownership and residence based on context.

Core Idea
In oaths, 'his house' covers both owned and rented if context indicates a general connection.

When someone swears 'I will not enter my house,' what counts as 'his house'? The literal meaning points to ownership, but what if he lives in a rented house? This passage from Usul al-Fiqh resolves the apparent contradiction by introducing the concept of 'idafa li al-ikhtisas' – a possessive that indicates a general connection, which can be either ownership or residence depending on the context. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly applying oaths in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the possessive in the oath
Locate the possessive construction (e.g., 'his house') in the oath. This is the starting point for analysis.
In Arabic, the possessive is formed by the idafa construction.
2
Determine the context (qarina) of the oath
Consider the purpose and circumstances of the oath. The passage mentions that a person abandons a house only due to aversion to the owner, indicating that the oath is about a house he lives in or avoids.
Context is key to interpreting the idafa.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule: idafa li al-ikhtisas
The possessive in oaths indicates a general connection (ikhtisas) that includes both ownership and residence. This resolves the objection that the same phrase cannot be both literal and metaphorical simultaneously.
This rule is specific to Hanafi usul.
4
Derive the ruling for the rented house
Since the rented house is a place of residence, the possessive covers it under ikhtisas. Entering it breaks the oath.
The same applies to an owned inhabited house.
5
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute on owned uninhabited houses
Qadikhan holds that ikhtisas includes ownership alone, so entering an owned uninhabited house breaks the oath. Al-Sarakhsi disagrees, arguing that the context (hijran) limits ikhtisas to residence.
Both opinions are within the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This is a usul rule passage resolving an objection about the possessive in oaths. If someone swears 'I will not enter my house,' and he lives in a rented house, entering it would break the oath even though the rented house is only metaphorically 'his.' But entering an owned and inhabited house also breaks it (literal). The objection is that the same word 'his house' would then be both literal and metaphorical in one oath. The Hanafi answer is that the possessive (idafa) indicates absolute connection (ikhtisas), which includes both ownership and residence. The context (qarina) is the oath's purpose: one abandons a house only due to dislike of the owner, so the possessive applies to any house associated with him—owned or rented. Thus the phrase covers both simultaneously, either by literal interpretation (if owned) or metaphorical (if rented), so no contradiction. However, scholars differ on whether entering an owned but unoccupied house breaks the oath. Qadikhan says yes because the connection is absolute (ownership alone suffices). Al-Sarakhsi says no because the context of abandonment suggests the house is inhabited; ownership alone is insufficient to cause aversion. The passage teaches that the interpretation of 'idafa depends on the purpose and context of the oath.

Key Terms
الاختصاص (al-ikhtisas): A general connection or relation that includes both ownership and residence.
الإضافة (al-idafa): The genitive/possessive construction in Arabic grammar, used here in legal contexts to denote connection.
القرينة (al-qarina): A contextual indicator that helps determine the intended meaning of a word or phrase.

Oaths on Euphrates Water and Wheat: Literal vs. Customary
Applying haqiqah vs majaz to specific oath examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of haqiqah versus majaz to oath scenarios, and evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: literal; Sahibayn: customary. The mufta bihi follows the Sahibayn.

In this passage, we see the usul principle of haqiqah (literal meaning) vs. majaz (figurative/customary meaning) applied to real oaths. If someone swears not to drink from the Euphrates or not to eat wheat, what counts as a violation? The answer depends on whether we take the words literally or as understood by custom. This dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two leading students (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) is a classic example of how usul rules produce differing fiqh outcomes.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and the absence of intention
The oath is sworn without any specific intention (niyyah) for literal or figurative meaning. This triggers the default rule.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position and his principle
Abu Hanifa holds that the oath refers to the literal meaning: drinking directly from the river (kir') and eating the actual wheat grains.
His principle: al-ḥaqīqah aṣlu al-kalām (the literal meaning is the default).
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position and their principle
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the oath refers to the customary meaning: water attributed to the Euphrates (even if scooped or from a canal) and anything made from wheat (e.g., bread).
Their principle: al-ʿurf yaḥkumu al-kalām (custom governs the understanding).
4
Note the thamarah al-khilaf on specific cases
For example, drinking from a canal from the Euphrates: Abu Hanifa says no violation; Sahibayn say violation if the water is still customarily attributed to the Euphrates. Similarly, for specified wheat, Abu Hanifa restricts to grains; Sahibayn include bread.
The difference impacts many practical oath scenarios.
5
Determine the tarjih
The author (Ibn Abd al-Shakur) states that the more appropriate (ashbah) view is that of the Sahibayn, and it is the relied-upon position.
Imam Abu Hanifa is noted as inconsistent on this principle.

Commentary

The Rule of 'Lakin' for Istidrak and Its Conditions
Defining 'lakin' and its role in correcting misunderstandings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of istidrak, identify the conditions for using 'lakin' for istidrak, and apply them to the case of acknowledgment.

Core Idea
Lakin corrects misunderstanding only if the preceding and following differ in affirmation/negation.

This lesson introduces the usul principle behind the Arabic particle 'lakin' (but) when used to correct a misunderstanding (istidrak). In Hanafi jurisprudence, understanding how 'lakin' operates is crucial for interpreting contractual statements and acknowledgment. We'll break down its definition, its two syntactic functions, and the condition of consistency (ittiraad).

Guided Path
1
Define istidrak
State the meaning: removing misunderstanding arising from the preceding speech.
Istidrak is a tool to correct a wrong inference.
2
Identify the condition
The preceding and following statements must differ in affirmation/negation. This is the core condition for 'lakin' to function as istidrak.
If both are affirmative or both negative, it cannot be istidrak.
3
Distinguish syntactic functions
If 'lakin' is followed by a complete sentence, it is a particle of beginning (huruf ibtida') and not for istidrak. If followed by a single word, it is a conjunction (waw) and requires consistency: the loci of negation/affirmation must differ.
When used as a conjunction, the default is to treat it as such if possible.
4
Apply to the acknowledgment case
When the acknowledged says 'No, but stolen' in response to a claim of loan, analyze how it corrects the cause: it denies the stated cause (loan) and introduces a new cause (usurpation).
If disconnected, it would be a rejection; connection shows change.

Issue: 'Lakin' light and heavy is for istidrak, which is removing the misunderstanding arising from the preceding. Its condition is that the preceding and following speech differ in affirmation and negation. Even if the difference is in meaning and 'lakin' comes for emphasis too, as in 'If he had come I would have honored him, but he did not come.' When 'lakin' light is followed by a sentence, it is a particle of beginning, and then it is not for explained istidrak. Or if it is followed by a single word, it is a conjunction, and the condition of conjunction is consistency, i.e., the loci of negation and affirmation are not identical. The conjunction is the original, so it is applied as much as possible. Thus, the statement of the acknowledged (in response to the acknowledgment) 'No, but stolen' in answer to the acknowledgment 'I have one hundred as a loan' is correct, and the acknowledged's statement is not a rejection of the acknowledgment but a denial of the cause he mentioned (the loan) and a statement of another cause using the istidrak word, which is usurpation. If there were no istidrak with 'lakin', the apparent would be rejection. Istidrak is a statement of change, so connection is necessary and it is not accepted disconnected. Contrary to the case of one who is informed of his slave girl's marriage with one hundred: a guardian married a man's slave girl for one hundred without the master's permission, then he was informed and said: 'I do not authorize the marriage with one hundred, but with two hundred' as in the principles of Imam Fakhr al-Islam and al-Badi'. His words 'but with two hundred' are taken as a new beginning authorizing another marriage with a dowry of two hundred. This is because the speech would be inconsistent if made conjunctive, since with the denial of authorization the first contract has become void, and the void does not return to become valid by authorization. Shaykh Ibn al-Humam said: If the master said 'I do not authorize the marriage but with two hundred', the speech is inconsistent because the loci of affirmation and negation are identical, due to the negation of the entire marriage by his denial; then beginning it with another amount after dissolution is impossible, unlike 'I do not authorize it with one hundred but with two hundred', because the istidrak is on the amount of the dowry, not the marriage itself. This contradicts the statement of Imam Fakhr al-Islam and al-Badi' and other reliable books, so the transmission must be corrected from those whose transmission contradicts these authorities. Then the distinction is also insufficient, because the 'lam' here is for the known [particular], as it is the earlier considered, so the meaning is 'I do not authorize this marriage which is for one hundred, but for two hundred.' Yes, an objection to the original statement: inconsistency is not granted because negation may apply to the dowry, i.e., 'I do not authorize the marriage with a dowry of one hundred, but I authorize this very marriage with a dowry of two hundred.' This is supported by the fact that the basis of the restricted ruling is the restriction; if it is negation, the intention is negation of the restriction, not the original ruling. In affirmation likewise. So then the object of negation and affirmation are different. The answer: The intended object of authorization or its absence is what was suspended on authorization, and what is suspended is the marriage contracted by the guardian, which is the marriage restricted to a dowry of one hundred. With the absence of authorization, this suspended matter has become void. Even if the intention of negating authorization is the restriction, it is intended only within the negation of the restricted, not that the restricted is established and the restriction is negated, which is incoherent. Nor is it established in another restricted, as that requires proof. It is apparent that there is one marriage suspended on the master's authorization, and it has become void, so it cannot be authorized for two hundred. It is a new beginning. If it is said: his intention is dissatisfaction with this restriction and restriction with two hundred, that is imposing something the husband did not undertake, unless it is said that he intends authorization conditionally: 'but I authorize this marriage restricted to two hundred if the husband accepts', but the wording does not support that. In summary, the suspended matter was the restricted, and it has fallen due to absence of authorization, so it does not return, and there is no other contract to which authorization can attach. Therefore, it must be a new beginning authorizing another marriage. And Allah knows best His rulings. (Branch): If a man acknowledges 'I owe one thousand', and the acknowledged says 'It was not mine but (lakin) it is for so-and-so', then the acknowledged's statement 'It was not mine but for so-and-so' is apparent in rejection i.e., rejection of the acknowledgment, and it is possible that it implies transfer (conversion) to so-and-so upon him, so the debt first belonged to the acknowledged then became for so-and-so by conversion. Since conversion is a change from the apparent meaning of the speech, it is valid only if his words 'but for so-and-so' are connected, because the first depends on the end, and this modifies it, so it is valid when connected and not valid when disconnected, because the statement of change is not separate and speech does not depend on something separate from it. Imam Fakhr al-Islam framed the issue regarding a slave, where the acknowledged says 'It was never mine, but for so-and-so'. According to this, conversion is not valid. Rather the preferred view is that his statement is apparent in rejection, but it is possible that his intention is that the slave or debt, even if well-known as my slave or debt, is in reality for so-and-so, so after accepting his acknowledgment for himself, it becomes an acknowledgment for another, and 'but for so-and-so' is a contextual indication. If it is connected, it is not a rejection; if it is disconnected, the rejection is complete, and his statement 'but for so-and-so' is not heard. So reflect.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and application of the particle 'lakin' (لكن) as used in Hanafi usul for istidrak (correction of misunderstanding). The author first defines 'lakin' heavy and light as a tool for removing a misunderstanding that arose from the preceding statement. The condition is that the preceding and following parts differ in affirmation (positive) and negation (negative). It can also be used for emphasis. The author then distinguishes when 'lakin' is followed by a complete sentence (then it is a particle of beginning, not for istidrak) vs. when followed by a single word (then it is a conjunction). As a conjunction, it requires that the loci of affirmation and negation are not the same. The default is to treat it as a conjunction when possible. The author applies these rules to two fiqh cases: (1) In acknowledgment: when the acknowledged says 'No, but usurped' in response to a claim of loan, it is not a rejection but a correction of the cause. (2) In the case of a marriage by a guardian without permission: if the master says 'I do not authorize the marriage with one hundred, but with two hundred', there is a dispute. According to Imam Fakhr al-Islam and al-Badi', it is taken as a new beginning (authorizing a new marriage) instead of a correction of the dowry, because the original marriage is void. Ibn al-Humam disagrees, arguing that if the negation targets the entire marriage, it cannot be revived. The preferred Hanafi view is that it is a new authorization of a different marriage. The branching case deals with acknowledgment of debt: if the acknowledged says 'It was not mine but for so-and-so', the apparent meaning is rejection, but it could be interpreted as a transfer (conversion) of the debt if the statement is connected. If disconnected, it is only rejection.

Key Terms
الاستدراك (al-istidrak): Removing a misunderstanding that arises from the preceding speech.
الاطراد (al-ittiraad): Consistency: the condition that the loci of negation and affirmation are not the same in conjunction.

The Khilaf in Interpreting 'Lakin' in the Marriage Authorization Case
Defining 'lakin' and its role in correcting misunderstandings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the master's statement 'I do not authorize the marriage with one hundred, but with two hundred' and compare it with the opposing view of Ibn al-Humam.

Core Idea
When the master denies authorization of a voidable marriage, the contract is void; 'lakin' starts a new authorization, not a correction of the dowry.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi dispute about the particle 'lakin' in a marriage authorization scenario. The master's statement creates ambiguity: does he authorize the same marriage with a higher dowry, or does he authorize a new marriage? We will see how the Hanafi masters (Fakhr al-Islam, al-Badi') resolve this using the rules of istidrak and the nature of void contracts.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
A guardian marries a slave girl without permission for 100; master says 'I do not authorize with 100, but with 200'.
2
Identify the Hanafi position
The statement is taken as a new beginning authorizing a new marriage with 200.
This is from Imam Fakhr al-Islam and al-Badi'.
3
Analyze the Hanafi dalil
The suspended marriage contract is void upon denial of authorization; it cannot be revived. Therefore, 'lakin' cannot correct the dowry because the contract no longer exists.
The original contract is one unit restricted to 100.
4
Present the opposing view (Ibn al-Humam)
Ibn al-Humam argues that the negation is on the dowry amount, not the marriage itself. Thus, istidrak is possible, and the same marriage can be authorized with a higher dowry.
He distinguishes between negating the contract and negating a condition.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi position is relied upon because the suspended contract is void and cannot be the subject of authorization.
Imam Fakhr al-Islam and al-Badi' are considered authoritative.

This is supported by the fact that the basis of the restricted ruling is the restriction; if it is negation, the intention is negation of the restriction, not the original ruling. In affirmation likewise. So then the object of negation and affirmation are different. The answer: The intended object of authorization or its absence is what was suspended on authorization, and what is suspended is the marriage contracted by the guardian, which is the marriage restricted to a dowry of one hundred. With the absence of authorization, this suspended matter has become void. Even if the intention of negating authorization is the restriction, it is intended only within the negation of the restricted, not that the restricted is established and the restriction is negated, which is incoherent. Nor is it established in another restricted, as that requires proof. It is apparent that there is one marriage suspended on the master's authorization, and it has become void, so it cannot be authorized for two hundred. It is a new beginning. If it is said: his intention is dissatisfaction with this restriction and restriction with two hundred, that is imposing something the husband did not undertake, unless it is said that he intends authorization conditionally: 'but I authorize this marriage restricted to two hundred if the husband accepts', but the wording does not support that. In summary, the suspended matter was the restricted, and it has fallen due to absence of authorization, so it does not return, and there is no other contract to which authorization can attach. Therefore, it must be a new beginning authorizing another marriage. And Allah knows best His rulings. (Branch): If a man acknowledges 'I owe one thousand', and the acknowledged says 'It was not mine but (lakin) it is for so-and-so', then the acknowledged's statement 'It was not mine but for so-and-so' is apparent in rejection i.e., rejection of the acknowledgment, and it is possible that it implies transfer (conversion) to so-and-so upon him, so the debt first belonged to the acknowledged then became for so-and-so by conversion. Since conversion is a change from the apparent meaning of the speech, it is valid only if his words 'but for so-and-so' are connected, because the first depends on the end, and this modifies it, so it is valid when connected and not valid when disconnected, because the statement of change is not separate and speech does not depend on something separate from it. Imam Fakhr al-Islam framed the issue regarding a slave, where the acknowledged says 'It was never mine, but for so-and-so'. According to this, conversion is not valid. Rather the preferred view is that his statement is apparent in rejection, but it is possible that his intention is that the slave or debt, even if well-known as my slave or debt, is in reality for so-and-so, so after accepting his acknowledgment for himself, it becomes an acknowledgment for another, and 'but for so-and-so' is a contextual indication. If it is connected, it is not a rejection; if it is disconnected, the rejection is complete, and his statement 'but for so-and-so' is not heard. So reflect.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and application of the particle 'lakin' (لكن) as used in Hanafi usul for istidrak (correction of misunderstanding). The author first defines 'lakin' heavy and light as a tool for removing a misunderstanding that arose from the preceding statement. The condition is that the preceding and following parts differ in affirmation (positive) and negation (negative). It can also be used for emphasis. The author then distinguishes when 'lakin' is followed by a complete sentence (then it is a particle of beginning, not for istidrak) vs. when followed by a single word (then it is a conjunction). As a conjunction, it requires that the loci of affirmation and negation are not the same. The default is to treat it as a conjunction when possible. The author applies these rules to two fiqh cases: (1) In acknowledgment: when the acknowledged says 'No, but usurped' in response to a claim of loan, it is not a rejection but a correction of the cause. (2) In the case of a marriage by a guardian without permission: if the master says 'I do not authorize the marriage with one hundred, but with two hundred', there is a dispute. According to Imam Fakhr al-Islam and al-Badi', it is taken as a new beginning (authorizing a new marriage) instead of a correction of the dowry, because the original marriage is void. Ibn al-Humam disagrees, arguing that if the negation targets the entire marriage, it cannot be revived. The preferred Hanafi view is that it is a new authorization of a different marriage. The branching case deals with acknowledgment of debt: if the acknowledged says 'It was not mine but for so-and-so', the apparent meaning is rejection, but it could be interpreted as a transfer (conversion) of the debt if the statement is connected. If disconnected, it is only rejection.

الاستدراك (al-istidrak): Removing a misunderstanding that arises from the preceding speech.
الاطراد (al-ittiraad): Consistency: the condition that the loci of negation and affirmation are not the same in conjunction.

Cautionary Figurative Interpretation in Amān
Resolving equivalence between Hanafi and Hanbali on a furu'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of cautionary figurative interpretation (tajawwuz li al-ihtiyat) to determine whether a general expression extends to include implied categories in matters of amān.

Core Idea
In cautionary matters, Hanafis may figuratively expand a term, but expansion remains case-specific.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut discusses a reported agreement between the Hanafi and Hanbali schools on including daughters in amān granted for 'sons'. The reasoning relies on a key usul principle: caution (ihtiyat) justifies interpreting the term figuratively as general. However, as the author notes, this does not transform the literal meaning into actual generality. Understanding this tension between caution and literalism is crucial for applying similar rules in other contexts.

Practical Custom as a Mukhassis: The Hanafi Position vs. Shafi'is
Arguing that practical custom specifies the general, contra Shafi'is

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi view on practical custom as a mukhassis, state the Shafi'i objection, and evaluate the Hanafi response based on the criterion of tabaddur.

Core Idea
Practical custom specifies the general through immediate understanding, just like verbal custom.

This passage addresses a key dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is: can practical custom (urf amali) specify the general (takhsis al-amm)? The Hanafi school affirms that it can, based on the immediate understanding of the speakers. The Shafi'is deny this, arguing that practical custom can only restrict a absolute term but not specify a general. The author defends the Hanafi position and refutes the Shafi'i arguments, relying on induction and the concept of tabaddur.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
What is the point of disagreement? The Hanafis hold that practical custom can specify a general term; Shafi'is restrict specification to verbal custom only. The example: the word 'meat' (lahm) – if the custom is to eat only beef, Hanafis say it specifies beef.
Verbal custom (urf qawli) is agreed upon as a mukhassis by both schools.
2
State the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof relies on immediate understanding (tabaddur). In the command 'Buy meat,' if the custom is to eat lamb, the immediate understanding is lamb. This tabaddur exists in practical custom as it does in verbal custom. Additionally, induction (istiqra) from many cases supports that tabaddur to a non-literal meaning necessitates metaphorical intent.
The author calls induction a valid proof, not qiyas fi al-lughah.
3
State the Shafi'i objection
Shafi'is argue that practical custom can only restrict a mutlaq (absolute), not specify an 'amm (general). They claim that in restriction, the absolute remains, but in specification the general's meaning changes. Also, analogizing 'amm to mutlaq is qiyas fi al-lughah, which is invalid.
They cite the phrase 'Buy meat' as a case of restricting the absolute, not specifying the general.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response dismisses the distinction between taqyid and takhsis because the criterion is tabaddur, which is present in both. As for the charge of qiyas fi al-lughah, the author argues it is induction (istiqra), not analogy. Induction from many cases shows that tabaddur determines meaning.
The author concludes with 'Ponder, for it is the truth', adopting the Hanafi view.
5
Determine the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilaf: In Hanafi fiqh, a general command like 'Buy meat' is restricted to the customary meat; in Shafi'i fiqh, it remains general unless custom is verbal, so buying any meat would fulfill the command.
This affects everyday transactions and legal interpretations.
