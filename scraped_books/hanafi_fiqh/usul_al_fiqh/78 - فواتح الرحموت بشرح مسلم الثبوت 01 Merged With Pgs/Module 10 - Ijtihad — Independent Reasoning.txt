Reason Obligating Faith Before Revelation: The Hanafi View
Discussion on reason's independent role in obligating faith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on reason's capacity to obligate faith before revelation and apply the distinction between immediate intellect and after reflection to determine accountability.

Core Idea
Reason alone, after reflection, obligates faith and prohibits disbelief—even without revelation.

This lesson explores the Hanafi view on whether reason can independently derive rulings before revelation. The majority of Hanafis, including al-Maturidi and Fakhr al-Islam, hold that the obligation of faith and the prohibition of disbelief are rationally evident, but with a crucial nuance: accountability begins only after a sufficient period of reflection. We will unpack the textual basis (a narration from Abu Hanifa) and its interpretation, including an analogy from Hanafi financial law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule
State the core Hanafi principle: reason can independently obligate faith and prohibit disbelief before revelation. This applies even without a prophetic call.
The rule is attributed to 'most Hanafis'.
2
Examine the Basis
Analyze the narration from Abu Hanifa: 'There is no excuse for anyone in ignorance of his Creator, due to the evidences of oneness.' This is the primary textual support.
The narration implies an obligation based on clear rational evidence.
3
Interpret the Narration
Understand that the 'no excuse' applies only after a period of reflection (muddat al-ta'ammul). The period varies per individual and is not fixed.
Fakhr al-Islam interprets reflection as akin to a prophet's call in awakening the heart.
4
Apply the Analogy
Relate this to the Hanafi rule that a foolish person (safih) is not restricted from property after age 25, as they have had sufficient trial. Similarly, after adequate reflection, one is accountable for faith.
No decisive proof for a specific limit; it depends on the person's intellect.
5
Distinguish from Mu'tazila
Both agree reason can know good and bad, but the Hanafis excuse at the very beginning of intellect, whereas Mu'tazila may not. This nuance prevents conflating the two positions.
The text explicitly states 'no difference' in the outcome of reason's role, but the Hanafi view adds an initial grace period.

Commentary
This passage presents the view of the majority of Hanafi scholars (Maturidi, Fakhr al-Islam, etc.) that reason can independently arrive at certain rulings before revelation: the obligation of faith in God and the prohibition of disbelief and anything unbecoming of Him. This applies to everyone, even if no prophet has reached them, including an intelligent child. The basis is a narration from Imam Abu Hanifa stating that there is no excuse for ignorance of the Creator given the clear evidences of oneness. However, the author clarifies that the 'no excuse' applies after a period of reflection, which varies per person. Fakhr al-Islam compares it to the Hanafi rule that a foolish person reaching 25 years of age is considered mature. This interpretation distinguishes between immediate obligation at the onset of intellect and obligation after sufficient time for reflection. The passage ends by noting that this Hanafi position effectively agrees with the Mu'tazila that some moral values are known by reason, but the difference lies in the scope and method.

Key Terms
الإيمان (al-iman): Belief in God, which reason obligates by inference from evidence.
مدة التأمل (muddat al-ta'ammul): The period of reflection needed for a rational person to infer the existence of God; varies between individuals.

Reason's Independent Knowledge of Rulings: The Hanafi Position
Hanafi position that reason can independently know obligation of Iman and prohibition of kufr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on reason's independent knowledge of rulings and explain the role of the period of reflection (muddat al-ta'ammul) in accountability.

Core Idea
Reason can independently know Iman is obligatory and Kufr is haram, but only after a period of reflection.

This passage delves into a distinctive Hanafi usul claim: that human reason ('aql) can perceive certain divine rulings without prophetic revelation. Specifically, the obligation of faith (Iman) and the prohibition of disbelief (Kufr) are knowable by reason alone, even for those who never received a prophet's call. However, this is not an instant liability; a period of reflection is required before one becomes accountable. The author traces this view to most Hanafis, citing a narration from Abu Hanifa, and then unpacks its conditions through an analogy with a foolish person's maturity. By the end, you will see how this fits into the broader Hanafi stance on the role of reason.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi Claim
Recognize that the text asserts a group of Hanafis hold reason can independently know some rulings, namely wajib (Iman) and haram (Kufr), without any external call.
This is a 'usul dispute' between Hanafis and Ash'aris; the passage argues for the Hanafi view.
2
Trace the Evidential Basis
Locate the narration from Imam Abu Hanifa: 'There is no excuse for ignorance of the Creator given the clear proofs.' Understand how this serves as the primary dalil for the position.
The narration is a qawl of a scholar, not a prophetic hadith, but is considered authoritative among Hanafis.
3
Analyze the Interpretation (Tawjih)
The author explains that 'no excuse' applies only after a period of reflection. Reflect on why this period is necessary and why it cannot be fixed universally.
The period varies with individual intellectual capacity; it's akin to the call of a messenger in awakening the heart.
4
Examine the Analogical Support
Study the analogy from Abu Hanifa's ruling on the foolish person (safih) who reaches 25 years—his property is no longer withheld because he has completed the period of experience. See how this supports the idea of a required reflection period.
This analogy (qiyas) strengthens the argument that a person must be given time to reflect before accountability.
5
Apply the Derived Rulings
Consider the two concrete outcomes: (a) if a person at the beginning of intellect has no belief, he is excused; (b) if he believes disbelief, he is not excused. Understand the reasoning behind each.
Belief in kufr after the reflection period indicates deliberate rejection of the truth.

Commentary
This passage presents a key Hanafi usul position: that human reason ('aql) can independently know some divine rulings without prophetic revelation. Specifically, the obligation of Iman (faith) and the prohibition of Kufr (disbelief) and anything improper to God are knowable by reason alone, even for those who have not received any prophet's call, including sane children. This view is attributed to most Hanafis like al-Maturidi, Fakhr al-Islam, and Sadr al-Shari'a. The textual basis is a narration from Imam Abu Hanifa: 'There is no excuse for anyone in ignorance of the Creator' because the proofs for God's oneness are so clear that no rational person can deny them—denial is due to flawed understanding, not genuine doubt. The author then explains this narration: the absence of excuse applies only after a 'period of reflection' (muddat al-ta'ammul), during which one is expected to contemplate the proofs. This period is not fixed but varies with individual intellect. Fakhr al-Islam's tawjih (interpretation) uses an analogy from Abu Hanifa's ruling on the foolish person (safih): if he reaches 25 years of age, his property is not withheld because he has completed the period of experience and should have gained maturity. Similarly, one must be given sufficient time to reflect before being held accountable. From this, two derived rulings: (1) If a person at the beginning of intellect does not believe anything (neither faith nor disbelief), he is excused because the reflection period hasn't passed; (2) If he believes disbelief, he is not excused because he consciously chose kufr over iman, showing he reflected and rejected the truth.

Key Terms
العقل (al-'aql): The human faculty of reason that, according to some Hanafis, can independently perceive certain divine rulings like the obligation of faith and prohibition of disbelief.
مدة التأمل (muddat al-ta'ammul): The period of reflection considered necessary for a rational person to contemplate the proofs for God's existence and arrive at faith; it varies per individual.

The Four Premises on Causality and Free Will
Establishing rational foundations for free will in divine action

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four premises of Sadr al-Sharia's proof for free will and explain how they rebut the necessitarian (al-illah al-mujibah) position.

Core Idea
Free will is real: the complete cause includes the agent's choice and relational matters, not mere determinism.

In this passage from Fawatih al-Rahmut, Sadr al-Sharia constructs a rational argument (dalil aqli) to defend human free choice (ikhtiyar) against necessitarian views. He lays down four premises about causality and existence, culminating in the conclusion that an agent can freely preponderate one of two equal alternatives. Understanding this argument is essential for grasping the Hanafi stance on ikhtiyar, which underpins their legal theory of obligation (taklif).

Guided Path
1
Identify the first premise
The distinction between masdar (source noun) as mental concept vs. external state. This sets up that acts have two aspects: the abstract meaning and the concrete occurrence.
This distinction is foundational for analyzing voluntary acts.
2
Explain the second premise
The existence of a contingent is necessary when its complete cause is present. Both Ahl al-Sunna and philosophers accept this, but for Hanafis the complete cause includes the agent's will.
The divergence lies in whether the cause is necessitating or includes choice.
3
Outline the third premise
The cause of events must include relational matters (idafat) that are neither existent nor non-existent. This avoids infinite regress and preserves free choice.
Relational matters like 'bringing about' (iqa') are not necessary; they depend on the agent's will.
4
State the fourth premise
A free chooser can preponderate one of two equals (or even the inferior) without external determinant. This is actual because otherwise no contingent would exist.
This premise directly affirms free will against determinism.
5
Apply the premises to the objection
The text uses these premises to show that the act as an existing state is necessary given its complete cause (which includes free choice), while the 'bringing about' (iqa') is not necessary but freely chosen.
The objection that complete cause implies jabr is refuted by including idafat and choice in the cause.

Commentary
This passage from Fawatih al-Rahmut, a commentary on Musallam al-Thubut, presents a rational argument for human free choice (ikhtiyar) within a Hanafi theological framework. The author, Sadr al-Sharia, lays down four premises (muqaddimat) to prove that an agent can freely choose between equal alternatives without compulsion. This is a classic Hanafi stance against the deterministic views of certain philosophers and theologians. The four premises are: (1) Words for actions (masadir) can refer either to the abstract meaning or the concrete external state; (2) A contingent thing's existence is necessary only when all conditions are met, otherwise impossible; (3) The cause of events must include 'relational' matters (idafat) that are neither existent nor non-existent, avoiding infinite regress or impossibility; (4) A free agent can prefer one of two equals without external determinant. The passage then applies these premises to answer an objection: if the act is taken as the existing state, it is necessary given its complete cause, but that cause includes the agent's free choice. The commentator later critiques this argument, but the core is a rational proof for free will consistent with Ahl al-Sunna.

Key Terms
اختيار (ikhtiyar): Free choice, the ability of a moral agent to preponderate one of two equally possible actions.
علة تامة ('illah tammah): The complete cause: the collection of all conditions whose existence necessitates the effect.
إضافة (idafah): Relational matters that are neither existent nor non-existent, such as the relation of bringing about (iqa').

The Six Types of Ignorance (Jahl) in Hanafi Usul
Classification of ignorance into six types with distinct legal effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify each of the six types of ignorance by their legal effect (excuse or not) and explain the Hanafi position that jahl al-kafir repels taklif.

Core Idea
Ignorance is not a single excuse – the Hanafis distinguish six types, each with its own legal consequence.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the acquired impediments to legal capacity, focusing on ignorance (jahl). He classifies ignorance into six distinct types, ranging from the obstinate ignorance of a disbeliever to the excusable ignorance of a Muslim in dar al-harb. Understanding this classification is crucial for deriving correct rulings, especially in matters of marriage, crime, and worship. We will walk through each type and its legal effect, noting where the Hanafi school differs from al-Shafi'i and within itself.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish celestial vs acquired impediments
The author notes two categories of impediments ('awarid): celestial (samaawiyya) and acquired (muktasaba). Our focus is on acquired, specifically jahl.
Celestial impediments include death, insanity, etc. – not covered here.
2
Identify the first type: jahl al-kafir
This is the ignorance of a disbeliever who rejects clear proofs. According to the Hanafis, this ignorance repels the divine address (taklif) – so the disbeliever is not obligated with furu' until he believes. Contrast with al-Shafi'i who holds that taklif applies.
The practical example: marriages of Magians among mahrams are valid according to Abu Hanifa, but invalid according to the Sahibayn.
3
Identify types 2-4: degrees of ignorance with different excuses
Type 2: ignorance of misguided sects (e.g., Mu'tazila) – not an excuse, but less severe than kufr. Type 3: ignorance from ijtihad in matters not open to ijtihad (contradicting qat'i texts) – excuse from sin but not from judgment. Type 4: ignorance in matters open to ijtihad – a complete excuse.
For type 3, a judge's ruling based on such ignorance is invalid.
4
Identify types 5-6: excusable ignorance
Type 5: ignorance due to shubha (doubt/error) – drops hadd punishment. Type 6: ignorance due to necessity (e.g., Muslim in dar al-harb unaware of rulings) – also drops hadd.
These are excuses for the most part.
5
Apply the classification to practical cases
For each type, the author gives concrete legal effects. Work through the examples: Magian marriage (type 1), usurpation by misguided sects (type 2), sale of unslaughtered animal (type 3), etc.
Focus on the Hanafi position and the intra-madhhab differences.

Commentary
This passage introduces the topic of impediments ('awarid) to legal capacity (ahliyyah). The author notes that the original matn (Musallam al-Thubut) omitted most of these, so he will mention them. He divides impediments into celestial (samaawiyya) and acquired (muktasaba), and begins with the acquired impediment of ignorance (jahl). He then lists six types of jahl: (1) the ignorance of the disbeliever (kafir), which is never an excuse in this world; (2) the ignorance of misguided Muslim sects (like Mu'tazila, Rawafid, Khawarij) due to ta'wil; (3) ignorance from ijtihad in matters not open to ijtihad (khilaf al-nass al-qat'i); (4) ignorance from ijtihad in permissible matters (mujtahadat); (5) ignorance due to doubt and mistake (shubha) in acts like intercourse; (6) ignorance due to necessity (like being in dar al-harb). For each type, the author gives specific legal effects, such as the validity of marriages of Magians among mahrams according to Abu Hanifa (disputed by the Sahibayn), or the dropping of hadd punishments. The passage also includes a usul dispute with al-Shafi'i regarding whether the ignorance of the kafir repels the divine address. According to the Hanafis, it does, whereas al-Shafi'i holds that the address still applies.

Hanafi vs Shafi'i: The Illa of Riba in Non-Measured Items
States Hanafi permissibility of selling non-kayli items with disparity, contrasting Shafi'i view.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on riba in non-kayli items, contrast it with the Shafi'i illa of ta'm, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference).

Core Idea
Riba requires kayl; non-measured items may be sold unequally.

This lesson dives into a classic usul al-fiqh dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools concerning the effective cause (illa) of riba in commodity exchanges. The Hanafis restrict riba to items measured by volume (kayl) when traded with their own kind, while the Shafi'is extend it to all food items based on taste/edibility (ta'm). Understanding how each school derives its position from the same hadith will sharpen your skills in analyzing textual evidence and identifying the role of qiyas in madhhab differences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi Position and Its Illa
State the Hanafi ruling: selling non-kayli items of the same kind in unequal amounts is permissible and not riba. Then identify the illa: kayl (volume measurement) combined with same genus.
The illa is taken from the explicit mention of kayl in the hadith versions.
2
Identify the Shafi'i Position and Its Illa
State the Shafi'i prohibition: all food items, whether kayli or not, are subject to riba when sold with disparity. Their illa is ta'm (taste/edibility).
The Shafi'is argue that the hadith's general prohibition on 'food for food' covers all food.
3
Analyze the Hadith and Its Interpretation
Examine the hadith of the six items. Note that the word 'kayl' appears explicitly in some narrations. Understand how the Hanafis use this to restrict the illa, while the Shafi'is focus on the category 'ta'am' (food) in the general part.
The Hanafi reading treats the exception 'except equal for equal' as a specification (takhsis) of the general prohibition, applying only to kayli items.
4
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Apply the positions to a concrete example: selling walnuts (non-kayli) in unequal amounts. Hanafis permit it; Shafi'is prohibit it because walnuts are food.
This practical difference highlights the real-world impact of the illa selection.

Commentary
This passage presents the third benefit (fa'ida), which highlights a key point of dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is regarding riba (usury). The Hanafis hold that riba only applies to items that are measured by volume (kayl) when exchanged with their own kind. Therefore, items that are not customarily measured by kayl (e.g., items sold by weight or count) may be sold in unequal quantities without being considered riba. The Shafi'is, however, use 'ta'm' (taste or edibility) as the effective cause (illa), so all foodstuffs are subject to riba. The text provides the hadith about six specified items (gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, salt) and notes that the word 'kayl' appears explicitly in some versions. Imam Fakhr al-Islam (al-Bazdawi) argues that the exception 'except equal for equal' in the hadith specifies only those items that are equally measured; thus, by the general negative 'do not sell food for food', all unequal sales are prohibited except where equality is met. This leads to the Hanafi conclusion that only items subject to kayl and within the same genus are ribawi. The passage cuts off while explaining that the exception only addressed equality, implying that non-kayli items are not covered by the prohibition of the first part.
