Sarih and Kinaya: How Clarity of Meaning Affects Legal Rulings
Classification of haqiqa and majaz by clarity of meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sarih and kinaya utterances in Hanafi usul and apply the rule for when intention is required to establish a legal effect.

Core Idea
Sarih establishes the ruling without intention; kinaya requires niyya or a qarina.

In Hanafi legal theory, not all words are equal: some are so clear that the ruling follows immediately, while others hide the speaker's true intent. This classification—sarih (explicit) versus kinaya (implicit)—governs everything from divorce pronouncements to contracts. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly deriving rulings from texts and utterances.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the classification criterion
The passage divides haqiqa and majaz based on whether the intended meaning is immediately understood (tabaddur al-murad) or hidden.
This is the foundational split that determines the need for intention.
2
Define sarih and its ruling
Sarih is when the meaning is apparent; the legal effect is established by the utterance itself without need for intention. Examples: explicit divorce (anti taliq) and manumission (anti hurr).
Sarih is binding even if the speaker did not intend the effect.
3
Identify examples of sarih
The passage lists: common words specialized in one meaning, conventional majaz whose literal has fallen into disuse, and majaz accompanied by a clear qarina (like 'this is my son'). Even a pronoun can be sarih if context makes the meaning clear.
Context (qarina) can elevate a normally ambiguous word to sarih.
4
Define kinaya and its ruling
Kinaya is when the meaning is hidden; the legal effect requires intention (niyya) or a qarina that indicates the intended meaning.
Without intention or context, kinaya has no legal effect.
5
Note the categories of hidden speech
The passage mentions khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih, and obscure majaz with hidden qarina. These are further gradations of unclear speech.
These categories help in analyzing ambiguous texts.

The Hanafi Ruling on Kināyah al-Talāq and the Shafi'i Dispute
Explaining the Hanafi ruling on kināyah al-talāq and the dispute with al-Shafi'i.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that kināyah expressions of divorce effect a bā'in divorce and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument based on the hadith of Sawda and the Quranic verse.

Core Idea
Kināyah expressions are irrevocable (bā'in) because they indicate separation (baynuna), not the legal concept of talaq.

This passage explores a key usul dispute: when a husband uses an indirect expression like 'Anti bā'in' intending divorce, does the divorce become revocable (raj'i) or irrevocable (bā'in)? The Hanafi school argues that the literal meaning of 'bā'in' is separation, not talaq, so the divorce is bā'in. The Shafi'i school holds that with intention, it becomes like explicit talaq and is raj'i. We will analyze the proofs for each view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position on kināyah al-talāq
Read the opening statement: kināyah expressions effect a bā'in divorce; the husband cannot take his wife back except with a new marriage.
The text says 'لا يملك الزوج الرجوع إليها إلا بنكاح جديد'.
2
Understand the Shafi'i objection and its proof
The Shafi'i argue that when the intention is determined, kināyah becomes like sarih (explicit), and sarih divorce is raj'i. They also cite the hadith of Sawda where the Prophet used 'I'taddī' and then took her back.
Identify the opposing dalil: the hadith and the analogy to sarih.
3
Examine the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i proof
The Hanafi response: the hadith does not show an actual divorce; the Prophet intended but refrained. Also, kināyah retains its literal meaning of baynuna; it does not become talaq.
The text clarifies that 'I'taddī' does not indicate baynuna at all.
4
Analyze the Quranic verse (2:229) as evidence for the Hanafi view
The verse attaches ruj'ah (return) to 'talaq', not to 'baynuna'. Therefore, only explicit talaq allows ruj'ah. Kināyah, indicating baynuna, does not allow ruj'ah.
The verse: 'الطَّلَقُ مَرَّتَانِ فَإِمْسَاكُ بِمَعْرُوفٍ...'
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Hanafi law, a husband who says 'Anti bā'in' cannot take his wife back without a new marriage. Under Shafi'i law, he can take her back during the 'iddah.
This difference affects reconciliation options.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the Hanafi ruling on indirect expressions (kinaayat) of divorce, with a dispute against al-Shafi'i. The author begins by stating the Hanafi position: expressions like 'Anti bā'in' (You are separate) effect an irrevocable divorce (bā'in), meaning the husband cannot take his wife back except through a new marriage. However, if the intention is a severe separation by three divorces, then a new marriage requires tahlil (the wife marrying another and divorcing). Then the author examines specific phrases: 'I'taddī' (Observe your waiting period), citing the hadith of the Prophet ﷺ divorcing Sawda with that word and later taking her back. The author clarifies, quoting 'Matali' al-Asrar', that the hadith actually shows he did not divorce her; it was a threat, and he refrained. The author dismisses the idea that these phrases are revocable, arguing that 'I'taddī' does not indicate separation at all. The Shafi'i position is then presented: according to them, such metonyms are revocable because when the intended meaning is determined (by intention), they become like explicit expressions (sarih), and explicit divorce is revocable. The Hanafi response is that baynuna remains in its original meaning; it is not the same as 'talaq'. The word 'bā'in' could mean separation from anything; it is ambiguous. Even if the intention specifies it is from marriage, the word still conveys actual separation (baynuna), not the legal concept of talaq. Therefore, it effects a bā'in divorce (irrevocable). The author then presents an investigation from 'Matali' al-Asrar': the Lawgiver has defined talaq as removal of the marriage tie, and the Quran (2:229) indicates that after talaq (in the sense of the first or second divorce), the husband may take back (ruj'ah) with any word. This shows that ruj'ah is allowed after any form of talaq that is not a final third. The author argues that since the metonyms indicate baynuna, but the Lawgiver has only attached ruj'ah to talaq, not every baynuna, we cannot assume ruj'ah applies. The verse shows that only talaq (explicit) is followed by ruj'ah. The author concludes with a citation from the shaykh: a light bā'in divorce (i.e., one not involving wealth) is nothing special to him. The author then adds his own reflection: the Quranic verse describes sequential divorces, each either followed by retention (ruj'ah) or release, and that taking wealth is only allowed in case of nushuz. The passage ends mid-sentence.

The Letter Fa' In Usul: Defining Immediate Sequence (Ta'qib)
Defining the fa' as indicating immediate succession without customary delay

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the usul meaning of fa' as indicating immediate succession and apply the relativity principle to determine customary delay in different contexts.

Core Idea
Fa' means immediacy relative to custom: no fixed time, only customary interval.

In Hanafi usul, every particle carries legal weight. The letter fa' is not merely a conjunction but a marker of sequential immediacy. Correctly identifying its implication is crucial for deriving rulings from Quranic verses and hadith. This lesson unpacks the definition of fa' as indicating 'ta'qib' – succession without a customary break – and shows how context determines what counts as immediate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the particle and its function
The passage focuses on the Arabic letter fa'. Its primary function is to indicate sequence (al-tartib). Recognize that fa' always implies order, not simultaneity.
Distinguish from waw (و) which indicates conjunction without order.
2
Understand the meaning of ta'qib
Ta'qib means immediate succession – but 'immediate' is not absolute; it is defined by what is customary (urf). The legal principle is: no delay that custom would consider a break.
Key point: if custom treats a delay as acceptable, the fa' still holds.
3
Analyze the example from the Quran
The verse 'Then Satan caused them to slip... then brought them out' (2:36) uses fa' twice. The sequence is clear: slipping precedes removal. The fa' here orders events in a tight causal chain.
The order is in mention (zikrī) and in reality – both are bound by ta'qib.
4
Apply the relativity principle
Consider the example of marriage and childbirth: up to a year apart is still ta'qib because customarily this is the minimum possible delay. The same principle applies to other acts: what is 'immediate' depends on the nature of the act.
No fixed time limit – only customary expectation.

Does 'Innama' Indicate Hasr? Hanafi Position on Restriction
Analyzes whether 'innama' indicates hasr, with mantuq/mafhum subdebate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on whether 'innama' indicates hasr and identify the usul principles (mantuq/mafhum, generality) that govern its interpretation.

Core Idea
Innama does not inherently give hasr; any restriction comes from context or generality, not from the particle itself.

This passage dives into a core linguistic debate in usul al-fiqh: does the word 'innama' (إنما) inherently indicate restriction (hasr)? The Hanafi school holds a nuanced position: 'innama' is merely affirmative, and any apparent restriction is a function of the definiteness (al-) in the following noun or contextual evidence. Understanding this distinction is critical when interpreting texts like 'Innama al-a'malu bi al-niyyat'—the Hanafi reading relies on generality, not on 'innama' implying exclusivity. Let's walk through the arguments to grasp the Hanafi method.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core linguistic issue
Recognize that the debate centers on whether the compound particle 'innama' (inna + ma) functions to affirm and negate simultaneously (hasr) or merely affirms. The Hanafi claim is that 'ma' is extra (za'ida) and nullifies 'inna's operation, so it is only affirmative.
The opposing view (e.g., some Shafi'is) holds that 'inna' is for affirmation and 'ma' for negation, yielding restriction.
2
Analyze the Hanafi proof from the riba hadith
Examine the hadith 'Innama al-riba fi al-nasi'a' (Usury is only in credit). The Hanafis argue that this is not understood as restricting riba to credit sales alone, because riba also exists in excess (riba al-fadl). Thus restriction would contradict consensus; the intended meaning is affirmation, not hasr.
This demonstrates that 'innama' is not a proof of hasr; rather the generality of 'al-riba' (definite) is at play.
3
Understand the opposing argument and Hanafi response
The opposition cites 'Innama al-wala' liman a'taqa' (Wala is only for the one who frees) and claims it negates wala for others. The Hanafi response: the negation of others is from the generality of 'al-wala' (the definite article) combined with the context of the ruling, not from 'innama' itself.
The Hanafi rebuttal emphasizes that the mafhum (implied meaning) is derived from the definiteness of the noun, not the particle.
4
Distinguish mantuq vs mafhum in this context
Some scholars argue that hasr from 'innama' is from the mantuq (explicit wording) because it is what the word is assigned to; others say it is from mafhum (implication). The Hanafi position: 'innama' does not assign hasr at all; any restriction must be independently proven.
This distinction affects how one argues in istinbat: a Hanafi usuli will not use 'innama' alone as a proof for hasr.
5
Apply the principle to the hadith on intentions
When Hanafis use the hadith 'Innama al-a'malu bi al-niyyat' to argue for the conditionality of intention in wudu, they do not rely on 'innama' for hasr. Rather they rely on the generality of 'al-a'malu' (all deeds require intention). This illustrates the practical thamarah al-khilaf.
Thus in fiqh, a Shafi'i might take the hadith as a strong indicator that intention is exclusive, while a Hanafi treats it as a general principle subject to exceptions.

Key Terms
إنما (innama): A compound particle consisting of 'inna' (affirmation) and 'ma' (preventive extra), used for affirmation, not necessarily for restriction.
حصر (hasr): Restriction or limitation of a ruling to a specific case, implying negation of others.
