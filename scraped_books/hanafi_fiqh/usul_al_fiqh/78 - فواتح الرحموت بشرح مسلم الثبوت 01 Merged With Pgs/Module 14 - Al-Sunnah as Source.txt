Rulings from Secondary Sources as Kashif of Divine Speech
Responding to an objection about secondary sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that rulings from Sunnah, Ijma‘, and Qiyas are included in the definition of hukm as khitab Allah, and distinguish between kashif and muzhir.

Core Idea
Sunnah, Ijma‘, and Qiyas are revealers of Allah’s speech; the ruling is the divine khitab.

The definition of hukm as 'speech of Allah' appears to exclude rulings from Sunnah, Ijma‘, and Qiyas, since they are not directly the divine word. The Hanafi school responds by classifying these sources as kashif (revealers) of the divine speech, thereby including them in the definition. This lesson unpacks this response, explains why the Quranic text is still called speech, and explores why qiyas alone is termed muzhir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Recognize that if hukm is defined strictly as khitab Allah, rulings from Sunnah, Ijma‘, and Qiyas would be excluded because they are not direct divine speech.
This objection targets the comprehensiveness of the definition.
2
Articulate the Hanafi response using kashif
Explain that these secondary sources are kashif (revealers) of the divine speech; the ruling established through them is ultimately established by Allah's speech.
The Hanafi position: al-kashif reveals the divine hukm, so the ruling is not independent.
3
Analyze why Quranic words are still considered khitab
Understand that although the Qur'anic words also reveal the internal speech, they are not called kashif but are treated as the speech itself out of respect (adab). The indicator is assimilated to the indicated.
This preserves the special status of the Qur'an as direct speech.
4
Distinguish qiyas as muzhir
Note that Hanafis specifically call qiyas a muzhir (manifestor) because it explicitly derives a subsidiary ruling from an original case, requiring an asl. Sunnah and Ijma‘ directly give the ruling without such reasoning.
Kashf is attributed to qiyas due to its explicit manifestation of subsidiary rulings.
5
Note the terminological dispute about khitab in pre-eternity
Be aware of the disagreement on whether the eternal speech (kalam nafsi) is called khitab; some scholars affirm it, others reserve the term for the revealed speech.
This is a side issue but affects the precise scope of the definition.

Hanafi Position on Intention for Ramadan Fast: Usul Analysis
Ruling on sufficiency of absolute or contrary intention for Ramadan fast

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of 'mudayyiq' (restrictive time) to determine that absolute intention suffices for the Ramadan fast, and evaluate the majority's counter-argument using the hadith 'innama al-a'mal bi al-niyyat'.

Core Idea
When the time is so restricted that only one act fits, no specific intention is needed — any intention fulfills the obligation.

In this passage, we examine a classic usul dispute: Is specifying the intention for the Ramadan fast required? The Hanafi school argues that because each day of Ramadan can only accommodate the obligatory fast, an absolute intention or even an intention for a different fast suffices. This is based on the concept of 'mudayyiq' (restrictive time) and a hadith. We'll walk through the reasoning and the counter-argument from the majority.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of mudayyiq
Mudayyiq (restrictive time) means the time allotted for an act is exactly equal to the act and cannot accommodate any other act. In such a time, no competitor act is possible.
This is in contrast to 'mawsu' (expansive time) where multiple acts could fit.
2
Apply mudayyiq to Ramadan
Each day of Ramadan can only hold one fast, and the Law designated that fast as the obligatory Ramadan fast. Therefore, that day is mudayyiq for the obligation.
The text says: 'اليوم الواحد لا يصلح محلاً للصومين' (one day cannot be a locus for two fasts).
3
Derive the Hanafi usul rule
When the time is mudayyiq and the Law has specified the act, specification of intention is not required because only one act is possible. Any valid intention (absolute, another obligation, or supererogation) necessarily leads to the obligatory act.
Thus, even intending a nafl fast on a Ramadan day results in the Ramadan obligation being fulfilled.
4
Contrast with the majority position
The majority (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) argue that since actions are judged by intentions (hadith 'innama al-a'mal bi al-niyyat'), one must intend the specific obligation. Intending something else means that one did not intend the obligation, so it is not fulfilled.
Ibn al-Humam supported the majority view, noting the strength of the intention hadith.
5
Analyze the Hanafi response using the hadith
The Hanafis cite the hadith 'When Sha'ban passes, there is no fast except for Ramadan' – this negates any other fast during Ramadan, establishing that only the obligatory fast can exist. Therefore, any intention is overridden by reality: the fast that occurs is necessarily the obligatory one, regardless of what was intended.
The hadith is zanni al-thubut but qati al-dalala in the Hanafi view.

The Traveler's Intention in Ramadan: Hanafi Dispute on Niyyah
Expansion on the traveler's intention requirement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi disagreement on whether a traveler's intention of nafl suffices for the fard fast of Ramadan, and apply the usul principle that a day can only accommodate one determined fast.

Core Idea
A day can only hold one fast; the traveler's concession changes its nature, so intention must specify the fard.

In this passage, the commentator expands on the rule that the days of Ramadan are exclusively for the obligatory fast. For the resident, any intention of fasting automatically fulfills the fard. But for the traveler, Imam Abu Hanifa requires specific intention, while the Sahibayn hold the general rule applies. This lesson unpacks the usul reasoning behind the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base rule
The commentator establishes that each day of Ramadan can accommodate only one fast, and the Lawgiver has determined that fast as fard. Thus, no other fast (whether nafl, qada, or vow) can occupy that day.
This is derived from the hadith indicating the day's exclusivity.
2
Apply to the resident
For a resident, any intention to fast (even if mistakenly thought to be nafl) automatically becomes the fard fast of Ramadan, because the day cannot hold another fast. The restriction of intention is irrelevant.
The commentator says: 'the absolute and the specific are the same'.
3
Identify the traveler's exception
The traveler has a concession (rukhsa) to break the fast. This concession makes Ramadan like Sha'ban in his regard: the day is no longer exclusively determined for the fard fast.
Imam Abu Hanifa's key reasoning.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's position
Because the traveler's day is not exclusively fard, a specific intention for the fard (or another wajib) is required. If the traveler intends a nafl fast, the fard is not discharged.
There is a second narration that allows the fard to be fulfilled by nafl intention, but the main position is the first.
5
Contrast with the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the original rule applies: any intention suffices. The concession only permits breaking the fast; it does not alter the determination of the day. Therefore, a nafl intention fulfills the fard.
The commentator considers this 'closer to correctness' (الأشبه بالصواب).

Basis of Qada: Same Command as Ada or New Command?
Usul dispute on the basis of qada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the basis of qada and evaluate the key evidence from the hadith and rational argument.

Core Idea
The obligation to make up a missed act arises from the same liability that demanded the original act.

This passage presents a foundational usul dispute: does the command to perform an act within its time also obligate making it up if missed? The Hanafi school argues that the liability (shughl al-dhimmah) created by the original command persists, and a like substitute (mithl) is implicitly demanded. In contrast, the Shafi'i and Maliki schools hold that a new, separate command is required. We will dissect the arguments, evidence, and the key hadith that supports the Hanafi view.

Guided Path
1
State the dispute
Identify the two positions: (1) qada is by a new command, (2) qada is by the same command as ada. Note which schools hold each view.
The majority of Shafi'is, Malikis, and some Hanafis (e.g., Abu al-Yusr) hold the first; the chosen view of the Hanafi imams (Abu Zayd, Fakhr al-Islam, etc.) holds the second.
2
Analyze the Hanafi rational argument
Explain the concept of shughl al-dhimmah: the command to perform ada creates a liability on the dhimmah. If the act is missed, the liability remains. The request to perform the act was to free the dhimmah. Therefore, when a like exists, it is implicitly demanded to free the dhimmah.
This is a rational entailment, not a textual one. The Hanafis do not claim the word of the original command includes qada.
3
Examine the hadith evidence
Read the hadith: 'Whoever sleeps through a prayer or forgets it, let him pray it when he remembers, for that is its time.' The Prophet commands the same prayer to be performed when remembered, showing continuity of the original obligation.
The hadith is narrated by Bukhari and Muslim (al-Shaykhan).
4
Refute the opposing objection
The opponent uses the example of fasting: Thursday's fast does not obligate Friday's fast. The Hanafi response: this only shows lack of textual entailment, but the Hanafi claim is rational entailment based on persistence of liability. The like (mithl) must share the same maslaha, which is shari'ah-defined, not calendar-equivalent.
The author emphasizes that the fasting example does not refute the rational argument.
5
Understand the distinction between rational and irrational like
Note that the dispute applies whether the like is rational (ma'qul, e.g., one prayer for another) or irrational (ghayr ma'qul, e.g., fidya for fasting). The core is the same cause and same occupation of the dhimmah.
The author argues that the distinction between rational and irrational like is not central; both follow the same principle.

Commentary
This passage addresses a fundamental usul dispute: whether the obligation to make up (qada) a missed obligatory act is established by a new, independent command, or by the same command that originally obligated the act within its time (ada). The author states that the majority of Shafi'is, Malikis, and some Hanafis (like Abu al-Yusr) hold the view of a new command. However, the chosen view of the great Hanafi imams (like Abu Zayd al-Dabusi, Fakhr al-Islam, Shams al-A'imma) is that the obligation of qada is by the same command that obligated ada. The author clarifies that this does not mean the command for ada literally includes the command for qada by textual implication; rather, the obligation to perform an act creates a liability (shughl al-dhimmah) that persists. If the act is not performed in time, the liability remains, and since the purpose was to free the dhimmah, a like substitute (mithl) that shares the same maslaha suffices to discharge it. Thus, the command for performance entails the command for the like when the original is lost. The author provides a rational argument: the shughl al-dhimmah remains after the time; the demand for the act is to free the dhimmah; if a like exists, it is implicitly demanded. He supports this with the hadith: 'Whoever sleeps through a prayer or forgets it, let him pray it when he remembers, for that is its time.' This shows that the same prayer is required, i.e., the same liability remains. The objection that fasting Thursday does not obligate Friday is refuted by clarifying that the Hanafis do not claim textual entailment but rather a rational entailment based on the persistence of liability. The passage also distinguishes between a 'rational like' (mithl ma'qul), whose likeness is understood by reason (like one prayer for another), and an 'irrational like' (mithl ghayr ma'qul), specified only by revelation (like fidya for fasting). The need for a 'definition' of qada is only to identify the correct like, not to create a new obligation. The author concludes that the distinction between rational and irrational like is not central to the dispute; the core is that the same cause (sabab) and same occupation (ishtighal) of the dhimmah continue.

Qada of a Vowed I'tikaf: New Fast Required?
Debate on whether qada of a restricted obligation is a new obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that qada is a new obligation (wajib mustaqill) when a condition (shart) was blocked by an impediment (mani'), and distinguish this from the intra-Hanafi view of Abu Yusuf and Hasan b. Ziyad.

Core Idea
Qada of a missed obligatory act is a new obligation, not a continuation of the original.

This passage from a commentary on 'Muslim al-Thubut' delves into a classic usul al-fiqh debate: when an obligation is conditioned by a specific time or act, and the condition is rendered ineffective by an impediment, does the obligation survive in a modified form? The scenario: a person vows to perform i'tikaf in Ramadan with fasting, but only fasts. The Hanafi chosen position holds that the qada requires a new fast because the condition (fasting) was not fulfilled for the vow. This lesson unpacks the proof, the impediment, and the intra-Hanafi dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The passage debates whether qada (make-up) of a vowed i'tikaf requires a new fast or not. The issue is whether the original obligation persists in a restricted form or a completely new obligation arises.
The root cause is the interplay between a condition (shart) and an impediment (mani') in usul.
2
Locate the proof (dalil)
The hadith 'لا اعتكاف إلا بصوم' is cited as the primary proof. This hadith establishes fasting as a condition (shart) for the validity of i'tikaf. Thus, a vow of i'tikaf implicitly includes a vow to fast.
The hadith is from Sunan al-Daraqutni and al-Bayhaqi, authenticated by critics. It is zanni al-thubut and zanni al-dalala.
3
Analyze the impediment (mani')
The pre-existing obligation of fasting in Ramadan acts as an impediment (mani'). It blocks the vow from imposing a new fast because fasting is already obligatory for Ramadan. Therefore, the effect of the vow on fasting was suspended.
In Hanafi usul, a mani' prevents the effect of a cause (sabab) from materializing without invalidating the cause itself.
4
Determine the consequence after removal of impediment
Once the impediment is removed (i.e., after Ramadan ends), the vow's effect on fasting becomes active. Since the condition (fasting) is necessary for the validity of the vowed i'tikaf, the qada of i'tikaf must be accompanied by a new fast. This is because the original fast was performed for Ramadan, not for the vow.
The passage emphasizes that qada is an independent obligation (wajib mustaqill), not a continuation of the original obligation with the same conditions.
5
Evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute
Abu Yusuf and Hasan b. Ziyad hold that no new fast is needed; qada of i'tikaf alone suffices. Their view is based on the unity of cause: the cause (vow) is the same, so the same fast should suffice. The chosen Hanafi position (mukhtar) requires a new fast because the condition must be fulfilled for the qada, and the original fast cannot be reapplied due to the impediment.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is clear: the mufta bihi requires a new fast; the minority view does not.

Commentary
This passage is an advanced discussion in usul al-fiqh about the nature of obligations when they are restricted by a condition (like time). The text is a commentary (sharh) on a matn, likely from 'Muslim al-Thubut'. The author is engaging with objections and clarifying the Hanafi position. The core issue: if an obligation (wajib) is restricted (muqayyad) by a specific time or condition, and that restriction is removed (e.g., the time passes without performance), does the obligation cease entirely, or does the unrestricted obligation remain? The Hanafi position, according to the chosen view (mukhtar), is that the unrestricted obligation remains on the legal liability, and making it up (qada) is a new obligation arising from the original cause (the vow or command). The text uses the example of a vow (nathr) to perform i'tikaf (seclusion) in Ramadan along with fasting. If someone vowed to do i'tikaf in Ramadan but only fasted (assuming fasting is a condition for i'tikaf according to a hadith), does he have to make up both i'tikaf and a new fast? Abu Hanifa and his followers (except Abu Yusuf and Hasan b. Ziyad) say yes, qada requires a new fast in a different month because the original fast was already performed for Ramadan, not for the vow. The author refutes objections that this contradicts the unity of cause or similarity. The hadith 'No i'tikaf except with fasting' is used as proof that fasting is a condition (shart) for i'tikaf, so the vow implicitly requires both. The impediment (mana') was that fasting was already obligatory as a Ramadan fast, so the vow could not impose a new fast at that time. When impediment is removed (after Ramadan), the condition becomes effective, so the qada of i'tikaf must be accompanied by a new fast. The commentary guides the student through the logical steps and refutes opposing views.

Travel for Disobedience and the Application of Concessions
Discussing whether travel for disobedience prevents rukhsah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that travel for disobedience does not prevent travel concessions, and analyze the opposing views using Quranic and rational arguments.

Core Idea
The concession of travel applies even to sinful journeys, because the cause is the journey itself, not the intention.

This passage addresses a key disagreement between the Hanafi school and the majority of jurists: whether travel undertaken for a sinful purpose still allows the legal concessions such as shortening prayer and breaking the fast. The Hanafi position is based on the absolute wording of the Quran and hadith, while the opposing school argues that a blessing cannot be obtained through sin.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that travel for disobedience (safar al-ma'siyah) does not prevent the application of travel concessions (rukhsah).
This applies to any sinful purpose, e.g., theft, rebellion.
2
Identify the opposing view
The three other imams (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) hold that such travel does prevent the concessions. The Rawafid (Shia) are said to require the travel to be for obedience.
The majority view is that concessions are blessings and should not be earned by sin.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafis rely on the absolute (mutlaq) wording of Quran 2:184 and the hadith from Sahih Muslim. The texts do not qualify travel by purpose, so they apply to all travel.
The principle: al-mutlaq yujra 'ala ithlaqih (the absolute is applied absolutely) unless there is a necessity to restrict it.
4
Examine the opponent's proof
The three imams argue that concession is a blessing (ni'mah) and blessings cannot be attained through disobedience. They analogize to intoxication, which cannot be a cause for ease.
They use the principle: al-ni'mah la tunalu bi al-ma'siyah (a blessing is not obtained by disobedience).
5
Study the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond by distinguishing the journey itself from the sinful intention. The disobedience is not the journey but something adjacent to it. If the intention were obedience, the same journey would be obedience. The cause (sabab) is the journey, not the sin. They analogize to prayer in usurped land.
Prayer in usurped land is valid because the act of prayer is not itself sinful; similarly, travel is not itself sinful.

Mistaken Divorce: Judicial vs Religious Effect
Analyzing the Hanafi ruling on mistaken divorce: distinguishing judicial and religious effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between diyanatan and qada'an to cases of mistaken utterances and identify the effective cause (illa) that governs the judicial and religious rulings.

Core Idea
A mistaken divorce is effective in court but not before Allah — the judge rules by the apparent, but the religious bond remains intact.

This passage tackles a classic Hanafi usul problem: when a person's words do not match their intent. The Hanafi school develops a two-tier system: the judicial (qada'an) ruling based on apparent words, and the religious (diyanatan) ruling based on inner reality. Here, we explore how this applies to a man who mistakenly utters divorce while intending something else, using analogies from sale and oaths and responding to objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios
Distinguish between the case where the man intended something else (e.g., tasbih) and where he intended the literal meaning (free from bondage). Note how the initial fatwas differ.
2
Trace the dual ruling structure
Understand the diyanatan vs qada'an framework: religiously (diyanatan) the marriage remains intact if there is no intent, but judicially (qada'an) the judge enforces divorce due to apparent words.
Ibn al-Humam holds that both scenarios give only qada'an effect.
3
Analyze the analogy with sale
The text considers why the sale of a jester (hazl) is void due to lack of consent to the ruling, but analogizing divorce to sale is flawed because sale requires mutual consent and is dissoluble, while divorce is not.
The jester consents to the cause (words) but not the ruling; the mistaken person consents to neither.
4
Apply the textual dalil
Use Quran 5:89 'Allah does not hold you accountable for unintentional oaths' to argue that just as unintentional oaths have no religious consequence, unintentional divorce should have no religious effect.
The hadith 'three are serious in jest' is then distinguished: it applies to jest (consent to cause) not mistake (no consent).
5
Evaluate the hadith objection
The hadith 'Three things are serious in seriousness and serious in jest' does not apply to the mistaken person because he does not consent to the cause; it only applies to the jester who consents to the cause.
Thus, the mistaken person's divorce is effective only qada'an, not diyanatan.

Meaning of Hatta in Conditional Oaths
Analyzing the meaning of حتى in a conditional oath formula.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the meaning of حتى (hatta) in a conditional oath by applying Hanafi linguistic principles and determine the ruling for oath fulfillment.

Core Idea
In Hanafi oaths, حتى indicates mere sequence unless intent indicates connection.

In Hanafi usul, the meaning of a particle like حتى in an oath formula is not fixed but determined by context and logical possibility. This passage demonstrates how a Hanafi jurist tests each possible meaning—ghaya (endpoint), sababiyya (causation), and tarrib (sequence)—and selects the one that is semantically and legally coherent. Understanding this method is essential for correctly applying oath rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath formula
Locate the exact phrase: 'إن لم آتك حتى أتغدى عندك' (If I do not come to you until I eat lunch at your place). Note that the oath is conditional on the non-occurrence of the event.
The ruling in question is the consequence if the oath is broken.
2
Test the possible meanings of حتى
Consider three meanings: غاية (endpoint), سبببية (causation), and مجرد الترتيب (mere sequence). Evaluate each for logical coherence within the phrase.
The author explicitly rejects ghaya and sababiyya as impossible.
3
Eliminate ghaya and sababiyya
Ghaya would mean coming is the endpoint of eating, but coming does not end eating. Sababiyya would mean coming causes eating, but coming is not a cause. Both are impossible, so they are discarded.
This elimination is based on the literal meaning and common sense.
4
Adopt mere sequence (tarrib)
Since only mere sequence is possible, حتى is interpreted as conjunctions of sequence (عطف مجرد الترتيب). This means the oath requires both actions (coming and eating) to occur, but they need not be immediate.
If the oath is limited by time, the actions must occur within that time.
5
Apply the ruling and exceptions
If the oath-taker intends connection (اتصال), the actions must be consecutive. If he makes حتى like فاء, connection is required even without intention. The default is mere sequence unless intention is present.
This nuance shows the role of intention (niyya) in Hanafi oath interpretation.

Abu Hanifa and the Hadith 'al-Walad li al-Firash': Did He Exclude the Specific Occasion?
This passage defends Abu Hanifa's usul consistency regarding the hadith 'al-walad li al-firash'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa's ruling on the child of a cohabited slave woman does not violate the usul principle that the specific occasion does not restrict the generality of a text.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa did not exclude the hadith's specific occasion; he defined 'firash' differently, excluding cases outside his definition.

This passage confronts a common objection against Abu Hanifa's usul consistency: does his ruling on paternity of a slave woman's child contradict the hadith 'al-walad li al-firash'? The text defends the Hanafi position by showing that Abu Hanifa did not exclude the specific occasion (Zam'a's case) but rather others, using tanqih al-manat to refine the effective cause. The core of the dispute lies in the definition of 'firash', which the Hanafis restrict to lawful intercourse with a claim.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The opponent claims that Abu Hanifa excluded the specific occasion (child of Zam'a's slave girl) from the generality of the hadith, which violates the principle that the specific occasion does not restrict a general text.
2
State the Hanafi response
The Hanafi reply denies the exclusion of the specific occasion; Abu Hanifa accepted the ruling for Zam'a's case and excluded other slave women based on his definition of 'firash'.
3
Analyze the tanqih al-manat argument
Tanqih al-manat shows that the effective cause (istifrash) is the cohabitation in a lawful context, not the particular identity of Zam'a. Hence, the ruling applies to all such cases, but Abu Hanifa's definition of lawful context excludes slave women without a claim.
4
Contrast the alternative explanations
Some suggested the hadith did not reach Abu Hanifa, but the text rejects this, noting the hadith is in his Musnad. The preferred explanation is based on his definition of 'firash'.
5
Understand the Hanafi definition of 'firash'
'Al-firash' metaphorically refers to a lawful sexual relationship aimed at procreation, requiring both permissibility of intercourse and a claim of paternity. Without a claim, the slave woman is not a 'firash'.

The Homonym Shafaq and the Non-Generality of Actions
Hanafi refutation of Shafi'i claim that shafaq homonym implies multiple prayers.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a homonym (mushtarak) does not imply multiple rulings without external evidence, using the example of shafaq and the `Isha prayer.

Core Idea
A homonym does not generate multiple legal rulings; one action can fulfill multiple linguistic meanings.

This lesson examines a key dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is over whether the Prophet's prayer after twilight implies two separate prayers. The disagreement hinges on the word 'shafaq', which can mean redness or whiteness. Understanding this helps clarify how Hanafis treat homonyms in legal texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested action
The Prophet prayed `Isha after the disappearance of the twilight (shafaq). The Shafi'is claim that because 'shafaq' is a homonym, he must have prayed twice: once after the red twilight and once after the white twilight.
2
Recognize the Shafi'i basis
They argue that a homonym generalizes the ruling to each of its meanings, thus leading to two prayers.
This is based on a principle that a mushtarak indicates all its meanings.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle on homonyms
Hanafis hold that a mushtarak does not simultaneously indicate all its meanings unless there is evidence. The word 'shafaq' is a homonym, but the action (prayer) is a single event; it can be after both twilights because the white twilight always follows the red.
The author argues that one prayer can be after both, so no repetition is necessary.
4
Explain the counter-argument
The Hanafi response: the ruling is that the prayer is after each twilight, but that does not necessitate two prayers. One thing can be after multiple things essentially.
The homonym does not create multiple rulings; the action remains singular.
5
Conclude the Hanafi position
Thus, the hadith does not prove two prayers. The action is specific to one occurrence, and generality requires external evidence.

Does 'Kaana Yaf'alu' Imply Repetition?
Analysis of whether 'kaana yaf'alu' indicates repetition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi view that the expression 'kaana yaf'alu' does not necessarily indicate habitual action, and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i interpretation.

Core Idea
The phrase 'kaana yaf'alu' does not automatically mean repetition; it may describe a single habit.

This lesson explores another aspect of the non-generality of actions: the linguistic construction 'kaana yaf'alu' (he used to do). The passage examines whether such phrasing indicates persistence. The Hanafi position, supported by later commentators, is that it does not necessarily indicate continuity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the construction
Recognize the Arabic expression 'kaana yaf'alu' (e.g., 'kaana yusalli al-‘asr') which some interpret as habitual action.
2
Recognize the Shafi'i claim
Shafi'is and some Hanafis argue that 'kaana yaf'alu' indicates repetition and perpetuity, based on the word 'kaana' implying continuity or the imperfect verb indicating habit.
They cite examples like 'he used to pray `Asr while the sun was white'.
3
Understand the Hanafi response
The passage presents three possible bases for understanding repetition: from 'kaana' alone, from the imperfect verb alone, or from the combination. The Hanafi position, supported by Imam al-Razi and Sheikh al-Dahlawi, is that none of these necessarily indicate repetition.
The word 'kaana' alone does not imply perpetuity; it may just report a past habit. The imperfect verb alone, though it can indicate habit, does not guarantee repetition in every context.
4
Analyze the strongest argument
The author considers that understanding repetition from the combination of 'kaana' and the imperfect verb is stronger, but even this is denied by Sheikh al-Dahlawi, who states that 'kaana yaf'alu' does not indicate repetition at all.
The induction from hadith contradicts the claim that 'kaana' always indicates perpetuity.
5
Conclude the Hanafi principle
Thus, the Hanafi school does not consider 'kaana yaf'alu' as automatic proof of repetition. External verbal evidence is required to establish generality.
This aligns with the overall principle that actions (hikayat fi'l) do not generalize by themselves.

The Principle of Taqdir: Supplying Omitted Words in Hadith Interpretation
Textual analysis of hadith through grammatical taqdir to establish umum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of taqdir (supplying omitted words) to interpret a hadith and identify when taqdir is necessary to avoid contradiction with consensus.

Core Idea
What is supplied (muqaddar) is treated as uttered — preserving grammatical and legal coherence.

This lesson explores the Hanafi usul principle of taqdir through the analysis of a well-known hadith: 'لا يقتل مسلم بكافر ولا ذو عهد في عهده'. Without taqdir, the phrase 'ولا ذو عهد في عهده' would prohibit killing a covenant-holder absolutely, which contradicts the consensus that a covenant-holder can be killed for apostasy or certain crimes. By supplying the omitted 'بكافر' from the first part, the meaning becomes coherent and general.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Text and Its Structure
Locate the hadith under analysis and note its two parts: 'لا يقتل مسلم بكافر' and 'ولا ذو عهد في عهده'. Understand that the second part is conjoined by 'wa'.
The text includes an addition 'ألا' (a particle of attention) in some narrations, but that does not affect the grammatical analysis.
2
Detect the Grammatical Gap
Notice that the second part lacks an explicit object; without taqdir, it would read 'Nor a covenant-holder in his covenant [is killed]' without specifying the doer or cause. This would imply an absolute prohibition of killing a covenant-holder.
This absolute interpretation is problematic because it contradicts the consensus that a covenant-holder can be killed for apostasy or violation of the covenant.
3
Apply the Principle of Taqdir
Supply the omitted word from the context—here, 'بكافر' (for a disbeliever) from the first part. This is justified by the قرينة (contextual clue) of its previous mention.
In Hanafi usul, what is supplied (muqaddar) is treated like an uttered word (malful) for legal interpretation.
4
Derive the Ruling and Generality
With the supplied word, the meaning becomes: 'A covenant-holder is not killed in his covenant for a disbeliever.' This ruling is general (amm) in form because the supplied word carries over the generality of the first part.
The Hanafi position is that the hadith now indicates that a Muslim is not killed for a disbeliever, and a covenant-holder is not killed for a disbeliever while in his covenant.
5
Contrast with Alternative Interpretation
Some commentators (e.g., on al-Minhaj) argue that no taqdir is needed and the hadith means a covenant-holder's killing is absolutely impermissible. Evaluate this view: it contradicts the consensus that a covenant-holder can be killed for apostasy, and it renders the phrase 'في عهده' meaningless.
The Hanafi response is that taqdir is necessary to avoid absurdity and uphold the intended legal nuance.

Key Terms
تواتر (tawatur): A level of transmission so widespread that collusion in falsehood is rationally impossible, yielding absolute certainty.
خبر واحد (khabar wahid): A report transmitted by one or a few narrators that does not reach the level of tawatur.

مشهور (mashhur): A hadith that was transmitted by three or more narrators in each generation but does not reach the level of tawatur.

Prophet's Action as a Mukhassis of General Texts
Rule concerning Prophet's action as specifier

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule identifying when the Prophet's action serves as a mukhassis (specifier) of a general text and distinguish it from abrogation.

Core Idea
The Prophet's action specifies a general text only if the text includes him linguistically and the action is connected.

Scholars discuss the legal effect of the Prophet's actions that seem to contradict general commands. In this lesson, we examine a key principle from Hanafi usul: when does such an action specify the general, and when does it abrogate it? The answer hinges on whether the general text's wording includes the Prophet himself and whether the action is connected temporally to the revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the General Text and its Scope
Read the general command carefully. Determine whether the wording linguistically includes the Prophet (e.g., 'every Muslim') or excludes him (e.g., 'my ummah') or is ambiguous.
2
Examine the Prophet's Action
Note that the Prophet performed an action contrary to the general command. Is this action connected (muwasil, i.e., after the general without a gap) or disconnected?
3
Apply the Hanafi Rule
If the general includes the Prophet and the action is connected, the action specifies the general (takhsis): the ruling applies to others but not to the Prophet. If the action is disconnected, it is abrogation (naskh) of part of the general.
4
Derive the Ruling
Conclude: the general command remains binding for all except the Prophet, unless there is a separate proof obligating following the action (then it is naskh).

The Prophet's Action as a Specifier of General Texts
Determining whether the Prophet's action can specify a general text.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the Prophet's action (fi'l) specifies a general text (amm) only if it is connected (mawsul) and contemporaneous, and distinguish specification from abrogation (naskh).

Core Idea
A Prophet's action specifies his own general statement only if connected in time; otherwise it abrogates.

In this lesson, we examine a crucial issue in Hanafi usul: can the Prophet's action (fi'l) narrow down the scope of a general statement he made? The answer depends on timing and connection. We will explore the conditions under which the Prophet's action serves as a specifier (mukhasis) and when it operates as an abrogator (nasikh), as well as the role of the evidence of emulation (dalil al-ta'si).

Guided Path
1
Identify the general text and the action
Locate the general statement (amm) made by the Prophet and the later action that seems contrary. Note whether the Prophet is included in the general address linguistically.
If the Prophet is not included (e.g., 'for my ummah'), the action cannot specify.
2
Check the connection (ittisal)
Determine if the action was connected (mawsul) to the general statement or separated in time. According to Hanafi, only if connected can it specify; otherwise it abrogates.
Connection means the action occurred in the same context without temporal gap.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule
If the action is connected and the Prophet is included in the amm, the action is a mukhasis. If not connected, it is nasikh (abrogation of part of the amm).
The action cannot be both a specifier and an abrogator; one must be chosen.
4
Consider the dalil al-ta'si
The evidence of emulation (e.g., verse 33:21) interacts with the amm. Its timing relative to the amm determines whether it supports specification or abrogation.
If dalil al-ta'si is later, it may support specification; if earlier or contemporaneous, different rules apply.

This is a nuanced discussion typical of Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A general text becomes ambiguous without bayan; the authentic, accepted sunnah clarifies the Quran.

The passage presents a dispute: should we take the hadith of wiping to the elbows or the hadith of wiping to the wrist? The answer lies in the acceptance of the Companions, here exemplified by Umar's rejection of Ammar's narration.

The Hanafi refutation exposes the flaws in each, reaffirming that the command form is assigned for obligation and that rank from a superior is the very reason for obligation.

Practical consequence: commands in the Quran and Sunnah are presumed obligatory unless a qarina shifts them.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul dispute: the meaning of the imperative (amr). The proponents of nadb (recommendation) present two arguments. The Hanafi refutation: The difference in rank is exactly why a command is for obligation. A superior has the authority to oblige. The commentator concludes that the correct view is the Hanafi position that amr is for wujub.

The Command to Command Dispute
Dispute on whether 'command to command' is a command to the second person

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on al-amr bi al-amr and evaluate the opposing argument using the concepts of ta'diyya and safir.

Core Idea
A command to an intermediary is not a command to the ultimate doer—only the intermediary is addressed.

This lesson tackles a subtle but important usul issue: when someone says 'order so-and-so to do X,' does that count as a command to so-and-so? The Hanafi school says no, and their reasoning involves avoiding absurd consequences (luzum al-ma'siyya) and the concept of transgression (ta'diyya). We will walk through the arguments and counterarguments presented in this passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
Determine what exactly is being disputed: does a command to an intermediary (like 'order Zayd to do X') constitute a command to Zayd himself? Note the Hanafi 'no' and the opposing 'yes'.
The text clarifies that direct commands like 'say to so-and-so: do X' are not disputed.
2
Analyze the first Hanafi argument: absurdity of disobedience
If the command were direct, then if the master disobeys by not ordering, the slave would still be commanded and become disobedient, which is absurd. Work through the logic.
This is a rational proof (luzum al-ma'siyya).
3
Examine the second argument: transgression (ta'diyya) and the role of the safir
Commanding another's slave without permission is transgression. The Hanafi response is that the master acts as a safir (messenger), giving implicit permission, so it is not transgression.
The text modifies the objection: transgression only occurs if the command is not by mediation.
4
Examine the third argument: contradiction with prohibition
If 'Order the slave to sell' is a command to the slave, then a simultaneous 'Do not sell' would contradict it. The opponent rebuts with naskh (abrogation) if the prohibition comes later.
The Hanafi argues that a concurrent prohibition is impossible, but the opponent invokes naskh to resolve the temporal order.
5
Conclude with the Hanafi position and practical consequence
The Hanafi view is that the command is to the intermediary only. The practical difference: if a master says 'order your slave to sell my slave,' the slave is not obligated to sell unless the master actually orders.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed Hanafi usul dispute about whether a command directed to an intermediary (like 'order so-and-so to do X') constitutes a command to the ultimate doer. The author, commenting on Muslim al-Thubut, begins by citing a hadith where the Prophet (peace be upon him) says 'Command them to pray at seven,' noting that this is not a direct command to the child. He clarifies that the dispute only applies to phrases like 'order so-and-so'—not to direct commands like 'say to so-and-so: do X,' which are commands by agreement. The first argument is that treating 'order so-and-so' as a command to the slave would lead to absurdity: the slave would be disobedient when the master fails to order, which is false. The second argument is that it would constitute transgression (tâ'diyya) since it commands someone else's slave without the master's direct order. This is refuted by noting that the master acts as a mediator (safir), giving implicit permission. The third argument is contradiction (naqd) with a simultaneous prohibition, but this is rebutted by the possibility of abrogation (naskh). Ultimately, the correct view is that such phrases are not direct commands to the doer; they are commands to the intermediary, who is merely a conveyor. The passage exemplifies Hanafi usul methodology: rational argument, textual evidence, and careful handling of objections.

Key Terms
الأمر بالأمر (al-amr bi al-amr): A command directed to someone to issue a command to another (e.g., 'order so-and-so to do X').
التعدي (at-ta'diyya): Transgression; disposing of another's property or rights without permission.
السفير (as-safir): The intermediary or messenger who conveys the command.

Why the Hadith of Ya'la Does Not Prove Mafhum al-Shart
Refutes the hadith as proof for mafhum al-shart

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school rejects the hadith of Ya'la as proof for mafhum al-shart and identify the alternative Hanafi explanation based on the principle of original absence (al-'adam al-asli).

Core Idea
What lies beyond the condition is silent; the original absence remains unless proven otherwise.

This passage addresses one of the strongest proof texts cited by proponents of mafhum al-shart: the hadith of Ya'la ibn Umayya, in which Umar expressed surprise that shortening prayer was permitted even when safe. Hanafi usulis argue that Umar's understanding was not a linguistic inference but a reliance on the original ruling of complete prayer. We will unpack the Hanafi response and its implications for usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposing argument
The proponents of mafhum al-shart claim that the hadith shows that the Prophet affirmed Umar's understanding that shortening is tied to fear, implying that when fear is absent, shortening should not be done. This is the concept of condition.
This is a prima facie reading of the hadith.
2
Examine the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond: Umar's understanding was not derived from the wording of the verse but from his reliance on the original ruling (al-asl) that prayer is complete (four rak'ahs) except when fear is present. The absence of a condition does not linguistically negate the ruling; it only leaves it silent.
The key principle: al-'adam al-asli (original absence) is not a linguistic concept.
3
Analyze the proof from Aisha's hadith
Opponents may cite the hadith that prayer was originally two rak'ahs, so completion cannot be the original. The Hanafis explain that this refers to the legislation after the abrogation: travel prayer remained two, while residence prayer was increased. So the original for travel is two, not four, but only when travel is coupled with fear? Actually, the Hanafi counter is that the two rak'ahs were tied to travel, not fear, and were established as a Sunnah. Umar's understanding was based on the fact that before the verse, the ruling was four, and the verse made an exception for fear. Beyond that, the original (four) applies.
This is a nuanced historical argument.
4
Conclude: the hadith does not support mafhum al-shart
The Hanafi school holds that the hadith is not a proof for the linguistic concept of condition. Instead, it shows that Umar relied on the original ruling. The principle of al-'adam al-asli means that silence beyond the condition is not a negation; it is simply not addressed, and the original state remains.
This has practical implications: shortening prayer is permissible even when safe because the Sunnah establishes it, not the verse.

Hanafi response to hadith objection against conditional divorce

Core Idea
The hadith prohibits immediate divorce only, not conditional divorce before marriage.

This lesson examines a key objection to the validity of conditional divorce (talaq mu'allaq) before marriage: the well-known hadith 'No divorce in what you do not possess.' The weakness of these narrations is affirmed by Ahmad, Ibn Ma'in, and others.
5
Conclude the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school maintains that conditional divorce before marriage is valid based on the specification of the hadith and the strength of their evidence.
The practical consequence is that a man can validly say 'If I marry you, you are divorced' before marriage.

Commentary
This passage presents the third objection against the validity of conditional divorce before marriage: the well-known hadith 'No divorce in what you do not possess.' The Hanafi response is multi-layered. First, they distinguish between the immediate (munajjaz) divorce, which the hadith prohibits in non-ownership, and suspended (mu'allaq) divorce, which is not included in the hadith's scope. The hadith is about 'talaq' as an immediate effect; conditional divorce is not called 'talaq' in that sense. Second, they cite numerous early scholars (az-Zuhri, ash-Sha'bi, Salim, al-Qasim, etc.) who interpreted the hadith as applying only to immediate divorce. Third, they address the opposing narrations from ad-Daraqutni and others, evaluating them as weak (da'if) due to untrustworthy narrators, and note that major authorities like Malik, Rabi'a, and al-Awza'i did not act upon them. The passage also mentions an objection based on common custom ('urf) about oaths, but dismisses it as weak.

Key Terms
الطلاق المعلق (al-talaq al-mu'allaq): Divorce suspended on a future condition.
التنجيز (al-tanjiz): Immediate, unconditional pronouncement.
الملك (al-milk): Ownership or rightful possession, e.g., of a spouse.
العموم (al-umum): Generality of a textual statement covering all instances.
التخصيص (al-takhsis): Specification of a general term to exclude some instances.
