The Virtue of Caution in Fatwa: Delaying Hajj with Risk of Sin
A benefit section refuting Imam Muhammad's view on Hajj delay with sin

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a mufti may permit an action while warning of possible sin, and apply this reasoning to a similar scenario.

Core Idea
Permissibility does not exclude risk of sin – a mufti can allow delay while advising of potential accountability.

This passage from *Fawātih al-Raḥamūt* refines a delicate question: if Hajj is obligatory immediately, may one delay? Imam Muhammad said yes, but with sin if death occurs. The author's critique and Shaykh al-Haddad's response reveal a sophisticated method for issuing fatwas when certainty is impossible. You will learn how to navigate such layered rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute: fawr vs. tarākhī
The background: does an unconditional obligation require immediate performance (fawr) or allow delay (tarākhī)? Imam Muhammad's position permits delay but holds the person sinful if death occurs before performance. The passage examines this tension.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf; Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf hold fawr.
2
Understand the dilemma presented
The critic presents three possible answers to the question 'May I delay Hajj?' and shows each is problematic: (1) 'Yes' removes sin, contradicting Imam Muhammad; (2) 'No' forces fawr; (3) 'It depends on Allah's knowledge' is useless since knowledge is hidden.
The dilemma aims to prove that Imam Muhammad's view leads to contradiction.
3
Examine Shaykh al-Haddad's resolution
Al-Haddad says the mufti should answer: 'Delay is permissible, but you risk sin if you die before performing.' This preserves permissibility while acknowledging the possibility of sin, avoiding the dilemma.
This is a fatwa that combines permissibility with caution (iḥtiyāṭ).
4
Analyze the usul implication
The ruling 'permissible with probable sin' is not a contradiction; it reflects that the act is allowed in itself, but the consequence (death) could make the delay blameworthy. This shows that fatwas can have layered conditions.
Usul principle: A ruling can be conditional on unknown future events; permissibility does not guarantee safety.
5
Apply to a similar scenario
Suppose someone asks about delaying obligatory zakat for a few months due to hardship. Using the same reasoning, a mufti could permit delay while warning that if the wealth is lost, the obligation remains as a debt.
The key is to balance feasibility and responsibility.

Commentary
This passage is a benefit (fā'ida) that applies the usul discussion on fawr vs. tarākhī to a concrete fatwa issue: delaying Hajj. The author of al-Kashf (likely a later commentator) attempts to refute Imam Muhammad's position that one may delay Hajj while being potentially sinful if death occurs before performance. He presents a dilemma: if the mufti says 'yes, delay' and the person dies, the mufti's answer would mean that the person dies sinless (contradicting Imam Muhammad's idea of sin); if 'no, delay is not allowed', that would force immediacy; and if the mufti says 'it depends on Allah's knowledge', that is useless because the knowledge is hidden. Shaykh al-Haddad provides a refined fatwa: the mufti should say that delay is permissible, but with the risk (iḥtimāl) of sin if death occurs before the next year; hence the person is held to be possibly sinful. This preserves both Imam Muhammad's permissibility and the notion that delay could lead to sin. The juristic point is that the ruling is not simply 'halal' or 'haram' but involves a probability of sin, which is acceptable in fatwa.

Key Terms
الإثم (al-ithm): Sin; the culpability incurred by failing to perform an obligatory act when it was possible.
التفويض (al-tafwīḍ): Delegation; leaving the choice of timing to the mukallaf within the possible period.
الفور (al-fawr): Immediacy; the requirement to perform an obligation at the first possible moment.
التراخي (al-tarākhī): Delay; permissibility to postpone an obligation beyond the first opportunity.

Key Takeaway
A mufti can permit an action while advising that sin may be incurred if circumstances change, demonstrating that permissibility and sinlessness are not identical.
