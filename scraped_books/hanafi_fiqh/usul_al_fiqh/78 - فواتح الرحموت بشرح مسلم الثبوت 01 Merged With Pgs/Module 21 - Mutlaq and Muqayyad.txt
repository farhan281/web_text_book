Attribution in Conjoining a Single Word to Another
Rule on how the conjoined element attributes to the same predicate as the first

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of 'atf al-mufrad to determine whether a conjoined element takes the exact same attribution or an estimated one.

Core Idea
Conjoined singular words share the same predicate; only a diverting factor forces estimation.

In Hanafi usul, when a singular word is conjoined to another, the conjoined element naturally attributes to the same predicate as the first. However, this principle has a crucial limit: if exact attribution is impossible, a similar predicate is estimated. This lesson dissects that rule and its application to conditional divorces and oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conjoined structure
Recognize that two singular words are linked by a conjunction (عطف). The first is المعطوف عليه, the second المعطوف.
Ensure the words are singular, not whole sentences.
2
Apply the default rule
State the rule: the conjoined takes the exact same predicate as the first. Reason: it is the principle in 'atf.
This is the default unless a diverting factor exists.
3
Check for a diverting factor (صارف)
If a linguistic or logical factor prevents identical attribution, then do not apply the default.
Examples include impossibility of the same event happening twice.
4
If identical attribution impossible, estimate a similar predicate (تقدير المثل)
For example, in 'Zaid came to me and Amr', the coming cannot be the exact same event, so a similar coming is estimated.
The estimated predicate shares the same type but not the identical occurrence.
5
Test with the divorce/oath examples
Apply the rule to the cases in the source (e.g., conditional divorce with conjoined 'divorced' and multiple oaths) to see how occurrence is determined.
Abu Hanifa's position gives one divorce; Sahibayn give two based on different interpretation.

Commentary
This passage discusses the principle of attribution when conjoining a singular word to another. The rule is that the conjoined element (معطوف) should attribute to the same predicate as the first (معطوف عليه) when possible. For example, in 'If you enter the house, then you are divorced, and (you are) divorced,' the second 'divorced' is linked to the same entry condition, not a new implied condition. This contrasts with the opinion of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (al-Shaybani) who might view it differently. The passage then delves into a debate about multiple oaths and conditions, using the example of someone saying 'Whenever I swear, my wife is divorced.' The Hanafi position is that if the oath is one, one divorce occurs; if multiple, two, based on the principle of t'allaq (suspension). The commentator refines the argument, stating that the attachment of the second via mediation does not necessarily delay occurrence. Finally, when exact attribution is impossible (e.g., 'Zaid came to me and Amr'), we estimate a similar predicate for the second.

The Particle Ba: Ilsaq and Its Functional Meanings in Hanafi Usul
Explaining the particle ba for ilsaq and its derived meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that the particle ba has a single core meaning (ilsaq) to classify its uses and derive the ruling on istibdal (substitution) before possession in a sale contract.

Core Idea
Ba has one core meaning — ilsaq — from which all its uses derive; it is not a homonym.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut explains the Hanafi position on the particle ba. Unlike grammarians who treat ba as having multiple independent meanings, Hanafi usul views all its functions — isti'ana, sababiyya, etc. — as instantiations of a single graded concept: adhesion. This linguistic principle directly impacts fiqh rulings, as we see in the case of substitution (istibdal) before possession in sales.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core meaning
The passage asserts that ba is placed for ilsaq (adhesion), and all its other meanings are types of ilsaq, not separate homonyms. Understand the Hanafi rejection of both homonymy and literal/metaphor division.
2
Recognize its instantiations
From ilsaq derive isti'ana (instrumentality), sababiyya (causality), zarfiyya (containment), musahaba (accompaniment). These are not independent but are particular applications of adhesion.
3
Apply to the ba of muqabala
The ba of exchange (muqabala) that enters upon prices is a type of isti'ana, because prices are means to acquire goods. This is the Hanafi view, countering those who see it as badaliyya (exchange).
4
Derive the fiqh ruling on istibdal
In the contract 'I bought this slave for a described kurr of wheat,' the ba enters upon the kurr, making it the price (thamān). Since the ba indicates isti'ana on the price, substitution before possession is permissible. In the reverse wording, the ba enters upon the slave, making it the price and the kurr the object of salam, requiring possession and disallowing substitution.
5
Consider the objection
Some grammarians argue the ba in the price is ba of badaliyya (exchange), not isti'ana. The Hanafi response: prices are fundamentally means, so isti'ana is primary. This objection does not affect the ruling.

Commentary
This passage discusses the particle ba and its primary meaning of adhesion (ilsaq). The author argues that ba is not a homonym with different independent meanings; rather, all its uses (isti'ana, sababiyya, zarfiyya, musahaba) are different manifestations of the graded concept of adhesion. This is the Hanafi position, opposing some grammarians who treat ba as homonymous and some who treat only the adhesion meaning as literal while others are metaphorical. The author provides two refutations: (1) applying the general (adhesion) to the specific (each particular usage) is not necessarily metaphor; (2) if ba were placed for general adhesion, it would become a noun, but particles denote particular relationships, so ba is actually placed for specific adhesion and other uses are metaphorical — but the author seems to argue against this too. The text then moves to the ba of muqabala (exchange) that enters upon prices. It is compared to isti'ana because prices are means to obtain goods. Despite an objection that both buyer and seller intend their respective items, the truth is that prices are primarily means, so the ba of isti'ana is appropriate. This has a fiqh application: In the contract 'I bought this slave for a described kurr of wheat,' the kurr is the price, so substitution (istibdal) is permissible before possession because the ba of muqabala/isti'ana is upon it. However, if the wording is 'I bought a described kurr of wheat with this slave,' then it is a salam contract (the slave is the price and the kurr is the object), and substitution before possession is not valid because salam requires possession. The author references At-Tahrir and mentions an objection that the ba is actually ba of badaliyya (exchange), not isti'ana. The author upholds the isti'ana view.

The Particle Ila and Inclusion of the Endpoint in Hanafi Usul
Usul definition of 'ila' with comparison to 'hatta'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi rule regarding the particle 'ila' and apply the distinction between inclusion and non-inclusion to Quranic verses and legal texts.

Core Idea
"Ila" excludes the endpoint by default; inclusion requires evidence of same genus or consumption.

In Hanafi usul, the particle 'ila' (to/until) marks the endpoint (ghaya) of a ruling. The critical question is whether the endpoint itself is included in the ruling. This lesson unpacks the default rule: non-inclusion, and the exceptions based on genus and consumption, illustrated by the Quranic verse on wudu.

Guided Path
1
Identify the particle and its function
Recognize that 'ila' indicates the limit or endpoint (ghaya) of a ruling. It delimits the scope of the preceding command or prohibition.
2
Understand the default: non-inclusion
In Hanafi usul, the established position for 'ila' is that the endpoint is not included in the ruling unless there is evidence to the contrary. This differs from 'hatta', which defaults to inclusion.
The text states: 'in 'ila' the school of non-inclusion'.
3
Compare with 'hatta'
Note that 'hatta' (until) is the counterpart particle that defaults to inclusion. Knowing this distinction helps in interpreting legal texts correctly.
The text says: 'as in 'hatta' there are schools regarding inclusion and non-inclusion, but the most well-known in 'hatta' is inclusion'.
4
Apply the exception: same genus
If the endpoint is of the same kind (jins) as what precedes it, inclusion may be indicated. For example, in 'wash your hands to the elbows', the elbows are part of the arm, same genus as the hand.
The text says: 'Inclusion if the following is of the same kind as what precedes'.
5
Apply the exception: consumption (tanaawul al-sadr)
When the action 'consumes' the initial part, the endpoint is included. The example of washing: the verb 'wash' consumes the hand, so the elbows are naturally included as part of the hand.
The text says: 'the detail is by the consumption of the initial part, if not for the endpoint having it, like the elbows.'

The Hanafi Rule for Ghayah: Context Determines Inclusion
Critical analysis of whether 'to' (إلى) includes the endpoint in legal commands

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule for ghayah (limit) to determine whether the endpoint is included or excluded in a legal command, using induction (istiqra).

Core Idea
The particle ‘to’ (إلى) does not fix inclusion or exclusion—context determines it.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rule for ghayah (limit). The central question is whether the endpoint mentioned after ‘to’ is included in the ruling or excluded. Hanafi usul uses induction (istiqra) to show that when the preceding phrase implies generality, the limit excludes what is beyond; when it does not, the limit extends to include the endpoint. We apply this to the wudu verse (5:6) and see why the Hanafi school holds washing the elbows as obligatory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic trigger
Locate the particle 'ila' (إلى) in the legal command and the wording before it. In the wudu verse, the command is 'wash your hands to the elbows.' The phrase 'wash your hands' is a general command without an initial specified extent.
2
Apply the induction (istiqra) principle
According to Hanafi usul, usage of 'ila' in Arabic shows that when the preceding phrase implies generality, the limit functions to exclude what is beyond it. Conversely, when the phrase does not imply generality, the limit extends the ruling to the endpoint.
Istiqra is induction from the language of the Quran and speech of the Arabs.
3
Determine inclusion or exclusion for wudu
Since 'wash your hands' without a limit would entail washing the entire hand (up to the shoulder), the limit 'to the elbows' excludes the upper arm. The endpoint (elbows) is included in the obligatory wash because the limit itself is the terminus; it is not excluded.
Thus the Hanafi position is that washing the elbows is obligatory.
4
Contrast with the opposing view
Some literalists argue that 'ila' always excludes the endpoint, or indicates nothing. The Hanafi response uses induction and the transmitted practice (the Prophet and Companions washed to the elbows) to affirm inclusion.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is whether washing the elbows is obligatory.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul analysis of the rule of ghayah (limit/endpoint) in legal commands, specifically addressing whether the limit is included in the ruling or excluded. The author critically refines various opinions. The primary question is the meaning of the particle 'to' (إلى) in the Quranic verse commanding wudu: 'wash your hands to the elbows' (Quran 5:6). The author argues that 'to' itself does not inherently indicate inclusion or exclusion; rather, the context matters. Using induction (istiqra), he shows that when the phrase before the limit implies generality (like a general command), the limit functions to exclude what is beyond it. When the phrase does not imply generality, the limit functions to extend the ruling up to the endpoint. In the case of wudu, the command 'wash your hands' without a limit would imply washing the entire hand (up to the shoulder), so the limit 'to the elbows' is brought to exclude the upper arm. Thus the ruling includes the elbows because they are the endpoint reached; the limit does not exclude them. The author critiques several alternative views, including those who interpret 'to' as 'with', those who argue for necessity of washing the elbows due to bone interlocking, and those who rely on precaution or ambiguity. He rejects the idea that precaution or practice alone can establish obligation. The Hanafi position, as elaborated here, is that the elbows are included in the obligation of washing based on linguistic analysis and transmitted practice. This analysis is central to Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

The claim that the verse is mujmal (ambiguous) fails because 'ila' is not shared between inclusion and exclusion. Precaution (ihtiyat) is invalid because the original state is non-obligation, not obligation. Moreover, practice (mulazamah) is problematic as it relies on the same particle.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul analysis of the rule of ghayah (limit/endpoint) in legal commands, specifically addressing whether the limit is included in the ruling or excluded. The author critically refines various opinions. The primary question is the meaning of the particle 'to' (إلى) in the Quranic verse commanding wudu: 'wash your hands to the elbows' (Quran 5:6). The author argues that 'to' itself does not inherently indicate inclusion or exclusion; rather, the context matters. Using induction (istiqra), he shows that when the phrase before the limit implies generality (like a general command), the limit functions to exclude what is beyond it. When the phrase does not imply generality, the limit functions to extend the ruling up to the endpoint. In the case of wudu, the command 'wash your hands' without a limit would imply washing the entire hand (up to the shoulder), so the limit 'to the elbows' is brought to exclude the upper arm. Thus the ruling includes the elbows because they are the endpoint reached; the limit does not exclude them. The author critiques several alternative views, including those who interpret 'to' as 'with', those who argue for necessity of washing the elbows due to bone interlocking, and those who rely on precaution or ambiguity. He rejects the idea that precaution or practice alone can establish obligation. The Hanafi position, as elaborated here, is that the elbows are included in the obligation of washing based on linguistic analysis and transmitted practice. This analysis is central to Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

The Hanafi Definition of Mutlaq (Absolute Term)
Defining mutlaq and differentiating from related terms.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of mutlaq and distinguish it from 'amm, nakira, and proper nouns of genus.

Core Idea
Mutlaq indicates a widespread individual, not the genus or all instances — it is a type of specific (khass).

In this lesson, we dive into the Hanafi definition of al-mutlaq, the absolute term. Understanding the precise meaning of mutlaq is essential for interpreting legal texts correctly, especially when determining whether a restriction (taqyid) can be applied. The Hanafi school defines mutlaq as indicating a widespread individual (fard muntashir), placing it as a particular (khass) rather than a general ('amm). We will explore how this definition differs from related categories and why it matters for deriving rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi definition of mutlaq
Write down the precise definition: 'ما دل على فرد ما منتشر' (that which indicates a widespread individual). Note that it is a portion of the genus that can accommodate many portions.
Remember: mutlaq is not 'amm, it is khass.
2
Identify what mutlaq is NOT
List the categories that are excluded from mutlaq: proper nouns of genera (like 'Usama'), all definite nouns (pronouns, demonstratives, etc.) unless mentally specified, and general terms ('amm) even if indefinite like 'every man'.
The negation of generality is key: mutlaq implies a single instance, not all.
3
Analyze the relationship between mutlaq and nakira
Mutlaq and nakira have a umum min wajh (general-specific in some respects) relationship. They coincide in examples like 'raqabah', but differ in that a general indefinite (like 'a man' unspecified) is not mutlaq, and a mentally known indefinite is not mutlaq.
This relationship is important for distinguishing when a term can be restricted.
4
Understand the practical significance
Because mutlaq indicates a widespread individual, it can be restricted (muqayyad) by adding a qualifier. This contrasts with 'amm which, if restricted, requires takhsis (specification).
This affects how we harmonize different texts (e.g., 'raqabah' and 'raqabah mu’minah').

Commentary
This chapter (fasl) begins the discussion of two important usul terms: al-mutlaq (the absolute) and al-muqayyad (the restricted). The Hanafi definition of mutlaq is that it indicates a widespread individual (fard ma muntashir), meaning a single instance of a category without specifying which instance. It is a portion of a genus that can have many portions. In the singular, it is a portion with the vague restriction of 'oneness', and in the plural, it is a group with the restriction of 'unity and spread', so it includes the indefinite plural (like 'men'). Mutlaq is distinguished from proper nouns of genera like 'Usama' (which indicate the genus itself), from all definite nouns (like pronouns, relative pronouns, definite with al, idafah, demonstratives—unless they are mentally specified), and from general terms ('amm) even if indefinite (like 'every man' or 'no man'). Importantly, mutlaq is a type of particular (khass), not general. The relationship between nakira (indefinite) and mutlaq is general-specific in some respects (umum min wajh): they coincide in examples like 'raqabah' (a slave), but differ in that a general indefinite (like 'a man' without specification) and a mentally known indefinite (like a specific man known to the listener) are not mutlaq. The Hanafi author critiques this by saying that it is based on the idea that nakira is placed for the essence; but in Hanafi usul, nakira is placed for individuals. The author argues that in common usage, the immediate understanding (tabadur) from an absolute term is an individual, not a nature, and this is the true indicator of linguistic meaning. He also discusses the difference between early and later logicians: early logicians' 'muhmalah' propositions judged on nature, while later ones judge on individuals. The author concludes that the Hanafi view is stronger because the purpose of speech is about individuals. Finally, he addresses the objection from Arab grammarians who say words are placed for natures and that spread comes from nunation; he allows that this could be a possibility but then the dispute is about the word itself, and it would lead to consequences like taking the definite with lam al-jins as metaphorical. This passage is foundational for understanding how Hanafi usul distinguishes between mutlaq, muqayyad, 'amm, and nakira.

Muqayyad and the Inter-Madhhab Dispute over Mutlaq
Defining muqayyad and presenting the dispute over mutlaq's definition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define muqayyad, identify the alternative definition of mutlaq proposed by al-Razi, and evaluate the Hanafi response using the criterion of tabadur.

Core Idea
Muqayyad is an absolute term restricted by an independent qualifier; the dispute over mutlaq's definition hinges on whether it indicates essence or individuals.

This lesson continues our study of mutlaq and muqayyad by examining the definition of muqayyad and the major scholarly disagreement regarding the definition of mutlaq. While the Hanafi school defines mutlaq as indicating a widespread individual, Imam al-Razi (Shafi‘i) and Sadr al-Shari‘ah (from our own school) argue that mutlaq indicates the reality as such. We will explore the basis of their claim, the Hanafi rejoinder based on common usage (tabadur), and the practical implications for textual interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Define muqayyad
According to the text, muqayyad is that which is taken out of spread by an independent qualifier (qayd mustaqill). For example, 'believing slave' (raqabah mu’minah) is muqayyad. Note that definite nouns are not muqayyad because they are not restricted by an independent qualifier.
The qualifier must be separate; this excludes definiteness which is inherent.
2
Present the alternative definition of mutlaq
Al-Razi and Sadr al-Shari‘ah hold that mutlaq indicates the reality (haqiqah) as such, i.e., the essence. On this view, the definite with lam al-jins (like 'al-insan' for humanity) would be considered mutlaq.
This definition is based on the idea that nakira is placed for the essence (mahiyyah) and that nunation adds spread.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The author argues that immediate understanding (tabadur) from an absolute term in common speech is an individual, not a nature. Additionally, the purpose of discourse (aghrāḍ) concerns individuals. Therefore, the Hanafi definition is more sound.
The author also addresses the grammatical argument: even if words are placed for natures, the nunation may indicate spread, but then the dispute is merely linguistic.

In the Hanafi view, 'al-insan' with lam al-jins is not mutlaq; it is definite and indicates the genus. In the other view, it would be mutlaq and could potentially be restricted by a qualifier. This affects how we interpret commands that are definite vs. absolute.

Commentary
This chapter (fasl) begins the discussion of two important usul terms: al-mutlaq (the absolute) and al-muqayyad (the restricted). The Hanafi definition of mutlaq is that it indicates a widespread individual (fard ma muntashir), meaning a single instance of a category without specifying which instance. It is a portion of a genus that can have many portions. In the singular, it is a portion with the vague restriction of 'oneness', and in the plural, it is a group with the restriction of 'unity and spread', so it includes the indefinite plural (like 'men'). Mutlaq is distinguished from proper nouns of genera like 'Usama' (which indicate the genus itself), from all definite nouns (like pronouns, relative pronouns, definite with al, idafah, demonstratives—unless they are mentally specified), and from general terms ('amm) even if indefinite (like 'every man' or 'no man'). Importantly, mutlaq is a type of particular (khass), not general. The relationship between nakira (indefinite) and mutlaq is general-specific in some respects (umum min wajh): they coincide in examples like 'raqabah' (a slave), but differ in that a general indefinite (like 'a man' without specification) and a mentally known indefinite (like a specific man known to the listener) are not mutlaq. The definition of muqayyad is that which is restricted from spread by an independent qualification, such as 'believing slave'; definite nouns are not muqayyad because their restriction is not by an independent word. The text then presents an alternative view from Imam al-Razi (Shafi‘i) and Sadr al-Shari‘ah (Hanafi) that mutlaq indicates the reality as such (haqiqah min haythu hiya hiya), which would include the definite with lam al-jins (like 'the man' meaning the human race) and the nature. The Hanafi author critiques this by saying that it is based on the idea that nakira is placed for the essence; but in Hanafi usul, nakira is placed for individuals. The author argues that in common usage, the immediate understanding (tabadur) from an absolute term is an individual, not a nature, and this is the true indicator of linguistic meaning. He also discusses the difference between early and later logicians: early logicians' 'muhmalah' propositions judged on nature, while later ones judge on individuals. The author concludes that the Hanafi view is stronger because the purpose of speech is about individuals. Finally, he addresses the objection from Arab grammarians who say words are placed for natures and that spread comes from nunation; he allows that this could be a possibility but then the dispute is about the word itself, and it would lead to consequences like taking the definite with lam al-jins as metaphorical. This passage is foundational for understanding how Hanafi usul distinguishes between mutlaq, muqayyad, 'amm, and nakira.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): An absolute term that indicates a widespread individual without specifying quantity or identity.
مقيد (muqayyad): A restricted term that has been taken out of spread by an independent qualifier, e.g., 'believing slave'.
نكرة (nakira): An indefinite noun; it can be general (like 'a man') or specific in mental context (like a particular man known to the listener).
عام ('amm): A general term that includes all individuals of its category, such as 'every man'.
معرف بلام الجنس (al-mu‘arraf bi-lam al-jins): A definite noun with the lam that indicates the genus or nature (e.g., 'al-insan' for humanity).

Hanafi Definition of Mutlaq and Muqayyad
Defining mutlaq and muqayyad and refuting opposing views

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mutlaq and muqayyad according to the Hanafi school and distinguish the Hanafi position from the opposing view of al-Razi and Sadr al-Shari'ah.

Core Idea
Hanafi mutlaq denotes an individual (fard muntashir), not essence; immediate understanding proves this.

This lesson unpacks the core Usul al-Fiqh concepts of mutlaq and muqayyad as defined by the Hanafi school. You will learn not only the definitions but also the crucial difference between the Hanafi position and that of Imam al-Razi and Sadr al-Shari'ah, who argue that mutlaq denotes essence. Understanding this dispute is foundational for correctly applying independent qualifiers in deriving rulings.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the Hanafi definition of mutlaq
Read the first part of the passage carefully: mutlaq is defined as 'ما دل على فرد ما منتشر' – that which denotes a certain general individual, a portion of a genus. In singular, it carries indefinite unity; in plural, group with unity and generality. Note what is excluded (proper nouns of genera, all definites, general words).
The exclusion of definites and general words shows mutlaq is a type of خاص (specific).
2
Understand muqayyad and its independent qualifier
Muqayyad is defined as that which is taken out of generality by an independent qualifier (قيد مستقل), like 'a believing slave'. Definite nouns are not muqayyad because their restriction comes from definiteness, not an independent qualifier.
This distinction matters when combining mutlaq and muqayyad verses.
3
Identify the opposing view
Imam al-Razi (Shafi'i) and Sadr al-Shari'ah (Hanafi dissenter) define mutlaq as denoting the essence as such (الحقيقة من حيث هي هي). They argue that indefinites are placed for the essence, and nunation adds generality.
This view would include definites like 'الطبيعة' as mutlaq.
4
Analyze Hanafi rebuttals
The Hanafi side argues that immediate understanding (تبادر) in ordinary language points to individuals, not essences. Many linguistic constructions (e.g., absolute object, predicates) would become metaphorical under the opposing view. The Hanafi position avoids unnecessary metaphor and is supported by the agreed-upon generality of plural definites.
The passage uses several rational proofs (dalil aqli) to support the Hanafi position.
5
Apply the practical consequence (thamarah)
The dispute affects how we combine mutlaq and muqayyad in the Qur'an and Sunnah. Under the Hanafi view, a mutlaq command applies to any individual of the kind; a muqayyad restricts it. Under the opposing view, mutlaq applies to the nature, which may lead to different interpretive outcomes.
For example, 'free a slave' (mutlaq) vs 'free a believing slave' (muqayyad).

Commentary
This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut explains the first two of five divisions concerning how mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (qualified) rulings interact. The author first outlines the five possible scenarios based on whether the rulings (hukm) and causes (sabab) are the same or different, and whether the texts are negative or affirmative. The first division: when the rulings differ. The example given is feeding a poor person and clothing a poor Tamimi – here the qualification 'Tamimi' in the second is not carried over to the first because the rulings are different. An exception is necessity, like freeing a slave when one can only own a believing slave. The commentator then discusses a disagreement between al-Amidi and al-Ghazali: al-Amidi reports that Hanafis and Shafi'is agree that the qualification is not carried over regardless of cause; al-Ghazali says most Shafi'is do carry it over when the cause is the same, citing the example of wudu (qualified to elbows) and tayammum (absolute). The commentator refutes al-Ghazali by showing that the Shafi'i position itself is not settled, and that the analogy is flawed. He then moves to the second division: same ruling, same cause, both negative. The example is from dhihar: 'Do not free a mukatib and do not free a disbelieving mukatib' – both prohibitions can be followed simultaneously, so no carrying over is needed. A debate ensues about whether this falls under the chapter of general ('amm) or remains a matter of absolute and qualified. The commentator concludes that the proper understanding is that the absolute here does not imply generality in the sense of 'amm, but still the two negatives can coexist.

Key Terms
المطلق (al-mutlaq): A word indicating an unrestricted reality, without qualification.
المقيد (al-muqayyad): A word that includes a qualification restricting its meaning.
الضرورة (al-darura): Necessity that overrides the default rule, as in the freeing slave example.

Mutlaq and Muqayyad with Same Ruling and Cause (Negative)
Explaining the second division of mutlaq and muqayyad with same ruling and cause, both negative.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule for negated mutlaq and muqayyad when the ruling and cause are the same, and explain the Hanafi-Shafi'i difference regarding nakira under negation.

Core Idea
When both texts are negative and share ruling and cause, they are applied together without carrying.

The second division involves cases where mutlaq and muqayyad share the same ruling and the same cause, and both are negative prohibitions. The default is to act on both, as there is no contradiction. However, this seemingly simple scenario opens a deeper debate about whether the negated indefinite (nakira) indicates generality or a single instance—a point where Hanafis and Shafi'is part ways.

Guided Path
1
Identify the second division
The second division is when mutlaq and muqayyad are in the same ruling with the same cause, and both are negative.
Example: 'Do not free a mukatib' and 'Do not free a disbelieving mukatib'.
2
State the agreed rule
Both texts are acted upon (tuw'amil bihima) by agreement; one is not carried to the other because there is no contradiction.
You can refrain from both acts easily.
3
Examine the dispute over nakira under negation
A discussion arises: is the negated indefinite (e.g., 'do not free a mukatib') general (shamil for all mukatibs) or does it only negate a single instance? Hanafis hold it is not necessarily 'amm; Shafi'is often treat it as 'amm.
The commentator defends the Hanafi view that it can be used for negation of a unit.
4
Apply the distinction
If the negated mutlaq is interpreted as negation of a single instance, there is no conflict with the muqayyad (which also negates a subset). Both can be observed.
This avoids the need for carrying or abrogation.
5
Conclude the tarjih
The commentator prefers the Hanafi reading, showing that the negated nakira is not necessarily 'amm, citing the author's earlier statement.
So the second division remains straightforward.

Commentary
This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut explains the first two of five divisions concerning how mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (qualified) rulings interact. The author first outlines the five possible scenarios based on whether the rulings (hukm) and causes (sabab) are the same or different, and whether the texts are negative or affirmative. The first division: when the rulings differ. The example given is feeding a poor person and clothing a poor Tamimi – here the qualification 'Tamimi' in the second is not carried over to the first because the rulings are different. An exception is necessity, like freeing a slave when one can only own a believing slave. The commentator then discusses a disagreement between al-Amidi and al-Ghazali: al-Amidi reports that Hanafis and Shafi'is agree that the qualification is not carried over regardless of cause; al-Ghazali says most Shafi'is do carry it over when the cause is the same, citing the example of wudu (qualified to elbows) and tayammum (absolute). The commentator refutes al-Ghazali by showing that the Shafi'i position itself is not settled, and that the analogy is flawed. He then moves to the second division: same ruling, same cause, both negative. The example is from dhihar: 'Do not free a mukatib and do not free a disbelieving mukatib' – both prohibitions can be followed simultaneously, so no carrying over is needed. A debate ensues about whether this falls under the chapter of general ('amm) or remains a matter of absolute and qualified. The commentator concludes that the proper understanding is that the absolute here does not imply generality in the sense of 'amm, but still the two negatives can coexist.

النكرة المنفية (al-nakira al-manfiyya): An indefinite noun in the scope of negation. The key interpretative question is whether it negates all individuals or just one.
الظهار (al-dhihar): A form of divorce where a husband compares his wife to his mother; he must expiate by freeing a slave before resuming relations.
المكاتب (al-mukatib): A slave who has entered into a contract with his master to earn his freedom by paying a sum.

The Later Muqayyad as Abrogator: Hanafi View on Mutlaq and Muqayyad
Rule statement with proof and intra-Hanafi clarification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a later muqayyad abrogates the mutlaq (naskh bi al-ziyada) and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-position.

Core Idea
A later muqayyad abrogates the mutlaq; it does not merely specify it.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position on the relationship between a mutlaq (absolute) and a muqayyad (restricted) ruling when they share the same cause but occur at different times. The debate with the Shafi'i school turns on whether the later muqayyad specifies the absolute (takhsis) or abrogates it (naskh). Mastery of this distinction is essential for deriving rulings from revealed texts, especially when later Qur'anic verses or well-known readings impose restrictions on earlier absolute commands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the temporal scenario
Determine whether the muqayyad appears before, simultaneously with, or after the mutlaq. The Hanafi rule depends on the temporal relationship. Here we focus on the scenario where the muqayyad is known to be later.
The source discusses three scenarios: simultaneous (haml), unknown (haml with caution), and later (naskh).
2
State the Hanafi ruling for a later muqayyad
The later muqayyad abrogates the absoluteness of the mutlaq and adds the restriction. The unrestricted individual (e.g., any three days) is no longer sufficient; only the restricted form is valid.
This is called naskh bi al-ziyada (abrogation by addition).
3
Examine the Hanafi dalil
The mutlaq is a haqiqa (reality) in its absoluteness. A haqiqa is not abandoned without a diverting proof (qarina sarifa). At the time of the mutlaq's utterance, no such proof exists because the muqayyad is yet to come. Therefore the mutlaq remains fully operative until the muqayyad abrogates it.
The text also argues that the mutlaq does not indicate particularity by any of the three indications (mantuq, mafhum, iltizam).
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
The Shafi'i school holds that the later muqayyad is a mukhassis (specifier), showing that the absolute was intended to be restricted from the beginning. This relies on interpreting the absolute as a majaz (metaphor) with the muqayyad as a contextual indicator.
The Hanafi response: The muqayyad is not a valid contextual indicator because it did not exist at the time of utterance, and metaphorical interpretation requires a present qarina.
5
Apply the rule to a concrete example
The verse 'fast three days' (al-Ma'idah 5:89) is mutlaq. Ibn Mas'ud's reading 'three consecutive days' is a later muqayyad (the reading is considered a legitimate restriction by Hanafis due to its wide acceptance). Hence, consecutive fasting is obligatory for kaffarah al-yamin.
The Shafi'i school rejects this because Ibn Mas'ud's reading is not mutawatir, so no valid muqayyad exists to restrict the absolute.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the Hanafi rule regarding haml al-mutlaq 'ala al-muqayyad (carrying the absolute upon the restricted) when both appear concerning the same ruling and cause. The author (from Fawatih al-Rahmut) first presents the scenario where the absolute and restricted come together simultaneously; here the absolute is understood to be restricted because a single cause cannot impose contradictory requirements at once. An example is the verse about fasting three days (al-Ma'idah 5:89) along with Ibn Mas'ud's reading specifying consecutiveness; the Hanafi school considers this reading a valid restriction (since it is mashhurah, widely accepted by the early community) and thus requires consecutive fasting in the expiation of an oath. The Shafi'i school rejects this because the reading is not mutawatir and thus not authoritative. When the date of the restricted is unknown, there is no basis for abrogation, so contemporaneity is assumed and the absolute is restricted. However, the stronger Hanafi position is that this is not a metaphorical reinterpretation but rather a precautionary practice: one acts by the restricted to be certain of discharging the obligation. When the restricted is known to be later (muta'akhkhir), it abrogates the absolute (naskh bi al-ziyada). The Shafi'i view that the later restricted is a mukhassis (specifier) of the absolute is rejected because the absolute was already fully operative at its time and nothing diverted its meaning. The author provides two main arguments: first, the absolute is a reality in its absoluteness, and a reality is not abandoned without a diverting proof (qarina sarifa), which does not exist at the time of the absolute's utterance; second, carrying is subsidiary to indication, and the absolute does not indicate particularity by any of the three indications (conformity, inclusion, implication), so the restricted merely abrogates the absolute. The passage then addresses objections: if the restricted precedes the absolute, Hanafis hold that the later absolute abrogates the earlier restricted (like general and specific). The example of 'raqaba' restricted by 'salama' is analyzed to show that the restriction may be merely a clarification of customary usage rather than a true restriction requiring abrogation. Finally, evidence from the Quran (5:101) and the saying of Ibn Abbas supports the Hanafi position that the absolute remains on its absoluteness until abrogated.

Key Terms
المطلق (al-mutlaq): A linguistic expression that is unrestricted in its meaning, carrying its full denotation without limitation.
المقيد (al-muqayyad): A linguistic expression that adds a restriction (qayd) to the mutlaq, limiting its scope.
النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation: the removal of a ruling by a later ruling. In this context, the later muqayyad cancels the absoluteness of the earlier mutlaq and adds a restriction.
القرينة (al-qarina): A contextual indicator that helps determine the intended meaning. In this text, it refers to evidence that would divert the mutlaq from its literal absoluteness.

Hanafi vs. Shafi'i: The Mutlaq-Muqayyad Dispute
Hanafi response to Shafi'i arguments on mutlaq and muqayyad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on mutlaq and muqayyad, apply the Hanafi principle of naskh when texts conflict, and evaluate the Shafi'i arguments for haml.

Core Idea
A later muqayyad abrogates a prior mutlaq; haml would disregard the absolute text's definitive indication.

In this passage, the Hanafi school defends its position that an absolute (mutlaq) text is not automatically carried (haml) upon a later restricted (muqayyad) text. Instead, if the muqayyad comes later, it abrogates (naskh) the mutlaq. The Shafi'i school argues that carrying is preferable because it 'works with both daleels' and is more cautious. This lesson unpacks the debate and shows why the Hanafis insist on abrogation unless a clear contextual indicator (qarina) exists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
The core question: When a muqayyad text (e.g., 'free a believing slave') appears after a mutlaq (e.g., 'free a slave'), does the muqayyad restrict the mutlaq automatically (haml) or abrogate it (naskh)?
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
Hanafis hold that the mutlaq is not carried; if the muqayyad comes later, it abrogates. They cite Quran 5:101 (prohibition of asking about non-apparent matters) and Ibn Abbas's statement 'Leave ambiguous what Allah has made ambiguous.' They argue the mutlaq is qat'i and cannot be abandoned without a qarina.
The hanafi dalil_block in the toolbox provides the Quranic proof.
3
State the Shafi'i position and its proof
Shafi'is argue that haml is 'working with both daleels' (the mutlaq is used metaphorically, and the muqayyad clarifies it). They also invoke caution (ihtiyat): carrying the silent (mutlaq) on the speaking (muqayyad) ensures certainty of fulfilling obligation.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
Hanafis rebut: haml actually disregards the mutlaq (its literal meaning is abandoned), while naskh allows both texts to be operative in their own times. The caution argument is refuted by inconsistency: Shafi'is do not carry in other similar cases (e.g., ikhtilaf hukman or difference in sabab), exposing a logical flaw.

5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
Practical consequence: In both schools, the final ruling is the same (only believing slaves). But the Hanafi reasoning preserves the integrity of the mutlaq text for its own time and avoids metaphor without a qarina. The Hanafi position is mufta bihi in this text.

Commentary
This passage is a usul al-fiqh dispute between the Hanafi school and the Shafi'i school concerning the relationship between absolute (mutlaq) and restricted (muqayyad) texts. The core issue: when one text is absolute (e.g., 'free a slave') and another text is restricted (e.g., 'free a believing slave'), does the restricted text constrain the absolute (carrying mutlaq upon muqayyad) or does it abrogate the absolute if it comes later?

The passage presents the Hanafi position: the absolute is not carried upon the restricted. The absolute, being definite in its indication (qat'i), remains on its absoluteness until a clear qarina (contextual indicator) diverts it. If the restricted comes later, it abrogates the absolute. If the restricted comes earlier, it may serve as a qarina, but only if the addressee knows of it at the time of the absolute and the speaker does not intend to lift the restriction. The Hanafis support this with the Quranic verse (5:101) forbidding asking about things that would distress if made plain, and with the statement of Ibn Abbas 'Leave ambiguous what Allah has made ambiguous.' They argue that carrying the absolute on the restricted effectively ignores the absolute text, while abrogation allows both texts to be operative in their own times.

The Shafi'i argument, as quoted from al-Minhaj, is that carrying the absolute on the restricted is 'working with both daleels' (evidence), whereas abrogation invalidates one. Also, caution (ihtiyat) demands carrying the silent (absolute) on the speaking (restricted) to ensure certainty in fulfilling obligation.

The Hanafi response refutes both: first, carrying actually disregards the absolute, while abrogation works with both in two times. Second, caution would also require carrying in other cases (e.g., difference in ruling or cause) which the Shafi'is do not do, exposing inconsistency. The Hanafis stress that the absolute's indication is definitive and cannot be abandoned without a clear qarina, which is absent in the case of a later restricted text.

The passage concludes by noting that the qarina must be present at the time of the absolute; otherwise, the absolute remains on its face, and the later restricted abrogates.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): In usul al-fiqh, a text that indicates a meaning without any qualification (qayd).
مقيد (muqayyad): A text that indicates a meaning with a qualification or restriction.
حمل (haml): To interpret the absolute text as being restricted by the restricted text, effectively constraining the absolute's scope.
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation; the later ruling supersedes the earlier one. Here, the later muqayyad abrogates the earlier mutlaq.
قرينة (qarina): A contextual indicator that diverts the meaning from the apparent to something else.
دلالة التزامية (dalala iltizamiyya): Implicational indication; a meaning that is necessarily implied by the literal meaning.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul dispute between the Hanafi school and the Shafi'i school regarding whether an absolute (mutlaq) term is to be restricted by a qualified (muqayyad) term when the two occur in different legal causes but share the same ruling (e.g., freeing a slave in two different expiations). The Hanafi position, as presented, is that the absolute is not carried to the qualified; each text is applied independently. The Shafi'i position is that the absolute is carried to the qualified via qiyas (analogy). The passage begins by discussing arguments about precaution and whether the qualified is a clarification (bayan) or an abrogator (nasikh). It then moves to the specific case where causes differ, citing Quranic verses: for zihar expiation the slave is absolute (any slave), for killing expiation the slave is restricted to a believing slave. Hanafis do not restrict the absolute by the qualified, so a disbelieving slave suffices for zihar expiation. Shafi'is carry the absolute to the qualified due to a common underlying cause (jami'). The text then argues that qiyas does not prove linguistic intent but only legal establishment. It also addresses objections: if qiyas can specify the general, why not here? The response is that specification by qiyas is a legal necessity to resolve contradiction, not a linguistic operation. The passage further distinguishes between restriction (taqyid) and specification (takhsis), arguing that restriction introduces a new ruling while specification merely repels part of the ruling. Finally, it provides two Hanafi arguments: first, that the absolute text opposes the qiyas, so the condition of qiyas is unmet; second, that the 'original' ruling in qiyas (non-acceptability of other than the qualified) is not a established legal ruling according to Hanafis, so it cannot serve as an analogical basis.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A term indicating a category without restriction, e.g., 'raqaba' (slave) without specifying belief.
مقيد (muqayyad): A term with an additional restriction, e.g., 'raqaba mu'mina' (believing slave).
تقييد (taqyid): Restriction: adding a qualification that limits the scope of a term, considered to introduce a new ruling.
تخصيص (takhsis): Specification: excluding some individuals of a general term without adding a new ruling; it repels the ruling from those individuals.

Mutlaq and Muqayyad with Different Causes: The Hanafi Position
Debating whether a mutlaq ruling is restricted by a muqayyad ruling from a different context

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school does not restrict a mutlaq command by a muqayyad from a different cause, and evaluate the Shafi'i qiyas argument for restricting.

Core Idea
A mutlaq command from one cause is not restricted by a muqayyad from a different cause — qiyas cannot create a linguistic intention.

This passage tackles a foundational usul dispute: when two Quranic verses give different treatments of a similar ruling, do we harmonize them by carrying the absolute to the restricted? The Hanafi school argues that if the causes (asbab) differ, the absolute stands on its own, even if another verse restricts a similar command in a different context. This lesson unpacks the reasoning, focusing on the zihar and accidental killing expiation verses, and critiques the Shafi'i reliance on qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two texts and their causes
Locate the mutlaq verse (zihar expiation: 'free a slave') and the muqayyad verse (killing expiation: 'free a believing slave'). Note that they address different legal causes (zihar vs. accidental killing).
The cause difference is key: Hanafi sees it as preventing restriction.
2
State the Hanafi position
The mutlaq remains unrestricted; an unbelieving slave suffices in zihar expiation. The muqayyad only applies to its own cause.
This is the mufta bihi position.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i qiyas argument
The Shafi'i school uses qiyas to extend the restriction (faith requirement) from the killing expiation to zihar, based on a common illah (both are expiations). They also argue that the unity of divine speech requires harmonization.
Understand the structure: asl (killing expiation), far' (zihar expiation), illah (expiation), hukm (believing slave required).
4
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal
Hanafi responds: qiyas is a legal proof, not a linguistic one; it cannot indicate that the Lawgiver intended restriction in the mutlaq text. Moreover, the mutlaq text opposes the qiyas, invalidating the qiyas requirement. Also, the ruling in the origin (non-validity of other than the restricted) is not a legal ruling, so qiyas cannot be based on it.
The rebuttal distinguishes between legal obligation and linguistic meaning.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul dispute regarding the relationship between mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (restricted) commands when they arise from different causes (asbab). The core example is the freeing of a slave in the expiation of zihar (mutlaq) and in the expiation of accidental killing (muqayyad with faith). The commentary by the author, Ibn Abd al-Shakur, explains the reasoning, including the conditions for qiyas and the distinct nature of legal proofs.

Key Terms

مقيد (muqayyad): A command with a qualifying restriction (e.g., 'free a believing slave').
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning used to extend a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a common effective cause (illah).

Hanafi Defense: Absolute Cause Not Restricted by Coexisting Restricted Cause
Hanafi defense of not carrying absolute cause to restricted

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on carrying an absolute cause to a restricted cause and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using usul principles.

Core Idea
An absolute cause is complete and sufficient; it does not require restriction to coexist with a restricted cause.

This passage addresses a critical usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over how to handle two texts: one with an absolute cause and one with a restricted cause. The Shafi'is argue that the absolute must be restricted to avoid contradiction; the Hanafis uphold that both can operate independently. You will analyze the reasoning behind each position and understand the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
Recognize that the core dispute is whether an absolute cause (al-mutlaq) must be carried (haml) to a coexisting restricted cause (al-muqayyad).
This is a fundamental usul al-fiqh debate between Hanafis and Shafi'is.
2
State the Shafi'i position and their proof
The Shafi'is argue that if both an absolute and a restricted cause exist, the absolute must be restricted to reconcile the texts, otherwise there would be contradiction. They rely on the concept of understood meaning (mafhum).
Their impetus is to avoid imagined contradiction between two statements.
3
State the Hanafi position and proof
The Hanafis hold that the absolute remains absolute and sufficient because there is no real contradiction: a thing can have multiple causes. The completeness of the absolute in causality prevents the need for restriction.
The key logical argument: requiring restriction would imply the absolute is incomplete, which contradicts its efficacy.
4
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal of the Shafi'i objection
The Hanafis respond that the absence of a part is not a real cause for non-existence; it is merely a correlate. Thus, the completeness of the absolute does not conflict with the causality of the restricted.
The objection regarding absence of parts is refuted by distinguishing real causes from mere correlates.

This principle has a direct application in fiqh: it explains the well-known rule that debts are paid with equivalents (al-duyun tuqda bi amthaliha). We will trace the reasoning from the usul principle to the fiqh ruling and distinguish debts from trusts, which require specific identities.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion on the object of a command when it is absolute (mutlaq). The matn states that the absolute command demands the essence (mahiyyah min haythu hiya hiya), even if embodied in an individual. The commentator elaborates with the example of debt repayment: if the command is 'pay the debt,' the demanded act is the essence of debt repayment, not a specific coin. The jurists' statement 'debts are repaid by equivalents' means that what is performed is an individual similar to the debt, not the exact same identity, because debts are qualities in the dhimmah (liability) not specific items. This reconciles with the command demanding the essence. Then various positions are presented: Ibn al-Hajib held that the absolute command demands a real particular (juz'i haqiqi). The commentator argues against this, saying that if a real particular were demanded, the command would be ambiguous. The Hanafi position is that the absolute command seeks the absolute essence, not a particular. The essence has multiple considerations: as free from conditions (la bi sharat shay'), as conditioned by absence (bi shart la shay'), and as conditioned by something (bi shart shay'). This nuanced view prevents the impossibility of demanding the essence. The passage ends with a discussion of these considerations, defending the Hanafi view against objections that the essence cannot exist externally.

Key Terms
الماهية (al-mahiyyah): The quiddity or essence of a thing, considered in itself without any determinations.
جزئي حقيقي (juz'i haqiqi): A real particular, an actually existing individual with specific determinations.
اللا بشرط (la bi shart): Considering something without any condition, i.e., pure essence.
