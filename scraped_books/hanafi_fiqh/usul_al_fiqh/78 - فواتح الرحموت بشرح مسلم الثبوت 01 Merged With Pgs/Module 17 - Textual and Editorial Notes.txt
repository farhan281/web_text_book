Two Definitions of Mufrad and Murakkab in Usul al-Fiqh
Presenting two definitions of مفرد and مركب with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a given word is mufrad or murakkab under each of the two definitions and explain the difference.

Core Idea
A single word may be mufrad by one definition and murakkab by another — it depends whether parts indicate parts of the meaning.

In usul al-Fiqh, classifying a word as mufrad or murakkab determines how its meaning is treated. This passage presents two competing definitions: one based on custom (urf) and one based on the indication of parts. Understanding both is essential for analyzing texts, especially when dealing with compound proper nouns and derived verbs. The passage also includes a debate about the verb 'adrib' where Ibn Sina's view is defended against the majority of grammarians.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions
The first definition (urf) considers a word mufrad if it is a single unit in custom. The second definition (istilah) considers a word mufrad if no part of the word indicates any part of its meaning. Both are technical terms used in usul.
These definitions are not contradictory; they apply to different analytical contexts.
2
Apply the first definition to examples
Take examples: 'al-rajul' is mufrad because it is one word in custom. 'Ba'labak' is murakkab because it consists of two words. 'Darabtu' is mufrad because it is one word.
Custom (urf) refers to common usage, not grammatical analysis.
3
Apply the second definition to the same examples
Under the second definition, 'Ba'labak' is mufrad because its parts (Ba'al and Bakk) do not indicate parts of its proper meaning. 'Darabtu' is murakkab because the ta indicates the subject and the root indicates the event, so parts indicate parts of the meaning.
The second definition is more precise for linguistic analysis.
4
Analyze the controversial example 'adrib'
The hamzah in 'adrib' indicates the speaker (subject) and the root d-r-b indicates the event. Thus, under the second definition, it is murakkab. The first definition considers it mufrad because it is one word. Ibn Sina supports murakkab, grammarians object.
The disagreement reveals different views on what constitutes a 'part' of a word.
5
Evaluate the different positions
The passage argues for Ibn Sina's view, noting that the letters suffice for indication. The grammarians' claim that the subject is intended (not explicit) is rejected as arbitrary. Understanding this dispute helps sharpen one's analytical skills.
In usul, such definitions are foundational for interpreting legal texts.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional discussion (taqsim) of words (alfaz) into singulative (mufrad) and composite (murakkab) according to two distinct technical definitions. The first definition (urf) considers a word mufrad if it is a single unit in custom (e.g., 'rajul'). The second definition (istilah) considers a word mufrad if no part of the word indicates any part of its meaning. The author provides examples: 'Ba'labak' is composite by the first definition (two words joined) but mufrad by the second (its parts do not indicate parts of its proper meaning). The word 'adrib' (I strike) is complex: Ibn Sina argues it is composite because the hamzah and root each indicate part of the meaning (subject and event), but most grammarians disagree. The author supports Ibn Sina's view with logical reasoning. The discussion also addresses verbs like 'daraba' (he struck) and 'darib' (beating), analyzing whether their parts indicate parts of meaning. The passage concludes with a critique and a note on 'darib' and 'daraba'.

Key Terms
مفرد (mufrad): A word that is either a single unit in custom (first definition) or a word whose part does not indicate part of its meaning (second definition).
مركب (murakkab): A word that is either composed of more than one unit in custom (first definition) or a word whose part indicates part of its meaning (second definition).
بعلبك (Ba'labak): A proper noun that is composite according to the first definition (two words) but mufrad according to the second (parts do not indicate parts of its proper meaning).

Two Interpretations of Wad': Resolving Majaz and Letters
Resolves apparent contradiction about assignment in majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two Hanafi interpretations of wad' and apply them to explain how majaz and letters each relate to assignment.

Core Idea
Assignment (wad') can be absolute or restricted; the restricted view excludes majaz, the absolute includes it.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut resolves an apparent contradiction: if majaz is defined as using a word in other than its assigned meaning, how can we speak of wad' in majaz? The author clarifies that wad' has two interpretations, and depending on which you adopt, majaz either has or lacks wad'. The discussion extends to letters (huruf), showing a deeper subtlety about how words indicate meaning.

Guided Path
1
Contrast the two definitions of wad'
Identify the first definition: wad' as specification for a meaning indicated by itself without any adjunct. Identify the second: wad' as absolute specification, even if indication requires an adjunct (qarina). Note that only the first definition excludes majaz.
The passage explicitly states: 'On this, there is no assignment in majaz.'
2
Apply each definition to majaz and letters
Under the first definition, majaz has no wad' because it needs a qarina. Under the second, majaz has wad' because the word is specified for the metaphorical meaning via the qarina. Then consider letters: they need a related word for their meaning to be conceivable, but the letter itself indicates its meaning. The passage explains that this need is a condition for the indication, not a complement to it.
The key distinction: in majaz the qarina and the word together indicate the meaning; in letters the word alone indicates, but the related word is external.
3
Resolve the objection about letters
The objection claimed that letters would be excluded from wad' under the first definition because they need a related word. The answer differentiates the nature of the need: in majaz it's for indication (together), in letters it's for conceptualization (condition). Thus letters still have wad' under both definitions.
The last sentence in the passage emphasizes this point: 'فإنها تكون فيه تابعة لمعقولية' (it is subordinate to conceivability).

Commentary
This passage (فائدة) addresses a potential contradiction: if majaz is defined as not being used in its assigned (real) meaning, how can we say there is wad' (assignment) in majaz? The author resolves it by distinguishing two interpretations of wad'. (1) Wad' as a specific assignment such that the word indicates the meaning by itself without any external aid. Under this, there is no wad' in majaz because it requires a qarina (contextual indicator). (2) Wad' as absolute assignment, even if it requires an adjunct (ضميمة). Under this, majaz does have wad' because the word is assigned to indicate the metaphorical meaning via the qarina. The passage then addresses an objection concerning letters (حرف) like prepositions, which also need a related word to be understood. The answer distinguishes: in majaz, the word and qarina together create the indication; in letters, the letter itself indicates its meaning but the related word is a condition for the very conceivability of that meaning. The passage ends abruptly but clarifies that letters are not excluded from wad' under the first interpretation because the need for a related word is different in nature from the need for a qarina in majaz. This is a subtle refinement of the definition of wad', showing the depth of Hanafi usul analysis.

Key Terms
الوضع (al-wad'): Two interpretations: (1) specification for a meaning indicated by itself; (2) absolute specification even if via an adjunct
قرينة (qarina): A contextual indicator that enables the intended metaphorical meaning
حرف (harf): A particle (e.g., preposition) whose meaning is not fully conceivable without a related word

Signs of Majaz: Sidq al-Nafi and Tabadur
Explaining the first two signs identifying majaz in usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul signs of sidq al-nafi and tabadur to classify words as haqiqa or majaz, and identify the conditions (primary attribution, homonymy, theoretical doubt) that refine their application.

Core Idea
Two key signs of majaz: truth of negation and immediate understanding — but both require careful theoretical application.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahmut delves into the methodological signs that distinguish literal from metaphorical usage in Hanafi legal theory. The first sign (sidq al-nafi) examines whether the real meaning can be truthfully negated; the second (tabadur) considers which meaning comes to mind first. The text addresses several objections and refinements, including the critical role of primary attribution (al-haml al-haqiqi al-awwali) and the challenge of homonyms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first sign: sidq al-nafi
State that if the real meaning can be truthfully negated from the usage, the word is majaz. Example: 'He is not a donkey' said of a dull person indicates 'donkey' is metaphorical.
The opposite (negation not correct) indicates haqiqa.
2
Refine with primary attribution (al-haml al-haqiqi al-awwali)
Understand that negation must be assessed based on whether the real and used meanings are identical in essence. If they are not identical (e.g., whole vs part), negation can be correct, confirming majaz.
This resolves the part/whole objection.
3
Evaluate the homonym objection
Consider that negating some real meanings does not prove majaz if the word is a known homonym (mushtarak). The discussion is about doubtful (mushkik) cases, not known homonyms.
The sign works under the condition that the word is not a known homonym.
4
Address the circularity objection
Note that negating all real meanings requires knowing all meanings, leading to circularity. The author resolves that the discussion is about theoretical proof (nazar) in cases of doubt, where certainty is not presupposed.
Negating some meanings can suffice by forcing majaz to avoid homonymy.
5
Introduce the second sign: tabadur (immediate understanding)
If the literal meaning comes to mind without context, it is haqiqa; if another meaning comes first, it is majaz. This is the converse of the negation sign.
Tabadur is also subject to theoretical refinement.

Commentary
This passage from 'Fawatih al-Rahmut' explains the signs (amarat) that indicate whether a word is used metaphorically (majaz) or literally (haqiqa) in usul al-fiqh. The author first presents the first sign: the truth of negating the real meaning. For example, if you say to a dull person 'He is not a donkey,' and it is true, then 'donkey' is used metaphorically because the real meaning (animal) is negated. Conversely, if you cannot truthfully negate the real meaning, it indicates literal usage; e.g., you cannot say of a dull person 'He is not a human' because 'human' is literal. However, a problem arises when a word is used for a part or a necessary attribute (as in metonymy). The author clarifies that the negation of the part is not the same as negation of the whole. The criterion is primary attribution (haml haqiqi awwali): if the real meaning and the used meaning are not identical in essence, then negation can be correct, indicating majaz. But if they are identical, it is haqiqa. The author then addresses a subtle objection: if primary attribution is used, then 'Zaid is an animal' would be majaz because Zaid is not identical to animal. The rebuttal is that the negation considered is of the real meaning from the used instance, not of the universal from the particular. Negating animality from Zaid is correct, but that negation is not the one under discussion; what is relevant is negating the real meaning (e.g., 'human') from the used instance (Zaid), which is not correct. So the example does not undermine the rule. The passage then moves to a second objection: negating some real meanings does not prove majaz because of homonymy (ishtirak). For instance, a homonym used in one meaning is literal, and negating the other meaning is correct. Thus, negating some meanings is not a decisive sign. The author discusses responses: negating all real meanings would require knowing all meanings, leading to circularity. He argues that the discussion is about theoretical proof (nazar) in cases of doubt, not about intuitive knowledge. Negating some meanings can suffice if it leads to a two-meaning situation, forcing majaz to avoid homonymy. However, this answer is refined: if the negation applies to all real meanings, it is too obscure; but if it applies to some, it may not rule out an unknown literal meaning. The author concludes by introducing the second sign: immediate understanding (tabadur). If the literal meaning comes to mind without context, the word is literal; if another meaning comes first, it is metaphorical. This sign is the converse of the first. The passage is dense and deals with fine points of usul al-fiqh reasoning, emphasizing that these signs work in theoretical contexts where the nature of the word is uncertain.

Key Terms
صدق النفي (sidq al-nafi): A sign of majaz: it is truthfully correct to negate the real meaning from the word's usage (e.g., negating 'donkeyness' from a dull person).
الحمل الحقيقي الأولي (al-haml al-haqiqi al-awwali): Primary real attribution: considering whether the used meaning is identical in essence to the real meaning. If they differ, negation is possible.
مجاز (majaz): Metaphorical usage: a word used for a meaning other than its original assigned meaning with a valid connection (qarina).
حقيقة (haqiqa): Literal usage: a word used for its original assigned meaning without any other intended meaning.
مشترك (mushtarak): Homonym: a word with multiple literal meanings. In such words, negating one meaning does not prove majaz because it remains literal in the other.

Preferring Conjunction to the Nearest Antecedent in Legal Texts
Stating and proving the rule of preferring nearer conjunction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule of preferring nearer conjunction to analyze conditional legal statements and identify when a sārif (indicator) shifts the conjunction to a distant antecedent.

Core Idea
Unless a clear indicator intervenes, conjunction defaults to the nearest antecedent.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut explains a key grammatical principle in Hanafi usul: when interpreting conditional legal statements, conjunctions are presumed to attach to the nearest clause unless a contextual indicator (sārif) points elsewhere. This rule has direct implications for deriving rulings from texts like the Quran and legal formulations. Understanding this principle helps us correctly determine the scope of conditional clauses in divorce, manumission, and other legal acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Default Rule
State the default rule: conjunction is to the nearest antecedent. In the example, 'If you enter, you are divorced and my slave is free,' the freedom is tied to entry because it is conjoined to the nearest clause 'you are divorced.'
2
Recognize the Exception
Note the exception: a sārif (indicator) can shift conjunction to a distant antecedent. The sārif is a contextual cue like explicit mention of a predicate (e.g., 'your co-wife is divorced' where 'divorced' is repeated) that shows the conjunction is not to the nearest.
3
Analyze the Quranic Example
Examine the verse from Surah al-Nur (النور: 4): 'وَأُولَئِكَ هُمُ الْفَاسِقُونَ' is conjoined to a distant clause due to a sārif of multiple addressees. The near clauses address the community (plural), while the distant clause addresses the Prophet (singular), making conjunction to the distant more appropriate.
The sārif here is the shift in addressee from plural to singular.
4
Evaluate the Objection
Consider the objection from al-Talwih that difference in addressee is permissible and occurs in eloquent speech. The author dismisses this as irrelevant because the rule is about preference (awlawiyya), not absolute prohibition.
The rebuttal hinges on the concept of 'preference'—the default is preferred, but an indicator can override it.

Rule of Attribution in Conjoining Single Words
Explaining the rule of attribution when conjoining single words and its applications in oath disputes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of attribution (intisāb) to determine whether a conjoined predicate is attributed identically or by similitude, and analyze the resulting legal effects in conditional oaths.

Core Idea
Conjoined elements in Arabic assume the exact attribution of the first; a different attribution requires clear evidence.

This passage from 'Fawatih al-Rahamut' delves into a key rule of Arabic syntax that directly impacts legal rulings: when two single words are conjoined (e.g., 'divorced and divorced' or 'Zayd and Amr came'), the second is attributed with the very same attribution as the first. This linguistic principle has significant consequences in usul al-fiqh, particularly in understanding how multiple conditions or oaths interact. The author explores a dispute with the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding the multiplication of divorces in repeated oaths, and then addresses the nuanced case where identical attribution is impossible due to distinct subjects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base rule of intisāb
Read the opening sentence: 'The second (the conjoined) is attributed with the very same attribution that the first (the conjoined-to) is attributed to, if such attribution is possible.' Understand that this is the default – the conjoined element inherits the exact same predicate relation as the first, not a similar one.
This rule is rooted in the nature of conjunction (al-‘aṭf) in Arabic: it implies sharing (mushāraka).
2
Apply the rule to the conditional oath example
Consider the statement: 'If you enter the house, you are divorced, and (you are) divorced.' According to the rule, the second 'divorced' attaches to the same condition (entry). Therefore, only one divorce occurs upon entry. This is Abu Hanifa’s position.
The key point: the second is not a separate condition – it is tied via the first.
3
Analyze the dispute with the Sahibayn
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) argue that two oaths were uttered, each independently triggering divorce, resulting in two divorces. The author critiques their view, showing that sequentiality in utterance does not imply sequentiality in legal effect; the second attaches through the first, leaving no room for a second occurrence.
The dispute illustrates the practical import of the rule: one divorce vs. two.
4
Handle cases where identical attribution is impossible
Example: 'Zayd and Amr came.' The coming of Zayd is distinct from the coming of Amr; identical attribution is impossible. The rule then requires us to estimate a similar (mithl) attribution – i.e., each has his own instance of 'coming'.
The objection that this leads to a single accident in two loci is refuted: the verb's predication is not tied to a specific agent but to a generic specific, allowing distribution.
5

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four main uses of 'bal' (in single word, with 'la', after prohibition/negation, in sentences) and apply the Hanafi usul principle that 'bal' is always a conjunction of turning away.

Core Idea
Bal always turns away from the first statement, never annuls it.

The particle 'bal' is common in Quran and hadith. Its precise grammatical function affects how we understand commands and negations. This lesson explains the Hanafi view that 'bal' is a conjunction of turning away (i'rad), not annulment, and distinguishes its various contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the context of 'bal'
Determine if 'bal' appears with a single word (mufrad), with 'la', after a prohibition/negation, or in a full sentence (jummala).
2
Apply the corresponding rule
For each context, state the effect: (1) single word: first becomes as if unspoken; (2) with 'la': negation of the first; (3) after prohibition/negation: affirms opposite while preserving first's negation; (4) in sentence: either annulment of the first sentence or transition to a new topic.
3
Recognise the Hanafi position on 'bal' as a conjunction
Understand that Hanafi usul rejects Ibn Hisham's view that 'bal' can be inceptive. The text argues that evidence supports that 'bal' is always a conjunction (عاطفة) for turning away.
The author says the inceptive view is 'prevented' and requires proof; the commonality (اشتراك) would be less preferred.
4
Note the practical consequence
The ruling affects how we interpret legal texts. For example, in an acknowledgment, 'bal' does not annul the first but turns away from its singularity.

Key Terms
بل (bal): A particle of conjunction with the primary meaning of turning away (إعراض) from a previous statement. It has different uses: in a single word it makes the first as if unspoken (مَسكوت عنه), in a sentence it can be for annulment (إبطال) or transition (انتقال).

The Hanafi Ruling on Acknowledgment with 'Bal': Two Dirhams, Not Three
Subsidiary issue on acknowledgment with 'bal'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate Imam Zufar's position on the acknowledgment 'I owe a dirham, but rather two dirhams' and apply the correct Hanafi position that only two dirhams are due.

Core Idea
Bal in numerical acknowledgments turns away from singularity, not from the entire acknowledgment.

When someone says 'I owe a dirham, but rather two dirhams', a dispute arises: does he owe one, two, or three? Imam Zufar argued for three, but the relied-upon Hanafi position is that only two are due. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and clarifies how 'bal' functions in this context.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario
Consider the acknowledgment: 'I owe a dirham, but rather two dirhams'. Identify the possible interpretations: (a) one dirham, (b) two dirhams, (c) three dirhams, (d) invalid.
2
Identify Imam Zufar's reasoning
Imam Zufar argued for three dirhams, based on two possible rationales: either 'bal' annuls the first acknowledgment (which he thinks is invalid, so both stand) or the turning away is a retraction (which is also invalid, so both stand). Thus, three dirhams.
Zufar's first rationale was rejected because 'bal' in a single word does not annul; the second rationale was also rejected.
3
Critique Zufar's reasoning using the text
The author points out that 'bal' in a single word is not for annulment (ibṭāl). Also, in numbers, custom ('urf) is to turn away from the attribute of singularity, not from the whole acknowledgment. So 'bal' does not retract the acknowledgment; it simply clarifies the intended number.
The text says: 'المتعارف في أسماء العدد الإضراب عن صفة الانفراد'.
4
Apply the correct ruling
The acknowledgment is valid for two dirhams. The first dirham is not annulled but is treated as if unspoken regarding its singularity. The acknowledgment of the extra (the second dirham) implicitly concedes the first, so the total is two.
The text says: 'في الزيادة تسليم المزيد عليه فلا يبطل بهذا الإضراب الإقرار فيصح'.

Hatta for Ghayah and the Condition of Partiality
Analyzing hatta for ghayah and partiality conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three grammatical functions of hatta (jar, atf, ibtida') and identify the condition of partiality for jar and atf, while explaining how the speaker's consideration can make hatta indicate a limit even if not a literal endpoint.

Core Idea
Hatta can indicate a subjective limit, but as a preposition or conjunction it requires that what follows be a part of what precedes.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut analyzes the particle hatta, a critical tool in legal interpretation. The author first addresses its role as a marker of ghayah (limit), defending its use even when based on the speaker's viewpoint. He then outlines the grammatical conditions for hatta as a preposition and conjunction, emphasizing the requirement of partiality (al-ba'diyyah). Understanding these conditions is essential for accurately interpreting legal texts that use hatta to define boundaries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim about hatta and ghayah
What does the author assert about hatta's indication of a limit, and how does he defend it?
The author argues that even if the limit is not literal, the speaker's consideration makes it valid, citing transmission from linguists.
2
Distinguish the three grammatical cases
Recognize hatta as jar (preposition), atf (conjunction), and ibtida' (inceptive). Note the condition of partiality for jar and atf.
Partiality means that what follows must be a part of what precedes, not just included in its ruling.
3
Analyze the poetry evidence
How does Imru' al-Qais's verse exemplify hatta in all three modes?
The verse 'siritu bihim hatta takilla matiyyuhum wa hatta al-jiyadu ma yuqadna bi-arsan' shows hatta as jar (al-jiyad), nasb (takilla), and raf' (estimated predicate).
4
Examine the schools on entry (dakhul) for jar
Summarize the four positions on whether what follows hatta enters into the ruling, and note the author's critique.
The four schools: (1) absolute entry, (2) absolute non-entry, (3) entry only if a suitable part, (4) no inherent indication.
5
Note the agreement on atf and ibtida'
What do scholars concur on regarding entry?
There is consensus that entry applies when hatta is used as a conjunction or inceptive.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition on the particle حتى (hatta) as used in legal and linguistic contexts, from the commentary on Musallam al-Thubut. It begins by affirming that حتى can indicate a limit (ghāyah) even if based on the speaker's subjective perspective, as in the examples 'people died, even the prophets' and 'the pilgrims arrived, even the pedestrians.' The commentator defends this against claims of artificiality, citing transmission from linguists. It then explains that حتى functions as a preposition (jārrah) and conjunction (ʿāṭifah), both requiring partiality (al-baʿḍiyyah): what follows must be part of what precedes. When used as an inceptive (ibtidāʾiyyah) with a full sentence, the predicate must be of the same kind or a necessary concomitant. The text then presents the debate on whether what follows حتى enters into the ruling (dakhūl) when it is a preposition. Four scholarly positions are given: (1) absolute entry, (2) absolute non-entry, (3) entry only if the following is a suitable part, and (4) no inherent indication, relying on context. The commentator critiques the third and fourth views, arguing they collapse into the first two. He then moves to até as a metaphor for causality (sababiyyah), as in 'I embraced Islam so that I enter Paradise.' He engages with objections from al-Talwīḥ, defending the possibility of a causal reading. Finally, he discusses حتى for absolute order (al-tartīb), where jurists permit it to mean 'after' (like 'Zaid came, even Amr'), despite grammarians' objections. The commentator defends the jurists' practice as legitimate metaphorical extension, emphasizing that linguistic precedence is not required for valid legal usage.

Key Terms
الغاية (al-ghāyah): The limit or endpoint of an action or state, which what follows even if it is not literally the final boundary.
البعضية (al-baʿḍiyyah): The condition that what follows hatta must be a part of what precedes it, not merely included in its ruling.
الجارة (al-jārrah): The prepositional use of hatta, which requires partiality.
العاطفة (al-ʿāṭifah): The conjunctive use of hatta, also requiring partiality.
الابتدائية (al-ibtidāʾiyyah): The inceptive use of hatta, where it introduces a new sentence and requires the predicate to be of the same kind as the preceding.

Hatta for Causality (Sababiyyah) as a Metaphorical Extension
Analyzing hatta for causality as a metaphorical extension

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the metaphorical use of hatta for causality (sababiyyah) and evaluate the objections raised against this extension using Hanafi usul principles.

Core Idea
Hatta can be metaphorically used to indicate causality, where the preceding is a cause for the following, based on a shared sense of completion.

This passage extends the analysis of hatta from literal limits to a metaphorical use for causality (sababiyyah). The author defends the juristic application of hatta in phrases like 'I embraced Islam so that I enter Paradise,' arguing that the cause appears complete through the effect. He engages with objections from al-Talwih and provides a nuanced defense. Understanding this metaphorical extension is crucial for interpreting legal texts where hatta may not indicate a temporal limit but a causal relationship.

Guided Path
1
State the metaphorical claim
How does the author claim hatta can be used for causality?
He says hatta is borrowed for sababiyyah when ghayah is not appropriate, as in 'aslamtu hatta adkhula al-jannah'.
2
Understand the relationship
What is the relationship between ghayah and sababiyyah?
The relationship is that both involve the completion of a ruling; in sababiyyah, the effect completes the cause.
3
Analyze the objection from al-Talwih
What objection does al-Talwih raise?
There is an objection that entry into Paradise is not the limit of Islam, so hatta cannot indicate ghayah here.
4
Evaluate the author's response
How does the author respond to the objection?
He argues that the relationship of metaphor does not require consistency across all instances; some instances of the effect can serve as ghayah.
5
Consider alternative interpretations
What alternative interpretation is suggested by the author of al-Talwih, and why is it rejected?
It suggests hatta indicates intendedness, but this is refuted by the example 'akaltu al-samakata hatta ra'saha' where the head is not intended.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition on the particle حتى (hatta) as used in legal and linguistic contexts, from the commentary on Musallam al-Thubut. It begins by affirming that حتى can indicate a limit (ghāyah) even if based on the speaker's subjective perspective, as in the examples 'people died, even the prophets' and 'the pilgrims arrived, even the pedestrians.' The commentator defends this against claims of artificiality, citing transmission from linguists. It then explains that حتى functions as a preposition (jārrah) and conjunction (ʿāṭifah), both requiring partiality (al-baʿḍiyyah): what follows must be part of what precedes. When used as an inceptive (ibtidāʾiyyah) with a full sentence, the predicate must be of the same kind or a necessary concomitant. The text then presents the debate on whether what follows حتى enters into the ruling (dakhūl) when it is a preposition. Four scholarly positions are given: (1) absolute entry, (2) absolute non-entry, (3) entry only if the following is a suitable part, and (4) no inherent indication, relying on context. The commentator critiques the third and fourth views, arguing they collapse into the first two. He then moves to até as a metaphor for causality (sababiyyah), as in 'I embraced Islam so that I enter Paradise.' He engages with objections from al-Talwīḥ, defending the possibility of a causal reading. Finally, he discusses حتى for absolute order (al-tartīb), where jurists permit it to mean 'after' (like 'Zaid came, even Amr'), despite grammarians' objections. The commentator defends the jurists' practice as legitimate metaphorical extension, emphasizing that linguistic precedence is not required for valid legal usage.

Key Terms
السببية (al-sababiyyah): The metaphorical use of hatta to indicate causality, where the preceding is a cause for the following.
الغاية (al-ghāyah): The limit or endpoint; here contrasted with sababiyyah in terms of metaphorical borrowing.
الاستعارة (al-istiʿārah): Metaphorical usage; the author argues that hatta can be borrowed for causality.

Hatta for Sequential Order (Tartib) in Juristic Usage
Defending hatta for sequential order in juristic interpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to defend the juristic use of hatta to indicate sequential order (tartib) against grammatical objections, using arguments from metaphorical extension and the authority of mujtahids.

Core Idea
Jurists legitimately use hatta to mean 'after' in sequential order, even if grammarians deny it, because metaphorical extension does not require direct linguistic precedent.

This passage addresses the juristic practice of using hatta to indicate simply that something follows another in sequence (tartib), not necessarily ghayah or causality. For example, 'Zaid came, even Amr' is understood by jurists as 'then Amr came.' Grammarians object that no such usage exists in classical Arabic. The author defends the jurists on the grounds that metaphorical usage does not require hearing of specific examples and that the mujtahids are authoritative in linguistic investigation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the juristic claim
What do jurists permit regarding hatta?
They permit it for absolute order (tartib), meaning 'after,' as in 'ja'a Zaidun hatta 'Amrun.'
2
Understand the grammatical objection
Why do grammarians object?
They claim no such usage has been transmitted in Arabic speech.
3
Analyze the author's defense
How does the author defend the juristic usage?
He argues: (1) In metaphor, the intended meaning does not have to be the direct signification; it can be implied. (2) The mujtahids are experts in language, so their usage is authoritative.
4
Address the counterarguments
What about the objection that hearing of particulars is required?
He dismisses it: hearing is not a condition, but the usage must not be clearly prohibited; here, grammarians claim prohibition but their authority is less than that of mujtahids.
5
Conclude the legal implication
What is the practical effect of this defense?
Jurists can legitimately interpret hatta as indicating sequence in legal texts, even if grammarians object.

The Hanafi school relies on the immediate understanding (tabadur) of the term without context as the decisive sign of literalness.

The passage states that when 'amr' is uttered without context, the immediate understanding (tabadur) is the specific command utterance. Hanafis take this as proof that 'amr' is literal for command and not homonymous or univocal.
Homonymy would require both meanings to be equally understood, which is not the case.

This argument assumes 'amr' in the sense of action is a masdar; otherwise the derivation argument may not hold.

The passage addresses objections: e.g., that using a general term for a specific instance is not metaphor. It also discusses differences of opinion on the sign of haqiqah among usulis.
The better view is that if 'amr' were shared, both meanings would be equal in tabadur, but they are not.

The relied-upon position: 'amr' is haqiqah for the specific command utterance. This has practical implications for interpreting imperative forms in legal texts as obligations unless a qarina indicates otherwise.
This aligns with the Hanafi principle that al-amr yufidu al-wujub.

Overall, the chosen opinion is that 'amr' is literal for the specific command utterance.

Key Terms
أمر (amr): The word composed of a, m, r; literal for the specific utterance of command, metaphorical for action.
حقيقة (haqiqah): Literal meaning; the meaning for which the word was originally coined and is understood without context.
مجاز (majaz): Metaphorical meaning; a meaning given to a word due to a relationship to its literal meaning, requiring a qarina.
مشترك (mushtarak): Homonymous; a word that has been placed for two or more distinct meanings independently.
متواطئ (mutawati'): Univocal; a word that applies to multiple instances by a single common meaning.
تبادر (tabadur): Immediate understanding; the meaning that spontaneously comes to mind upon hearing a word without context.
اشتقاق (ishtiqaq): Derivational morphology; the ability to form derived words (like verbs) from a root, used as evidence for literal meaning.
