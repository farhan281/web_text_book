The Simple Cannot Acquire or Be Acquired
States a rule: al-basit is neither kasib nor muktasab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that al-basit is neither kasib nor muktasab and explain the reasoning based on its simplicity.

Core Idea
The simple, having no parts, cannot act or be acted upon in acquisition.

In Hanafi usul, the nature of entities matters for legal attribution. This passage establishes a kalam principle that the simple (al-basit) is excluded from acquisition (kasb) both as agent and object. The reasoning ties directly to the indivisible nature of the simple and the limits of accidental knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject
The rule concerns al-basit (the simple), defined as that which has no parts.
In Hanafi kalam, al-basit is contrasted with the composite (al-murakkab).
2
Extract the two negative propositions
The passage negates two things: al-basit is not a kasib (acquirer) and not muktasab (acquired).
3
Analyze the first reason (not kasib)
The text states 'because it does not accept action' i.e., secondary motion (al-harakah al-thaniyah). Kasb involves movement or change, which the simple cannot undergo.
Secondary motion is the act of acquisition itself.
4
Analyze the second reason (not muktasab)
The simple cannot be acquired in its essence because accidents (arad) do not inform of the essence (al-kunnah), and the simple has no essential property (dhati) that could be acquired.
Only composites can have attributes that are acquired.

The Definer in Hanafi Usul: Conditions, Types, and Objections
Definition of al-mu'arraf and its logical analysis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a valid definition (tard wa 'aks), classify definitions as haqiqi, rasmi, or lafzi, and explain the nature of objections to definitions.

Core Idea
A definition must be coextensive with the defined, and is classified by the type of properties it uses.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi Usuli concept of al-mu'arraf (the definer). You will learn the essential requirement that a definition must perfectly match its defined term (tard wa 'aks), the threefold classification of definitions, and why objections to definitions are handled differently than arguments in other fields.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core metaphor
The definer 'prevents the inside from going out and the outside from coming in' — understand this as a metaphor for precise delineation.
2
Extract the condition of coextension
The definer must be true of everything the defined is true of (tard) and vice versa ('aks). State this in your own words.
This is the central requirement.
3
Understand the nature of definitions
Definitions are pure conceptualizations (taswir), not proofs. They involve implicit claims, so objections are possible but limited.
Three types of objections: mu'arada, naqd, man'.
4
Classify definitions by their content
Identify the three types: haqiqi (essential properties), rasmi (external concomitants), lafzi (synonymous word).
Note the difference from the logic tradition.
5
Examine the debate on 'al-dhati'
Consider the critique of 'al-dhati' as 'what is not caused' and the counterexample of 'imkan' (possibility).
This illustrates that even essential definitions can be contested.

Disbelievers and Obligation of Furu': Hanafi Positions and Critique
Usul dispute on disbelievers and furu'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three main Hanafi positions on whether disbelievers are obligated with the furu' of Shari'ah, and evaluate the Hanafi critique of the Shafi'i formulation of the dispute.

Core Idea
A disbeliever's obligation in branches is disputed: Iraqi Hanafis agree with Shafi'is; Bukhari/Samarqandi Hanafis restrict to belief only.

This passage addresses a classic usul al-fiqh dispute: are disbelievers accountable for the practical rulings (furu') of Islam in addition to belief? The text presents multiple positions within the Hanafi school and critiques a common formulation of the disagreement. Understanding this nuanced discussion sharpens your ability to distinguish between worldly legal consequences and religious accountability, a key skill in usul analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three positions on obligation
Read the opening lines to find the three views: (1) obligated with all furu' (Shafi'is and Iraqi Hanafis), (2) not obligated with furu' (Bukhari/Samarqandi Hanafis), (3) obligated only with prohibitions. List them clearly.
The position 'only prohibitions' is a minority view.
2
Distinguish worldly from religious obligation
Note the role of the dhimma contract: it enforces worldly consequences (penalties, transactions) but does not necessarily create religious accountability (i.e., punishment in the afterlife for neglecting acts of worship). This distinction is key to the Hanafi argument.
The dhimma contract does not require iman for avoidances (tark) but does for acts of worship.
3
Understand the Shafi'i formulation and its critique
The Shafi'is formulate the dispute as: when conditions of obligation are complete but a legal condition (e.g., wudu for prayer) is absent, is obligation valid? The Hanafi commentator rejects this framing as it would imply absurdities like the impure not being obligated to pray. Recognize why this formulation is considered misdirected.
The commentator calls this attribution 'appalling' and criticizes al-Badi' for following it.
4
Extract the real point of dispute
According to the commentator, the true disagreement is whether the furu' are obligatory in terms of performance (ada') just like belief, or only obligatory in terms of belief (i'tiqad). The Iraqi Hanafis and Shafi'is hold the former; the Bukhari/Samarqandi Hanafis hold the latter.
This reframing avoids the problematic implications about legal conditions.

When a Common Word Becomes Ambiguous: The Hanafi Rule on Mujmal
Classification of common word scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a common word (mushtarak) as mujmal or non-mujmal according to the Hanafi school, based on the presence and type of qarinah, and identify the points of consensus and ikhtilaf with the Shafi'is.

Core Idea
A common word without qarinah is mujmal; with qarinah, its status depends on the type of qarinah.

This lesson deals with a fundamental usuli issue: when does a word that can carry multiple meanings become legally ambiguous (mujmal)? The Hanafi school holds that a common word stripped of any contextual clue (qarinah) is mujmal because its intended meaning cannot be determined. However, if a qarinah is present, it may clarify the meaning or leave ambiguity depending on its nature. We will explore the different scenarios outlined in the passage and compare the Hanafi view with that of Imam al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
Identify the common word and check for qarinah
First, determine whether the word in question is a common word (mushtarak) that can convey multiple meanings. Then check if there is any contextual clue (qarinah) that could indicate which meaning is intended.
Qarinah can be linguistic or situational.
2
Apply the rule if no qarinah exists
If there is absolutely no qarinah, the Hanafi ruling is that the word is mujmal (ambiguous). Note the Shafi'i exception: they interpret it as carrying all possible meanings, so it is not mujmal.
This is a key intra-madhhab difference.
3
Classify the type of qarinah
If a qarinah exists, classify it into one of the categories mentioned: specifying a single meaning (mu'ayyan), specifying a non-specific single meaning (ghayr mu'ayyan), indicating the majority of meanings (akthar), nullifying some (ilgha' li al-ba'd), or nullifying all (ilgha' li al-kull).
Each type leads to a different ruling.
4
Determine the ruling based on qarinah type
Apply the corresponding rule: For a qarinah specifying a specific single meaning, the word is interpreted accordingly by consensus. For a non-specific single meaning, it is mujmal by consensus. For majority, it is interpreted as the majority for those who permit generality of a common word (Hanafis allow it), otherwise it remains mujmal. For nullification of some, the remaining specific single meaning is taken; if the remaining is not specific, it is mujmal except for those who allow combining. For nullification of all, the word is understood as a metaphor (majaz) by consensus.
Some of these are points of ikhtilaf between Hanafis and Shafi'is.
5
Consolidate the classification
Summarize the final status of the word: either it is clarified (mubayyan) or remains mujmal. Also note if there is a consensus or a known difference.
This step trains the student to produce a complete analysis.

Imam al-Razi's definition of the scope of majaz is foundational for understanding how metaphorical language is treated in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The Real and Metaphorical Uses of 'في'
Defines the real and metaphorical uses of 'في' and applies them to iqrar.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the real (haqiqi) and metaphorical (majazi) uses of the preposition 'في' and apply this distinction to iqrar cases.

Core Idea
'في' is real for containment; when containment is impossible, it is metaphorical and requires interpretation.

In Hanafi usul, prepositions carry legal weight. The preposition 'في' primarily indicates containment (zarfiyya), but when the container cannot actually contain the object, the usage becomes metaphorical. This distinction affects rulings in iqrar, such as when someone says 'I robbed a cloth in a handkerchief'—the handkerchief is a subordinate and is considered robbed as well.

Guided Path
1
Identify the real use of 'في'
Recognize that 'في' literally indicates temporal or spatial containment. The container must be capable of containing the object for the usage to be real.
e.g., 'the water in the cup' is real; 'the house in his hand' is metaphorical.
2
Apply to the iqrar 'I robbed a cloth in a handkerchief'
Since a handkerchief can contain a cloth, the usage is real. Therefore, robbing the cloth in the handkerchief implies robbing the handkerchief itself, as it is the container.
Unlike 'I robbed a horse in a stable' where stable is not subject to robbery.
3
Distinguish from extraction with 'min'
If the phrase were 'I robbed a cloth from a handkerchief', the immediate understanding is extraction, not containment, so only the cloth is robbed.
The preposition changes the ruling.
4
State the Hanafi ruling on the iqrar
The better view is that the handkerchief is a subordinate; the immediate understanding is that robbing the contained item also encompasses the container.
This applies unless the container cannot be robbed (e.g., real estate).

The Iqrar 'Ashara fi Ashara': Multiplication or Mere Repetition
Analyzes the iqrar 'ashara fi ashara' and the dispute over whether it means ten or one hundred.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on the iqrar 'ashara fi ashara' and evaluate the dispute regarding the interpretation of multiplication.

Core Idea
'Ashara fi ashara' in iqrar defaults to meaning ten, not one hundred, due to the impossibility of containment.

When someone acknowledges 'I owe ten in ten', classical Hanafi scholars debated whether this means ten (repetition) or one hundred (multiplication). The default Hanafi ruling is that it means ten, because a number cannot contain another number. However, if the custom of arithmetic is intended, some scholars argue for one hundred. This lesson explores the ikhtilaf and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the nature of the iqrar
The phrase 'alayya 'asharatun fi 'asharatun' is an iqrar of a debt. The challenge is that 'في' implies containment, but a number cannot contain another number, so the usage is metaphorical.
The Hanafi view: the metaphor here is seen as repetition or explanation, not multiplication.
2
Apply the default Hanafi ruling
Since containment is impossible, the iqrar is understood as a repetition: he owes ten (not one hundred). This is the position of Imam Muhammad and the majority.
Exception: a narration from al-Hasan ibn Ziyad and Zufar holds that it means one hundred, interpreting 'في' as multiplication.
3
Analyze the argument for multiplication
If the acknowledger intends the convention of calculation (urf al-hisab), then 'ashara fi ashara' customarily means ten times ten = one hundred. Supporters of this view argue that the iqrar should be interpreted according to the speaker's intention and the common understanding.
Al-Hasan and Zufar adopt this view.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The mainstream Hanafi response is that multiplication does not increase the original number but only multiplies its parts. Thus, even if the custom is intended, the iqrar is still for ten because the debt itself remains ten, just divided into parts. Fath al-Qadir criticizes this, arguing that if the custom is intended, the understanding is one hundred, so the iqrar should be for one hundred.
The text presents both sides but leans toward the default ruling of ten.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The relied-upon position is that the iqrar is for ten, unless the acknowledger explicitly intends multiplication and the context supports it. But judicially, the default is ten.
This illustrates the principle that uncertainty in iqrar favors the debtor.

Temporal Comprehensiveness: 'في' in Talaq and Fasting
Explains the effect of 'في' on temporal comprehensiveness and applies it to talaq and fasting, including a branch on divine attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference in temporal comprehensiveness when 'في' is used or omitted in talaq and fasting, and distinguish between judicial and religious rulings.

Core Idea
Omitting 'في' implies full time coverage; using 'في' allows partial coverage and acceptance of restrictive intention.

The presence or absence of 'في' crucially affects whether an action is understood to cover the entire time period. In talaq, saying 'anti taliq ghadan' (without 'في') means the divorce occurs at the first moment of tomorrow, covering the whole day, while 'anti taliq fi ghadin' allows the husband to intend the end of the day. Similarly, 'sawtu sanah' means fasting the whole year, but 'sawtu fi sanah' means fasting some days in the year. This lesson explores the Hanafi distinction and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Understand the general rule about temporal 'في'
When 'في' is omitted, the verb is directly attached to the time, implying completeness. When 'في' is used, the action only needs to occur somewhere within the time frame.
Example: 'sawtu sanah' (I fasted a year) means every day; 'sawtu fi sanah' means I fasted at some point in the year.
2
Apply to talaq: 'anti taliq ghadan' vs 'anti taliq fi ghadin'
Without 'في', the divorce occurs at the first moment of 'ghad' (tomorrow), covering the entire day. The husband cannot judicially intend the end of the day. With 'في', the husband can intend the end of the day, and the divorce occurs then.
Religiously (diyanatan), intention is accepted in both cases.
3
Analyze the ikhtilaf with the Sahibayn
Abu Hanifa holds that without 'في', the divorce occurs at the first moment. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, accepting the husband's intention even without 'في'. The text sides with Abu Hanifa.
The ikhtilaf is based on whether the literal meaning or the intention is given priority.
4
Examine the branch on talaq suspended on divine attributes
Saying 'anti taliq fi mashiyyat Allah' suspends the divorce on God's will, which is unknown, so it does not occur. 'Fi 'ilm Allah' means the divorce is attached to God's knowledge of its existence, so it occurs. 'Fi qudrat Allah' is problematic; some say it is like 'fi taqdir Allah' (God's decree) and does not occur, but a rebuttal argues that God's power is always present, so it should occur.
The author settles the discussion without a definitive ruling.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a complete definition is not synonymous with the defined term, and identify the conditions for synonymy (taraduf).

Core Idea
The defined term is simple; the definition is composite—no complete identity, hence no synonymy.

In usul al-fiqh, precision of terms is critical. One common confusion is whether the definition (al-ḥadd al-tāmm) is just a clearer synonym of the defined term (al-maḥdūd). Commentary

Definition of Shart (Condition) in Usul al-Fiqh
Defines shart according to Ghazali and responds to objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of shart, distinguish it from sabab, and identify the key objections and responses to the definition.

Core Idea
A condition is a prerequisite without exigency: without it the thing fails, but its presence does not force existence.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational definition of 'shart' (condition) as presented by Imam al-Ghazali and defended within the Hanafi usul tradition. This definition is crucial for distinguishing conditions from causes (asbab) and for correctly applying specifications (takhsis) in legal reasoning. We will walk through the definition, the objections raised against it, and the Hanafi responses that refine our understanding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of shart
Locate the exact wording of the definition given by al-Ghazali. Note the two parts: what is required for existence and what is not necessitated.
The definition is: 'ما لا يوجد المشروط دونه ولا يلزم أن يوجد المشروط عنده'.
2
Understand the first objection (circularity)
The objection is that 'mashrut' (conditioned) is defined in terms of shart, leading to circularity. The response clarifies that 'mashrut' means the thing itself, not its knowledge.
This resolves the circularity because the definition is about existence, not epistemology.
3
Analyze the second objection (part of cause)
The objection is that a part of a cause (juz' al-sabab) also fits the definition: the caused does not exist without that part. The response distinguishes by type of relation: the definition considers the type of connection, not a specific instance.
A condition is related by a specific type of tawqif (dependency), whereas a part of a cause is related by causal exigency.
4
Distinguish shart from sabab (cause)
The passage emphasizes that a cause (sabab) necessitates existence via exigency (iqtida), while a condition (shart) is a prerequisite without such exigency. Example: time is a condition for the validity of prayer but a cause for its obligation.
This distinction is a hallmark of Hanafi usul and prevents confusion between the two categories.
5
Apply the definition to a fiqh example
Consider the condition of qabd (possession) in hibah (gift). Without qabd, ownership does not transfer; but the presence of qabd does not necessitate ownership (it could be invalid for other reasons). This illustrates the definition.
This example shows how the definition works in practice across different chapters of fiqh.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and defense of the concept of 'shart' (condition) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, attributed to Imam al-Ghazali. The definition is: 'The condition is that without which the conditioned does not exist, but the conditioned's existence is not necessitated by it.' Two main objections are addressed: (1) circularity—since the condition is defined by the conditioned and vice versa. This is resolved by clarifying that 'conditioned' here means the thing itself, not the knowledge of it. (2) That part of a cause (juz' al-sabab) also fits the definition, since the caused does not exist without it. The response is that the definition considers the type of relationship: a condition is related to the thing in a particular way (by type), so that a part of a cause that is specific to one cause does not qualify because the caused might exist via another cause. The discussion further distinguishes between cause and condition: a cause necessitates existence via exigency, while a condition is a prerequisite without exigency. Examples like time being a condition for Friday prayer are given to illustrate that the same entity can be both a cause (for obligation) and a condition (for validity). The passage concludes by noting that a condition may be specific to a particular cause, like possession (qabd) being a condition for ownership in the contract of hibah.

Names of Meanings: Singular vs. Multiple Instances
Critique of Ibn al-Humam's linguistic argument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate Ibn al-Humam's claim about names of meanings applying to multiple instances and apply the Hanafi linguistic principle that the singular form of such words denotes a single occurrence.

Core Idea
Not all names of meanings apply to multiple instances; singular forms denote a single act unless custom or law dictates otherwise.

In Hanafi usul, distinguishing between names of meanings and names of objects is crucial for deriving rulings. Ibn al-Humam argued that names of meanings like 'talaq' can apply to multiple instances in the singular form, but this passage refutes that claim. We will dissect the argument, examine counterexamples, and understand the linguistic rule that governs such words.

Guided Path
1
Identify Ibn al-Humam's Claim
State Ibn al-Humam's argument: names of meanings (e.g., 'talaq') can be applied to multiple instances in the singular form, similar to some names of objects like 'standing' (qiyam) which can refer to continuous standing.
He used this to argue that intending two divorces with the word 'talaq' should be valid.
2
Examine the Author's Initial Rejection
The author first points out that the claim is not generally correct, citing 'sawm' (fasting) as a name of meaning that is not applied to a whole month in the singular form.
This challenges the universality of Ibn al-Humam's assertion.
3
Analyze the Counterexamples
Analyze each example: (a) 'sawm' is not called 'sawm' for a month; (b) 'talaq' is not applied to two divorces in law and custom; (c) 'qiyam' only applies to continuous standing, not interrupted action.
The interruption or distinct units prevent the singular form from covering multiplicity.
4
Understand the Arabic Grammatical Principle
The author invokes the consensus of Arabic grammarians on the maf'ul mutlaq (absolute object), which changes form (singular, dual, plural) to indicate number.
This firmly establishes that the singular form does not denote multiplicity.
5
Conclude the Correct Ruling
Therefore, Ibn al-Humam's argument is unsound. The Hanafi position that the word 'talaq' in singular form refers to one divorce only is upheld.
This impacts the validity of divorce pronouncements.

Commentary

For example, the word 'sawm' (fasting) is not used for a month of fasting in the same singular form; rather, one would say 'a month of fasting.' Similarly, 'talaq' is not applied to two divorces in legal or customary usage. The Object of Command: Absolute Essence or Particular Individual?
Debate on whether the commanded act is a particular individual or the absolute nature.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that the commanded act is the absolute essence (mahiyya la bi-shart shay') and evaluate the opposing view of Ibn al-Hajib.

Core Idea
The commanded act is the absolute essence, not an unknown particular — command is valid only when the demanded thing is known.

Commands require a known object. How can a command like 'Strike!' be understood if the specific act is unknown before performance? The Hanafi usulis resolve this by arguing that the object of command is the absolute essence (mahiyya) of the act, not a particular instance. This lesson unpacks the debate between Ibn al-Hajib's 'real particular' view and the Hanafi 'absolute essence' view, drawing on the three considerations of essence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State the difference: Ibn al-Hajib holds the commanded act is a real particular (juz'i haqiqi); the Hanafis hold it is the absolute essence (mahiyya mutlaqa) considered as not conditioned by anything (la bi-shart shay').
The passage presents both views.
2
Understand the Hanafi argument from ambiguity
If the command targeted a particular unknown before existence, the command would be ambiguous (mujmal). The Hanafis argue this is invalid.
The passage states: 'if the real particular were demanded, then 'strike!' would be ambiguous.'
3
Examine Ibn al-Hajib's response and the Hanafi counter
Ibn al-Hajib's defender might say 'whichever particular' is known. The Hanafis counter that this reduces to the absolute essence, not a particular.
The passage discusses 'the demanded is the specified—whichever specified'.
4
Grasp the three considerations of mahiyya
Mahiyya can be considered: (1) la bi-shart shay' (absolute, not conditioned), (2) bi-shart la shay' (conditioned by no accident, non-existent), (3) bi-shart shay' (conditioned by accidents, i.e., individuals). The Hanafi view uses the first.
This distinction is crucial to the Hanafi defense.
5
See how the Hanafi view resolves the impossibility
The absolute essence exists by the existence of its individuals. Demanding it is not demanding the impossible. The commanded act is the essence realized in any individual.
The passage concludes that the demanded individual exists necessarily, even if the 'title' (unwan) is accidental.

Types of Textual Indications: 'Ibarah and Ishara
Defining and exemplifying 'ibarah and ishara

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'ibarah and ishara, apply their definitions to Quranic verses, and identify the intra-Hanafi disagreement on the scope of 'ibarah.

Core Idea
Explicit meaning includes primary and secondary intentions; implicit allusion conveys an unintended yet necessary concomitant.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, understanding how a text indicates its ruling is fundamental. This passage from 'Fawatih al-Rahamut' classifies textual indications into four types, focusing on the first two: 'ibarah (explicit indication) and ishara (allusion). Mastering this classification allows you to determine whether a meaning is directly authoritative or requires further inference. The passage also presents an intra-Hanafi debate on whether 'ibarah includes secondary intentions, and a critical analysis of a claim that some indications are iqtida' rather than ishara.

Identify the core criterion: 'ibarah is the meaning directly conveyed by the wording, whether intended primarily or secondarily. Ishara is a concomitant meaning not intended at all (neither primarily nor secondarily) and not required for the correctness of speech.
The passage contrasts ishara with iqtida', which is a requirement for speech correction.

This shows 'ibarah includes both primary and secondary intentions.

Section. The indication of the word according to us is four, and as for others, they add to it. Among them is `ibarah (explicit indication), which is what is established, i.e., indication established and realized by the wording (bi al-nazm), such that it indicates by itself without the mediation of a conceptual meaning (as in dalala), and without the correction of speech (as in iqtida'), even if it is concomitant (iltizami), intended by it, even if the intention is secondary (taba'an), as a precaution against ishara. Like His statement: 'Allah has permitted trade and prohibited usury' (2:275) – so permissibility, prohibition, and the necessary distinction between them are all by `ibarah, because the first two are intended secondarily, and the distinction is intended primarily because the verse is a rebuttal of their equating them. So in `ibarah, the conveyance of the understood meaning in the sentence is considered, whether primarily or secondarily, as affirmed by the author of al-Kashf and transmitted from Imam Sadr al-Islam, and attributed to Imam Shams al-Aimmah. There is a disagreement with Sadr al-Shari'ah who conditioned primary conveyance such that he ruled that the indication of the permissibility of sale and prohibition of usury is ishara, and it was responded that changing the terminology without benefit is akin to error among scholars. And among them is ishara, which is a concomitant indication not intended at all, neither primarily nor secondarily, and it requires an additional restriction: that it not be for correction of speech, to exclude iqtida'. Minds differ in understanding it because it is based on a relation of concomitance. It may be clear, so its indication is clear, and it may be hidden, so its indication is hidden, and it may be theoretical due to the hiddenness of concomitance. Like His statement: 'And upon the father (al-mawlud lahu) is their provision and clothing in the customary manner' (2:233) – it is for obligating maintenance on fathers, but He expressed them as 'mawlud lahu' (the one for whom the child is born) and attributed the child to them with the letter lam, so in it is an ishara to the child's exclusive lineage to the father, since possession is definitely not intended, so he is solely responsible for his maintenance and nothing is due from the mother. And this child follows (yastatbi'u) eligibility for the greater imamate, which is general authority, so he deserves it if the father is Qurayshi, and compatibility (kafa'a), so he becomes compatible for one whose father is compatible for him, except freedom and slavery, for he is not free or enslaved pending the freedom or slavery of the father, by a specific evidence for them, and other related rulings like intellect, etc. Then there is consideration whether the indication of the child's exclusive relation to the father is ishara, for the lam is placed for specification, and here specific specification is intended, so the meaning of 'mawlud lahu' is the one to whom the child is attributed, and this meaning is intended even if the intention is to obligate maintenance on him, so the indication is `ibarah not ishara. Indeed, the indication of the aforementioned rulings following from the establishment of lineage is definitely ishara. So understand. And like His statement: 'For the poor emigrants who were expelled from their homes and their properties' (59:8) – although it was conveyed to obligate a share of the spoils for them, it indicates the removal of ownership from what they left behind, because the poor person is one who owns no property, so in expressing them as poor there is an ishara to the removal of ownership; otherwise they would be rich. It cannot be said that the word 'poor' is a metaphor for the attribution of wealth to them, so they would be owners of wealth, not poor. But it is a metaphor for one whose hope of benefiting from wealth has been cut off, because the attribution indicating ownership at the time of expulsion from homes and properties does not contradict poverty now, so the attribution cannot serve as a context for the metaphor, so 'poor' is left in its literal sense. And he said in al-Tahrir: the correct view is that it (removal of ownership) – rather the indication of it – is iqtida', because the validity of calling someone poor after having ownership of wealth depends on removal, so removal is a necessary antecedent and the indication of it is iqtida'. I say: the use of 'poor' although dependent on removal of ownership, does not depend on removal by seizure (istila'), so the fact that seizure removes the ownership of the believers and establishes ownership for them (i.e., the conquering disbelievers) is established by ishara, as indicated by His statement: 'expelled from their homes and their properties', because attachment to a derived term necessitates its origin, so expulsion is the cause of poverty. So reflect. But this is insufficient, for the fact that seizure is remover and establisher is a ruling, and the removal of ownership itself is another ruling, and the author of al-Tahrir only ruled on the second as iqtida', not the first. So reflect. The better answer is what the master of divine secrets (may his secret be sanctified) said: that the dependence of a term on something does not make it iqtida', otherwise all concomitants would be iqtida' because the term depends on them. Rather, iqtida' is an indication of something on which the validity of the understood meaning depends, and this is not the case here, for removal of ownership and poverty are meanings without dependence of one on the other, so it is only ishara. After this, there remains speech: the word 'poor' indicates by conformity (mutabaqa) the one who owns nothing, so the fact that the emigrants (may Allah be pleased with them) are not owners of what they left behind is intended in general, and if not primarily intended, it is `ibarah. Indeed, the indication that seizure is remover and establisher is unintended, so it is ishara. So understand. And like His statement: 'Permitted to you on the night of fasting is intimacy with your wives; they are garments for you and you are garments...'

Commentary
This passage from the section on linguistic principles introduces the Hanafi classification of textual indications, which are four: `ibarah (explicit indication), ishara (allusion), dalala (indication through meaning), and iqtida' (requirement). The focus here is on the first two. `Ibarah is the primary indication directly conveyed by the wording, even if the intended meaning is secondary. For example, in the verse 'Allah has permitted trade and prohibited usury,' the permissibility and prohibition are explicitly stated, and the distinction between them is also directly conveyed because the verse rebuts those who equated them. `Ibarah includes meanings that are primarily or secondarily intended. Ishara is an implied meaning that is not primarily or secondarily intended but arises from the relation of concomitance (luzum). It may be clear or hidden. For instance, in the verse 'And upon the father (mawlud lahu) is their provision and clothing,' the word 'mawlud lahu' (the one for whom the child is born) alludes to the child's exclusive lineage to the father, though the primary purpose is to obligate maintenance. The passage also discusses the example of the poor emigrants, where the term 'poor' alludes to the removal of their ownership, since poverty implies lack of property. A debate arises as to whether this indication is ishara or iqtida'; the author sides with ishara. The text then critiques the view of al-Tahrir that it is iqtida', and presents a counter-argument that the dependence of the term 'poor' on removal of ownership does not make it iqtida', as iqtida' requires that the validity of the understood meaning depends on the implied element, which is not the case here. Finally, the verse about intimacy during fasting is given as another example of ishara. The discussion shows the precision of Hanafi usul in distinguishing direct from indirect meanings.

Key Terms
العبارة (al-ibarah): Explicit indication: the meaning conveyed directly by the wording, whether intended primarily or secondarily.
الإشارة (al-ishara): Allusion: an implied meaning not intended primarily or secondarily, arising from a relation of concomitance, and not needed for the correctness of speech.
الاقتضاء (al-iqtida'): Requirement: an indication that depends on an omitted word for the correctness of the speech.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi classification of 'ibarah and ishara hinges on whether a meaning is intended (even secondarily) or completely unintended; ishara conveys necessary concomitants that are not part of the speaker's aim.
