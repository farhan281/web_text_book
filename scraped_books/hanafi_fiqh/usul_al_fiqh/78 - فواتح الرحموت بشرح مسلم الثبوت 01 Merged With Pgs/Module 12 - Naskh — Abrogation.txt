Essential vs Accidental Good and Evil: The Hanafi Defense of Abrogation
Defending the possibility of naskh by distinguishing essential and accidental moral qualities

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi distinction between essential and accidental moral qualities and apply it to defend the possibility of naskh (abrogation).

Core Idea
Good and evil can be essential yet overridden by accident — enabling naskh without contradiction.

The Mu'tazila argued that if good and evil are essential to acts, then abrogation would require an act to change its moral nature, which is impossible. The Hanafi response distinguishes essential qualities that can be overridden by accidental factors. This lesson unpacks that distinction and shows how it preserves the consistency of divine commands across changing laws.

Guided Path
1
State the Mu'tazila objection
Identify the Mu'tazila claim: good and evil are intrinsic to acts, so an act cannot change from good to evil; thus naskh is impossible.
This objection is based on their rationalist epistemology.
2
Distinguish two meanings of 'essential'
The Hanafi author introduces two meanings: (1) what a thing would entail by its nature unless prevented (e.g., coldness of water), which can be overridden by accident; (2) what a thing entails necessarily (e.g., evenness for four), which cannot change. The Mu'tazila meant the first meaning.
The first meaning allows for accidental change without contradiction.
3
Apply the distinction to examples
Lying is essentially evil but accidentally good when it saves a prophet's life. Marriage to a sister was essentially evil but accidentally good for preserving lineage, so it was permitted temporarily. When the accident ceased, the original evil remained.
These examples show how an act can have both essential and accidental moral qualities.
4
Conclude the defense of naskh
Since accidental good can override essential evil, a ruling can change without contradicting the essential nature of the act. Thus, naskh is possible, as seen in the change of qibla and the permissibility of marriage to a sister in earlier laws.
The Hanafi position avoids the Mu'tazila problem of requiring an act to change its essential moral nature.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary defending the possibility of abrogation (naskh) against Mu'tazila objections. The author argues that good and evil can be both essential (dhati) and accidental ('aradi), allowing a thing to be good in one respect and evil in another. He uses the example of lying to save a prophet's life: lying is essentially evil but accidentally good because it entails a greater good. Similarly, the prohibition of marriage to a sister was not essential but accidental due to the need for procreation; once that need ceased, the original evil remained, so it was forbidden. The author distinguishes two meanings of 'essential': (1) what a thing would entail by its nature unless prevented, like coldness in water, which can change by accident; and (2) what a thing entails necessarily, like evenness for four, which cannot change. The Mu'tazila meant the first meaning, so they can accept that an accidental good can override essential evil. This allows naskh without contradiction. The author then addresses the case of facing Jerusalem vs. the Kaaba, arguing that facing Jerusalem was good accidentally due to divine command, and when that command was abrogated, it became not good, while facing the Kaaba became good by a new command. The refutation of the Mu'tazila's objection that essential goodness/evil cannot change is thus complete.

Key Terms
الحسن والقبح (al-husn wa al-qubh): Good and evil as moral qualities; can be essential (dhati) or accidental ('aradi).
الذاتي (al-dhati): Essential attribute that is inherently part of the thing's nature, though it may be overridden by accident.
العرضي (al-'aradi): Accidental attribute that occurs due to external factors, not inherent to the thing's essence.

Resolving Objections on the Condition of Fasting in a Vow of Itikaf
Resolving objections about fasting condition in itikaf vow

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a vow obligates both the act and its condition, and explain why the intended fasting becomes qada despite existing obligations.

Core Idea
A vow of itikaf obligates both seclusion and fasting; the Ramadan fast prevents simultaneous obligation, so the intended fast becomes qada.

In this passage, the author addresses three objections to a previous ruling that a vow of itikaf in Ramadan requires an additional fast (qada). The objections challenge the necessity of the intended fasting and the validity of the vow. The resolution reveals a key Hanafi usul principle: the vow obligates the conditioned act and its condition concurrently, but when the condition is already fulfilled by an existing obligation (the Ramadan fast), the additional obligation is deferred.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three objections
Extract the three objections (iradaat) from the text: first, that the hadith only indicates absolute fasting, not intended; second, that including intended fasting renders the vow invalid; third, that qada cannot be in another Ramadan.
Objections are clearly marked as 'أول' (first), 'ثاني' (second), 'ثالث' (third) in the Arabic.
2
Understand the author's response to each objection
Read the author's answer: he argues that the vow obligates both itikaf and its condition (absolute fasting). The intended fasting becomes necessary as a consequence, but its effect is blocked during Ramadan because the month's fast is already obligatory. Thus, qada is required when the preventer is removed.
Key distinction: condition is absolute fasting, not intended; intended fasting arises from the vow's necessity.
3
Recognize the role of the preventer (mani')
The existing Ramadan fast acts as a preventer that stops the obligation of the intended fasting from taking effect during the month. It does not invalidate the vow; it merely delays the obligation.
This is similar to how an existing obligation can prevent a new one from becoming due at the same time.
4
Apply the Hanafi principle of vow obligation
The Hanafi rule: a vow (nazr) for a conditioned act (mashrut) obligates both the act and its condition (shart). Here, itikaf is the conditioned act, fasting is the condition. The vow creates an obligation for both, but if the condition is already met by an existing duty, the intended obligation is deferred.
This principle is derived from the legal maxim: 'النذر بالمشروط نذر بالشرط' (the vow by the conditioned is a vow by the condition).

Commentary
This passage is a detailed resolution of objections regarding a previous ruling about a vow (nazr) of itikaf with fasting. The author, after summarizing the ruling that the vow obligates both itikaf and its condition (fasting), presents three objections. Abrogation of Obligation and the Remaining Permission: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Explaining types of abrogation of obligation and whether permission remains

Core Idea
When an obligation is abrogated without specifying, the inherent permission vanishes; a new evidence is needed for permissibility.

When a legal obligation (wujub) is lifted by abrogation (naskh), does the act become permissible (jawaz) by default? The answer depends on the type of abrogation. In this passage, the author presents three types and dives into the third—where no permission or prohibition is indicated. The Hanafi school holds that permission does not automatically remain, while the Shafi'i school argues it does. Understanding this subtle distinction is key to mastering Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

The Shafi'is argue that obligation includes permission, so abrogation only removes hardship, leaving permission. The Hanafi rebuttal distinguishes between jawaz muqarin lil-haraj and absolute jawaz. Without a new evidence, no ruling remains.

Abrogation of Obligation: Three Types and the Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Classifying naskh and debating jawaaz after naskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the three types of naskh al-wujub and explain the Hanafi position that permissibility does not remain after the third type, distinguishing it from the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
When obligation is abrogated without new evidence, Hanafis say the act is suspended; Shafi'is say permissibility remains.

This passage from 'Fawatih al-Rahamut' establishes a core principle in Hanafi usul: abrogation (naskh) of obligation can occur in three ways. The third type—abrogation without declaring permissibility or prohibition—triggers a famous dispute with the Shafi'is. By understanding this debate, you will see how the Hanafi school defends the concept of suspension (tawaqquf) as a valid interim legal status.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of naskh al-wujub
List the three categories as given in the first part of the passage. Note the examples: Ashura fast (text indicating permissibility), facing Jerusalem (prohibition), and an unnamed third type (no statement).
The third type is the key: obligation is removed but neither permission nor prohibition is declared.
2
Locate the dispute on the third type
The passage states: the Hanafis say permissibility (jawaz) does not remain; the Shafi'is say it does. The author sides with the Shafi'is. Al-Ghazali agreed with the Hanafis.
This is a structural ikhtilaf about the effect of naskh on the original hukm.
3
Examine the Shafi'i proof
The Shafi'i argument: obligation entails permissibility (al-wujub yatadammanu al-jawaz). Since the abrogating text does not prohibit, the permissibility remains by the original command.
This relies on the claim that jawaz is a necessary concomitant of wujub.

The Hanafi response: the entailed permissibility is specific—permissibility with hardship (haraj) in abandonment. When the obligation is removed, the hardship is lifted, but the permissibility itself is also gone because it was linked to the command. A new evidence is required to establish any permissibility.
The Hanafi position is that without evidence, the act is suspended (tawaqquf).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the abrogation of obligation (naskh al-wujub). It classifies three types of abrogation: (1) abrogation by a text indicating permission (ibaha) and permissibility (jawaz), e.g., abrogation of fasting Ashura; (2) abrogation by a text prohibiting the act, e.g., abrogation of facing Jerusalem; and (3) abrogation without any statement of permissibility or prohibition. The discussion centers on the third type: does the original permissibility (jawaz) remain after the obligation is removed? According to the Hanafi school, it does not remain; the act becomes suspended (tawaqquf) until a new evidence establishes either permissibility or prohibition. The Shafi'is, however, argue that permissibility remains because obligation entails permissibility (al-wujub yatadammanu al-jawaz). The author, al-Sayyid al-Sharif al-Jurjani (the commentator on Muslim al-Thubut), sides with the Shafi'is, contrary to al-Ghazali who agreed with the Hanafis. The text then presents the Hanafi rebuttal: the permissibility entailed by obligation is a specific kind—permissibility with hardship in abandonment (jawaz ma'a al-haraj fi al-tark). When obligation is abrogated, this hardship is removed, but the permissibility itself is not automatically separated; a new evidence is needed. The analogy of a growing body and an inanimate object illustrates that when the differentia (growth) is removed, the genus (body) remains but with a new differentia. However, obligation is a mental composite, not an external one, so the analogy is flawed. To clarify, the text then defines the various meanings of ja'iz (permissible): (1) what the Lawgiver has judged as not prevented—includes mubah, wajib, mandub; (2) what the law has not judged as prevented—this is the Hanafi position of suspension; (3) what is not prevented by reason; (4) what is equal in both respects by law or reason; (5) what is doubtful. The Shafi'is claim that after abrogation, the first meaning (non-prevention by law) remains, but the Hanafis reject this because the original command's evidence is gone. The Hanafi view is that until a new evidence comes, the act is in a state of suspension (tawaqquf), neither clearly permissible nor forbidden.

الوقف (al-tawaqquf): Suspension of judgment on an act's legal status until evidence appears.

The Multiple Meanings of Ja'iz and Their Role in Naskh
Semantic analysis of ja'iz to resolve the naskh dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the five meanings of ja'iz (permissible) in Hanafi usul and identify which meaning underlies the Hanafi position of tawaqquf after naskh.

Core Idea
Jawaz has five meanings; after naskh, only the meaning 'not prevented by law' remains as suspension, not 'judged as permissible'.

The previous lesson established the dispute over whether permissibility remains after naskh. This lesson dives into the linguistic and conceptual complexity of the term 'jawaz'. The passage clarifies that jawaz is not a single concept but a term with multiple meanings. Understanding these meanings is essential to grasp why the Hanafis reject the Shafi'i argument and why they insist on suspension until evidence appears.

Guided Path
1
Recall the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on the third type of naskh
The Hanafis say permissibility does not remain; Shafi'is say it does. The disagreement partly stems from different understandings of what 'jawaz' means.
This lesson focuses on the semantic clarification.
2
Identify the five meanings of ja'iz listed in the passage
Find the five meanings: (1) what the Lawgiver has judged as not prevented (includes mubah, wajib, mandub); (2) what the law has not judged regarding prevention (suspension); (3) what is not prevented by reason; (4) what is equal in both respects (by law or reason); (5) what is doubtful.
The passage explicitly lists these. Note that meaning (2) is the Hanafi position of tawaqquf.
3
Analyze the genus-differentia argument and its critique
The passage presents an argument: jawaz is the genus of wujub, and when the differentia (absence of hardship) is removed, the genus remains but needs a new differentia. However, the critique says this applies only to external composites, not mental ones like wujub. Obligation is a mental composite where genus and differentia are the same reality, so removing the differentia removes the whole composite.
This critique supports the Hanafi view that the specific jawaz (with hardship) is inseparable from wujub.
4
Apply the correct meaning of jawaz to the post-naskh scenario
After naskh, the Hanafis say the act is not 'judged as permissible' (meaning 1) but rather 'not judged at all' (meaning 2). The Shafi'is assume meaning 1 remains. The Hanafis require new evidence to move from meaning 2 to meaning 1.
This is the crux of the disagreement.

And on this, equality means no hardship in the act. And it is also applied to what is doubtful, similarly by reason or law, like the urine of a donkey.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the abrogation of obligation (naskh al-wujub). It classifies three types of abrogation: (1) abrogation by a text indicating permission (ibaha) and permissibility (jawaz), e.g., abrogation of fasting Ashura; (2) abrogation by a text prohibiting the act, e.g., abrogation of facing Jerusalem; and (3) abrogation without any statement of permissibility or prohibition. The discussion centers on the third type: does the original permissibility (jawaz) remain after the obligation is removed? According to the Hanafi school, it does not remain; the act becomes suspended (tawaqquf) until a new evidence establishes either permissibility or prohibition. The Shafi'is, however, argue that permissibility remains because obligation entails permissibility (al-wujub yatadammanu al-jawaz). The author, al-Sayyid al-Sharif al-Jurjani (the commentator on Muslim al-Thubut), sides with the Shafi'is, contrary to al-Ghazali who agreed with the Hanafis. The text then presents the Hanafi rebuttal: the permissibility entailed by obligation is a specific kind—permissibility with hardship in abandonment (jawaz ma'a al-haraj fi al-tark). When obligation is abrogated, this hardship is removed, but the permissibility itself is not automatically separated; a new evidence is needed. The analogy of a growing body and an inanimate object illustrates that when the differentia (growth) is removed, the genus (body) remains but with a new differentia. However, obligation is a mental composite, not an external one, so the analogy is flawed. To clarify, the text then defines the various meanings of ja'iz (permissible): (1) what the Lawgiver has judged as not prevented—includes mubah, wajib, mandub; (2) what the law has not judged as prevented—this is the Hanafi position of suspension; (3) what is not prevented by reason; (4) what is equal in both respects by law or reason; (5) what is doubtful. The Shafi'is claim that after abrogation, the first meaning (non-prevention by law) remains, but the Hanafis reject this because the original command's evidence is gone. The Hanafi view is that until a new evidence comes, the act is in a state of suspension (tawaqquf), neither clearly permissible nor forbidden.

Key Terms
الجائز (al-ja'iz): Has five meanings: (1) what the Lawgiver has judged as not prevented; (2) what the law has not judged; (3) not prevented by reason; (4) equal in both respects; (5) doubtful.
التوقف (al-tawaqquf): Suspension of judgment on a ruling until evidence arises.
المباح (al-mubah): The legally permissible, contrasted with wajib and mandub.

Hanafi Defence of Eternal Divine Command against Absurdity Objection
Usul dispute on eternal divine command and absurdity objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between intellectual relation (ta'alluq 'aqli) and actual relation (ta'alluq munajjaz) to defend the eternity of divine command against the objection of absurdity.

Core Idea
Eternal command is suspended, not actual; absurdity only follows from actual pre-eternal command.

This lesson explores a central debate in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: how can Allah's command be eternal if there were no subjects to command in pre-eternity? The Mu'tazila argued this leads to absurdity (safah wa 'abath). The Hanafi response introduces a key distinction that safeguards divine speech's eternity without requiring a subject.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
State the Mu'tazili argument: eternal command without an existing subject is absurd.
The objection rests on the impossibility of a command without a commanded subject (ma'mur).
2
Distinguish between actual and intellectual relation
Explain the Hanafi distinction: actual relation (ta'alluq munajjaz) requires existence of the subject; intellectual relation (ta'alluq 'aqli) does not.
The eternal command has an intellectual relation to future subjects, not an actual one in pre-eternity.
3
Apply the distinction to the objection
Since the command is suspended (mu'allaq) on the condition of existence, it is not actual in pre-eternity, so absurdity does not follow.
The suspension is like a conditional command; it becomes actual only when the subject exists.
4
Respond to further implications
Show that the same objection would apply to reports lacking a subject, but the Hanafi response handles that similarly.
The Quranic verse about Adam's sin written before creation is a report, not a command, and the Mu'tazila's own logic would imply absurdity there too.
5
Summarize the thamarah al-khilaf
Identify the practical consequence: the Mu'tazila say the Quran is created; Hanafis say it is uncreated.
This affects rulings on the Quran's authority and its role in legal derivation.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute (usul dispute) concerning the nature of divine speech (kalam) and the implication of absurdity (safah wa 'abath) if command is eternal without an existing subject. The commentator, a Hanafi, defends the position that divine speech is eternal (qadim) as an attribute of Allah, and that His command is eternal in essence but not actual (munajjaz) until the creation of the obligated. He responds to Mu'tazila and Karamiyya objections. The Mu'tazila argued that if command is eternal, it would require a subject at the time of command, which would be absurd. The Hanafi response is that the command is suspended (mu'allaq) upon the existence of the obligated; it is an intellectual relation (ta'alluq 'aqli) not an actual one. This avoids absurdity. The commentator then discusses the view of Abdullah ibn Sa'id al-Qattan, who attempted to avoid the implication by saying that the eternal speech is not inherently command or prohibition but becomes so through temporal relations. The commentator critiques this view, arguing that the coexistence of a genus or divided without any division is impossible, even if the division is by accidents. The commentator ultimately affirms the Hanafi stance: divine speech is one, eternal, and indivisible in essence; its multiplicity is due to relations (ta'alluqat) that occur in time. The passage emphasizes that the Quran is uncreated, citing Imam Abu Hanifa's strong stance.

Key Terms
الكلام النفسي (al-kalam al-nafsi): Internal speech: the eternal attribute of Allah consisting of command, prohibition, etc., in essence without articulation.
التعلق المنجز (al-ta'alluq al-munajjaz): Actual relation: a command that is immediately directed to an existing subject.
السفه والعبث (al-safah wa al-'abath): Foolishness and futility: actions without purpose; objection raised by Mu'tazila against eternal command.

Critique of Abdullah ibn Sa'id al-Qattan’s View on Eternal Speech
Usul dispute on the nature of eternal speech and critique of al-Qattan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate and critique the view that eternal divine speech is a common element that becomes command or prohibition through temporal relations.

Core Idea
Eternal speech cannot be a genus without a division; the critique shows that the divided cannot exist without any division.

Some scholars, like Abdullah ibn Sa'id al-Qattan, attempted to avoid the absurdity objection by arguing that eternal speech is not inherently a command or prohibition but a common element that acquires these designations through relationships. This lesson examines and critizes that view from a Hanafi perspective.

Guided Path
1
State al-Qattan's position
Al-Qattan argued that eternal speech is not a command or prohibition in pre-eternity; it becomes so through temporal relations. The eternal element is the common essence (mush tarak).
This view tries to avoid the implication that eternal speech is absurd.
2
Understand the objection raised
The objection: command, prohibition, etc. are types (anwa') of speech. A genus (jins) cannot exist without a type. So the common element would need to be instantiated in some type even in pre-eternity.
This objection relies on the logical relation between genus and species.
3
Analyze al-Qattan's response
Al-Qattan denied that these are types; they are accidents (awarid) that attach by relation. The common element can exist without them.
He also argued that divine speech is one determinate thing, not a universal genus.
4
Critique the response
The commentator argues: even if not types, they are divisions (aqsam). Existence of a divided (maqsum) without any division is impossible. Even if division is by accidents, the divided cannot be without at least one division.
Moreover, al-Qattan's claim that the eternal is the common element contradicts his own assumption that it is free of divisions.
5
Affirm the Hanafi conclusion
Divine speech is one, eternal, indivisible in essence. Multiplicity arises through relations (ta'alluqat) that occur in time, not through inherent division.
The intention of address (qaṣd al-takhaṭub) requires some division, but that division is in the future.

Commentary

The commentator, a Hanafi, defends the position that divine speech is eternal (qadim) as an attribute of Allah, and that His command is eternal in essence but not actual (munajjaz) until the creation of the obligated. The commentator critiques this view, arguing that the coexistence of a genus or divided without any division is impossible, even if the division is by accidents. The commentator ultimately affirms the Hanafi stance: divine speech is one, eternal, and indivisible in essence; its multiplicity is due to relations (ta'alluqat) that occur in time. The passage emphasizes that the Quran is uncreated, citing Imam Abu Hanifa's strong stance.

The Hanafi Ruling on Erroneous Talaq: Intent vs. Apparent
Hanafi ruling on mistaken talaq with usul reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that intention (niyyah) is required for religious validity (diyanatan) while the judge rules by the apparent (zahir), and distinguish the ruling for a mistaken speaker (ghalit) from a jester (hazi).

Core Idea
No religious effect for mistaken talaq; only judicial (qada'an) because judge rules by the apparent.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut explores the tension between what is valid before Allah (diyanatan) and what is enforceable in court (qada'an). The case: a man utters talaq mistakenly while intending tasbih. Through analogies with unintentional oaths and sleeping persons, the Hanafi school establishes that intention is crucial for religious validity, while the judge must go by external words. The passage also rebuts the hadith that 'three things are serious,' restricting it to jesters.

Guided Path
1

What is the legal effect of a talaq uttered mistakenly? The passage presents multiple views. Determine whether the talaq occurs religiously (diyanatan), judicially (qada'an), both, or neither.
Focus on the distinction between diyanatan and qada'an.
2
Examine the Hanafi Position
Ibn al-Humam's view: talaq occurs only judicially, not religiously. The judge applies the apparent, but before Allah it is invalid due to lack of intent.
Contrast with the first cited opinion that it occurs both.
3
Analyze the Principal Dalil
The verse on laghw oaths (Quran 5:89) is used by analogy. Unintentional oaths have no religious consequence; similarly, unintentional talaq should have none. Also analogized to the sleeping person's talaq.
Identify the underlying illa: lack of intent.
4
Address the Opposing Proof
The hadith 'Three things are serious' suggests intention is irrelevant. Hanafi response: it applies only to jesters who are satisfied with the cause, not to the mistaken who intends neither cause nor ruling.
Understand the distinction between hazl (jest) and ghalat (error).
5
Determine the Practical Consequence (Thamarah)
If a man utters mistaken talaq, the wife remains permissible religiously; if he remarries her without a new contract, it is valid diyanatan. Judicially, the talaq is effective, so he would need to go through formal process to return to her.
This thamarah reflects the difference between the schools.

Conjunction and Specification: The Case of Prayer and Zakat
Refuting the claim that conjunction implies shared ruling for independent sentences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that conjunction of independent sentences does not force shared specification (takhsis) and evaluate the opposing argument based on the 'illa of hardship.

Core Idea
Conjunction does not transmit specification; each sentence stands on its own reason.

In this lesson, we delve into a nuanced debate in Hanafi usul al-fiqh concerning the implications of conjunction (عطف) between two independent sentences. Critics argued that if the command to 'establish prayer' is specific to adults due to hardship, then the conjoined command 'give zakat' must also be specific to adults. We will see how the Hanafi school refutes this by highlighting the distinct nature of the two obligations and the role of necessity-based specification.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
One Qur'anic verse can specify another only if they are connected — otherwise, it is abrogation or conflict.

A key difference between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools concerns whether a later general verse can be specified by an earlier specific verse, or vice versa. The Hanafi school insists that the specific verse must be directly connected (mawsul) to the general for it to count as specification; otherwise, the operative mechanism is abrogation (naskh) or conflict resolution. This lesson examines the proofs and practical implications of this rule.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul dispute on whether a specific text (khas) can be abrogated (naskh) by a general text (amm). The author presents multiple arguments from both sides, refuting the opponents step by step. The discussion begins with examples from Surah al-Ma'idah, citing narrations that it is the last surah and that some verses were abrogated. The core issue: if a general text is probabilistic (zanni) and a specific text is decisive (qati), can the decisive be abrogated by the probabilistic? The author argues that the general text is not probabilistic; both are equal in indicatory strength. He counters that specification (takhsis) is not more appropriate than abrogation; rather, abrogation employs both proofs fully in different times. The passage ends with a hypothetical example about honoring and not honoring people to illustrate the point.

Key Terms
النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation of a legal ruling by a later textual evidence
الخاص (al-khas): A text that indicates a specific meaning, not general
العام (al-amm): A text that encompasses all individuals of a category
قطعي (qati): Decisive indication, leaving no room for doubt
ظني (zanni): Probabilistic indication, open to interpretation

Refinements: Frequency of Specification vs Abrogation and Rational Decisiveness
Detailed rebuttal of frequency and decisiveness arguments

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the arguments that specification is more frequent than abrogation and analyze the rational vs linguistic aspects of decisiveness in the context of abrogation.

Core Idea
Abrogation employs both proofs fully in different times, making it more appropriate than specification in some cases.

This lesson delves deeper into the dispute by examining two more arguments. First, opponents claim specification is more frequent than abrogation, so it should be preferred. The Hanafi response argues that abrogation actually employs both proofs in all their significations over time, making it more appropriate. Second, the discussion turns to the nature of decisiveness: is it rational or linguistic? The author argues that the specific text's decisiveness regarding an individual is rational, not linguistic, and that abrogation essentially involves probabilistic elements, so there is no impossibility.

Guided Path
1
Understand the third argument: frequency of specification
The opponent says specification (takhsis) occurs more often than abrogation (naskh), so it should be the preferred interpretation when a general text conflicts with a specific text.
The Hanafi response is that abrogation employs both proofs fully at different times.
2
Analyze the response: abrogation is more appropriate
The author argues that in specification, one text is carried upon the other, reducing its effect. In abrogation, both texts are fully operative in their respective times, so it is more comprehensive.
This is a methodological preference.
3
Examine the rational decisiveness argument
The discussion moves to whether the decisiveness of a specific text is rational (aql) or linguistic (lughawi). The author argues it is rational: the text is assigned to each individual, but the understanding that a general text's invalidation removes the specific individual's ruling is due to entailment.
This nullifies the opponent's claim that a decisive specific cannot be abrogated by a probabilistic general.
4
Synthesize the Hanafi position
Combine all three arguments: the general is decisive, both are probabilistic if taken as such, and abrogation is not invalidating decisive by probabilistic but rather operating on probabilistic individuals.
The author concludes that the opponent's objection is not valid.

Between Takhsīṣ and Naskh: Which Conflict Resolution is Preferable?
Debating preference between specification and abrogation in conflicting texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi argument for preferring naskh over takhsīṣ when conflicting texts are independent rulings, and evaluate the counter-claim based on frequency.

Core Idea
Naskh applies both texts fully in different times; takhsīṣ merely imposes one text on the other.

When two revelatory texts conflict, Muslim jurists must determine which method of reconciliation to apply. A key debate asks whether specification (takhsīṣ) — limiting a general text by a specific one — should be preferred over abrogation (naskh) — replacing an earlier rule with a later one. Some argue that takhsīṣ is more frequent and thus preferable. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi rebuttal: true application of both proofs occurs only in naskh, not in takhsīṣ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposing claim
State the argument that takhsīṣ is preferable because it is more frequent and partially applies both texts.
The opponents claim 'التخصيص أولى من النسخ لأنه أغلب'.
2
Examine the Hanafi limitation
Note that the discussion is about independent rulings (kalam mustaqill) that convey a specific ruling, not general-specification contexts like 'أقيموا الصلاة' and such.
The author says 'الكلام في الكلام المستقل المفيد الحكم'.
3
Challenge the frequency premise
The Hanafis deny that takhsīṣ is more frequent in this specific context; it is actually very rare.
The text states 'ولا نسلم أنه فيه أغلب بل أقل القليل'.
4
Analyze the nature of 'applying both proofs'
Takhsīṣ forces one text onto the other, it does not give each its full meaning; naskh applies each text fully in its own time.
The author says 'ليس في التخصيص إعمال الدليلين في مدلوليهما، بل حمل لأحدهما على الآخر'.
5
Conclude with the Hanafi preference
Naskh is superior because it honors both texts completely across different periods.
Hence 'في النسخ إعمال الدليلين في تمام مدلوليهما في زمانين فهو أولى'.

The Mufassilun Argument: Does a Later General Abrogate a Prior Specific?
Arguing that a later specific text abrogates a prior general, using the example of Zayd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the temporal conditions under which a later general text abrogates or is specified by a prior specific text, according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A prior specific can specify a later general if attached; if separate, the later abrogates the earlier.

The Mufassilun (detailers) argue that a later general text should abrogate an earlier specific text, using the example of 'Kill Zayd' followed by 'Do not kill idolaters.' The Hanafi response refines this: the temporal sequence and whether the texts are attached or separate determine the outcome. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
What is the Mufassilun's claim? They say that if a specific command (e.g., 'Kill Zayd') is followed by a general prohibition (e.g., 'Do not kill idolaters'), the general abrogates the specific because it includes that individual.
The key issue: does the later general override the earlier specific?
2
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The author argues that the prior specific can serve as a qarīna (context) that specifies the general, so the general is understood to exclude the individual. Thus no abrogation occurs.
The prior specific indicates that the general was not intended to include that individual.
3
Examine the temporal scenarios
The text distinguishes two cases: (a) the specific comes after the general and is separate (munfasil) – then it abrogates the general for that individual. (b) The specific comes after but is attached (muqārin, e.g., in the same speech) – then it specifies the general, not abrogate.
Attachment prevents true abrogation because the general is immediately qualified.
4
Apply the rule to the example
In the example, if 'Do not kill idolaters' is later and separate from 'Kill Zayd', the Hanafi view is that the prior specific provides context, so the general does not abrogate; rather, it is specified. If the order were reversed (specific later and separate), the specific would abrogate the general.
The overarching principle: the later text (regardless of generality) abrogates the earlier, unless attached.

The Hanafi Rule on Absolute and Restricted Commands with the Same Cause
usul rule on handling absolute and restricted with same cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for when an absolute and a restricted command share the same cause, and apply it to derive the ruling on fasting three consecutive days in oath expiation.

Core Idea
When an absolute and a restricted command share the same cause, the restricted governs—by carry, suspension, or abrogation temporally.

This lesson examines the third case in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: when both an absolute (mutlaq) and a restricted (muqayyad) text are positive (obligatory) and relate to the same cause. You will learn how the Hanafi school resolves the apparent conflict, the temporal factors involved, and how this differs from the Shafi'i approach.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two texts
Locate the absolute command (e.g., 'fast three days') and the restricted command (e.g., 'three consecutive days'). Confirm they address the same ruling and share the same cause.
2
Check the temporal relationship
Determine whether the two texts appeared together, with unknown precedence, or with known later restricted text. Each scenario triggers a different rule.
Together: carry absolute to the restricted. Unknown: carry to restrict. Known later: abrogation by addition.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule
If together, carry the absolute to the restricted as a bayan. If unknown, carry to the restricted due to caution. If later, the restricted abrogates the absolute by adding the restriction.
The Hanafi view considers the absolute literal at its time; only naskh can change it.
4
Compare with the Shafi'i position
Note that Shafi'is treat a later restricted text as a specification (takhsis) of the absolute, not abrogation. This difference stems from the epistemological status of non-mutawatir readings.
Hanafis accept well-known (mashhur) readings as proofs; Shafi'is require tawatur.

Abrogation or Clarification? The Hanafi Rebuttal of Shafi'i on Mutlaq and Muqayyad
Hanafi refutation of Shafi'i proofs for mutlaq-muqayyad as bayan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three Shafi'i arguments for carrying mutlaq to muqayyad as bayan, and state the Hanafi rebuttals that classify it as naskh.

Core Idea
A later qualified text abrogates an earlier absolute one, not merely clarifies it.

The relationship between an absolute (mutlaq) and a later qualified (muqayyad) text is a key usul dispute. The Shafi'i school treats the qualified text as a clarification (bayan), while the Hanafi school considers it an abrogation (naskh). This passage systematically refutes three Shafi'i proofs, grounding the Hanafi position in the impossibility of applying both texts simultaneously and the definitive nature of the earlier absolute ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
Determine the central question: does a later muqayyad clarify or abrogate an earlier mutlaq? Recognize that both schools agree on the need to resolve the apparent conflict but differ on the method.
2
Outline the Shafi'i Arguments
List the three proofs: (1) acting on both dalils is better than nullifying one; (2) precaution (ihtiyat) requires following the explicit qualified; (3) if muqayyad were naskh, then every takhsis would be naskh, which is unacceptable.
These are presented in the passage as the standard Shafi'i reasoning.
3
Analyze the Hanafi Rebuttals
For each argument, state the Hanafi response: (1) carrying mutlaq actually ignores the absolute ruling, while naskh allows applying both in different times; (2) precaution also applies in naskh and the absolute was definitive before the qualified; (3) taqyid introduces a new ruling unlike takhsis, so the analogy fails.
The Hanafi rebuttals rely on the principle that a later contradictory ruling abrogates the earlier one.
4
Examine the Key Distinction
Understand the Hanafi differentiation between taqyid (introduces a new ruling opposing the absolute) and takhsis (merely removes part of the general ruling without establishing a new ruling). This distinction refutes the third Shafi'i argument.
The passage emphasizes that taqyid provides a new hukm, making it naskh, while takhsis is a mere restriction.
5
Apply to a Concrete Example
Consider the ruling on freeing a slave: an absolute command 'free a slave' and a later qualified 'free a believing slave'. Under the Hanafi view, the absolute is abrogated, so only a believing slave suffices. Under the Shafi'i view, both are valid but the qualified is better.
This thamarah al-khilaf shows the practical impact of the usul dispute.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed Hanafi rebuttal of Shafi'i arguments regarding the relationship between absolute (mutlaq) and qualified (muqayyad) legal texts. The Shafi'i school holds that when a later qualified text appears, it serves as a clarification (bayan) of the earlier absolute text, and both are applied. The Hanafi school, by contrast, considers the later qualified text as an abrogation (naskh) of the absolute. The passage systematically addresses three Shafi'i proofs.

First, the Shafi'is argue that carrying the absolute to the qualified allows acting upon both proofs (dalilayn), while considering it abrogation nullifies one. The Hanafi response is that this is not true: carrying the absolute actually negates the absolute ruling and only applies the qualified. In abrogation, both proofs are acted upon in different time periods (the absolute before the qualified, the qualified after), which is better. If the Shafi'is claim that carrying involves a metaphorical application of both proofs, the Hanafis respond that this metaphorical application is only valid when there is no way to apply both literally, but here literal application in different times is possible.

Second, the Shafi'is argue that carrying the absolute to the qualified is precautionary (ihtiyati), because the absolute is silent about the restriction while the qualified is explicit; by acting on the qualified, one is certain to fulfill the obligation. The Hanafi response is that this precaution is also present in abrogation, since acting on the qualified also ensures exit from liability. Moreover, the claim that clarification is easier than abrogation is rejected for independent contradictory statements, where abrogation is more common. Additionally, the precaution argument is rebutted by cases where the absolute and qualified differ in ruling (hukman) or cause (sababan), and the Shafi'is themselves do not carry in those cases, showing inconsistency.

Third, the Shafi'is argue that if the qualified were an abrogator, then every specification (takhsis) would also be abrogation, since both remove some individuals. The Hanafi response is that specification removes part of the ruling without establishing a new contrary ruling, whereas restriction establishes a new ruling that opposes the absolute. Hence, restriction is abrogation, but specification is not. The difference is elaborated through examples and responses to objections. The passage concludes by introducing the fourth division: when the absolute and qualified share the same ruling but arise from different causes (sababan), the Hanafi view is that the qualified abrogates the absolute only if the ruling differs; this case remains to be further discussed.

Key Terms
المطلق (al-mutlaq): A text that is not qualified by any condition or description, carrying its ruling universally without restriction.
المقيد (al-muqayyad): A text that includes a qualifying condition or description limiting the ruling.
البيان (al-bayan): Clarification; the explanation of a text that removes ambiguity.
النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation; the removal or replacement of a prior legal ruling by a later one.
التخصيص (al-takhsis): Specification; the limitation of a general ruling to some of its individuals without introducing a new contrary ruling.
الاحتياط (al-ihtiyat): Precaution; acting in a manner that ensures certainty of fulfilling an obligation.

Intention in Commands and Oaths: Number and Genus in Hanafi Usul
Extending intention principle to past tense and oath case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a command indicates a single instance to both imperative and past tense, and analyze an oath case using the rule of nakirah in negation.

Core Idea
A command inherently seeks one act; adding a number is restriction, not interpretation.

This passage addresses two interconnected issues: whether the past tense of a command allows the same intention of number as the imperative, and how an oath involving a generic term like 'water' is analyzed. The Hanafi school distinguishes between interpretation (changing meaning) and restriction (adding a condition without altering original signification). Understanding this distinction is crucial for deriving rulings from textual commands and oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem of Past Tense Analogy
Recognize that if the imperative 'divorce' allows intending three as a genus, then the past tense 'I divorced' should similarly allow intending three. This creates a strong challenge requiring resolution.
The author acknowledges the difficulty but points to a difference in nature: command vs. report.
2
Examine the Counterargument on Number in Command
The opponent claims that if a command can be interpreted with a number (e.g., 'Divorce yourself two'), it proves the command can bear that number. Understand this claim.
This is a common objection to the Hanafi position.
3
Analyze the Hanafi Response: Interpretation vs. Restriction
The Hanafi response distinguishes between interpreting the command's meaning (which would change it) and imposing a restriction (qayd) that keeps the original meaning intact. The number is a separate qualification, not part of the command's signification.
The number is attached externally; the ruling occurs by the combination, not the command alone.
4
Apply the Principle to the Oath Branch
In the oath 'I will not drink water,' 'water' is a nakirah in negation, generalizing over all instances. If the swearer intends all water, the term covers the entire genus, similar to three divorces. The negation applies to the whole, so drinking any part does not break the oath.
This demonstrates how genus terms operate in legal contexts.
5
Critique the Universal Collective Application
The author refutes the idea that a universal can apply to multiple individuals simultaneously with one application, using the absurd example 'Zayd and Bakr are a human.' Understand why this is invalid: the universal applies to each individual separately, not collectively.
This clarifies the Hanafi view on the application of universals.

The Hanafi Rule on Prohibition in Shari'ah Matters
Defending the Hanafi position on nahy in shar'iyyat.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that prohibition in shari'ah indicates fasad of description and evaluate the opposing argument based on analogy with sensory matters.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, a shari'ah prohibition does not make the act inherently evil; it only invalidates an external description.

This passage clarifies a foundational Hanafi usul principle: the distinction between prohibition in sensory matters (hissiyat) and shari'ah matters (shar'iyyat). While sensory prohibitions indicate inherent evil, shari'ah prohibitions only affect an external description, preserving the act's validity. The text defends this view against the three imams and their followers who argue for essential invalidity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposing claim
Understand that some followers of the three imams argue that prohibition in shari'ah is like prohibition in sensory matters, both indicating essential ugliness (qubh dhati) and thus essential invalidity.
The opposing school includes some Hanafi followers and Malik and Ibn al-Hajib.
2
Locate the Hanafi counter-argument
The Hanafi response rejects the analogy because in sensory matters the act is not created by the Lawgiver, whereas in shari'ah matters the act's reality is established by the Lawgiver's enactment; legislating evil would be evil, so the prohibited act cannot be inherently evil.
The key point: the Lawgiver's role differs between sensory and shari'ah matters.
3
Analyze the Quranic verse interpretation
The opponents cite Q. 4:22 (do not marry your fathers' wives). The Hanafi response interprets 'nikah' linguistically as intercourse, not the contract. The contract is invalid because its fruit (halal intercourse) becomes impossible, not because of inherent evil.
This shows how a seemingly shari'ah-based prohibition can be re-interpreted as a description-based prohibition.
4

The Hanafis give priority to what necessitates the prohibition (al-muqaddam) over the requirement of prohibition (al-muqtada). The act's possibility is preserved; only the description is affected.
This is a methodological preference established earlier in usul.
5
Recognize the thamarah (practical difference)
The practical outcome: a prohibited shari'ah act is valid but sinful (e.g., divorce during menses). The act's essence remains sound; only the description is invalid.
This contrasts with the Maliki view that such acts are invalid.

The Principle of Abrogation of Intrinsically Evil Acts
Ruling that intrinsically evil acts cannot be abrogated unless they acquire a good aspect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi usul principle that intrinsically evil acts cannot be abrogated and identify the exception where a good aspect overcomes the evil.

Core Idea
Intrinsically evil acts cannot be abrogated unless they acquire a good aspect that overcomes their evil.

This lesson introduces a fundamental usul principle: acts that are inherently evil by nature cannot have their prohibition lifted by a later revelation. However, the principle allows an exception when the act acquires a good aspect that either removes its evil or outweighs it. Understanding this principle is key to reconciling apparent contradictions in the sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the rule
Recognize that the rule applies to acts that are intrinsically evil (al-qabih li-aynihi), i.e., evil by their very nature.
Examples include lying, injustice, and oppression.

The default ruling is that such acts cannot be abrogated (do not accept naskh) – their prohibition cannot be lifted by a later revelation.
Naskh here means annulment of the ruling, not mere specification.
3
Recognize the exception
Identify the condition under which abrogation is possible: the act must acquire a good aspect (jihat muhasina) that either removes its evil nature or overcomes its harm.
The good aspect can be a beneficial circumstance that outweighs the evil.

Apply the example
Consider the example given: lying to protect a prophet or save an innocent person. Lying is intrinsically evil, but the necessity to protect a prophet provides a good aspect that outweighs the evil, making it permissible.
This is not abrogation but a limited exception based on overriding benefit.
5
Understand the rationale
Reflect on why intrinsically evil acts cannot be abrogated without a good aspect: because divine law cannot command evil; therefore, a later revelation cannot make evil good by mere abrogation.
The principle preserves the consistency of God's law with His wisdom.

Inherently Evil Acts Cannot Be Abrogated: Principle and Shafi'i Objections
Expanding the rule that inherently evil cannot be abrogated with three Shafi'i counterarguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle that inherently evil acts cannot be abrogated, distinguish between inherently evil and evil due to an aspect, and apply the principle to respond to Shafi'i objections regarding adultery, usurpation, and conquest.

Core Idea
An inherently evil act cannot directly cause a lawful effect, but accidental consequences may produce legal rulings.

This lesson explores a key usul al-fiqh principle: acts that are evil in themselves (qabih li-aynihi) cannot have their prohibition removed, unless a overriding good aspect exists. The Hanafi school defends this principle against Shafi'i objections that cite cases where prohibited acts seem to cause lawful effects, such as adultery creating marriage prohibitions by affinity. Understanding this principle reveals how the Hanafis use the concept of accidental causality (al-sababiyyah bil-'arad) to reconcile the inherent evil of an act with its incidental legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its exception
State the rule: inherently evil acts cannot be abrogated. Note the exception: if a beneficial aspect outweighs the evil, abrogation is possible (e.g., lying to save a prophet).
The exception applies only when the good aspect is clear and outweighs the harm.
2
Distinguish between qabih li-aynihi and qabih li-jihah
Understand that 'inherently evil' (qabih li-aynihi) refers to acts with no intrinsic good, like adultery. 'Evil due to an aspect' (qabih li-jihah) refers to acts that are evil because of an external factor. Both are treated similarly unless a good aspect emerges.
Adultery is considered inherently evil because it always causes confusion of lineage.
3
Examine the first Shafi'i objection: adultery causing marriage prohibition
The Shafi'is argue that if adultery is inherently evil, it cannot be a cause for a lawful prohibition (marriage by affinity). The Hanafi response: the prohibition arises from the reality of intercourse and potential childbirth, which are neutral, not from the sin itself. Causality is accidental.
The Hanafis analogize this to using dust for tayammum: dust is impure but stands in place of water for purification.
4
Examine the second and third objections: usurpation and conquest
The Shafi'is ask how a usurper can own usurped property and a conqueror own Muslim property. Hanafi response: ownership arises from liability (daman) for usurpation when the original is lost, and from removal of legal protection (isma) for conquest. The prohibited act itself does not directly cause ownership.
For usurpation, the usurper must compensate, and the compensation justifies ownership. The usurper does not own the original but owns a substitute.
5
Generalize the principle: direct vs accidental causation
The core idea is that an inherently evil act cannot be the direct cause (sabab) of a lawful ruling, but it can be an incidental cause due to some neutral aspect (e.g., the physical act of intercourse, the obligation to compensate).
This distinction prevents the argument from contradiction.

Commentary
This passage presents a usul principle: inherently evil acts (qabih li-aynihi) cannot be abrogated, meaning their prohibition cannot be lifted. However, an exception exists if a beneficial aspect arises that outweighs the evil, like lying to save a prophet's life. Similarly, acts evil due to an aspect (qabih li-jihah) also cannot be abrogated if no outweighing good aspect exists. Adultery is cited as inherently evil because it confuses lineage and has no redeeming good aspect. The text then responds to three Shafi'i objections: 1) How can adultery cause marriage prohibition by affinity? The Hanafi response is that the prohibition arises not from the sin but from the reality of sexual intercourse that generates a child, which is a neutral created entity. Thus, the causality is accidental. 2) How can a usurper own the usurped property? The answer is that ownership arises due to liability (daman), not the act of usurpation. When the original property name disappears (e.g., consumed), the usurper must compensate, and this compensation transfers ownership. 3) How can a conqueror own the property of Muslims? The answer is that the property loses its inviolability (isma) when the owner becomes a non-Muslim under a non-Muslim rule, and the conquest severs legal protection, allowing ownership. The passage defends these positions as consistent with the principle that inherently evil acts do not directly cause beneficial rulings.

Key Terms
القبيح لعينه (al-qabih li-aynihi): An act that is evil in itself, regardless of external factors, such that its prohibition cannot be lifted without an overriding beneficial aspect.
القبيح لجهة (al-qabih li-jihah): An act that is evil due to an external aspect, not its essence; if no good aspect outweighs, it also cannot be abrogated.
نسخ (naskh): The lifting of a legal ruling; here specifically the removal of prohibition (hurmah).
