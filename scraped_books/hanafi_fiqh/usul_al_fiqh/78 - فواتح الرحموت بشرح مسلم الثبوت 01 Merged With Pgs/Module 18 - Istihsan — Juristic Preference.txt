Defending Eternal Attributes: The Hanafi Refutation of Mu'tazila on Creation
Refutation of Mu'tazila on divine attribute of creation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that the attribute of creation is eternal and reconstruct the refutation of the Mu'tazila objection of infinite regress using ta'alluq and i'tibar.

Core Idea
The Hanafi attribute of creation is eternal; the effect is contingent via a contingent relation, not a contingent attribute.

The Mu'tazila argued that if creation were an eternal attribute, the world would be eternal; if contingent, infinite regress follows. How can the Hanafi school maintain that God is eternally Creator without falling into either absurdity? This passage provides the key: the divine power has a contingent relation (ta'alluq) to the created effect, while the attribute itself remains eternal. This subtle distinction, grounded in the concept of 'considerations' (i'tibar), is the cornerstone of the Hanafi response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the point of dispute
Locate the central issue: Is the name 'Creator' derived from an eternal attribute inherent in God, or from the created effect itself? The Mu'tazila claim it is derived from the effect.
2
State the Mu'tazila objection
Understand the dilemma they pose: if the effect (tathir) is eternal, the world is eternal; if it occurs in time, it needs another effect, leading to infinite regress.
This is a classic kalam argument against attributing temporal effects to an eternal being.
3
Trace the Hanafi response using ta'alluq and i'tibar
The answer: the divine power has a relation (ta'alluq) that is contingent in its specific occurrence, but the attribute of power (and thus creation) is eternal. The relation is a 'consideration' (i'tibar) that does not entail regress because considerations cease when the relation ceases.
Key Arabic phrases: 'التعلق حادث' and 'الاعتبارات تنقطع بانقطاع الاعتبار'.
4
Evaluate the final refinement
The commentator adds: the relation is actually eternal, but bound to the existence of the effect after a cause. This avoids both eternity of the world and regress.
The final position is that the attribute is eternal but its relation is specified to the moment of creation.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion on the derivation of the name of the agent (ism fa'il) from a principle (mabda') in the context of divine attributes, specifically the attribute of creation (khalq). The text begins by stating that the point of dispute lies in the fact that while typically an agent's name is derived from a principle that is established in the agent itself, with God, creation is not an attribute that inheres in Him. The text rejects an earlier objection that creation consists of substances and accidents, calling it arbitrary. It then distinguishes between natural derivations (like active participles from verbs) and artificial derivations (like 'khammar' from 'khamr'). The main argument is with the Mu'tazila, who claimed that creation (al-khalq) is the effect itself (al-makhlouq), not a divine attribute. They argued that if the effect (tathir) were eternal, the world would be eternal; if it were contingent, it would require another effect, leading to infinite regress. The commentator refutes this by clarifying that the divine power (qudra) has a relation (ta'alluq) that is contingent in terms of its specific connection to the created effect, but the attribute itself is eternal. He argues that the relationship of effecting is a consideration (i'tibar) that does not lead to infinite regress because considerations cease by the cessation of the relation. He concludes that the correct view is that the relation is eternal but specifically tied to the existence of the effect after a cause, avoiding both eternity of the world and infinite regress. The argument is rational (dalil aqli) and employs logical principles to defend Hanafi theology against Mu'tazila.

Conditional Oaths: Intention and Partial Fulfillment
Deriving rulings for conditional oaths using metaphor between purchase and ownership.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the ruling (qada'an and dianatan) for a conditional oath involving 'purchase' or 'ownership' when the act is partially fulfilled, applying the Hanafi principle of majaz and the distinction between apparent and intended meaning.

Core Idea
In oaths, intention can override the apparent meaning: partial purchase may not trigger qada', but full ownership does dianatan.

This passage examines a nuanced scenario in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: a conditional oath using the word 'purchase' but intending ownership. When the speaker buys half, sells, then buys the other half, does the condition trigger? The analysis hinges on whether the condition is interpreted literally (the act of buying) or according to the intended meaning (acquisition of full ownership). The Hanafi school distinguishes between qada' (external judgment based on the apparent) and dianah (internal judgment based on intention). Here, the speaker's niyyah can produce different rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional oath
The person says 'If I buy him, he is free' but intends 'ownership' by 'buy'. The condition is the occurrence of the act denoted by 'shira''.
The word 'shira'' can be literal (the act of buying) or metaphorical (ownership).
2
Analyze the partial transaction
The speaker buys half, sells it, then buys the other half. The condition did not occur as a complete purchase of the whole slave at once.
Partial purchase followed by sale and repurchase does not constitute a single act of buying the whole.
3
Apply the principle of majaz
Since the speaker intended ownership, the condition is 'full ownership', not the act of buying. Full ownership requires complete possession at one moment, which did not happen.
The Hanafi rule: words are interpreted according to their apparent meaning unless there is a qarinah (indication) of metaphorical intent, which then governs.
4
Distinguish qada' and dianah
Qada'an: the apparent condition (buying the whole) was not met, so the slave is not freed. Dianatan: if the speaker truly intended ownership, even partial fulfillment might be considered, but the text says 'only the All-Knowing knows'. So no dianatan ruling is given.
Qada' follows the apparent; dianah is between the person and Allah.
5
Derive the opposite case
If the oath says 'If I own him, he is free' but intends purchase, then partial ownership (buying half) is considered ownership qada'an because the condition of ownership does not require acquiring all shares at once. Thus freed qada'an.
The key difference: 'ownership' customarily requires collection of all shares; 'purchase' does not.

Prohibition of Majaz from Effect to Cause
States Hanafi rule that figurative usage from effect to cause is invalid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that figurative usage from effect to cause is invalid, explain the reasoning (illah) based on the coiner's establishment of direction, and identify the exception when the effect is exclusive to the cause.

Core Idea
Hanafis forbid using the effect for the cause figuratively; only the cause-to-effect direction is valid.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi usul principle regarding the direction of figurative language. The school strictly restricts majaz (figurative usage) to the direction from cause (sabab) to effect (musabbab), unless the effect is so exclusively associated that it practically becomes a cause itself. We will explore the reasoning behind this rule and its practical implications for interpreting texts and oaths.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Identify the exact Hanafi position: using the pure effect (musabbab mahd) for the cause is invalid (la yajuz). Note the exception: when the effect is specific to the cause ('inda al-ikhtisas al-'urfi).
The text uses 'musabbab mahd' to emphasize a pure effect, not something that can also be a cause.
2
Analyze the illah
The reason (illah) is that the coiner (wadi') only established the relation (al-‘alaqah) from root (asl, cause) to branch (far', effect). There is no evidence that the reverse direction was established.
This is based on linguistic convention: we say 'sky brought rain' but not 'rain brought sky'.
3
Identify the exception
If the effect is so exclusively associated with a particular cause (e.g., rain with sky, in some contexts), then using the effect for that cause may be permissible. But this is rare and must be customarily established.
The text says 'illa an yakhtassa al-musabbab bi al-sabab'.
4
Apply to examples
Work through examples: (a) 'sky for rain' is valid (cause for effect). (b) 'rain for sky' is invalid unless exclusive. (c) 'talaq for itq' (Shafi'i allows, Hanafi does not). (d) 'I see myself pressing wine' — wine is an effect (outcome) not specific to grapes, so not a counterexample.
The Quranic example is explained as a metaphor based on future state (majaz bi i'tibar ma yu'al ilayhi).
5
Compare with Shafi'i
Imam al-Shafi'i permits the reverse direction (effect for cause) because he considers the relation of cause and effect sufficient for majaz in either direction. The Hanafis disagree, insisting on the coiner's original intent.
The practical difference (thamarah) appears in interpreting oaths and legal documents.

Are Words of Contracts Causes? Sababiyyah vs. Dalalah
Analyzing whether utterences for contracts and dissolutions are causes for external effects or merely indicative

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi usul rule that words of contracts and dissolutions become external causes after being placed in the position of meanings and distinguish this from mere indication (dalalah).

Core Idea
Contractual words after placement cause external effects like killing causes death.

This passage from *Fawatih al-Rahmut* tackles a profound question: Do the words used in contracts, divorces, and manumissions function as causes of legal effects, or do they merely indicate pre-existing meanings? The author navigates objections and presents the refined Hanafi position that such words, once placed, have causal force akin to external causes. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping the Hanafi concept of *sabab* and the role of *lafz* in *hukm*.

Guided Path
1
Identify the refuted claim
Note the quoted *Hashiyah* that some doubt whether contractual words carry any meaning at all. The author finds this astonishing.
The claim would make metaphor, suspension, and truthfulness impossible.
2
Grasp the core rule
The truth is that consideration is for the meaning (*ma'na*) first, but because meaning is hidden, the ruling is attached to the word as evidence.
Analogy: the *rukhsah* of travel is based on hardship, but since hardship is hidden, the ruling revolves around travel.
3
Understand the two categories of words
Words are either mere indicators (like *iqrar*) or causes that bring about external rulings (like contracts). The latter, after placement, become external causes.
Killing causes death: the words of divorce, after being placed in position of meanings, cause the external effect of divorce.
4
Appreciate the subtlety of iqtida'
These words also indicate the cause of the ruling by implicit entailment (*iqtida'*). Because this cause is known only through them, they are given causal status.
Unlike acknowledgments where the underlying cause can be known independently.
5
Note the final nuance
Words unaffected by jest and coercion have causality like external causes, because the occurrence of divorce from a jester shows lack of dependence on intention.
The passage ends with caution, admitting residual unease.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul discussion about the nature of words used in legal transactions (contracts, divorces, etc.) — whether they function as causes (asbab) for external effects or merely as indicators (dilalat). The author begins by quoting a Hashiyah that critiques a view attributing to Qutb al-Din al-Sahali that such words have no meaning. The author finds this problematic, raising objections: if words have no meaning, how can metaphor be valid? How can they be called 'literal'? How can suspended (mu'allaq) expressions exist? Etc. The correct view, the author asserts, is that the primary consideration is the meaning (internal speech) for causality of the ruling; but due to hiddenness, the ruling is attached to the external evidence (the word). This is analogous to the concession of travel: the actual cause is hardship, but because hardship is hidden, the ruling revolves around travel. Then the author presents a refined view from 'Matali' al-Asrar al-Ilahiyyah' (a Hanafi usul commentary). According to this, words are of two types: (1) those that indicate an existing or non-existing referent, like acknowledgments (iqrar); (2) those that are causes for external rulings that would not exist without them, like the words of contracts and dissolutions. These second-type words, after being placed in the position of meanings, are like external causes (e.g., killing causes death). They are not like reports because they do not have pre-existing meanings; rather, they themselves are meanings that can be reported. The master investigator (likely al-Sa'ati or al-Sahawi) does not deny meaning entirely; he says these words, once placed, become external causes. The author then points out a subtlety: the forms of contracts and dissolutions, like reports, only indicate the occurrence of divorce due to its cause (the husband's action). So they are like acknowledgments in not being causes. But then he reconciles: because the cause is known only through these words, the words are given the status of causes, and their effect is attributed to them. Finally, the author suggests another interpretation: that the causality of manumission for freedom is like external causality; words of contracts and dissolutions that are unaffected by jest/coercion and do not require consent have causality like external causes. The passage ends with a note of caution, admitting unease and leaving the true intention to Allah.

Key Terms
سبب (sabab): A cause; something upon which the existence of a ruling is dependent in the sense of a sign or indicator, not an efficient cause. Here, the word is a sabab after placement.
دلالة (dalalah): Indication; the function of a word to convey meaning. Contrasted with sababiyyah here.
إنشاء (insha'): Composition; utterances that bring about legal effects directly.

The Second Proof: Ishtirak as a Basis for Command Form Indicating Wujub
Rational proof that the command form cannot be ambiguous and must be for wujub

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that ishtirak is khilaf al-asl to argue that the command form indicates wujub, and evaluate the counterargument regarding ishtirak ma'nawi using the specificity principle.

Core Idea
Ishtirak is against the original principle; therefore, the command form cannot be shared and must be for wujub.

In this lesson, we explore the second proof from Fawatih al-Rahamut ala Musallam al-Thubut that the command form (sigha if'al) is for obligation (wujub). The proof rests on the foundational Hanafi principle that linguistic ishtirak (shared meaning) is contrary to the original state (khilaf al-asl). By analyzing the difference between the imperative 'asqini' and the explicit recommendation 'nadabtuka an tasqini', the Hanafis establish that the command form carries blame for leaving, which points to wujub. We will also address the objection that the proof only negates ishtirak lafdhi and not ma'nawi, and see how the Hanafi response from al-Tahrir strengthens the argument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
State the key principle: 'al-ishtirak khilaf al-asl' – shared meaning is a departure from the original state of language. This is a foundational axiom in Hanafi usul.
The original state for language is that each word has a single meaning unless proven otherwise.
2
Apply the Principle to the Command Form
The command form (e.g., if'al) cannot be shared between two or more meanings (wujub, nadb, ibaha, etc.) because that would be ishtirak. Therefore, it must be for one specific meaning.
Other meanings like ibaha are ruled out because a command implies preference (tarjih), leaving only wujub and nadb.
3
Use the Linguistic Test: Blame for Leaving
Compare the imperative 'asqini' with the explicit recommendation phrase 'nadabtuka an tasqini'. In the imperative, the speaker blames the listener for not doing the act; in the recommendation, there is no blame. This difference proves the imperative indicates wujub, not nadb.
If the command form were for nadb, both expressions would be conceptually identical in terms of blame, but they are not.
4
Address the Ishtirak Ma'nawi Objection
The opponent may argue that the proof only negates ishtirak lafdhi (verbal homonymy), not ishtirak ma'nawi (conceptual sharing) – e.g., 'command' meaning a general demand that includes both wujub and nadb. The Hanafi response is that even ishtirak ma'nawi is khilaf al-asl when a more specific meaning (wujub) is available, because specificity aids communication (ifada).
This refinement from al-Tahrir cements the proof: the command form is for the most specific meaning, which is wujub.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof that the command form is for wujub, based on the principle that linguistic ishtirak (ambiguity) is against the original principle (khilaf al-asl). The argument goes: the command form cannot be shared between two or more meanings (like wujub and nadb) because ishtirak is a departure from the original principle of univocality. Therefore, it must be for one specific meaning among the possible ones (wujub, nadb, ibaha, etc.). The meanings other than wujub and nadb are discarded because there is a definite understanding of preference (tarjih) in the commanded act, implying it is either wujub or nadb. Then the author argues that nadb is negated by the linguistic difference between an imperative form like 'asqini' (give me to drink) and a request using the word 'nadabtuka' (I recommend to you). In the imperative, the speaker blames the listener for not performing the act, whereas in the recommendation phrase, there is no blame. Thus, the imperative form is for wujub, not nadb. The opponent (who holds the form is for nadb) may deny this difference, claiming both expressions have the same concept. Even if the difference is conceded, the opponent might argue that 'nadabtuka an tasqini' is a clear text (nass) for recommendation, whereas 'asqini' is not a clear text for obligation and can be diverted by a countervailing indication. The author then addresses another objection: the proof only negates ishtirak lafdhi (verbal homonymy), not ishtirak ma'nawi (conceptual ambiguity), where the same term can refer to a broader concept that includes both wujub and nadb (e.g., 'command' meaning 'demand'). Ishtirak ma'nawi is not as problematic. The response from al-Tahrir is that even ishtirak ma'nawi with respect to a more specific meaning (e.g., 'demand' being general and wujub being specific) is against the original principle, because specificity aids communication. So the proof stands: the command form must be for one specific meaning, and that is wujub. The absolute notion of 'preference and permission' is more general than the specific wujub, but the form is understood as a particular meaning.

Key Terms
الاشتراك (al-ishtirak): Linguistic or conceptual ambiguity where a word has more than one meaning. The text distinguishes lafdhi (verbal homonymy) from ma'nawi (conceptual sharing).
المعنى الأخص (al-ma'na al-akhass): The more specific meaning; in contrast to the more general. Wujub is akhass relative to the more general 'demand'.
الترجيح (al-tarjih): Preference; here, understanding that the commanded act has a preference (over not doing it). This is used to narrow down the possible meanings to wujub or nadb.
