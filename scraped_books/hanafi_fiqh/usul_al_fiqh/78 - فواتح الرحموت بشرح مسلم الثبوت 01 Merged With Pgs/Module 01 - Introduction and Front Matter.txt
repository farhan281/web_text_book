Introducing Fawatih al-Rahamut and Muslim al-Thubut
Book title, authors, and publisher

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the authors, titles, and significance of Fawatih al-Rahamut and Muslim al-Thubut within the Hanafi usul al-fiqh tradition.

Core Idea
Fawatih al-Rahamut is a sharh of Muslim al-Thubut, a key text in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

This lesson introduces two foundational works in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: Muslim al-Thubut by Muhibb Allah al-Bihari and its commentary Fawatih al-Rahamut by Abd al-Ali Lakhnawi. The title page tells us who wrote them and when, setting the stage for deeper study. Understanding the structure of such texts is the first step in navigating Hanafi legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the title and text type
Look at the main title 'فواتح الرحموت' and recognize it as a commentary (شرح). The base text is 'مسلم الثبوت'. Note the relationship: sharh explains the matn.
A sharh is a commentary that expands on the original text, often resolving ambiguities.
2
Identify the authors
The commentator is Abd al-Ali Lakhnawi (d. 1225 AH). The author of the matn is Muhibb Allah al-Bihari (d. 1119 AH). Learn their names and death dates to place them historically.
Both were prominent Hanafi scholars from the Indian subcontinent.
3
Recognize the disciplinary context
Muslim al-Thubut is a work on usul al-fiqh, not fiqh itself. This title page signals that the content deals with principles of jurisprudence.
Usul al-fiqh studies the sources and methods of deriving rulings, distinct from substantive fiqh.
4
Note publication details
The edition is published by Dar al-Kutub al-Ilmiyya in Beirut, edited by Abdullah Mahmud Muhammad. Understanding the edition helps in sourcing reliable texts.
Modern editions may include corrections and annotations; always verify the edition.
5
Appreciate the work's significance
Muslim al-Thubut is a standard reference in Hanafi usul, and Fawatih al-Rahamut is a highly regarded commentary. This combination is widely taught in traditional seminaries.
The commentary is known for its analytical depth and is a key text for advanced students.

The Nobility of Fiqh and Usul al-Fiqh: Reason and Revelation Combined
Opening praise and biographical introduction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state why the Hanafi tradition considers usul al-fiqh a noble science that combines reason and transmission, and identify the author of the base text from the biography.

Core Idea
Fiqh and usul al-fiqh are noblest because they merge pure reason with pure revelation.

The opening of Fawatih al-Rahmut presents usul al-fiqh as a science that uniquely integrates rational thought with transmitted texts. This lesson unpacks that claim and introduces the author of the base text, al-Bahari.

Guided Path
1
Understand the author's praise of intellect and knowledge
The introduction begins by praising Allah for making intellect and knowledge the most valuable assets. This sets the stage for the importance of these faculties in deriving fiqh.
Note how 'aql and 'ilm are placed at the center of the discourse.
2
Identify the claim about fiqh and usul
The text explicitly states that fiqh and its principles (usul) are among the noblest sciences because they combine pure reason (al-'aqli al-mahd) and pure transmission (al-naqli al-mahd).
This is the key epistemological statement of the introduction.
3
See the purpose of this combination
The combination leads to ijtihad in legislation, which is followed by those unable to derive rulings themselves, from common Muslims to scholars not reaching the rank of ijtihad.
Ijtihad is the practical outcome of the union of reason and revelation.
4
Recognize the book's structure
The author introduces the book as a commentary (sharh) on Musallam al-Thubut, and promises biographies of the original author and the commentator.
The biography of al-Bahari gives his name, lineage, and birthplace.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the book Fawatih al-Rahmut bi-Sharh Muslim al-Thubut. It begins with the basmala and a hamd (praise) that highlights the value of intellect and knowledge, specifically for deriving fiqh from the Quran and Sunnah. The author then states that fiqh and its principles (usul al-fiqh) are the noblest sciences because they combine rationality ('aqli) and revelation (naqli), leading to ijtihad. He introduces the book as a well-received commentary. Then he promises two biographies: first, of the author of the base text Muslim al-Thubut, al-Bahari, quoting from al-Qanuji's Abjad al-Ulum. The biography gives his name, lineage, birthplace, and early education. The passage ends with a footnote reference. This is typical scholarly front matter establishing the work's credentials.

Key Terms
الفقه (al-fiqh): The knowledge of practical shar'i rulings derived from detailed evidence; here described as a noble science combining reason and transmission.
أصول الفقه (usul al-fiqh): The principles of jurisprudence; here described as the science that, together with fiqh, is among the noblest sciences.
الاجتهاد (al-ijtihad): Independent legal reasoning; the result of combining fiqh and its principles, leading to legislation that must be followed by those incapable of derivation.

This preface to Fawatih al-Rahamut sets the stage for studying Muslim al-Thubut, a classic Hanafi usul text. In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful.
Introduction
Praise be to Allah who made intellect the most excellent of treasures and stores, and knowledge the most profitable of gains and trades; and He employed them both as a pleasing path for deriving the fiqh of His noble Book and the sunnah of His chosen Prophet among the earlier and later ones. And blessings and peace be upon Muhammad ibn Abdullah, His noble Messenger, possessor of virtues and distinctions, sent with his law to abrogate every preceding law, and upon his family and companions, possessors of ranks and noble deeds.
To proceed: Indeed, fiqh and its principles (usul) are among the noblest of sciences because they combine pure rational and pure transmitted knowledge; they take from this and take from that, resulting in ijtihad in legislation, which every Muslim who lacks the ability to derive rulings—whether laymen or elites who have not reached the rank of ijtihad—follows (taqlid).
This book that we place before the noble readers, Fawatih al-Rahamut bi-Sharh Muslim al-Thubut, is among the books that have received acceptance and admiration from scholars and students due to its smooth exposition and comprehensive coverage. We hope from Allah Almighty to accept our sincere intention in publishing this book with good acceptance.
Before commencing the book, we mention below two brief biographies of each: the author of Muslim al-Thubut and its commentator, the author of Fawatih al-Rahamut.
Biography of al-Bahari, author of Muslim al-Thubut
Al-Qanuji al-Bukhari translated him in his book Abjad al-Ulum, saying: “The judge Muhibbullah al-Bahari, attributed to Bihar (with a kasra on the b), a great city in eastern Purba, known in ancient times as al-Subah, then that name was applied to a well, and the two towns are connected.
The judge was born in a place called Karah, a dependency of Muhibb on Pur, which is a populated area of the outskirts of Bihar. The judge’s tribe is known as Malik. The judge traveled to the land of Purba and studied the beginning of the standard books in various places. Then he devoted himself entirely to the study circle of Qutb al-Shams Abadi, becoming a sea of sciences and a full moon among the stars. He traveled to the Deccan and accompanied Sultan Alamgir, who appointed him judge of Lucknow, then after a while judge of Hyderabad, which is the seat of the eastern provinces of the Deccan. Then he dismissed him, then ordered him to teach the son of his son, Rafi‘ al-Qadr ibn Muhammad Mu‘azzam. Then when Alamgir at the end of his life delegated the government of Kabul to his son Muhammad Mu‘azzam, titled Shah Alam, and he traveled with his son Rafi‘ al-Qadr from the Deccan to Kabul, the judge accompanied him. When Alamgir died in the Deccan in 1118 AH, and Shah Alam set out from Kabul to the Indian lands, he gave the judge a lofty position and appointed him as chief justice (sadarat) of all the kingdoms of India, and titled him Fadil Khan in 1119 AH. He died in that year.
Among his works are: Sullam al-Ulum on logic, Muslim al-Thubut on usul al-fiqh, and al-Jawhar al-Fard on the issue of the indivisible particle. These three are accepted and widespread in the schools of scholars.” End quote. Navigating the Structure of Muslim al-Thubut
Author's opening remarks and book outline

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the six major divisions of Muslim al-Thubut and explain the purpose of each division in the study of Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Muslim al-Thubut is structured into an introduction, three discourses, principles on objectives, and a conclusion — a roadmap for mastering usul.

In this lesson, we examine the opening of the great Hanafi usul work Muslim al-Thubut. The commentator, in his sharh, clarifies the book's organization, which includes a muqaddimah, three maqalat, a section on usul, and a khatimah. Understanding this structure is essential for navigating the text and for appreciating how classical usul works were designed to guide the student from foundational concepts to advanced derivation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the six parts
The commentator lists: introduction (muqaddimah), three discourses (maqalat) on theological, legal, and linguistic principles, a section on usul (principles) concerning the objectives (maqasid), and a conclusion (khatimah) on ijtihad and taqlid.
These parts are not merely organizational; each corresponds to a key stage in usul al-fiqh study.
2
Understand the purpose of each part
The introduction defines the science, its subject, and goal. The three discourses cover theological premises (e.g., divine attributes), legal principles (e.g., commands and prohibitions), and linguistic principles (e.g., general and specific terms). The usul section addresses the four primary sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas). The conclusion deals with the qualifications of a mujtahid and taqlid.
This sequencing reflects a logical progression from meta-theory to practical derivation.
3
Resolve the apparent contradiction
The phrasing 'usul fi al-maqasid' might imply that the usul are separate from the maqasid, but later the text says 'the usul are four'. The commentator offers two interpretations: either the word 'kalam' (discussion) is omitted, meaning 'a discussion on usul while they are the established maqasid', or the 'usul' here refer to the four main sources, and the division is actually four parts, not five.
This shows the commentator's careful harmonization, a hallmark of Hanafi scholarship.
4
Relate to the Hanafi usul tradition
This structure mirrors other Hanafi usul works (e.g., Usul al-Karkhi, Usul al-Pazdawi), which begin with definitions, then cover theological and linguistic foundations, then the sources, and finish with ijtihad. Knowing this helps you predict where to find specific rules.
For instance, the discussion on 'amr' (command) is found in the linguistic discourse, not the usul section.

The Subject and Purpose of Usul al-Fiqh: Evidences as the Core
Defining the subject and utility of usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the well-known Hanafi position on the subject-matter of usul al-fiqh and explain the incidental role of rulings within the science.

Core Idea
The true subject of usul al-fiqh is only the evidences; rulings are incidental.

This passage from Musallam al-Thubut defines the scope of usul al-fiqh. A key debate arises: should the subject include both rulings and evidences, or only the evidences? The author adopts the view that the subject is exclusively the evidences, and rulings are discussed only for illustration. Understanding this helps us focus on the primary object of usul: the proofs themselves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disagreement
Note that some scholars (e.g., Sadr al-Shari'ah and the Shafi'i author of al-Ahkam) consider both rulings and evidences as the subject because the science investigates their states.
2
State the well-known (mashhur) view
The author affirms that the subject is only the evidences; rulings are excluded from being the primary subject.
3
Explain the reasoning
Rulings are mentioned only istitradan (incidentally) for depiction and categorization, not as essential accidents. Every science includes digressions for completion.
4
Recognize the purpose (fa'idah)
The benefit of usul al-fiqh is knowledge of legal rulings from the evidences, which leads to eternal bliss.
