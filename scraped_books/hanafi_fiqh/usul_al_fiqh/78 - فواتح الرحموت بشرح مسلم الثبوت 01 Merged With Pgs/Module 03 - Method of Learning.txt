Definition and Subject of Usul al-Fiqh
Defining the science of Usul al-Fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the definition of Usul al-Fiqh as the science of principles of rulings (usul al-ahkam) and explain its role in achieving happiness through verification and moral formation.

Core Idea
Usul al-Fiqh is the science of principles that perfects the soul and matter, leading to happiness.

This passage opens the formal introduction to Usul al-Fiqh, presenting it not just as a methodology but as a path to spiritual and intellectual perfection. The commentator explains the theological foundation, the Prophet's comprehensive words, and the need for deep understanding (tafaqquh) to achieve the science's goals.

Guided Path
1
Locate the formal definition
Find the phrase 'علم أصول الأحكام' in the text and note how it is described as part of the greatest Islamic sciences.
The text mentions it among kalam and fiqh.
2
Understand the prerequisites
Identify the terms 'التحقق' (verification) and 'التخلق' (moral formation) and how they lead to perfection.
These are the means to perfect soul and matter.
3
Identify the disputed interpretation
Read the discussion on 'جوامع الكلم' and note the two views: comprehensive of divine attributes vs. comprehensive of rulings.
The well-known view among jurists and usul scholars is the latter.
4
Grasp the spiritual dimension
Understand how the science of usul leads to happiness (السعادة) through the perfection of soul and matter.
This elevates the discipline beyond mere methodology.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the formal introduction to the science of Usul al-Fiqh. The commentator starts with the basmalah and hamdalah, explaining them in a theological and linguistic manner, emphasizing Allah's reality and the metaphorical nature of all else. He then praises the Prophet Muhammad as the completer of morals and the one given comprehensive words. He mentions the difference among scholars regarding the interpretation of 'comprehensive words'; some say they cover all divine attributes, while the well-known view among jurists and usul scholars is that they are words comprehensive of all types of rulings. He then turns to his own work, referring to himself as 'al-Shakur' (the grateful one) and mentions his father's name and death date. He describes the path to happiness through perfection of soul and matter, achieved by verification (al-tahqiq) and moral formation (al-takhalluq), both of which come through deep understanding (tafaqquh) in religion and profound study. He states that the science of the principles of rulings (usul al-ahkam) is among the greatest Islamic sciences, along with kalam and fiqh. He asserts his own mastery of this science, claiming that no reality or subtlety was hidden from him, but then humbly notes that complete mastery is only for some mujtahids. This sets the stage for a detailed exposition of usul al-fiqh.

Key Terms
أصول الأحكام (usul al-ahkam): The science of the principles of legal rulings, identified as a part of Usul al-Fiqh.
جوامع الكلم (jawami' al-kalim): Comprehensive words; according to some verifiers, words covering all divine attributes; according to jurists and usul scholars, speech comprehensive of all types of rulings.
التفقه (al-tafaqquh): Deep understanding, specifically in religion, leading to perfection of soul and matter.

The Meanings of 'Asl' and the Definition of Usul al-Fiqh
Defining 'asl' and clarifying 'usul al-fiqh'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four technical meanings of 'asl' and explain why 'usul' in 'usul al-fiqh' refers to proofs (adilla) rather than rules (qawa'id).

Core Idea
In 'usul al-fiqh', 'asl' means proof, because the foundation of a science is its proof.

This passage from the commentary on Musallam al-Thubut unpacks the key term 'asl', which is central to understanding the discipline of usul al-fiqh. The author first outlines his comprehensive work, then delves into the linguistic and technical meanings of 'asl', showing how the compound 'usul al-fiqh' must be interpreted as 'the proofs of fiqh' rather than 'the rules of fiqh'. This distinction is crucial for correctly grasping the scope of the science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four technical meanings of 'asl'
Analyze the four meanings listed: preponderant (rajih), continued (mustashab), rule (qa'ida), and proof (dalil). For each, note the example given.
The passage provides clear examples: Book vs qiyas, purity of water, grammatical rule, and the verse 'aqimu al-salah' as proof.
2
Distinguish linguistic from technical meanings
Note that linguistically, 'asl' means 'that upon which something is built' (like clay for a pot). Technically, it is used as a homonym for four distinct concepts. Understand why the author insists these are technical, not merely metaphorical.
The author mentions the possibility of metaphor in some senses but affirms the technical homonymy.
3
Apply the rule for compound 'asl al-'ilm'
When 'asl' is added to a science (e.g., usul al-fiqh), it does not directly mean 'proof' by lexical transfer. Instead, 'asl' retains its linguistic meaning of 'foundation', and the foundation of a science is its proof. Thus 'usul al-fiqh' means 'the proofs of fiqh'.
This avoids the need for multiple transfers (naql) and aligns with how the commentator al-Mufid explained it.
4
Evaluate the alternative interpretation (usul = qawa'id)
The passage rejects interpreting 'usul' in the book's structure as 'rules' (qawa'id). Consider why this interpretation is flawed: it ignores the apparent meaning of the compound 'usul al-fiqh' in the definitional context.
The author is upholding a precise technical understanding against a looser one.

The Hanafi Defense of Definitions Against Tahsil al-Hasil
Defense of definitions against the charge of تحصيل الحاصل

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi response to the charge of tahsil al-hasil in definitions, distinguishing between summary and detailed knowledge.

Core Idea
Definitions do not repeat the known; they transform implicit summary into explicit detail.

Some object that definitions are futile because they merely state what is already known. This passage from Fawatih al-Rahmut presents the classic Hanafi response: the definiendum is known only in summary, while the definition unpacks it into detailed parts. Thus, the definition provides new knowledge. This defense is crucial for understanding the role of definitions in usul al-fiqh methodology.

1

The objection assumes that knowledge of the definiendum includes full knowledge of its parts.

The author accepts that the definition is composed of the parts of the definiendum. But he argues that the definiendum is known only in summary (ijmal), while the definition provides the same parts in detail (tafsil).
The key distinction: summary vs. detailed knowledge.

Since the detailed knowledge was not previously held, the definition genuinely provides new cognitive content. Hence, it is not tahsil al-hasil.

This shows the practical benefit of definitions in usul.

Does Speculation Yield Certain Knowledge? The Hanafi Refutation of Skepticism
Rational dispute on the epistemic value of speculation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that correct speculation yields necessary knowledge and identify the two responses used to refute the Samaniyya skeptics.

Core Idea
Correct speculation yields necessary knowledge; denying it leads to self-defeating skepticism.

In this lesson, we examine a foundational dispute in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: whether speculative reasoning (naẓar) can produce certain knowledge. The Samaniyya skeptics argued that since certainty may be ignorance, we can never be sure that speculation yields truth. The Hanafi response—using two distinct arguments—establishes that speculation is a valid source of knowledge. We will walk through the debate, the Hanafi proofs, and the commentator's refinement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim and the skeptic's objection
The text states that speculation yields necessary knowledge. The Samaniyya deny this, arguing that certainty after speculation may be ignorance (like knowledge initially). Understand the skeptic's challenge: how do we distinguish true knowledge from false conviction?
The skeptic's argument is based on the indistinguishability of knowledge and ignorance at first appearance.
2
Analyze the first Hanafi response (argument from symmetry)
The first response turns the skeptic's argument against itself: if certainty after speculation could be ignorance, the same applies to sense perception. Therefore, the skeptic must either accept both or reject both. This shows the skeptic's position is self-defeating.
This is an ad hominem argument in the logical sense—refuting by showing internal inconsistency.
3
Analyze the second Hanafi response (appeal to intuition)
The second response claims that unfalsifiable intuition (badiha) distinguishes knowledge from ignorance by its attributes. The commentator then objects: how do we know the speculation is correct? The skeptic's doubt about the correctness of speculation remains.
The commentator's critique targets the notion of 'correct speculation'—how can we be sure it is correct?
4
Follow the further discussion on the correctness of speculation
The text suggests that the correctness of speculation could be known necessarily or through premises known necessarily. The probability of error is blocked once necessity is established. The commentator dismisses this objection as weak.
The Hanafi position maintains that speculation can lead to certainty if its premises are certain.
5
Extract the final Hanafi resolution: denial of likeness
The ultimate answer is to deny that knowledge and ignorance are alike (tamāthul). They are two distinct types, though they may appear similar initially. This position is the Hanafi school's view, but the commentator notes that it does not fully resolve the skeptic's worry about initial indistinguishability.
The debate ends with a call for fairness and reflection, acknowledging the depth of the issue.

Four Views on Knowledge After Speculation
Presenting and comparing four views on knowledge after speculation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four views on how knowledge arises after speculation and explain why the Hanafi-Maturidi tradition prefers Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's position.

Core Idea
Knowledge after speculation is necessary by God's custom, not by human generation or emanation.

This lesson presents four theological views on the mechanism by which knowledge is produced after correct speculation (nazar). The issue is central to usul al-fiqh because it affects the epistemological certainty of rulings derived through ijtihad and qiyas. We will examine al-Ash'ari's 'by custom' (adah), the Mu'tazila's 'by generation' (tawlid), the philosophers' 'by preparation' (i'dad), and Fakhr al-Din al-Razi's refinement that knowledge is necessary after speculation by God's custom. The Hanafi-Maturidi tradition adopts al-Razi's view as closest to correctness.

Read the source passage and list the four positions: al-Ash'ari (by custom), Mu'tazila (by generation), philosophers (by preparation), and al-Razi (necessary by custom).

The author rejects Mu'tazilite tawlid as attributing creation to humans, and rejects philosophers' i'dad as implying necessity from a source other than God. Al-Ash'ari's view is accepted but refined by al-Razi.
The Hanafi response is based on tawhid and the scriptural principle that only Allah creates.

Al-Razi adds that knowledge is wajib after speculation by God's custom, meaning the constant conjunction is necessary by God's decree, not by nature. Speculation has no causal role; both speculation and knowledge are created by God.
This view is preferred because it affirms necessary relationship (luzum) similar to logical necessities.
5
Connect to usul al-fiqh
Reflect on the thamarah al-khilaf: if speculation cannot produce certain knowledge, then ijtihad is unreliable. The Hanafi position upholds the reliability of rational inference in deriving legal rulings.
This dispute underpins the validity of qiyas as a source of law.

Commentary
This passage (usul dispute) presents four theological views on how knowledge is produced after correct speculation (nazar). Al-Ash'ari holds that God creates knowledge after speculation by His custom ('adah) without necessity. The Mu'tazila hold that the speculator creates speculation, and knowledge is generated (tawlid) from it, which the author condemns as invalid. The philosophers (hukama) hold that speculation prepares (i'dad) the mind to receive knowledge from the Active Intellect necessarily. Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi, from the Ash'aris, modifies al-Ash'ari's view: knowledge is necessary (wajib) after speculation by God's custom, but speculation does not cause this; both are created by God. The author (the commentator) prefers al-Razi's view as closest to correctness, because it simply states a necessary relationship (luzum) similar to the necessity of an accident requiring a substance, or a whole requiring its greater part, or the first figure of a syllogism requiring its conclusion. This upholds the Ash'ari principle that God is the sole creator while also affirming the apparent necessary connection. It is the relied-upon position in Hanafi usul (which follows Maturidi theology, close to Ash'ari on this point).

Key Terms
العادة (al-'ādah): God's continuous habit of creating knowledge after speculation, without necessity.
التوليد (al-tawlīd): The Mu'tazilite idea that the speculator's act of speculation generates knowledge as its natural effect.
الإعداد (al-i'dād): The philosophers' view that speculation prepares the mind to receive knowledge from an external source necessarily.
الوجوب (al-wujūb): Necessity; in al-Razi's view, knowledge necessarily occurs after speculation by God's custom, not intrinsically.

The Principle of Impossibility of Preference Without a Cause (al-Tarjih bila Murajjih)
Rational proof for necessity of a cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that preference without a cause is impossible and apply it to demonstrate that every contingent event requires a preponderator.

Core Idea
Preferring one equal possibility over another without a cause is logically impossible and leads to denial of the Creator.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahmut presents a central rational proof in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: that preference (tarjih) without a preponderating cause (murajjih) is impossible. This principle underpins the proof of the existence of Allah as the Lawgiver and the necessity of a cause for all contingent events. In this lesson, you will analyze the argument step by step, understand the Hanafi position on divine will and choice, and see how this principle grounds the entire structure of Islamic legal epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim
The author argues that a preponderator (murajjih) is necessary for any occurrence that had equal possibility of existence and non-existence. Identify the key phrase: 'al-tarjih min ghayr murajjih batil'.
2
Examine the reductio arguments
The author presents several proofs: if no cause existed, the agent's indifference would entail no effect; if a cause existed but was indifferent, effect would still not occur; if one alternative is preponderant, the preponderating is for the preponderant. Trace each logical step.
3
Refute counterarguments
The author anticipates objections: e.g., that the cause could be an eternal will that chooses without a cause. Analyze his refutation showing this does not lead to absurdity but actually supports the necessity of will as the murajjih.
4
Establish the theological conclusion
The argument leads to the necessity of a necessary being (the Creator) and clarifies that divine will is the murajjih that specifies one of two equal possibilities.
5
Apply to Usul al-Fiqh
Understand that this principle is foundational for proving the existence of a Lawgiver whose commands and prohibitions have binding force, and that all rulings derive from a cause (the divine command).

Commentary
This passage is a rational proof establishing that preference without a cause is impossible, which is a fundamental principle in Usul al-Fiqh as it leads to proving the existence of a necessary being (God), the Lawgiver. Are Mutabaqa and Tadammun Significations Distinct?
Analyzing the relationship between two types of linguistic signification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that دلالة المطابقة and دلالة التضمن are distinct and cannot be united in a simple word, and evaluate the opposing argument using rational proof.

Core Idea
Two things cannot become one: mutabaqa and tadammun are distinct significations despite appearing unified initially.

In Hanafi usul, the way a word conveys meaning is crucial for deriving legal rulings. This passage tackles a foundational question: when a simple word is spoken, does it simultaneously indicate its whole meaning and its parts? Some scholars argue they are one until analysis; the Hanafi master here argues they are fundamentally distinct. We will follow his rational demonstration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of dalaalah
Define المطابقة and التضمن as complete and partial significations. Understand that they apply to simple (non-compound) words.
e.g., 'insan' indicates the whole human (mutabaqa) and the part 'animal' (tadammun).
2
State the opposing claim
Some scholars claim that in simple words, before mental analysis, المطابقة and التضمن are one and the same; the word indicates the whole in an aggregate manner that implicitly includes the parts.
The claim is that they are 'واحدة بالذات' (one in essence).
3
Analyze the critique
The author examines two interpretations of 'aggregate': if it means a real unity without multiplicity, it's an unproven claim; if it means a collection with a type of unity, the parts are still many, so no true union.
The author says: 'فإن أرادوا بالإجمال... فلا نسلم' and 'وإن أرادوا الصور الكثيرة... فلا يلزم منه المطلوب'.
4
Handle objections
The author addresses homonyms (e.g., 'bayt') and words for contraries (e.g., 'balqa') to show that the unity of signification fails in cases of multiple meanings or opposite meanings.
Homonyms have multiple conventions; contraries require different loci.
5
Finalize the Hanafi position
The author concludes with a rational argument: two things cannot become one without either remaining two (no union) or one perishing (no union). Therefore, المطابقة and التضمن are always distinct.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul al-fiqh dispute about the nature of linguistic signification (dalaalah), specifically the relationship between two types: دلالة المطابقة (whole meaning) and دلالة التضمن (partial meaning). The author, a Hanafi commentator, begins by praising Allah for creating languages and explaining that a word signifies by convention. He then states that in simple (non-compound) words, these two significations are essentially one until the mind analyzes the meaning; after analysis, they become distinct. However, he immediately raises an objection: some scholars (the 'they' referred to) claim that a simple word only indicates the whole in a general way, not the parts, so the two are not truly united. The author critiques this view, arguing that if by 'general' they mean a real unity without multiplicity, that's a mere claim; if they mean a collection with a type of unity, then the desired union is not proven. He then discusses homonyms (common words) and words for contraries, using examples like 'bayt' (house/verse) and 'balqa' (piebald). He explains that a word cannot be set for two contraries with one convention unless the locus differs. The discussion then shifts to an analogy with divine knowledge: some philosophers say God knows all things in a simple, all-encompassing knowledge. This is compared to the idea of the one image that can be analyzed into many realities. The author cites a marginal note that critiques this analogy, stating that intuition rejects the analysis of one image into distinct realities. He then presents a detailed refutation of the union of the two significations, concluding that it is absolutely impossible for two things to become one. The final position is that مطابقة and تضمن are distinct significations, and any claim of their unity is philosophically unsound. This passage is a typical Hanafi usul analysis, relying on rational argument (dalil aqli) and illustrating the school's methodological rigor in defining linguistic principles.

Key Terms
دلالة المطابقة (dalaalat al-mutabaqa): The signification of a word for its complete, entire meaning, e.g., the word 'human' signifies the whole human being.
دلالة التضمن (dalaalat al-tadammun): The signification of a word for a part of its meaning, e.g., 'human' implies 'animal' or 'rational' as parts.
لفظ مفرد (lafz mufrad): A simple word that is not compounded; it indicates a single concept without grammatical combination.
إجمال (ijmal): An aggregate or summary understanding of the whole without detailed analysis of parts.

Classification of Words in Hanafi Usul: Noun, Verb, Particle and Independence of Meaning
Definition and rational analysis of word classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul criterion of independence (istiqlal) to classify a word as a noun, verb, or particle, and explain why a verb cannot be a subject (mahkūm ʿalayhī) in its formal meaning.

Core Idea
A word's ability to be a subject or predicate depends on independent meaning; particles and verbs formally lack it.

In this lesson we examine the foundational linguistic classification used in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the tripartite division of the singular word. The key is independence of meaning: whether a word's meaning can stand alone without reference to another. Understanding this distinction is crucial for analyzing how words carry legal judgments and for correctly identifying subjects and predicates in legal texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The passage states that the singular word is either a noun, verb, or particle. Determine the basis for this division.
The basis is independence of meaning.
2
Define independence (istiqlal)
The author explains that independence means the meaning can be considered by itself, suitable to be judged upon (subject) or judged with (predicate).
Particles are non-independent; nouns are independent.
3
Analyze the verb's dual meaning
The verb has two meanings: formal (muṭābaqī) includes event, time, and relation; implicative (taḍammunī) is only the event. Independence differs for each.
Formal meaning is non-independent due to relation; implicative meaning is independent.
4
Apply to legal capacity (mahkūm ʿalayhi / mahkūm bihī)
A word can be subject only if fully independent. Thus the verb in formal meaning cannot be subject; but in implicative meaning it can be predicate.
The passage resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing these perspectives.
5
Address the objection about sentence predicates
The common saying that a sentence can be a predicate is by way of expansion (tawassuʿ). The author refutes figurative interpretations and affirms the verb's unified independent meaning in usage.
This reinforces the criterion: the verb as used conveys an overall independent meaning.

Also, the same reasoning could apply to compounds, but the condition for compound indication is knowing the coinage of its individual parts, not the compound itself. The passage then adds a 'benefit' (fā'idah) classifying coinage into two types: (1) specific coinage for a specific object (khāṣṣ li-khāṣṣ), where only one concept is intended without multiplicity, e.g., the proper name 'Zaid' or the common noun 'rajul' (man)—though the object may be particular or universal. (2) general coinage for a general object (ʿāmm li-ʿāmm), where the coinage is for a universal meaning. This classification is foundational for understanding how words relate to meanings in usul al-fiqh, especially for analyzing commands, prohibitions, and textual interpretation.

Key Terms
الوضع الخاص لموضوع له خاص (al-waḍʿ al-khāṣṣ li-mawḍūʿ lahu khāṣṣ): Coinage where a word is assigned to a specific meaning without considering multiple aspects
الوضع العام لموضوع له عام (al-waḍʿ al-ʿāmm li-mawḍūʿ lahu ʿāmm): Coinage where a word is assigned to a general, universal meaning
الإفادة (al-ifāda): Conveying a meaning not previously known to the listener
الإعادة (al-iʿāda): Repetition of a meaning already known but forgotten

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of النهي, identify its seven usages, and evaluate the dispute over its primary meaning.

Core Idea
Prohibition is a decisive demand to refrain, and its form primarily indicates prohibition but can convey six other meanings.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut presents the Hanafi definition of النهي (prohibition) as a foundational usul concept. It carefully unpacks each condition, addresses objections, and then catalogs the seven distinct meanings the prohibition form can convey in the Quran and Sunnah. Finally, it engages with the scholarly debate over whether the form's default indication is tahrim or karahah—a debate with far-reaching fiqhi implications.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and explanation of the legal term النهي (prohibition). It begins by defining النهي as 'اقتضاء كف عن فعل حتماً استعلاء' (a demand to refrain from an action decisively with superiority). Each condition is then analyzed: 'اقتضاء' indicates it is a demand, not mere advice; 'كف' specifies refraining; 'عن فعل' identifies the object; 'حتماً' adds decisiveness; 'استعلاء' means the demand is from a superior to an inferior. An objection is raised that the example 'كف عن الزنا' (refrain from Zina) is itself an imperative, not a prohibition. The commentator gives two responses: first, that 'كف' here is the action to be refrained from, not the prohibition itself; second, by considering the addition to 'كف' makes it an order, while the addition to 'الزنا' makes it a prohibition. The passage then follows definitions from other scholars, like al-Qadi's definition: a statement requiring obedience by refraining from the prohibited. It then lists seven meanings that the prohibition form (صيغة النهي) can convey: 1) تحريم (prohibition), e.g., 'لا تقتلوا أولادكم' (do not kill your children); 2) كراهة (dislike), e.g., 'لا يمسكن أحدكم ذكره' (none of you should touch his penis) in the context of prayer; 3) دعاء (supplication), e.g., 'لا تزغ قلوبنا' (do not cause our hearts to deviate); 4) إرشاد (guidance), e.g., 'لا تسألوا عن أشياء' (do not ask about things); 5) تحقير (humiliation), e.g., 'لا تمدن عينيك' (do not extend your eyes); 6) بيان العاقبة (statement of consequence), e.g., 'لا تحسبن الله غافلا' (do not think Allah is unaware); 7) يأس (despair), e.g., 'لا تعتذروا اليوم' (make no excuse today). Other meanings like التسوية (equality) and التهديد (threat) are also mentioned. Finally, it discusses the dispute about the primary meaning of the prohibition form: whether it is for تحريم (as most scholars hold), or for كراهة, or a homonym, etc., and notes that consensus holds it indicates prohibition after an obligation.

Key Terms
النهي (al-nahy): اقتضاء كف عن فعل حتماً استعلاء (a demand to refrain from an action decisively with superiority)
اقتضاء (iqtida'): طلب (demand) as opposed to mere recommendation
استعلاء (isti'la'): Being in a superior position, implying the speaker has authority
التحريم (al-tahrim): The meaning of prohibition (hazr) that the nahy form can convey, indicating the act is haram.
الكراهة (al-karahah): Dislike; a meaning of the nahy form indicating the act is makruh, not haram.
