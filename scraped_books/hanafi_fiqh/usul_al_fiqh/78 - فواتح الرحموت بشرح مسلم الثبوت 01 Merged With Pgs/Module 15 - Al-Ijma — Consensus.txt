Is Qada Obligated by a New Command or the Original Command?
Examining the nature of qada obligation in Hanafi usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi and Shafi'i/Maliki positions on the obligation of qada, and explain the Hanafi argument that the original command suffices.

Core Idea
Qada is obligated by the same command as ada; the original command extends beyond the time if missed.

This lesson examines a foundational usul dispute: when a Muslim misses an obligatory act within its time, does the obligation to make it up (qada) arise from a new, independent divine command, or does the original command itself continue to obligate? The Hanafi school, along with Hanbalis and Ahl al-Hadith, takes the latter view, while most Shafi'is and Malikis require a new command. We will explore the proofs from both sides and the Hanafi response.

Guided Path
1
Identify the competing positions
Read the first part of the passage to identify the two views: qada by a new command (جمهور الشافعية والمالكية وأبو اليسر) or qada by the same command as ada (الحنفية والحنابلة).
Note that 'some of us' refers to a minority Hanafi view.
2
Understand the Hanafi definition of 'ada'
The Hanafis clarify that 'ada' here means the act performed in the time, not the obligation of performance. This subtle distinction prevents a counterexample (sleeper, menstruating woman, traveler) from invalidating their position.
If 'ada' were the obligation, then qada would not be obligatory on those who never had the obligation (e.g., sleeper).
3
Examine the opposing proof (Thursday/Friday analogy)
The opposition argues that just as the fast of Thursday does not imply the fast of Friday, the original command cannot imply qada. The Hanafi response distinguishes textual implication (al-intizam al-lafzi) from the continuation of the same command.
The Hanafis do not claim textual implication; they claim the command itself remains binding after the time.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: under the Hanafi view, a person who misses a prayer automatically must make it up; under the opposing view, he would not be obliged unless a new command is found.
Consensus on qada for sleepers, travelers, and menstruating women supports the Hanafi view.
5
Determine the tarjih (preferred view)
The text states that the Hanafi position is al-mukhtar (chosen) by the generality of Hanafis, Hanbalis, and Ahl al-Hadith. The opposing minority view (Abu al-Yusr) is considered weak.
Later Hanafi authorities like Fakhr al-Islam and Shams al-A'imma affirmed the majority view.

Commentary
This passage discusses a fundamental usul dispute: Does the obligation to make up a missed obligatory act (qada) arise from a new, independent divine command, or from the same original command that made the act obligatory in its time? The majority of Shafi'is and Malikis, along with some Hanafis like Abu al-Yusr, hold the first view: that qada requires a new command. The chosen view (المختار) of the majority of Hanafis, including great imams and also Hanbalis and Ahl al-Hadith, is that qada is obliged by the same command as the original ada, but with a specific interpretation: 'ada' here does not mean the obligation of performance, but rather the act itself that is performed in the time, whether obligatory or not. Thus, the cause that obliges the act in time also obliges its performance outside the time (qada). The author clarifies that the command for qada is not a separate command but the same original command understood to extend beyond the time if the act is missed. The text then addresses the scope of the dispute: whether it is only about qada that is similar in nature (مثل معقول) or absolutely. The author favors the latter. An example is given: just as the obligation of fasting on Thursday does not imply an obligation to fast on Friday, similarly, the original command for an act does not automatically imply a duty to perform it later. However, the Hanafi response is that they do not claim a textual implication (انتظام لفظاً); rather, the original command itself, without a new command, suffices to obligate qada because the time was extended for performance and the command remains in force beyond the time.

Key Terms
القضاء (al-qada'): Performing an obligatory act after its designated time has passed, as a make-up.
الأداء (al-ada'): Performing an obligatory act within its designated time.
الانتظام اللفظي (al-intizam al-lafzi): Textual implication — that a text's wording directly implies a secondary ruling.

The Concession of Removal: Wiping Over Khuff and the Invalidation Dispute
Usul dispute on rukhsa classification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute over whether wiping over khuff is a rukhsa of removal, and evaluate the two positions regarding invalidation of mash when the foot is washed.

Core Idea
A rukhsa of removal means the original act is sinful; here the question is whether washing the foot while wearing khuff is even legislated.

The Hanafi school accepts wiping over khuff as a substitute for washing the feet, but a deeper usul question remains: does this concession remove the legislation of washing entirely? This passage presents an internal Hanafi dispute over whether washing the foot while wearing khuff invalidates the mash, and whether the omission qualifies as a rukhsa of removal (Rukhsat al-Isqat). Understanding this debate clarifies how Hanafi usuli scholars classify concessions and weigh conflicting evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
Recognize that the debate is whether the omission of washing the foot with the khuff is a rukhsa of removal (the fourth type of concession). This involves whether the original washing is unlegislated (ghayr mashru‘) and whether performing it would be sinful.
The passage frames this as: 'They said: The omission of washing the foot with the khuff is from the fourth category of concession.'
2
Understand the two conflicting positions
One position holds that washing is unlegislated because the khuff is a barrier to hadath; the other argues that washing is still legislated because if one washes, wudu is valid, and water entering the khuff invalidates the mash.
The first position is called the 'concession of removal' view; the second is the 'legislation still present' view.
3
Analyze the evidence for each side
First side: khuff prevents transmission of hadath, so washing is like washing the belly. Second side: narrations in reliable books (al-Zahiriyya) show that washing completes wudu and that water entry invalidates mash.
The second side also uses the fact that washing is not repeated after the period ends.
4
Examine the Hanafi response and counter-response
Some deny the authenticity of the invalidation narrations, citing fatwas of Imam Muhammad ibn al-Fadl and al-Mujtaba that say mash is not invalidated. The response also argues that even if washing occurs, the previous mash remains effective, similar to not repeating wudu after urination.
The author of the passage favors the stronger evidence of non-invalidation and questions the soundness of opposing narrations.
5
Determine the preferred view and its implications
The passage concludes that the narration about invalidation is weak and not acted upon. The preferred view is that washing the foot does not invalidate the mash, and the omission of washing is indeed a rukhsa of removal.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is whether a person who washes his foot while wearing khuff must repeat wudu or not.

Specifying the Hadith of Intentions: Validity or Reward?
Analyzing the need for taqdir in the hadith to determine whether it refers to validity or reward

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of takhsis by ijma' to interpret the hadith 'إنما الأعمال بالنيات' and distinguish between its implications for validity and reward.

Core Idea
The hadith 'إنما الأعمال بالنيات' refers to reward, not validity, as proven by ijma'.

The famous hadith 'Actions are but by intentions' is often cited to demand intention for every act. But the Hanafi school, using the tool of taqdir (specification) and the decisive proof of ijma' (consensus), shows that the hadith actually concerns reward in the Hereafter, not the worldly validity of acts. This distinction has major practical consequences: for example, wudu and ghusl performed without intention are valid but unrewarded. In this lesson, we will walk through the Hanafi argument, including the refutation of an objection from al-Talwih and the supporting evidence from the occasion of revelation (Hijrah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
Recognize that the hadith 'إنما الأعمال بالنيات' requires a specification (taqdir) of its implied meaning: either 'validity of deeds' or 'reward of deeds'. The Hanafi position is that it means reward.
The two possibilities stem from the general word 'الأعمال'.
2
Examine the Hanafi proof: ijma'
The Hanafis argue that ijma' (consensus) of the Ummah has established that intention is a condition for reward, not for validity. This consensus is transmitted by trustworthy narrators and is definitive.
ijma' is a qat'i (definitive) proof in Hanafi usul.
3
Analyze the objection from al-Talwih
An objection claims that the consensus on reward does not prove the hadith is about reward—it simply shows reward requires intention. The Hanafi response defends the consensus and argues that the context of the hadith (Hijrah) directly supports the reward interpretation.
The objection is methodological; the Hanafi answer uses both transmitted and rational evidence.
4
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if the hadith meant validity, then acts like wudu and ghusl without intention would be invalid. But according to Hanafis, they are valid. Thus, the hadith cannot be about validity.
This shows the real-world impact of usul analysis.
5
Understand the usul principle: takhsis by ijma'
The general ('amm) word 'الأعمال' is specified (takhsis) by the definitive proof of ijma'. This is a standard Hanafi method: a general text can be restricted by a stronger proof.
Without this specification, the hadith would contradict other proofs (e.g., ayat of wudu).

Ibn Abbas' Statement as Evidence for Abrogation
Using Ibn Abbas' statement as evidence for abrogation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Mufassilun's argument based on Ibn Abbas' statement and apply the Hanafi response of ta'wil to reconcile conflicting proofs.

Core Idea
Ibn Abbas' words 'take the latest' are not absolute; they are limited to texts that cannot be specified.

This lesson examines the third argument of the Mufassilun, who cite a statement of Ibn Abbas to support the view that a later general text abrogates an earlier specific one. The Hanafi response interprets the statement restrictively, preserving the principle of specification. We will walk through the dispute, the evidence, and the Hanafi critique.

Guided Path
1
State the Mufassilun's argument
Identify the claim: Ibn Abbas' statement 'We used to take the latest' proves that any later text abrogates an earlier one, including general abrogating specific.
The Mufassilun treat this as a general principle.
2
Analyze the evidence presented
The Mufassilun consider the statement as indicating consensus (ijma) and being like a marfu' report. Also, it is a linguistic principle that the earlier cannot specify the later.
They see it as a strong dalil.
3
Apply the Hanafi critique
The Hanafis respond by interpreting 'the latest' to apply only to texts that cannot be specified (e.g., texts that are not general). This reconciles with other proofs for specification.
The ta'wil preserves the validity of specifying general texts by earlier specific ones.
4
Understand the author's conclusion
The author dismisses the Mufassilun's evidence as flawed, arguing that no conflict exists because their dalil is already weak.
The Hanafi position remains intact without need for further reconciliation.
5
Identify the practical consequence
In cases where a later general text appears after an earlier specific one, the Hanafi view allows the specific to specify the general, while the Mufassilun would hold the general abrogates.
This thamarah al-khilaf affects rulings derived from such texts.

Ijma as Specifier: Conditions and Reality
Stating rule and verifying true nature of ijma as specifier

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule on when ijma specifies the Quran and Sunnah, and articulate why ijma is not a real specifier but an indicator of an underlying evidence.

Core Idea
Ijma specifies the Quran and Sunnah not independently but by revealing a pre-existing specifier.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut explores the Hanafi view on ijma (consensus) as a specifier (mukhassis) for general texts. The author first lays down the rule: only mashhur or mutawatir ijma can specify the Quran, while solitary ijma requires prior specification; ijma specifies absolute Sunnah. Then he deepens the discussion by revealing that ijma in itself is not a specifier—it merely indicates that there exists a decisive proof (even qiyas) that the community has implicitly accepted. The distinction between apparent and real specification is crucial for understanding Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
State the rule and its conditions
Identify the core claim: ijma (mashhur/mutawatir) specifies the Quran, but not solitary ijma unless the Quran was already specified by a qat'i evidence. For the Sunnah, ijma can specify absolutely.
The rule distinguishes between types of ijma (mashhur vs. wahid) and between sources (Quran vs. Sunnah).
2
Examine the supporting examples
Understand the two examples: (1) Half hadd for slave from qadhf – Quranic general verse specified by ijma. (2) Silent consensus on Zamzam water removal – hadith 'water is pure' specified by ijma.
These show practical applications of the rule.
3
Understand the verification (tahqiq)
The author clarifies: ijma is not a real specifier; it implies existence of a specifier, even if qiyas. The qiyas agreed upon is considered decisive.
This addresses the objection that qiyas is zanni – the answer is that agreed-upon qiyas attains qat'i status.
4
Reflect on the historical absence
Ijma was not considered during the Prophet's time because revelation was ongoing and his word was decisive. This does not invalidate ijma after him.
This explains why ijma cannot be an independent specifier during the prophetic era.

Commentary
This passage states a rule regarding ijma (consensus) as a specifier of general texts. The rule: Ijma that is mashhur (well-known) or mutawatir (massively transmitted) can specify the Quran. However, ijma that is solitary (i.e., based on the opinion of a minority) cannot specify the Quran unless the Quran has already been specified by a decisive proof (qat'i). In that case, such ijma becomes like a solitary report (khabar wahid). As for the Sunnah, if it consists of solitary reports (akhbar ahad), then ijma can specify it absolutely. The author gives two examples: (1) The reduction of the hadd for false accusation of adultery (qadhf) for a slave to half the punishment for a free person, because the Quranic verse (Surah al-Nur: 4) is general for both free and slave, but ijma specified it to half for slaves. (2) The silent consensus (ijma suktuti) on the removal of water from Zamzam when a Zanjī (a black person) fell into it, specifying the hadith "Water is pure, nothing makes it impure" — i.e., the water needed to be removed due to contamination. The author then clarifies that ijma is not a specifier in reality; rather, it implies the existence of a specifier (such as an evidence from the Quran or Sunnah) that was known to the consensus participants. Even if the specifier is an analogy (qiyas), if that analogy is agreed upon, it is considered decisive and can specify. The author notes that the writer of al-Mukhtasar (likely Ibn al-Hajib) considered the general of the Book to be uncertain (zanni), but the author suggests reflection. Finally, the author explains that ijma was not a specifier during the time of the Prophet because revelation was ongoing, and the Prophet's own statement was the decisive proof. The Companions did not act on their own opinions during that time, so no ijma existed during the prophetic era. This is the meaning of 'lack of consideration' at that time, not that ijma is inherently non-existent.

تخصيص (takhsis): Limiting the scope of a general text by an evidence.
مشهور (mashhur): Well-known report transmitted by at least three chains but not reaching tawatur.

Ijma' as Specifier: The Hanafi Position on Consensus and the Quran
Discussion of whether ijma' can specify the Quran

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule that ijma' can specify the Quran only if it is mashhur or mutawatir, and identify why ijma' is not a direct specifier but entails a specifier.

Core Idea
Ijma' is not a real specifier of the Quran; it indicates a pre-existing specifier.

This passage tackles a key usul question: Can consensus (ijma') specify a general Quranic verse? The Hanafi position distinguishes between types of ijma' and explains that ijma' is not an independent source of specification but points to a prior specifier. Understanding this clarifies the limits of consensus in legal interpretation and the ongoing debate with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its condition
The passage states that ijma' can specify the Quran, but only if it is mashhur or mutawatir; ahad ijma' (like khabar al-wahid) cannot. Note the examples given for specifying the sunnah.
This condition limits the scope of consensus as a specifier for the Quran.
2
Understand why ijma' cannot be a real specifier
The author argues ijma' is not a specifier in reality because it did not exist during the time of revelation. The Prophet's word was the ultimate authority, so early companions did not use ijma' as a source.
The key phrase: 'لعدم اعتباره زمن الوحي' (it was not considered during revelation).
3
Analyze the Shafi'i counter-argument
The Shafi'is allow delaying the specifier, meaning ijma' after revelation can be a specifier. The Hanafi response: delay is only allowed until the time of need, not absolutely.
This nuance refutes the objection without undermining the Hanafi stance.
4
Grasp the entailment theory
Ijma' is not a specifier per se, but it entails the existence of a specifier—like a definitive analogy (qiyas qati). The passage distinguishes specification (takhsis) from abrogation (naskh): ijma' can specify but not abrogate.
Abrogation by ijma' is not accepted because ijma' cannot abrogate a text; it only indicates an abrogator.
5
Evaluate the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
The Hanafi position means that when faced with a general Quranic verse and a later consensus, we must search for a pre-existing specifier (hadith, qiyas) that the consensus reflects, rather than treating consensus as an independent source.
Contrast with the Shafi'i view, which can accept consensus as a direct specifier.

Repetition of Conditional Commands (Takrur al-Mu'allaq)
Analyzing the rule of repetition for commands attached to conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between an 'illa and a shart in conditional commands, and apply the Hanafi rule that the conditional command repeats only when its condition is an 'illa.

Core Idea
A conditional command repeats only if its condition is a cause; mere conditions do not trigger repetition.

In this passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut, the author details a crucial Hanafi usul principle: when a command is attached to a condition (mu'allaq), does the command repeat each time the condition occurs? The Hanafi position is that repetition depends on whether the condition is a true cause ('illa) or merely a condition (shart). Causes necessitate their effects and thus lead to repetition; conditions do not. This distinction has direct practical implications, such as understanding why Hajj is obligatory only once in a lifetime despite repeated ability, while wudu is required each time one intends to pray.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its initial evidence
The author begins by stating that by induction (istiqra) from the commands of the Shari'ah, the conditional command is repeated with the repetition of its condition. Examples include wudu when standing for prayer, flogging for zina, and purification for janabah. Recognize these as the prima facie evidence for the rule.
These examples are later refined; not all conditions cause repetition.
2
Understand the refinement: 'illa vs. shart
The author then refines: repetition only occurs if the condition is a cause ('illa). If it is a mere condition (shart), repetition does not happen unless there is separate textual evidence. The key distinction: an 'illa necessitates its effect whenever it exists; a shart does not.
This is the core Hanafi contribution.
3
Apply the refinement to the case of Hajj
The author gives the example of Hajj attached to istita'ah (ability). Istita'ah is a shart, not an 'illa, so Hajj is not repeated annually. The Qur'anic command (3:97) indicates obligation only once. This demonstrates the practical consequence.
Other schools may differ; the Hanafi position is clear.
4
Analyze the objection from opponents
Opponents argue: if repetition occurs in the cause, then the condition is more deserving of repetition. The author presents their reasoning: the condition is the basis for the conditional, so if the cause triggers repetition, the condition should too. Understand this objection.
The opponents are unnamed but represent a possible alternative view.
5
Respond to objection and conclude
The Hanafi response: the cause necessitates by existence (bi-i'tibar al-wujud); whenever the cause exists, the effect exists. The condition, however, does not necessitate; it only brings about multiplicity when its essence (mahiyyah) changes, not by mere recurrence. Thus, repetition of the same condition does not entail repetition of the conditional. The hadith on cutting the hand for theft is a specific sunnah, not a general rule.
This distinction preserves consistency across Shari'ah.

Ijaz: Does Perfect Performance Preclude Qada?
Arguments on whether performing the commanded act as required obviates the need for qada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that perfect performance (ijzā') removes the requirer (iqtidā') and thus precludes qaḍā', and will be able to evaluate the rational proof and the Mu'tazilī objection.

Core Idea
Perfect performance extinguishes the demand – no qaḍā' can remain.

This passage examines a foundational usul rule: does performing an act exactly as commanded (with all conditions and pillars) automatically suffice and remove the need for a subsequent make-up (qaḍā')? The Hanafi school answers definitively yes, while the Mu'tazilī theologian 'Abd al-Jabbār argues it is possible for the liability to remain. Understanding this debate sharpens your ability to reason with qiyas and identify the implications of a command's nature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
Discern what ijzā' means in this context – the dropping of qaḍā' from liability – and note that the Hanafīs hold perfect performance entails it, while 'Abd al-Jabbār denies it.
The author first considers the dispute verbal but then insists it is meaningful.
2
Analyze the first Hanafī argument: qiyas with transactions
Follow the analogy: in debts and trusts, performance extinguishes the demand; the same applies to acts of worship because the nature of command (waḍ') is uniform.
The objector claims worship differs because its purpose is the act itself, not a one-time transfer. Reflect on this.
3
Analyze the second Hanafī argument: impossibility of knowing compliance
If performance did not drop qaḍā', the requirer would persist, so we could never know if compliance occurred – an absurdity by agreement.
This argument uses reductio ad absurdum.
4
Evaluate the Mu‘tazilī objection and the Hanafī response
'Abd al-Jabbār claims the Wise may leave liability occupied. The Hanafī response: language and custom show no requirer remains; the analogy holds.
The author attributes the opposing view to 'dominance of passion over reason', but later scholars treat it as a meaningful debate.
5
Apply the principle to a furū‘ case
Consider: if someone prays perfectly and then doubts, the Hanafī view says no qaḍā' is required; the Mu'tazila might allow it. This is the thamarah al-khilāf.
This practical consequence shows why the rule matters.

Thus the demand does not remain after performance because the nature (waḍ') of commands is one in all commands. And when the demand and requirer do not remain, there is nothing on the liability, so no qaḍā'. The objector may say: Granted that the natures of commands are one, but the non-remaining of the requirer in transactions is not due to their being commanded, but for an external reason: the intent there is the transfer of property once to restore his right, contrary to acts of worship, for the intent there is the act, so it may be demanded again after another. So reflect on it, for it is a place of reflection. And for us, secondly: If performing it as required did not necessitate the fall of qaḍā', compliance would never be known, which is invalid by agreement. As for the entailment, it is because its requirer remains after—after performance, otherwise there would be no place for demanding qaḍā' (since there is no dispute about the remaining of the requirer by another command, for it would be an independent obligation, not a qaḍā' of the first). And if the requirer remains, then it was not a performance as required, so it is not compliance. The opponent may argue that when there is non-performance as required, just as qaḍā' is obligatory by the first command, likewise at the time of performance...

Commentary
This passage (mas'ala) examines the principle of ijzā': whether performing a commanded act perfectly (with all conditions and pillars) automatically removes the need for any subsequent qaḍā' (make-up). The author distinguishes two interpretations of ijzā': as mere compliance (imtithāl), which is trivial, and as the dropping of qaḍā' from liability. The chosen Hanafi view is that perfect performance does entail the dropping of qaḍā', a position agreed upon by all uṣūliyyūn. The Mu'tazilī scholar 'Abd al-Jabbār dissented, arguing that even after performance, the liability could still require qaḍā'. The author first suggests the dispute might be verbal but then insists it is meaningful. He presents two Hanafi arguments: first, analogically from transactions (like debt repayment) where performance removes the demand; second, if qaḍā' were still possible, compliance could never be known (since the requirer would persist), leading to infinite regress. The objection that acts of worship differ because their purpose is the act itself is noted but left for reflection. The passage ends mid-argument.

Key Terms
الإجزاء (al-ijzā'): Sufficiency or completion: the quality of a performed act that fulfills the command and removes liability.
القضاء (al-qaḍā'): The make-up or re-performance of an act due to initial non-performance or deficiency.
الاقتضاء (al-iqtidā'): The requirer or demand inherent in a command; its existence implies an outstanding obligation.
