Tanqīḥ, Takhrīj, and Taḥqīq al-Manāṭ in Hanafi Usul
Defining three types of munāsaḥāt and Hanafi terminology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between tanqīḥ, takhrīj, and taḥqīq al-manāṭ, and explain the Hanafi position on each term, including the clarification regarding takhrīj.

Core Idea
Hanafis accept the concepts of tanqīḥ, takhrīj, and taḥqīq but did not coin the terms; the denial of takhrīj refers only to istihsān.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut clarifies the Hanafi stance on three key methods of identifying the legal cause ('illah): tanqīḥ al-manāṭ (refinement), takhrīj al-manāṭ (extraction), and taḥqīq al-manāṭ (verification). While Hanafis employ these methods, they did not coin the terms, and a common attribution that they reject takhrīj is shown to be a misunderstanding—they reject it only in the sense of pure conjecture (istihsān), not the systematic extraction of the 'illah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three methods
List tanqīḥ al-manāṭ (refinement by eliminating irrelevant attributes), takhrīj al-manāṭ (extracting the 'illah from a text), and taḥqīq al-manāṭ (verifying the 'illah's existence in particular instances).
These are the three types of munāsaḥāt discussed.
2
Understand the Hanafi stance on terminology
Note that Hanafis accept the underlying concepts of all three but did not coin the specific Arabic terms; they describe the processes procedurally.
This is a terminological difference, not a methodological rejection.
3
Analyze the tanqīḥ example from the Bedouin story
The passage uses the example of the Bedouin who broke his fast: eliminate irrelevant attributes (the person being a Bedouin woman, the locus being suitable for offense, the invalidator being intercourse) to arrive at the true 'illah: deliberately spoiling the fast of Ramadan.
This elimination of non-causative attributes is the essence of tanqīḥ.
4
Clarify the attribution about takhrīj
When some sources claim Hanafis deny takhrīj, it refers only to the sense of istihsān (juristic preference without clear textual basis), not the valid method of deriving an 'illah from text.
This resolves the apparent contradiction.

Commentary
This passage discusses the Hanafi perspective on the three types of munāsaḥāt: tanqīḥ al-manāṭ (refinement of the relevant ground), takhrīj al-manāṭ (extraction of the ground), and taḥqīq al-manāṭ (verification of the ground). The author explains that while Hanafis do not use these terms, they accept the underlying concepts. The example given is from the story of the Bedouin who broke his fast by intercourse: the Hanafis eliminate irrelevant attributes (like being a Bedouin) and focus on the core: intentionally spoiling the fast. This process is tanqīḥ al-manāṭ. The author also clarifies that when some Hanafis are said to deny takhrīj, it means they reject it only in the sense of istihsān (juristic preference), not the valid method of deriving 'illah. This highlights the Hanafi emphasis on methodical identification of the 'illah through elimination of irrelevant factors.

Key Terms
تنقيح المناط (tanqīḥ al-manāṭ): The process of eliminating irrelevant attributes from a textual indication to arrive at the precise 'illah.
تخريج المناط (takhrīj al-manāṭ): The process of extracting and defining the 'illah from among different possible attributes in a text.
تحقيق المناط (taḥqīq al-manāṭ): Verifying that the identified 'illah actually exists in a particular instance.

Key Takeaway
