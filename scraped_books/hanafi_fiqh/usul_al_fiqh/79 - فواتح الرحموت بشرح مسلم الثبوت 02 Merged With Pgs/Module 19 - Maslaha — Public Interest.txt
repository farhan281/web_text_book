The Five Daruriyyat and the Illah of Jihad
Lists the five universal necessities and their preservation methods

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five daruriyyat and explain how each is preserved, and evaluate the Hanafi position on the cause of jihad.

Core Idea
The five necessities are preserved by legal measures; the cause of jihad is hirabah, not kufr.

Maqasid al-Shari'ah classifies interests into three levels: daruriyyat (necessities), hajiyyat (needs), and tahsiniyyat (embellishments). This lesson focuses on the first and highest level – the five universal necessities that must be preserved in every legal system. We will examine each necessity, its specific preservation mechanism, and a critical dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is over the cause of jihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five daruriyyat
List the five necessities mentioned: religion, life, intellect, lineage, and property. These are considered essential in every religion.
2
Examine the preservation means
For each necessity, note the primary legal means of preservation: jihad for religion, qisas for life, hadd for intoxication for intellect, hadd for zina for lineage, and hadd for theft/hirabah for property.
Some means like qisas are not necessities themselves but stronger methods of preservation.
3
Analyze the dispute on jihad's 'illah
Hanafis hold that the cause ('illah) of jihad is hirabah (hostile aggression), not mere kufr (disbelief). Shafi'is argue kufr alone suffices. Trace the evidence: Hanafis point to the prohibition of killing non-combatants.
This dispute has practical consequences for the treatment of civilians in warfare.
4
Understand complements (mukammilat)
Complements are additional measures that support the preservation of necessities, like the hadd for small amounts of wine (which leads to intoxication) and prohibition of preludes to haram.
Complements are not necessities but reinforce them.

Commentary
This passage discusses the first category of maqasid: the daruriyyat (necessities). It lists the five universal necessities that are preserved in every religion: religion, life, intellect, lineage, and property. For each, it explains the means of preservation and notes differences. For religion, jihad is prescribed; the Hanafis say the cause is 'hirabah' (hostility) not mere disbelief, so non-combatants are not killed. For life, qisas is a deterrent but the Quran states it gives life; note that preservation of life is the necessity, while qisas is a means that is not necessary in all laws (e.g., not in Christianity). For intellect, the hadd for intoxication is to preserve the mind; wine was previously allowed, but in Islam it is prohibited even in small amounts. For lineage, the hadd for zina preserves lineage, and zina was never allowed. For property, the hadd for theft and highway robbery preserve property. Complements include the hadd for small amounts of wine because it leads to intoxication, and prohibition of preludes to impermissible acts, like in i'tikaf and hajj.

Key Terms
الضرورة (al-darurah): Necessities are those interests that reach the level of necessity, such that their absence would lead to chaos and destruction of order.
التعليل بالحرابة (al-ta'lil bi al-hirabah): Causing jihad by the 'illah of hirabah (hostile aggression) rather than mere kufr.
مكملات الضروريات (mukammilat al-daruriyyat): Complements of necessities: additional measures that support the preservation of the necessities, like the hadd for small amounts of wine.

The Three Categories of Maqasid: An Overview
Classifying Shari'ah objectives into necessity, need, and improvement.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three categories of Maqasid al-Shari'ah (daruriyyat, hajiyyat, tahsiniyyat) and explain the hierarchy and relationship between them.

Core Idea
Shari'ah objectives are classified into three levels: necessities, needs, and improvements — each with distinct legal consequences.

The scholars of usul al-fiqh have categorized the objectives of Islamic law to help understand the priorities in legislation. This lesson introduces the threefold classification: daruriyyat (necessities), hajiyyat (needs), and tahsiniyyat (improvements). Understanding this hierarchy is essential for deriving rulings that balance preservation of essential interests with ease and excellence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
From the passage, list the three Arabic terms and their English equivalents.
The categories are ضرورية, حاجية, تحسينية.
2
Define daruriyyat
Explain that these are essential for preservation of the five universals: religion, life, intellect, lineage, property.
The passage gives examples like jihad for religion, retribution for life.
3
Define hajiyyat
Explain that these are needed for ease and removal of hardship, but their absence does not cause loss of the five universals.
Examples: sale, lease, mudaraba.
4
Define tahsiniyyat
Explain that these are commended for moral excellence and beauty, such as prohibiting impurities.
They perfect character.
5
Summarize the hierarchy
State that the Lawgiver considers all three, but daruriyyat take precedence over hajiyyat, and hajiyyat over tahsiniyyat.
This priority matters when rulings conflict.

This passage is a detailed exposition of the three categories of Maqasid al-Shari'ah (objectives of Islamic law) as explained by Hanafi usul scholars. The author begins by stating that there are classifications from the perspective of objectives, their order, and the Lawgiver's consideration. The first classification divides objectives into three levels: daruriyyat (necessities), hajiyyat (needs), and tahsiniyyat (improvements). The first classification divides objectives into three levels: daruriyyat (necessities), hajiyyat (needs), and tahsiniyyat (improvements). Daruriyyat are those things that are essential for the preservation of the five fundamental universals (kulliyat khamsah): religion, life, intellect, lineage, and property. For each, the author provides examples and rulings, such as jihad for preservation of religion (with a note on the Hanafi view that the cause is hostility, not disbelief), retribution for life, penalty for intoxication for intellect, penalty for adultery for lineage, and penalty for theft and highway robbery for property. The second level is hajiyyat, which are needed for ease and removal of hardship but not to the extent of necessity; examples include contracts like sale and lease. The author notes that some particulars of these contracts may rise to necessity, e.g., hiring a wet nurse to save a child's life. The third level is tahsiniyyat, which are commendable and include prohibitions of impure things and removal of guardianship from slaves. The passage ends by noting that most issues in the book of istihsan are derived from these tahsiniyyat.

The Concept of Mukammilat (Completions) for the Three Categories of Masalih
Explaining the mukammilat (completion) for each category of maslaha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories of masalih and explain how mukammilat perfect each category, using examples from the passage.

Core Idea
Every category of maslaha has completions that safeguard its purpose, with exceptions based on textual evidence or the guardian's special status.

In this passage, the author completes the discussion of the three categories of interests (masalih) by explaining the rulings that perfect each category. These completions (mukammilat) are not separate categories but ancillary rules that protect the primary objective. We will examine examples from daruriyyat (necessities), hajiyyat (needs), and tahsiniyyat (improvements), and explore a notable internal Hanafi disagreement regarding the marriage of a minor daughter by her father.

Does Predominant Harm Invalidate Suitability?
Dispute on suitability invalidation by predominant harm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate whether a description's suitability (munasaba) for a ruling is invalidated by a predominant harm, and apply the Hanafi principle of multiplicity of aspects.

Core Idea
Suitability remains if the benefit and harm arise from different aspects—no contradiction.

This lesson addresses a key dispute in qiyas: when a suitable attribute (wasf munasib) also entails a harm that outweighs or equals the benefit, does it lose its suitability as an effective cause (illa)? The text presents the view of Imam al-Razi, which is adopted as the chosen view (al-mukhtar), that the suitability is not invalidated due to the multiplicity of aspects (ta'addud al-jiha). The opposing view of Ibn al-Hajib holds that it is invalidated because the overall harm predominates. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying qiyas when a description has mixed consequences.

The key concept is ta'addud al-jiha (multiplicity of aspects).
