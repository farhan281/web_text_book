The Four Fundamental Sources: Book, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas
Defining the four fundamental sources of Shari'ah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four fundamental sources of Shari'ah and classify each as revelation-based or non-revelation-based.

Core Idea
Legal proofs are either revelation (Quran & Sunnah) or non-revelation (Ijma' & Qiyas).

This lesson opens the study of usul al-fiqh by establishing the four pillars from which all Shari'ah rulings are derived. The author clarifies that the Quran and Sunnah are divine revelations, while Ijma' and Qiyas are human processes grounded in revelation. Understanding this classification is key to grasping the Hanafi hierarchy of sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four usul
From the text, list the four fundamental sources: Kitab (Quran), Sunnah, Ijma' (consensus), and Qiyas (analogy).
2
Classify each source as revelation or non-revelation
The author divides proofs into wahy (revelation) and ghayr wahy (non-revelation). The Quran is recited revelation; the Sunnah is unrecited revelation. Ijma' and Qiyas are non-revelation, but Ijma' is based on a prior proof, while Qiyas depends on an original case.
Revelation is exclusive to the first two; Ijma' and Qiyas are derivative.
3
Understand the role of Qiyas as a subsidiary source
Qiyas is not an absolute source; it requires an analogized-from (maqis alayh) to extract a ruling. Unlike the first three, it does not stand alone.
The text quotes Fakhr al-Islam: the real usul are three; Qiyas is the fourth only in a derived sense.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the sharh where the author sets out the four fundamentals of usul al-fiqh: the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. He begins by noting that legal proof is either revelation (wahy) or non-revelation. Revelation is only of two types: recited revelation (the Quran) and unrecited revelation (the Sunnah). Non-revelation proof is either the consensus of qualified mujtahids (Ijma') or analogical deduction (Qiyas). He clarifies that Qiyas is not an independent source like the first three; it derives its authority from them. He quotes Imam Fakhr al-Islam (a Hanafi authority) who states that the real usul are three, and Qiyas is derivative. Then the author addresses objections about other sources like the laws of previous prophets (shara'i' man qablana), istihsan (juristic preference), and istishab (presumption of continuity). He argues that all these are subsumed under the four: laws of previous prophets are only valid if mentioned in Quran or Sunnah; istihsan is actually a proof from one of the four; and istishab is also included. This establishes the Hanafi position that the four usul are comprehensive, and any other source is either a subset or a method of extracting rulings from them.

Key Terms
الكتاب (al-kitab): The Quran, the recited revelation.
السنة (al-sunnah): The non-recited revelation, i.e., the sayings, actions, and tacit approvals of the Prophet.
الإجماع (al-ijma'): Consensus of all qualified mujtahids of the ummah on a ruling.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical extension of a ruling from an original case to a new case due to shared 'illah.

The Position of Qiyas: An Auxiliary Source
Explaining the subsidiary status and limitations of Qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Qiyas is considered a subsidiary source in Hanafi usul and identify when it is applicable.

Core Idea
Qiyas yields probability, not certainty, and is only used when the first three sources offer no ruling.

Having established the four sources, the author delves into the nature of Qiyas. He emphasizes that Qiyas does not produce certainty and is thus inapplicable in creedal matters. Moreover, its authority is conditioned on the absence of a ruling from the Quran, Sunnah, or Ijma'. This lesson clarifies the epistemological status of Qiyas and its limitations.

Guided Path
1
Understand that Qiyas produces probability
The text states that Qiyas is 'maznun al-ifada' (probable in benefit). Most scholars agree it does not yield certainty, so it cannot be used in matters of creed (aqa'id).
Certainty is required for belief; probability is insufficient.
2
Recognize when Qiyas is applicable
Qiyas is only valid when there is no direct ruling from the Book, Sunnah, or Ijma'. It is not considered if any of these three are present. This is agreed upon by the four Imams.
The text says: 'la yu'tabar 'inda mu'aradati ahad min al-thalatha' (it is not considered when opposing any of the three).
3
Note the majority view on Qiyas
The author notes that some scholars, like Ibn 'Arabi, argued Qiyas should not be counted as a source. However, the Hanafi school maintains its necessity for new cases (nawazil).
The text calls it 'daruriyyan' (necessary) when the three are absent.

Subsuming Secondary Sources: Shara'i' Man Qablana, Istihsan, and Istishab
Answering objections about the comprehensiveness of the four usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi school subsumes laws of previous prophets, istihsan, and istishab under the four primary sources.

Core Idea
All secondary proofs are ultimately derived from or verified by the four usul.

The author anticipates an objection: if there are only four usul, what about other commonly cited proofs like the laws of previous prophets (shara'i' man qablana), juristic preference (istihsan), and presumption of continuity (istishab)? He responds by showing that each of these is actually subsumed under the four. This lesson demonstrates the comprehensiveness of the Hanafi framework.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three secondary sources questioned
The objector lists: shara'i' man qablana (laws of previous prophets), istihsan (juristic preference), and istishab (presumption of continuity).
2
Understand how shara'i' man qablana are subsumed under Quran and Sunnah
These laws are only binding if they are mentioned in the Quran or Sunnah, because we cannot rely on the transmission of those who claim to follow them. Thus they are subsumed under the first two sources.
The text says: 'la yu'tad biha illa idha qussafi kitab Allah aw sunnat Rasulih'.
3
Understand how istihsan is subsumed
Istihsan is not an independent source; it is a proof derived from the Book, Sunnah, Ijma', or hidden qiyas. Hence it is included within the four.
The text calls it 'dahl shari' min al-kitab aw al-sunnah aw al-ijma' aw al-qiyas al-khafi'.
4
Note that istishab is also subsumed
The text implies that istishab is similarly covered, as it is a principle of continuity that relies on the existing ruling from the four sources.
The author does not elaborate further.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the sharh where the author sets out the four fundamentals of usul al-fiqh: the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. He begins by noting that legal proof is either revelation (wahy) or non-revelation. Revelation is only of two types: recited revelation (the Quran) and unrecited revelation (the Sunnah). Non-revelation proof is either the consensus of qualified mujtahids (Ijma') or analogical deduction (Qiyas). He clarifies that Qiyas is not an independent source like the first three; it derives its authority from them. He quotes Imam Fakhr al-Islam (a Hanafi authority) who states that the real usul are three, and Qiyas is derivative. Then the author addresses objections about other sources like the laws of previous prophets (shara'i' man qablana), istihsan (juristic preference), and istishab (presumption of continuity). He argues that all these are subsumed under the four: laws of previous prophets are only valid if mentioned in Quran or Sunnah; istihsan is actually a proof from one of the four; and istishab is also included. This establishes the Hanafi position that the four usul are comprehensive, and any other source is either a subset or a method of extracting rulings from them.

Key Terms
شرائع من قبلنا (shara'i' man qablana): Religious laws revealed to previous prophets.
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): Juristic preference departing from a clear qiyas for a stronger proof.
الاستصحاب (al-istishab): Presumption of continuity of a ruling until evidence changes it.

Commentary
This passage is the table of contents for the second volume of 'Fawatih al-Rahmut bi-Sharh Muslim al-Thubut'. It lists the chapters and individual mas'alah (issues) covered in the book. The work begins with a chapter on Tarjih (preference among evidences), then proceeds to the third principle of Usul al-Fiqh: Ijma (consensus). Under Ijma, various issues are enumerated, such as the impossibility of consensus according to some sects, the definitiveness of consensus as proof, conditions regarding the participants (disbelievers, mujtahids, Companions, etc.), and the possibility of consensus based on analogy. The fourth principle is Qiyas (analogy), with chapters on conditions for qiyas, the 'illa (cause), and the Hanafi division of causes. The volume concludes with a discussion on Ijtihad and Taqlid. As a table of contents, this passage provides a structural overview but does not contain the detailed rulings themselves. It serves as a finding aid for the reader.

Key Terms
الإجماع (al-Ijma'): Consensus of the mujtahidun of the Muslim community on a religious ruling.
القياس (al-Qiyas): Analogical reasoning from an original case (asl) to a new case (far') based on a shared effective cause ('illa).
الاجتهاد (al-Ijtihad): Exerting utmost effort by a jurist to derive legal rulings from the sources.
الترجيح (al-Tarjih): Preferring one evidence over another when they conflict.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major topics covered in the second volume of Fawatih al-Rahamut and explain the logical progression from qiyas to ijtihad to taqlid.

Core Idea
The table of contents reveals the logical progression from theoretical qiyas to practical ijtihad and taqlid.

This lesson orients you to the second volume of Fawatih al-Rahamut, a major Hanafi commentary on Musallam al-Thubut. The table of contents lists the key topics in usul al-fiqh, organized from the theory of qiyas (analogy) to the rules of ijtihad (independent reasoning) and taqlid (following a mujtahid). Understanding this structure helps you see how Hanafi jurists build their methodology from foundational principles to applied practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three main sections
Scan the entries for the three overarching themes: qiyas (قياس), ijtihad (اجتهاد), and taqlid (تقليد). Notice that qiyas comes first, followed by ijtihad, then taqlid.
These correspond to the three major parts of the text.
2
Analyze the sub-topics under each section
Under qiyas, look for issues on the cause (علة), the obligation of qiyas, its application to hudud and shurut, and analogical preferences. Under ijtihad, note discussions on its divisibility, permissibility for the Prophet, and the status of the mujtahid. Under taqlid, find the permissibility of following a deceased mujtahid and conditions for fatwa.
This shows the detailed coverage of each theme.
3
Recognize structural elements
Note the 'Supplement' (تتمة) on Hanafi classification of the cause, and the 'Conclusion' (خاتمة) on ijtihad. These are organizational markers that frame the main content.
The author uses these to separate special topics from core discussions.

Commentary
This passage is the table of contents (فهرس المحتويات) of the second volume of 'Fawatih al-Rahamut' by Muhammad Abd al-Halim al-Laknawi, a commentary on 'Musallam al-Thubut' of Muhibballah al-Bihari. It lists the main topics discussed in the usul al-fiqh text, covering: causes (علة) and their suitability, the Hanafi classification of causes; analogy (قياس) including its obligation, extension, and application to hudud and shurut; ijtihad, its divisibility, permissibility for the Prophet, and the status of the mujtahid; and taqlid, including its permissibility in legal matters versus intellectual ones, following the deceased, and the conditions of fatwa. This overview shows the structure of a typical Hanafi usul work, moving from theoretical principles of qiyas to practical issues of ijtihad and taqlid.

Key Terms
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogy in Islamic law; extending a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a shared effective cause (علة).
الاجتهاد (al-ijtihad): The exertion of mental effort by a jurist to derive a legal ruling from the sources.
التقليد (al-taqlid): Following the opinion of another jurist without demanding proof.
