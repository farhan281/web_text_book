Tafwid: The Invalidity of Delegating Arbitrary Judgment
Examines the validity of tafwid in light of rational and textual proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on tafwid to distinguish valid delegation from invalid discretionary judgment, and evaluate the rational and textual proofs presented.

Core Idea
Tafwid — delegating arbitrary judgment — is invalid because divine commands are objective, not subject to personal whim.

When a scholar is told 'judge as you wish, it is correct,' is that permissible? This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut explores the issue of tafwid, clarifying the Hanafi stance that such arbitrary delegation is neither rationally sound nor practically possible. The discussion involves rational arguments about objective good and evil, and a textual rebuttal using the example of Prophet Ya'qub.

Guided Path
1
Define Tafwid
Identify the precise meaning of tafwid in this passage: saying to a scholar or mujtahid 'judge as you wish, it is correct.' This implies that any judgment they give is automatically valid regardless of its basis.
Tafwid is not simply delegation of authority, but delegation of absolute discretion without regard to objective correctness.
2
Identify the Hanafi Position
State the Hanafi position clearly: tafwid is invalid both intellectually (عقلاً) and in practice (وقوعاً). This is attributed to Abu Bakr al-Jassas and declared 'al-haqq' (the truth).
The Hanafi view is contrasted with the permissibility held by most Shafi'is and Malikis.
3
Examine the Rational Proof
Follow the argument that good and evil are objective (حسن وقبح عقليان). Judgment must conform to reality, so there is no room for arbitrary choice. If a scholar judges something, it must be because it is actually good or evil, not because of personal preference.
This rational premise is foundational in Hanafi usul.
4
Analyze the Textual Proof (Quran 3:93)
Consider the verse about Prophet Ya'qub prohibiting something on himself. Opponents argue this shows tafwid. The Hanafi response: Ya'qub acted on ijtihad (conjectural evidence), not arbitrary choice. Thus the verse does not support tafwid.
The Hanafi response emphasizes that even prophets base their rulings on evidence, not whim.
5
Address the Opposing View and Conclude
Summarize the opposing schools' arguments and the Hanafi rebuttals. Conclude why the Hanafi position is chosen (المختار).
The practical consequence: if a ruler delegates judgment in this way, the delegation is invalid.

Commentary
This passage addresses the validity of 'tafwid', where a scholar or mujtahid is told 'judge as you wish, it is correct'. The Hanafi position, following Abu Bakr al-Jassas, is that it is invalid (both rationally and in practice). Rational good and evil are objective; judgment must conform to them, so arbitrary choice has no place. Practically, since every Muslim is obliged to follow ijtihad or taqlid, there is no room for tafwid. The text discusses a possible proof from the Quran about Prophet Ya'qub (Israel) prohibiting something for himself, but the author argues this was based on ijtihad, not arbitrary tafwid.

Key Terms
التفويض (al-tafwīḍ): Saying to a scholar or mujtahid: 'Judge as you wish, it is correct' — delegation of absolute discretionary judgment.

Conditions for Valid Istifta': The Mufti Whose Ijtihad Is Unknown
Mas'ala on agreement and disagreement regarding permissibility of seeking fatwa from a mufti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that ijtihad is a necessary condition for a mufti and evaluate the opposing view regarding unknown ijtihad.

Core Idea
Ijtihad is rarer than red sulfur; a mufti whose ijtihad is unknown cannot be followed, even if he is just.

This lesson examines a critical rule in Hanafi usul: the conditions for seeking a fatwa. While there is agreement on clear cases, the dispute arises when a mufti's ijtihad is unknown but his justice is assured. The chosen Hanafi position relies on a probabilistic maxim: presumption follows abundance. Understanding this rule helps you assess the validity of a fatwa in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the agreed cases
State the two cases where seeking a fatwa is agreed permissible (known ijtihad and justice) and the two where it is agreed prohibited (known absence of either, or both unknown).
These form the baseline for the dispute.
2
Isolate the disputed case
Focus on the scenario where the mufti's ijtihad is unknown but his justice is known (or presumed). This is the core of the mas'ala.
The question is whether taqlid is allowed in this case.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
Understand the rarity argument: ijtihad is rarer than red sulfur (al-kibrit al-ahmar), so its absence is abundant; presumption follows abundance, thus ijtihad is presumed absent.
This is based on the maxim 'al-thann tabi' li al-kathrah'.
4
Examine the counterargument and response
The opposing view argues by analogy: if unknown ijtihad prohibits, then unknown justice should also prohibit, but the latter is allowed. The Hanafi response: justice is common among mujtahids, so its absence is not presumed.
The difference in frequency breaks the analogy.
5
Understand the ruling on claiming ijtihad
Note the added issue: if a just person claims to be a mujtahid, the dominant view accepts his claim (similar to a Companion's claim). This is separate from the previous case.
The claim is accepted unless evidence contradicts.

And there is agreement on its prohibition if one supposes the absence of either [ijtihad or justice]. As for when one supposes lack of ijtihad, it is because it is a supposition of ignorance. [They argue that] ijtihad, due to the abundance of its principles, is rarer than red sulfur; thus its non-existence is abundant, and presumption follows abundance, so it is presumed absent. Thus the condition is not fulfilled according to those who permit. They said: If taqlid were prohibited in [the case of unknown knowledge but known justice], then taqlid would also be prohibited in the opposite [i.e., unknown justice but known knowledge], yet it is agreed to be permissible. Commentary
This mas'ala examines the conditions for validly seeking a fatwa (istifta'). There is consensus that one may ask a mufti whose ijtihad and justice (adālah) are known, and that it is prohibited if either is known absent. The disputed case is when the mufti's ijtihad is unknown but his justice is presumed. The chosen Hanafi view is that it is prohibited to follow or ask such a person, because ijtihad is extremely rare (like red sulfur), so the default is its absence. However, some weak opinions allow it. An objection is raised: if taqlid were prohibited when ijtihad is unknown but justice is known, then by the same logic it should be prohibited when justice is unknown but ijtihad is known, yet the latter is agreed to be permissible. The response is that justice is common among mujtahids, so ignorance does not harm, but ijtihad is rare. Finally, the text discusses whether a just person's claim to be a mujtahid is accepted. The dominant view is yes, similar to a Companion's claim of companionship, though there is a concern about claiming a high rank.

Key Terms
استفتاء (istiftā’): The act of seeking a fatwa (legal ruling) from a qualified mufti.
عدالة (‘adālah): Moral probity; a quality that makes a person trustworthy in reporting and giving fatwa.
الكبريت الأحمر (al-kibrīt al-aḥmar): Red sulfur, a metaphorical expression for extreme rarity.
