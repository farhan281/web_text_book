Hanafi Classification of Textual Clarity: Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, Muhkam
Defining the four levels of textual clarity in Hanafi usul: zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four Hanafi categories of textual clarity (ẓāhir, naṣṣ, mufassar, muḥkam) and apply the hierarchy in case of conflict.

Core Idea
The four categories form a hierarchy; the stronger (naṣṣ over ẓāhir, muḥkam over mufassar) prevails.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, not all texts carry the same weight. The school developed a systematic classification of textual clarity, ranging from the ambiguous to the absolutely certain. This lesson unpacks the foundational categories of ẓāhir, naṣṣ, mufassar, and muḥkam, and shows how they determine the strength of a legal evidence.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish ẓāhir and naṣṣ by primary intention
Ẓāhir has an apparent meaning but is not primarily intended; naṣṣ is primarily intended for that meaning and admits takhṣīṣ and ta’wīl.
2
Identify mufassar and muḥkam by admission of takhṣīṣ, ta’wīl, and naskh
Mufassar does not admit takhṣīṣ or ta’wīl but admits naskh; muḥkam admits none and is eternal.
3
Compare earlier and later Hanafi definitions
The later scholars (mutā’akhkhirūn) held categories as mutually exclusive; earlier (mutaqaddimūn) allowed overlap.
This affects whether a single text can be both ẓāhir and naṣṣ.
4
Apply the hierarchy to the Quranic example
Qur'an 4:24 is ẓāhir for permissibility of four wives; 4:3 is naṣṣ for the number; naṣṣ prevails, establishing the limit.
5
Consider the objection regarding mafhūm al-‘adad
Hanafis generally reject the concept of number (lā mafhūm li’l-‘adad), creating a tension—yet the naṣṣ classification is upheld by context.
This is a legitimate internal ikhtilaf.

Commentary
This passage is a foundational classification (taqsīm) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh distinguishing four categories of textual clarity: ẓāhir, naṣṣ, mufassar, muḥkam. The author presents the later Hanafi view (mutā’akhkhirūn). A text is ẓāhir if its meaning appears but is not primarily intended for that meaning. Naṣṣ is primarily intended but admits of takhsīṣ (specification) and ta’wīl (interpretation). Mufassar does not admit takhsīṣ or ta’wīl but does admit naskh (abrogation) in the Prophet’s time. Muḥkam admits none of these, implying eternal certainty. The passage then discusses additional definitions: some define naṣṣ as any transmitted text (sam‘ī), mufassar as any statement clarified by a qaṭ‘ī (certain) evidence, and mu’awwal as a probabilistic clarification. The earlier scholars (mutaqaddimūn) did not require mutual exclusivity. The key practical rule: in case of conflict, the stronger category prevails – naṣṣ over ẓāhir. The example from Qur’an 4:24 and 4:3 illustrates: the first verse (4:24) is ẓāhir for permitting marrying four, while the second (4:3) is naṣṣ for the number, so it is preferred despite the objection that we do not accept the concept of number (lā mafhūm li’l-‘adad ʿindanā). The passage ends with that objection, which is a reference to the Hanafi position that numbers do not imply exclusivity.

Key Terms
الظاهر (al-ẓāhir): A text whose meaning is apparent but not intended as the primary purpose; it admits of takhsīṣ and ta’wīl.
النص (al-naṣṣ): A text primarily intended for a meaning, but admits takhsīṣ and ta’wīl.
المفسر (al-mufassar): A text that does not admit takhsīṣ or ta’wīl but admits naskh during prophetic times.
المحكم (al-muḥkam): A text that does not admit takhsīṣ, ta’wīl, or naskh; the most certain category.

The Hanafi Textual Hierarchy: Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, Muhkam
Defining the four levels of textual clarity in Hanafi usul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the four levels of textual clarity (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) according to Hanafi usul, and distinguish between the later and early Hanafi positions on their definitions.

Core Idea
Zahir, nass, mufassar, and muhkam form a hierarchy of increasing clarity and strength in Hanafi usul.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut presents the Hanafi classification of texts based on their clarity and the possibility of specification, interpretation, and abrogation. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for resolving apparent conflicts between texts. We will explore each category and the differences between later and early Hanafi scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories and their definitions
Read the definitions of zahir, nass, mufassar, and muhkam as given in the source. Note the key conditions for each.
Zahir: not primary intended meaning. Nass: primary intended meaning, allows takhsis and ta'wil. Mufassar: primary intended meaning, no takhsis/ta'wil, allows naskh. Muhkam: primary, no takhsis/ta'wil/naskh.
2
Understand the conditions for each category
For nass, note that it allows takhsis and ta'wil as recessive possibilities. For mufassar, no takhsis/ta'wil but allows naskh. For muhkam, none of these are allowed.
The possibility of naskh was only during the Prophet's lifetime; after his death, all texts are muhkam in that sense.
3
Analyze the coexistence of categories in one text
The text states that every zahir has a nass with it, but not every nass has a zahir. This means a single text can be both zahir (in a secondary meaning) and nass (in its primary meaning) simultaneously.
This is the later scholars' view; early scholars did not insist on mutual exclusivity.
4
Contrast later and early Hanafi views
Later scholars define the categories as mutually exclusive in definition but coexisting in existence. Early scholars defined zahir as absolute clarity, nass as absolute expression, and mufassar as no ta'wil, without mentioning naskh.
The thamarah al-khilaf is how to classify texts with both primary and secondary meanings.
5
Apply the hierarchy to a Quranic example
The source uses Quran 4:24 (zahir for permissibility of more than four) and Quran 4:3 (nass for limitation to four). The nass takes precedence over the zahir.
This illustrates the principle that the later (stronger) category prevails over the earlier (weaker) in case of conflict.

The first principle: The Book. One of its meanings is not intended originally, so it is zahir. And if it is expressed (for itself and it is possible) along with the expression specification and interpretation, it is nass. And nass is also said of every transmitted thing, be it Book, Sunnah, or consensus. It may be restricted to the first two. And if it does not allow specification and interpretation while being expressed for a meaning (if it allows abrogation, it is mufassar. It is unambiguous, and therefore interpretation (by opinion) is prohibited, because opinion does not provide certainty (without ta'wil). That is, ta'wil by it is not prohibited. (And it is said) mufassar also means everything clarified by a certain proof. This includes the vague clarified. And by it, i.e., this terminology, what is clarified by a conjectural proof, whether a khabar wahid or qiyas or other conjectural matters, is mu'awwal in contrast. Imam Fakhr al-Islam interpreted mu'awwal as the common term that is preponderated by predominant opinion, and apparently that is another terminology. And it is said: His meaning, may Allah have mercy on him, is mu'awwal from the common. And it is said: The meaning of predominant opinion is what provides conjecture even if through a report. And what does not allow abrogation, while being expressed for a meaning not subject to ta'wil, is muhkam. The meaning of possibility of abrogation considered existent in mufassar and absent in muhkam is its possibility in the time of the Prophet, peace be upon him, and after him all is muhkam for others because the abrogator is only revelation, and its possibility ceased with the end of the life of the Seal of the Messengers. So the categories, as mentioned, are distinct; one does not apply to the other, but coexistence is not impossible in existence, because every zahir has a nass with it, since the intended meaning must be present, and not every nass has a zahir, because it may have no meaning other than the intended. This is the position of the later scholars. As for the early scholars, they did not consider distinctness; rather they took in zahir absolute clarity, whether with expression or not, and in nass absolute expression whether possible for ta'wil or not, and in mufassar absence of probability of ta'wil and silence about probability of naskh. Then the ruling of all is obligation to act with certainty and conviction, but in the first two with probability of ta'wil, preponderant that is severely preponderant or less, and in the last two with no possibility of diversion at all even if preponderant, and it is certainty in the specific sense. And that is what is meant in beliefs. And what occurs from the statements of some scholars, may Allah have mercy on them, that nass and zahir are conjectural in indication and mufassar and muhkam are certain, their meaning is conjecture in the general sense and certainty in the specific sense. Then the later is stronger than the former, and it is apparent from their meanings, so it is given precedence in case of conflict, due to the principle agreed upon of giving precedence to the stronger in action over the weaker. An example is His statement, the Exalted, 'And lawful to you are what is beyond that' (Quran 4:24). This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut explains the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity: zahir, nass, mufassar, and muhkam. The text begins by defining zahir as a meaning not intended primarily. If the text is explicit for itself (mashugh lahu bil-dhat) and allows specification and interpretation, it is nass. However, ta'wil (interpretation based on valid grounds) is not forbidden. The text then contrasts mufassar with muhkam, which is immune to abrogation. The passage also clarifies that these four categories are mutually exclusive in definition but can coexist in a single text: a zahir text always has an intended meaning (making it also nass in that aspect), but not every nass has a zahir meaning beyond its intended one. The author then distinguishes between the later scholars' position (insisting on mutual exclusivity) and the early scholars' view (which considered broader criteria). The ruling for all categories is obligation to act with certainty, but the strength of certainty varies. Later texts (mufassar and muhkam) are stronger than earlier ones (zahir and nass) in the hierarchy, so they take precedence in case of conflict. The example given is the apparent conflict between Quran 4:24 (permissibility of marrying beyond the prohibited categories) and Quran 4:3 (restriction to four). The former is zahir in permitting more than four, while the latter is nass on the number, so the latter prevails. The passage also addresses an objection that the second verse does not explicitly prohibit more than four since there is no concept of number in Hanafi usul. The commentary ends with a garbled continuation.

Key Terms
الظاهر (al-zahir): A textual meaning that is not the primary intended one but is still apparent from the wording.
النص (al-nass): A text that is expressed (مسوق) for a specific intended meaning and may allow specification (تخصيص) or interpretation (تأويل) as recessive possibilities.
المفسر (al-mufassar): A text that is expressed for a specific intended meaning and does not allow specification or interpretation, but may be abrogated.
المحكم (al-muhkam): A text expressed for a specific intended meaning that does not allow specification, interpretation, or abrogation.

Tarjih of the Closer Metaphor over the Farther
Explaining the preference for nearer metaphorical meaning over farther when texts conflict

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a text using a closer metaphorical meaning is given precedence over a text using a farther metaphorical meaning when they conflict, and explain the rationale.

Core Idea
A closer metaphor prevails over a farther one because it is stronger in immediate understanding.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, when two textual evidences conflict and both are interpreted metaphorically, the school prioritizes the metaphor with a closer relationship to the original literal meaning. This lesson examines the logic behind this preference, addresses a key objection, and clarifies the scope of the principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational principle
When two texts conflict and both are metaphorical, the closer metaphor (majāz aqrab) is preferred over the farther metaphor (majāz abʿad). This is a rule of tarjīḥ mentioned in both Hanafi and Shafi'i works.
The closer metaphor has a more direct semantic link to the literal meaning, e.g., 'lion' for a brave man.
2
Understand the key objection
Some scholars object: since both metaphors abandon the literal meaning and rely on contextual clues (qarāʾin), the indications are equal, so no preference should be given. This objection claims that closeness/distance does not affect strength of indication.
The objector focuses on the presence of clues, not the nature of the metaphorical relationship.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that the closer metaphor is stronger in understanding (aqwā fī al-fahm). This means the intended meaning is grasped more immediately, even if both metaphors have clues.
The strength lies in the directness of the relationship, not just the clue.
4
Evaluate the counter-response
The objector further argues that the farther metaphor, with its specific contextual clue, may hasten to the mind just as quickly as the closer one. The author acknowledges this but refines the scope of the principle.
The basis of speed is the clue; both metaphors could be equally immediate if clues are strong.
5
Refine the scope of the principle
The principle clearly applies when a single utterance carries both metaphorical possibilities with their clues. In such a case, the closer meaning is adopted. For two separate texts, the closeness still provides an advantage due to inherent strength of understanding.
The passage concludes that the closer metaphor is indeed preferred in the context of conflicting texts.

الأصل الثاني/ السنة أقرب يترجح على نص آخر مستعمل في مجاز أبعد إذا تعارضا، وهذا الترجيح أيضاً مذكور في كتب الشافعية ( لأنه) أي المجاز الأقرب أقوى في الفهم غالباً من المجاز الأبعد (فاندفع ما قيل إن المعنى (الحقيقي متروك في كل منهما بدليل و المعنى المجازي متعين في كل) منهما (بدليل) فدلالتهما على المعنى المجازي على السواء فلا أثر للقرب والبعد في قوة الدلالة وضعفها فلا وجه للترجيح وجه الدفع أن الأقرب أقوى في الفهم، وهذا الدفع مندفع، لأن المجاز الأبعد مع القرينة المعينة له يتسارع إليه عند سماع ذلك النص، مثل المسارعة إلى الأقرب، لأن مناط السرعة القرينة، وقد يقال : المراد بترجح المجاز الأقرب على الأبعد إذا احتملهما كلام واحد وتكون قرينتهما ،موجودة، فإنه يحمل على الأقرب، وهذا واضح، لكنه ليس له تعلق فيما نحن فيه فإن الكلام في تعارض النصين ومحصول ما ذكره هذا القائل أنه لا إجمال في لفظ احتمل لمعنيين مجازيين مع قرينة صارفة عن الحقيقة أحدهما أقرب والآخر أبعد، فإنه يترجح الأقرب
The second principle: A text using a closer metaphorical meaning is given precedence over another text using a farther metaphorical meaning when they conflict. This precedence is also mentioned in Shafi'ite books. Because the closer metaphor is generally stronger in understanding than the farther one. Thus it refutes what was said: 'The literal meaning is abandoned in both due to evidence, and the metaphorical meaning is determined in both due to evidence, so their indications on the metaphorical meaning are equal, and closeness/distance has no effect on strength or weakness of indication; hence no basis for preference.' The response is that the closer is stronger in understanding. But this response is itself refuted because the farther metaphor, with the specific contextual clue, hastens to the mind upon hearing that text just like the closer, because the basis of speed is the clue. It may be said: The intended meaning of the preference of the closer metaphor over the farther is when one speech carries both possibilities and their clues exist, then it is interpreted as the closer; this is clear but not relevant to our case, which concerns two conflicting texts. The core of what this objector said is that there is no ambiguity in a word that bears two metaphorical meanings with a clue turning away from the literal, one closer and one farther; then the closer is preferred.

Commentary
This passage discusses a principle of tarjih when two texts conflict, both being used metaphorically. The Hanafi position (aligned with Shafi'is) is that the closer metaphor is stronger. An objection claims equal strength, but the response highlights stronger understanding. A refined distinction clarifies that if a single speech carries both interpretations with clues, the closer is chosen; but for two separate texts, the closer metaphor still carries more weight due to immediacy of understanding.

Key Terms
المجاز الأقرب (al-majāz al-aqrab): The metaphorical usage with a close relationship to the original literal meaning (e.g., 'lion' for a brave man).
القرينة (al-qarīna): A contextual evidence indicating the intended meaning when the literal is not possible.

Key Takeaway
The Hanafi principle of tarjīḥ al-majāz al-aqrab establishes that when conflicting texts are both metaphorical, the metaphor with the closer relation to the literal meaning is given precedence due to its stronger immediate comprehension.
