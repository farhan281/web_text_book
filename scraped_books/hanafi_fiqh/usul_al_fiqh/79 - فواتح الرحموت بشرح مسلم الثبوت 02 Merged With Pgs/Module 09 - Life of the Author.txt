The Three Compilations of the Quran: A Historical Overview for Usul al-Fiqh
Narrating the three historical compilations of the Quran

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three historical compilations of the Quran, identify the reasons for each, and explain why these compilations uphold the principle of tawatur in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
The Quran was compiled three times, each ensuring its preservation through tawatur.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut narrates the three compilations of the Quran: during the Prophet's lifetime, under Abu Bakr, and under Uthman. Understanding these historical events is crucial for Hanafi usul al-fiqh because they establish the tawatur (continuous transmission) of the Quranic text, which is a foundational principle for the authority of the Quran as a source of law. The passage emphasizes that the compilations were done with the consensus of the Companions and based on the original recitation taught by the Prophet.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first compilation
Locate the description of the first compilation: it occurred during the Prophet's lifetime, with Zayd ibn Thabit and others writing the Quran on pieces of leather. This was the original arrangement.
The passage states that this compilation is the 'original' and what is meant by 'the compilation of Allah.'
2
Identify the second compilation
Find the cause of the second compilation: the death of many Huffaz (memorizers) at the Battle of Yamama prompted Abu Bakr to order a written compilation to preserve the Quran.
This compilation preserved the same surah arrangement as in the Prophet's time.
3
Identify the third compilation
Understand the third compilation: after Hudhayfa reported differences in recitation, Uthman ordered a committee to copy the standard mus-haf and send copies to the regions.
The passage emphasizes that this compilation was also based on the original and done with consensus.
4
Relate to tawatur
Note how each compilation relies on continuous transmission. The first was supervised by the Prophet; the second used that same text; the third standardized it with unanimous approval.
The citation of Ali's testimony highlights the consensus, reinforcing tawatur.
5
Apply to Hanafi usul
Recognize that this historical tawatur establishes the Quran's text as qat'i al-thubut (certain of transmission), a key principle for deriving rulings in Hanafi usul.
For example, any ruling from a clear Quranic verse is considered obligatory without doubt.

Commentary
This passage is a narration of the three compilations of the Quran, presented as a benefit (fa'ida) in the commentary. The first compilation occurred during the Prophet's lifetime, with Zayd ibn Thabit and others writing the Quran on pieces of leather. This was the original compilation, with verses arranged in surahs. The second compilation was initiated by Abu Bakr after the Battle of Yamama, where many Huffaz died, to preserve the Quran. This compilation preserved the same arrangement. The third compilation was ordered by 'Uthman after Hudhayfa reported concerning disagreements over recitation. 'Uthman commissioned a committee to copy the standard mus-haf and send copies to various regions. The passage emphasizes that these compilations were not arbitrary but based on tawatur and consensus of the Companions, refuting any suggestion of deficiency. The final exhortation to 'understand and affirm' underscores the importance of accepting this historical process as preserving the Quran's authenticity.

Key Terms
جمع القرآن (jamʿ al-Qurʾān): The compilation and arrangement of the Quranic text in written form.
تواتر (tawātur): Continuous transmission ensuring certainty; here applied to the Quran's compilation process.

Conditions for Accepting a Muzakki's Testimony in Jarh wa Ta'dil
Conditions for the validator (muzakki) in jarh wa ta'dil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions for a muzakki's testimony to be accepted and identify when bigotry invalidates it.

Core Idea
A muzakki must be impartial and expert; bigotry makes his testimony worthless.

In the science of jarh wa ta'dil, not every claim about a narrator is accepted. The validator (muzakki) must meet strict conditions to ensure objectivity. This lesson explores those conditions as outlined by Hanafi usul scholars, and highlights why bigotry (ta'assub) is a disqualifying factor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the role of the muzakki
The muzakki is the one who declares a narrator as 'adl or majruh. Understand that his testimony influences the acceptance of hadith.
2
List the required qualities
The text mentions: being 'adl, knowledgeable about causes of jarh and ta'dil, impartial, sincere, and not bigoted or self-conceited.
3
Define bigotry (ta'assub)
Partisan bias that prevents objective assessment. In the Hanafi school, it is a mani' that invalidates the muzakki's statement.
4
Apply the condition to a scenario
Consider a critic who attacks a scholar due to madhhab rivalry. Such criticism is rejected because it stems from ta'assub.

And the first of them among the boys was Ali, among women Khadija, among freed slaves Zayd ibn Haritha, and among slaves Bilal, for he believed while a slave then Abu Bakr (may Allah be pleased with him) bought him and freed him. The discussion remains as to who is the first among these. The majority hold that the first in faith was Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, and consensus was claimed on this. Muhammad ibn Ishaq, author of al-Maghazi, held that it was Umm al-Mu'minin Khadija, then Amir al-Mu'minin Ali. The first opinion is supported by what Muslim narrated from Amr ibn Utba that he asked the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings upon him and his Companions): 'Who is with you in this matter?' He said: 'A free man and a slave.' And it is narrated in al-Isti'ab from Ibn Abi Shayba: Ibn Abbas was asked: 'Who was the first to embrace Islam?' He said: 'Have you not heard Hassan's verses: And the first of them who believed in the messengers?' In it also: And it is narrated that the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings upon him and his Companions) said to Hassan: 'Did you say about Abu Bakr?' So he recited the verses, including this line, and the Prophet (peace and blessings upon him and his Companions) was pleased and said: 'You have done well, O Hassan.' And it is authentically reported that the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings upon him and his Companions) said: 'Let me be with my companion, for you said I lied and he said I told the truth.' And al-Bukhari narrated from Ammar ibn Yasir: 'I saw the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings upon him and his Companions) and with him were only five slaves, two women, and Abu Bakr.' This clearly indicates that he (Abu Bakr) was ahead in faith than Ali, contrary to what Ibn Ishaq claimed. The five slaves were Bilal, Zayd ibn Haritha, Amir ibn Fuhayra, Abu Fukayha, and Ubayd ibn Zayd. The two women were Umm al-Mu'minin Khadija and Umm Ayman (may Allah be pleased with them all). The second opinion is supported by what Muhammad ibn Ishaq said: And among Allah's blessings upon him was that he was under the guardianship of the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings upon him and his Companions) before Islam, because Quraysh suffered hardship, and Abu Talib had many dependents. So the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings upon him and his Companions) said to his uncle al-Abbas (who was better off): 'Your brother Abu Talib has many dependents, and people have suffered as you see. Let us go to him and lighten his load: I will take one of his sons, and you take one, to support them for him.' So they went to Abu Talib and told what they wanted. He said: 'If you leave me Aqil, do as you wish.' (And it is also said: Aqil and Talib.) So the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings upon him and his Companions) took Ali and kept him close, and al-Abbas took Ja'far and kept him close. Ali continued with the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings upon him and his Companions) until Allah sent him as a prophet, so he believed in him, affirmed him, and followed him. Ja'far remained with al-Abbas until he embraced Islam. Do not overlook that this does not indicate that he (Ali) was the first in faith, ahead of the Commander of the Faithful, the Greatest Siddiq. Moreover, this is from the annotations of Muhammad ibn Ishaq, so it is not a proof, especially when contradicted by what is in Sahih Muslim. It is also supported by what Muhammad ibn Ishaq narrated from Afif al-Kindi who said: 'Al-Abbas ibn Abd al-Muttalib was my friend, and he used to travel to Yemen to buy perfume and sell it during the season. While I was with al-Abbas at Mina, a man came to him, performed wudu carefully, then stood to pray. Then a woman came out, performed wudu, then stood to pray. Then a youth who had reached puberty came out, performed wudu, then stood beside him. I said: 'Woe to you, O Abbas, what is this religion?' He said: 'This is the religion of Muhammad ibn Abdullah, my nephew; he claims that Allah sent him as a messenger. And this nephew of mine, Ali ibn Abi Talib, has followed him in his religion, and this woman, his wife Khadija, has followed him in his religion.' Afif said after he embraced Islam and became firmly rooted in it: 'Would that I had been the fourth.' In another narration: al-Abbas said: 'And no one followed him in his affair except his wife and his cousin, and he claims that he will conquer the treasures of Chosroes.' Do not overlook that the evidence is only what al-Abbas narrated to Afif, and his narration was before he embraced Islam, while Islam is a condition for accepting narration at the time of testimony. So this hadith is nothing; it does not even reach the level of weak. However, there are other supporting indications, including what is mentioned in al-Isti'ab without a chain from Salman as a marfu' hadith: 'The first of this nation to arrive at the Basin will be the first among them to embrace Islam: Ali ibn Abi Talib.' And among them is the statement of Ibn Abbas: 'The first to pray with the Messenger of Allah (peace and blessings upon him and his Companions) after Khadija was Ali,' as mentioned in al-Isti'ab by the narration of Abu Dawud al-Tayalisi. But this is contradicted by what has passed as a proof. And some said: 'Abu Bakr was the first to manifest Islam, and Ali was the first to believe, but he did not manifest it before Abu Bakr out of fear of Abu Talib.' This is narrated from Muhammad ibn Ka'b al-Qurazi. And Allah knows best the states of His special servants. And the most prolific in hadith are Abu Hurayra, Umm al-Mu'minin Aisha, Abdullah ibn Umar ibn al-Khattab, and I think Abdullah ibn Amr ibn al-As, for he has more hadith than him, but writing does not accommodate it, and Abdullah ibn Abbas, Jabir, and Anas. This is as is not hidden from anyone who has investigated. So he does not establish.

Commentary
This passage is a historical analysis of who was the first to embrace Islam. The author presents the majority view that it was Abu Bakr al-Siddiq, supporting this with hadith from Sahih Muslim and Sahih al-Bukhari showing that Abu Bakr was present with the Prophet and a few others early on. The opposing view of Muhammad ibn Ishaq that Khadija or Ali preceded is addressed and countered with evidence about the reliability of reports and the condition that a narrator must be Muslim at the time of testimony. The author dismisses one report because the narrator (al-Abbas) was not yet Muslim when he related it. The passage also lists the most prolific hadith narrators among the Companions. Overall, the text demonstrates how historical claims are weighed using principles of hadith criticism.

Key Terms
الصحابي (al-Sahabi): A person who met the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) while believing in him and died as a Muslim.
الإجماع (ijma'): Consensus of scholars; here claimed on the priority of Abu Bakr's faith.
شرط قبول الرواية (shart qabul al-riwaya): Condition for accepting a narration: the narrator must be Muslim at the time of testimony (ada').
