The Hanafi Methodology on Taqlid of Companions
Note on the dynamic practice of Hanafi imams regarding Companion sayings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify cases where Abu Hanifa and his students followed or departed from Companion sayings and explain the usul principle behind their practice.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa revered Companion sayings but prioritized stronger evidence, sometimes departing from a Companion's view.

This passage clarifies a common misconception: the Hanafi imams did not uniformly accept or reject Companion sayings. Through two examples—salam and liability of a common hired worker—we see their method: sometimes they do taqlid, sometimes not, based on the strength of the evidence. Let's explore how this informs Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue: Taqlid of Companions in Hanafi practice
The passage begins by noting that no explicit narration from Abu Hanifa or his students exists on this point; their practice varied.
The phrase 'تنبيه' signals an important clarification.
2
Examine the first example: Salam contract and Ibn Umar's saying
In salam, they did not require informing the capital amount because pointing suffices, but they required it based on Ibn Umar's saying (also attributed to Umar). This shows they did taqlid in requiring the information, but not in the initial ruling.
They partially followed the Companion saying, not blindly.
3
Examine the second example: Liability of the common hired worker and Ali's saying
The two students (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) jointly imposed liability based on Ali's saying, while Abu Hanifa negated liability, treating the worker as a trustee. Here, one followed the Companion, the other did not.
This intra-Hanafi difference highlights the nuance.
4
Note the intra-Hanafi difference: Abu Hanifa vs. his students
The students followed Ali's saying; Abu Hanifa did not. The passage suggests that reason might be that Ali retracted or a stronger hadith existed.
Abu Hanifa's departure was based on evidence, not disregard.
5
Synthesize the principle: Priority of stronger evidence over mere taqlid
From the examples, the Hanafi method is: accept Companion sayings generally, but if stronger evidence (like Prophetic hadith, qiyas, or retraction) contradicts, they may depart. This balances respect with ijtihad.
This is consistent with Abu Hanifa's statement quoted later.

Commentary
This passage is a clarification (tanbih) about the practice of the Hanafi imams—Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad—regarding taqlid of Companions. It acknowledges that no explicit narration from them on this issue exists, but their actions show variation: sometimes they followed a Companion's saying, sometimes not. Two examples are given. First, in salam (forward sale), Abu Hanifa and his students did not require informing the buyer of the capital amount because pointing suffices, but they required it based on Ibn Umar's saying (and also Umar's), showing they did taqlid in one aspect but not in another. Second, in the liability of a common hired worker (ajir mushtarak), they collectively imposed liability for theft based on Ali's saying, but Abu Hanifa alone negated liability, treating the worker as a trustee. This shows that while Abu Hanifa generally respected Companion sayings, he could depart when he believed there was stronger evidence. The passage then quotes Ibn al-Mubarak reporting that Abu Hanifa explicitly stated he accepts whatever comes from the Prophet and the Companions. The apparent contradiction is resolved by noting that in cases where Abu Hanifa did not follow a Companion's saying, it was likely because the Companion himself retracted or because there was a stronger Prophetic hadith. Thus, the principle is that the Hanafi imams valued Companion sayings but gave priority to Prophetic texts and ijtihad.

Key Terms
تنبيه (tanbih): A note or clarification on a point that might be misunderstood.
الأجير المشترك (al-ajir al-mushtarak): A hired worker who serves multiple people or works generally, as opposed to a private hire.

This lesson addresses a practical question in taqlid: once a layperson receives a fatwa, does he need to re-consult if the same situation repeats? The answer is not unanimous; two positions exist. Understanding this prepares you for the nuances of following a madhhab.

Effect of Judicial Ruling on Personal Ijtihad
Explaining that qada does not change the actual hukm for a mujtahid or muqallid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a judicial ruling (qada) does not alter the actual divine ruling in the personal belief of a mujtahid, and apply the same principle to a muqallid whose imam changes his ijtihad.

Core Idea
A judge's ruling settles outward disputes but never changes what the mujtahid believes is halal or haram.

The relationship between judicial rulings and personal religious belief is a subtle but crucial point in Hanafi usul. This passage from 'Fawatih al-Rahamut' discusses the scope of qada: it resolves disagreements in the courtroom but leaves the mujtahid's inner conviction untouched. We will also explore how this principle extends to a muqallid whose imam has changed his ijtihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the main thesis
Read the opening statement: the judicial ruling does not make what was forbidden in belief permissible. The qada only removes conflict in external enforcement.
Note the phrase 'لا أنه يجعل ما كان في معتقده حراماً حلالاً'.
2
Examine Abu Hanifa's exception
Abu Hanifa held that rulings based on false witnesses are valid both externally and internally. The author says this is unrelated to the general principle.
This exception shows that the effect of qada can vary based on the type of evidence.
3
Analyze Abu Yusuf's position on al-batta
For a mujtahid who divorced with 'al-batta', the judge rules it revocable. Abu Yusuf says the mujtahid must follow his own belief (final separation).
The mujtahid's belief is certain; acting on the ruling would mean committing what he believes is forbidden.
4
Understand the rational evidence
The passage provides a rational proof: if the mujtahid acted on the ruling, he would commit a forbidden act in his belief. Hence he must follow his ijtihad.
This is a talil (rational evidence) based on the principle of avoiding sin.
5
Apply to a muqallid
When a muqallid's imam retracts a fatwa, there is disagreement: some say he must follow the new view, others let him stay on the old unless a court ruling intervenes.
The muqallid has no personal belief; his knowledge is based on the imam's fatwa.

Definition and Rulings of Taqlid
Defining taqlid and affirming validity of faith through taqlid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of taqlid and explain why faith through taqlid is valid according to the four Imams.

Core Idea
Taqlid is acting on another's statement without proof; faith via taqlid is valid.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut defines taqlid and clarifies its scope. It distinguishes legitimate following of proofs from blind imitation. The text then addresses a key debate: is faith obtained through taqlid valid? The Hanafi position, supported by the four Imams, affirms it, contrary to the Ash'ari requirement of rational reflection.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition
Identify taqlid as 'acting on the statement of another without a proof related to the action.' Understand that 'proof' means a proof from the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, or Qiyas.
2
Distinguish taqlid from legitimate following
Reverting to the Prophet or to Ijma is not taqlid, as it is reverting to evidence. Similarly, a judge or mufti acting on a textual obligation is not taqlid in that act; it is acting by evidence.
The layperson's action after taking from a mufti is considered taqlid by custom.
3
Identify the custom
Recognize that 'custom indicates the layman is a muqallid of the mujtahid by reverting to him.' This is the conventional usage.
4
Analyze the faith debate
Note the Hanafi position: faith through taqlid is valid and the muqallid is a believer. Contrast with Ash'ari: faith requires rational reflection (nazar); taqlid is insufficient.
The text states this is the correct madhhab according to the four Imams and many theologians.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response
Reflection (nazar) is a means to faith, not an end. Once faith is obtained, the obligation to reflect drops. Sins for leaving reflection are not established from the Salaf. Analogous to jihad becoming unnecessary when all disbelieve.
This is a qiyas from the principle that obligations contingent on a cause drop when the cause is fulfilled.

The Obligation of Taqlid for the Non-Mujtahid
Argument establishing taqlid obligation for non-mujtahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a non-mujtahid must follow a mujtahid without requiring knowledge of the proof.

Core Idea
Taqlid is wajib for a non-mujtahid; understanding the proof is not required.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut addresses a fundamental question in usul al-fiqh: must a scholar who is not an absolute mujtahid understand the proof behind a fatwa, or is blind following sufficient? The Hanafi school, relying on the practice of the Companions and the Quranic command to 'ask the people of remembrance', holds that simple questioning and following are enough. This lesson unpacks the ruling, its two variants based on the theory of partial competence (tajazzi), and the rebuttal of objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject and the ruling
Who is addressed? 'Non-absolute mujtahid' – a scholar who can perform ijtihad only in some areas or not at all. The ruling: taqlid is obligatory (wajib).
Note the terminology: 'mujtahid mutlaq' vs 'non-mujtahid mutlaq'.
2
Distinguish the two views on partial competence (tajazzi)
If tajazzi is accepted, taqlid is only needed for matters the person cannot derive. If tajazzi is rejected, taqlid is required in all matters. The chosen view (al-mukhtar) is the former.
The author states 'the truth is the first'.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The proof has two parts: (a) the practice (sunnah 'amaliyya) of the Companions who gave fatwas without proofs and were followed without objection; (b) the Quranic verse 16:43, 'Ask the people of remembrance if you do not know.' The command is general and unconditional regarding presenting proof.
The verse's dalala is zanni (speculative) but sufficient as proof for a rule that is not certain.
4
Examine the objection and the Hanafi response
Objection: Without knowing the proof, the follower might follow error. Response: Even if the proof is presented, the follower's understanding is still speculative (zanni), so the risk is the same. The mufti himself is not certain.
The author critiques the response by noting that after clarification, the follower's mind is at ease, but before, it is not. The verse only requires asking, not demanding proof.
5
Determine the mufta bihi
The chosen position (al-mukhtar) is that simple questioning suffices. The mufti does not need to present evidence; the questioner must obey.
This is the operative legal rule in Hanafi fatwa.

The Obligation of Taqlid for a Non-Mutlaq Mujtahid
Mas'ala on taqlid obligation and evidence requirement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the correct view (mufta bihi) on when a non-mutlaq mujtahid must do taqlid, apply the istinbat from the Quranic verse, and evaluate the objection regarding following error.

Core Idea
A non-absolute mujtahid need only ask for the ruling, not the proof.

This lesson navigates a key debate in Hanafi usul: when must a scholar who is not a fully independent mujtahid follow another mujtahid? The correct view distinguishes between matters he can derive himself and those he cannot. We will examine the evidence from Quran and Salaf practice, and address a common objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three positions
The text presents three views on when a non-mutlaq mujtahid must do taqlid. State each view clearly.
First view: taqlid only in matters he cannot derive (based on tajazzī). Second: taqlid absolutely (denying tajazzī). Third: taqlid only if evidence is shown.
2
Locate the chosen view and its proof
The author chooses the first view. Identify the two proofs: the practice of the Salaf and the Quranic verse (al-Nahl: 43).
The Salaf gave fatwas without citing sources; the verse commands asking without specifying that proof must be demanded.
3
Analyze the istinbat of the verse
The verse 'Ask the people of remembrance' is general (amm). The cause is lack of knowledge. Apply the Hanafi rule: a command tied to a cause repeats with the cause. Here, the obligation to ask repeats whenever lack of knowledge exists.
The verse does not require asking for the evidence; it only requires asking for the ruling.
4
Examine the objection and rebuttal
The opposing view says taqlid without evidence leads to following error. The rebuttal: the objection also applies when evidence is shown, because the evidence itself is speculative. After evidence is clear, one is reassured.
This is a shared obligation (ilzām mushtarak). The mufti himself acts on his opinion, which is probable, not certain.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: whether the mufti must cite his source when giving a fatwa. According to the Hanafi position, he need not.
The fatwa is valid without citing sources; the lay person is not required to understand the evidence.

The Condition for Valid Taqlid: Attributing the Ruling to Allah

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Taqlid is valid only when the mufti explicitly attributes the ruling to Allah.

This passage from Shaykh al-Akbar Muhyiddin Ibn Arabi lays down a critical condition for following a mufti: the mufti must state that the ruling is from Allah. Without this attribution, the layperson should not act upon the fatwa. This aligns with the Hanafi principle that the mufti is a conveyor of divine ruling, not a legislator. Let's unpack the nuance and see how intention plays a role.

Guided Path

If the mufti says 'I give a ruling by opinion' without attributing to Allah, then the questioner should not act but seek another mufti. Why? Because the only ruling is Allah's.
The mufti's personal opinion is not authoritative.
3
Analyze the exception for intended meaning
Ibn Arabi adds that if the mufti intends the ruling to be Allah's and knows it by opinion, then it is permissible and obligatory to follow. The actual intention matters.
This softens the requirement, focusing on substantive attribution.
4
Relate to Hanafi usul principles
The Hanafi school also holds that the mufti conveys Allah's ruling via the sources, not his own opinion. This passage supports that view and adds a specific verbal condition.
Consider how this condition might affect fatwa practice.

Core Idea
Ijtihad is rarer than red sulfur; without knowledge of it, taqlid is impermissible.

In this lesson, we examine the Hanafi conditions for seeking a fatwa (istifta'). The passage from *Fawatih al-Rahamut* clarifies that a mufti must be known for both ijtihad and moral integrity. Identify the agreed conditions
The text states agreement that istifta' is permissible when the mufti is known for both ijtihad and 'adala. Identify these two conditions.
2
Identify prohibited cases
Istifta' is prohibited if either condition is conjectured absent. Note the default rule.
3
Focus on the disputed scenario
When ijtihad is unknown but 'adala is known, the Hanafi position is prohibition (المنع). Some unnamed scholars permit it.
This is the core ikhtilaf in the passage.

Analyze the Hanafi evidence
Ijtihad is extremely rare (like red sulfur), so conjecture follows its absence. Thus the condition of ijtihad is not met.
This is a probabilistic reasoning based on abundance (kathra).

Opponents argue by symmetry: if prohibition applies here, it should also apply when 'adala is unknown but ijtihad is known, which is allegedly permissible. Hanafi response: prohibition also applies there (due to possibility of lying), and the difference is that 'adala is the norm among mujtahids, so ignorance does not harm.
Understanding this rebuttal is key to grasping the Hanafi position.

Commentary
This is a ruling (hukm) on the permissibility of seeking a fatwa (istifta') from a mufti. The text begins by stating the agreed-upon rule for valid istifta': the mufti must be known for both ijtihad (competence in deriving rulings) and 'adala (moral integrity). This knowledge can come from the general acceptance of the community. If either is conjectured to be absent, istifta' is impermissible. The text then addresses a nuanced case: if the mufti's ijtihad is unknown but his 'adala is known (i.e., he is a known righteous person but his scholarly competence is uncertain). The relied-upon Hanafi position (المختار) is that following (taqlid) and istifta' from such a person is prohibited. The reason is that ijtihad is extremely rare (like red sulfur), so the default conjecture is its absence; thus the condition is not met. Some unnamed scholars (whose opinions are not authoritative) permitted it. Their objection—that if this were prohibited, then the opposite case (unknown 'adala but known ijtihad) would also have to be prohibited, which is not the case—is answered. The Hanafi response is that prohibition applies in both cases: unknown 'adala also makes istifta' impermissible due to the possibility of lying, and it is not agreed that the opposite is always permissible. Furthermore, the difference is that 'adala is the norm among mujtahids, so ignorance of it is not harmful; conjecture follows the majority. Ijtihad, however, is extremely rare, so ignorance of it is harmful. Finally, the text discusses whether a just person's claim of being a mujtahid is accepted. The view is that it is accepted, like a claim of being a Companion (صحابي), but there is a suspicion of claiming status.

Taqlid of the Less Preferred Mujtahid: Hanafi Rule
Proves taqlid of a less preferred mujtahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that taqlid of a mafdool is permissible, identify the Quranic and ijma proofs, and evaluate the opposing argument that the muqallid must perform tarjih.

Core Idea
Following a less qualified scholar is valid — the Quran and the Sahaba's practice confirm it.

This passage addresses a key usul question: when multiple mujtahids exist, is a layperson bound to follow the most knowledgeable (afdal), or may they follow a less preferred (mafdool)? The Hanafi school, based on the generality of the Quranic command 'Ask the people of remembrance' and the established practice of the Companions, permits following the mafdool. We will examine the proofs, the opposing view, and the reasoning that underlies the Hanafi tarjih.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The core question: May a muqallid perform taqlid of a mafdool mujtahid when a fadil is present? The Hanafi school says yes; some later scholars (e.g., Ahmad ibn Hanbal) say no and require following the arjah.
The issue is not about the validity of ijtihad but about the obligation of the muqallid.
2
State the Hanafi position and its primary proof
The Hanafi position: permissibility. The primary proof is the generality of Quran 16:43: 'Ask the people of remembrance' – a general command to all qualified scholars, not only the best. This indicates that following any qualified scholar is legally valid.
The term 'ahl al-dhikr' is general ('amm'), covering both mafdool and fadil.
3
Identify the second proof: Companion consensus
Second proof: the certain practice of the Sahaba. They would ask lesser Companions for fatwa despite the presence of more excellent ones. This repeated, well-known practice forms a consensus (ijma) that taqlid of a mafdool is permissible.
Examples: Ibn Mas'ud fatwa opposed to 'Ali; Zayd and 'Ali opposed to Abu Bakr.
4
Understand the opposing argument and its weakness
Opponents argue by analogy: for the mujtahid, conflicting evidences require tarjih (weighing). Similarly, for the muqallid, conflicting opinions of mujtahids require tarjih, so he must seek the most preponderant opinion. The Hanafi response: consensus overrides this analogy, and the muqallid is not required to perform tarjih because it causes hardship and the muqallid's own opinion (zann) is irrelevant; he follows an authoritative statement.
The author adds that even if tarjih is required, it can be done by taharri (simple investigation), not necessarily determining the most knowledgeable.
5
Appreciate the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: In the Hanafi school, a layperson may validly follow a less knowledgeable mufti. In the opposing view, they must exert effort to find the most preponderant and may not follow a mafdool. The Hanafi position is relied upon for fatwa due to its ease and consensus support.
This principle affects modern day taqlid, e.g., following a local scholar vs. a more famous one.

Core Idea
A muqallid who acts with taharri cannot revert; leaving the preponderant is irrational.

This passage tackles a fundamental question in usul al-fiqh: once a layperson (muqallid) follows a specific ruling from a mujtahid and acts upon it, can they later change to another opinion on the same matter? If the muqallid acted with taharri, he should not revert as long as he remains in that state. This is the author's preferred view.

Commentary
This passage deals with several interconnected rules of taqlid. The first issue: a muqallid cannot revert a partial ruling he has acted upon. Although some (like al-Amidi and Ibn al-Hajib) claim this is agreed upon, the author notes that there is actual disagreement, particularly in later works. However, if the muqallid acted based on taharrī (preference of his heart), he should not revert as long as that preference remains. The second issue: a muqallid may follow different mujtahids in different matters; this is proven by the practice of the Companions who sought fatwa from different imams at different times. The third and most extensive issue: if a person commits himself to a particular madhhab (ilitzām), is he required to continue on it? The author rejects the notion that such commitment is binding, arguing that it is often based on whim rather than evidence, and that no legal obligation exists to adhere to a specific madhhab. He then addresses the permissibility of following the concessions (rukhaṣ) of different madhhabs, derived from the principle that one may choose the easier option if no legal prohibition exists. However, this is prohibited if done for frivolity (talahhī). The author refutes the claim that following concessions is unanimously prohibited, noting that there are two narrations from Imam Ahmad. He also addresses the objection that combining madhhabs might lead to a void practice, giving the example of a marriage combining elements that no single madhhab endorses (e.g., no mahr, no witnesses, no guardian). The author responds that this does not invalidate the principle, as the combined issue is not unified across madhhabs.

No Obligation to Follow a Single Madhhab
Argues against obligation to follow one madhhab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why, according to the Hanafi school, a layman is not obligated to continue following a particular madhhab and evaluate the opposing claim that continuation is required.

Core Idea
No one is forced to follow a single imam; the difference of scholars is a mercy, not a burden.

Many Muslims assume that once they start following a particular madhhab, they must continue in all matters. This lesson examines the Hanafi position that no such obligation exists. The author argues that requiring adherence to one imam would be a novel legal imposition, and that the diversity of scholarly opinions is actually a source of ease for the community.

Commentary
This passage addresses two sub-issues in the usul of taqlid. First, the permissibility of doing taqlid of a deceased mujtahid. The author argues that it is permissible because the widespread practice of Muslims without objection constitutes consensus, and the opinion of a dead mujtahid remains a valid legal position until overridden by a later consensus of living mujtahids. He refutes the objection that a dead person cannot have a valid opinion using the principle that consensus can abrogate earlier opinions. The second issue is whether laymen can directly follow the Companions. The author reports that the 'researchers' (al-muhaqqiqun) forbid it because extracting rulings requires deep analysis beyond a layman's ability; laymen must follow professional jurists (the four imams). He criticizes Ibn al-Salah who, based on this, restricts taqlid to the four imams. The author refutes this by citing two consensuses: first, that a new Muslim may follow any scholar, and second, that the Companions themselves allowed following different Companions. He also argues that the ban on following other mujtahids is not due to lack of scholarship but because their rulings are no longer preserved; if authentic narrations exist, they can be followed. He gives the example of later Hanafis issuing fatwa on requiring witnesses' oath following Ibn Abi Layla. The passage ends with the colophon of the author, Abd al-Ali Muhammad al-Ansari.

Taqlid for Laypersons: Following Organized Mujtahids
Usul rule on taqlid for laypersons with refutation of Ibn al-Salah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi position that laypersons may follow any preserved madhhab, and critique the opposing view that taqlid is restricted to the four Imams.

Core Idea
Laypersons must follow organized mujtahids because deriving rulings requires effort beyond their ability.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut addresses a critical usul issue: can a layperson directly follow the Companions or only the four Imams? The text presents and refutes a view restricting taqlid, arguing that the restriction is practical—based on preservation of rulings—not a matter of inherent superiority.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on layperson taqlid
Identify the ruling that laypersons must follow organized mujtahids who have systematized fiqh, not directly follow the Companions. Note the Hanafi view that this restriction is based on the layperson's inability to derive rulings, not on the Companions' authority.
The Companions are mujtahids, but their statements require the same level of investigation as the Sunnah.
2
Identify the opposing view and its basis
State Ibn al-Salah's position that taqlid is restricted to the four Imams because only they have organized and refined fiqh. Understand his reasoning: the methodology of the four Imams (sabr, tabwib, tanqih, ta'lil) is not found in other mujtahids.
Ibn al-Salah uses the lack of systematic organization as a reason to limit taqlid.
3
Analyze the Hanafi refutation of Ibn al-Salah
Examine the two consensuses cited from al-Iraqi: (1) a layperson may follow any scholar, (2) the Sahaba allowed following various Companions. Understand why these undermine the claim of a binding consensus against following non-four mujtahids.
The phrase 'ajma'a al-muhaqqiqun' is not a binding ijma' but a consensus of some scholars.
4
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Determine the practical impact: a layperson may follow a preserved ruling from a mujtahid outside the four schools, such as the example of Ibn Abi Layla's position on administering oaths to witnesses. Recognize that the restriction is due to lack of preservation, not validity.
The example shows that later Hanafi scholars relied on Ibn Abi Layla's madhhab when needed.
5
Synthesize the usul principle
Formulate the principle: the permissibility of taqlid depends on the preservation and accessibility of the mujtahid's rulings, not on the number of followers or historical limitation. The Hanafi school prioritizes practical usability over arbitrary restriction.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on maqasid and adaptability.

Commentary
This passage is a usul rule regarding taqlid for laypersons. The author discusses a view attributed to 'the Imam' (likely Abu Hanifa) that laypersons are prohibited from directly following the Companions because deriving rulings from their statements requires detailed investigation. Instead, laypersons must follow the organized mujtahids who have systematized fiqh into chapters, refined issues, etc. This view is used by Ibn al-Salah to restrict taqlid to the four Imams. The author then refutes this by citing two alleged consensuses from al-Iraqi: one that a layperson may follow any scholar, and another that the Sahaba allowed following various Companions. The author argues that the prohibition of the Imam is not a binding consensus, and that the restriction to the four is only due to lack of preservation of other madhhabs, not because other mujtahids are inferior. He gives the example of Ibn Abi Layla's madhhab being used for oaths of witnesses.

تبويب (tabwib): Organization into chapters; systematic arrangement of rulings.
إجماع (ijma'): Consensus of the mujtahids on a legal ruling.
