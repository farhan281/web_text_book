The Hanafi Position on Ta'wil of Mutashabihat: A Usul Dispute
Analyzing the Hanafi derivation from Quran 3:7 regarding knowledge of mutashabihat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul methodology to identify the ta'wil of mutashabihat as known only to Allah, and evaluate the opposing Shafi'i reading using the concepts of waqf and istinaf.

Core Idea
The pause after 'except Allah' in Quran 3:7 determines that the rasikhun do not know the ta'wil by acquisition.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut presents a key usul dispute: whether the true interpretation (ta'wil) of the Quran's ambiguous verses (mutashabihat) is known to anyone besides Allah. The Hanafi school, following the Salaf, argues that only Allah knows the full ta'wil, while those firmly rooted in knowledge (al-rasikhun) simply affirm belief. The opposing view, attributed to some later scholars and Imam al-Shafi'i, holds that the rasikhun do know the ta'wil. The dispute hinges on the interpretation of a single verse (3:7) and the rules of Arabic grammar and qira'at.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested issue and the two positions
The issue is whether the rasikhun have acquired knowledge of the ta'wil of mutashabihat. The Hanafi position says no; they only believe. The opposition says yes.
2
Analyze the Hanafi primary proof: Quran 3:7 with waqf
The Hanafis argue that the pause (waqf) in recitation is after 'except Allah', making 'and those firmly rooted in knowledge' a separate sentence (istinaf). This grammatical analysis excludes the rasikhun from the exception.
3
Examine the supporting evidence: the reading of Ibn Mas'ud
The passage cites Ibn Mas'ud's reading 'its ta'wil is only with Allah, and those firmly rooted say...' which explicitly separates the two. This qira'ah shadhdha reinforces the Hanafi argument.
Irregular readings are not definitive in creedal matters, but they support the grammatical analysis.
4
Consider the opponent's proof and the Hanafi response
The opponent cites the apparent conjunction without pause, and reports of Companions interpreting verses. The Hanafi reply distinguishes between acquired knowledge and conjectural speech, and notes that the prohibition of interpreting mutashabihat proves it is not obligatory.
5
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf and the final tarjih
The practical consequence: according to the Hanafi view, one should refrain from interpreting mutashabihat and simply believe. The text gives tarjih to the Hanafi position based on the qira'at and the context pairing deviants with rasikhun.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul dispute about whether the ta'wil (ultimate interpretation) of the Qur'an's mutashabihat (ambiguous verses) can be known by anyone other than Allah. The author presents the Hanafi position (following the Salaf) that the true ta'wil is known only to Allah, and that the rasikhun (those firmly rooted in knowledge) only affirm belief in the verses without claiming to know their full meaning. The opposing view, attributed to some later scholars and Imam al-Shafi'i, holds that the entire Quran is understandable to some. The author begins by stating the issue: there are matters that no one understands except Allah, and the two great imams (likely Fakhr al-Islam and Shams al-A'imma) excepted the Prophet and his Companions from this. The main proof text is Quran 3:7, and the author argues that the correct pause (waqf) in recitation is after 'except Allah', making 'and those firmly rooted in knowledge' a new sentence (istinaf), not conjoined to 'Allah'. This is supported by the reading of Ibn Mas'ud. The author also responds to objections from the other side, including the claim that if some verses were not understandable, it would be meaningless speech. The Hanafi reply distinguishes between the Prophet (who understood) and others, and notes that the purpose might be trial (ibtilā') by requiring belief without full understanding. The author also addresses arguments based on reports of Companions interpreting the Quran, saying they spoke conjecturally, not with certainty. The passage concludes by noting that the dispute is about acquired knowledge, not the mystical knowledge (al-ilm al-kashfi) attained by saints through spiritual exertion. Throughout, the author follows a systematic method: stating the issue, presenting the Hanafi proofs (Qur'an, readings, rational arguments), addressing counterarguments, and providing a tarjih (preference) for the Hanafi view. The passage is a classic example of usul al-fiqh methodology applied to a creedal issue.

Key Terms
متشابه (mutashabih): Quranic verses whose meaning is ambiguous or not immediately clear; they require tafsir or ta'wil.
محكم (muhkam): Quranic verses that are clear and decisive in meaning; they form the 'mother of the Book'.
تأويل (ta'wil): The ultimate interpretation or real meaning of a verse, especially of mutashabih, known only to Allah.
راسخون في العلم (al-rasikhun fi al-'ilm): Those firmly rooted in knowledge; they believe in the mutashabihat without claiming to know their ta'wil.

Commentary
This passage is an extended definition of the usul terms mujmal (ambiguous) and mutashabih (unclear) from a Hanafi perspective. The author first illustrates the concept of mujmal using the word 'adha' (harm), showing that its legal meaning is specific to menstrual impurity, not all harm. A mujmal term is one whose meaning cannot be determined by reason alone but requires transmitted explanation (naql). Examples include homonyms (mushtarak) with no contextual clue, such as 'mawali' in a bequest (which could mean manumitters or manumitted), and purely legal terms like salat and riba, whose linguistic meanings are not intended in the legal context. The second category, mutashabih, refers to meanings not attainable by reason or transmission, known only by divine gift; examples include the disjointed letters in the Qur'an and attributes like 'hand' and 'eye'. The Salaf's position is to affirm these without enquiring into modality. The author then presents the Shafi'i classification of textual clarity: Zahir (probabilistic), Nass (certain), Mu'awwal (interpreted away from literal), Mufassar (explained), and Muhkam (clear), noting that these categories are overlapping and the Hanafi usage is consistent. The passage concludes that what clarifies is Bayan, and introduces three chapters on Ta'wil, Ijmal, and Bayan.

Key Terms
المجمل (al-mujmal): A term whose intended meaning is not perceived by reason but requires transmitted explanation (naql).
المتشابه (al-mutashabih): A term whose intended meaning is not known at all by reason or transmission, only by divine gift (mushahada mawhuba).
الظاهر (al-zahir): A term that indicates its meaning with probability (zann), allowing for ta'wil.
النص (al-nass): A term that indicates its meaning with certainty (qat') and does not admit ta'wil.

Definition and Types of Ijmal in Usul al-Fiqh
Defining Ijmal and its two causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-ijmal (ambiguity), distinguish its two main types (mufrad and murakkab), and identify the third type caused by contextual association, applying the Hanafi definition to textual examples.

Core Idea
Ijmal requires bayan; it can arise from a single homonym or a compound with ambiguous referent.

This lesson introduces the concept of ijmal (ambiguity) in Islamic legal texts. Understanding when a text is ambiguous is crucial because ambiguous texts cannot be applied until clarified. The author of Fawatih al-Rahamut explains that ijmal occurs either in a single word or in a compound expression, and provides a Quranic example illustrating how ambiguity leads to ikhtilaf among schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two primary types of ijmal
Read the definition: ijmal is divided into (1) ambiguity in a single word itself (al-mufrad) and (2) ambiguity in a compound expression (al-murakkab). Note that the author also mentions a third type: ambiguity in a single word due to association with something else (e.g., a pronoun with two possible antecedents).
The third type is a subcase of mufrad but caused by context.
2
Analyze the example of ijmal in a single word
Consider the word 'al-ayn' which can mean eye, spring, or spy. Its ambiguity is inherent in the word itself. The author indicates this can be due to homonymy (ishtirak) or inflection (i'lal).
Homonyms are the most common source of mufrad ijmal.
3
Analyze the Quranic example of ijmal in a compound
Examine Quran 2:237: 'alladhi bi-yadihi 'uqdatu al-nikah' (the one in whose hand is the marriage knot). The individual words are clear, but the referent of the compound is ambiguous: does it mean the husband or the guardian? This is ijmal in a compound because the overall intent is unclear.
The Hanafi/Shafi'i and Maliki schools differ on the interpretation.
4
Understand the third type: ijmal due to contextual association
Look at the anecdote: when asked who is better, Abu Bakr or 'Ali, the answer was 'the one whose daughter is in his house'. The pronoun 'his' (from 'baytihi') could refer to either the first mentioned (Abu Bakr) or the second ('Ali), causing ambiguity. The word itself is not ambiguous, but the context creates uncertainty.
This type is ijmal in a single word due to association with other elements.
5
Note the immediate legal consequence
A mujmal text cannot be acted upon until the ambiguity is resolved by bayan (clarification) from the Lawgiver or through ijtihad. This lesson sets the foundation for understanding bayan.
The absence of bayan means the text is inoperative.

Commentary
This passage opens the second chapter on Ijmal (ambiguity) in usul al-fiqh. Ijmal is defined as a word or expression whose meaning is not clear and requires explanation (bayan). The author distinguishes two types: 1) Ijmal in a single word itself (al-mufrad) — where the word itself is capable of multiple meanings, e.g., 'al-ayn' (which can mean eye, spring, etc.). 2) Ijmal in a compound (al-murakkab) — where the individual words are clear but the overall intended meaning is unclear, e.g., the Quranic verse (2:237) about 'the one in whose hand is the marriage knot'. This phrase is ambiguous: does it refer to the husband or the guardian? The Hanafi and Shafi'i take it as the husband, while Maliki takes it as the guardian. The author is explaining the causes and examples of Ijmal, setting the stage for a detailed discussion on how ambiguity is resolved.

Key Terms
الإجمال (al-ijmal): Ambiguity: an expression that is not clear in its intended meaning, requiring explanation (bayan).
المفرد (al-mufrad): A single word that is ambiguous by itself (e.g., homonyms like 'ayn).
المركب (al-murakkab): A compound expression where the overall meaning is ambiguous even though individual words are clear.
البيان (al-bayan): The clarification that resolves ijmal, whether from the Lawgiver or through ijtihad.

Ijmal: Definition and Types of Ambiguity in Mufrad and Murakkab
Defining ijmal and its two primary types: mufrad and murakkab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify instances of ijmal as mufrad or murakkab, and apply the Hanafi principles to resolve ambiguity using bayan.

Core Idea
Ijmal is ambiguity requiring bayan; it can reside in a single word or a compound construction.

In usul al-fiqh, understanding textual ambiguity (ijmal) is essential for correct istinbat. This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut defines ijmal and its two primary types: ambiguity in a single word itself (mufrad) and ambiguity in a compound expression (murakkab). It also discusses subtypes like ambiguity due to homonymy, pronoun reference, attribute conjunction, multiple metaphors, and even actions of the Prophet (ﷺ). Mastering these distinctions enables you to identify when a text requires clarification (bayan) before it can be applied.

Guided Path
1
Define Ijmal and Its Types
Start by stating the definition of ijmal: a word or construction that has multiple possible meanings and requires bayan. Then identify the two main categories: ijmal in mufrad (single word) and ijmal in murakkab (compound).
The passage opens with 'al-ijmal imma fi mufrad nafsihi...' so you can trace this division.
2
Analyze the Mufrad Type
Examine the mufrad subcategories: (a) inherent homonymy (like 'ayn), (b) due to accompaniment (e.g., pronoun with two antecedents), (c) attribute with two references (e.g., 'skilled' after 'physician'), (d) multiple metaphors when literal meaning is impossible. For each, explain why the ambiguity arises and what type of bayan would resolve it.
The passage provides examples: 'mukhtar', the story of Abu Bakr and Ali, and 'Zayd is a physician, skilled'.
3
Analyze the Murakkab Type
Focus on compound ambiguity where each word is clear but the overall meaning is unclear. Use the Quranic verse 2:237 as the primary example: 'alladhi bi yadihi 'uqdatu al-nikah' – is it the husband or the guardian? Note the different madhhab interpretations.
The passage contrasts Hanafi/Shafi'i (husband) with Maliki (guardian). This is key for understanding ikhtilaf.
4
Consider Action-Based Ijmal
Note that even actions of the Prophet (ﷺ) can be mujmal. The examples given are standing from the second rak'ah (could be deliberate or forgetful) and the salam after two rak'ahs. The principle is that the Prophet is not established upon forgetfulness, so deliberate interpretation preponderates.
This shows that ijmal is not limited to words; actions also require careful contextual analysis.
5
Synthesize the Role of Qarina and Bayan
Conclude that ijmal is not absolute; it exists only until a qarina (indication) clarifies the intended meaning. Bayan can come from context, other texts, or rational proofs. The passage emphasizes that what resolves ijmal is the presence of a clear qarina that specifies one meaning.
For example, the Prophet's established practice serves as a qarina for his actions.

Commentary
This passage defines Ijmal (ambiguity) and its primary types. First, Ijmal can occur in a single word that is inherently capable of multiple meanings, like the word 'ayn (eye, spring, etc.) or mukhtar (chosen, etc.). Second, it can occur in a compound construction where each word is known but the overall meaning is ambiguous. The verse about the one who holds the knot of marriage is an example: does it refer to the husband or the guardian? The Hanafi and Shafi'i positions take it as the husband, while Maliki takes it as the guardian. Another type of Ijmal occurs when a single word is accompanied by another, like a pronoun with multiple possible antecedents. The story of Abu Bakr and Ali illustrates this. Actions can also be ambiguous, such as the Prophet's action of standing from the second rak'ah: it could be deliberate (indicating the permissibility of leaving the first tashahhud) or forgetful. However, since the Prophet is not established in forgetfulness, the deliberate interpretation is preferred.

Key Terms
الإجمال (al-ijmāl): Ambiguity in a word or construction such that it has multiple possible meanings and requires clarification.
البيان (al-bayān): The clarification that removes ambiguity from a mujmal text.
المفرد (al-mufrad): A single word; in the context of ijmal, a word that is inherently ambiguous.
المركب (al-murakkab): A compound expression; in the context of ijmal, a phrase where each word is clear but the overall meaning is ambiguous.

The Rule: No Ijmal in Tahrim Mudaf Ila al-Ayn
Arguing that prohibition attached to a specific substance does not cause ijmal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that prohibition attached to a specific ‘ayn is not mujmal and explain the role of istiqra in determining the intended action.

Core Idea
Prohibition of a specific substance: istiqra reveals the intended action, removing all ambiguity.

This lesson addresses a key usul dispute: when the Lawgiver prohibits a specific substance (e.g., ‘wine is forbidden’), does the ruling become ambiguous (mujmal) because many actions could be involved? The dominant Hanafi position, supported by induction (istiqra) from Qur’anic examples, holds that the prohibition is clear and the intended action is known. We will explore the reasoning and the opposing views.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
State the claim: some scholars (al-Karkhi, Abu Abd Allah al-Basri) consider tahrim mudaf ila al-ayn to be mujmal because multiple actions could be intended. The Hanafi position is that it is not mujmal.
Focus on the core disagreement: ambiguity vs clarity.
2
Analyze the Hanafi Proof: Istiqra
The Hanafis use inductive reasoning (istiqra) from shari'a examples. For instance, 'forbidden to you are your mothers' is understood as prohibiting marriage/intercourse; 'forbidden is wine' as prohibiting drinking; 'forbidden is silk' as prohibiting wearing. This shows that the immediate understanding (al-mutabadir) is a single, specific action.
Istiqra is a method to determine the intended meaning from multiple instances.
3
Examine the Opposing Argument
Al-Karkhi and others argue that the word 'ayn could imply many actions (e.g., wine could be drunk, sold, used for cooking), and without a qarina (contextual indicator) specifying one, it is ambiguous.
The opposition relies on the possibility of multiple meanings.
4
Understand the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response: through istiqra, the customary practice in shari'a points to one intended action. Moreover, the Salaf (early generations) used these verses to declare those who permit the prohibited action as disbelievers, showing the clarity of the prohibition.
Consensus of the Salaf is cited as additional evidence.
5
Apply to Specific Examples
For each substance, identify the intended action: mother → marriage/intercourse; wine → drinking; silk → wearing; pig → eating. This removes any ambiguity.
These examples are given in the passage.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul al-fiqh dispute about whether phrases like 'Forbidden to you are your mothers' (Quran 4:23) contain ambiguity (ijmal). The author, commenting on Musallam al-Thubut, presents the Hanafi position that there is no ambiguity in such constructions, contrary to the views of al-Karkhi (a Hanafi scholar) and Abu Abdullah al-Basri (a Mu'tazili). The passage begins by stating the rule: 'There is no ambiguity in prohibition added to the substance' and gives examples. It then reports that al-Karkhi and al-Basri considered such phrases ambiguous. The Hanafi proof is that custom (urf) and induction (istiqra) from legal examples show that the intended meaning is always a specific action (e.g., drinking for wine, wearing for silk, marriage for mothers). Since the immediate understanding is clear, there is no ambiguity. A counterargument is presented: the substance might be intended for multiple actions, and implying all is not permissible, so the specific one is unknown, leading to ambiguity. The Hanafi reply is that the custom (urf) determines which action is intended, and the immediate understanding (al-mutabadir) is always one specific action based on induction from particular instances in the Shari'ah. Additionally, the passage notes that consensus (ijma) among the Salaf existed before the emergence of the ambiguous view; they used these verses as definitive proof for prohibition and deemed those who deny their apparent meaning as disbelievers. After agreeing on the negation of ambiguity, the scholars differed on the nature of the construction: Shafi'is and some Hanafis say it involves metaphor (majaz) or implication (idmar), while the verified Hanafis (including Imam Fakhr al-Islam) maintain it is literal (haqiqa) with no metaphor. The author elaborates on the Hanafi view: tahrim (prohibition) is of two types: (1) prohibition of an action while the locus is capable of it, and (2) prohibition by removing the locus from being capable of the action (like wine). In the second type, the prohibition is attributed to the substance because the substance is made impermissible in itself, so the word 'haram' is literal in its meaning of 'prevented' but with a different kind of prevention (preventing the locus from being suitable for the act). This makes the construction a customary literal meaning (haqiqa 'urfiyya), not a metaphor. The passage concludes with reference to al-Kashf's explanation: tahrim linguistically means man' (prevention), and the prevention in such constructions is of the second type (preventing the locus from being suitable), so the word is literal. The author notes that this explanation resolves the issue. The passage is dense and includes multiple layers of commentary, but the core point is that the Hanafi school defends the clarity and literalness of prohibitory phrases in the Quran.

Key Terms
الإجمال (al-ijmal): Ambiguity in a text that does not clearly indicate its intended meaning, requiring external clarification.
التحريم المضاف إلى العين (al-tahrim al-mudaf ila al-'ayn): Prohibition attributed directly to a substance, e.g., 'wine is forbidden' or 'your mothers are forbidden'.
الحقيقة العرفية (al-haqiqa al-'urfiyya): A phrase that is literal in customary usage even if not literal in original linguistic usage.

Literal or Metaphorical? The Nature of Prohibition Added to Substances
Dispute on whether prohibition added to substances is literal or metaphorical

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the Hanafi literal interpretation (haqiqa 'urfiyya) and the metaphorical interpretation (majaz) of prohibition added to substances, and explain the two types of prohibition that support the literal view.

Core Idea
The Hanafi view that 'haram' is literal when added to a substance—it means the substance is prevented from its intended use, not a metaphor.

After agreeing that there is no ambiguity, scholars differed on whether the construction is literal or metaphorical. The Shafi'is and some Hanafis say it involves metaphor or implication. The verified Hanafis, however, insist it is a customary literal meaning (haqiqa 'urfiyya), supported by a nuanced analysis of prohibition types. We will explore this deeper layer of the debate.

Guided Path
1
State the agreement and the new disagreement
All parties agree there is no ambiguity. The new disagreement is: is the phrase literal or metaphorical? Identify the two positions from the text.
2
Explain the Hanafi argument against metaphor
Imam Fakhr al-Islam argues that prohibition added to a substance is a sign of its bindingness, not a metaphor. Prohibition is of two types: (1) prohibition of an action on a capable locus, and (2) prohibition by removing the locus from capability. The second type applies to substances like wine.
3
Analyze the concept of haqiqa 'urfiyya
The author of al-Tahrir explains that the construction has become a customary literal meaning: it indicates the removal of the locus from being suitable for the action. No estimation (taqdir) or metaphor is needed.
4
Consider the objection and alternative explanation
Some object that Fakhr al-Islam's explanation actually confirms metaphoricity by explaining the reason for the shift. The passage then presents al-Kashf's resolution: tahrim linguistically means man' (prevention), and the prevention in these cases is of the second type (preventing the locus). This makes the word literal.
The passage ends with this resolution as the soundest view.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul al-fiqh dispute about whether phrases like 'Forbidden to you are your mothers' (Quran 4:23) contain ambiguity (ijmal). The author, commenting on Musallam al-Thubut, presents the Hanafi position that there is no ambiguity in such constructions, contrary to the views of al-Karkhi (a Hanafi scholar) and Abu Abdullah al-Basri (a Mu'tazili). The passage begins by stating the rule: 'There is no ambiguity in prohibition added to the substance' and gives examples. It then reports that al-Karkhi and al-Basri considered such phrases ambiguous. The Hanafi proof is that custom (urf) and induction (istiqra) from legal examples show that the intended meaning is always a specific action (e.g., drinking for wine, wearing for silk, marriage for mothers). Since the immediate understanding is clear, there is no ambiguity. A counterargument is presented: the substance might be intended for multiple actions, and implying all is not permissible, so the specific one is unknown, leading to ambiguity. The Hanafi reply is that the custom (urf) determines which action is intended, and the immediate understanding (al-mutabadir) is always one specific action based on induction from particular instances in the Shari'ah. Additionally, the passage notes that consensus (ijma) among the Salaf existed before the emergence of the ambiguous view; they used these verses as definitive proof for prohibition and deemed those who deny their apparent meaning as disbelievers. After agreeing on the negation of ambiguity, the scholars differed on the nature of the construction: Shafi'is and some Hanafis say it involves metaphor (majaz) or implication (idmar), while the verified Hanafis (including Imam Fakhr al-Islam) maintain it is literal (haqiqa) with no metaphor. The author elaborates on the Hanafi view: tahrim (prohibition) is of two types: (1) prohibition of an action while the locus is capable of it, and (2) prohibition by removing the locus from being capable of the action (like wine). In the second type, the prohibition is attributed to the substance because the substance is made impermissible in itself, so the word 'haram' is literal in its meaning of 'prevented' but with a different kind of prevention (preventing the locus from being suitable for the act). This makes the construction a customary literal meaning (haqiqa 'urfiyya), not a metaphor. The passage concludes with reference to al-Kashf's explanation: tahrim linguistically means man' (prevention), and the prevention in such constructions is of the second type (preventing the locus from being suitable), so the word is literal. The author notes that this explanation resolves the issue. The passage is dense and includes multiple layers of commentary, but the core point is that the Hanafi school defends the clarity and literalness of prohibitory phrases in the Quran.

Key Terms

التحريم المضاف إلى العين (al-tahrim al-mudaf ila al-'ayn): Prohibition attributed directly to a substance, e.g., 'wine is forbidden' or 'your mothers are forbidden'.
الحقيقة العرفية (al-haqiqa al-'urfiyya): A phrase that is literal in customary usage even if not literal in original linguistic usage.

Refuting Ambiguity in the Wiping Verse
Defending the verse against claims of ambiguity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a text is not mujmal when the preposition 'ba' indicates adhesion, and evaluate the counter-arguments from other schools.

Core Idea
The verse is clear: 'ba' indicates general adhesion, not partitive, so any part of the head suffices.

This passage defends the Quranic verse for wiping the head against claims of ambiguity (ijmāl). The author presents two main proofs: the lack of transmitted pause by the Companions, and the linguistic function of the preposition 'ba'. You will learn how Hanafi usul classifies this text as clear (mubayyan) rather than ambiguous, and how it refutes the arguments of the Maliki and Shafi'i schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim of ambiguity
State the claim that the verse is mujmal because the extent of wiping is unspecified. Recognize that some Hanafis (e.g., author of al-Hidayah) also held this view.
The claim is that the verse needs external clarification.
2
Examine the first proof: companion transmission
If the verse were mujmal, the Companions would have paused and their pause would have been transmitted. Since no such transmission exists, the verse is not mujmal.
This is a historical argument based on tawātur.
3
Analyze the second proof: the role of 'ba' and custom
The preposition 'ba' in 'bi ru'ūsikum' indicates adhesion (ilṣāq), not partitive (tab'īḍ). Custom ('urf) does not restrict it to a part. Therefore the verb applies to the whole head in a general sense, not a specific part.
Contrast with Shafi'i claim that 'ba' can be partitive based on custom in phrases like 'masaḥtu yadī bi al-mindīl'.
4
Refute opposing views
The Hanafi response rejects the analogy to the tool (mindīl) and denies that 'ba' for tab'īḍ is linguistically proven. The Maliki view requiring full wiping is also addressed.
The author emphasizes that the instrumental analogy does not apply to the locus (head).
5
Conclude the Hanafi position
The verse is clear (mubayyan). Wiping any part of the head fulfills the obligation. The practical consequence (thamarah) is that the act is valid with any amount of wiping.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Context and common usage can turn a seemingly ambiguous number into a clear statement—'urf is a legitimate source of bayan.

In this lesson, we examine a classic usul dispute over the legal clarity of a common financial acknowledgment. The Hanafi school argues that the phrase 'he has a hundred such that 'aliy and dirham' is mubayyan (clearly specified) because common usage allows the omission of the specifier of the number when the second noun indicates the category. The Shafi'i school, however, insists that the conjunction requires the conjoined items to be distinct, leaving the number ambiguous. Understanding this debate sharpens your ability to evaluate how different usul principles lead to different rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed phrase and the two positions
Read the phrase 'lahu mi'atu 'aliy wa dirham'. Note that the Hanafis consider it mubayyan, meaning the hundred is understood as dirhams, while the Shafi'is consider it mujmal, requiring clarification from the acknowledger.
The agreement on other cases (like 'hundred dirhams' explicitly) is not disputed.
2
Analyze the Hanafi dalil: common usage ('urf)
The Hanafis argue that 'urf permits silence about the specifier of a number when the second noun indicates the type. This is based on frequent usage that omits the specifier for brevity, relying on context.
This principle is specific to numbers and measures.
3

The Shafi'is counter that the conjunction (wāw) inherently requires taghāyur (difference) between the conjoined items, so the second cannot be an interpretation of the first. Therefore, the hundred remains unspecified.
Taghāyur is the default for 'aṭf.

The Hanafis clarify that they do not claim the conjoined item interprets the number; rather, the silence about the specifier of the first is resolved by the second indicating the genus. This does not violate taghāyur because the conjoined items themselves (the commodity and the currency) are different. They also provide a counterexample: 'hundred ten dirhams' where conjunction with differentiation is accepted.
The counterexample shows that taghāyur does not prevent the second from clarifying the first.
5

The text notes that the expression involves an estimated word (the omitted specifier), which is considered like the uttered word, making it dalālat al-manṭūq, not silence. This strengthens the Hanafi position that the meaning is explicitly conveyed.
This finer point highlights the nuanced methodology in Hanafi usul.

Delaying explanatory clarification to the time of need is permissible but delaying beyond need or change clarification is impermissible.

This passage continues the discussion on delaying the clarification (bayan) of an ambiguous text (mujmal). The author addresses two objections from those who argue that delay renders the text ineffective. He distinguishes the mujmal from the utterly meaningless term (muhmal). Then he moves to a branch issue: if delaying bayan is permitted, then delaying the hearing of a specifier (mukhassis) is even more so. He refutes an objection from al-Tahrir and answers the Shafi'i argument based on the case of Fatima (may Allah be pleased with her).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two objections against delaying bayan of mujmal
The deniers argue: (1) delay prevents understanding of the intended meaning, so the text cannot be acted upon; (2) addressing with mujmal before bayan is like addressing with muhmal, which conveys nothing initially.
These objections are the basis for the subsequent Hanafi response.
2
Analyze the Hanafi response distinguishing mujmal from muhmal
The response states that mujmal indicates one of two possibilities, giving a general understanding that allows the listener to intend to act and believe, unlike muhmal which conveys nothing. Also, there is no obligation before bayan, so delay is not detrimental.
This distinction is crucial for the permissibility of delaying bayan.
3
Examine the branch (farʿ) on delaying hearing of the mukhassis
The Shafiʿi view claims that if delaying bayan is permissible, then delaying the hearing of the specifier is even more so. The author then presents the objection from al-Tahrir that the general before specification is like the mujmal. Understand the claim and the refutation.
The refutation: the general (ʿamm) has a manifest meaning and can be acted upon; it is not ambiguous like mujmal.
4

The Shafiʿis cite that Fatima acted on the general inheritance verse without having heard the specifier. The Hanafi response: the specifier was heard by some companions and transmitted; also she may have forgotten.
This shows that delayed hearing is permissible as long as some scholars hear and transmit it.

Commentary
This passage is a continuation of the discussion on delaying the clarification (bayan) of an ambiguous text (mujmal). The author first addresses an objection: if the clarification is delayed, the text remains unknown and cannot be acted upon. He responds that there is no obligation prior to clarification, and the ambiguous text still provides a general understanding that allows intention and belief, unlike an incoherent term (muhmal) which conveys nothing. Then a branch (far') is discussed: if delaying the bayan of a mujmal is permissible, then delaying the hearing of a specifier (mukhassis) is even more so. The author refutes an objection from al-Tahrir that the general (amm) before specification is like the mujmal. The general has a manifest meaning and can be acted upon, whereas the mujmal does not. Finally, the Shafi'i argument from the case of Fatima (the hadith of inheritance) is answered: the specifier was heard by some and transmitted; also she may have forgotten. The key point is that the Hanafi school permits delayed hearing of a specifier as long as it reaches some scholars who can transmit it.

مجمل (mujmal): An ambiguous term that requires clarification but still gives a general understanding.
مخصص (mukhassis): A specifier that restricts a general ruling.

Ruling from Mujmal with Zanni Bayan is Not Qat'i
Arguments for and against whether a ruling from a mujmal with zanni bayan is qat'i.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the argument that a ruling from a mujmal with a zanni bayan is qat'i, and identify the logical flaw in the majority's reasoning as presented by the author.

Core Idea
The conclusion follows the weaker premise: a certain text clarified by a probable bayan yields only probability.

This passage presents a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute about the epistemological status of rulings derived from an ambiguous (mujmal) text of certain transmission (qat'i al-thubut) when clarified by a probable (zanni) explanation (bayan), such as a single report (khabar wahid). The majority of Hanafis hold that the resulting ruling becomes certain (qat'i), but a minority, including the author, argue that it remains probable. The debate hinges on whether the preponderance (tarjih) provided by the zanni bayan is itself certain or probable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and the two positions
The issue is whether a ruling derived from a mujmal (ambiguous) text of certain transmission, when clarified by a zanni (probable) bayan, is qat'i (certain) or zanni. The majority of Hanafis say qat'i; the author says zanni.
The 'bayan' here is a clarification from a khabar wahid (single report), which is zanni al-thubut.
2
Analyze the majority's proof
The majority argues: khabar wahid gives zann (probability); consensus (ijma') establishes that zann is a preponderant (murajjih); preponderance removes equality, thus removing ambiguity; with ambiguity gone and the original text certain, the ruling becomes certain.
They rely on the idea that zann as a preponderant is established by qat'i ijma'.
3
Examine the author's initial rebuttal
The author counters that the ruling is a necessary consequence of two premises: one certain (the text) and one probable (the bayan). The conclusion follows the weaker premise, so it is probable. He analogizes to the case of a common term (mushtarak) clarified by opinion (ra'y).
This is a simple logical point: a syllogism with one certain and one probable premise yields a probable conclusion.
4
Follow the logical distinction about tarjih
The author questions whether the preponderance (tarjih) from zann is itself certain or probable. If it is only probable (as he insists), then the preventer (ambiguity) is not certainly removed, so the ruling cannot be certain.
This shifts the burden back to the majority to prove that tarjih from zann is qat'i.
5
Understand the logical resolution using propositional types
The author uses a distinction between conditional universal (e.g., 'zann is preponderant as long as it exists') and possible universal (e.g., 'zann may not be preponderant') to show that the majority's claim does not force a contradiction. He argues that the two propositions can coexist without implying a conjunction of opposites.
This is a sophisticated logical move that refutes the claim that zann cannot be both a preponderant and not a preponderant under different conditions.

Companion's Interpretation: Mujmal vs. Zahir
Ruling on Companion's interpretation for mujmal vs. zahir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi ruling on accepting a Companion's interpretation for a mujmal versus a zahir text, and apply the distinction to the khiyar hadith.

Core Idea
A Companion's interpretation of a zahir text is accepted due to uprightness; for a mujmal, it is not because the text lacks definitive meaning.

In usul al-fiqh, the authority of a Companion's interpretation depends on the nature of the text he narrates. This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut clarifies that Hanafis accept such interpretation only when the text is zahir (apparent in meaning), not when it is mujmal (ambiguous). The distinction rests on whether the text itself is proof (hujja) or requires external clarification. We will examine the rationale, the example of the khiyar hadith, and the Hanafi response to opposing views.

1
Identify the two cases
The passage presents two scenarios: (1) a Companion narrates a mujmal text and interprets it on one of its possible meanings; (2) a Companion narrates a zahir text and interprets it contrary to its apparent meaning.
The ruling differs in each case.

Mujmal is a text whose meaning is unknown without explanation from the speaker (the Lawgiver). Zahir is a text whose meaning is apparent but not certain; it can be interpreted contrary to appearance with evidence.
Hanafis hold that mujmal is not proof (hujja) in itself.

Since the mujmal text is not proof in itself, the Companion's interpretation may be based on his own opinion, not on hearing or definite evidence. Thus, it is not binding.
Most Hanafi shaykhs do not accept such ta'wil.
4

A zahir text is already proof in itself. The Companion, being upright, would not depart from the apparent meaning without a definite reason (hearing or witnessed qarina). Therefore, his interpretation is accepted.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on whether a Companion's interpretation of a text he narrates is authoritative. The author compares two cases: when the narrated text is mujmal (ambiguous) and the Companion specifies one meaning, and when the text is zahir (apparent in meaning) but the Companion interprets it contrary to its outward meaning. In the first case, most Hanafi shaykhs do not accept the Companion's interpretation because the text is not evidence in itself and requires external explanation; the Companion's interpretation could be based on opinion, not necessarily on hearing or a definite qarina. In the second case, Hanafis and Hanbalis do accept the Companion's interpretation because the Companion, being upright, would not leave the apparent meaning without a strong reason (either direct hearing or a witnessed qarina). So the difference is: in the first case, there is no certainty that the interpretation is from the Prophet, so it is not binding; in the second, there is an assumption of a compelling reason. The example given is the hadith 'al-bayyi'ani bi'l-khiyar ma lam yatafarraqa'. Ibn Umar interpreted it as khiyar al-majlis (option of session), which the Shafi'is follow. Hanafis, however, interpret it as khiyar al-qabul (option of acceptance) based on the Quranic principle of mutual consent, rejecting Ibn Umar's interpretation. This shows that Hanafis do not accept the Companion's interpretation of a mujmal. But if the same Companion had interpreted a zahir contrary to its appearance, Hanafis would accept it. The passage ends with the beginning of a response to a possible objection about the Companion's error.

Key Terms
المجمل (al-mujmal): A text whose meaning is not known except through explanation from the speaker. According to Hanafis, it cannot be interpreted by opinion.
الظاهر (al-zahir): A text whose meaning is apparent but not certain; it can be interpreted contrary to appearance with evidence.
تأويل الصحابي (ta'wil al-sahabi): The interpretation of a text by a Companion who is also the narrator.

Resolving Apparent Conflict Between Verses on Oaths
Analyzing the meaning of 'aqd al-yamin and resolving the conflict between Quranic verses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the apparent conflict between Quranic verses on oaths (al-Baqarah and al-Ma'idah) and resolve it by applying the Hanafi usul principle that defines 'aqd al-yamin as binding with intent to fulfill a future act.

Core Idea
The two oath verses do not conflict because 'aqd al-yamin means intentional binding to fulfill a future action.

This lesson examines a textual analysis from Fawatih al-Rahamut that resolves an apparent contradiction between Surah al-Baqarah 2:225 and Surah al-Ma'idah 5:89 regarding accountability for oaths. The key lies in correctly understanding the term 'aqd al-yamin (binding oath) as exclusive to oaths made with the intention to fulfill a future act. We will walk through the author's reasoning, linguistic evidence, and legal implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent conflict
Observe that Surah al-Baqarah mentions Allah does not hold you accountable for heedless oaths but holds for what hearts earn, while al-Ma'idah mandates expiation for binding oaths. Some might think they contradict or that one abrogates the other.
The verses: al-Baqarah 2:225 and al-Ma'idah 5:89.
2
Analyze the term 'aqd al-yamin
The author argues that 'aqd in oaths means restriction with intention to fulfill, not mere intention. This is proven linguistically (covenant = 'aqd) and legally (it applies only to future oaths). The heedless oath (without intent to fulfill) is excluded.
Distinguish 'aqd from mere intention: a heedless oath involves intention but not intent to fulfill.
3
Resolve the conflict
With this definition, the verses complement each other: al-Baqarah talks about accountability for intentional oaths (which are 'aqd), and al-Ma'idah prescribes expiation for those same oaths. There is no contradiction.
The apparent conflict is due to misunderstanding 'aqd; once defined correctly, the verses harmonize.
4
Exclude the excluded
The heedless oath (without intention to fulfill) and the false oath (ghamus) are either excluded or covered under different rulings. The verses are silent about these, which is why no conflict arises.
Ghamus is false oath on past; laghw is unintended slip; both are outside 'aqd.
