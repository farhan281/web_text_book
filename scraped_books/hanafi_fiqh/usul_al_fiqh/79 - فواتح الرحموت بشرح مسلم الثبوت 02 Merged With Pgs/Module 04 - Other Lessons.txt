Defining Internal Speech and Its Distinction from Knowledge
Defining internal speech and proving its distinctness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define internal speech (kalam nafsi) and explain why it is distinct from knowledge and uttered speech, using the Hanafi rational proof.

Core Idea
Internal speech is the real meaning in the soul, not the same as knowing or uttering it.

Internal speech is a key concept in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, especially in understanding the nature of the Quran. This lesson unpacks the definition and the rational proof that internal speech is a distinct attribute of the speaker.

Guided Path
1
Define the term
State what internal speech (kalam nafsi) is and what it is not. Distinguish it from uttered speech and knowledge.
2
Identify the opponent's view
Understand the opposing position that equates internal speech with knowledge or mental existence.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
Examine the example of a command (طلب): conceptualizing a command is not the same as having the command subsist in the soul. This proves distinctness.
Key: one can conceptualize a command without being a commander.
4
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
Determine the practical difference: the status of the Quran as uncreated internal speech vs created utterances.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed critical analysis of the concept of internal speech (kalam nafsi) and its relationship to uttered speech (kalam lafzi) and knowledge. The author begins by stating the topic of the four sources (Book, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) but immediately delves into a philosophical discussion. The main argument is that internal speech is a distinct mental entity, not identical to knowledge or will. It is the meaning underlying uttered words, but it is not a simple entity; rather, it is a composite of meanings with relationships. The author refutes the idea that internal speech is the same as knowledge, using the example of constructural speech (like commands): a request is not merely the conceptualization of a requestive relationship, but something that subsists in the soul. The author also discusses whether mental existence is required for internal speech, engaging with views from al-Mukhtasar and Sharh al-Sharh. He argues that the relationship in speech is depictive (hakiya) and cannot be presential knowledge because depiction requires a mental image of the depicted, not the thing itself. The debate includes whether the two extremes of a relationship are conceptualized in the mind or present externally. The author sides with the mutakallimun (theologians) against the philosophers on the unity of knowledge and known. Finally, the passage addresses the theological implications: if internal speech is the real speech of Allah, and uttered speech is a creation, then the Quran would be created, which is unacceptable. The author concludes with the orthodox position that the recited Quran is the uncreated speech of Allah, referencing al-Mawaqif. Throughout, the author uses rational proofs (dalil aqli) and engages in critical refinement of earlier commentaries.

Key Terms
الكلام النفسي (al-kalam al-nafsi): Internal speech: the meanings subsisting in the soul or in Allah's essence that are indicated by uttered words.
الكلام اللفظي (al-kalam al-lafzi): Uttered speech: the physical words pronounced by the tongue.
النسبة (al-nisba): Relationship: the connection between the subject and predicate in a sentence.
التعقل (al-ta'aqqul): Conceptualization: the act of forming a mental image of a thing.
العلم الحضوري (al-'ilm al-huduri): Presential knowledge: knowledge gained by direct presence of the thing known, without a mental image.
الحكاية (al-hikaya): Depiction: the representation of a thing through its image.

Self-Evidence of the Conception of Khabar: A Rational Dispute in Hanafi Usul
Rational argument that conception of khabar is self-evident, with objections and replies

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the argument that the conception of khabar is self-evident, analyze the objections based on knowledge types and vagueness, and evaluate the Hanafi resolution of the dispute.

Core Idea
If distinguishing khabar from other speech is immediate, its conception is self-evident; but opponents argue presence or vague conception suffices.

This lesson examines a foundational rational dispute in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: is the concept of 'khabar' (report) self-evident? The text presents a proof from the necessity of distinction (tamyiz) and grapples with objections involving the difference between presential (huduri) and representational (husuli) knowledge, and the possibility of vague (ijmali) conception. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to analyze the epistemological foundations of legal evidence and the nature of axiomatic concepts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim and the Primary Proof
The author argues that the conception of khabar is self-evident (badihah). The main proof is that we immediately distinguish khabar from other types of speech, and that distinction requires having a conception of khabar. Since the distinction is spontaneous, the conception must be self-evident.
This is a typical argument from the necessity of distinction (tamyiz).
2
Differentiate Huduri and Husuli Knowledge
The text distinguishes between huduri (presential) knowledge, where the object is directly present, and husuli (representational) knowledge, where a mental picture (surah) is required. The objection is that we may have huduri knowledge of a particular khabar, which does not necessarily entail a husuli conception of the absolute khabar.
The author uses this to refute the claim that knowing a particular khabar automatically gives conception of the absolute.
3
Analyze the 'Mere Obtaining' Objection
The opponent says that mere obtaining (husul) – i.e., having the object present – suffices for distinction without requiring a conception. The author counters that distinction requires at least some knowledge, but not necessarily the detailed husuli conception.
This objection challenges the premise that distinction entails conception (tasawwur).
4
Examine the 'Vague Conception' Objection
Even if the proof yields a conception, the opponent argues it may be vague (ijmal) rather than detailed (tafsil). The author notes that the dispute is about conception of the essence (kunnah), not just a vague notion.
This nuance shows that the self-evidence claimed is of the essence, not a mere aspect.
5
Evaluate the Overall Force and the Author's Conclusion
The author ultimately points out that the proof is attributed to Imam al-Razi, who holds that all conceptions are self-evident. The text ends inconclusively, inviting reflection (fa-taramal). This indicates the depth of the dispute and the need for careful analysis.
The author does not decisively settle the issue but presents the arguments for advanced scrutiny.

Commentary
This passage is a rational dispute about whether the tasawwur (conception) of 'khabar' (report) is badihah (self-evident). The author, commenting on Sharh al-Mukhtasar, presents an argument that since we immediately distinguish khabar from other types of speech, we must already have a conception of it, and that conception must be self-evident because we do not acquire it through reflection. He then considers and refutes several objections: that mere presence (husul) is enough for distinction, that the conception could be vague (ijmal) rather than detailed (tafsil), and that the proof would also apply to other concepts like commands. He also engages with the distinction between huduri (presential) and husuli (representational) knowledge, arguing that huduri knowledge of the particular does not entail husuli knowledge of its essentials. The author ultimately notes that the proof is attributed to al-Razi, who holds all conceptions self-evident, and leaves the issue unresolved, instructing the reader to reflect (fa-ta'amal). This is a dense philosophical discussion typical of later Hanafi usul works.

خبر (khabar): A report or proposition that is capable of being true or false. The subject of this analysis regarding its conception.

Critique of the Mutakallimun's Definition of Khabar
Definition and critique of khabar by the Mutakallimun.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Mutakallimun's definition of khabar, identify the two objections raised against it (divine speech and permanence of truth/falsehood), and explain why the definition is considered inadequate in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A khabar's truth value is permanent, so defining it as 'admitting truth and falsehood' fails for every actual report.

The definition of terms in usul al-fiqh is not merely semantic; it directly impacts how evidence is classified and used. In this passage, the author examines the definition of khabar (report) proposed by the Mutakallimun — al-Baqillani, Abd al-Jabbar, and the Mu'tazila — and subjects it to a rigorous critique. Understanding this critique sharpens your own ability to evaluate definitions in usul.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition
Identify the exact definition given by the Mutakallimun: 'speech that admits truth and falsehood' (kalam yadkhuluhu al-sidq wa al-kidhb). Note who proposed it (al-Qadi al-Baqillani or Abd al-Jabbar and the Mu'tazila).
2
Formulate the first objection
The definition fails to include the speech of Allah and the prophets, which are always true and never false. Thus the definition is too narrow (cannot encompass divine and prophetic speech).
This is a simple counterexample, but the author presents a deeper objection.
3
Formulate the second, stronger objection
The better objection is that the definition does not apply to any individual report. A true report is always true, a false report always false. No single report admits both truth and falsehood simultaneously or at different times, because truth and falsehood are permanent attributes of a given report.
This objection targets the very notion of 'admitting' both.
4
Refute a potential counterargument
Someone might claim that the definition means a report can be true at one time and false at another. The author refutes this by distinguishing between the realization of the report (its occurrence) and its truth value. The truth value of a proposition, once fixed, does not change. Therefore the definition still fails.
This shows the author's thoroughness in addressing all possible responses.

Defining Khabar: Truth, Falsehood, and the Capacity Objection
Critical analysis of khabar definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul distinction between a report's capacity for truth/falsehood and its actual truth value, and evaluate the objections to the Mu'tazili definition of khabar.

Core Idea
Khabar is defined by capacity for truth and falsehood, not by their actual occurrence.

What makes a statement a 'report' (khabar)? Is it the fact that it can be true or false, even if in reality it is always one or the other? In this lesson, we examine the influential definition of khabar by al-Baqillani and the Mu'tazila and the sophisticated objections raised against it. You will learn to distinguish the capacity for truth/falsehood from the actual truth value, a distinction critical for Hanafi legal theory when handling divine speech and perpetual truths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
State the definition of khabar proposed by al-Baqillani and the Mu'tazila: 'speech that admits truth and falsehood' (kalam yadkhuluhu al-sidq wa al-kidhb).
This is a real definition (ta'rif haqiqi) aiming at the essence.
2
Understand the strong objection
The objection: a true report is always true, falsehood never enters it; a false report is always false, truth never enters it. Thus no individual report admits both, so the definition fails to apply to any of its supposed individuals.
This objection targets the definition's extension, not just divine speech.
3
Examine the responses
Several responses are given: (1) The definition refers to capacity (i.e., the essence can admit both), not that both actually occur together. (2) The conjunction 'and' can be interpreted as 'or' (one of them). (3) Rational possibility: when the report is abstracted from its context, the intellect deems both truth and falsehood possible. (4) Linguistic possibility: language permits both truth and falsehood for any report, even if reason denies it for divine speech.
The commentator finds some responses to be takalluf (unwarranted stretch).
4
Distinguish real vs nominal definition
The author notes that if the definition is taken as nominal (rasm), the objections about individual application may not hold because the definition describes the defined by accidents, not essence.
This suggests a way out: the definition of khabar might not be a true real definition.
5
Determine the Hanafi position
Hanafi usul ultimately prefers either a nominal definition or the definition 'what admits verification and falsification' (tasdiq wa takdhib) to avoid the circularity and applicability issues.
This impacts how the Qur'an is classified as khabar in legal reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a critical analysis of the definition of khabar (report) as proposed by al-Baqillani, Abd al-Jabbar, and the Mu'tazila. They define it as 'speech that admits truth and falsehood'. The author raises several objections. First, divine speech and the speech of prophets are never false, so the definition seems to exclude them. The better objection is that every report is either always true or always false, so it never admits both. The true report is true forever, so falsehood never enters it; likewise, a false report is false forever, so truth never enters it. Hence the definition does not apply to any individual report. The author responds by clarifying that the definition means that the essence of a report has the capacity for truth and falsehood, not that both simultaneously occur in any single instance. A further objection about circularity (that verifying the report-ness of a report depends on another report) is dismissed as not entailing circularity; it merely requires sequential awareness. Another response interprets the conjunction 'and' as 'or', meaning either truth or falsehood can enter. A more sophisticated answer distinguishes rational possibility from actual occurrence: by abstracting from external factors, the very nature of a report (as an informational statement) makes truth and falsehood conceptually possible. Additionally, a linguistic answer: language permits both truth and falsehood for any report, even if the intellect denies it for certain cases like divine speech. Finally, the commentator clarifies that truth is not the meaning of 'report' per se; rather, truth is the correspondence of the report to reality, which may or may not obtain. Thus, the definition is defended as sound.

Resolving the Circularity Objection in Defining Khabar
Addressing circularity in defining khabar and proposing a definition via affirmation/negation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the circularity objection to defining khabar by truth/falsehood, identify three Hanafi responses, and apply the final resolution using affirmation/negation (ijab/salb).

Core Idea
Defining khabar by truth is circular unless truth is reinterpreted as affirmation/negation.

In usul al-fiqh, defining khabar (report) as 'what is either true or false' seems intuitive. However, if truth and falsehood themselves are defined in terms of correspondence of a report to reality, we fall into circularity: to know what a report is, we need truth; to know truth, we need report. This lesson examines the Hanafi responses to this objection, culminating in a definition that escapes circularity by using pure affirmation and negation — a solution attributed to Ibn Sina.

Guided Path
1
Identify the circularity objection
Understand how defining khabar by truth/falsehood leads to circularity: if truth 'is correspondence of khabar to reality', then understanding truth requires prior knowledge of 'khabar', but the definition of khabar requires truth. This is vicious circularity.
The objection was raised against earlier definitions and considered fatal by Ibn al-Hajib.
2
Examine the three Hanafi responses
The text presents three ways to break the circle: (1) truth/falsehood are self-evident (badihi) concepts, so their conception does not depend on khabar. (2) Redefine truth as correspondence of the relation, not of the report itself, avoiding the term 'khabar'. (3) The concept of truth already assumes knowledge of 'report' in a different respect, so the definition is not circular.
Each response targets the dependence of truth on khabar from a different angle.
3
Analyze the attempted escape via tasdiq/takdhib
Some scholars defined khabar as 'what admits verification (tasdiq) and falsification (takdhib)', thinking these do not presuppose knowledge of khabar. However, tasdiq and takdhib are judgments of truth and falsehood, so they still involve the circularity.
Thus, the escape fails unless tasdiq/takdhib are redefined.
4
Understand the final resolution: ijab/salb
The text, citing Ibn Sina, reinterprets tasdiq and takdhib as pure affirmation (ijab) and negation (salb), which are logical operations that do not involve truth value. Therefore, khabar is 'what admits affirmation' — a definition free of circularity.
This solution preserves the intuitive link between khabar and truth while avoiding the logical circle.

Commentary
This passage deals with the objection of circularity (al-dawar) in defining khabar by truth and falsehood. If truth is defined as the correspondence of a report to reality, then understanding truth requires prior knowledge of 'report', creating a circle. Ibn al-Hajib claimed no answer exists. Third, the concept of truth and falsehood already assumes knowledge of 'report' in a different respect, so no circularity. تكذيب (takdhib): Falsification: judging a proposition as false.

Defining Khabar and Resolving the Circularity Objection
Commentary defending the definition of khabar against circularity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of khabar, identify the circularity objection, and explain at least two Hanafi responses that resolve it.

Core Idea
Khabar is speech that admits truth and falsehood; circularity is avoided because truth and falsehood are self-evident.

In Hanafi usul, defining khabar (news/report) as speech that can be true or false seems straightforward, but critics like Ibn al-Hajib argue it is circular: truth is defined as correspondence of khabar to reality, so the definition presupposes what it defines. This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut presents several cogent responses, showing that the definition is sound. We will walk through the definitions, the objection, and the Hanafi resolutions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proposed definitions of khabar
Locate the two main definitions in the text: (1) speech that admits truth and falsehood, and (2) speech that by itself conveys a ratio (Abu al-Husayn).
The first is the standard Hanafi definition.
2
State the circularity objection
The objection: truth is defined as correspondence of khabar to reality, and falsehood as lack thereof. Defining khabar by truth/falsehood would be circular because the definiens depends on the definiendum.
Raised by Ibn al-Hajib.
3
Explain the primary response: self-evidence
The commentator argues that the concepts of truth and falsehood are simple and self-evident (ḍarūrī); their conception does not depend on the concept of khabar. Everyone understands 'true' and 'false' without knowing 'khabar'.
This is the strongest response.
4
Outline alternative responses
Other responses include: (a) interpreting 'wa' as 'aw' (or) so that one of them enters; (b) truth/falsehood are correspondence/non-correspondence of the ratio, not of khabar itself; (c) using tasdiq/takdhib (confirmation/denial) instead, though that leads back to circularity.
The commentator refines these.
5
Relate to the definition of speech
The passage also clarifies that khabar must convey a ratio by itself, distinguishing it from insha' (performatives). Imperatives like 'Stand up' are not khabar because their primary denotation is demand, not information.
This connects the definition to usul classification.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed defense of the definition of khabar (news/report) as that which admits truth and falsehood, responding to the objection of circularity (dawr). The author begins by addressing a previous claim that the definition would require that the khabar-ness of each report depends on another, showing that this is not necessary due to the possibility of a drawing definition (rasm). He then presents multiple answers to the circularity objection: (1) truth and falsehood are simple, self-evident concepts not dependent on khabar for their conception; (2) defining khabar by what admits affirmation and negation (tasdiq/takdhib) still leads to circularity because they are judgments of truth/falsehood; (3) following Ibn Sina, affirmation and negation themselves are khabar, so the definition is circular unless we interpret them as assent/denial (idhan/inkar), which avoids circularity. The author finally endorses the definition of Abu al-Husayn al-Basri: speech that by itself conveys a ratio, and clarifies that imperative forms do not qualify as khabar because their primary denotation is the demand, not the informative ratio, unless through implication. The passage also classifies non-khabar speech as insha' and notes the logicians' terminology.

Are Sale Formulas Reports or Performatives? The Hanafi–Shafi'i Usul Dispute
Usul dispute on nature of contract utterances

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that contract utterances are declarative reports (ikhbar) about a mental contract, and rebut the three Shafi'i objections using Hanafi usul principles.

Core Idea
Contract utterances are reports of a mental contract; the Lawgiver accepts them as truthful by implication (iqtida').

In Hanafi usul, the words used for contracts like 'biftu' (I sold) are not performatives that create the contract; they are declarations (ikhbar) about a mental agreement (al‑`aqd al‑qalbi). The Shafi'i school objects on three grounds: lack of external reality, no truth valuation, and tense incompatibility with conditions. This lesson unpacks the debate and the Hanafi responses, showing how the Hanafis maintain the linguistic default while accounting for the Law's treatment.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the core claim: contractual utterances are ikhbar (reports) about a mental contract (al‑`aqd al‑qalbi) that the Lawgiver considers the effective cause (sabab).
The mental contract is the real event; the utterance merely reports it.
2
Understand the Shafi'i objections
List the three objections: (1) no external reality to report about, (2) no truth/falsehood possible, (3) past tense cannot be conditional on the future.
These objections target the very possibility of ikhbar in contracts.
3
Analyze the Hanafi responses
Work through each response: (1) the mental contract is the external reality; (2) truth/falsehood is possible if mental intent is absent, and the Law deems the report true by iqtida'; (3) condition transforms the report into a future-oriented report.
Focus on the mechanism of iqtida' (implication) for truth.
4
Examine the thamarah al‑khilaf
Determine the practical consequence: for Hanafis, contract is formed by mental intent (confirmed by utterance); for Shafi'is, the utterance itself creates the contract.
This difference affects rulings on jest, mistake, and unconscious speech.

Commentary
This passage presents a classic usul al-fiqh debate on whether verbal formulas used for contracts (such as 'biftu' — I sold) are performative utterances (insha') or declarative reports (ikhbar). The Shafi'i school argues that they are performative because they create the contract at the moment of utterance, while the Hanafis maintain that they are declarative, reporting about a prior mental contract (al-`aqd al-qalbi) which the Law recognizes as the effective cause (sabab). The author examines and rebuts three Shafi'i objections. First objection: a report requires an external reality to be reported, but there is no prior sale. Response: the external reality is the mental contract, which the Law considers an effective cause. Second objection: reports can be true or false, but these are never false. Response: they can be false if the mental intent is absent; but because the Law treats them as valid, they are considered true 'by implication' (iqtida'). Third objection: past tense forms cannot be conditional on the future, but these accept conditions. Response: the condition transforms the report into a future report. The author concludes that the Hanafi view is correct: these utterances are reports (ikhbar) about the mental contract that the Lawgiver has designated as the cause. The commentary also addresses tricky cases like jest (hazl) and mistake (khata'), explaining that divorce occurs even in jest due to the underlying mental intent, and in mistake it occurs only in judicial ruling based on the apparent.

الإنشاء (al-insha'): Performative speech that creates a new reality; not subject to truth/falsehood.
العقد القلبي (al-`aqd al-qalbi): The mental contract (intention) that the Lawgiver considers the effective cause (sabab) for the legal effect.
الاقتضاء (al-iqtida'): Implication; the Law's treatment of an utterance as true for legal purposes even if it might not correspond to reality.
النقل (al-naql): Linguistic transfer from the original meaning to a new meaning by convention.

Accepting Narrations from Ahl al-Bid'a al-Ghayr Jaliya
Rule on accepting narrations from those with unclear innovation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule distinguishing unclear innovation from clear innovation and state when an innovator's narration is accepted or rejected.

Core Idea
Unclear innovation does not disqualify a narrator unless he actively calls others to it.

In Hanafi usul, not every innovator is automatically rejected as a narrator. The school distinguishes between clear innovation (bid'a jalīya), which contradicts a definitive proof and renders the narrator a fāsiq, and unclear innovation (bid'a ghayr jalīya), which does not. This lesson unpacks this distinction and the crucial exception: active propagation of the innovation.

Guided Path
1
Define the two types of innovation
Identify the key terms: 'clear innovation' (bid'a jalīya) contradicts a definitive textual proof, while 'unclear innovation' (bid'a ghayr jalīya) does not. Understand that unclear innovation does not make the narrator a sinner (fāsiq).
Example from text: denying that attributes are distinct from the essence is not a clear contradiction.
2
State the default ruling for unclear innovation
The default ruling is that the testimony and narration of such an innovator are accepted by consensus (ittifāqan).
Reason: no opposition to a clear legal command, hence no fisq.
3
Identify the exception
If the innovator actively calls (da'ā) to his innovation, he becomes contentious and is no longer trustworthy. Then his narration is rejected.
The passage argues: 'the caller to desire is contentious and not safe from lying'.
4
Apply the rule to clear innovation
Contrast: a clear innovator is always considered a caller (dā'ī) to his innovation, so his narration is never accepted.
Clear innovation inherently contradicts a clear proof, so the narrator is automatically fāsiq and untrustworthy.
5
Summarize the epistemological basis
The rule is rooted in the Hanafi principle that narrators must be trustworthy (‘adl). Unclear innovation does not break trustworthiness unless accompanied by da'wa, which indicates a willingness to lie for the cause.
This reflects a balanced approach: not all deviation destroys legal reliability.

Core Idea
'Adālah is evidenced by action, not mere inner quality.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut defines 'adālah, a key condition for accepting a hadith narrator. It clarifies that 'adālah must be present when the narrator performs (ada') the narration, not just when they heard it (tahammul). The definition centers on piety (taqwā) and manliness (murū'a), but the author emphasizes that the decisive factor is observable conduct—avoidance of major sins and base actions—since the inner quality is hidden and cannot be directly verified.

Hanafis consider both religious and social dimensions in 'adālah.

Three viewpoints on tazkiya via narration

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three positions on riwāyat al-‘adl ‘an al-majhūl and identify the preferred Hanafi view.

Core Idea
A trustworthy narrator's report may validate an unknown narrator, but only if the reporter is known to vet his sources.

When a trustworthy narrator reports from a person whose reliability is unknown, does that automatically validate the unknown? In Hanafi usul, there are three positions. This passage presents them clearly, and the preferred view is a detailed one based on the narrator's habit.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Issue
Recognize that the core problem is whether a trustworthy narrator's narration from an unknown person constitutes tazkiya (validation) of that unknown person's reliability.
The unknown person is 'majhūl' – one whose 'adāla (trustworthiness) is not established.
2
State the First Position
The first position holds that the narration itself is a ta'dil (validation) of the unknown person. The reasoning is that a trustworthy narrator (al-‘adl) does not ordinarily narrate from someone unless they are also trustworthy.
This view assumes the narrator's habit is to only transmit from reliable sources.
3
State the Second Position
The second position denies that the narration serves as ta'dil. It argues that the narrator might rely on the listener's own verification and does not necessarily vouch for the unknown person's reliability.
This view gives more weight to the possibility that the narrator did not verify the source.
4
State the Third Position
The third position distinguishes based on the narrator's known practice. If the narrator is known to only narrate from trustworthy persons, then the narration is ta'dil. Otherwise, it is not.
This is called 'tafsīl' – a detailed approach.
5
Identify the Preferred View
The text explicitly states that the third position is the most just (al-a‘dal) and apparent (al-zāhir). In Hanafi usul, this detailed view is the operative position.
Thus, when evaluating a narration from an unknown person, one must check the reporter's known practice.

The Condition Does Not Exceed the Conditioned in Tazkiyah
Establishes the principle of parity with nass exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that the number of tazkiyahs matches the number of required witnesses or narrators, and identify the two textual exceptions.

Core Idea
Tazkiyah's number matches the requirement of the original act, except by nass.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut explains a key usul rule: the condition for verifying integrity (tazkiyah) should align with the number of witnesses or narrators needed in the original act. While the general principle is parity, there are two exceptions explicitly based on textual evidence (nass). Understanding this rule helps in Hanafi usul when determining the requirements for accepting testimony and narration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its condition
Determine which act (shahada or riwaya) is being conditioned by tazkiyah. The passage states that tazkiyah is a condition for both testimony and narration.
2
Derive the principle by induction
The text uses induction (istiqra) from shariah practice to establish that the condition equals the conditioned. For example, since one narrator is accepted, one tazkiyah suffices for narration; since two witnesses are required, two tazkiyahs are needed for testimony.
Induction is an accepted method of proof in Hanafi usul.
3
Apply to specific cases
Apply the principle: for narration, one tazkiyah suffices; for testimony, two tazkiyahs are required. Also, the strongest Hanafi position accepts tazkiyah from any just person (even a slave or woman) because their narration is accepted.
This is the general rule; note the objector's remark about induction.
4
Recognize the two textual exceptions
First exception: for the hilal (crescent moon) witness, one witness suffices, so one tazkiyah is enough—this is an increase of condition over conditioned. Second exception: for zina witnesses, four are required, so two tazkiyahs are insufficient—a decrease. Both exceptions are established by nass.
These exceptions are explicitly based on texts and do not contradict the general principle.
5
Understand that exceptions do not invalidate the principle
The author concludes that the two cases are exceptions from the universal principle and do not harm it. The principle remains valid as the default rule.
This is a common usul technique: specific exceptions leave the general rule intact.

Definition of Ta'arud and Its Impossibility in Divine Law
Defining ta'arud and proving its impossibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ta'arud, explain why it cannot occur in reality in divine law, and identify the correct Hanafi procedure when an apparent conflict between proofs arises.

Core Idea
Ta'arud is impossible in Allah's law; apparent conflict demands abrogation, preponderance, or recourse to lower proofs.

This lesson introduces the concept of ta'arud (conflict) between legal proofs, a key topic in usul al-fiqh. The Hanafi position is that true conflict cannot occur in divine law because the Lawgiver's proofs are necessarily consistent. Any apparent conflict stems from human limitations such as ignorance of chronology, misunderstanding, or flawed reasoning. The passage then details the steps to resolve such apparent conflicts: checking for abrogation, seeking preponderance, attempting reconciliation, and ultimately resorting to lower-ranking evidences.

Guided Path
1
Define Ta'arud and Its Conditions
Identify the definition of ta'arud: the mutual repulsion of two proofs, requiring unity of time, ruling, and subject. Understand that these conditions are necessary for a true conflict.
The term 'تدافع الحجتين' emphasizes a reciprocal opposition.
2
Rational Argument for Impossibility in Reality
Follow the logical argument: if both proofs are valid and have true premises, their conclusions must both be true in reality. Conflicting conclusions would require a contradiction in the Lawgiver's intent, which is impossible. Therefore, conflict cannot occur in reality.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) based on the nature of divine legislation.
3
Distinguish Apparent from Real Conflict
Recognize that what appears as conflict is only due to ignorance of history, error in understanding, or faulty analogy. This applies equally to definitive (qat'i) and speculative (zanni) proofs.
The author criticizes the Shafi'i view that conflict is possible in zanni proofs, calling it arbitrary.
4
Outline the Ruling Hierarchy
Learn the hierarchical rules: if chronology is known, the later abrogates the earlier; if unknown, seek preponderance; if not possible, attempt reconciliation; if still not possible, both proofs fall and recourse is taken to a lower evidence.
The specific order: if both are verses, go to khabar wahid; if both are khabars, go to Sahaba statements or qiyas.
5
Analyze the First Investigation
Examine the objection that a lower proof (like khabar) cannot serve as a preponderator for one of the conflicting verses. Understand the Hanafi response: proofs from the same source (e.g., two verses from Allah) fall together, but a proof from another source (Sunnah) remains valid and can be used.
This leads to the principle that upon conflict of two verses, the mutawatir Sunnah is consulted.

Commentary
This passage defines ta'arud (conflict) as the mutual contradiction of two evidences in a single legal case, requiring unity of time, ruling, and subject. It argues that in divine law, ta'arud cannot occur in reality because the Lawgiver's proofs are consistent; any apparent conflict arises from human ignorance (e.g., unknown chronology, misinterpretation, or faulty analogy). The author refutes the Shafi'i view that ta'arud is possible in zanni (speculative) evidences but not in qat'i (definitive) ones, calling it arbitrary, and insists that if the premises of a proof are true, the conclusion must follow, so conflict would entail contradiction in Allah's law. The ruling on apparent conflict: if the earlier proof is known, the later is abrogated; if chronology is unknown, seek tarjih (preponderance); if still impossible, combine if possible; otherwise, both fall (tasqut). When both fall, recourse to a lower evidence in the hierarchy: for verses, go to khabar wahid; for khabars, go to Sahaba statements or qiyas. The first investigation rebuts the idea that a subordinate evidence (like khabar) can serve as a preponderator; instead, the Hanafi position is that two evidences from the same source (e.g., two verses) fall together, but an evidence from a different source (Sunnah) remains valid. This leads to the principle that upon conflict of two verses, one takes the mutawatir Sunnah.

The Principle of Tarjih: Preferring the Rajih over the Marjuh
Explaining the principle of tarjih with a hadith example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of tarjih to resolve an apparent conflict between two proofs by identifying the rajih and justifying why it is preferred.

Core Idea
When two proofs conflict, the stronger (rajih) is preferred; the weaker (marjuh) is set aside as if not a proof.

This passage demonstrates the core usul principle of tarjih—preferring the stronger evidence over the weaker. Using the example of conflicting hadiths on drinking urine, Imam Abu Hanifa shows how to apply tarjih in practice. Understanding this principle is essential for resolving apparent contradictions in Islamic legal sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting proofs
Find the two hadiths that appear to conflict: one warning against urine (prohibition) and the story of the Prophet allowing camel urine for medicine (permission).
The prohibition hadith is narrated by al-Hakim; the permission hadith is in Bukhari and Muslim.
2
Classify which is rajih and which is marjuh
According to the passage, the prohibition hadith is stronger because it warns of punishment, making it rajih. The permission hadith is marjuh.
Strength can be from the text itself or external factors like chain authenticity.
3
Apply the principle of tarjih
Prefer the rajih over the marjuh. The marjuh is not considered a proof in the presence of the rajih.
This does not mean the marjuh is invalid; it is simply not acted upon when a stronger proof exists.
4
Note alternative interpretations
Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Yusuf attempted reconciliation (jam‘) by restricting the general hadith, but Abu Hanifa’s tarjih leads to absolute prohibition.
This shows tarjih is sometimes preferred even when jam‘ is theoretically possible.
5
Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Abu Hanifa prohibits drinking urine absolutely; others permit it in medicinal or specific contexts.
This affects fatwa and daily practice.

Precedence of Affirmation over Negation in Usul
Rule with applications and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that affirmation is preferred over negation when the negation is based solely on the original state, and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute on this rule.

Core Idea
Affirmation trumps negation only when negation lacks direct evidence.

When conflicting reports arise in legal reasoning, how do we prioritize? The Hanafi school holds that affirmation (ithbat) is generally preferred over negation (nafi), but this lesson reveals a crucial condition: this preference holds only when the negation is based on the original state (al-asl) without specific evidence. If the negation has its own proof, the reports are in conflict and require further preponderance. We will explore this principle through two case studies and the intra-Hanafi disagreement of Imam 'Isa ibn Aban.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and the ikhtilaf
Read the opening of the passage: 'Affirmation is preferred over negation when they conflict, as in testimony according to al-Karkhi and the Shafi'ites. Imam 'Isa ibn Aban said they conflict.' Note that the disagreement is not between schools but within the Hanafi school itself. The author sides with al-Karkhi and Fakhr al-Islam.
We are dealing with an intra-Hanafi, not cross-madhhab, dispute.
2
Understand the condition: negation based on original state
The chosen view distinguishes whether the negation is based on the original state (al-asl) or on specific evidence. If the negation is merely 'he was originally so' without proof of change, affirmation with evidence is preferred. If both have evidence, they conflict.
The original state (asl) here refers to the initial state before any change is known.
3
Apply the rule to the case of Barirah's husband
The passage presents two reports: her husband Mughith was originally a slave (negation of freedom), and later a report says he was free (affirmation). The report of slavery is based on the original state (he was known as a slave before), while the report of freedom has evidence (his emancipation). Therefore, affirmation (freedom) is given precedence, and the choice for Barirah is established even if he was free.
The legal effect: Barirah's right to choose remains, and the reason is not disgrace but the prevention of increased ownership.
4
Apply the rule to the case of Maymunah's marriage
Ibn 'Abbas reports the Prophet (s) married Maymunah while in ihram (negation of permissibility). Other reports say he was halal (affirmation). Both reports seem to have evidence. The author examines the strength of chains and also argues that the halal report may be based on original state (no ihram before departure). The discussion illustrates when negation is based on evidence (like Ibn 'Abbas's report) and when it is based on asl.
The case is more complex, and the author ultimately finds the evidence for negation stronger, showing the nuance of the rule.
5
Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence of the disagreement: if one follows 'Isa ibn Aban and treats affirmation and negation as equal, then in every case of conflicting reports, further preponderance (tarjih) would be needed immediately. The majority Hanafi view streamlines the process by giving affirmation automatic preference when negation is asl-based.
This distinction affects how we resolve contradictions in hadith and legal testimony.

Core Idea
When a report's basis is principle, it is not legal evidence; when it is evidence, istishab tips the scale.

This lesson examines a nuanced usul rule: how do we treat a report about a ruling when its basis is unclear? Dispute on Liability of Condition Witnesses in Suspended Oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two positions in the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether witnesses who testify to the condition (shart) in a suspended oath case are liable if they retract, and evaluate the chosen view.

Core Idea
When retracting witnesses of a condition cause loss, some Hanafis hold them liable as transgressors; others blame only the oath witnesses.

The text presents a scenario: two witnesses testify that a man swore to manumit upon entering a house, another two testify to the entry (the condition). Guided Path

Understand the scenario

State the assumed case
If only the condition witnesses retract (without the oath witnesses retracting), there is ikhtilaf. The ruling is still in effect because the oath witnesses still stand.
The condition witnesses retract alone, claiming false testimony.

Present Fakhr al-Islam's position (chosen view)
They are liable because they committed false testimony, which is a major sin and transgression. The loss is attributed to their transgression.

Present al-Sarakhsi's position

Ibn al-Humam chose this view.

Istishab: The Hanafi Rejection and Its Proof
Full Hanafi dialectical analysis of istishab.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi mukhtar position on istishab and explain why existence does not necessitate continuance.

Core Idea
Past existence does not prove present existence; istishab is not a legal proof.

Istishab, the presumption of continuity, is a key dispute in usul. While the Shafi'is accept it absolutely, the Hanafi school, in its mukhtar view, rejects it entirely. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi proof and its profound implication: the missing person does not inherit because life is unknown, not presumed.

Guided Path
1
Define istishab
State the definition: inferring present occurrence from past occurrence. This is the starting point.
al-istishab: al-istidlal bi al-tahaqquq fi al-madi 'ala al-wuqu' fi al-hal.
2
Identify the three Hanafi positions
Note that some Hanafis (like al-Maturidi) accept istishab absolutely, some (like Shams al-A'imma) accept it only defensively, and the mukhtar (Ibn al-Humam) negates it entirely.
The text says: 'wa nafahu kathir... wa 'alayhi al-shaykh ibn al-humam wa huwa al-mukhtar'.
3
Apply to the missing person case
Consider the missing person (mafqud): Shafi'is rule he inherits because he is presumed alive by istishab; Hanafis rule he does not because life is unknown and istishab is not a proof.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf.
4
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The proof: 'ma yujib al-wujud la yujib al-baqa' (what necessitates existence does not necessitate continuance). The cause may cease, so existence alone does not guarantee continuance.
This is a rational argument (dalil 'aqli).
5
Respond to the first objection
Opponents say prior existence with no known opposite gives speculation of continuance, like sending gifts. The Hanafi response: speculation is not legal proof; it requires appointment by the Lawgiver.
The text says: 'al-ghann al-hadith bi nash al-shari' huwa al-mu'tabar'.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul dispute on istishab (presumption of continuity). It defines istishab as inferring present occurrence from past occurrence. The positions: Shafi'is and some Hanafis (like al-Maturidi) accept it absolutely; other Hanafis (Abu Zayd, Shams al-A'imma, Fakhr al-Islam) accept it only for defense (daf'), not for imposition (ilzam); the chosen (mukhtar) view is total negation (Ibn al-Humam). The practical consequence (thamaratu al-khilaf) is inheritance of a missing person (mafqud): Shafi'is allow inheritance because he is presumed alive, Hanafis deny because he is not known alive. The Hanafi proof: existence does not necessitate continuance; the cause of existence may cease. Objections and responses: opponents argue that prior existence with no known opposite gives speculation of continuance (like rational people sending gifts). Reply: speculation is not certain, and legal proof requires appointment (nassb) by the Lawgiver. Further: istishab is not needed for the continuance of Sharia because decisive proofs (tawatur) and divine obligation ensure it. The passage ends with a note that the Prophetic Sharia is certain by decisive proofs, not istishab.

Key Terms
الاستصحاب (al-istishab): Inference of present occurrence from past occurrence; presumption of continuity.
الحجة الدافعة (al-hujja al-dafi'a): A proof that repels a claim against the current status quo, but does not establish a new obligation.
المفقود (al-mafqud): A person who has disappeared and whose life or death is unknown.

Istishab: Speculation, Doubt, and the Certainty of Sharia
Full Hanafi dialectical analysis of istishab (continued).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Shafi'i argument from rational practice and explain why the Hanafi school holds that istishab is unnecessary for the continuity of Sharia.

Core Idea
Legal proof requires appointed speculation; istishab gives only doubt, and Sharia's continuity is secured by decisive proof.

The Shafi'i school argues that rational people act on istishab—sending gifts assumes the messenger is alive. The Hanafi response cuts deeper: such action is based on doubt, not legal speculation. Moreover, the continuity of Sharia itself does not depend on istishab but on tawatur and divine obligation. This lesson examines these key exchanges.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Shafi'i argument from rational conduct
Opponents claim that sending gifts and messengers relies on istishab; if existence did not imply continuance, one could not assume the messenger lives.
This is the 'tasrifat al-'uqala' argument.
2
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal: speculation vs. legal proof
The Hanafi response: even if speculation arises, it is not legal speculation because it lacks appointment (nassb) by the Lawgiver. Only legislated speculation counts.
Key condition: 'al-zann alladhi hadatha bi nash al-shari''.
3
Examine the alternative explanation: actions based on doubt or illusion
Rational people may act based on doubt or precaution (ihtiyat), not speculation. So the argument from practice collapses.
The text says: 'rubba takun al-tasarrufat mabniyyatan 'ala al-shakk wa al-wahm'.
4
Refute the Sharia continuity argument
Opponents say without istishab, continuance of Sharia is uncertain. Hanafi: tawatur and the Lawgiver's obligation to act until abrogation suffice; istishab is not needed.
The text: 'al-jawab man' al-mulazima li jawaz al-tawatur wa ijab al-'amal'.
5
Identify the final epistemological conclusion
Certainty (qat') is not claimed for all laws; only the Prophetic Sharia is certain by decisive proofs, not istishab.
The text ends: 'al-qat' fima qama 'ala baqa'ihi dalil qat'i'.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

One of the most debated usul issues is whether istishab (presumption of continuity) serves as an independent proof. The Hanafi school takes a firm stance: rulings persist not because of istishab, but because the original act (in-sha') necessitates continuity until a contradictor appears. (Ponder), for it is worthy of acceptance.

Commentary
This passage explains talazum (correlation between two legal rulings) as a method of inference that some consider additional to the four primary sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas). The Shafi'is classify talazum and istishab under 'istidlal', which they define as any proof outside the four. The Hanafis here clarify that talazum is not a separate source; rather, it is a specific way of using the four sources. Talazum exists when two rulings are linked such that one necessarily implies the other. The passage categorizes talazum into types: between two affirmatives (mutual implication), between one side (one-way implication), between negation and affirmation, etc. Examples are given, such as the case of a hermaphrodite (khuntha) where rulings are deduced from the impossibility of both sexes. The key insight is that talazum is a logical form of argument (like a syllogism) but its content is derived from the four sources, especially the revealed texts. The correlation itself is not rational (since reason cannot independently know legal rulings) but shar'i, established through the divine law. Therefore, talazum is a tool of inference, not an independent evidence.

Key Terms
التلازم (talazum): Necessary correlation between two legal rulings such that one implies the other without specification of the cause.
الاستدلال (istidlal): Inference; for Shafi'is, it denotes proof beyond the four sources; for Hanafis, it is a method of applying the sources.
العلة (illa): The effective cause that necessitates a ruling; specifying it transforms talazum into qiyas.
