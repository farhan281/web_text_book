The Hanafi Method for Resolving Ta'arud (Conflict of Evidences)
States the sequential rule for resolving ta'arud.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the sequential Hanafi method for resolving ta'arud (apparent contradiction) between two evidences, correctly identifying the appropriate step (naskh, tarjih, jam', tasaqut, or resorting to lower evidence) based on the conditions described in the text.

Core Idea
When two evidences conflict, apply a strict sequence: abrogate if possible, prefer if possible, reconcile if possible, drop both only as last resort.

This lesson presents the Hanafi framework for handling apparent contradictions between legal proofs. Unlike some other schools, the Hanafis do not allow arbitrary selection; instead, they prescribe a step-by-step methodology that prioritizes the preservation of both proofs as much as possible. The text from Fawatih al-Rahamut outlines this hierarchy, which is fundamental to Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two conflicting evidences
Determine the two sources (e.g., two Quranic verses, two hadiths, or a verse and a hadith) that appear to conflict. Note their legal rulings.
The conflict must be genuine and irreconcilable at first glance.
2
Check for naskh (abrogation)
If the chronological order of revelation is known and both evidences are eligible for abrogation, apply naskh: the later evidence abrogates the earlier. This is the first and most straightforward step.
Naskh requires knowledge of which came first and that the rulings are of a type that can be abrogated.
3
Attempt tarjih (preference)
If naskh is not possible, look for a preponderant factor (murajjih) that makes one evidence stronger (e.g., more narrators, greater certainty). Act upon the preponderant evidence.
Tarjih is based on objective criteria, not personal opinion.
4
Attempt jam' (reconciliation)
If tarjih is not possible, try to reconcile the two evidences by interpreting each in a way that both can be applied, even if one is given a specific application. Reconcile to the extent possible.
Jam' preserves both evidences, which is preferred over discarding any.
5
Apply tasaqut (falling away) and resort to lower evidence
If jam' is impossible, both evidences are dropped (tasaqut) because acting on one arbitrarily is invalid. Then, resort to the next lower category of evidence: if the conflict was between verses, use a khabar wahid; if between two reports, use sayings of Companions or qiyas.
Arbitrary selection (takhayyur) is rejected because one of the conflicting evidences must be invalid.

Commentary
This passage is a statement of the Hanafi rule for resolving ta'arud (conflict) between evidences. The commentator explains the sequential steps: first, check if naskh (abrogation) applies by knowing the chronological order. If not, perform tarjih (preference) if a preponderant factor exists. If tarjih is not possible, attempt jam' (reconciliation) between the two evidences. If reconciliation is impossible, both evidences are dropped (tasaqut) because arbitrarily choosing one would be invalid. The text explicitly rejects takhayyur (arbitrary selection) as it would imply choosing between a valid and an invalid ruling. Once both evidences are dropped, one must resort to the next lower evidence in the hierarchy: if the conflict is between two Quranic verses, one uses a khabar wahid (single-narrator hadith); if between two khabars, one uses sayings of the Companions or qiyas (analogy). النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation of a ruling by a later evidence, applicable when chronological order is known.
الترجيح (al-tarjīḥ): Preferring one evidence over another due to a preponderant factor.
الجمع (al-jam‘): Reconciling two evidences by interpreting each in a way that both can be applied.
التساقط (al-tasāquṭ): Both evidences are considered invalid and dropped due to irreconcilable conflict.

Why Conflicting Qiyas Do Not Fall Away Like Texts
Proof that conflicting qiyas do not fall away while conflicting nass do

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that conflicting qiyas do not fall away (tasāquṭ) and explain the reasoning based on the purpose of nass vs qiyas.

Core Idea
Nass aims at certainty, so conflict nullifies knowledge; qiyas aims only at action, so conflict does not remove obligation to act.

This passage presents a foundational Hanafi usul argument distinguishing between conflicting texts (nass) and conflicting analogies (qiyas). You will learn why nass fall away when they conflict, while qiyas do not, and how this difference stems from the Lawgiver's intention for each type of evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of evidence
Recognise that nass (Quran/Sunnah) and qiyas are different categories. Nass is placed to convey God's actual ruling with certainty; qiyas is a tool for deriving rulings based on zann.
2
Understand the rule for conflicting nass
When two nass conflict, one must be abrogated, but we cannot identify which. Since nass aims at certainty, we lose knowledge of the actual ruling, so acting on either is prohibited. Both fall away (tasāquṭ).
3
Understand the rule for conflicting qiyas
When two qiyas conflict, we know one is wrong but not which. However, qiyas does not aim at certainty – it only obligates action based on zann. The conflict does not remove the obligation to act on each individually, so both remain valid for action. They do not fall away.
4
Contrast the outcomes
Compare: nass fall away because the purpose is knowledge of God's actual decree; qiyas do not fall away because the purpose is practical obligation. This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school.
5
Apply to hypothetical scenarios
Practice by considering a case of contradictory hadith vs contradictory analogies. According to Hanafis, you must abandon both hadiths but can choose one qiyas after taharri (investigation).

Commentary
This passage is a sustained Hanafi argument distinguishing between conflicting nass (Quran and Sunnah) and conflicting qiyas. The Hanafis argue: The Book and Sunnah were established to definitively convey God's actual ruling. When they conflict, one must be abrogated, but we cannot identify which, so we have no reliable knowledge; acting on either would risk contradicting God's actual ruling. Hence, both are left aside (tasāqut). In contrast, qiyas was not established to convey God's actual ruling but only to obligate action based on zann (speculative opinion). Even if qiyas points to a ruling that is actually wrong, the Lawgiver still obligates action upon it. So when two qiyas conflict, we know one must be wrong, but we do not know which; however, the obligation to act on each individually remains because qiyas aims only at zann for action, not at certainty. We cannot act on both together because that would be contradictory, but we can act on one after taharri. The key difference is the purpose: nass gives certainty and is meant to identify God's actual decree; qiyas only gives practical obligation. The text uses the same reasoning for qawl al-sahabi (Companion's statement) when considered authoritative, as it is based on opinion like qiyas.

Preferring Prohibition Over Permission: A Usul Principle for Conflicting Reports
Stating the rule of giving precedence to prohibition and its justification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of giving precedence to a prohibiting report over a permitting report in cases of conflict, and evaluate the role of naskh and precaution in this rule.

Core Idea
When reports conflict, act on the prohibition; chronologically, assume the permissive came first to avoid repeated abrogation.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut presents a key usul principle for resolving conflicting reports. When one report prohibits an action and another permits it, the Hanafi school prioritises the prohibition in practice. The text explains the naskh rationale and also addresses an objection regarding the original state of permission, ultimately grounding the rule in precaution (ihtiyat).

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting reports
Isolate the two reports: one prohibits the action (e.g., eating lizard meat), the other permits it. Note the ruling categories: haram vs mubah.
The example comes from Sunan Abi Dawud.
2
Apply chronological priority
Assume the permitting report is earlier in time. This allows the later prohibiting report to abrogate (naskh) the earlier permission, so only one abrogation occurs.
This avoids repeated naskh, which would happen if the prohibition came first and then a later permission abrogated it, followed by another prohibition.
3
Recognise the objection
Imam Fakhr al-Islam objects that this reasoning assumes an original state of permission (ibaha), which the Hanafi school does not accept. The phrase 'lam yutrak sudan' means man was not left without guidance.
The objection challenges the very foundation of the naskh-based argument.
4
Apply the precautionary approach
The text responds by shifting to a precautionary basis: if the prohibition is given precedence in practice, avoiding it involves no sin, whereas if one acts on a later-abrogated permission, one risks falling into hardship. This is safer, as transmitted from Ali (RA).
This principle applies even without assuming ibaha.

Two Actions Never Contradict: Resolving Apparent Conflicts in the Sunnah
Rules on conflict of actions and conflict between action and speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that two actions reported from the Prophet never truly contradict because of difference in time, and determine whether the later action abrogates or specifies the earlier one when repetition is established.

Core Idea
Two actions at different times do not conflict; if repeated, the later action abrogates or specifies the earlier.

When we encounter reports of two actions of the Prophet that appear contradictory, such as raising hands in prayer in one report and not raising in another, we might think the Sunnah is inconsistent. The Hanafi usul provides a clear rule: actions occur at different times, so they can be valid in their respective contexts. Only when repetition is established as a custom does the later action resolve the conflict by abrogating or specifying the earlier. This lesson unpacks that rule and its application.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between two types of apparent conflicts
The passage discusses two scenarios: (1) a report that could be based on original state or evidence, and (2) a conflict between two actions of the Prophet. Identify which scenario the rule addresses.
The rule 'Two actions never contradict' is the core; the first part is a sub-rule on resolving when a report's basis is unknown.
2
Understand the role of time in resolving action conflicts
The key principle is that actions occur at different times, so an action and its opposite can both be valid if done at separate occasions. This eliminates contradiction.
Even if one action is reported later, it does not necessarily negate the earlier one; both can be legitimate.
3
Identify when repetition turns a sequence into a custom
If reports indicate that an action was repeated regularly (e.g., the Prophet always raised hands in a certain manner), the repeated action becomes a custom ('āda). Then the later occurrence can serve to specify or abrogate the earlier general command.
The linguistic marker for repetition is the use of 'kāna' with the imperfect, which indicates habitual action.
4
Resolve the conflict: abrogation or specification?
Hanafis hold that the later action specifies (takhsīṣ) the earlier general command, while Shāfi'īs consider it abrogation (naskh). Understand the difference and its practical implications.
The passage states: 'فالثاني ناسخ أو مخصص على اختلاف قولي الحنفية والشافعية'.
5
Apply the rule to a concrete example
Take the example of raising hands in prayer: reports say the Prophet sometimes raised and sometimes did not. Since both are reported and repetition is not established in a specific way, the later action (if known) would resolve; if unknown, preference (tarjīḥ) is sought.
This illustrates the practical method of using preference (tarjīḥ) when dates are unknown.

Rule on contradiction between two prophetic actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when two prophetic actions contradict with proven repetition (takrār), the later action is nāsikh (abrogating), and contrast this with the Shāfi‘ī view of takhsīṣ (specification).

Core Idea
When a prophetic action is repeated, a later contradictory action abrogates (Hanafi) or specifies (Shāfi‘ī) the earlier.

The Hanafī and Shāfi‘ī schools differ on how to resolve an apparent contradiction between two reported prophetic actions when repetition is established. The Hanafīs hold that the later action abrogates (naskh) the earlier, while the Shāfi‘īs consider it a specification (takhsīṣ) limiting the ruling to a particular context. This lesson unpacks the key dispute and its practical implications.

This stems from a different usul principle on how to reconcile apparent contradictions.

Condition of the 'Illah: Not Adding to the Text
Condition that derived 'illah must not add to text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi condition that a derived 'illah must not add to the text and compare it with the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
A derived 'illah that adds a new ruling to the text is abrogation (naskh), which qiyas cannot perform.

In Hanafi usul, the derived 'illah (effective cause) for qiyas must not introduce a ruling that goes beyond the original text. This condition prevents qiyas from acting as abrogation. The Shafi'i school takes a more permissive stance, viewing non-contradictory additions as specification or restriction. This lesson explores the reasoning and the practical implications of this difference.

2
Understand the Hanafi Reasoning
Recognize that any addition via qiyas changes the original ruling, which constitutes naskh (abrogation). Since qiyas is inferior to nass, it cannot abrogate.
This is based on the hierarchy of evidences: nass > qiyas.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i View
Note that the Shafi'i school permits additions that do not contradict the text, considering them as takhsis (specification) or taqyid (restriction), not naskh.
For Shafi'is, absolute prohibition of addition would prevent takhsis and taqyid, which are valid.
