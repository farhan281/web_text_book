The Four Objections to Suitability (Munāsabah) in Qiyās
Four objections to suitability with answers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four objections to the suitability of an 'illah and explain the corresponding Hanafi answers.

Core Idea
Objections to suitability are answered by showing benefit preponderates, conduciveness is realistic, description is externally indicated, or description is well-defined.

In this passage, the author outlines four classical objections that can be raised against the suitability (munāsabah) of a legal cause ('illah). These objections challenge the 'illah's appropriateness. The answers demonstrate how to defend the 'illah by showing that the benefit outweighs harm, the conduciveness is realistic, the description can be indicated externally, and the description can be well-defined by custom or law. The Hanafis combine these under the objection to suitability (man' al-ṣalāḥiyyah).

Guided Path
1
Identify the four objection types
List the four objections: (1) presenting a preponderant/equal harm, (2) challenging conduciveness to benefit, (3) description being hidden, (4) description being ill-defined.
These are found in the text after the introductory note.
2
Understand the first answer: preponderance of benefit
For the first objection, the answer is that benefit is preponderant over harm generally, or specifically by detailed comparison (e.g., khiyār al-majlis example).
The Shafi'i example shows how to weigh specific benefits and harms.
3
Analyze the second objection: conduciveness
The objection claims the ruling does not conduce to the intended benefit. The answer is that the ruling customarily prevents the harm, and the possibility of opposite effect is rare.
Example: prohibition of marriage to in-laws prevents temptation customarily.
4
Understand the third and fourth answers: hidden and ill-defined descriptions
For hidden descriptions (e.g., consent), the answer is to specify an outward indicator (formulas, actions). For ill-defined descriptions (e.g., hardship), the answer is that they are defined by custom or law through an indicator (e.g., travel defines hardship).
These answers show how to make the 'illah verifiable.
5
Note the Hanafi position on these objections
The text states Hanafis did not list them separately because they include them under the objections to suitability (man' al-ṣalāḥiyyah) and conditions.
This reflects the Hanafi method of structuring objections in qiyās.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed discussion of objections to the suitability (munāsabah) of the ‘illah in qiyās, which is a topic in uṣūl al-fiqh. The author notes that according to the Shafi‘is, there are specific objections related to suitability, but the Hanafis also accept them since suitability is a condition for all. The four objections are: (1) Challenging suitability by presenting a preponderant or equal harm that disrupts the benefit; answered by showing the preponderance of benefit over harm, either generally or specifically. An example is the Shafi‘i argument for the option of the session (khiyār al-majlis) where the seller’s repelling harm outweighs the buyer’s loss of benefit. (2) Challenging the conduciveness (ifḍā’) to the benefit, e.g., prohibition of marriage to remove the veil may lead to increased desire; answered by showing that the prohibition customarily prevents hope and the natural desire is subdued. (3) The description being hidden (khafī), e.g., consent (riḍā) in contracts; answered by specifying an outward indicator like formulas or actions. (4) The description not being well-defined (ghayr munḍabiṭ), e.g., hardship; answered by noting that it can be well-defined by itself, custom, or law. The author then mentions that the Hanafis did not include these as separate objections because they combine them under the objection to suitability (man‘ al-ṣalāḥiyyah). Finally, the fifth objection is naqḍ (inconsistency), which the Hanafis call munāqaḍah. There is a dispute: Imam Fakhr al-Islam holds that naqḍ only applies to tardi (non-effective) ‘illals, while the majority (including the author) hold that it applies to effective ones as well, because naqḍ addresses the suspicion of causality, not the reality. This is akin to the topic of qalb (reversal), which also occurs in effective ‘illals.

Key Terms
المناسبة (al-munāsabah): Suitability of the ‘illah to the ruling, meaning the ‘illah is appropriate for bringing about the wisdom behind the ruling.
الإفضاء (al-ifḍā’): Conduciveness: the 'illah must lead to the intended benefit or wisdom in the ruling.
الغَيْر مُنْضَبِط (ghayr munḍabiṭ): A description that is not well-defined, i.e., it varies in degree and is not fixed.

Naqḍ (Inconsistency) and Its Applicability to Effective 'Illals
Dispute on applicability of naqḍ to effective 'illals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi majority view that naqḍ applies to both effective and tardi 'illals and evaluate the opposing view of Fakhr al-Islam.

Core Idea
Naqḍ addresses suspicion of causality, not reality; thus it can apply to effective 'illals without implying contradiction in divine law.

This passage introduces the fifth objection to an 'illah: inconsistency (naqḍ), also called munāqaḍah by Hanafis. There is a key dispute: Imam Fakhr al-Islam holds that naqḍ only applies to tardi (non-effective) 'illals, because effective 'illals are established by certain proofs and cannot admit contradiction. The majority of usul scholars, including the author (Ibn al-Humam), hold that naqḍ applies to all 'illals because it is directed at the suspicion of causality, not the reality itself.

Guided Path
1
Define naqḍ (inconsistency)
Naqḍ is an objection that the claimed 'illah is present but the ruling is absent, or vice versa. The Hanafis call it munāqaḍah, and others call it man'.
This is the fifth objection mentioned after the four objections to suitability.
2
State the opposing view: Fakhr al-Islam
Imam Fakhr al-Islam argued that naqḍ is specific to tardi 'illals (those that are purely coincidental and not effective). He reasoned that a correct effective 'illah is established by clear proofs (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) which do not contradict, so the effectiveness cannot be contradicted.
This view is also attributed to Shams al-A'imma and others.
3
State the majority view (author's choice)
The majority of usul scholars, including the author, hold that naqḍ applies to both effective and tardi 'illals. The reason is that naqḍ is directed at the suspicion (ẓann) of the observer, not at the reality of the 'illah in itself. The suspicion can be mistaken, so even an effective 'illah can appear to be contradicted in a particular case.
The author uses the analogy of qalb (reversal), which also applies to effective 'illals.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if naqḍ is allowed against effective 'illals, opponents can challenge even strong 'illahs with counterexamples. The Hanafi must then show that the counterexample does not truly contradict because the suspicion is mistaken.
This affects the debate strategy in usul.
5
Identify the author's tarjih
The author chooses the majority view, stating that naqḍ is not specific to tardi 'illals. The evidence is that naqḍ comes on suspicion, which can err, so it does not imply contradiction in the divine law.
The author supports this with the example of qalb, which also runs in effective 'illals.

Commentary
This passage presents a detailed discussion of objections to the suitability (munāsabah) of the ‘illah in qiyās, which is a topic in uṣūl al-fiqh. The author notes that according to the Shafi‘is, there are specific objections related to suitability, but the Hanafis also accept them since suitability is a condition for all. The four objections are: (1) Challenging suitability by presenting a preponderant or equal harm that disrupts the benefit; answered by showing the preponderance of benefit over harm, either generally or specifically. An example is the Shafi‘i argument for the option of the session (khiyār al-majlis) where the seller’s repelling harm outweighs the buyer’s loss of benefit. (2) Challenging the conduciveness (ifḍā’) to the benefit, e.g., prohibition of marriage to remove the veil may lead to increased desire; answered by showing that the prohibition customarily prevents hope and the natural desire is subdued. (3) The description being hidden (khafī), e.g., consent (riḍā) in contracts; answered by specifying an outward indicator like formulas or actions. (4) The description not being well-defined (ghayr munḍabiṭ), e.g., hardship; answered by noting that it can be well-defined by itself, custom, or law. The author then mentions that the Hanafis did not include these as separate objections because they combine them under the objection to suitability (man‘ al-ṣalāḥiyyah). Finally, the fifth objection is naqḍ (inconsistency), which the Hanafis call munāqaḍah. There is a dispute: Imam Fakhr al-Islam holds that naqḍ only applies to tardi (non-effective) ‘illals, while the majority (including the author) hold that it applies to effective ones as well, because naqḍ addresses the suspicion of causality, not the reality. This is akin to the topic of qalb (reversal), which also occurs in effective ‘illals.

Key Terms
النقض (al-naqḍ): An inconsistency in the ‘illah where the claimed ‘illah is present but the ruling is absent, or vice versa.
العلة الطردية (al-‘illah al-ṭardiyyah): A cause that is purely coincidental and does not have a real effect on the ruling.
العلة المؤثرة (al-‘illah al-mu’aththirah): A cause that is effective, i.e., it has a real impact on the ruling as established by textual evidence.
القلب (al-qalb): Reversal: an argument that flips the cause and effect to demonstrate a flaw.

Three Objections to Suitability of an Attribute in Qiyas and Their Hanafi Responses
Presents three objections to suitability of an attribute in qiyas and their Hanafi answers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three objections to munasaba (suitability) of an attribute in qiyas and apply the Hanafi responses to determine whether a given attribute qualifies as a valid illa.

Core Idea
A suitable attribute must be perceptible, well-defined, and actually lead to the benefit—otherwise it fails as a cause.

In Hanafi usul, the suitability (munasaba) of an attribute is a key condition for it to serve as a cause (illa) in qiyas. However, opponents raise three objections: the attribute may be hidden (khafi), ambiguous (ghayr mundabit), or counterproductive (yufdi ilā zidd al-maṣlaḥa). This lesson examines each objection and the Hanafi school's responses, clarifying how a valid illa is established.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Objections
List the three classical objections to the suitability of an attribute: hidden (like consent), ill-defined (like hardship), and counterproductive (like prohibiting marriage to close relatives). Note that each challenges whether the attribute can truly serve as the cause for its ruling.
The passage also briefly discusses a dispute on marriage vs. worship, but that is a side point.
2
Analyze the Hidden Attribute Objection
The objection: hidden attributes (e.g., consent) cannot be known and thus cannot be causes. The Hanafi response: such attributes can be determined by manifest proxies (mazinnah), such as verbal expressions (ṣīgha) or actions (like mutual giving in sale). Proxy makes the hidden attribute acceptable.
This principle allows hidden mental states to be legally considered through external indicators.
3
Analyze the Ill-Defined Attribute Objection
The objection: attributes like hardship (ḥaraj) are ambiguous and not well-defined (ghayr mundabiṭ). The Hanafi response classifies well-defined attributes into three types: (1) self-defined (e.g., absolute faith), (2) custom-defined (e.g., benefit/harm), and (3) law-defined by a proxy (e.g., travel for hardship, fixed penalty for deterrence).
Thus, ambiguity is resolved by referring to convention, direct definition, or legal indication.
4
Analyze the Counterproductive Attribute Objection
The objection: a suitable attribute might lead to the opposite of the intended benefit. Example: prohibiting marriage to a female relative aims to prevent fornication, but the soul craves the forbidden, so it may increase fornication. The Hanafi response: the prohibition becomes natural and removes desire customarily, just as with mothers, so the objection fails.
This shows that the causative link is based on customary (ʿādī) outcomes, not rare exceptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the objection of naqd, distinguish its application to formal and effective causes, and evaluate the Hanafi versus majority positions on its scope.

Core Idea
Naqd attacks the assumption of causality, not the cause itself — it can invalidate even effective causes.

In qiyas (analogical reasoning), one of the most powerful objections is naqd (invalidation), also called munaqada by Hanafis. Naqd occurs when the claimed cause (illa) is present in a case but the ruling is absent, thereby challenging the cause's purported effect. However, there is a key debate: does naqd apply only to weak, formal causes (tardiyya) or also to strong, effective causes (mu'aththira) established by scripture or consensus? This lesson unpacks that debate, examining the arguments of Imam Fakhr al-Islam (Hanafi) and the majority of usuliyun.

Guided Path
1
Define naqd and its synonyms
Recognize that naqd (invalidation) is also called munaqada and sometimes equated with man' (prevention). Identify its function: showing that a claimed cause fails to produce its ruling in a specific instance.
The Hanafi terminology differs slightly from the common usage.
2
Understand the two types of causes
Distinguish between formal (tardiyya) causes, based solely on correlation, and effective (mu'aththira) causes, established by decisive proofs like the Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma.
Effective causes are presumed strong and non-contradictory.
3
Present the two positions
State Imam Fakhr al-Islam's view that naqd is limited to formal causes, and the majority view (including the author) that naqd applies to both formal and effective causes. Note the supporting arguments for each.
The author chooses the majority position.
4
Analyze the resolution
Understand why naqd can target effective causes: because it attacks the subjective assumption of causality, not the objective reality. Also explore the response techniques (denying existence of the cause in the disputed case) and the dialectical nuances.
The debate illustrates the depth of usul al-fiqh reasoning.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of the fifth objection to the cause in qiyas: naqd (invalidation), also called munaqada by the Hanafis. Naqd occurs when a cause is present but the ruling is absent, challenging the claimed causality. The text presents a disagreement: Imam Fakhr al-Islam and some Hanafis hold that naqd only applies to formal (tardi) causes—those based on correlation—not to effective (mu'aththira) causes that are established by scripture, consensus, or legal proofs, since those cannot be contradictory. However, the majority of usuliyun, including the author (likely the commentator), argue that naqd can apply to effective causes as well, because naqd targets the subjective assumption of causality, not the objective reality. They support this with the argument that if naqd were applied, it would reveal an error in one of the assumptions without creating actual contradiction in divine law, similar to qalb (inversion) which can affect effective causes. The text then discusses various opinions: some hold that naqd forces the claimant to abandon formal causes and adopt effectiveness; others discuss distinctions based on whether the claim is established by correlation or suitability. The passage also addresses how to respond to naqd—by denying the existence of the cause in the disputed case or by arguing that the objector's assumption is wrong. There is also a dialectical point about whether invalidating the proof of the cause is equivalent to invalidating the cause itself; most dialecticians say no, but some consider it a valid objection. The overall lesson is that naqd is a powerful tool in qiyas debate and its applicability is not limited to one type of cause.

Key Terms
النقض (al-naqd): An objection in qiyas where the claimed cause exists but the ruling is absent in a certain case, thereby invalidating the cause's purported effect.
العلة الطردية (al-illa al-tardiyya): A cause based solely on correlation (tard) without independent effectiveness; it lacks legislative support and can be easily invalidated by naqd.
العلة المؤثرة (al-illa al-mu'aththira): A cause whose effect is established by scripture, consensus, or clear legal reasoning; it is considered sound and not subject to contradiction.

Third Answer to Naqd and the Sixth Objection Fasād al-Waḍ‘
Defends qiyas by showing preventing factor and defines fasād al-waḍ‘

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the third answer to a counterexample (naqd) by showing a preventing factor (māni‘) and distinguish fasād al-waḍ‘ from fasād al-i‘tibār.

Core Idea
A preventing factor (māni‘) blocks a ruling despite the cause; fasād al-waḍ‘ occurs when text or consensus puts the attribute opposite.

This passage from Fawātiḥ al-Raḥamūt clarifies how Hanafī scholars defend their analogies against counterexamples. They use four methods, the third being showing a preventing factor (māni‘) that explains why the ruling is absent. The passage also introduces the sixth objection to the cause, fasād al-waḍ‘, which occurs when the common attribute is established in the opposite of the ruling by text or consensus, invalidating the qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the third answer
Locate the statement 'wal-jawāb ‘an al-naqḍ thālithan bi-ibdā’ al-māni‘' and understand that this method introduces a preventing factor to explain why the ruling does not apply despite the cause being present.
The preventing factor can be a benefit (maṣlaḥah) or a harm (mafsadah).
2
Distinguish the two types of preventing factors
Note that the preventing factor is either the attainment of a benefit (taḥṣīl maṣlaḥah) or the repulsion of harm (daf‘ mafsadah). Examples: the ‘arāyā transaction (benefit) and the allowance of carrion for the compelled person (repelling harm).
The examples illustrate how the preventing factor overrides the normal ruling.
3
List the four methods of repelling counterexamples
According to the text, Hanafī scholars who forbid specification of the cause use four methods: showing absence of the description, preventing the meaning that makes the cause effective, preventing the absence of the ruling, and stating the purpose of the analogy.
These methods are detailed with examples from purification (wudu) and wiping the head.
4
Define fasād al-waḍ‘
Read the definition: 'wa sādisuhā... fasād al-waḍ‘ wa huwa thubūt i‘tibār al-waṣf al-jāmi‘ fī naqīḍ al-ḥukm bi-naṣṣ aw ijma‘.' This means the common attribute is considered valid in the opposite of the ruling by text or consensus.
Fasād al-waḍ‘ is more specific than fasād al-i‘tibār, which is a general corruption of consideration.
5
Apply the definition to a sample analogy
Consider a qiyas that wine (khamr) is impure because it intoxicates. If a text says that a certain intoxicant is pure, then the attribute 'intoxicating' is established in the opposite of impurity, making the analogy invalid due to fasād al-waḍ‘.
If no such text or consensus exists, it may be fasād al-i‘tibār instead.

Hanafi Rebuttals to Naqd: Four Methods Collapsing into Two
Exposition of four Hanafi rebuttals to the naqd objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two essential Hanafi responses to the objection of naqd (contradiction) in qiyas and apply them to analogical arguments.

Core Idea
Naqd rebuttals reduce to denying the attribute/meaning or denying the absence of the ruling.

When an analogy is challenged by pointing to a case where the cause exists but the ruling is absent (naqd), Hanafi scholars who disallow takhsis al-illa have developed specific rebuttals. This passage outlines four methods that ultimately collapse into two core strategies. Understanding these methods equips you to defend a valid qiyas against apparent contradictions.

Guided Path
1
Understand the naqd objection
Define naqd: a counterexample where the cause is present but the ruling is missing according to the opponent's own school. This is a serious challenge to the validity of the analogy.
2
List the four methods
Identify the four methods as presented: (a) denying the attribute exists (e.g., blood not flowing), (b) denying the operative meaning despite attribute presence (e.g., devotional vs. rational), (c) denying the absence of the ruling (e.g., ruling delayed), and (d) clarifying the purpose of the analogy (e.g., both exits are events).
3
Classify the reduction
Recognize that methods (a) and (b) both deny the existence of the cause's effective meaning; methods (c) and (d) both deny that the ruling is absent. Thus the four reduce to two essential responses.
The passage states: 'فقد انحصر وجه الدفع عندهم في الوجهين المذكورين أولا'
4
Apply to examples
Use the passage's examples (blood from wound, wiping head, istinja) to see each method in action. Practice identifying which method applies to a given naqd.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi position
Note that these methods allow the Hanafi to maintain qiyas without resorting to takhsis al-illa, preserving the generality of the cause.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling exposition: the Hanafi scholars who disallow takhsis al-illa (specification of the cause) respond to the objection of naqd (contradiction) using four methods. These are systematically presented. First, they deny the existence of the attributed quality in the counterexample (e.g., blood that remains within the wound is not considered 'exiting,' so the attribute of impurity is absent). Second, they deny the operative meaning behind the cause even if the apparent attribute exists (e.g., wiping the head is not repeated due to its devotional nature, while istinja is rational purification and benefits from repetition). Third, they deny the absence of the ruling in the counterexample, arguing that the ruling is merely delayed due to necessity (e.g., a bleeding wound causes hadath but the obligation to renew wudu is postponed until after prayer time). Fourth, they clarify the purpose of the analogy, showing that the ruling in the counterexample is actually consistent (e.g., both exits cause hadath, but if bleeding continues, it is pardoned, so no contradiction). The author concludes that these four reduce to two essential aspects: denying the attribute/meaning and denying the absence of the ruling.

تخصيص العلة (takhsis al-illa): Specifying the cause so that it applies only to some cases, not all that share the attribute.
الدفع (al-daf'): Rebutting the objection of naqd by showing the ruling is not actually absent.

Fasad al-Wad': The Sixth Objection to the 'Illa in Qiyas
Definition and examples of fasad al-wad' as an objection to qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define fasad al-wad', distinguish it from fasad al-i'tibar, and apply it to critique an analogy using nass or ijma evidence.

Core Idea
Fasad al-wad' invalidates an illa when a text or consensus proves the attribute works in the opposite ruling.

This lesson unpacks the sixth objection against the cause in qiyas: fasad al-wad' (invalidity of postulation). Understanding this objection is crucial for critiquing analogical reasoning, as it exposes when a proposed cause is contradicted by explicit textual or consensus evidence. We will explore its definition, distinction from the broader fasad al-i'tibar, and work through examples from both Shafi'i and Hanafi usul.

The author explains that the answer to fasad al-wad' is similar to answering naqd but with an added element: either challenging the authority of the text that shows the opposite, or interpreting it away, or other methods mentioned earlier.

Mu'arada and Mufaraqa: The Seventh Objection to Qiyas
Dispute exposition: definition of mu'arada and rejection of mufaraqa by Hanafis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of mu'arada (opposition) in qiyas, explain the Shafi'i objection of mufaraqa using the liwat-zina analogy, and evaluate the Hanafi rejection of mufaraqa as a valid objection.

Core Idea
Mufaraqa (divergence in maslaha) is not a valid objection because it reduces to either contradiction or coexistence of causes.

In Hanafi usul, objections to qiyas are systematically categorized. The seventh objection is mu'arada (opposition), where an alternative attribute is proposed that is suitable for causality but absent in the disputed branch. Hanafis term a specific subtype as mufaraqa, which Shafi'is accept but Hanafis reject. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to evaluate analogical reasoning across madhhabs.

Guided Path
1
Define mu'arada (opposition)
Mu'arada is the presentation of another attribute that is suitable for causality (either independently or as part of a composite cause) but is not found in the disputed branch. This objection targets the original cause in the original case.
Hanafis call the subtype where the alternative attribute differs in the genus of maslaha as mufaraqa.
2
Understand the Shafi'i example: liwat and zina
Imam al-Shafi'i argued that liwat (anal intercourse) should be punished by hadd (the prescribed penalty) like zina, because both involve prohibited insertion. The objection from mufaraqa: the maslaha (interest) in zina is preserving lineage (preventing mixing of lineage), while in liwat it is repelling a different vice (no lineage concern). Thus the analogy fails.
The Shafi'i accept this as a valid objection (mu'arada) that breaks the qiyas.
3
State the Hanafi position
Hanafis do not accept mufaraqa as a valid independent objection. Their reasoning: the proponent's attribute (the cause) is assumed to be established via a sound path. If the objector's alternative attribute is not proven independently, it becomes a contradiction (mumana'a) rather than a divergence. If it is proven, then two independent causes can coexist—so there is no opposition.
Thus, mufaraqa reduces either to a contradiction (invalid) or to acceptance of multiple causes (harmless).
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to the liwat example
In the liwat case, a Hanafi would say that if the Shafi'i attribute (prohibited insertion) is sound as a cause, then the objector's attribute (preventing lineage mixing) does not oppose it. If the objector's attribute is not independently proven, it is not a valid objection. The difference in maslaha does not invalidate the original cause.
The thamarah al-khilaf: Shafi'is rule that liwat carries hadd; Hanafis do not, using a different cause (e.g., it is not zina).
5
Synthesize: When does a difference in maslaha matter?
The Hanafi position is that a mere difference in the genus of maslaha is not sufficient to break an analogy if the attribute (cause) is sound. The objector must either prove that the original attribute is not the cause (naqd) or that his alternative attribute is the sole cause (contradiction). Otherwise, multiple causes are permissible.
This principle strengthens the Hanafi methodology in qiyas, allowing broader analogical extension.

Fasad al-Wad': Definition and Distinction from Naqd and Qalb
Definition and types of qiyas objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish fasad al-wad' from naqd and qalb, identify its conditions, and apply the definition to evaluate a qiyas objection.

Core Idea
Fasad al-wad' is naqd with a positive assignment of the description to the contradictory ruling—an extra element that invalidates the causation.

In Hanafi usul, objections to qiyas are categorized into several types. Among them, fasad al-wad' (corruption of status) is a higher degree of defect than simple naqd, because it involves actively attributing the description to the opposite of the intended ruling. Understanding this distinction is crucial for properly criticizing a legal analogy. This lesson unpacks the definition, provides examples, and clarifies how to respond to such objections.

Fasad al-wad' occurs when a description is considered in a way that contradicts a text (nass) or consensus (ijma), or when the description is used to prove the contradictory of its natural ruling. This invalidates the causation (illah).

Distinguish naqd from fasad al-wad'

Mu'arada: The Seventh Objection and the Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute
Explaining the seventh objection to qiyas and the madhhab dispute on its validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mu'arada (mufariqa) and man' (prevention) as objections to qiyas, and state the Hanafi position on mu'arada with its evidence.

Core Idea
Mu'arada does not invalidate the original cause unless proven as a man' (prevention).

This passage explains the seventh objection to the cause in qiyas: mu'arada, also called mufariqa by Hanafis. It occurs when an opponent presents an alternative description suitable for causation that is absent in the branch. The Hanafi and Shafi'i schools differ fundamentally on whether such an objection is valid. Understanding this distinction is crucial for analyzing arguments in legal analogy (qiyas).

Guided Path
1
Define mu'arada and mufariqa
Identify the core objection: presenting an alternative description suitable for causation, either independent or as a part, absent in the branch. Note the Hanafi synonym mufariqa.
2
Identify the two positions
State the Shafi'i position (accepted) and the Hanafi position (rejected). Explain the Hanafi rationale: the original description is established by a valid route; the alternative does not automatically contradict it.
3
Examine the Hanafi evidence
Study the argument that mu'arada becomes man' (prevention) if the alternative is proven. Two independent causes can coexist; the alternative must block the original to be valid.
4
Apply to examples from the text
Work through the liwat case, the mortgaged slave case, and the coerced murderer case to see how Hanafis treat mu'arada as man' or reject it.

And the seventh: of the objections to the cause of the origin is mu'arada in the origin, and its meaning is presenting another description suitable for causation, either independent in effect or not independent but a part, but such that it does not exist in the disputed branch. The Hanafis call it mufariqa, and it includes the question of difference in the type of benefit in the origin and the branch, like the Shafi'i's statement: liwat is insertion of a penis into a vagina, a forbidden insertion definitively, so the liwat practitioner is punished like the adulterer, because he commits a forbidden insertion. It is objected that the benefit in the origin in legislating the punishment is prevention of mixing of lineage, for it is possible that the child is from adultery, while in the branch (ghayat) it is liwat, driving away another vice, because it is a possibility of mixing, so the type of benefit differs, so transference of the ruling is not necessary. Then regarding its acceptance (the mufariqa), the Shafi'is said: yes, it is accepted. The Hanafis said: no, it is not accepted. For us: it is assumed that the disputant's description is established by a valid route according to the distinguisher, otherwise it would be that objection not this. So if his description is not independent in causality, then its refutation is necessary because part of a cause is not a cause, so the objection is this, not farq, rather it becomes man'un (prevention) of the disputant's description if the prevention is valid and possible. After accepting the cause, nothing remains in hand to object. And then his description does not contradict the opponent's description, because if he did not prove it with evidence, then it is apparent that it does not contradict it, for what is not established does not contradict what is established. If he proves it with evidence, then the coexistence of two independent causes is permissible by agreement, so both are causes, so no contradiction. So if the Hanafis said in answer to the Shafi'i's analogy regarding freeing a mortgaged slave: it is a transaction that affects even the mortgagee, so it is invalid like its sale, and the statement differed. He said: the cause in the origin is that it is susceptible to revocation, which is absent in the branch; that is not accepted, because it does not contradict that the cause is what the opponent claimed and it exists in the branch, so the ruling applies, so the farq does nothing. Rather he says by way of prevention: if you claim that the ruling in the origin is invalidity, we deny it, for the sale of a mortgaged property by the mortgagor is not invalid, but suspended on repayment of debt or permission of the mortgagee. Or if the ruling in the origin is suspension, then you changed your ruling in the branch, for you do not establish suspension in the freeing, so the ruling of origin and branch differ, thus the condition of qiyas is lost. This type of statement is accepted. The proponents of farq said first: since the description of the one giving the cause could be independent or not, independence is a claim, so it is not established, so mu'arada by presenting another additional description is valid. We said: since the one giving the cause proved its independence as before, there is no possibility of its absence. If he did not bring a route that yields independence, then the objection is this, i.e., prevention of causality, not farq. They said second: the discussions of the Companions were collecting by a general description and distinguishing by another specific one, and they did not reject farq, so it is consensus on acceptance. We said: that was before the appearance of independence... one of them... establishing by evidence from his routes. After that it is prevented, and the discussion is in it. Then the claim that their discussions were farq is prevented, rather they were prevention of causality, but it could be with presenting another cause as a basis for prevention, not that they were mu'arada, and the discussion is therein. Then among those who accept farq, the chosen view is that the distinguisher is not required to explain the absence of the description of the one giving the cause from the branch unless he claimed it, because his purpose is to demolish independence. That does not depend on explaining its absence in the branch. If he claimed, then he must fulfill it. It is also said that he must explain the absence, otherwise it could exist in the branch and the ruling would be established. It is also said that he is not required absolutely, whether he claimed or not, because the purpose is only to demolish independence, and anything extra is voluntary. The distinguisher is not required to mention an origin that is known to have an effect, because he is a probability that what he presented is a cause. So it suffices that it exists in the origin of the disputant. If you say: the distinguisher is a mu'arid, so he is a claimant, so he must prove his claim. I say: he objects to the validity of the cause and its probability, and it suffices that it exists in the origin only. Its existence in another origin and effect is extra. The answer to farq is by denying its existence, i.e., the presented description in the origin of the disputant, or its manifestation, or its precision, or its suitability, even if the disputant clings to proof by reconnaissance, because suitability and appropriateness in reality are conditions in the cause absolutely. It is said: prevention of suitability is not valid when he uses reconnaissance, because he did not claim suitability, and prevention only addresses what is claimed. Or that the presented description is not contradictory in the branch and is not a suitable description, it is a mere coincidence, so it is not a cause, like the Shafi'is analogizing the coerced to the willing in the obligation of qisas due to the commonality of murder with wrongful aggression. So the objector presents volition: the cause in the willing is murder with volition. He answers that it is absence of coercion, which is suitable for absence of qisas, so it is not contradictory, so it has no role in causality. If it were made a prevention (man') as is the chosen view with us, the answer they gave for farq would not apply. Or the answer is that the description is nullified in some case by text or consensus, so it is not suitable for part-hood, like 'Do not sell food with food' in answer to the mu'arada of taste claimed as a cause for riba with volume, by saying: the prohibition of riba is found in some foods.

Commentary
This passage discusses the seventh objection to the cause in qiyas: mu'arada (contradiction) in the origin, meaning presenting an alternative description that is suitable for being a cause, either independently or as a part that is absent in the branch. Hanafis call it mufariqa. It includes the objection of different types of benefit, exemplified by the Shafi'i analogy of liwat with zina. The Shafi'is accept such a farq, but Hanafis reject it because the original description is already established by a valid route; presenting another description does not automatically invalidate it. If the alternative is not independent, it may become a man' (prevention) rather than a farq. The text then addresses arguments by proponents of farq: first, the possibility of non-independence; second, the practice of the Companions. Hanafis refute both. It also discusses whether the distinguisher must prove the absence of the original description in the branch—the preferred view is that he is not required. The passage provides examples and answers, including the case of freeing a mortgaged slave and the analogy of the coerced person. The answer to farq can be by denying the existence, manifestation, precision, or suitability of the alternative description, or by showing it is a mere coincidence (tard), or that it is nullified by text or consensus.

Key Terms
المعارضة (al-mu'arada): The seventh objection to the cause in qiyas: presenting an alternative description that is suitable for being a cause, either independently or as a part, and which is absent in the branch.
المفارقة (al-mufariqa): Synonym for mu'arada used by Hanafis; the objection of difference in the type of benefit or description.
الممانعة (al-mman'a): Prevention: an objection that blocks the effectiveness of the original description by presenting a contrary description that is proven to be the actual cause.

The Farq Objection in Qiyas: Hanafi and Shafi'i Dispute
Analyzing the validity of the farq objection in qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a farq is not a valid objection unless it denies causation or proves the illah's absence, and you will be able to apply this rule to distinguish Hanafi from Shafi'i positions in qiyas debates.

Core Idea
A mere distinction between cases does not break a Hanafi analogy — only a denial of causation does.

In this passage from Musallam al-Thubut, the commentator tackles a core debate between Hanafis and Shafi'is: when does a difference between two cases invalidate an analogy? You will learn why Hanafis require the objector to either deny the ruling of the original case or show that the cause is absent, rather than simply pointing out a different attribute. This lesson will equip you to handle farq arguments in actual juristic debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opposing claim
The distinguisher (fariq) argues that the illah in the asl is not independent, so presenting another attribute shows the analogy fails. They claim this is supported by Companion practices.
This is the Shafi'i position.
2
Recognize the Hanafi response
The mustadill can establish the illah's independence via valid methods (e.g., suluk al-illah). Once independence is proven, the farq becomes a denial of causation (mumana'a), not a valid distinction.
Hanafis reinterpret Companion debates as denials, not pure distinctions.
3
Apply the chosen tarjih
The chosen Hanafi view: the distinguisher is not required to prove absence of the claimed cause from the branch unless he claims it. Otherwise, his objection is not substantive.
Three opinions exist; this is the mufta bihi.
4
Trace the thamarah
In practice, Hanafis uphold analogies that Shafi'is might break. For example, rulings on coerced acts (e.g., sale under duress) or riba cases differ between the two schools.
The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is that Hanafi fiqh allows more analogies.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the 'farq' (distinction) objection in qiyas (analogy) within Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The commentator, drawing from the matn of Musallam al-Thubut, explains the positions of the 'fariq' (distinguisher) and the mustadill (proponent of the analogy). The central issue is whether a distinction proposed by the opponent to break an analogy is valid and how it should be answered. The passage begins by explaining that a mere claim that the cause (illah) in the original case (asl) is subject to lifting and does not exist in the branch (far') is not accepted because it does not negate the opponent's claim that the cause exists in the branch. Instead, the proper response is to deny the original ruling or alter it in the branch (mumana'a). This type of response is accepted. The distinguishers (mostly from the Shafi'i school) argue that the attribute claimed as cause might or might not be independent; if it is not independent, then objecting by presenting another attribute is valid. Hanafis respond that once the mustadill has established the independence of the attribute through a valid method, there is no possibility of its absence, and the objection becomes a denial of causation, not a valid distinction. The passage then discusses whether the distinguisher must prove that the claimed cause is absent from the branch. The chosen Hanafi view is that he is not required to prove absence unless he claims it; merely denying independence is sufficient. However, there are differing opinions. The passage further elaborates on the proper way to answer a farq: either by denying the existence of the attribute in the mustadill's source, or by showing that the attribute is not an objection (mu'arada) in the branch because it is not appropriate (munasib) and is merely coincidental (tard). Concrete examples are provided, such as the Shafi'i analogy of coercion in retaliation, and the debate over riba causes. The text also covers cases where the attribute is rejected (mulgha) by a specific text or consensus, and situations where the distinguisher proposes a substitute (khalaf) for the attribute. The final section addresses the multiplicity of original cases for a single analogy and the two opinions on whether the objector can confine his rebuttal to one original case. The passage concludes with the fourth type of question on qiyas: denial of the cause's existence in the branch, with examples from sales and aman (security). Throughout, the text contrasts Hanafi positions with Shafi'i ones, often preferring the Hanafi view. The commentary is meant to equip the advanced student with the tools to handle farq in actual juristic debate.

Key Terms
الفرق (al-farq): A distinction claimed by the opponent to show that the cause (illah) of the original case is not present or applicable in the branch case.
العلة (al-illah): The effective cause that carries the ruling from the original to the branch in qiyas. It must be appropriate (munasib) and independent (mustaqill).
الممانعة (al-mumana'a): A denial or opposition to the opponent's claim, either by denying the ruling in the asl or by altering the ruling in the far'.
الاستقلال (al-istiqlal): The independence of the illah, meaning it alone is sufficient to bring the ruling without needing another attribute.

First Objection to Qiyas: Denying the 'Illa in the Branch
Explains the first sub-question: denial of 'illa existence in the far'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the first type of objection to qiyas (denial of 'illa in the far'), and explain the Hanafi method of responding by showing existence or clarifying the 'illa.

Core Idea
Denying the 'illa in the branch does not end the debate; the proponent can clarify or show a common controlling characteristic.

In qiyas, the objector may claim that the cause (ʿilla) does not exist in the branch case (farʿ), thus blocking the extension of the ruling. This lesson examines the first of two such objections, using examples from Hanafi-Shafiʿi debates on riba and aman. You will learn how a Hanafi jurist answers this denial and keeps the analogy valid.

Guided Path
1
Understand the objection
The objector denies that the 'illa exists in the far', so the ruling from the asl cannot extend.
This is the first of two sub-questions.
2
Identify the objector's basis
The Shafi'i argues that estimation (mujazafa) only applies to weight/capacity, not countable items like apples.
Example: selling one apple for two apples.
3
State the Hanafi response
The proponent shows that the 'illa does exist in the far' either by analogy to the asl or by clarifying the intended meaning.
The answer is بيان وجودها in the far'.
4
Analyze the aman example
The objector denies a slave's eligibility for aman; the proponent clarifies that the 'illa is rationality and faith. The objector may then shift to deny causality.
This shows a nuance: after clarification, the objector may change his objection.
5
Note the inclusion of difference in controlling characteristic
If the objector claims the controlling characteristics differ between asl and far', the answer is to identify the common characteristic (e.g., intentionally causing forbidden killing).
This is included in the first type.

Second Objection to Qiyas: Counter-Argument (Mu'arada) and Inversion (Qalb)
Explains the second sub-question: mu'arada and its subtype qalb

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish mu'arada (counter-argument) from denial, and explain the Hanafi rules for responding to mu'arada and qalb (inversion) using preponderance (tarjih) or rebuttal of the inversion.

Core Idea
Mu'arada turns the tables: the objector becomes the proponent, but the Hanafi answer is to repel through tarjih or disprove the inversion (qalb).

The second type of objection attacks the 'illa in the far' by adducing a competing qiyas that yields the opposite ruling. This lesson explores mu'arada, its acceptance in Hanafi usul, and the special case of qalb (inversion) where the objector flips the cause and effect. Mastering these tools is essential for defending a qiyas in scholarly debate.

Guided Path
1
Understand mu'arada
The objector brings a qiyas from a different origin leading to a contradictory ruling, thus becoming a proponent himself.

3
Know the methods of response
The original proponent can answer by countering with another proof or by preponderance (tarjih) to break equality.

Qalb invalidates the proponent's qiyas unless defended.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of mu'arada bil-qalb (counterargument by inversion) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author first explains that mu'arada can occur in the branch by presenting something that negates the ruling, requiring an origin to compare. This is considered a counterargument of two analogies. The preferred position (al-mukhtar) is to accept such mu'arada because it blocks acceptance of the ruling unless the opponent defends it. The response can be by countering with another mu'arada or by tarjih (preponderance). The author then distinguishes two types of mu'arada: one with naqd (contradiction), which is qalb. Qalb means turning the mustadill's 'illa (cause) into the hukm (ruling) and vice versa, thereby destroying his analogy. Examples are given: the Shafi'i argument about zina penalties for non-Muslims is inverted by the Hanafi to show that the 'illa is reversed. The passage also covers the Hanafi classification of qalb into types: qalb to correct one's own madhhab, qalb to explicitly invalidate the opponent's madhhab, and qalb to invalidate it implicitly through a necessary consequence. The author references Imam Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi and discusses the acceptability of naqd in effective 'illa (mu'aththira). The overall purpose is to explain the mechanism of qalb as a legitimate form of objection in qiyas.

Key Terms
المعارضة بالقلب (al-mu'āraḍah bil-qalb): A type of counterargument where the objector inverts the mustadill's claimed 'illa (cause) and hukm (ruling), making the 'illa the hukm and vice versa, thereby invalidating the analogy.
النقض (al-naqḍ): Contradiction or invalidation of the opponent's argument by showing an inconsistency in their reasoning.
العلة المؤثرة (al-'illa al-mu'aththira): An effective 'illa that produces its hukm in all cases; it cannot be overridden by qalb because the cause is proven effective.

Types of Qalb (Inversion) in Qiyas: Definition, Dispute, and Classification
Defining qalb and listing its types according to Shafi'is

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four types of qalb (inversion) used by Shafi'i usulists and evaluate the Hanafi objection regarding qalb's application to effective causes.

Core Idea
Qalb turns the arguer's own description against him, but its use against effective causes is disputed due to the impossibility of contradiction in religious matters.

In this lesson, we examine a sophisticated objection technique in qiyas known as al-qalb (inversion). The Shafi'i usulists classify it into four types, each with a distinct logical structure. However, the Hanafi school restricts qalb to descriptive causes only, raising questions about contradiction in religious legal reasoning. We will work through the definition, the key dispute, and the practical examples.

Guided Path
1
Define qalb technically
Understand qalb as an objection where the same description used by the arguer is turned around to prove the opposite of his claim. It is a form of mu'arada (opposition) in qiyas.
The passage begins with an example of selling umm walad to illustrate inversion.
2
Identify the central dispute
Imam Fakhr al-Islam holds that qalb can be applied to both descriptive (tardiyya) and effective (mu'aththira) causes. The objection is that this involves contradiction, which is impossible in religious matters. The response is that the contradiction is subordinate and based on assumption.
Note the Hanafi position: effective causes cannot be inverted because contradiction is impossible. The response uses the concept of subordinate assumption.
3
Analyse the four Shafi'i types
The Shafi'is divide qalb into four types: (1) qalb to correct one's own school, (2) qalb to explicitly invalidate the opponent's school, (3) qalb to invalidate by implication, (4) qalb al-musawa (inversion of equality). Each aims at a different logical outcome.
Study the examples: i'tikaf, wiping a few hairs, sale of absentee, and coerced divorce.
4
Note the hypothetical nature of the examples
According to al-Kashf, these examples are only hypothetical (فروض) cited by Shafi'is for illustration, not actual Hanafi arguments, because the descriptions used are descriptive (tardiyya) and not accepted by Hanafis who require effectiveness.
This highlights a deeper methodological difference: Hanafis reject descriptive causes in qiyas.

Al-'Aks (Inverse Qiyas): Definition, Types, and Validity
Explaining 'aks as a type of inversion and the dispute over its acceptance

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Al-'aks attempts to prove by inversion but fails to establish the required universal equality.

Al-'aks (inverse qiyas) is a controversial form of qalb in which one deduces the opposite of a ruling from the opposite of its cause. This lesson unpacks the definition, the classic example of supererogatory fasting, and the debate between the majority who accept it and Hanafi scholars who reject it. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to evaluate qiyas arguments in usul al-fiqh.

Analyze the Hanafi objection

This passage from Sharh Muslim al-Thubut discusses two types of inverse reasoning in qiyas: qalb al-musawaa (inversion of equality) and 'aks (inverse). It begins by defining qalb al-musawaa through the example of a coerced divorce: the Hanafis argue that a coerced person's divorce is valid like a voluntary one, and the Shafi'i response is that both confession and effectuation are valid from him, but his confession is not considered by consensus, thus establishing an equality that supposedly proves the Shafi'i point. The objections of the rejecters and the responses are given in detail, focusing on whether 'aks necessarily leads to a contradiction or merely to equality, and whether the cause can be generalized. This is a typical Hanafi usul debate on the validity of certain qiyas methods.

Pure Opposition (Mu'arada al-Khalisa) in Qiyas: Definition and Subtypes
Defining pure opposition and its three subtypes with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three subtypes of pure opposition (mu'arada khalisa) in qiyas and apply the condition that the origin and cause must differ from the arguer's.

Core Idea
Pure opposition requires a different origin and cause; it either negates the ruling, specifies it, or entails its opposite.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, objections to qiyas are categorized. This passage introduces 'pure opposition' (mu'arada khalisa), which is a valid objection provided the opponent brings a different origin (asl) and cause (illa). Understanding its three subtypes helps you recognize how jurists counter analogies without contradiction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for valid pure opposition
Read the first line: 'it requires another origin and another cause'. This is the essential condition that distinguishes pure opposition from contradiction (munāqaḍa).
If the origin and cause are the same as the arguer's, it is not pure opposition but contradiction.
2
Study the first subtype: cause necessitates the direct opposite
Examine the example of wiping the head. The Shafi'i claim that wiping is a pillar (rukn) and should be done in thirds is countered by an analogy to tayammum, which results in the opposite ruling.
This subtype directly negates the ruling of the original qiyas.
3
Analyze the second subtype: cause necessitates something more specific than the opposite
Consider the minor with a grandfather. The opponent argues that the brother lacks compassion, so he is not entitled to guardianship for marriage. This is more specific than the original absolute guardianship.
The opposite is 'guardianship is required absolutely'; the specific negation is 'guardianship is not required in marriage'.
4
Examine the third subtype: cause necessitates what entails the opposite
Imam Abu Hanifa's view on the child of a woman whose husband's death was announced: the child belongs to the first husband (valid bed). The opponent analogizes to a marriage without witnesses, attributing the child to the second husband, which entails negation of paternity from the first due to consensus.
The opponent's ruling is not directly opposite but logically entails the opposite.
5
Understand the dispute in the third subtype
Recognize that Abu Hanifa differs from the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and the three imams (Shafi'i, Malik, Ahmad). The basis is the general hadith 'al-walad li al-firash'.
This shows how pure opposition leads to ikhtilaf in fatwa.

Al-Qawl bi al-Mujib: The Fifth Objection in Qiyas
Defining the fifth type of objection in qiyas and its first category

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-qawl bi al-mujib, identify its essence as denial of entailment (istilzam), and apply it to examples from Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Admitting the evidence but disputing the ruling — the objection of incomplete entailment.

In the science of qiyas, a key skill is identifying valid objections. One such objection, known as al-qawl bi al-mujib, allows an opponent to accept the proffered evidence while maintaining that the desired ruling does not follow. This lesson unpacks its definition, its essence (denial of entailment), and its first category with concrete examples from Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Define al-qawl bi al-mujib
Read the first sentence of the passage: it is 'accepting the evidence while the dispute remains as it was'. Restate this in your own words.
Note the dual element: concession of evidence + persistence of disagreement.
2
Identify the essence (hassil)
The text says 'its essence is the denial of entailment (istilzam)'. Understand that the objector does not reject the evidence but claims it does not necessarily lead to the claimed ruling.
istilzam = the relationship of necessary consequence between evidence and ruling.
3
Classify the objection's scope
Note that this objection is not exclusive to qiyas; it applies to any inference. Also, it does not require the cause to be a mul'im (tardiy concept) – a correction of some earlier Hanafis.
This expands the applicability of the objection.
4
Analyze the first category
The first category occurs when the arguer is confused about the ruling (ishtibah al-hukm). The opponent concedes the non-disputed point but insists the real dispute remains.
Example: the heavy object case – conceding it does not prevent retaliation, but disputing whether it necessitates it.
5
Apply to the given examples
Work through the two examples: (1) killing with a heavy object, (2) the head as a pillar in wiping. In each, state what is conceded and what remains disputed.
In example 2, the Shafi'is claim wiping in thirds is required; the Hanafi response concedes that but says their own ruling is based on complete wiping (istiy'ab), so the dispute stands.

Order of Objections in Qiyas Debate
Hanafi analysis of permissibility of multiple objections out of order.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school permits objections in qiyas in any order and differentiate between hypothetical acceptance (taslim fardi) and actual belief.

Core Idea
Acceptance of premises in debate is hypothetical; therefore objections need not follow a fixed order.

When debating a qiyas argument, the order in which objections are raised may seem to follow a natural logic: first challenge the original ruling, then the cause, then the branch. However, the Hanafi school holds that flexibility is possible because the opponent's acceptance of premises is merely hypothetical, not an expression of actual belief. This lesson unpacks that principle and its implications for usul debate.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The passage discusses whether objections in a qiyas debate must follow a specific order (original → cause → branch) or can be raised in any order.
The majority (akthar al-nuzzar) require natural order; the Hanafi chosen view (mukhtar) allows any order.
2
State the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi view relies on the concept that the opponent's acceptance of a premise is taslim fardi (hypothetical), not taslim i'tiqadi (belief-based). Therefore, accepting a premise hypothetically does not prevent later objection to it.
If acceptance were genuine, one could not later object; but since it is hypothetical, any order is permissible.
3
Apply to a debate scenario
Imagine a Hanafi debater wants to object to the branch first. Under the Hanafi view, he can say: 'Even if we hypothetically accept the original and cause, the branch does not share the effective cause.' This is valid.
The majority would require objecting to original and cause first, but the Hanafi debater is free to reorder.
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: In a live debate, a Hanafi can skip to the branch objection if it is more powerful, while a follower of the majority must follow the logical order.
This flexibility can be strategically advantageous.

Commentary
This passage discusses the order of objections in a qiyas debate within the Hanafi usul tradition. The author notes that while many scholars require objections to follow the natural order (first to the original ruling, then to the cause, then to the branch), the chosen (mukhtar) view is that any order is permissible. The reason is that the opponent's acceptance of premises is merely hypothetical (fardi), not a genuine belief that prevents later objection. Thus, one can object to the original first, then if the opponent hypothetically accepts, object to the cause, and then to the branch. This flexibility avoids the problem of implicitly accepting earlier premises by addressing the branch first. The passage emphasizes that all premises are accepted hypothetically, so no harm in any order.

Key Terms
التسليم الفرضي (al-taslim al-fardi): Hypothetical acceptance of a premise in debate, not an actual belief.
التسليم الاعتقادي (al-taslim al-i'tiqadi): Acceptance based on genuine belief, which would bind a person.
المختار (al-mukhtar): The chosen or preferred position within the school.

Objections to Qiyas as Revelation and Hanafi Responses
Four objections against qiyas as revelation and Hanafi rebuttals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four objections against qiyas as revelation, state the Hanafi rebuttals, and explain the concept of wahy batin.

Core Idea
The Prophet's qiyas is inner revelation (wahy batin), confirmed by God, distinguishing it from mere opinion.

This passage from 'Fawatih al-Rahamut' addresses a key usul dispute: is the Prophet's use of qiyas (analogical reasoning) a form of revelation? The objectors raise four arguments based on Quranic verses, logical consequences, historical delays, and the Prophet's capacity for certainty. The Hanafi school responds by specifying the verses, affirming taqrir (prophetic confirmation), explaining delays, and limiting the obligation of certainty. The outcome is a robust defense of the Prophet's ijtihad as protected inner revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four objections
List the four objections raised by the opponents: (1) Quran 53:3-4 implies all speech is direct revelation, (2) if qiyas were binding, contradiction would be permissible, (3) the Prophet delayed answers indicating he waited for revelation, (4) since he could achieve certainty through wahy, he must avoid probability (zann).
These objections target the very foundation of qiyas as a prophetic source.
2
Analyze the Hanafi response to each objection
For each objection, understand the Hanafi rebuttal: (1) the verse is specific to the Quran; qiyas is wahy batin. (2) Contradiction is prevented by taqrir (prophetic confirmation). (3) Delays are due to awaiting a clear ruling or exhausting ijtihad. (4) Revelation is not in his control; he only refrains when hopeful of direct revelation.
The Hanafis consistently reframe qiyas as a protected form of divine guidance.
3
Understand the concept of wahy batin
Wahy batin (inner revelation) is the inspiration and guided ijtihad of the Prophet, confirmed by God through taqrir. This distinguishes it from personal opinion and gives it binding authority.
This concept is central to the Hanafi theory of prophetic ijtihad.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: whether the Prophet's rulings based on qiyas have the same legal force as explicit revelation. Hanafis affirm they are hujjah (binding proof), while opponents may deny their authority.
This affects the foundation of many legal rulings derived from prophetic practice.

Commentary
This passage opens the 'Conclusion on Ijtihad and Taqlid' and presents a debate about whether qiyas (analogical reasoning) qualifies as wahy (revelation). The author first notes that permissibility of reasoning by ra'y (opinion) establishes it as a divine proof. He then gives a fourth proof for the Prophet's ijtihad being more rewarding due to exertion. The main dispute is with the 'objectors' (al-munkirun) who raise four points. First objection: The Quranic verse 'He does not speak from desire' implies all his speech is direct revelation, not qiyas. The Hanafi response specifies that the verse refers only to the Quran itself, and moreover qiyas is 'inner revelation' (wahy batin). Second objection: If qiyas were binding, then contradicting it would be allowed (like any ra'y), but that is invalid. The rebuttal explains that contradiction is prevented by prophetic confirmation (taqrir). Third objection: The Prophet sometimes delayed answers, showing he waited for revelation. The response lists possible reasons like awaiting a clear ruling or exhausting ijtihad effort. Fourth objection: Since he could achieve certainty via revelation, he must avoid probability (zann) of ijtihad. The Hanafi reply is that revelation is not in his control; he only refrains from ijtihad while still hopeful (rajiyan) of revelation, a distinctly Hanafi condition. The entire dispute underscores that the Prophet's ijtihad is a legitimate and protected form of inner wahy, not free opinion.

Key Terms
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning, a method of deriving legal rulings from established cases based on shared effective cause (illah).
الوحي الباطن (al-wahy al-batin): Inner revelation, i.e., the inspiration and guided ijtihad of the Prophet, confirmed by God through taqrir.
التقرير (al-taqrir): The Prophet's tacit approval of a statement or action, indicating divine confirmation.

Non-Mujtahid's Fatwa by Takhrij: Qualification and Proof
Ruling on takhrij-based fatwa by a qualified non-mujtahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the qualifications required for a non-mujtahid to give fatwa by takhrij and explain the Hanafi proof of its permissibility via ijma.

Core Idea
A mujtahid fi al-madhhab may give fatwa by takhrij; the Hanafi proof is continuous practice without disapproval, constituting ijma.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut clarifies a crucial distinction in Hanafi usul: not every scholar can issue fatwas by derivation (takhrij) within a madhhab. Only one who understands the madhhab's foundations and can defend it—a 'mujtahid fi al-madhhab'—is permitted. The Hanafis prove this by the unanimous practice of scholars across ages, and they rebut objections using the concept of divisible ijtihad (tajazzi).

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its subject
Locate the hukm: the permissibility of a non-mujtahid giving fatwa by takhrij, but only for those qualified as 'mujtahid fi al-madhhab'. Note the conditions: awareness of foundations, ability to examine and defend.
The subject is not just any non-mujtahid, but one with madhhab-level ijtihad.
2
Examine the Hanafi proof: ijma
The author presents the proof: the practice of 'penetrating scholars' (العلماء المتبحرين) in all ages giving fatwa by takhrij without disapproval. This continuous practice without denial is the basis of ijma.
Ijma here is established by practice (sukuti), not explicit agreement.
3
Analyze the objection and response
An objection: if no absolute mujtahids exist, ijma cannot occur because its people (mujtahids) are absent. The response: ijtihad is divisible (tajazzi), so scholars of each age are considered mujtahids relative to the madhhab.
This shows the Hanafi doctrine of partial ijtihad.
4
Consider the historical support
The author adds that this practice occurred even during the time of early mujtahids like al-Shafi'i and Ahmad, where the companions of Abu Hanifa gave fatwa by his madhhab without disapproval, strengthening the ijma.
This addresses the objection that ijma is only valid after the era of mujtahids.
5
Distinguish takhrij from transmitting texts
The passage compares takhrij to transmitting a text (mansus). Transmitting a text is like narrating a hadith and has its own conditions; takhrij requires deeper knowledge of principles and ability to face objections.
This clarifies the higher standard for takhrij-based fatwa.

مسألة: إفتاء غير المجتهد فيما يفتي به بمذهب مجتهد لا بأن يجده منصوصاً منه، بل إنما يفتي تخريجاً على أصوله إن كان مطلعاً على مبانيه أي مباني مذهب المجتهد أهلاً للنظر فيه والمناظرة للذب عما يرد عليه وهو المسمى بالمجتهد في المذهب في الاصطلاح جاز خبر لقوله إفتاء وعن أئمتنا: لا يحل لأحد أن يفتي بقولنا ما لم يعلم من أين قلنا أي من أي أصول قلنا وأفتينا، فإن كان من الخبر فمن أي سند روي، وإن كان من القياس فبأي علة قيس، ويعلم موانع تلك العلة. ثم في النص يعلم ما يتعلق به كذا نقل في «التيسير» عن الشيخ أبي بكر الجصاص الرازي وقيل: يشترط فيه عدم مجتهد وقال أبو الحسين: لا يجوز أصلاً وأما النقل لقولهم: المنصوص كالأحاديث أي كنقلها فاتفاق في الجواز، ويقبل بشرائط الرواية إن لم يكن متواتراً، وإلا فيقبل مطلقاً لنا وقوعه أي الإفتاء المذكور تخريجاً من العلماء المتبحرين في جميع الأعصار بلا نكير وينكر الافتاء تخريجاً من غيرهم أي غير المتبحرين فكان إجماعاً على جوازه لهم دون غيرهم قيل: إذ فرض عدم المجتهدين فلا إجماع لأن أهله المجتهدون وأجيب باعتبار التجزي يعني الاجتهاد متجز والعلماء الأعلام في كل عصر أفتوا زعماً منهم بجواز هذا الافتاء اجتهاداً فتأمل فيه أقول: وأيضاً وقع هذا الافتاء في زمان المجتهدين فإن أصحاب الإمام أبي حنيفة كانوا يفتون بمذهبه في زمان الإمام الشافعي و الإمام أحمد وغيرهما، كابن معين وابن عيينة وعطاء وغيرهم بلا نكير من أحد فكان هذا إجماعاً وحينئذ أي حين جاز عند وجودهم جاز عند عدمهم بذلك الإجماع أو بطريق أولى فإذا جاز عند وجود من يمكن الاستفتاء منه فعند عدمهم يجوز بالطريق الأولى على أن اتفاق العلماء المحققين على ممر الأعصار وإن كانوا غير مجتهدين حجة كالاجماع فإنه يأبى العقل من اجتماعهم من غير أن يكون واضحاً لديهم وإنه كان بالسماع عن مجتهديهم. قال: المانع لو جاز لعالم لجاز لعامي إذا عرف هو أيضاً حكم حادثة بدليلها كما أن العالم يعلم كذلك فهما سواء، ولا يجوز للعامي بالاتفاق، فلا يجوز للعالم أيضاً قلنا: الحكم موقوف على عدم المعارض وهو أي العامي غير عالم به فلا علم لديه بدليله بخلاف العالم.
Issue: The fatwa of a non-mujtahid regarding what he gives fatwa on according to the madhhab of a mujtahid—not that he finds it as a text from him (the mujtahid), but rather he gives fatwa by derivation (takhrij) based on his principles, provided he is aware of the foundations (i.e., the foundations of the mujtahid's madhhab), qualified to examine it and to debate in defense against objections. He is called in terminology a 'mujtahid fi al-madhhab'. It is permissible (the predicate of 'giving fatwa'). And from our imams: 'It is not permissible for anyone to give fatwa by our statement unless he knows from where we said it'—i.e., from which principles we said and gave fatwa. If it is from a report, then from which chain of transmission; if it is from analogy, then by which cause it was analogized; and he knows the preventers of that cause. Then in the text, he knows what relates to it. Thus it is transmitted in 'al-Taysir' from Shaykh Abu Bakr al-Jassas al-Razi. And it was said: it requires the absence of a mujtahid. And Abu al-Husayn said: it is absolutely not permissible. As for transmitting their statements: the textual (al-mansus) like hadiths—i.e., like transmitting them—there is agreement on its permissibility, and it is accepted with conditions of narration if it is not mutawatir, otherwise it is accepted absolutely. To us, its occurrence—i.e., the aforementioned fatwa by derivation—from penetrating scholars in all ages without any disapproval, and the fatwa by derivation from others (i.e., non-penetrating ones) is disapproved, so it was a consensus on its permissibility for them but not for others. It was said: if we assume the absence of mujtahids, then there is no consensus because its people are mujtahids. The answer is given by considering partiality (tajazzi), meaning ijtihad is divisible, and the great scholars of every age gave fatwa claiming the permissibility of this fatwa by ijtihad, so reflect. I say: also this fatwa occurred in the time of mujtahids, for the companions of Imam Abu Hanifa used to give fatwa according to his madhhab in the time of Imam al-Shafi'i and Imam Ahmad and others, such as Ibn Ma'in, Ibn 'Uyaynah, 'Ata', and others, without any disapproval from anyone, so this was a consensus. Then—i.e., when it was permissible in their presence—it is permissible in their absence by that consensus or by a stronger path. So if it was permissible in the presence of those from whom fatwa could be sought, then in their absence it is permissible by the stronger path. Moreover, the agreement of verified scholars throughout the ages (even if they are not mujtahids) is a proof (hujja) like consensus, for reason cannot accept their agreement without it being evident to them, and it was by hearing from their mujtahids. The objector said: if it were permissible for a scholar, it would be permissible for a layman if he also knows a ruling of an incident with its evidence, just as the scholar knows it, so they are equal. And it is not permissible for a layman by agreement, so it is not permissible for the scholar either. We say: the ruling is dependent on the absence of a counter (mu'arid), and the layman is not aware of it, so he has no knowledge of its evidence, unlike the scholar.

Commentary
This is a ruling (hukm) on the permissibility of a non-mujtahid giving fatwa by way of takhrij (derivation) within a madhhab, as opposed to simply transmitting a text (nass) from the mujtahid. The text defines the qualified person as a 'mujtahid fi al-madhhab'—one who understands the foundations (mabani) of the madhhab, can examine issues, and defend the madhhab against objections. This is permissible. It then quotes the Hanafi imams: one must know the source of the ruling—whether from a hadith (and its chain) or from qiyas (and its 'illa and preventers). There is disagreement: some require the absence of a mujtahid (i.e., only when no absolute mujtahid is available), and Abu al-Husayn forbids it outright. However, the Hanafi position relies on the continuous practice of scholars across ages (ijma). The argument is that this type of fatwa by takhrij occurred without disapproval, especially in the time of the earlier mujtahids (e.g., Abu Hanifa's companions in the presence of al-Shafi'i and Ahmad), so it is established by ijma. An objection that if there are no absolute mujtahids then the consensus does not hold is answered by the concept of partial ijtihad (tajazzi al-ijtihad). The text also distinguishes between transmission of a text (like hadiths) which is accepted with conditions, and takhrij which requires deeper knowledge. Finally, an objection that if a non-mujtahid scholar can do takhrij, then a layman who knows a ruling with its evidence could also be allowed, is rebutted: the layman lacks knowledge of counter-objections (adam al-mu'arid), which the scholar possesses.

Key Terms
تخريج (takhrij): Deriving rulings within a madhhab based on the principles and foundations of the madhhab's founder.
المجتهد في المذهب (al-mujtahid fi al-madhhab): A scholar competent to derive rulings within a specific madhhab, understanding its principles and able to defend it.
المنصوص (al-mansus): A textual ruling explicitly stated by the madhhab's founder or from primary sources.
إجماع (ijma): Consensus of qualified scholars on a ruling; here, the continuous practice without disapproval is considered evidence of consensus.

Key Takeaway
A mujtahid fi al-madhhab may give fatwa by takhrij, proven by the ijma of scholarly practice, provided he knows the madhhab's foundations and can defend against objections.
