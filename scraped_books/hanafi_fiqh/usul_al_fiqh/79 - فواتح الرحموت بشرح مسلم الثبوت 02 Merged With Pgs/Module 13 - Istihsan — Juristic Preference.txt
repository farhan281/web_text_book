Istinbat al-Manat vs. Ta'wil: The Hanafi Case for Zakat Value Substitution
Defending Hanafi istinbat al-manat against accusation of ta'wil in zakat value substitution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish istinbat al-manat from ta'wil by applying the Hanafi usul principle that the financial purpose of zakat justifies substituting value for the specified animal.

Core Idea
When the 'illah is financial need, the text's mention of 'sheep' defines the measure, not the wajib itself.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi usul distinction: extracting the ratio legis (istinbat al-manat) versus allegorical interpretation (ta'wil). The debate centers on whether giving cash value in zakat instead of the specified sheep is permitted. Hanafis argue it is istinbat based on the purpose of zakat (repelling needs), while opponents claim it is ta'wil that changes the text. We will analyze the Hanafi argument, the proofs cited, and the rebuttal to objections.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested issue
Determine whether the word 'sheep' in the zakat hadith refers to the animal itself or to its value. The objectors claim Hanafis twist the text; Hanafis claim they extract the underlying ratio.
This is the core of the dispute.
2
Outline the Hanafi position
State the Hanafi view: the obligation is financial; the sheep is a standard (mi'yar) for the wajib amount, not the wajib itself. Therefore, giving value fulfills the obligation.
3
Trace the Hanafi dalil
Identify the proofs: the hadith of Mu'adh (RA) where the Prophet allowed substituting clothes for grain, and the practice of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (RA) accepting substitute animals with additional payments.
These show that the form is not essential.
4
Understand the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafi response is that this is istinbat al-manat, not ta'wil. The sheep remains in its meaning; it is the standard. The objectors confuse extracting the ratio with allegorical interpretation.
The author insists this is not changing the text.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis allow giving cash value in zakat; Shafi'is generally do not. Understand how this usul dispute affects actual fiqh.

Commentary
This passage defends the Hanafi position that giving the value (qimah) instead of the specified animal in zakat is permissible based on istinbat al-manat (extraction of the ratio legis), not ta'wil (allegorical interpretation). The author begins by presenting the objection: interpreting ‘sheep’ as ‘value’ is far-fetched because it would change the wajib from the animal to its value. The objectors claim that Hanafi reasoning—that zakat aims to meet needs and value does so more effectively—invalidates the text. The author refutes this by asserting that the obligation of the sheep inherently implies the permissibility of its substitute because the underlying consideration (manat) is financial (maliya), not the specific form. This is proven by the hadith of Mu'adh (RA) where the Prophet (SAW) allowed giving clothes instead of grain, and by the practice of Abu Bakr al-Siddiq (RA) in accepting substitute animals with additional payments. The author emphasizes that the sheep is a standard (mi'yar) for determining the wajib amount, not the wajib itself. Thus, giving value suffices because it fulfills the financial purpose. The passage then addresses an objection from the commentary of al-Sharh and marginalia of Mirzajan, concluding that since the wajib is the financial worth (which exists in the sheep), giving that worth is valid.

Key Terms
استنباط المناط (istinbāṭ al-manāṭ): Deriving the ratio legis from a text to extend the ruling to similar cases, as opposed to ta'wil which changes the meaning of the text.
تأويل (taʼwīl): Interpreting a word in a non-literal sense based on evidence; here, objectors claim Hanafis interpret 'sheep' as 'value'.
قيمة (qīmah): Monetary value or substitute payment in zakat.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, the obligation in zakat is value, not form; istihsan and 'urf allow substitution.

This lesson explores the elaborate usul reasoning behind the Hanafi ruling that a person may give the monetary value of a sheep instead of the sheep itself when paying zakat. The passage wrestles with objections, alternative arguments, and the interplay between textual indications, customary practice, and qiyas. Learning Objective

Core Idea
Istihsan overrides qiyas when strict analogy leads to hardship or injustice.

In this passage, the author explores two scenarios where indirect causation raises liability questions. Through careful reasoning, the text shows how Hanafi usul may override strict analogy (qiyas) with juristic preference (istihsan) to achieve equitable outcomes, especially when societal conditions change.

For the Muhrim pointer, the baseline rule from a non-Muhrim pointer (no liability) leads to a qiyas that would absolve the Muhrim. Istihsan is a valid source of law in the Hanafi school when strict qiyas leads to outcomes contrary to the spirit of Shari'ah or causes widespread harm. The informant case is a classic example: the later scholars acted on istihsan to protect the community.
Other schools (e.g., Shafi'i) reject istihsan, leading to different rulings in such cases.

Applying istihsan to the mutual oath case

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Before possession, the seller's oath is based on istihsan (qiyas); after possession, it is based on text alone.

This lesson takes the concept of istihsan and demonstrates its application in a concrete fiqh scenario: the mutual oath (tahaluf) between seller and buyer when they disagree on the price. The passage shows how istihsan governs before possession, while a specific hadith governs after possession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario of tahaluf
Seller and buyer differ about the price before the buyer takes possession. Each makes a claim against the other.
The seller claims a higher price; the buyer claims delivery.
2
Determine the qiyas and istihsan
Qiyas would require both to swear because both are claimants and defendants. Istihsan here is that the mutual oath is imposed, which is actually a stronger qiyas recognizing mutual claims.
The text calls it 'استحسان قياسي' (qiyasi istihsan).
3
Extend to hire and heirs
The same principle applies to hire contracts before benefits are received, and to heirs of the seller and buyer, because the same mutual claim exists.
This extension shows the 'illa (effective cause) is mutual claim, so the ruling is transitive.
4
Contrast with after possession
After possession, only the seller swears. This is not by qiyas but by the hadith of mutual oath, because the buyer is no longer a claimant (he possesses the item).
Qiyas would not require the seller to swear, but the text overrides.
5
Address the objection
An objection is raised: if the buyer's testimony is accepted, doesn't that make him a claimant? The answer is that the defendant's testimony can also be accepted in certain contexts, as in the case of the possessor's evidence on birth.
This preserves the distinction between before and after possession.

Core Idea
Istihsan is a proof that overrides qiyas when a stronger evidence (nas, ijma', or hidden qiyas) exists.

Core Idea
Istihsan is hidden qiyas with strong effect (al-qiyas al-khafi al-qawi al-athar), not arbitrary preference.

This lesson opens with a fiqh example illustrating that substituting an obligatory act is not allowed, then defines istihsan as hidden qiyas with strong effect. You will learn the Hanafi justification for istihsan as a valid source and the Shafi'i objection.

Extract the definition of istihsan from Imam Muhammad

Strength (quwwa) here refers to the strength of the illa's effect (athar).

Shafi'is reject istihsan as a source, arguing it is arbitrary. The Hanafi response is that istihsan is not arbitrary but a valid form of reasoning grounded in the stronger hidden meaning, supported by practice of the Companions.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf; both schools recognize qiyas but differ on istihsan.

Scenarios of Conflict Between Qiyas and Istihsan
Analysis of conflict scenarios between qiyas and istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the sixteen possible conflict scenarios between qiyas and istihsan and explain why two sound (in hidden) qiyas/istihsan cannot truly conflict.

Core Idea
Conflict between qiyas and istihsan is possible only when one or both are unsound in hidden; two sound or strong ones cannot truly conflict.

This lesson unpacks the theoretical framework of conflict between qiyas and istihsan. You will learn the sixteen scenarios derived from combinations of soundness and strength, and the key insight that genuine conflict indicates ignorance, not a real contradiction in divine law.

Guided Path
1
Understand the classification of qiyas and istihsan by strength and soundness
Both qiyas and istihsan can be classified as strong (qawi) or weak (da'if) in effect, and sound (sahih) or unsound (fasid) in their apparent (zahir) and hidden (batin) aspects. This yields four states each.
Soundness in batin means the illa is valid; soundness in zahir means the analogy is apparent.
2
Derive the sixteen conflict scenarios
Multiplying the four states of qiyas with the four states of istihsan gives sixteen combinations. But the author notes that cases where either is fasid in both aspects are not valid as proofs, so effectively fewer.
The author suggests dropping the fasid zahir and batin scenarios.
3
Analyze the impossibility of conflict between two sound (batin) or two strong ones
Sadr al-Shari'ah argues that if both qiyas and istihsan are sound in batin (or strong in effect), they cannot conflict because the Shari'ah would not contain contradictory rulings. Any apparent conflict is due to ignorance.
This is a key principle: the Law is consistent.
4
Understand the role of preference (tarjih) factors
When conflict is possible (e.g., one is sound and the other is not), preference is determined by the preponderance factors (marjihaat) other than mere strength. The passage notes that 'immediate understanding' (tabadur) is not a valid basis for preference.
Tarjih is done through other criteria, not simply which is more obvious.
5
Apply the framework to a hypothetical case
Suppose qiyas gives one ruling and istihsan another. Determine the soundness in batin and strength. If both are sound in batin, there is no true conflict. If one is sound and the other unsound, prefer the sound. This helps a mujtahid resolve dilemmas.
Practice makes this framework intuitive.

The Status of al-Istiqra' (Induction) in Usul
Definition and critique of istiqra'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why al-istiqra' is not an independent source and how it reduces to qiyas.

Core Idea
Istiqra' is not evidence; it either falls under general proofs or reduces to qiyas.

This lesson addresses al-istiqra' (induction), which some Shafi'i scholars (like al-Baydawi) considered a source. The author argues that it is not a valid proof for Allah's ruling, because the Shariah does not provide detailed rulings for every particular. If a universal is given, it is the general (amm) that is the proof, not induction. If a common description is found, it becomes qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Define istiqra'
Induction is reasoning from particular instances to a general rule.
Example: observing many instances of a ruling leads to a general conclusion.
2
Identify the Shafi'i support
Al-Baydawi (a Shafi'i usuli) considered istiqra' a proof.
This view is not accepted by the majority.
3
State the author's refutation
The author argues that the Shariah does not give every particular ruling in detail, so induction cannot be used to derive a universal. If a general (amm) text exists, that is the proof, not induction. If a common description is found among particulars, the method becomes qiyas.
Therefore, istiqra' is reducible to other accepted sources.
4
Conclude the Hanafi position
Istiqra' is not an independent source. It either relies on a general text or becomes qiyas.
The Hanafi school does not accept it as a separate proof.

The Principle of al-Akhdh bi Aqall ma Qil
Critique of adopting minimal opinion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the author considers the principle of 'adopting the minimal opinion' (al-akhdh bi aqall ma qil) corrupt and not a valid inference.

Core Idea
Adopting the minimal opinion is not inference; it is merely choosing certainty.

This lesson examines the principle of adopting the minimal reported opinion (al-akhdh bi aqall ma qil), which Imam al-Shafi'i used in determining the diyah of a Jew. The author criticizes this, arguing that it is not a valid istidlal but only a preference for action based on certainty. The real inference, if any, is based on the original principle (al-asl) when things conflict.

Guided Path
1
Define the principle
Al-akhdh bi aqall ma qil means adopting the smallest value or least amount among conflicting opinions or reports.
Example: diyah of a Jew reported as 1/3, 1/2, or full: al-Shafi'i took 1/3.
2
Identify the Shafi'i application
Al-Shafi'i used it in a specific case (diyah of a Jew) to choose the minimal amount.
The author says this is fāsid (corrupt).
3
Analyze the author's criticism
The author argues: from where did al-Shafi'i negate the excess? It is not valid inference. Some claimed it is consensus, but that is false. The correct view is that this action is merely a preference (tarjīḥ) for the lesser amount because it is certain (mutayaqqan), not a proof (istidlāl).
It is similar to preferring what agrees with the original principle (al-asl) when evidences conflict.
4
Compare with al-asl
When things conflict, we act on what agrees with the original principle (e.g., the original state of permissibility). This is a preponderator, not an independent source.
Example: the leftover of a donkey (sawr al-himar) – the original ruling is tahara until proven otherwise.

The Invalidity of 'Absence of Evidence' as Proof
Critique of absence of evidence as proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the absence of evidence after investigation does not prove the non-existence of a ruling.

Core Idea
Lack of evidence does not prove non-existence; only Shariah rules can indicate non-existence.

This lesson discusses the principle that 'lack of evidence after investigation' (adam al-dalil ba'd al-fahs) is not a valid proof. Some Shafi'is claimed it indicates the non-existence of a ruling, but the majority (including Hanafis) reject it. The exception is when Shariah rules explicitly state that what has no specific evidence is permissible (ibaha).

Guided Path
1
State the principle
The principle: if after thorough search no evidence is found for a ruling, some argue this proves the ruling does not exist.
Example: no hadith found on a matter → some say it is not legislated.
2
Identify the supporting view
Some Shafi'is accepted it as evidence.
Not accepted by the majority.
3
State the majority position
The correct view (al-haqq) according to the majority is that it is not evidence, because the absence of evidence does not necessitate the absence of the evidenced.
Logical principle: lack of proof is not proof of lack.
4
Explain the exception
Exception: if Shariah rules indicate that what has no specific evidence remains permissible (ibaha asl), then the permissibility is derived from that rule, not from the absence of evidence.
This is the principle of al-ibaha al-asliyya.

(The correct view is that it is a preference for action because the lesser is certain, not a real inference. Like adopting the original principle (al-asl) when things conflict, for one acts on what agrees with the original, so it is a preponderator, as we said regarding the leftover of a donkey. (Among them: al-istiqra' (induction): al-Baydawi from the Shafi'is chose it. The correct view is that it does not indicate Allah's ruling because the Shariah has not come with every ruling of every particular in detail, so that one could infer the universal ruling from the particulars. However, if it is said that it came in a general way, then there is no induction, but the general is the evidence, unless it indicates a comprehensive description for the particulars; then the ruling is by this description, and induction is only for its verification in the particulars, so it reduces to qiyas. Commentary
This passage is an appendix (تكملة) listing various subsidiary sources of Islamic law where the four imams agreed on the four main sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) but differed on others. The author presents each source and the positions of the imams. First: شرائع من قبلنا (laws of previous prophets) – some followers denied it. Second: الاستحسان (juristic preference) – it is not a disputed evidence. Third: المصالح المرسلة (unrestricted public interest) – attributed to Malik. Fourth: قول الصحابي (saying of a Companion) – accepted by some Hanafis, Malikis, Hanbalis, and Shafi'i in his old view. Fifth: عدم الدليل بعد الفحص (absence of evidence after investigation) – some Shafi'is accepted it, but the majority hold it is not evidence, except that the Shariah rules indicate permissibility. Sixth: الأخذ بأقل ما قيل (adopting the minimal opinion) – Shafi'i used this for diyah of a Jew, but the author criticizes it as corrupt, stating it is mere preference for certainty, not inference. Seventh: الاستقراء (induction) – al-Baydawi chose it, but the correct view is that it is not evidence unless it reduces to qiyas. Eighth: الاستصحاب (presumption of continuity) – Shafi'is and some Hanafis (like al-Maturidi) accept it absolutely; other Hanafis (Abu Zayd, Shams al-A'immah, Fakhr al-Islam) accept it only for defense; many negate it entirely, including Ibn al-Humam. The author states the chosen position is the negation. As a fruit (thamara), the case of a lost person (mafqud): according to Shafi'i, he inherits because he is presumed alive.

Key Terms
شرائع من قبلنا (shara'i' man qablana): Laws of previous prophets (e.g., Torah, Gospel) that are not abrogated.

قول الصحابي (qawl al-sahabi): Saying or opinion of a Companion of the Prophet.
