Refuting the claim that Mu'adh's hadith omitted Ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the flaw in the third objection to Ijma from the hadith of Mu'adh and state the Hanafi response.

Core Idea
Ijma was not mentioned because it had not yet occurred; absence does not disprove validity.

In this lesson, we examine a common objection raised against Ijma as a source of law. The opponent points to the well-known hadith where Mu'adh ibn Jabal lists only the Quran, Sunnah, and ijtihad, and argues that the omission of Ijma proves it is not a valid source. The Hanafi response is simple but decisive: Ijma had not yet taken place at the time of that hadith. Understanding this contextual point is crucial for defending the authority of Ijma.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
Identify the opponent's argument: Mu'adh did not mention Ijma, so it cannot be a valid source.
2
Identify the dalil
The hadith of Mu'adh is the proof cited. Recognize that it is a sound hadith, but its interpretation is contested.
3
Analyze the opponent's inference
The opponent assumes that the list in the hadith is exhaustive and that anything omitted is invalid. This is a logical leap.
4
Present the Hanafi response
Ijma only becomes a source after the Prophet's death; at the time of the hadith, no Ijma had occurred. Hence the omission is natural.
This is a chronological argument.
5
Evaluate the strength
The Hanafi response effectively refutes the objection by providing a contextual explanation. The burden of proof shifts back to the opponent.

Deriving the Impossibility of Collective Apostasy from the Hadith of Protection from Error
Proving that the entire ummah cannot apostatize due to the hadith of protection from error

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the impossibility of collective apostasy from the hadith 'لا تجتمع أمتي على ضلالة' using the Hanafi usul framework and evaluate the objection that apostates cease to be the ummah.

Core Idea
The ummah is protected from collective error; even apostasy cannot be agreed upon because at the moment of apostasy the ummah still exists.

This passage from Fawatih al-Rahamut addresses a theoretical but profound question: can the entire ummah of the Prophet (peace be upon him) collectively renounce Islam? While the intellect does not deem it impossible, the sacred law (shar') negates it. The Hanafi school bases this on the well-known hadith that the ummah will never unite on error. The text then navigates a subtle objection: if they apostatize, they are no longer the ummah, so the hadith does not apply. The response uses a logical distinction between the moment of transformation and the subsequent state, illustrating the depth of Hanafi usul reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary textual evidence
Locate the hadith that serves as the foundation for the ruling. In this passage, it is the well-known statement: 'لا تجتمع أمتي على ضلالة' (My ummah will not unite on error). This hadith is cited as the main dalil.
The hadith is mutawatir in meaning in Hanafi usul.
2
Classify the text in Hanafi usul
Determine the linguistic and legal category of the hadith. Here, it is an 'amm (general) text because it negates unity on any kind of error (ضلالة) without specification. In Hanafi usul, 'amm is considered qat'i al-dalalah (certain in meaning) unless a specific exclusion is proven.
The term 'ضلالة' encompasses all forms of misguidance, including apostasy.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle to derive the ruling
Apply the rule that a general negative (لا تجتمع) covers all instances of the general term. Since apostasy (riddah) is the gravest form of error, the hadith implicitly prohibits the ummah from collectively agreeing on it. Thus, collective apostasy is religiously impossible.
This derivation relies on the principle 'al-amm yufidu al-'umum wa al-istighraq'.
4
Address the objection: after apostasy they cease to be the ummah
Consider the counterargument: if they apostatize, they are no longer the ummah, so the hadith does not apply. Respond using the logical analysis in the text: at the exact moment of apostasy, the attribute 'ummah' still holds (like clay becoming stone). The name persists temporarily, so the statement 'the ummah apostatized' remains true.
The text uses the analogy of clay (طين) turning into stone (حجر) to illustrate the point.
5
Note the difference of opinion and additional evidence
Acknowledge that some scholars hold collective apostasy to be religiously possible. The Hanafi position is supported by additional authentic hadith, such as the one in Jam' al-Usul, which confirms that a group of the ummah will always remain on truth until the Day of Judgment.
This reinforces the impossibility of complete apostasy of the entire ummah.

Classification of Wahy: Inner (Batin) and Outer (Zahir)
Definition of inner and outer wahy, and the role of taqrir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi classification of wahy into batin (inner) and zahir (outer) and state the role of taqrir (divine confirmation) in making the Prophet's ijtihad constitute wahy.

Core Idea
The Prophet's ijtihad becomes wahy only after divine confirmation; before taqrir it is not termed wahy.

In this section from Fawatih al-Rahamut, the Hanafi school defines two categories of revelation (wahy). Inner wahy (batin) is the Prophet's ijtihad after it has been divinely confirmed (taqrir), removing any possibility of error. Outer wahy (zahir) includes audible revelation from the angel, angelic indication, and inspiration (ilham) that brings immediate understanding. The crucial concept is taqrir, which transforms the Prophet's reasoning into binding divine communication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of wahy
The passage divides wahy into batin (inner) and zahir (outer). Inner wahy is the Prophet's ijtihad after divine confirmation. Outer wahy includes what is heard from the angel, angelic indication, or ilham that brings necessary knowledge without reflection.
Note that ilham is debated as either batin or zahir.
2
Understand the role of taqrir for batin wahy
The Prophet's ijtihad is not automatically wahy. It becomes wahy only after God confirms it (taqrir), which eliminates the possibility of error. Before taqrir, it is not called wahy.
This is a key Hanafi doctrine distinguishing the Prophet's ijtihad from the ordinary ijtihad of mujtahids.
3
Recognize the subtypes of zahir wahy
Outer wahy includes (a) hearing from the angel (Quran or hadith qudsi), (b) angelic indication (as in the hadith about provision), and (c) ilham (inspiration) that brings immediate understanding without reflection.
Ilham is included as zahir because it conveys meaning without reflection, unlike qiyas.
4
Distinguish ilham from qiyas
Qiyas requires reflection (nazar) to derive ruling, while ilham provides direct understanding. This is why the majority classifies ilham as zahir wahy.
This distinction is important for understanding the epistemology of ilham.

The Authority of Inspiration (Ilham): Prophetic and Non-Prophetic
Evaluation of the authority of ilham for the Prophet and for awliya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that the Prophet's ilham is a definitive proof (hujjah qati'ah) and distinguish between the three scholarly positions on the ilham of awliya (saints).

Core Idea
The Prophet's inspiration is definitive proof like the Quran; for awliya, its authority is debated: binding only on the inspired, on all, or not at all.

This passage continues the discussion of wahy by focusing on ilham (inspiration). It establishes that for the Prophet, ilham is a definitive proof (hujjah qati'ah) — rejecting it is kufr and abandoning it is fisq, just as with the Quran. For non-prophets (awliya), there are three positions: some Sufis consider it a proof in rulings; most scholars limit it as a proof only for the inspired person; and others deny it any authority. The passage also critiques the Ja'fariyya Shia doctrine of imam infallibility, distinguishing it from true ilham.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitive status of the Prophet's ilham
The passage states that the Prophet's ilham is a definitive proof (hujjah qati'ah) upon him and others; denying it is kufr, abandoning it is fisq.
This is like the Quran in binding force.
2
List the three positions on the ilham of awliya
The passage presents three positions: (1) Some Sufis say it is a proof in rulings. (2) Most scholars say it is a proof only for the inspired person. (3) Another position denies it being a proof at all (the text cuts off).
Each position has a different scope of authority.
3
Understand the distinction between prophetic and saintly ilham
Awliya receive ilham only in agreement with the Shariah of their prophet, through mediation; prophets can receive new legislation or abrogation directly from Allah without mediation.
This is why prophetic ilham is universal and definitive.
4
Critique of the Ja'fariyya position
The passage dismisses the Rawafid claim of imams' infallibility as based on misunderstanding; if they mean infallibility like prophets, it is false; if they mean ilham, they do not understand it.
This shows the Hanafi rejection of extreme Shia doctrines.

Critique of the Musawwibun's Hadith Proof: Companions as Stars
Refutation of a proof for taswib via hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the musawwibun's second proof and evaluate the Hanafi response using qualification of 'following error' and hadith weakness.

Core Idea
Following error can be guidance if the Lawgiver commands it — the Hanafi distinction saves taswib from the charge of misguidance.

In this passage, the musawwibun (those who hold every mujtahid is correct) cite a famous hadith: 'My companions are like stars; whichever you follow, you are guided.' They argue that following error is misguidance, so each companion must be correct. The Hanafi response is twofold: first, error commanded by the Lawgiver is guidance in terms of duty, not misguidance; second, the hadith itself is weak. Let us examine the structure of this dispute.

Guided Path
1
State the musawwibun's proof
Identify the hadith and their logical inference: following any companion yields guidance, so each ijtihad is correct.
2
Identify the weak link
Where does the musawwibun assume that error cannot be guidance? The premise 'following error is misguidance' is taken as absolute.
3
Apply the Hanafi rebuttal
Distinguish between error that is commanded by the Lawgiver (obligatory to follow) and error that is not. The commanded error is guidance in terms of fulfilling duty.
Key: the obligation transforms the act of following into guidance, not the ruling itself.
4
Evaluate the second rebuttal
The hadith is weak in transmission (da'if), so it cannot establish a fundamental usul principle like taswib.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: Hanafis follow the stronger companion's view over a weaker one; musawwibun accept all as equally valid.

و) قالوا (ثانياً) قال صلى الله عليه وآله وأصحابه وسلم (أصحابي كالنجوم فبأيهم اقتديتم اهتديتم عزي إسناده إلى ابن عدي، فإنه دل على أن الاقتداء بكل هداية فيكون صواباً لأن الاقتداء بالخطأ ضلال، وأجيب بأنه هدي من وجه لإيجاب الشارع العمل به فلا نسلم أن الاقتداء بالخطأ مطلقاً ضلال، بل بالخطأ الذي لم يوجب الشارع العمل به، وهذا الخطأ قد أوجب العمل به، فالاقتداء به هداية، ثم الحديث قد ضعف أيضاً.
And they said: Secondly, the Prophet ﷺ said: 'My companions are like stars; whichever of them you follow, you will be guided.' (Attributed to Ibn 'Adi) This indicates that following any of them is guidance, so it is correct, because following error is misguidance. And it was answered that it is guidance in one sense because the Lawgiver has made it obligatory to act upon it. So we do not concede that following error is absolutely misguidance, but only following error which the Lawgiver has not made obligatory to act upon. And this error (the mujtahid's) has been made obligatory to act upon, so following it is guidance. Moreover, the hadith is weak.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof of the musawwibun: a hadith stating that following any of the Prophet's companions leads to guidance, implying each is correct. Since error cannot be guidance, each companion must be correct. The Hanafi rebuttal: following the companion's ijtihad is guidance because the Lawgiver (Shari') has obligated us to act on it — even if it is mistaken in reality, the act of following is rewarded as guidance. The absolute statement that 'following error is misguidance' is qualified: error that is not commanded to be followed is misguidance, but commanded error (like a mujtahid's ijtihad) is guidance in terms of duty. Additionally, the hadith is considered weak in transmission, weakening the proof.

Key Terms
الاقتداء بالخطأ (al-iqtidā' bi'l-khaṭa): Following an erroneous ruling; the point of contention is whether such following is misguidance or can be guidance.
