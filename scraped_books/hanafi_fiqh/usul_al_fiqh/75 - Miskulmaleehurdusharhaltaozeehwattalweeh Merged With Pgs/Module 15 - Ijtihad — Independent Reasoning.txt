Direct Derivation of Knowledge: Excluding the Muqallid
Answer to objection about muqallid's knowledge

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between direct knowledge from evidence and indirect knowledge, and explain why the muqallid's knowledge is excluded from the definition of fiqh in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Fiqh requires direct inference; following a mujtahid without evidence reflection does not yield fiqh.

This lesson examines a key objection to the definition of fiqh as knowledge derived from detailed evidence. Some argue that a muqallid's knowledge, since it traces back to evidence via the mujtahid, should also be considered as derived from evidence. The commentator Taftazani clarifies the precise meaning of 'deriving knowledge from evidence,' showing that the definition requires personal, direct reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
Recognize the implied question: since the muqallid's knowledge ultimately depends on evidence through the mujtahid, it might be considered as derived from evidence.
The objector conflates indirect causal chain with direct derivation.
2
Understand Taftazani's clarification
He defines 'husul al-ilm min al-dalil' as obtaining knowledge directly by personally reflecting, pondering, and deducing from the evidence.
The key is the absence of intermediaries in the act of derivation.
3
Apply to the muqallid
The muqallid obtains his knowledge from the statement of the mujtahid, not from the evidence itself. He does not engage in direct reflection on the sources.
Thus his knowledge does not meet the definition.
4
Conclude the exclusion
Therefore, the muqallid's knowledge is excluded from the definition of fiqh, which is reserved for mujtahids.
This maintains the technical boundary between ijtihad and taqlid.
5
Note the implication
Even though the muqallid's knowledge is valid and based on evidence indirectly, it is not 'ilm fiqhi in the technical sense.
This is a crucial distinction in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

And the meaning of obtaining knowledge from evidence... The purpose of the commentator Taftazani is to answer an implied question. The question: The statement that the knowledge of the muqallid is excluded by 'min adillatiha' is incorrect, because the knowledge of the muqallid is also obtained from detailed evidences. This is because the muqallid's knowledge is attributed to the statement of the mujtahid, then the statement of the mujtahid is attributed to his knowledge, then the knowledge of the mujtahid is based on the evidence of the ruling. So through these intermediaries, the muqallid's knowledge is also obtained from detailed evidences and is based on evidences. So saying that the muqallid's knowledge is excluded from detailed evidences is not correct. Answer: The commentator Taftazani answers that obtaining knowledge from evidence means that the one who obtains it himself directly reflects, ponders, and deduces the ruling from the evidence. Therefore, the muqallid's knowledge is excluded because, although it is ultimately based on evidences and obtained from them, the muqallid did not obtain it through reflection on the evidences; rather, he obtained it from the statement of the mujtahid. Hence, the knowledge of the muqallid is excluded from the definition of fiqh.

Commentary
This passage addresses a potential objection: since the muqallid's knowledge ultimately traces back to detailed evidence through the chain of mujtahid, it could be considered knowledge from evidence. The commentator clarifies that 'knowledge from evidence' in the definition of fiqh means direct, personal inference from evidence through reflection and reasoning. The muqallid relies on the mujtahid's statement, not on the evidence itself. Therefore, his knowledge is not considered fiqh. This preserves the definition that fiqh is the knowledge of a mujtahid derived directly from the sources.

Key Terms
حصول العلم من الدليل (husul al-ʿilm min al-dalil): Directly obtaining knowledge by reflecting on evidence, not through intermediaries.
مقلد (muqallid): One who follows a mujtahid without knowing the evidence.

Definition and Refutation of Tahayyu' Qarib in Ijtihad
Debate on definition of tahayyu' qarib as condition for ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi critique of Ibn al-Hajib's definition of tahayyu' qarib and identify the key points of disagreement with Taftazani's defense.

Core Idea
Tahayyu' qarib requires actual removal of obstacles, not mere potential — otherwise, ijtihad becomes too broad or too narrow.

This passage delves into a core usul dispute: what does it mean to have the 'near readiness' required for ijtihad? Ibn al-Hajib offers a definition that the author challenges with four objections. Taftazani defends Ibn al-Hajib, but the super-commentary refutes Taftazani point by point. Understanding this debate sharpens your ability to assess qualifications for ijtihad and recognize the strictness of Hanafi usul methodology.

Guided Path
1
State the initial definition of tahayyu' qarib
Identify Ibn al-Hajib's definition: a faqih who can derive rulings for any incident when it occurs, based on mastery of sources, causes, and conditions.
This is the 'near readiness' that opponents claim is determinate.
2
List the author's four objections
Outline the four critiques: (1) excludes recognized mujtahids, (2) allows for error, (3) ijtihad is not permissible in all rulings, (4) misinterprets 'ilm as tahayyu'.
These objections aim to show the definition is either too strict or misguided.
3
Examine Taftazani's responses
Taftazani addresses each objection by arguing that external factors (e.g., conflict of evidences, obstacles, illusion, forgetfulness) can prevent actual knowledge without negating capability.
He uses the hadith of Mu'adh to support ijtihad in all matters without text.
4
Analyze the super-commentary's refutations
The super-commentary rejects Taftazani's excuses: conflict of evidences still yields a ruling via tarjih/qiyas/tawaqquf; obstacles are part of the condition, so if present, tahayyu' is absent; a firm faculty cannot be constantly overcome by illusion; inability to distinguish truth/falsehood negates the capability altogether.
Also refutes the hadith argument by noting the Prophet did not ask about non-ijtihad cases because it was obvious.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The dispute affects who qualifies as a mujtahid: the Hanafi view (via the super-commentary) upholds the author's critique, meaning tahayyu' qarib must be defined such that recognized mujtahids are not excluded, and external factors do not excuse failure to derive rulings.
This leads to a stricter qualification for ijtihad than Taftazani's broader definition.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection against the definition of fiqh as 'ilm (certain knowledge) because most fiqh rulings are derived through ijtihad and qiyas, which are speculative. The commentator (in the sharh of Al-Tawdih) provides three responses. The first response denies the minor premise: fiqh, as defined by the Hanafi school, is only about rulings that are certain, either because they come from clear revelation or from consensus. Thus, the objection does not apply to the definition. The second response denies the major premise: the term 'ilm is commonly used for both certain and speculative disciplines, such as astronomy. The third response argues that all fiqh rulings, even qiyasi ones, are certain in the eyes of the Lawgiver. This certainty arises because the mujtahid's strong presumption (ghalabat al-zann) is ratified by the Lawgiver, making the ruling effectively certain for that mujtahid. The commentary then notes that this third response is valid only for the school that holds 'every mujtahid is correct' (kull mujtahid musib). For the school that holds mujtahids can err, the certainty is not objective but rather practical: the ruling is binding and must be acted upon as if certain, though it may not be certain in divine knowledge. This distinction reflects a key debate in usul al-fiqh about the epistemological status of ijtihadi rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement by the commentator al-Taftazani. He first presents an attempt by some scholars to prove that fiqh is certain knowledge ('ilm qat'i) through a series of four logical syllogisms (ashkal). The syllogisms combine premises about the obligatory nature of acting on a speculative ruling and that any ruling obligatory to act on is certainly from Allah. Al-Taftazani refutes this by rejecting the major premise that every ruling obligatory to act on is certainly from Allah, as it is possible that a speculative ruling is obligatory to act on. He also criticizes the proof for the premise, since it assumes what is in dispute. Finally, he notes that even if one accepts the syllogism of the Musawwiba school (that every speculative ruling is certainly from Allah), the mention of obligatory action becomes superfluous. The commentator concludes that this attempt is flawed.

دليل قاطع (dalil qati'): A definitive proof that yields certainty, here identified as ijma' and ahadith that are mutawatir in meaning.
فرقہ مصوبہ (firqa musawwiba): A theological group that holds every mujtahid's ijtihad is correct (musib), implying that the ruling reached is certainly from Allah.
شکل (منطق) (shakl (mantiq)): A figure or form of a syllogism. The text presents four syllogistic figures to prove the point.

The Definition of Muttawassil: Mujtahid or Muqallid?
Commentary on condition for qiyas and debate on muttawassil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two possible definitions of muttawassil in Hanafi usul and evaluate the arguments for each, applying the definition of fiqh to determine which position is more consistent.

Core Idea
Whether a muqallid is muttawassil depends on whether fiqh requires direct knowledge from the four proofs.

In this passage from the commentary on al-Talwih, the author (Sadr al-Shariah) presents two possibilities for the meaning of 'muttawassil'—the person who uses usul to reach fiqh. The commentator then criticizes the second possibility, arguing that a muqallid cannot reach fiqh proper. This lesson unpacks the dispute and clarifies the implications for the definition of usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the two interpretations
Identify the two possible meanings of muttawassil mentioned in the text: (1) restricted to mujtahid, (2) including muqallid.
The author presents the second as 'not far-fetched'.
2
Analyze the commentator's objection
The commentator argues that the second possibility is far-fetched because no predecessor adopted it, and the mention of taqlid in usul books is only for contrast.
Key principle: 'Things are clarified by their opposites'.
3
Examine the definition of fiqh
According to the text, fiqh is knowledge of rulings from the four proofs. The muqallid's knowledge comes from the mujtahid's statement, not from direct proof analysis.
This supports the commentator's restriction.
4
Evaluate the author's response
The author's phrase 'it is not far-fetched' indicates he does not strongly endorse the second possibility; he merely presents it as a plausible scholarly conjecture.
Novel possibilities are not automatically invalid.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
If muttawassil includes muqallid, the definition of usul al-fiqh changes from 'that by which one reaches fiqh' to 'that by which one reaches the issues of fiqh'.
This subtle shift has implications for the subject matter of usul.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on al-Talwih dealing with two issues: the condition that qiyas must be such that an earlier mujtahid's opinion has already been expressed on the matter (to avoid violating composite consensus), and the definition of 'muttawassil' (the person who uses the tools of usul to reach fiqh). On the first issue, the condition applies only when earlier mujtahids had expressed views; if not, a later mujtahid may dissent. On the second, there is a debate: should 'muttawassil' be limited to a mujtahid or include a muqallid? The author (following Sadr al-Shariah) suggests both possibilities, but the commentator argues the second is far-fetched because none of the predecessors considered a muqallid as reaching 'fiqh' (which requires knowledge from the four proofs directly). The muqallid only reaches masai'l al-fiqh (the specific issues) through rules of taqlid, not fiqh proper. The commentator ultimately refutes the criticism, noting that the author only presented it as a possibility.
