Rebuttal of the Objection that the Hanafi Definition of Fiqh is Invented
Refuting the claim that the definition of fiqh is arbitrarily invented

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the objection that the definition of fiqh is 'invented' is invalid, and identify the four precision elements in the Hanafi definition of fiqh.

Core Idea
All definitions are conventional; the Hanafi definition of fiqh is precise by including kull, zuhur, ijma, and malakat al-istinbat.

This passage addresses a powerful objection: that the Hanafi definition of fiqh is merely invented. The commentator responds by showing that all definitions are human constructs, and then highlights four elements that make this definition particularly precise. Understanding this response strengthens your grasp of what fiqh really means in the Hanafi tradition.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the objection
Identify the objection being raised: Taftazani claimed the definition of fiqh is 'invented' (mukhtara'), implying it is arbitrary and lacks foundation.
This is a common critique against definitions that are not directly from revelation.
2
Understand the rebuttal
The commentator argues that no definition is sent down from heaven; all definitions are conventional and human constructs. Therefore, the objection is invalid.
This reframes the discussion from divine origin to usefulness and precision.
3
Identify the four precision elements
The commentator praises the definition for its precision due to four matters: (1) the word 'kull' (all), (2) the condition that the rulings have appeared with revelation (zuhur), (3) the condition of ijma, and (4) the inclusion of malakat al-istinbat.
These elements ensure that only comprehensive knowledge combined with deductive skill qualifies as fiqh.
4
Analyze the specific objection about plural 'al-ahkam'
The objector notes that the definition uses the plural 'al-ahkam', which indicates more than two. So knowing one or two rulings would not make one a faqih, but three would, which seems arbitrary.
This objection rests on a literal reading of the plural.
5
Grasp the response regarding malakat al-istinbat
The commentator replies that malakat al-istinbat cannot be acquired by knowing just a few rulings; it requires repeated perception of many rulings over a long period. Thus, knowing one or two rulings does not yield the skill, and the objection is based on a false assumption.
This shows that the definition is not arbitrary; the skill is the key.

Commentary
This passage is an objection and rebuttal regarding the definition of fiqh. The author of the matn defined fiqh, and Taftazani objected that the definition is 'invented' (mukhtara'). The commentator here rebuts that all definitions are conventional and human constructs, so the objection is invalid. Then the commentator praises the definition for being precise due to four elements: the word 'kull', the condition of revelation appearing (zuhur), ijma, and malakat al-istinbat. Then a specific objection is raised: using the plural 'al-ahkam' implies that knowing only one or two rulings does not make one a faqih, but three does, which is contradictory because malakat al-istinbat could be present with few rulings. The answer is that malakat al-istinbat is not acquired with just a few rulings; it requires repeated and long-term engagement with many rulings. Therefore, the objection is based on a false assumption.

Key Terms
الفقه (al-fiqh): Knowledge of practical shar'i rulings accompanied by the skill of deduction
ملكة الاستنباط (malakat al-istinbat): The ingrained skill of deducing rulings from their evidences or deriving analogical branches from primary rulings

Why Analogical Issues Are Excluded from Fiqh's Definition
Expansion on the definition of fiqh and the circularity condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi usul al-fiqh excludes knowledge of analogical issues from the definition of fiqh and identify the circularity (dawr) argument.

Core Idea
Including analogical issues in fiqh's definition creates circularity: fiqahah depends on them, and they depend on fiqahah.

In this lesson, we explore a key debate in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: whether knowledge of analogical issues (masa'il qiyasiyyah) should be part of the definition of fiqh. The commentator explains why it must be excluded to avoid a logical circularity, and responds to objections from later jurists. This discussion reveals the deep structure of the Hanafi concept of fiqh as a faculty (malakah) of derivation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core claim: malakah is required
The passage begins by stating that a faqih must possess a faculty (malakah) of deriving rulings from evidences, not just knowledge of texts.
2
Understand the circularity argument
If knowing analogical issues were a condition, then a person must already be a faqih to know them (since they result from ijtihad), creating a vicious circle.
This is a classic usul argument against including derived issues in definitions.
3
Apply the argument to first and later analogists
The text considers an objection: circularity only applies to first analogists, not later ones. But the response shows that later mujtahids cannot rely on previous analogies—they must derive anew, so circularity persists.
This reinforces the necessity of independent ijtihad.
4
Analyze the 'qiyas reveals divine law' objection
An opponent argues that analogical issues are still revealed by divine law (via qiyas), so they should be included. Two answers are given: (1) the revelation was for the earlier mujtahid, (2) even if included, they are indirect and thus not required.
This shows the nuanced Hanafi position on qiyas as a revealer, not an establisher.
5
Note Taftazani's counter-critique
Finally, the commentator Taftazani objects that the matn's definition implies fiqh can increase and decrease, contradicting its fixed terminological meaning. This sets up further refinement.
This critique leads to the next stage of definitional rigor.

The outcome of the expression is: 'with a faculty of deriving rulings from their evidences'. The intent is that it is necessary for a faqih that he, along with knowledge of that which was mentioned, also possesses a faculty of deriving rulings from their evidences. If a person merely hears the text and understands the rulings due to familiarity with language, but does not possess the faculty of deriving rulings from evidences, he will not be called a faqih. His statement: 'Not the analogical issues' means they are not a condition. The purpose of the commentator is to explain the text. The commentator explains: Knowledge of analogical issues is not a condition in the definition of fiqh because these analogical issues are the result of the faqih's fiqahah and ijtihad. Since analogical issues are branches that are derived by ijtihad, their knowledge depends on the person being a faqih. Then if knowledge of them were made a condition for the mujtahid and faqih, the faqih's fiqahah would depend on them, and circularity would arise. If it is said: This only applies to the first analogists. Here the commentator mentions an objection and answers it. The objection is that the circularity you mentioned is correct regarding the first mujtahids and analogists, because when the first analogists derived the analogical issues, these analogical issues are the result of their fiqahah and depend on their capacity. Then if knowledge of these analogical issues were made a condition for them, undoubtedly circularity would arise because their fiqahah depends on the analogical issues and the analogical issues depend on their fiqahah, so there would be circularity. But if knowledge of these analogical issues were made a condition for later analogists, no circularity would be necessary because the analogists' fiqahah depends on these analogical issues, but the analogical issues do not depend on the fiqahah of later analogists, because these analogical issues are not the result of these analogists' fiqahah; rather, they are the result of the earlier analogists' fiqahah. Therefore, regarding later analogists, there is no circularity. He responds: Nevertheless, circularity arises. If knowledge of analogical issues were made a condition for later analogists, circularity would still be necessary because the mujtahid who comes later is not allowed to follow the first mujtahid and first analogist in analogical issues; rather, it is necessary for him to perform ijtihad again in these analogical issues and act according to his ijtihad. Now, if knowledge of these analogical issues were made a condition for the later mujtahid, circularity would arise because these analogical issues are the result of this later mujtahid's fiqahah, so circularity is certain. Commentary
This passage is an expansion (sharh) on a definition of fiqh from the matn (Al-Tawdih). The commentator explains that the definition of fiqh requires not just knowledge of rulings but also a faculty (malakah) for deriving them from their evidences. Then it discusses why knowledge of analogical issues (masa'il qiyasiyyah) is not a condition in the definition, to avoid circularity. The argument follows Hanafi usuli reasoning: if knowledge of analogical issues were required, then a person must already be a faqih to have that knowledge, leading to a vicious circle. The commentator addresses an objection that circularity only applies to the first analogists, but for later jurists, knowledge of analogical issues could be required without circularity since those issues were derived by earlier mujtahids. The response shows that even for later jurists, circularity arises because taqlid is not allowed in analogical issues; each mujtahid must independently derive them, so their knowledge still depends on their own fiqahah. The passage then addresses another objection: analogical issues are also revealed by divine law through qiyas (which is a revealer, not an establisher). Two answers are given: first, the apparent revelation in those issues was for the earlier mujtahid, not for the later one; second, even if they are considered revealed, they are revealed indirectly via qiyas, whereas the definition refers to directly revealed rulings. Finally, the commentator Taftazani begins his own objections to the definition, arguing that fiqh as a technical term should be fixed and not subject to increase or decrease, contrary to what the matn's definition implies.

Key Terms
ملكة استنباط الاحكام (malakat istinbat al-ahkam): A faculty or ability in the jurist to derive legal rulings from their evidence through ijtihad.
المسائل القياسية (al-masa'il al-qiyasiyyah): Legal issues that are derived through qiyas (analogical reasoning) rather than from explicit texts.
دور (dawr): Circular reasoning or mutual dependence, considered a logical fallacy in usul al-fiqh.

The Hanafi definition of fiqh excludes analogical issues to avoid logical circularity; fiqh is a faculty of derivation (malakah) applied to directly revealed rulings, not to the products of ijtihad.

Taftazani's Objections to the Definition of Fiqh
Taftazani raises two objections against the author's definition of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state Taftazani's two objections to the Hanafi definition of fiqh and explain the Hanafi response regarding specificity and inclusiveness.

Core Idea
Fiqh is a generic science that expands and contracts; technical definitions allow such variation.

In this passage, the commentator Taftazani challenges Sadr al-Shariah's definition of fiqh. He argues that technical fiqh should be fixed, but the definition implies change over time. The Hanafi response clarifies that 'specific' can mean generic, not limited to individual rulings. A second objection concerning the Companions is also addressed, showing that the definition can be applied contextually.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first objection
Taftazani argues that technical fiqh is a specific, fixed science—like other sciences—and should not increase or decrease. But the author's definition makes fiqh a general concept that grows and shrinks over time.
The objection centers on the necessity of boundary (hadd) in definitions.
2
Understand the Hanafi response to the first objection
The response distinguishes between 'specific in a personal sense' (individual rulings) and 'specific in a generic sense' (a type). Since fiqh is a type, it can have individuals that change over time; generic specification does not prevent increase or decrease.
This aligns with how other sciences (e.g., medicine) also expand.
3
Identify the second objection
Taftazani contends that the author's definition requires knowledge of both revealed rulings and consensus-based rulings, plus the ability of istinbat. Since consensus did not exist during the Prophet's time, the definition would exclude the Companions, making it non-comprehensive.
This attacks the definition's jami'iyah (inclusiveness).
4
Analyze the attempted fix and its rejection
One might try to make the definition conditional: if consensus exists, include it; if not, just revealed rulings. But such conditional definitions are considered far-fetched and inappropriate in formal definitions.
The response implies that definitions should be unconditional and universally applicable.
5
Summarize the Hanafi stance
The Hanafi school maintains that the original definition is valid: fiqh is knowledge of practical rulings from detailed evidence, which naturally expands and contracts over time without compromising the definition's essence.
The objections are refuted, and the definition stands.

Defining Fiqh: The Correct Meaning of 'Zuhur al-Wahy'
Refuting two wrong interpretations of 'zuhur' in the definition of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and refute two incorrect interpretations of 'zuhur al-wahy' in the Hanafi definition of fiqh, and explain the correct interpretation as 'zuhur 'ind al-mujtahid'.

Core Idea
Fiqh is knowledge of rulings evident to the individual mujtahid, not to the entire community.

The definition of fiqh as 'knowledge of the rulings whose revelation has become evident' raises a key question: what does 'evident' (zuhur) mean? Two plausible but flawed answers are examined and dismissed, leading to the precise Hanafi position that evidentness is relative to the individual mujtahid who undertakes ijtihad.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two incorrect interpretations of 'zuhur'
Locate in the text where the author proposes two possible meanings: 'zuhur fi al-jumlah' (partial) and 'zuhur 'ala al-a'mm wa al-agh lab' (majority). Understand each option.
These correspond to manifestation to some Sahaba and manifestation to most Sahaba.
2
Analyze the refutation of the first option (partial)
Follow the argument: if 'zuhur' means 'to some Sahaba', then a faqih would need to know only those rulings known to some Sahaba. But this excludes great jurists like many Sahaba who did not know rulings known to Aisha, yet they are considered fuqaha. Contradiction.
The example of Aisha illustrates that knowledge varies among Sahaba.
3
Analyze the refutation of the second option (majority)
The author gives two reasons: (a) the majority is undefined because the total number of Sahaba is unknown, so 'most' is unknown; (b) if majority were required, rulings from ahad reports would be excluded, but many fiqh rulings are based on ahad reports.
Historical dispersion of Sahaba also undermines the possibility of universal knowledge.
4
Identify the correct interpretation and its justification
The author states that 'zuhur' means 'zuhur 'ind al-mujtahid'. This preserves the inclusion of all valid rulings from both mutawatir and ahad sources, as long as the mujtahid attains certainty.
This is the Hanafi position: fiqh is personal to each mujtahid's knowledge.
5
Reflect on the broader implications
Consider how this interpretation allows different madhahib to have different bodies of fiqh (e.g., Hanafi, Shafi'i) and includes qiyasi rulings within compiled fiqh.
Compiled fiqh (fiqh mudawwan) includes rulings from ijtihad even if not 'real fiqh' in the narrowest sense.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional analysis of the term 'zuhur al-wahy' (manifestation of revelation) used in the definition of fiqh. The author, commenting on the matn (Al-Tawdih), explains that the phrase 'ma zahara nuzul al-wahi' in the definition of fiqh requires clarification. The discussion reflects the Hanafi emphasis on the mujtahid's personal certainty (qat') in the rulings he derives, as opposed to requiring public dissemination.

Text of Tawdih: 'And what was said that fiqh is speculative' – From here the author mentions an objection and answers it. The objection is that fiqh is a speculative thing because most issues of fiqh are ijtihadi and qiyasi, and 'ilm (knowledge) is not applied to the speculative because 'ilm is used only for certainties. So why is 'ilm applied to the speculative rulings of fiqh? Answer: The author mentions three answers to this objection. (1) First answer: There is an assumed minor premise and major premise. For example, it would be said: 'Fiqh is speculative, and everything speculative is not called 'ilm, therefore fiqh is not called 'ilm.' The summary of the first answer is that we do not accept your minor premise; rather, according to our definition, fiqh is the name for definitive rulings: that which the revelation descended with is also definitive, and that upon which consensus has been formed is also definitive. Therefore this objection will not apply to us. (2) Second answer: We do not accept your major premise that 'ilm cannot be applied to the speculative. Rather, 'ilm is applied to both certainties and speculative matters; for example, the science of seeking knowledge, astronomy, cosmology – see that 'ilm is applied to all of these even though they contain speculative matters. (3) The gist of the third answer is that all rulings of fiqh, even if they are analogical rulings, are definitive, so 'ilm can be applied to them. The question becomes: Analogical and ijtihadi rulings are speculative; how do you claim they are definitive? The answer is that whenever a mujtahid, through reflection and thought, derives a ruling from his predominant conjecture, this predominant conjecture is considered valid by the Lawgiver. So much so that the ruling obtained from this predominant conjecture is, as it were, established from the Lawgiver as definitively established. It is as if the Lawgiver said: 'Whenever the mujtahid's predominant conjecture occurs, the ruling is definitively established.' Thus it is proven that the ruling which the mujtahid derived from his ijtihad and predominant conjecture has become definitive before the Lawgiver; it is no longer speculative. The conjecture was only in its method and premises; once the objective is established, it becomes definitive with no doubt. Like a person who reaches his goal by traveling a conjectural path; even though the path is conjectural, reaching the goal is definite. Therefore 'ilm can be applied to these analogical issues. This answer is correct according to the group that says 'every mujtahid is correct'. But for the group that says 'the mujtahid may err or be correct', this answer will not be correct, because they do not accept that whenever the mujtahid has a predominant conjecture, the ruling is established. For them it is not necessary that the mujtahid's predominant conjecture is correct; it could also be wrong. Therefore, according to this group, the meaning of 'whenever the mujtahid's predominant conjecture occurs, the ruling is definitively established' would be that the ruling established from the mujtahid's predominant conjecture becomes definitive in the sense that acting upon it is obligatory, just as acting upon a definitive ruling is obligatory, or in the sense that it is definitive from the perspective of the evidence even if it is not definitive in Allah's knowledge. Therefore, according to this school, analogical issues and rulings can sometimes be called definitive. Summary of the answer: It is established that all rulings of fiqh are definitive, whether they are textual rulings, consensus rulings, or analogical rulings. Hence, applying 'ilm to fiqh is correct.

Commentary
This passage is a defense of the definition of fiqh as 'ilm (certain knowledge). An objector claims fiqh is primarily speculative (zanni) because most rulings come from ijtihad and qiyas, which are uncertain. The author gives three counter-arguments. First, he rejects the minor premise that fiqh is zanni, arguing that according to the Hanafi definition, fiqh includes only definitive rulings from revelation (wahy) and consensus (ijma). Second, he rejects the major premise that 'ilm cannot apply to the speculative, showing that fields like astronomy are called 'ilm despite containing conjecture. Third, he argues that even analogical rulings become definitive because the Lawgiver validates the mujtahid's predominant conjecture. However, this third answer is accepted differently by two groups: those who hold every mujtahid is correct (musib) and those who allow error (mukhti'). For the latter, the ruling is definitive in terms of obligatory action and evidentiary certainty, though not absolutely in divine knowledge. The summary reaffirms that all fiqh rulings are definitive in the sense relevant to calling it 'ilm.

Defends fiqh's qat'iyyah against objections from qiyas and thubut.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two objections against the qat'iyyah of fiqh and explain the Hanafi responses distinguishing fiqh haqiqi from fiqh mudawwan, and the condition of 'arid in nass and ijma.

Core Idea
Qiyasi rulings are not part of real fiqh; nass and ijma are qat'i unless impeded — fiqh's certainty is upheld.

This lesson addresses the first two objections to the claim that all fiqh rulings are qat'i. The first objection argues that qiyasi rulings are zanni and thus cannot be part of certain knowledge. The author counters that nass and ijma are inherently qat'i unless an impediment ('arid) is present; the discussion concerns unimpeded texts.

The second objection to the first response is: The author's statement that 'what is established by revelation of wahy and what is agreed upon by ijma is qat'i' is not correct, because not every ruling established by nass is qat'i; rather, it is qat'i only when its thubut (establishment) is also qat'i. This is why rulings established by khabar al-wahid (single-narrator reports) are zanni, even though khabar al-wahid is also a kind of nass, but because this nass is zanni in its thubut, the ruling derived from it is zanni, not qat'i. So, absolutely saying that rulings accompanied by revelation of wahy or on which ijma has occurred are qat'i is incorrect. The commentator, while explaining, says: This assumption that qiyasi rulings are zanni is not correct; rather, these rulings are actually qat'i. However, the mujtahid's method and path by which he arrives at this ruling is a zanni path. That is, the mujtahid strives with his ghalabat al-zann (prevailing opinion) to reach the ruling; when he reaches it, the ruling is established as qat'i. The zanniness of the path does not necessitate the ruling being zanni. For example, a person intends to go to Makkah for Hajj but does not know the route. He makes ijtihad and starts on a path according to his ghalabat al-zann, and reaches Makkah. Now, the path he chose was zanni, but the goal (Makkah) was definitely attained. In this case, his zann led to certainty. Similarly, qiyasi rulings are themselves qat'i, but there is zann in their path. 'Wa taqdiruhu' elaborates further on this third response. But before this elaboration, understand that there are two schools of thought among scholars regarding the ijtihad of a mujtahid: (1) Some scholars hold that every mujtahid's ijtihad is correct (sawab) and the ruling established by it is also established in the knowledge of Allah. This group is called Musawwibah. (2) Some scholars hold that not every mujtahid is correct; only one mujtahid is correct. This is the view of the majority, called Ghayr Musawwibah, as the famous saying goes: 'al-mujtahid yukhti'u wa yusibu' (the mujtahid errs and is correct). Commentary
This passage deals with a fundamental usul issue: whether all fiqh rulings are qat'i (definitive). The author defends the view that they are, responding to two objections: first, that qiyasi rulings are zanni and thus cannot be part of certain knowledge; second, that rulings from nass or ijma are only qat'i if their thubut is qat'i. The first response distinguishes between fiqh haqiqi (real fiqh) and fiqh mudawwan (codified fiqh) — qiyasi rulings are included only out of necessity. The second response states that nass and ijma are always qat'i in themselves if not impeded. The third response, from al-Mahsul, argues that the ruling itself is qat'i even if the path (tariq) to it is zanni; an analogy is given of the pilgrim reaching Makkah through a zanni route. Then the two schools on ijtihad are introduced: Musawwibah (every mujtahid correct) and Ghayr Musawwibah (only one correct). Under the Musawwibah view, since ghalabat al-zann is validated by ijma and mutawatir-in-meaning reports, the ruling becomes qat'i in Allah's knowledge. An objection that this combines opposites (zanni becoming qat'i) is refuted, though the refutation is cut off. The passage illustrates the Hanafi concern with establishing the certainty of fiqh as a science.

Key Terms
قَطْعِيّ (qat'i): A ruling that is definitively established, free from the possibility of its opposite, either through certain proof or by divine knowledge after ijtihad.
ظَنّ (zann): A probabilistic belief that allows for its opposite; here it refers to the method (tariq) of ijtihad or the thubut of a khabar.
فِقْه حَقِيقِيّ (fiqh haqiqi): Real fiqh, consisting of rulings that are certain (qat'i) in their thubut and dalalah, primarily from explicit nass and ijma.
فِقْه مُدَوَّن (fiqh mudawwan): Codified fiqh, which includes rulings from qiyas and other zanni sources, compiled for the practical needs of the community.

The Third Response: Ruling is Qat'i, Path is Zanni
Presents the third response and the Musawwibah view to defend qat'iyyah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the third response that a ruling is qat'i even if the path is zanni, and analyze the Musawwibah view that every mujtahid is correct using ijma and mutawatir-in-meaning reports.

Core Idea
A ruling reached by ijtihad is qat'i in divine knowledge; the path is zanni, but the destination is certain.

This lesson unpacks the most profound response to the objection from qiyas. The third response, attributed to al-Mahsul, distinguishes between the ruling (hukm) itself and the method (tariq) by which it is derived. The ruling, once reached by a mujtahid through ijtihad, becomes qat'i in the knowledge of Allah, even if the method involved zann. The pilgrim analogy illustrates this: the route may be uncertain, but reaching Makkah is certain. The lesson also explores the two schools of thought on ijtihad—Musawwibah (every mujtahid correct) and Ghayr Musawwibah (only one correct)—and shows how the Musawwibah view supports the third response by invoking ijma and mutawatir-in-meaning reports to validate ghalabat al-zann.

Guided Path
1
Understand the third response from al-Mahsul
The third response states that the ruling itself is qat'i (maqtu' bihi) while the path (tariq) is zanni. This is attributed to al-Mahsul.
This separates the certainty of the outcome from the uncertainty of the means.
2
Grasp the analogy of the pilgrim
A pilgrim who does not know the route to Makkah chooses a path based on ijtihad (zanni). When he reaches Makkah, the goal is attained with certainty, though the path was uncertain.
The analogy clarifies that zann in the method does not affect the certainty of the result.
3
Learn the two schools on ijtihad
The Musawwibah hold that every mujtahid is correct and his ruling corresponds to Allah's will. The Ghayr Musawwibah hold that only one mujtahid is correct.
The third response is presented in two expositions: one for each school.
4
Analyze the Musawwibah justification
The Musawwibah view is supported by ijma that ghalabat al-zann obligates action, and by khabar ahad that reach tawatur in meaning, indicating that the ruling becomes qat'i in divine knowledge.
Thus, even if the path is zanni, the ruling is certain in Allah's knowledge and therefore qat'i.

Summary of the response: It is established that all rulings of fiqh are qat'i, whether they are textual (mansusah), consensus-based (ijma'iyyah), or qiyasi. Therefore, applying the term 'ilm (knowledge) to fiqh is correct. Commentary of al-Talwih: His statement 'fa-jawabuhu awwalan' indicates an objection against the author. The objection is: from the author's first response it is established that fiqh is called knowledge of qat'i rulings, so rulings through which the revelation of wahy (divine revelation) appears together with qiyas would be excluded from the definition of fiqh because they are zanni, whereas they constitute most issues of fiqh. Response: This objection has already been answered in detail: rulings through which the revelation of wahy appears by way of qiyas—these are exactly the qiyasi issues, and these qiyasi issues are excluded from real fiqh (fiqh haqiqi); they were only included in codified fiqh (fiqh mudawwan) due to the need and requirement of the common people. Hence, there is no defect in the author's statement. '...you have brought forward [objections] regarding nass and ijma...' The second objection to the first response is: The author's statement that 'what is established by revelation of wahy and what is agreed upon by ijma is qat'i' is not correct, because not every ruling established by nass is qat'i; rather, it is qat'i only when its thubut (establishment) is also qat'i. Similarly, a consensus-based ruling is not absolutely qat'i; it is qat'i only when its thubut is qat'i. This is why rulings established by khabar al-wahid (single-narrator reports) are zanni, even though khabar al-wahid is also a kind of nass, but because this nass is zanni in its thubut, the ruling derived from it is zanni, not qat'i. So, absolutely saying that rulings accompanied by revelation of wahy or on which ijma has occurred are qat'i is incorrect. Response: The commentator's objection is also not valid because the author's intent is the same: 'what is manifested by revelation of wahy in a qat'i and certain manner.' Response (2): Nass and ijma always confer qat'iyyah (definiteness), and the ruling established by them is necessarily qat'i. However, sometimes due to some impediment ('arid) qat'iyyah is not established. Our discussion is about nass and ijma that are free from impediments; they are always qat'i. Therefore, the author's statement that they are qat'i is correct. His statement: 'wa thalithuha huwa alladhi dhukira fi al-Mahsul wa ghayrihi anna al-hukm maqtu'un bihi wa al-zann fi tariqihi...' In the text, the author gave a third response: 'The application of 'ilm to fiqh is valid because all rulings of fiqh—whether those with manifest revelation, those of ijma, or qiyasi—are all qat'i.' The doubt here was: qiyasi rulings are zanni, how can you call them qat'i? So the author responded. The commentator, while explaining, says: This assumption that qiyasi rulings are zanni is not correct; rather, these rulings are actually qat'i. However, the mujtahid's method and path by which he arrives at this ruling is a zanni path. That is, the mujtahid strives with his ghalabat al-zann (prevailing opinion) to reach the ruling; when he reaches it, the ruling is established as qat'i. The zanniness of the path does not necessitate the ruling being zanni. For example, a person intends to go to Makkah for Hajj but does not know the route. He makes ijtihad and starts on a path according to his ghalabat al-zann, and reaches Makkah. Now, the path he chose was zanni, but the goal (Makkah) was definitely attained. In this case, his zann led to certainty. Similarly, qiyasi rulings are themselves qat'i, but there is zann in their path. 'Wa taqdiruhu' elaborates further on this third response. But before this elaboration, understand that there are two schools of thought among scholars regarding the ijtihad of a mujtahid: (1) Some scholars hold that every mujtahid's ijtihad is correct (sawab) and the ruling established by it is also established in the knowledge of Allah. This group is called Musawwibah. (2) Some scholars hold that not every mujtahid is correct; only one mujtahid is correct. This is the view of the majority, called Ghayr Musawwibah, as the famous saying goes: 'al-mujtahid yukhti'u wa yusibu' (the mujtahid errs and is correct). Now there are two expositions of the third response: the first exposition corresponds to the view of the Musawwibah, and the second to that of the Ghayr Musawwibah. 'Taqriruhu' presents the first exposition. Its summary: There is ijma of the ummah that when a mujtahid acquires ghalabat al-zann regarding a ruling, it becomes obligatory to act upon it. Reports (akhbar ahad) on this matter are numerous (kathirah) even reaching the level of tawatur in meaning. And the author's statement that 'ghalabat al-zann is considered valid by the Lawgiver in rulings' also means that khabar al-wahid which are mutawatir in meaning indicate that acting on ghalabat al-zann is obligatory. When both ijma and khabar al-wahid indicate that ghalabat al-zann demands obligation to act upon rulings, then it is as if there is a qat'i text from the Lawgiver that when a mujtahid's ghalabat al-zann is established on a ruling, that ruling becomes definitively established in the knowledge of Allah. Once this ruling is established in Allah's knowledge, that zanni ruling becomes qat'i in itself (fi nafs al-amr); it is no longer zanni. Therefore, the word 'ilm can be applied to it, because the ruling has become qat'i both in reality and in Allah's knowledge. Commentary
This passage deals with a fundamental usul issue: whether all fiqh rulings are qat'i (definitive). The author defends the view that they are, responding to two objections: first, that qiyasi rulings are zanni and thus cannot be part of certain knowledge; second, that rulings from nass or ijma are only qat'i if their thubut is qat'i. The first response distinguishes between fiqh haqiqi (real fiqh) and fiqh mudawwan (codified fiqh) — qiyasi rulings are included only out of necessity. The second response states that nass and ijma are always qat'i in themselves if not impeded. The third response, from al-Mahsul, argues that the ruling itself is qat'i even if the path (tariq) to it is zanni; an analogy is given of the pilgrim reaching Makkah through a zanni route. Then the two schools on ijtihad are introduced: Musawwibah (every mujtahid correct) and Ghayr Musawwibah (only one correct). Under the Musawwibah view, since ghalabat al-zann is validated by ijma and mutawatir-in-meaning reports, the ruling becomes qat'i in Allah's knowledge. An objection that this combines opposites (zanni becoming qat'i) is refuted, though the refutation is cut off. The passage illustrates the Hanafi concern with establishing the certainty of fiqh as a science.

Key Terms
مُصَوِّبَة (musawwibah): Those who hold that every mujtahid is correct (musib) and his ruling corresponds to divine will.
غَيْر مُصَوِّبَة (ghayr musawwibah): Those who hold that only one mujtahid is correct, based on the hadith that the mujtahid errs or is correct.
غَلَبَة الظَّنّ (ghalabat al-zann): The prevailing opinion of a mujtahid, which, when reached, obligates action and makes the ruling qat'i in divine knowledge.
طَرِيق (tariq): The method or path of ijtihad (e.g., qiyas, istihsan); it is zanni, but this does not affect the certainty of the ruling.

Key Takeaway
The qat'iyyah of fiqh is preserved by distinguishing the ruling (qat'i in divine knowledge) from the path (zanni), and by the Musawwibah view that every mujtahid's ruling is correct.

Refuting the Claim that Fiqh is Certain Knowledge
Taftazani refutes a syllogism claiming fiqh is certain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical flaw in the claim that fiqh is qat'i and explain the Hanafi position that fiqh is zanni yet binding.

Core Idea
A ruling can be zanni in derivation yet obligatory to act upon.

This passage from Taftazani's commentary addresses a key epistemological question in usul al-fiqh: Is the body of fiqh rulings certain (qat'i) or probable (zanni)? The opponent argues via syllogism that because fiqh is the mujtahid's zann, it must be certain. Taftazani dismantles this argument, showing that the major premise is flawed and that a zanni ruling can still be binding. Understanding this distinction is crucial for appreciating the Hanafi approach to legal authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's syllogism
The opponent uses a syllogism: minor premise = fiqh is the mujtahid's zann; major premise = every mujtahid's zann is certainly known; conclusion = fiqh is certainly known. Locate this in the text.
The text mentions this as the fourth shape (shakl rabi').
2
Locate Taftazani's refutation point
Taftazani says 'la nusallim' (we do not accept) the major premise. Find the exact phrase and read his reasoning.
The major premise is: 'A ruling that it is obligatory to act upon is certainly known as Allah's ruling.'
3
Understand the counterexample
Taftazani argues it is possible for a ruling to be zanni from Allah and yet obligatory to act upon. This undermines the major premise's universality.
This is the key logical move: obligation to act does not require certainty of the ruling's origin.
4
Examine the musawwiba response
If one adopts the musawwiba view (that the mujtahid's zann is itself Allah's ruling), then the mention of 'obligation to act' becomes superfluous, exposing redundancy.
This shows the opponent's argument is either unsound or unnecessary.
5
State the Hanafi conclusion
Taftazani's refutation establishes that fiqh remains zanni in its derivation. The obligation to act (wujub al-amal) does not require qat'.
This aligns with the Hanafi usul principle that rulings can be zanni yet binding.

Here the author explains the conditions of legal proofs: each proof from the legal proofs establishes a ruling only when all its conditions are found, whose mention is in its appropriate place. The author has mentioned a few conditions: (1) A condition is that the proof is not abrogated; if it is abrogated, the ruling will not be established by it, e.g., the permissibility of donkey meat: there is a hadith as proof but it is abrogated, as it was declared prohibited at Khaybar, so the ruling is not established by this proof. (2) There should be no equal contradicting evidence. (3) There should be no stronger contradicting evidence. (4) If qiyas is the proof for a ruling among the four proofs, then there is a specific condition: the qiyas of a later mujtahid is valid and a proof only when it agrees with the opinion of one of the earlier mujtahids. If his qiyas opposes all earlier mujtahids and does not agree with any single opinion, then this qiyas is invalid. Apparently this condition seems strange because it implies that a later mujtahid must follow one of the earlier ones, but the reality is not so. The actual situation is that the later mujtahid has two scenarios regarding the issue in which he is using qiyas and ijtihad: either earlier mujtahids have expressed different opinions on this issue or not. If they have not expressed opinions, or only one opinion exists, then there is no condition that the later mujtahid's opinion must agree with earlier ones, because no opinion previously passed or only one opinion exists, and the later mujtahid may oppose it. If earlier mujtahids have expressed opinions, then there are two possibilities: either all earlier mujtahids agree on one opinion or they differ. If all opinions agree, then the earlier mujtahids have formed an ijma; if the later mujtahid adopts a new opinion, it will necessarily oppose ijma, so his new opinion is invalid and his qiyas is void. If the earlier mujtahids differ, then also it is not permissible for the later mujtahid to adopt a new qiyas and new opinion, because when the earlier mujtahids deliberated and expressed their opinions, they effectively formed an ijma that only these opinions are possible and no other opinion can be; this is called ijma murakkab. Therefore, adopting a new qiyas would oppose ijma murakkab. For this reason, it is made a condition for the later mujtahid that his qiyas must reach one of the opinions of the earlier mujtahids, otherwise it would oppose the ijma of mujtahids. These conditions the author has mentioned in his book. There are additional conditions: e.g., if the proof is from the Book of Allah, a condition is that it is known in meaning; if unknown in meaning like mutashabihat, no ruling is established by it. If the proof is from the Sunnah and is khabar wahid, a condition is that its narrator is known for narration. If the proof is ijma, it is established only when all people of ijma agree. If the proof is qiyas, its specific condition has already been stated. In any case, the proof establishes the ruling only when all these conditions are present. Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Tawdih explains the conditions that must be present for a legal proof (dalil) to establish a ruling. The author first states the general principle that a proof only yields a ruling when its conditions are met. He then lists four conditions explicitly mentioned in the matn: (1) the proof must not be abrogated (mansukh); an example is the temporary permission of donkey meat later abrogated by the prohibition at Khaybar. (2) There must be no equal contradictory evidence. (3) There must be no stronger contradictory evidence. (4) A specific condition for qiyas by a later mujtahid: his qiyas must agree with at least one earlier mujtahid's opinion. The commentary clarifies that this is not a requirement of blind taqlid but a safeguard against violating ijma. If earlier mujtahids have not expressed an opinion, the later mujtahid is free. If they have, and it is unanimous (ijma), a new opinion is invalid. If they differ (ijma murakkab), a new opinion is also invalid because the earlier scholars implicitly agreed that the valid opinions are limited to those they expressed. Commentary
This passage transitions from discussing the universal proposition (qadiyya kulliyya) with respect to its proof (dalil) to discussing it with respect to the ruling (hukm). The commentator explains that for a universal proposition to serve as a valid proof, one must know the types of rulings (obligation, recommendation, prohibition) and which type of ruling can be derived from which type of proof. For example, obligation (wujub) requires a definitive proof (dalil qat'i), not a conjectural one. Similarly, rulings involving punishment (like hudud) cannot be established through analogy (qiyas). The commentator then adds two more perspectives: mahkum bih (the act of the mukallaf) and mahkum alayh (the mukallaf himself). The type of act influences what proof is acceptable; for instance, acts of punishment require definitive proofs. The condition of the mukallaf (e.g., adulthood, sanity) also affects the ruling. Thus, the universal proposition is valid as a proof only when all these aspects are properly aligned.

Key Terms

حکم (hukm): Legal ruling, classified into obligation, recommendation, prohibition, etc.
محکوم بہ (mahkum bih): The act of the mukallaf that is the subject of the ruling
محکوم علیہ (mahkum alayh): The mukallaf to whom the ruling applies
دلیل قطعی (dalil qat'i): A definitive proof that yields certainty, such as mutawatir or Quran
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning; a type of conjectural proof

Conditions for a Universal Proposition as Proof
Explaining necessary conditions for a universal proposition to serve as a legal proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four categories of conditions that a universal proposition must satisfy in Hanafi usul and apply them to assess the validity of a qiyas-based argument.

Core Idea
A universal proposition is proof only when conditions of hukm, mahkum bih, mahkum 'alayh, and dalil are met.

This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary explains that universal legal maxims cannot be applied blindly. To derive rulings correctly, a mujtahid must understand the types of rulings, the nature of actions, the status of the legal subject, and the types of proofs. The author also demonstrates the logical form such a proof takes in the first figure of syllogism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories of conditions
Recognize that the passage divides conditions into those related to the ruling (hukm), the act (mahkum bih), the subject (mahkum 'alayh), and the proof (dalil).
These categories are essential for any universal proposition to function as a valid dalil.
2
Analyze the conditions of hukm
List the required knowledge: types of rulings (wajib, mandub, haram, etc.) and which types of proofs can establish each. For instance, obligation requires a definitive proof.

3
Analyze the conditions of mahkum bih
Identify the action types (e.g., worship, punishment, transaction) and note the evidentiary restrictions: punishments cannot be proven by qiyas.

4
Analyze the conditions of mahkum 'alayh
Consider the subject's majority, sanity, and accidentals (travel, sickness, menstruation) that affect the ruling's applicability.
The ruling changes with the subject's state.
5

Study how the universal proposition becomes the major premise in a first-figure syllogism, with the minor premise specifying the attributes of the case.
The conclusion follows only when all conditions in the minor premise are satisfied.

Refining the Definition of Hukm and Resolving Objections
Refining the definition of hukm and addressing objections about its derivation from sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two meanings of hukm, apply the graded sense of ithbat, and resolve the objections about qiyas and combination of literal and metaphorical meanings.

Core Idea
Hukm is either eternal divine discourse or temporal ruling; establishment from sources is about our knowledge, graded by certainty.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text examines a key statement: that rulings are established from the four sources. Two objections arise: if hukm means khitab Allah (eternal discourse), how can it be 'established' (thubut implies temporality)? If it means the resulting obligation, qiyas does not establish but only manifests. The commentator resolves these by reinterpreting 'thubut' as relative to our knowledge and 'ithbat' as graded. A further objection about combining literal and metaphorical meanings is deflected by positing a general meaning of perception (idrak). This lesson will guide you through these nuanced resolutions.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two meanings of hukm
Identify the two possible meanings: (1) khitab Allah (divine discourse), which is eternal, and (2) ma thabata bi al-khitab (the resulting ruling, e.g., obligation, prohibition), which is temporal. Note which one is problematic for 'establishment from sources'.
2
Analyze the first objection and its resolution
If hukm means khitab Allah, saying it is 'established' (thabit) from sources is problematic because thubut implies temporal origination (hadath). The resolution: thubut here is bi i'tibar 'ilmina (according to our knowledge), not ontological existence. The hukm is eternal in reality but known to us through the sources.
3
Analyze the second objection and its resolution
If hukm means ma thabata bi al-khitab, the objection is that qiyas does not establish the ruling; it only manifests it. The resolution: ithbat (establishment) is used in a graded sense—for Book, Sunnah, and Ijma it means certain knowledge (ilm), for qiyas it means preponderance of opinion (ghalabat al-zann). Thus, qiyas 'establishes' in a weaker sense.
4
Address the jam' bayn al-haqiqah wa al-majaz objection
The objection: by using ithbat in both a literal (certainty) and metaphorical (zann) sense for different sources, one commits the forbidden combination. The resolution: the intended meaning of ithbat is a single general concept—perception (idrak)—which includes both certain and conjectural knowledge. This is a general metaphor ('umum al-majaz), not a combination.
5
Synthesize the usul principles from this analysis
From the passage, extract two principles: (1) qiyas is muzhir (manifestor) not muthbit (establisher) in the sense of certainty, and (2) usul terminology must be precise about the level of proof (qat'i vs. zanni). These principles guide the Hanafi approach to deriving rulings.

إثبات (ithbat): Ithbat is used here in a graded sense: for Book, Sunnah, and Consensus it means certain knowledge; for Qiyas it means preponderance of opinion (ghalabat al-zann).
جمع بين الحقيقة والمجاز (jam' bayn al-haqiqah wa al-majaz): The forbidden practice of using a single word in both its literal and metaphorical meanings simultaneously. Here, the commentator argues it is avoided by taking a general meaning (idrak) that includes both.

The Critique of the Division of Haythiyya into Juz' and Bayan
Critical analysis of the division of haythiyya in subject matter definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the dispute over the classification of haythiyya in defining subject matters of sciences and evaluate the Hanafi response to Taftazani's criticism.

Core Idea
The Hanafi position holds that the second type of haythiyya is an expression, not a restriction, and that shared subjects across sciences are permissible.

In defining the subject matter of a science, the aspect (haythiyya) under which it is studied plays a crucial role. This lesson examines a debate between al-Musannif (Sadr al-Shari'ah) and al-Taftazani on whether that aspect is a part, expression, or restriction. The author of the hāshiya defends the original Hanafi stance, arguing that Taftazani's objections are based on wordplay and sectarian bias.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of haythiyya
Al-Musannif divides haythiyya into a first type which is a part (juz') of the subject, and a second type which is an expression (bayan) for the subject. Note how these relate to the examples given: the human body in medicine (aspect: health/sickness) and the body in physics (aspect: motion/rest).
The first type is inherent to the subject; the second type is external but explanatory.
2
Understand Taftazani's objection
Al-Taftazani rejects both classifications. He argues that the first type is a restriction (qayd) for the subject, not a part, and the second type is also a restriction, not an expression.
Taftazani's aim is to avoid problems he sees in al-Musannif's scheme.
3
Examine Taftazani's first proof
If the second type were a part, discussing it in the science would mean discussing parts of the subject, which is impermissible in a science (since sciences discuss accidents, not parts). But if it is a restriction, discussing it is permissible because restrictions can be discussed.
This proof tries to show that the second type cannot be a part, but Taftazani uses it to argue that it must be a qayd.
4
Examine Taftazani's second proof
If the second type is an expression (bayan), then the unqualified subject (e.g., 'word') would be the same for both morphology and syntax, leading to two sciences sharing a subject by essence and consideration, which is generally impermissible. But if it is a restriction, each science has a differently restricted subject, so they differ even if only by consideration.
This proof targets the bayan classification.
5
Evaluate the hāshiya author's rebuttal
The hāshiya author argues that 'part' and 'restriction' are functionally identical—both specify the subject—so Taftazani's first proof is based on wordplay. Moreover, al-Musannif actually held that two sciences can share a subject by essence and consideration, so the second proof is also invalid.
This rebuttal defends the original Hanafi position and accuses Taftazani of sectarian bias.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hāshiya) within the commentary on Al-Tawdih wa Al-Talwih. It addresses a methodological dispute regarding the concept of 'haythiyya' (aspect/consideration) in defining the subject matter (mawdu') of a science. The author (al-musannif, likely Sadr al-Shari'ah) divided haythiyya into two types: the first is an integral part (juz') of the subject, and the second is an explanatory expression (bayan) for the subject. The commentator (al-Taftazani) criticizes this division, arguing that both types should be considered restrictions (qayd) on the subject, not a part or an expression. Taftazani provides two rational proofs: first, if the second type were a part, discussing it in the science would be discussing a part of the subject, which is impermissible; but if it is a restriction, discussing it is permissible. Second, if the second type is an expression outside the subject, then the same unqualified subject could be shared by two sciences (e.g., 'word' in both morphology and syntax), violating the principle that two sciences cannot share a subject identically; but if it is a restriction, each science has a distinct restricted subject, even if the difference is only by consideration (i'tibari). The author of this hāshiya (Misku al-Malih) then defends the original author, arguing that Taftazani's objection fails because there is no real difference between a 'part' and a 'restriction'—both serve to specify the subject. He further claims that the author actually held that two sciences can share a subject by essence and consideration, so Taftazani's objection based on sharing is invalid. This passage exemplifies the nuanced debates in Hanafi usul al-fiqh regarding the logical structure of sciences and the precise role of considerations in differentiating subject matters.

Key Terms
الحیثیة (al-haythiyya): The aspect or consideration under which a subject is investigated in a science; it is either a part (juz') of the subject, an expression (bayan), or a restriction (qayd).
جزء (juz'): An integral part of the subject matter; if the haythiyya is a juz', it is inseparable from the subject.
بیان (bayan): An expression or explanation that clarifies the subject but is not itself part of the subject.
قید (qayd): A restriction or qualification attached to the subject, modifying it without being a part of its essence.

This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn, explaining the definition of the Qur'an. The commentator clarifies that the term 'Qur'an' can refer to either the eternal divine attribute (kalam nafsi) or the created verbal expression (kalam lafzi). In usul al-fiqh, scholars are primarily concerned with the verbal speech because legal rulings (ahkam) are derived from the recited words, not from the unspoken attribute. The definition given in the matn is diagnostic (tashkhis), not essential (hadd); it aims to identify the verbal Qur'an and distinguish it from the eternal attribute. The commentator anticipates an objection: why include multiple qualifiers like 'between the two covers' when 'transmitted' already suffices? He explains that a proper definition must not only distinguish but also be co-extensive (musawi) with the defined. The qualifier 'transmitted' alone would exclude some instances (like the verbal Qur'an in the Prophet's heart before recitation), necessitating additional restrictions. Thus the definition is precise and complete.

The Qur'an Cannot Be Defined Because It Is a Personal Entity
Proving the Qur'an is not definable by a logical syllogism.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the syllogistic argument that the Qur'an is a personal entity and therefore not definable, and distinguish the valid from invalid interpretation of 'shakhsi'.

Core Idea
The Qur'an's unique composition makes it a personal entity; personal entities are identified, not defined.

In Usul al-Fiqh, understanding the nature of the Qur'an is foundational. This passage tackles the question: can the Qur'an be defined? The author argues it cannot, because it is a personal entity (shakhsi) that does not admit multiplicity in its essence. We will walk through the syllogism, examine two interpretations of 'shakhsi', and see why the second one is valid despite an apparent objection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and the syllogism
State the author's claim: the Qur'an cannot be defined. Recognize that he proves it using a syllogism: minor premise (the Qur'an is shakhsi) and major premise (every shakhsi cannot be defined).
The syllogism is a dalil aqli (rational proof).
2
Analyze the major premise
Examine the proof for 'every shakhsi cannot be defined'. Definition gives knowledge through parts and is predicated; but a personal entity is known through pointing, not through universal concepts.
The term 'hadd' here means a definition that captures the essence.
3
Examine the minor premise and two interpretations
Identify the two interpretations of 'the Qur'an is shakhsi'. First (invalid): Qur'an is only the words on Jibril's tongue. Second (valid): Qur'an is the specific composition of words unique to it.
The first interpretation leads to denying the authenticity of our recited Qur'an.
4
Address the objection
Understand the objection: if shakhsi means unique composition, then multiple recitations imply multiple Qur'ans. The author's response (implied) is that the personal entity is in terms of the essence, not the accidents of recitation.
The passage ends mid-sentence, but the answer is that the Qur'an is one in essence.

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary on the matn, now addressing the question of whether the Qur'an can be defined (hadd). The author argues that it cannot, using a syllogism: (1) The Qur'an is a personal entity (shakhsi). (2) Every personal entity cannot be defined. Therefore, the Qur'an cannot be defined. The proof for the major premise is that definition gives knowledge through parts, but a personal entity is known through pointing, not through universal concepts. The proof for the minor premise: the Qur'an is a unique, specific entity. Two interpretations of 'personal' are discussed. The first, that it is only the words on Jibril's tongue, is rejected because it would deny that our recited Qur'an is the actual Qur'an. The second, that it is the specific composition of words unique to the Qur'an and not replicated in any other book, is adopted. Even though this composition is recited by many people, it remains one personal entity because its essence is not multiplied. An objection is raised: if 'personal' means unique composition, then each recitation is a different instance, so the Qur'an would be multiple, not singular.
