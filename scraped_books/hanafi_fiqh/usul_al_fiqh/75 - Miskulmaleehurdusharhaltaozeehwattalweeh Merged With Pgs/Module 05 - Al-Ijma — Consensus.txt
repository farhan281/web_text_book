Why Ijma is a Perfect Principle: Three Responses to the Objection
Objection and three Hanafi responses about ijma's independence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three Hanafi responses to the objection that ijma requires a proof and explain why ijma is considered an asl kamil while qiyas is not.

Core Idea
Ijma strengthens weak rulings to certainty, making it an independent source; qiyas weakens certain rulings, making it dependent.

In Hanafi usul, ijma (consensus) is regarded as a perfect principle (asl kamil) alongside the Quran and Sunnah. However, a hypothetical objection claims that if ijma needs a proof (sanad) for its own existence, then it is no better than qiyas. This lesson unpacks the three sophisticated Hanafi responses to that objection, each reinforcing the unique authority of ijma.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objector's Claim
The objector argues that ijma, like qiyas, depends on evidence (sanad) to establish its ruling, so it cannot be a complete (kamil) principle.
This claim is the target of the three responses.
2
First Response: Denial of Necessity
The first answer simply rejects the premise: ijma does not necessarily need a proof because Allah may directly inspire consensus.
This is the 'non-acceptance' (adam al-taslim) argument.
3
Second Response: Two Levels of Ijma
Even if a proof exists, ijma functions as an independent proof (asil) in its indicatory capacity; one does not need to cite the original proof when using ijma, unlike qiyas which constantly relies on three principles.
Ijma's existence may depend on a proof, but its authority as a proof does not.
4
Third Response: Strengthening vs. Weakening
Ijma elevates a weak (zanni) ruling to certainty (qat'i), thereby adding value; qiyas does the opposite, turning a certain original ruling into a speculative branch ruling.
This asymmetry proves ijma's completeness and qiyas's imperfection.
5
Conclusion: Ijma as Asl Kamil
Thus, despite any reliance on an originating proof, ijma's ability to strengthen rulings and its self-sufficient indicatory power make it a perfect principle.
The three answers collectively defend the hanafi position.

Objection on the Condition 'ala Wajh al-Tahqiq'
Objection that the condition is redundant and the response defending it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the objection that the condition 'ala wajh al-tahqiq' is redundant and articulate the Hanafi response that clarifies why it is necessary to exclude ilm al-khilaf from usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
The condition 'ala wajh al-tahqiq' is needed because ilm al-khilaf appears to lead to fiqh but does so only indirectly.

In defining usul al-fiqh, scholars included the phrase 'directly leading to fiqh' (tawassul qarib ila al-fiqh). An objection arose that the additional condition 'ala wajh al-tahqiq' (in a verifiable manner) is redundant. This lesson examines the objection and the response, which shows that the condition removes an apparent ambiguity caused by the science of debate (ilm al-khilaf).

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
State clearly what the objector is claiming about the condition 'ala wajh al-tahqiq'.
The objector says the condition is useless because its purpose is already achieved by 'directly leading to fiqh'.
2
Understand the reasoning behind the redundancy claim
Why does the objector think ilm al-khilaf is already excluded? Because ilm al-khilaf does not directly lead to fiqh; it protects or opposes rulings.
Ilm al-khilaf relates equally to fiqh and non-fiqh.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The commentator argues that ilm al-khilaf often discusses fiqh issues and applies its principles to fiqh, so it appears to lead to fiqh. Hence, the condition is needed to clarify that only direct leading counts.
The response uses the phrase 'apparently' to highlight the ambiguity.
4
Draw the conclusion
The condition is not redundant; it resolves an apparent ambiguity and precisely delineates the scope of usul al-fiqh.
This conclusion is essential for correctly distinguishing usul al-fiqh from ilm al-jadal.

Commentary
This passage presents an objection and its response regarding the inclusion of the phrase 'ala wajh al-tahqiq' in the definition of usul al-fiqh. The Condition for Qiyas: Alignment with Earlier Mujtahids
Clarifying the condition for qiyas alignment and composite consensus.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi condition that qiyas must align with an earlier mujtahid's opinion and identify when a later mujtahid may differ without violating composite consensus.

Core Idea
Qiyas must lead to the same ruling as earlier mujtahids; but if no consensus exists, a later mujtahid may differ.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, qiyas is a valid proof only if it does not contradict a pre-existing ruling from earlier mujtahids. This lesson clarifies the condition that the qiyas must lead to an opinion that an earlier mujtahid has already reached. The Subject Matter of Usul al-Fiqh: Proofs and Rulings
Dispute on whether subject is only adillah or includes ahkam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate the disagreement on whether the subject matter of usul al-fiqh includes only the four proofs or both proofs and rulings, and explain the Hanafi position.

Core Idea
Usul al-fiqh studies both the proofs and the rulings derived from them.

Usul al-fiqh is the discipline that provides the foundations for deriving Islamic legal rulings. A fundamental question arises: what exactly does this discipline study? Does it only examine the four proofs (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas), or does it also include the rulings (ahkam) themselves? This passage presents a key dispute between Imam al-Amidi (Shafi'i) and the majority of scholars, including the Hanafis. Understanding this dispute clarifies the scope of usul al-fiqh and its relationship to fiqh.

What is al-Amidi's argument for limiting the subject to only the four proofs? He argues that rulings are only conceptualized to establish or negate them by evidence, but the actual verification and universal propositions belong to usul, not the rulings themselves.

The Hanafi school, by including rulings, emphasizes the practical link between evidence and law.

Commentary
This passage presents a dispute about the subject matter (mawdu') of usul al-fiqh. The commentator Taftazani, in his expansion of the author Sadr al-Shariah's words, explains two positions. The first, held by Imam al-Amidi (a Shafi'i scholar), restricts the subject to only the four proofs (adillah arba'ah: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas). According to this view, usul al-fiqh does not discuss the rulings (ahkam) themselves; only a minimal conceptualization of rulings is necessary to establish or negate them by evidence. The second, which is the position of the majority (jumhur) and preferred by the author and commentator, holds that both the proofs and the rulings constitute the subject. The commentator argues for this by noting that the discussions in usul al-fiqh naturally cover both proofs and rulings: some masail relate to the proofs (e.g., how they serve as evidence) and others to the rulings (e.g., their types and conditions). Therefore, both must be included. However, he acknowledges that the proofs are more numerous and important, but that does not exclude rulings from being part of the subject. The ruling is that usul al-fiqh encompasses both adillah and ahkam as its subject matter.
