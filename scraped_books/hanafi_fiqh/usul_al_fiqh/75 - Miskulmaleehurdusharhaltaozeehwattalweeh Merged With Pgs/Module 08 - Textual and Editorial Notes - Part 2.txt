Defining Compound Terms: The Case of Usul al-Fiqh
Commentary on the matn's statement about the definition of the compound term

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that defining an idafi compound requires defining its parts unless the parts are clear, and evaluate the order of definitions based on the compound's structure.

Core Idea
A compound term's definition hinges on its parts, but only if those parts are not already self-evident.

In this lesson, we explore the structure of the compound term 'Usul al-Fiqh' and what it takes to define it. The commentator explains why we must define both 'usul' and 'fiqh', and why the idafa itself is left undefined. Along the way, we encounter subtle principles about when definitions are needed and the order of presentation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
The definition of a compound term depends on the definition of its parts, but only if the parts are not already clear. This is based on the principle that knowledge of the whole depends on knowledge of its parts.
If the parts are manifest, no definition is needed.
2
Apply to Usul al-Fiqh
Both 'usul' (mudaf) and 'fiqh' (mudaf ilayh) need definition because they are not entirely self-evident in the context of the compound.
The compound is idafi, so the idafa relation is also relevant.
3
Examine the order of definitions
Although the mudaf ilayh typically takes precedence when both are unknown, here the mudaf is defined first. Three reasons are given: brevity of discussion, following the order of the term, and the type of definition needed (essence vs. idafa).
This is a legitimate departure from the default rule for a specific benefit.
4
Consider the omission of idafa definition
Idafa is considered a formal part of the compound, but it is not defined because its meaning (specification) is self-evident to every speaker.
The commentator uses 'bimanzilat' (like) to indicate it's not a word but a relation.
5
Understand 'ma fi ma'anahu al-mushtaqq'
This refers to words that are not derivative in form but are derivative in meaning, such as 'asl' in 'asl al-mas'alah', which means 'proof' (a derivative concept).
This helps understand idafa in such cases.

He said: 'As for its definition in terms of idafa, it requires defining the mudaf which is al-usul...' The purpose of the commentator of al-Tawdih is that the author said that the definition of the compound (idafi) needs the definition of the mudaf and mudaf ilayh. The commentator is explaining the proof. The summary of the proof is that Usul al-Fiqh is an idafi compound, and the rule is that the definition of the compound depends on and is contingent upon the definition of its compositional parts and individual elements that are neither manifest nor clear, because the compound is a whole and its compositional parts have the status of parts, and knowledge of the whole is contingent upon knowledge of the parts. On this basis, the definition of the idafi compound needs the definition of the mudaf (al-usul) and the definition of the mudaf ilayh (al-fiqh). Question: Why did the commentator say 'individual elements that are neither clear'? Answer: Because if the individual elements and compositional parts are manifest and clear, then the definition of the compound is not contingent upon them, nor is there a need to define those individual elements. Question: Usul al-Fiqh is an idafi compound, and the rule is that knowledge of the mudaf as mudaf is contingent upon the mudaf ilayh, so the definition of the mudaf ilayh (al-fiqh) should precede the definition of the mudaf, as some scholars defined al-fiqh first and al-usul later. But the author, Sadr al-Shari'ah and Ibn al-Hajib, mentioned the definition of the mudaf first. What is the reason and point? Answer (1): The precedence of the definition of the mudaf ilayh over the definition of the mudaf is necessary and obligatory only when both the mudaf and mudaf ilayh are absolutely unknown. Where the definition of the mudaf and mudaf ilayh is known in some respect, as in Usul al-Fiqh, then precedence of the definition of the mudaf ilayh is not obligatory but preferable. And abandoning what is preferable for some benefit or point is permissible. Here the point is that the discussion in al-usul is brief and in al-fiqh is lengthy, so the definition of the mudaf was given first to finish quickly and then return to the definition of al-fiqh. Answer (2): The definition of the mudaf was given first because it is mentioned first in the term. Answer (3): If both the mudaf and mudaf ilayh need definition in terms of the idafa, then precedence of the mudaf ilayh is preferable. If both need definition in terms of essence (dhat), then precedence of the mudaf is preferable. Since here the second case applies, the definition of the mudaf was given first. And it also needs a definition of the idafa itself. The purpose of commentator Taftazani is to answer a question: It is argued that just as the definition of the idafi compound needs the definition of the mudaf and mudaf ilayh, it also needs the definition of the idafa itself, because the idafa is like a formal part of the idafi compound. So the author should have also defined the idafa. Why did he not do so? The answer: They did not address it. Commentator Taftazani gives the answer: It is accepted that the idafa is a formal part and the definition of the idafi compound depends on it, but the author and other authors do not address the definition of idafa because it is obvious. Every person knows well that the meaning of idafa of a derivative or something in the meaning of a derivative to something is that the mudaf is specific to the mudaf ilayh in terms of its meaning and concept. For example, in 'ghulam Zaid', the ghulam is specific to Zaid in terms of the concept of being a slave, not in terms of essence. Or as the commentator gave the example: 'dalil al-mas'alah' – its meaning is something that is specific to the issue in terms of being a proof. Similarly, another example: 'asl al-fiqh' – here 'asl' is mudaf to 'fiqh', and its meaning will be: the thing that is specific to fiqh in terms of being a foundation, basis, and source. Because in idafa, the meaning of the mudaf is considered, and here the meaning of 'asl' is this, i.e., basis and source. The lexical meaning of 'asl' is 'that upon which something is built' (ma sabqani 'alayhi amri min haythu huwa 'alayhi). Question: Commentator Taftazani said about idafa: 'because it is like a formal part'. A question arises: why did the commentator add 'like'? Why did he not say 'it is a formal part'? What is the point? Answer (1): There is no point, and idafa is a formal part, not like; but the habit of authors is that they sometimes add the word 'like' even when referring to the thing itself, especially where there is some subtlety. Answer (2): He said 'like' because our discussion is about the parts of the compound and its individual elements, which are from the perspective of words, and since idafa is not a word, it is not exactly a formal part but is like a formal part. Question: What is meant by 'wa ma fi ma'anahu' (and that which is in its meaning)? Answer: There are multiple opinions: (1) It refers to a word that is not a derivative in form but is derivative in meaning and can be interpreted as another derivative, which may be called 'derivative by judgment' (mushtaqq hukmi). For example, in 'asl al-mas'alah', 'asl' is not a derivative but is in the meaning of a derivative, because 'asl' means 'what is taken as proof or foundation', and these are derivative. Similarly, 'ghulam Zaid' – 'ghulam' is not a derivative but is in the meaning of a derivative, because 'ghulam Zaid' means 'the slave of Zaid'. (2) Some say it refers to a noun that is dependent on another, like 'ilm', but this is weak. Question: What is the point of mentioning the word 'concept' (mafhum) in the definition of idafa: 'bi i'tibari mafhum al-mudaf'? Only 'bi i'tibari al-mudaf' would suffice?

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the matn statement about the definition of the compound term (idafi) such as Usul al-Fiqh. The commentator first establishes the rule: knowledge of a whole depends on knowledge of its parts. Therefore, to define the compound 'Usul al-Fiqh', one must define its two parts: 'al-usul' (the mudaf) and 'al-fiqh' (the mudaf ilayh). However, this only applies if the parts are not already clear; if they are clear, no definition is needed. Then a question arises about the order of definitions: since the mudaf is known relative to the mudaf ilayh, it would seem logical to define the mudaf ilayh first. Yet the author defined 'al-usul' first. The commentator provides three reasons: (1) the absolute priority of defining the mudaf ilayh only applies when both are completely unknown; here they are partially known, so it is only preferable, and it can be abandoned for a benefit. The benefit here is that the discussion of usul is brief, so defining it first allows quick transition to the longer discussion of fiqh. (2) The order follows the order of the term itself. (3) The principle of whether the definition is in terms of idafa or essence: here both need definition in essence, so defining the mudaf first is preferable. Next, the commentary addresses why the definition of idafa itself is not given, even though it is a formal part of the compound. The answer is that the meaning of idafa is self-evident (badihi): it denotes specification of the mudaf to the mudaf ilayh in meaning. Examples like 'ghulam Zaid' and 'dalil al-mas'alah' illustrate this. Then a subtle point is raised: why Taftazani said idafa is 'like' a formal part (bimanzilat) instead of being an actual part. Two answers: either it is a mere habit of authors to use 'like' for emphasis, or because idafa is not a word but a relational concept, so it is not exactly a formal part but akin to one. Finally, the passage discusses the term 'wa ma fi ma'anahu' (that which is in its meaning), explaining it refers to nouns that are not derivative in form but are derivative in meaning, like 'asl' in 'asl al-mas'alah' (meaning 'proof' or 'foundation') and 'ghulam' in 'ghulam Zaid' (meaning 'slave'). This concludes the commentary's exploration of the definition of the compound term.

Key Terms
مرکب اضافی (murakkab idafi): A compound term consisting of mudaf and mudaf ilayh in a construct state (idafa).
اضافت (idafa): The relation of specification between mudaf and mudaf ilayh, considered like a formal part of the compound.
ما فی معناہ المشتق (ma fi ma'anahu al-mushtaqq): A word that is not formally a derivative but is in the meaning of a derivative.

Why the Definition of Idafah Was Omitted: Precision in Hanafi Usul
Question-answer about omitting idafah's definition and related precision issues

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafi usul authors omit definitions of self-evident terms and analyze the precise wording used in definitions like 'bi-manzilati' and 'wa ma fi ma'nahu'.

Core Idea
Definitions of self-evident terms are omitted; precision in wording prevents misunderstanding of non-literal components.

This passage addresses a subtle but important question in Hanafi usul: why do authors sometimes leave obvious terms undefined? Through a series of questions and answers, the text reveals that idafah (the construct state) is considered badihi (self-evident), but even then, commentators like Taftazani use precise phrasing (e.g., 'bi-manzilati') to avoid confusion when a term is not a literal part. The discussion also clarifies the meaning of 'wa ma fi ma'nahu' and the need to specify 'concept' in the definition—all highlighting the Hanafi commitment to linguistic and logical exactness.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central question
Recognize that the passage begins with a question: Why did the author define mudaf and mudaf ilayh but not idafah itself?
The question implies that if the compound needs definition, its components (including the relationship) should also be defined.
2
Understand the answer: badihi
The answer given is that idafah is self-evident (badihi) – everyone understands that in an idafah construction, the mudaf is specific to the mudaf ilayh in terms of meaning. Therefore, a separate definition is unnecessary.
'Badihi' is a key term of art meaning self-evidently known.
3
Analyze the use of 'bi-manzilati'
A subsidiary question asks why Taftazani said 'like a formal part' instead of 'a formal part'. Two reasons are given: (1) authors sometimes use 'like' even for exact things to add emphasis; (2) idafah is not a word (it is a relationship), so it is not literally a part of the compound but only 'like' a part.
This shows Hanafi attentiveness to ontological categories—literal vs. metaphorical.
4
Examine 'wa ma fi ma'nahu'
The passage explains that this phrase refers to words that are not derived in form but carry a derived meaning, e.g., 'asl' (foundation) implicitly meaning 'that which is foundational'. A second opinion that it refers to nouns subsisting through another is deemed weak.
This distinction is crucial for correctly interpreting derived terms in usul.
5
Explore the specification of 'concept'
Finally, the text clarifies why the definition of idafah includes 'in terms of the concept of the mudaf'. This is to exclude specificity in essence (e.g., a physical relationship) and limit it to conceptual specificity (e.g., ghulam being specific to Zayd in terms of slavery meaning).
This prevents overextension of the definition to non-linguistic relationships.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion probing the definition of idafah (the construct state) and related terms. The author is answering several anticipated objections. First, why the author of the matn defined the mudaf and mudaf ilayh but not idafah itself. The answer is that idafah is badihi (self-evident) — everyone understands that in an idafah construction, the mudaf is specific to the mudaf ilayh in terms of meaning, not in essence. Then a subsidiary question: why did Taftazani say 'bi-manzilati al-juz' al-suri' (like a formal part) instead of saying it is a formal part? Two answers: sometimes authors use 'like' for emphasis even when it is the thing itself; more importantly, since idafah is not a word (it is a grammatical relationship), it is not literally a part of the compound, so 'like' is more precise. Next, what is meant by 'wa ma fi ma'nahu'? The first opinion: words that are not derived in form but are in the meaning of derived words (e.g., 'asl' meaning 'foundation'). The second opinion (weak): it refers to nouns that subsist through another. Finally, why mention 'concept of the mudaf' instead of just 'mudaf'? Because specificity can be in essence or in concept/attribute; the definition intends the latter. This shows the Hanafi concern for precise linguistic classification in usul al-fiqh.

Key Terms
اضافت (idafah): The relationship where the mudaf (first term) is specific to the mudaf ilayh (second term) in terms of meaning/concept, not essence.
و ما فی معناه (wa ma fi ma'nahu): Words not formally derived but carrying a derived meaning, e.g., 'asl' (origin) which means 'foundation' (a derived concept).
بمنزلہ (bi-manzilati): Used to indicate something is like but not exactly a part, used where there is subtle obscurity.

How is a Ruling Proven by Evidences? The Objection and Hanafi Resolution
Resolving an objection about the meaning of 'hukm' and its proof by evidences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two possible meanings of 'hukm' and explain how the Hanafi school resolves the objection regarding qiyas not proving rulings with certainty.

Core Idea
The ruling is proven relative to our knowledge: certain from text and consensus, probable from qiyas.

This passage addresses an objection about the statement that rulings are proven by the four evidences. The objector argues that if 'hukm' means the eternal divine address, it cannot be proven by temporal evidences; if it means the derived legal value, then qiyas does not prove it but only reveals it. The commentator resolves this by interpreting 'proof' in terms of our knowledge: for certain sources it gives certainty, for qiyas it gives dominant opinion. However, this leads to another issue of combining literal and metaphorical meanings of 'ithbat'. This lesson will walk through this nuanced discussion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings of hukm
Distinguish between the eternal divine address (khitāb Allāh) and the derived legal value (mā thabata bil-khiṭāb) like obligation or prohibition.
The passage allows both interpretations but shows objections to each.
2
State the objection for each meaning
For khitāb Allāh: it is eternal, so cannot be 'proven' by temporal evidences. For mā thabata bil-khiṭāb: qiyas does not prove it; it only reveals it.
The objector uses the Hanafi principle that qiyas is muẓhir, not muthbit.
3
Apply the Hanafi response
The author replies that 'thubūt' (proof) is relative to our knowledge: from certain sources we gain certain knowledge, from qiyas we gain dominant opinion (ghalabat al-ẓann). Thus both meanings work if understood epistemologically.
This resolves the first objection but raises a second.
4
Recognize the second objection (jam' bayn al-ḥaqīqah wa al-majāz)
Using the same word 'ithbāt' for both certain knowledge (literal) and dominant opinion (metaphorical) when attributed to different evidences is a case of combining literal and metaphorical meaning, which is debated in uṣūl.
The text raises this objection but does not resolve it, leaving it as a known issue.
5
Understand the unresolved nature
Some Hanafis allow jam' bayn al-ḥaqīqah wa al-majāz in cases of necessity; others prohibit it. This passage acknowledges the problem without choosing sides.
This reflects the depth of uṣūl al-fiqh discussions.

His statement 'and it is the ruling, etc.' from the matn, from here answers an objection. The objection is: You said, 'and what is annexed to it is the discussion of what is proven by these evidences, and it is the ruling' — that the ruling is proven by the evidences. We ask: What meaning is intended by 'ruling'? Is it the address of Allah (khitāb Allāh) or what is established by the address (mā thabata bil-khiṭāb)? If you intend the first meaning, it is false because the ruling in the sense of the address of Allah is eternal, and saying that it is proven by evidences is wrong because proof necessitates temporality, while we have taken the eternal meaning which already exists and is proven. Therefore, this meaning cannot be intended. And if you say that by 'ruling' you mean what is established by the address, this is also false because by 'what is established' is meant obligation, prohibition, etc. So the meaning would be that obligation, recommendation, etc. are proven by evidences. However, not all evidences prove them. From three evidences — the Book, Sunnah, and consensus — obligation etc. are proven. But the fourth evidence, analogy (qiyās), does not prove the ruling (obligation, recommendation, etc.), rather it only reveals the ruling because the established principle has passed that analogy is a revealer (muẓhir), not a prover (muthbit) of the ruling. So it is wrong to say that all evidences prove the ruling; rather, some prove and some reveal. The author responds: Here both probabilities are correct. If 'ruling' means the address of Allah, it is also valid. The objection was that it is eternal while proof necessitates temporality. The answer is that by 'proof' (thubūt) is meant the establishment of the ruling from the evidences relative to our knowledge. The point is that in terms of our knowledge, the ruling is established from the evidence because we only know the ruling from the evidence, even if in itself and in reality that ruling is eternal and already established. And if 'ruling' means what is established by the address, this is also correct. The objection to this was that the proof of the ruling does not come from all evidences because analogy is not a prover of the ruling. Our response is that we do not take 'proof' (ithbāt) as absolute proof, but as dominant opinion (ghalabat al-ẓann). The meaning is that from the evidences, the ruling is proven, i.e., dominant opinion of the ruling is obtained. Then if it is attributed to the Book, Sunnah, and consensus, it means certain knowledge; if attributed to analogy, it means speculative knowledge. And if one raises an objection regarding that, the author responds to an objection: The objection is that you said that if 'ruling' means what is established by the address, then 'proof' means dominant opinion so that it becomes correct to say that all evidences provide proof of the ruling. By this interpretation, you have removed the aforementioned objection, but it raises another objection: that this necessitates combining the literal and metaphorical (jam' bayn al-ḥaqīqah wa al-majāz). Because 'proof' (ithbāt) is one word; its literal meaning is establishing certain knowledge, and its metaphorical meaning is establishing dominant opinion. You intend both literal and metaphorical meanings from the same word, because when proof is attributed to the Book, Sunnah, and consensus, its meaning is establishing certain knowledge; when attributed to analogy, it means establishing dominant opinion. Commentary
This passage is a commentary addressing an objection (ishkāl) regarding the statement from the matn that 'the ruling is proven by these evidences.' Reinterpreting Discussions: From Object to Means
Showing how to reinterpret object-focused discussions as means-focused discussions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the method of ta'wil (reinterpretation) to reframe discussions about the object of knowledge (muwasal ilayh) into discussions about the means of knowledge (muwasil) in usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
A discussion about the led-to can be turned into a discussion about what leads through creative reformulation.

In this passage, the commentator demonstrates that seemingly peripheral discussions about the object of knowledge (muwasal ilayh) can be reinterpreted and turned into central discussions about the means of knowledge (muwasil). This is achieved by reformulating propositions so that the subject shifts. This technique, known as ta'wil, is a powerful analytical tool in usul al-fiqh, allowing us to reframe debates about rulings into debates about evidence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the muwasil vs muwasal ilayh distinction
Identify that in logic and usul, 'muwasil' (what leads) is the means of acquiring knowledge (e.g., definition, proof), while 'muwasal ilayh' (the led-to) is the object of knowledge (e.g., the defined concept, the ruling).
In usul al-fiqh, the muwasil is the dalil (proof), and the muwasal ilayh is the hukm (ruling).
2
Identify the original proposition
Take a statement that seems to be about the muwasal ilayh, e.g., 'The conception is simple and does not accept definition.' This proposition describes a property of the conception (the led-to).
The original proposition in the passage is reconstructed: 'al-tasawwur basīt lā yaqbal al-ḥadd.'
3
Reformulate to shift the subject
Change the subject from the conception to the definition: 'The definition leads to the composite, not the simple.' Now the discussion is about what the definition does (muwasil), making composite and simple essential properties of the definition.
The new proposition is: 'al-ḥadd yūṣal ilā al-murakkab lā al-basīt.'
4
Apply the method to usul al-fiqh
Take a discussion about a ruling (e.g., 'Witr is wajib') and reformulate it as a discussion about the proof (e.g., 'The hadith of witr indicates wujub'). This shifts the focus from the object to the means.
This technique helps in structuring arguments and clarifying the subject of a discourse.

The Problem of Circularity in Defining 'Mushaf'
Refuting attempts to avoid circularity in defining Mushaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical problem of circularity (al-dawr) in defining 'Mushaf' as 'that which contains the Qur'an' and evaluate the proposed solutions using Hanafi usul principles.

Core Idea
Defining 'Mushaf' by 'Qur'an' is circular because the two are synonymous; alternative definitions fail as they ignore customary reality.

In this critical refinement from a late Hanafi commentary on al-Tawdih, the commentator engages with a subtle logical flaw: if we define 'Mushaf' as 'that which contains the Qur'an', and 'Qur'an' as 'that which is recited from the Mushaf', we have a vicious circle (al-dawr). Some scholars proposed alternative definitions to break the circle, such as 'that which contains written revelation' or 'that which contains sheets'. The commentator systematically refutes these, appealing to the established customary (urf) meaning of 'Mushaf' in the Shari'a. This lesson unpacks the first part of the passage, focusing on the core circularity and the initial attempts to resolve it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core circularity
Recognize that if Kitab and Qur'an are treated as synonyms, then defining Mushaf by either term creates reciprocal dependency: Mushaf depends on Qur'an, and Qur'an depends on Mushaf.
The passage states: 'The book and Qur'an are synonymous; both are two names for the same thing.'
2
Analyze the first attempted solution
Some define Mushaf as 'that which contains written revelation' (al-wahy) instead of 'the Qur'an'. Assess whether this breaks the circle.
The commentator says this is identical to using 'Qur'an' because 'revelation' and 'Qur'an' are synonymous.
3
Analyze the second attempted solution
Others define Mushaf as 'that which contains sheets' (sahaif) in a general sense, including non-Qur'anic scriptures. Consider if this avoids circularity.
This at least does not mention the Qur'an directly, but the commentator rejects it on methodological grounds.
4
Evaluate the commentator's rejection
The second solution departs from the apparent (zahir) meaning of Mushaf. Moreover, in Shari'a custom, 'Mushaf' has become a reality (haqiqah) referring specifically to the Qur'an; the proposed shift to the linguistic meaning is a move to metaphor (majaz), which is not commendable in definitions.
The commentator also notes the objection that defining Qur'an by Mushaf would include abrogated recitations.

Here he mentioned only the Qur'an, not the Book, although just as circularity arises in the Qur'an, it also arises in the Book. So by mentioning one, he indicated that both are one thing; mentioning the Qur'an is exactly mentioning the Book. Therefore one mention suffices for the other. Thus in these two expressions the synonymy between al-Kitab and al-Qur'an is established. If it is said: 'Mushaf is not...' - question and answer. The question is: Circularity does not arise in the definition of Qur'an because the definition of Mushaf is not 'that which contains the Qur'an' so that mentioning the definiendum in the definition occurs; rather the definition of Mushaf is 'that which contains the sheets', and by 'sheets' is meant absolute sheets, whether they be of the Holy Qur'an or of the Torah or the Gospel. In this case circularity does not arise because knowledge of the essence of the Qur'an depends on knowledge of the essence of the Mushaf, but knowledge of the essence of the Mushaf does not depend on the essence of the Qur'an. It was objected that then the definition of Qur'an would include abrogated recitations. They answer that abrogated recitation [will be] later excluded, so circularity ends. Note: defining Mushaf as 'that which contains sheets' is in terms of linguistic meaning, because in language the coinage of Mushaf is for this meaning. We said in reply: Defining Mushaf as 'that which contains sheets' is a departure from the apparent to the hidden, because the apparent and well-known meaning of 'Mushaf' is 'that which contains the Qur'an'. The question occurs: Circularity does not arise here because the usuli defines the Holy Qur'an in terms of its meaning and universal concept, which applies both to the totality as a whole and to each individual verse and part. Then in the definition of Mushaf, the 'Qur'an' mentioned does not refer to this meaning but to the specific collection, which applies only to the totality as a whole but not to each individual verse. And knowledge of the specific collection does not depend on the essence of the Mushaf because it is well-known among people; people memorize it, recite it, teach it, it is not ambiguous for them requiring definition. Therefore whenever a specific collection is actualized, it is actualized within the framework of the universal meaning and concept. So saying that the essence of the Mushaf depends on the specific collection but not on the universal concept is wrong, because the dependency of the essence of the Mushaf on the specific collection is exactly its dependency on the universal meaning. Thus circularity will arise: the essence of the Qur'an, in terms of the universal concept and meaning, depends on the essence of the Mushaf, and the essence of the Mushaf depends on the Qur'an as a specific collection, and then that specific collection depends on the same essence of the Qur'an in terms of the universal meaning. Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hashiya) on the definition of Mushaf and Qur'an, specifically addressing the problem of circularity (al-dawr) when defining Mushaf as 'that which contains the Qur'an'. The commentator engages with several objections and defenses, all within the Hanafi usul tradition. The main issue is that if we define Mushaf by referring to the Qur'an, and the Qur'an is itself defined by reference to Mushaf, we get a vicious circle. The commentator first notes that the author (Sadr al-Shariah in al-Tawdih) treats Kitab and Qur'an as synonymous, so any definition that uses one will be circular if the other is used reciprocally. Some scholars tried to avoid circularity by defining Mushaf as 'that which contains written revelation' rather than 'the Qur'an', but the commentator points out that since 'revelation' and 'Qur'an' are synonymous, the problem remains. Another attempted solution is to define Mushaf as 'that which contains sheets' (sahaif) in a general sense, including non-Qur'anic scriptures, so that knowledge of the Mushaf does not depend on knowledge of the Qur'an. The commentator rejects this because it departs from the customary (urf) meaning of Mushaf in the Shari'a, which has become the real (haqiqah) meaning; shifting to a metaphorical (majaz) meaning is not commendable in definitions. A more sophisticated objection distinguishes between the universal concept (ma'na kulli) of the Qur'an and the specific collection (majmu' shakhsi). The objector claims that the usul al-fiqh definition is of the universal meaning, which depends on the Mushaf, but the Mushaf's definition refers to the specific collection, not the universal, so there is no circularity. The commentator gives two responses: The first (ta'limi) argues that even the specific collection cannot be known without the universal meaning, because the universal is the genus for the specific, so circularity remains. The second (ta'limi also? Actually the text says first answer is 'adam ta'limi' and second is 'ta'limi') concedes for argument's sake that the specific collection is independently known, but still circularity arises because the definition of Mushaf is of the specific collection, and that specific collection depends on the Mushaf again. Finally, the commentator explains that the author's definition is actually a specification (tashkhis) rather than a definition of essence, giving evidence that 'Qur'an' is applied to both the eternal attribute and the recited collection. This is a technical usul al-fiqh discussion characteristic of late Hanafi supercommentaries like this one, engaging deeply with the logical structure of definitions.

Key Terms
المصحف (al-mushaf): The physical codex containing the written text of the Qur'an or divine revelation; discussed whether it should be defined as 'that which contains the Qur'an' or 'that which contains sheets/revelation'.
القرآن (al-qur'an): The speech of Allah; it can refer to the eternal attribute (kalam azali) or the specific collection (majmu' shakhsi) recited by people. The author treats it as synonymous with al-Kitab.
الدور (al-dawr): Logical circularity in definition, where the definiendum is implicitly or explicitly used in its own definition, rendering it invalid.

The Universal and the Specific in Defining Qur'an and Mushaf
Critically examining the universal/specific distinction to defend against circularity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the distinction between universal concept (ma'na kulli) and specific collection (majmu' shakhsi) to analyze the circularity objection in defining 'Mushaf' and 'Qur'an', and explain the two responses of the Hanafi commentator.

Core Idea
The universal meaning of 'Qur'an' depends on the Mushaf, but the Mushaf's definition refers to the specific collection, not the universal — yet circularity still arises because the specific collection depends on the universal.

This part of the commentary addresses a more nuanced objection: the usuli's definition of the Holy Qur'an is of its universal meaning (ma'na kulli) — the eternal divine speech — which depends on the Mushaf as a physical entity. However, in the definition of Mushaf, the term 'Qur'an' is used to refer to the specific collection (majmu' shakhsi) that people recite, not the universal meaning. If these are different respects, circularity might be avoided. The commentator provides two responses: one arguing that even the specific collection requires the universal concept as its genus, and another conceding the point but still finding circularity because the definition is of the specific collection itself. Finally, the commentator clarifies that the author's definition is actually a specification (tashkhis), not a definition of essence.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
The objector claims that the usul al-fiqh definition of the Qur'an is of its universal meaning (the divine attribute), which depends on the Mushaf. But in the definition of Mushaf, 'Qur'an' refers to the specific collection. Since these are different aspects, no circularity arises.
The objection is based on a distinction between ma'na kulli and majmu' shakhsi.
2
Analyze the first response (adam ta'limi)
The commentator argues that we do not concede that knowledge of the specific collection is independent of the universal meaning. The universal concept is the genus (jins) for the specific collection; therefore, any specific collection is only actualized within the framework of the universal. Thus, the dependency chain: universal → Mushaf → specific collection → universal, leading to circularity.
This response says the specific collection cannot be known without the universal.
3
Analyze the second response (ta'limi)
Even if we concede for argument's sake that the specific collection is independently known, circularity still occurs because the definition of Mushaf is of the specific collection. That specific collection depends on the Mushaf as a physical codex, and the Mushaf in turn depends on the specific collection (as its content), creating a circle.
This response shows that the objector's own premise leads to circularity.
4
Conclude with the author's point
The text then explains that the author's definition is actually a specification (tashkhis), not a definition of essence (ta'rif bi al- mahiyyah). This is evidenced by the fact that 'Qur'an' is applied to both the eternal attribute and the recited collection. A specification simply distinguishes the thing from others by its characteristics.
This avoids the circularity problem because it does not claim to capture the essence.

Commentary
This passage is a critical refinement (hashiya) on the definition of Mushaf and Qur'an, specifically addressing the problem of circularity (al-dawr) when defining Mushaf as 'that which contains the Qur'an'. The commentator engages with several objections and defenses, all within the Hanafi usul tradition. The main issue is that if we define Mushaf by referring to the Qur'an, and the Qur'an is itself defined by reference to Mushaf, we get a vicious circle. The commentator first notes that the author (Sadr al-Shariah in al-Tawdih) treats Kitab and Qur'an as synonymous, so any definition that uses one will be circular if the other is used reciprocally. Some scholars tried to avoid circularity by defining Mushaf as 'that which contains written revelation' rather than 'the Qur'an', but the commentator points out that since 'revelation' and 'Qur'an' are synonymous, the problem remains. Another attempted solution is to define Mushaf as 'that which contains sheets' (sahaif) in a general sense, including non-Qur'anic scriptures, so that knowledge of the Mushaf does not depend on knowledge of the Qur'an. The commentator rejects this because it departs from the customary (urf) meaning of Mushaf in the Shari'a, which has become the real (haqiqah) meaning; shifting to a metaphorical (majaz) meaning is not commendable in definitions. A more sophisticated objection distinguishes between the universal concept (ma'na kulli) of the Qur'an and the specific collection (majmu' shakhsi). The objector claims that the usul al-fiqh definition is of the universal meaning, which depends on the Mushaf, but the Mushaf's definition refers to the specific collection, not the universal, so there is no circularity. The commentator gives two responses: The first (ta'limi) argues that even the specific collection cannot be known without the universal meaning, because the universal is the genus for the specific, so circularity remains. The second (ta'limi also? Actually the text says first answer is 'adam ta'limi' and second is 'ta'limi') concedes for argument's sake that the specific collection is independently known, but still circularity arises because the definition of Mushaf is of the specific collection, and that specific collection depends on the Mushaf again. Finally, the commentator explains that the author's definition is actually a specification (tashkhis) rather than a definition of essence, giving evidence that 'Qur'an' is applied to both the eternal attribute and the recited collection. This is a technical usul al-fiqh discussion characteristic of late Hanafi supercommentaries like this one, engaging deeply with the logical structure of definitions.

Key Terms
المعنى الكلي (al-ma'na al-kulli): The universal meaning or concept, which applies to all particulars; in this context, it refers to the definition of the Qur'an as eternal divine speech.
المجموع الشخصي (al-majmu' al-shakhsi): The specific collection, i.e., the particular publicly recited and memorized text of the Qur'an.
التشخيص (al-tashkhis): Specification: defining something by mentioning its characteristics to distinguish it from others, without claiming to express its essence.

The Four Divisions of Quranic Speech and Their Order
Explaining the four divisions and the author's ordering rationale.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four divisions of Quranic speech according to the Hanafi method, explain the order (wad', isti'mal, zuhur, dalala), and justify why the author placed isti'mal before zuhur.

Core Idea
Quranic speech is analyzed in four aspects: coinage, usage, clarity, and indication — each step building logically on the previous.

This lesson introduces the author's systematic classification of Quranic speech into four distinct analytical categories. Understanding this framework is essential for any student of usul al-fiqh because it provides the basis for how Hanafi scholars derive rulings from the Quran. The author's ordering differs from that of Fakhr al-Islam, and we will explore the reasoning behind his choice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four perspectives
Recognize that the author classifies speech from four angles: wad' (coining), isti'mal (usage), zuhur/khafa' (clarity/obscurity), and kayfiyya dalala (manner of indication).
2
Understand the definition of Quran used
Note the definition: 'nazm dal 'ala al-ma'na' (arrangement indicating meaning), which includes both arrangement and meaning but primarily focuses on the arrangement indicating meaning.
3
Trace the author's ordering argument
The author argues that isti'mal logically precedes zuhur because a word must be used before its clarity can be assessed. This is why he placed isti'mal as the second division and zuhur as the third, contrary to Fakhr al-Islam.
4
Relate the divisions to the definition
Each division is 'bil-nisba ila al-ma'na' (relative to meaning), meaning they all relate to the arrangement (nazm) but with consideration of meaning.

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of the author's methodological framework for classifying Quranic speech. The author begins by defining the Quran as 'the arrangement (nazm) that indicates meaning', aligning with the view of earlier Hanafi scholars (Mashayikh) who said the Quran is both arrangement and meaning, but not as two separate entities; rather, the arrangement inherently carries meaning. The author then introduces four divisions of speech based on different perspectives: first by wad' (coining), second by isti'mal (usage), third by zuhur and khafa' (clarity/obscurity of meaning), and fourth by kayfiyya dalala (manner of indication). The author defends his ordering (wad', isti'mal, zuhur, dalala) against Fakhr al-Islam's ordering (wad', zuhur, isti'mal, dalala), arguing that usage logically precedes considerations of clarity because a word must first be used before its clarity can be assessed. The passage then delves into the fourth division, where Fakhr al-Islam used various expressions (e.g., istidlal bil-'ibara, al-thabit bil-nass, al-wuquf). Taftazani clarifies that these variations do not contradict the author's unified approach; rather, the author's phrasing that all divisions are 'related to arrangement with respect to meaning' (bil-nisba ila al-ma'na) is superior because it encompasses both aspects and avoids fragmentation. Taftazani also resolves an apparent conflict between the author and Fakhr al-Islam by stating that both ultimately agree; Fakhr al-Islam's fourth division is about meaning, but the author's framing includes that under arrangement relative to meaning. The Fourth Division: Manner of Indication and Harmonizing the Author with Fakhr al-Islam
Explaining the fourth division and the author's harmonization with Fakhr al-Islam.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the fourth division (kayfiyya dalala), identify the different expressions used by Fakhr al-Islam, and understand how the author's unifying framework resolves apparent contradictions.

Core Idea
The fourth division focuses on how words indicate meaning — by explicit text, implication, semantic allusion, or necessary inference — and this fits under 'arrangement relative to meaning'.

This lesson deepens the analysis of the fourth division, which deals with the manner in which Quranic speech indicates its meanings. Fakhr al-Islam used varied terminologies to describe these modes, leading some later scholars to think the fourth division belongs solely to meaning rather than arrangement. The author's expression 'bil-nisba ila al-ma'na' (relative to meaning) elegantly resolves this, keeping the division within the domain of nazm while acknowledging the role of meaning. Taftazani defends this as the superior formulation.

Distinction Between Nazm and Lafz: Taftazani's Critical Refinement
Taftazani's critical refinement of the synonymity claim

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the technical distinction between nazm and lafz and apply Taftazani's two answers to resolve apparent synonymy.

Core Idea
Nazm and lafz are not synonyms; nazm requires ordered composition while lafz applies to any utterance.

In this passage, Taftazani addresses a critical objection against the author's equation of nazm with lafz. The objector argues that nazm and lafz are distinct in both linguistic and technical senses, and that sections like 'amm and khass belong to lafz, not nazm. Understanding Taftazani's two answers — metaphorical extension and relational attribution — is essential for correctly classifying usul terms and avoiding confusion in istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
Read the passage to understand the question: the author's use of 'nazm' to mean 'lafz' implies synonymy, but the objector argues they are distinct. Note the two differences: (1) nazm relates only to compound speech, while lafz is general; (2) nazm requires ordering of words, meanings, and proportion of implications, while lafz has no such conditions.
The objector also points out that usul sections like 'amm and khass are attributes of singular words (lafz), not of composition (nazm).
2
Analyze Taftazani's First Answer
Taftazani concedes the distinction but argues that 'nazm' here is used metaphorically to mean absolute speech — mentioning the specific (nazm) and intending the general (lafz). This resolves the apparent synonymy without requiring nazm and lafz to be the same in technical terms.
This is supported by the principle that a specific term can be used for a general meaning in certain contexts.
3
Analyze Taftazani's Second Answer
Alternatively, 'aqsam al-nazm' (sections of nazm) means 'sections related to nazm' — i.e., the attributes of the singular words that occur within a nazm. The idafah (construct) indicates minimal association, not that the sections themselves are attributes of nazm. Thus, nazm retains its real meaning.
In common usage, 'amm, khass, etc. are attributes of lafz, not nazm. The idafah here is for affiliation, not possession.
4
Apply the Distinction to Usul Analysis
When analyzing a Quranic or hadith text, first identify whether you are dealing with a singular word (lafz) or a composed phrase (nazm). This affects how you apply usul categories like 'amm (general) and khass (specific): these are properties of words (lafz), but their interaction within a composition (nazm) may restrict or expand their scope.
For example, the word 'الذين آمنوا' is a composition (nazm), but the word 'آمنوا' itself is a lafz that may be 'amm or khass based on its form.

The Hanafi Definition of Sifah: Derived Noun with Exact Meaning and Derived Pattern

A sifah must have the exact root meaning with a derived pattern; time/place/instrument nouns are excluded.

Why did the commentator add 'مع وزن المشتق' (with the derived pattern) to the definition of sifah?
A) To include all derived nouns
B) To exclude ism zaman and ism alat
C) To emphasize the importance of weight in Arabic morphology
D) To include pronouns and demonstratives

Key Takeaway
Sifah is a derived noun whose meaning is exactly the root meaning with a derived pattern; ism zaman and ism alat are excluded because their meaning is not simply the root meaning with that pattern.
