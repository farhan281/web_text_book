Lexical and Syntactic Analysis of the Author's Opening Sentence: Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas
Parsing key sources of Islamic law: Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the linguistic and technical definitions of Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas, and parse their grammatical relationships in the author's opening sentence.

Core Idea
The Sunnah is the niche of light; Ijma' and Qiyas are derived from its traces to form clear paths and measurements.

This lesson unpacks the opening sentence of the sharh, a poetic and precise introduction to the three foundational sources of Islamic law after the Quran: Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. By analyzing each term both linguistically (لغة) and technically (اصطلاحا), we gain clarity on how Hanafi usul defines these sources. The grammatical parsing (حل ترکیب) then reveals the logical relationships between them, showing that the Sunnah is the primary source from which Ijma' and Qiyas derive their authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Metaphorical Structure
Recognize that 'mishkat al-sunnah' (the niche of the Sunnah) is a metaphor for the Sunnah as the source of light. The lamp (siraj) represents the clear rulings derived from it. Understand that 'awqada' (kindled) and 'awdaha' (made clear) are actions of Allah.
Metaphors are common in classical usul texts to elevate the subject matter.
2
Define Each Term Linguistically and Technically
For each of the three key terms (Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas), extract both the literal meaning (e.g., sunnah = path/custom) and the technical meaning as given in the passage (e.g., Sunnah = what originates from the Prophet in word, deed, or tacit approval).
Distinguishing between linguistic and technical definitions is crucial for precise legal reasoning.
3
Analyze the Syntactic Relationships
Parse the sentence grammatically: 'min mishkati' and 'iqtibas' are related to 'awqada'; 'sirajan wahhajan' is its object. 'li-ijma'i al-ara' is related to 'awdaha'; 'ala iqtifa' is related to 'ijma'; 'qiyasan wa minhajan' are objects of 'awdaha'. 'sadafta' has 'bihar al-'ulum' as its object, with circumstantial clauses.
Understanding syntax ensures the correct logical flow: the Sunnah is the source, Ijma' and Qiyas are derived from it.
4
Appreciate the Hierarchy of Sources
Note that Ijma' and Qiyas are mentioned as following the traces (athar) of the Sunnah, indicating their secondary and derivative nature. This aligns with the Hanafi usul principle that Quran and Sunnah are primary, Ijma' is a source only when based on them, and Qiyas is a last resort.
This hierarchy is foundational for all subsequent istinbat exercises.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed lexical and syntactic analysis of the author's opening sentence in the sharh. The author uses poetic and metaphorical language to introduce the three foundational sources of Islamic law besides the Quran: Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. The 'mishkat al-sunnah' refers to the Sunnah as a source of light; 'ijma' al-ara' refers to consensus of scholars' opinions; 'qiyas' is analogical reasoning; and 'minhaj' is the clear path derived from these sources. The vocabulary section (حل مفردات) explains each term in both linguistic and technical senses, showing how Hanafi usul defines these key sources. The syntax section (حل ترکیب) shows grammatical relationships, emphasizing the structure of the sentence. This type of detailed parsing is common in Hanafi usul texts to ensure precise understanding of the foundational principles.

Key Terms
السنة (al-sunnah): Path and custom; technically: what originated from the Prophet (peace be upon him) in word, deed, or tacit approval.
الاجماع (al-ijma'): Consensus of the mujtahids of the Ummah of Muhammad in any age on a legal matter.
القياس (al-qiyas): Equating a branch (far') to a root (asl) in the effective cause ('illah) of a ruling.

Reconciling Conflicting Hadiths on Beginning Important Matters
Explaining author's choice of ḥāl for ḥamdala and reconciling conflicting hadiths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of reconciling apparently conflicting textual evidence (taʿāruḍ) by distinguishing between real, relative, and customary meanings of 'beginning' (ibṭidā’).

Core Idea
Apparent conflict in hadiths is resolved by interpreting 'beginning' differently: real for tasmiyah, relative/customary for tahmid.

This passage from a traditional usul commentary delves into the author's choice to phrase the praise of Allah as 'hāmidan'—a circumstantial accusative—rather than the conventional nominal sentence. By doing so, the author aims to reconcile two hadiths that seem to conflict: one commanding every important matter to begin with bismillāh, the other with al-ḥamdu lillāh. Understanding how this reconciliation works is essential for mastering the Hanafi approach to handling apparent textual contradictions (taʿāruḍ).

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent conflict (taʿāruḍ)
The two hadiths appear to require different beginnings for any important matter. Recognize that both are considered authentic and must be acted upon if possible.
2
Recognize the author's grammatical solution
By making both tasmiyah and taḥmīd as ḥāl (circumstantial qualifiers) of the same beginning, the author attempts to act upon both simultaneously. This grammatical structure allows both actions to be present at the inception.
The ḥāl construction links each qualifying phrase directly to the act of beginning.
3
Analyze the distinction of 'beginning' types
The text introduces three types of beginning: real (ḥaqīqī), relative (iḍāfī), and customary (ʿurfī). Understand how assigning different types to each hadith resolves the conflict without discarding any.
The hadith of tasmiyah is taken as real beginning; the hadith of taḥmīd as relative or customary beginning.
4
Evaluate the evidence for precedence (tarjīḥ)
The author gives precedence to tasmiyah based on three proofs: Quran (all surahs except Bara'ah begin with bismillah), consensus (ijmāʿ) on its precedence, and the letter of Prophet Sulaymān.
Precedence does not mean rejecting the other; it means ordering them in real and secondary senses.
5
Note the omission of conjunction
The author leaves out the conjunctive wāw to avoid implying subordination (tābiʿiyyah), thus preserving equality (taswiyah) between the two qualifiers.
This linguistic choice reinforces the intended reconciliation.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed textual analysis by the commentator (shāriḥ) of the author's (muṣannif) choice to introduce the ḥamdala (praise of Allah) using the word 'ḥāmidan' as a circumstantial accusative (ḥāl) rather than the conventional nominal or verbal sentence. The passage explains the wisdom behind this stylistic decision and the underlying legal principles concerning conflicting hadiths. The analysis proceeds through a series of questions and answers typical of scholarly discussion.

The author, by making 'ḥāmidan' a ḥāl attached to the implied subject (ḍamīr) of the verb 'abda’u' (I begin), achieves two purposes: (1) creating equality (taswiyah) between the tasmiyah (saying bismillāh) and the taḥmīd (praise) grammatically, and (2) ensuring that both are qualifiers (qayd) for the act of beginning, thereby allowing the practitioner to act upon both hadiths simultaneously. The hadiths quoted are: 'Every important matter not begun with bismillāh is devoid of blessing' and 'Every important matter not begun with al-ḥamdu lillāh is maimed.' These two hadiths appear conflicting because one beginning cannot literally be with both. The resolution is given by recognizing that the term 'ibtidā'' (beginning) has multiple meanings: real (ḥaqīqī), relative (iḍāfī), and customary (ʿurfī). By interpreting the hadith of taḥmīd as referring to relative or customary beginning, both hadiths can be upheld: precedence is given to tasmiyah in the real sense (based on Quran and Ijma), while taḥmīd is included in a relative or customary sense. The commentator also explains three proofs for giving precedence to tasmiyah: Quranic practice (every sūra except Barā’ah begins with bismillāh), consensus of the ummah, and the precedent of Prophet Sulaymān's letter. Finally, the author omits the conjunction (wāw) between tasmiyah and taḥmīd to avoid implying subordination, preserving their equal status as qualifiers.

Key Terms
تسمیہ (tasmiyah): Saying 'bismillāh' at the beginning of an action.
تحمید (taḥmīd): Saying 'al-ḥamdu lillāh' in praise of Allah.
حال (ḥāl): A circumstantial accusative describing the state of the subject at the time of the action.
ابتداء حقیقی (ibṭidā’ ḥaqīqī): Real beginning: what is first in any respect.
ابتداء اضافی (ibṭidā’ iḍāfī): Relative beginning: what is first relative to some things but later relative to others.
ابتداء عرفی (ibṭidā’ ʿurfī): Customary beginning: what is considered first according to common usage.

Why Salawat Follows Hamd: Hanafi Usul Analysis of the Book's Opening
Analysis of reasons for combining hamd and salawat in book openings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi justifications for including salawat after hamd in a book's opening and evaluate their strength using usul principles.

Core Idea
Salawat upon the Prophet after hamd is a necessary recognition of his role as the mediator of Islam, the greatest blessing.

This lesson explores the commentary on why salawat (blessings) on the Prophet Muhammad is mentioned immediately after praise of Allah in classical Hanafi book openings. The commentator provides multiple proofs, including a well-known hadith, Quranic command, and rational obligation. Understanding this reasoning sharpens your ability to analyze how Hanafi usul integrates diverse sources to justify a practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal question
The commentator addresses a potential objection: why mention salawat after hamd when the book is about praising Allah? Recognize that the issue is the appropriateness of combining praise of Allah with supplication for the Prophet.
The objection is implicit: 'Why not just praise Allah?'
2
Examine the five answers
List and understand each of the five justifications given: (1) the Prophet is the greatest benefactor after Allah; (2) the Prophet's perfect essence deserves blessings; (3) Quranic command (Quran 33:56); (4) hadith qudsi; (5) the hadith 'every important matter without salawat is deficient'.
These are not mutually exclusive; they provide cumulative evidence.
3
Analyze the hadith evidence
Focus on the hadith 'كل امر ذى بال لم بداء با الصلوة على فهو اقطع' (every important matter not begun with salawat is deficient). Classify it as a hadith ahad (zanni al-thubut) and discuss its dalala (meaning) – it implies recommendation, not obligation.
In Hanafi usul, ahad hadith gives zanni knowledge, but it can still be relied upon for non-obligatory acts.
4
Evaluate the strongest justification
The commentator emphasizes that the Prophet is the greatest benefactor because through him we received Islam, the greatest blessing. Compare this rational justification with the textual proofs. In Hanafi thought, rational obligation (wajib 'aqli) supports the textual evidence.
The combination of rational and textual proofs strengthens the case.
5
Understand the omission of the Prophet's name
The passage also explains why the author does not explicitly name the Prophet: for exaltation (tanzih) and because the description 'best of messengers' removes any ambiguity. This is a literary and legal point about clarity versus respect.
This shows the commentator's attention to both linguistic and legal dimensions.

The Function of 'Zuhur' in the Definition of Fiqh
Explaining the benefit of the condition 'bi al-zuhur'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the condition 'bi al-zuhur' is included in the Hanafi definition of fiqh and apply the rule that a mujtahid must follow revelation over his own ijtihad when it arrives.

Core Idea
A mujtahid may validly rely on ijtihad until revelation reaches him, but must then abandon ijtihad if it contradicts.

The Hanafi definition of fiqh specifies knowledge of practical legal rulings derived from their detailed evidences. One key condition is that these rulings must have 'appeared' (zuhur) to the mujtahid. This passage from a commentary explains why this qualification is necessary and what happens when ijtihad precedes the arrival of revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition and its purpose
Notice that the definition of fiqh includes the phrase 'bi al-zuhur' (with appearance). The commentator explains this is to exclude rulings that have been revealed but have not yet reached the mujtahid.
The condition protects the mujtahid from being held accountable for rulings he could not have known.
2
Understand the scenario
Consider a situation where a ruling has been revealed by Allah but the mujtahid is unaware of it. The passage says 'if the revelation of a ruling has occurred but that ruling has not yet appeared' – meaning it hasn't reached him.
This could happen due to distance, time, or lack of transmission.
3
Recognize the ruling for ijtihad before revelation
The passage states that in such a case, the faqih (mujtahid) is not required to know that ruling. Moreover, if he makes ijtihad on the matter, his ijtihad is valid. This shows that ijtihad is a legitimate source until revelation is known.
Ijtihad means independent reasoning based on available evidence.
4
Apply the rule after revelation arrives
Once the revelation reaches the mujtahid, if his ijtihad matches it, it is fine. If it contradicts, he must retract his ijtihad and follow the revelation. This establishes the supremacy of revelation over ijtihad.
The command is clear: retract (ruju') from qiyas and ijtihad.
5
Note the hierarchy
The condition illustrates a key usul principle: revelation (wahy) takes precedence over human reasoning. Ijtihad is a fallback when revelation is absent or ambiguous.
This is foundational for understanding the authority of textual evidence.

The Meaning of 'Zuhur' in the Definition of Fiqh
Clarifying that 'zuhur' refers to the mujtahid's own knowledge, not general or majority knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify why the Hanafi definition of fiqh uses 'zuhur' to mean the mujtahid's personal knowledge and evaluate the objections against alternative interpretations.

Core Idea
Fiqh is personal knowledge of rulings whose revelation is apparent to the mujtahid, not to some or the majority.

This lesson tackles a key objection against the classical Hanafi definition of fiqh: what does 'zuhur' (appearance of revelation) mean? If it means appearance to only some companions, great jurists unaware of certain rulings would not be fuqaha. If it means majority, rulings from solitary reports would be excluded. The answer refines the definition precisely.

Guided Path
1
Restate the objection
The objector asks: does 'zuhur' mean appearance to some companions (zuhur 'ala al-ba'd) or to the majority (zuhur 'ala al-a'amm wal-agh lab)?
2
Analyze the first alternative
If 'zuhur' means to some, then only rulings known to a few companions count as fiqh, which would deny the title 'faqih' to companions like Umar who did not know certain rulings known to Aisha. This is absurd as their juristic status is agreed upon.
The example of Aisha vs. other companions illustrates the flaw.
3
Analyze the second alternative
If 'zuhur' means to the majority, two problems arise: (i) the total number of companions is unknown, so 'majority' is undefined; (ii) many fiqh rulings come from solitary reports (akhbar ahad) which were not widely known. These would be excluded from fiqh, contradicting its actual scope.
Solitary reports are a major source of Hanafi fiqh.
4
Understand the author's solution
The author resolves the objection by clarifying that 'zuhur' means appearance to the individual mujtahid ('inda al-mujtahid). Thus, fiqh is knowledge of those rulings whose revelation has become known to that particular mujtahid, regardless of general knowledge.
This interpretation accommodates solitary reports and respects the varying knowledge levels of scholars.
5
Apply the definition
Test the definition: a ruling proven by a solitary authentic hadith is 'zuhur' to the mujtahid who knows that hadith, so it is part of his fiqh. Another mujtahid unaware of it does not have that ruling in his fiqh until he learns it.
This highlights the personal nature of fiqh in the Hanafi tradition.

The fourth objection: If by 'appearance of revelation' is meant... The basis is that you said in the definition of fiqh that fiqh is the name of knowledge of those rulings with which revelation has appeared. Now the question is: what do you mean by this appearance? Does 'zuhur' mean partial appearance, i.e., to some (companions), or does it mean appearance to the general and predominant (majority)? If appearance means to some, then the purpose would be that fiqh is the name of knowing those rulings whose appearance has occurred to some companions. Then to be a faqih, it would be necessary to know those rulings that have appeared to some of the companions. But this is false. For example, there were many rulings that were known to Lady Aisha. They had become apparent to her, but other companions who were great jurists did not know those rulings. They used to go to Aisha to inquire about them. Would those companions not be called jurists just because they did not know those rulings? But their being jurists is agreed upon. Therefore this alternative is invalid. And if the second alternative is taken and by 'appearance' is meant appearance to the common and predominant, this too is invalid. First, because there is no defined measure for 'majority'—how many companions are meant? The total number of companions is unknown, so half is also unknown. Due to the ignorance of half, the majority is also unknown because 'majority' means more than half. Also, it is not possible for most companions to have the appearance of every revealed ruling, because there were many narrators and companions were generally scattered due to travels, jihad, trade, and other occupations. Therefore taking 'zuhur' to mean appearance to the majority is problematic. Second, even if we accept the scattering due to travels and occupations and still say that appearance to the common and predominant occurred, then this flaw would follow: those rulings that are proven by solitary reports (akhbar ahad) would not be included in fiqh until their appearance to the majority occurs and the revelation becomes widespread. However, many rulings of fiqh are proven by solitary reports. So both alternatives are invalid. The answer: By 'zuhur' is meant neither appearance in general nor appearance to the common and predominant. Rather, by 'zuhur' is meant appearance to the mujtahid. The purpose is that fiqh is the name of knowledge of those shari'a rulings whose revelation has appeared to the mujtahid.

Conditions for Valid Shar'i Proofs
Conditions for validity of shar'i proofs, with focus on later mujtahid's qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four conditions for a shar'i proof to be valid and apply the condition regarding qiyas by a later mujtahid, distinguishing between cases of simple and compound consensus.

Core Idea
A later mujtahid's qiyas is invalid if it violates simple or compound consensus of earlier mujtahids.

In this passage from al-Tawdih, the author lays down the conditions that a shar'i proof must satisfy to establish a ruling. These conditions ensure the reliability and authority of the proof. We will examine each condition, focusing especially on the nuanced rule for qiyas by later mujtahids, which prevents breaking the consensus of the early scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four conditions
The author lists four conditions: (1) not abrogated, (2) no equal opposing proof, (3) no preponderant opposing proof, (4) for qiyas by a later mujtahid, it must agree with at least one earlier mujtahid's opinion.
These conditions are general for all proofs except the fourth which is specific to qiyas.
2
Understand the rationale for the first three conditions
A proof that is abrogated no longer carries legal force. An equal opposing proof creates a conflict that cannot be resolved without preponderance. A preponderant opposing proof outweighs the proof at hand.
These conditions maintain consistency and hierarchy among proofs.
3
Analyze the special condition for qiyas
The condition applies only when early mujtahids have given opinions on the matter. There are multiple scenarios: (a) no opinion exists → later mujtahid may freely exercise qiyas; (b) one opinion exists → he may oppose it; (c) multiple opinions agreeing → simple ijma', cannot oppose; (d) multiple differing opinions → compound ijma', cannot introduce new opinion.
This condition prevents violation of ijma', both simple and compound.
4
Distinguish between simple ijma' and compound ijma'
Simple ijma' is when all early mujtahids agree on one ruling. Compound ijma' is when they give multiple differing opinions, effectively agreeing that no other opinion besides these is possible. Both block later ijtihad.
Compound consensus is a unique Hanafi concept; other schools may not recognize it as binding.

Matn of Tawdih: 'Then know that every proof from the shar'i proofs only establishes the ruling when...' From here the author explains the conditions of shar'i proofs. Every proof from the shar'i proofs only establishes a ruling when all conditions are found, conditions that are mentioned in their appropriate places. The author has mentioned several conditions: (1) One condition is that the proof is not abrogated; if it is abrogated, then the ruling is not established by it. For example, the permissibility of donkey meat is a ruling for which a proof (hadith) exists, but it is abrogated; in Khaybar it was declared forbidden, so this proof does not establish the ruling. (2) There is no equal opposing proof. (3) There is no preponderant opposing proof. (4) If qiyas is the proof for a ruling among the four proofs, then there is a special condition: the qiyas of a later and subsequent mujtahid is only valid and authoritative when his qiyas agrees with one of the opinions of the early mujtahids. If his qiyas and opinion are against all the early mujtahids' opinions, not agreeing with even one, then this qiyas is invalid. Superficially, this condition seems strange because it appears that a later mujtahid must imitate one of the early mujtahids, but the reality is not so. The actual situation is that the later mujtahid, in the matter he is applying qiyas and ijtihad, has two cases: either early mujtahids have given different opinions in that matter or not. If they have not given opinions, or only one opinion exists, then there is no condition that the later mujtahid's opinion must agree with earlier opinions, because no opinion passed before or only one exists, and the later mujtahid can oppose it. If early mujtahids' opinions exist, then there are two cases: either all opinions agree on one view, or each mujtahid differs. If all opinions agree, i.e., the early mujtahids have reached consensus (ijma'), then if the later mujtahid adopts a new opinion, it necessarily opposes the ijma', so his new opinion is invalid and his qiyas is void. If the early mujtahids' opinions differ, even then it is not permissible for the later and subsequent mujtahid to adopt a new qiyas and new way, because when the early mujtahids deliberated and presented their opinions, it is as if they reached a consensus that only these opinions are possible in this matter and no other opinion besides them. This is called 'ijma' murakkab' (compound consensus). Therefore, the later mujtahid's adopting a new qiyas would oppose the compound consensus. For this reason, a condition was laid for the later mujtahid that his qiyas must reach (agree with) one of the early mujtahids' opinions; otherwise, it would oppose the mujtahids' consensus. These conditions the author has mentioned in his book. Commentary
This passage is an expansion of the conditions for the validity of shar'i proofs as stated in the matn of al-Tawdih. It lists four main conditions: (1) the proof must not be abrogated; (2) there must be no equal opposing proof (mu'āriḍ musāwi); (3) there must be no preponderant opposing proof (mu'āriḍ rājiḥ); (4) for qiyas by a later mujtahid, his qiyas must agree with at least one earlier mujtahid's opinion. The commentator then explains the rationale behind the fourth condition: it prevents breaking ijma' (both simple and compound). If earlier mujtahids have given unanimous or multiple opinions, the later mujtahid cannot introduce a new opinion because that would violate the implicit consensus that only those opinions are valid. However, if no opinion or only one opinion exists, the later mujtahid may differ. This condition ensures continuity and respect for established scholarly authority within the Hanafi school. The passage concludes by noting further conditions, such as that a proof from the Quran must be clear in meaning (ma'lum al-ma'na).

Key Terms
إجماع مركب (ijma' murakkab): A situation where early mujtahids have given multiple differing opinions on a matter, effectively agreeing that no other opinion besides these is valid.
معارض (mu'arid): An opposing proof that contradicts the proof in question.

Key Takeaway
A shar'i proof is valid only if it meets conditions including non-abrogation, no equal or preponderant opposing proof, and for qiyas by a later mujtahid, it must align with at least one earlier mujtahid's view under the constraints of simple or compound consensus.
