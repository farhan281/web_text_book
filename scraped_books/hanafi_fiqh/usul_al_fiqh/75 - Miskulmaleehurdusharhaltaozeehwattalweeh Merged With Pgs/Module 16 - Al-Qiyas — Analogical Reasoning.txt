Rulings from Qiyas Included in Definition of Hukm
Third objection to definition of hukm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position that rulings derived by qiyas are still considered part of khitāb Allāh and evaluate the opposing view from the Mutakallimun.

Core Idea
Qiyas reveals hidden rulings of the Divine address; it does not create new law.

In this passage, we examine a critical objection to the definition of hukm as 'khitāb Allāh': does it exclude rulings established by qiyas? The Hanafi answer clarifies that qiyas is not an independent source but a tool to uncover what is already in the divine address. Understanding this distinction is essential for grasping the epistemological foundation of Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
State the third objection as presented by Taftazani: that the definition of hukm (as khitāb Allāh) does not cover rulings derived by qiyas because qiyas is not directly khitāb Allāh.
The objector is from the Mutakallimun school.
2
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that qiyas does not establish a new ruling but reveals a hidden ruling already present in khitāb Allāh. Thus the ruling is still from khitāb Allāh.
Key term: qiyas is 'muzhir' (manifestor), not 'muthbit' (establisher).
3
Extend the reasoning
The author then notes that the same objection could be applied to rulings from sunnah and ijma, implying they too are ultimately from khitāb Allāh. This shows the broader principle that all valid rulings trace back to divine address.
This is a meta-critique: Taftazani's objection is too narrow.
4
Determine the practical implication
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is whether rulings from qiyas are considered epistemologically equivalent to directly revealed rulings for purposes of definition and authority.
For the Hanafi school, they are; for the Mutakallimun, they are not.

The Two Interpretations of 'Ma'a Malakat al-Istinbat'
Analyzing the phrase 'ma'a malakat al-istinbat' in the definition of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two grammatical interpretations of 'ma'a malakat al-istinbat' in the definition of fiqh and identify the preferred interpretation according to Hanafi usul scholars.

Core Idea
The skill of fiqh is the ability to deduce analogical branches from primary rulings, not just understanding rulings from texts.

In this lesson, we examine the subtle grammatical analysis of the definition of fiqh. The commentator unpacks the phrase 'ma'a malakat al-istinbat' to reveal two possible meanings. Understanding these nuances is crucial for grasping the Hanafi conception of ijtihad and the distinction between mere textual knowledge and true juristic reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the grammatical connection
Recognize that 'ma'a' is a connecting adverb linking 'al-ilm' (knowledge) to 'malakat al-istinbat' (the skill of deduction). This means knowledge must be accompanied by this skill.
2
Analyze the definite article on 'al-istinbat'
The 'al' on 'al-istinbat' is not for definiteness but replaces a mudaf ilayh (genitive construct). Two possibilities exist for what this mudaf ilayh is.
This grammatical substitution is common in Arabic definitions.
3
Propose the first interpretation
If the mudaf ilayh is 'furu' qiyasiyya' (analogical branches), then the phrase becomes: 'with the skill of deducing analogical branches from the primary rulings' (min al-ahkam al-mansusa). The pronoun in 'minha' refers to al-ahkam.
This interpretation focuses on deriving new cases from established rulings.
4
Propose the second interpretation
If the mudaf ilayh is 'al-ahkam' (rulings), then the phrase becomes: 'with the skill of deducing rulings from their evidences' (min adillatiha). The pronoun in 'minha' now refers to al-adilla.
This interpretation emphasizes deriving rulings directly from sources.
5
Evaluate the preferred interpretation
The commentator al-Taftazani states that the first interpretation is more face-centered (awjah) and prevalent (ra'ij). The skill of deducing analogical branches from primary rulings is more specific to the faqih's craft, while the second is more about general textual knowledge.
The first interpretation aligns with the Hanafi emphasis on qiyas as a core competency.

The Circularity Argument in Excluding Masa'il Qiyasiyya from Fiqh
Arguing why analogical issues are not part of the definition of fiqh due to circularity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the circularity argument (dawr) used by Hanafi usulis to exclude analogically derived issues from the definition of fiqh, and apply the same reasoning to evaluate objections.

Core Idea
If masa'il qiyasiyya were required for fiqh, then fiqh would depend on itself – pure circularity.

This lesson explores a sophisticated logical argument from Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author of the matn claimed that analogically derived issues (masa'il qiyasiyya) are not part of the definition of fiqh; otherwise, circularity (dawr) would occur. The commentator expands on this, addressing an objection that the circularity only applies to the first generation of mujtahids. Understanding this argument sharpens your ability to analyze definitions and logical dependencies in usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn's claim
Recognize that the matn states knowledge of masa'il qiyasiyya is not a condition for fiqh, because including them would cause circularity.
This is the starting point of the commentary.
2
Understand the circularity mechanism
The commentator explains: if masa'il qiyasiyya (products of ijtihad) are a condition, then fiqh (juristic skill) depends on them, and they depend on fiqh – a loop.
The key is that these issues are results (furu'), not sources (usul).
3
Analyze the objection
An objector argues that the circularity only applies to the first mujtahids; later mujtahids can learn these issues from earlier ones, so no loop.
4
Evaluate the commentator's response
The commentator refutes: later mujtahids cannot merely do taqlid; they must perform their own ijtihad, thus the issues still depend on their own juristic skill, recreating the circularity.
The response uses `qultu` (I said) to assert the flaw.

Defining Fiqh: Real vs. Compiled Fiqh in the Hanafi Tradition
Defense of the definition of fiqh against Taftazani's objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi definition of fiqh and defend it against objections by distinguishing between real fiqh (al-fiqh al-haqiqi) and compiled fiqh.

Core Idea
Real fiqh is only revealed and consensus-based rulings; qiyas is included only for practical necessity, not essence.

In this passage, the commentator defends the author's definition of fiqh against three objections raised by al-Taftazani. The objections challenge whether the definition can account for the increase and decrease of rulings, apply to the Companions' era, and include analogical rulings. You will see how the Hanafi usul tradition resolves these challenges by distinguishing between the essence of fiqh and its compiled form, a distinction critical for understanding legal epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three objections
Al-Taftazani raises three objections: (1) fiqh cannot increase/decrease if defined as specific knowledge, (2) the definition excludes Companions' fiqh, (3) the definition excludes qiyasi issues which form the bulk of fiqh.
These objections target the comprehensiveness and applicability of the definition.
2
Analyze the response to the first objection
The commentator rebuts by distinguishing 'specific' as generic type (naw'i) not individual (shakhsi), allowing increase and decrease of instances within the type.
Familiarity with the distinction between kulli (universal) and juz'i (particular) is helpful.
3
Analyze the response to the second objection

Commentary
This passage presents a defense of the author's definition of fiqh against three objections raised by al-Taftazani. The passage is a critical refinement from the commentary (sharh) tradition, responding to challenges. The first objection concerns the possibility of increase and decrease in fiqh: if fiqh is a specific knowledge, it cannot change; but the author's definition treats it as a universal (kulli) that can expand or contract. The response clarifies that 'specific' may mean 'generic' (naw'i) not 'individual' (shakhsi); a type can have more or fewer instances over time. The second objection is that the definition fails to include the Companions' fiqh because it mentions consensus, which did not exist at the time. The response argues that the definition is conditional: fiqh is either only revealed rulings (when no consensus) or both (when consensus exists), so it covers the Companions' era as well. The third objection is about qiyasi (analogical) issues, which form the bulk of fiqh but are excluded by the definition. The response initially suggests that qiyasi issues are included relative to the mujtahid who derives them, but this leads to relativism. The final resolution is that real fiqh (al-fiqh al-haqiqi) is only revealed and consensus-based rulings; qiyasi issues were included in the compilation of fiqh works solely due to practical necessity for the public, not because they are part of the essence of fiqh. This reflects a Hanafi usul position that fiqh in its true sense is limited to direct textual and consensus-based knowledge, while analogy is a derived extension incorporated for practical purposes.

Key Terms
الفقه (al-fiqh): Knowledge of rulings revealed by revelation and upon which consensus is formed, along with the ability to derive from them, conditionally including consensus only when it exists.
المسائل القياسية (al-masa'il al-qiyasiyya): Rulings derived through analogy (qiyas), which are not part of the essential definition of fiqh but are included in compiled fiqh works.
ملكة الاستنباط (malakat al-istinbat): The faculty or ability to derive rulings from textual and consensus-based sources, a condition for being a faqih according to the author's definition.

The Third Objection: Qiyasi Issues and the Definition of Fiqh
Responding to the objection that qiyasi issues are excluded from the definition of fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between haqiqi fiqh and mudawwan fiqh and explain why qiyasi issues are included in compiled fiqh but not in real fiqh.

Core Idea
Qiyasi issues are not part of haqiqi fiqh but are included in mudawwan fiqh due to public need.

The third objection challenges the Hanafi definition of fiqh by arguing that it excludes qiyasi issues, which form the majority of fiqh rulings. The initial response—that qiyasi issues are included from the perspective of the deriving mujtahid—is critiqued for leading to fragmentation of fiqh across mujtahids. The final answer introduces a crucial distinction: haqiqi fiqh (essential fiqh) consists only of mannus and ijma'i rulings, while mudawwan fiqh (compiled fiqh) includes qiyasi issues for practical application without altering the essential definition.

The third objection: The objection is that according to the author's definition, qiyasi issues would be excluded from the definition of fiqh because he defined fiqh only as knowledge of what is revealed by revelation and what consensus is established upon. Then he himself said that fiqh is not the name of knowledge of qiyasi issues, even though qiyasi issues are the majority of fiqh issues and most of fiqh consists of these qiyasi issues. The response given by 'illa an yuqal' is summarized: these qiyasi issues are included in fiqh from the perspective of the mujtahid who derived them through ijtihad, because from his perspective they are included in 'ma qad zahara nuzul al-wahy'. But for a mujtahid who has not derived them, they are not included in fiqh. Therefore, fiqh would be something different for each mujtahid. The commentator points out the weakness of this response: it leads to each mujtahid having a separate fiqh, so Imam Abu Hanifa's qiyasi issues are different, and Imam Shafi'i's qiyasi issues are different, leading to different fiqhs. The answer: It is accepted that real fiqh is only knowledge of two things: (1) mannus issues revealed by revelation, (2) issues of consensus. Qiyasi issues are not included in real fiqh, as the commentator himself mentioned earlier when he said that knowledge of qiyasi issues is not required for fiqh. Then the question arises: if qiyasi issues are excluded from fiqh, why are they discussed in fiqh books? The answer: When fiqh was compiled and arranged, these qiyasi issues were included in fiqh based on the need of the common people for them in their actions, just as they needed mannus and consensus rulings. Due to people's need, these qiyasi issues were included in fiqh based on necessity. Otherwise, they are not part of real fiqh. Summary: Qiyasi issues are not part of real fiqh, but they are part of compiled fiqh. Since compiled fiqh is separate for each mujtahid (e.g., Hanafi fiqh, Shafi'i fiqh, Maliki fiqh, etc.), there is no harm in fiqh being different.

Commentary
This passage addresses the third objection to the definition of fiqh as knowledge of what is revealed by revelation and what consensus is established upon. The objector argues that this definition excludes qiyasi issues, which form the majority of fiqh. The initial response attempts to include qiyasi issues by considering them as revealed for the mujtahid who derives them. However, the commentator criticizes this response because it leads to each mujtahid having a different fiqh. The final answer distinguishes between real fiqh (haqiqi) and compiled fiqh (mudawwan). Real fiqh consists only of mannus and ijma'i issues. Qiyasi issues are not part of real fiqh but were included in compiled fiqh due to public need. This explains why fiqh books contain qiyasi issues even though they are not part of the essential definition of fiqh. The example of different madhhabs (Hanafi, Shafi'i, Maliki) shows that each mujtahid's compiled fiqh can differ.

Key Terms
المسائل القياسية (al-masail al-qiyasiyya): Issues derived through qiyas (analogy); they are not part of haqiqi fiqh but are included in mudawwan fiqh due to public need.
الفقه الحقيقي (al-fiqh al-haqiqi): Real fiqh: only knowledge of mannus (textually revealed) and ijma'i (consensus) rulings.
الفقه المدون (al-fiqh al-mudawwan): Compiled fiqh: includes qiyasi issues along with mannus and ijma'i, organized for practical use.

The Musawwiba Exposition: How Ijtihad Transforms Speculative Rulings into Definitive
Epistemological status of ijtihad: Musawwiba exposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Musawwiba argument that a ruling becomes qat'i in divine knowledge after ijtihad, and construct the qiyas used to support it.

Core Idea
Speculative path, definitive ruling: Musawwiba holds that ijtihad makes a ruling qat'i in nafs al-amr.

This lesson examines the first exposition of how rulings reached by ijtihad can be considered definitive. According to the Musawwiba school, when a mujtahid's dominant opinion settles on a ruling, that ruling becomes definitively established in Allah's knowledge, and thus in reality. The path (tariqa) to the ruling remains speculative, but the ruling itself becomes qat'i. We will walk through the proof from ijma and mutawatir al-ma'na ahad reports, and the formal qiyas that transforms the speculative into known.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two schools
The passage presents two schools of ijtihad: Musawwiba (all mujtahids are correct) and Ghayr Musawwiba (only one is correct). This lesson focuses on the Musawwiba.
Understand the difference before proceeding.
2
Understand the Musawwiba claim
According to Musawwiba, when a mujtahid's dominant opinion (ghalabat al-zann) settles on a ruling, that ruling becomes definitively established in Allah's knowledge and in reality (nafs al-amr). This is because the Lawgiver has made dominant opinion a valid path to certainty.
3
Examine the proof from consensus and mutawatir al-ma'na
The proof is that the Ummah has consensus that acting upon dominant opinion is obligatory, and ahad reports of mutawatir al-ma'na also indicate this. This implies that when a mujtahid's dominant opinion prevails, the ruling is as if communicated definitively by the Lawgiver.
Note that the evidence itself is not definitive in transmission (ahad) but its meaning is agreed upon.
4
Construct the formal qiyas
The commentator provides a qiyas: It is known that this ruling is speculative for the mujtahid; everything known to be speculative for the mujtahid is known to be definitively established in reality; therefore, this ruling is known to be definitively established in reality. This qiyas transforms the speculative into known after considering the evidence.
Analyze each premise: the major premise is established by the consensus and mutawatir al-ma'na.
5
Address the objection of contradiction
An objection says that a speculative thing cannot become known without contradiction. The reply: (1) speculation is in the path, not the ruling; (2) the statuses are temporally distinct: before qiyas it was speculative, after qiyas it becomes known.
This resolves the apparent contradiction between maznun and ma'lum.

The commentator answered clarifying that what is thought — that rulings are analogical — is not so; rather, in reality these rulings are definitive, but the path by which the mujtahid reaches the ruling is speculative. That is, the mujtahid strives through his dominant opinion to reach the ruling; when he reaches the ruling, it is definitively established, and the path being speculative does not necessitate that the ruling is speculative. For example, a person intends Hajj and wants to go to Mecca, but he does not know the path. He exerts ijtihad and follows a path according to his dominant opinion, and eventually reaches Mecca. The path he chose is speculative, but what was sought was obtained definitively. So he is happy that his speculation led to certainty. Such is the case with analogical rulings: they are definitive, but they involve speculation in the path. This clarifies the third answer. But before this clarification, note that scholars have two schools regarding the ijtihad of a mujtahid: (1) Some scholars hold that every mujtahid's ijtihad is correct and sound, and the ruling established is established in the knowledge of Allah — this is called the Musawwiba sect. (2) Some scholars hold that not every mujtahid is correct; rather, only one mujtahid is correct — this is the view of the majority, called the Ghayr Musawwiba sect, as per the famous saying: 'The mujtahid both errs and is correct.' Now the third answer has two expositions: the first according to Musawwiba, the second according to Ghayr Musawwiba. He presents the first exposition: its summary is that there is consensus of the Ummah that acting upon the dominant opinion of the mujtahid is obligatory, and the ahad reports regarding this are numerous to the extent of reaching the meaning of tawatur. The author's statement that dominant opinion is considered valid in rulings has the same meaning: ahad reports that are mutawatir in meaning indicate that acting upon dominant opinion is obligatory. So when both consensus and ahad reports indicate that dominant opinion in rulings necessitates the obligation of acting upon those rulings, it is as if there is a definitive text from the Lawgiver that when a mujtahid's dominant opinion prevails on a ruling, that ruling becomes definitively established in the knowledge of Allah. Once it is established in Allah's knowledge, that previously speculative ruling now becomes definitive in reality (nafs al-amr) as well; it is no longer speculative. Hence, the word 'knowledge' can be applied to it because that ruling has become definitive in both reality and divine knowledge. This exposition applies when every mujtahid is accepted as correct (as per the view of the Musawwiba). Objection: 'The speculative' — the commentator raises an objection to the previous exposition: How can it be correct that what was speculative becomes known and definitive? This would be a contradiction, because speculative is what admits the possibility of the contrary, while known is what does not admit that possibility. So saying a speculative ruling becomes known and definitive is to assert the union of opposites. Reply: (1) There is no union of opposites here because we have already said that speculation is in the path and definitiveness is in the ruling itself. (2) The summary of the commentator Taftazani's reply is that there is no union of opposites; rather, the two statuses have different times. The ruling was speculative before qiyas and becomes definitive after qiyas. When we consider the qiyas and proof, only then does the speculative ruling become known and definitive. The qiyas is: 'It is known that this ruling is speculative for the mujtahid; and everything that is known to be speculative for the mujtahid is known to be definitively established in reality. Conclusion: This ruling is known to be definitively established in reality.' So the ruling was speculative before considering this qiyas, and became known and definitive after considering the qiyas. Hence, there is no union of opposites. This exposition accords with the school of the Musawwiba. As for the assumption that only one is correct — that is the exposition of the Ghayr Musawwiba. According to them, the exposition is: When it is established by consensus and ahad reports that acting upon the mujtahid's dominant opinion is obligatory, then it is as if the Lawgiver gave a definitive text: 'Every ruling upon which the mujtahid's dominant opinion prevails is definitively obligatory to act upon.' That is, every ruling upon which the mujtahid's dominant opinion prevails, even if it is not definitively established in reality, acting upon it is obligatory, so in terms of the obligation to act, it is definitive (because the obligation from the Lawgiver is definitive). Or it can be said: 'Every ruling upon which the mujtahid's dominant opinion prevails is established with respect to the evidence.' That is, the ruling on which his dominant opinion prevails is definitively established with respect to the evidence, even if it is not established in reality (nafs al-amr) in divine knowledge. Nevertheless, that speculative ruling becomes definitive with respect to the evidence. However, there are objections to the first branch of the second exposition: Since it is known that the mujtahid's speculative [ruling] is obligatory to act upon, then fiqh would be the knowledge of the obligation to act upon rulings, because this evidence establishes the obligation to act, whereas fiqh is knowledge of the rulings themselves, not knowledge of the obligation to act. As for the second branch: a ruling established by a speculative evidence cannot be definitive even with respect to the evidence, because a definitive ruling is only that which is established in reality and admits no possibility of the contrary. Reply: The objection to the first branch is answered by saying that 'obligation to act upon the mujtahid's dominant opinion' means that the mujtahid is certain and asserts that what his dominant opinion indicates as obligatory is indeed obligatory, and what it indicates as prohibited is indeed prohibited. This is itself called knowledge of rulings, because obligation and prohibition are among the rulings, and their knowledge is fiqh. The answer to the second branch is that 'knowledge of rulings' here means knowledge as opposed to speculation, so it includes both definitive and non-definitive rulings. And that is the utmost possible in this.

Commentary
This passage is a super-commentary (hashiya) on the discussion of whether rulings reached by ijtihad are definitive or speculative. The commentator begins by rebutting the misconception that analogical rulings (ahkam qiyasiyya) are purely speculative. He insists that the rulings themselves are definitive (qat'i); only the path (tariqa) by which the mujtahid arrives at them is speculative (zanni). He illustrates with the analogy of a pilgrim heading to Mecca: the route chosen is uncertain, but reaching Mecca is certain. The passage then delineates two classical schools (madhab) on ijtihad: the Musawwiba (all mujtahids are correct) and the Ghayr Musawwiba (only one is correct). For each, the commentator presents an exposition (taqrir) of how a speculative ruling can be considered definitive. According to Musawwiba, consensus (ijma) and mass-transmitted meaning (mutawatir al-ma'na) ahad reports establish that when a mujtahid's dominant opinion (ghalabat al-zann) settles on a ruling, that ruling becomes definitively established in Allah's knowledge, and thus in reality (nafs al-amr). An objection arises: how can a maznun (speculative) become ma'lum (known) without contradiction? Two answers are given: (1) the speculation is in the path, not the ruling; (2) the temporal distinction — before the qiyas (analogical reasoning) it was speculative, after the qiyas it becomes known. A formal qiyas is provided to demonstrate this transformation. For the Ghayr Musawwiba, the exposition is different: the ruling is only definitively obligatory to act upon (wajib al-'amal qat'an) or definitively established with respect to evidence (bil-nazar ila al-dalil), not necessarily in reality. Objections are raised: (a) if fiqh is defined as knowledge of rulings, then this exposition would make it knowledge of obligation to act, rather than of the rulings themselves; (b) a ruling based on speculative evidence cannot be called definitive even relative to evidence. The responses clarify that 'knowledge' here is used in a broader sense, encompassing both definitive and speculative rulings, and that the mujtahid's certainty about the indication of his dominant opinion constitutes 'knowledge of rulings' because obligation and prohibition are part of the rulings. The passage underscores the subtle distinction between the epistemological status of the ruling in itself versus its practical obligation.

The Ghayr Musawwiba Exposition: Definitive Obligation, Speculative Reality
Epistemological status of ijtihad: Ghayr Musawwiba exposition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Ghayr Musawwiba position that a ruling reached by ijtihad is qat'i only in terms of obligation to act (wajib al-amal qat'an), not in reality, and evaluate the objections and replies.

Core Idea
Ghayr Musawwiba: the ruling is qat'i for practice, but may be erroneous in reality.

This lesson presents the second exposition, that of the Ghayr Musawwiba (the majority). While consensus and ahad reports establish that acting upon dominant opinion is obligatory, this does not mean the ruling itself is necessarily correct in reality. Rather, the Lawgiver's command to follow dominant opinion makes the obligation to act definitive. This raises important questions about the nature of fiqh and the scope of 'knowledge'. We will explore the objections and the commentator's replies.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the Ghayr Musawwiba claim
The ruling itself may not be correct in reality (nafs al-amr), but acting upon it is definitively obligatory because the Lawgiver has commanded following dominant opinion.
2
Understand the two sub-expositions
(a) The ruling is qat'i in terms of wujub al-amal (obligation to act). (b) The ruling is qat'i with respect to the evidence (bil-nazar ila al-dalil).
These are two ways of expressing the same basic idea.
3
Analyze the objections
Objection to (a): If fiqh is knowledge of rulings, this would make it knowledge of obligation to act, not the rulings themselves. Objection to (b): A ruling based on speculative evidence cannot be called definitive even relative to evidence.
These objections challenge the coherence of the Ghayr Musawwiba exposition.
4
Study the replies
Reply to (a): The mujtahid's certainty about the indication of his dominant opinion is itself knowledge of rulings because obligation and prohibition are part of rulings. Reply to (b): 'Knowledge of rulings' here is broader, including both definitive and speculative.
The replies defend the Ghayr Musawwiba position.
5
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
Under Ghayr Musawwiba, a later mujtahid can overturn a previous ruling if he disagrees, because the previous ruling may have been erroneous in reality. Under Musawwiba, no overturning.
This practical consequence is crucial for understanding the stakes.

Commentary
This passage is a super-commentary (hashiya) on the discussion of whether rulings reached by ijtihad are definitive or speculative. The commentator begins by rebutting the misconception that analogical rulings (ahkam qiyasiyya) are purely speculative. He insists that the rulings themselves are definitive (qat'i); only the path (tariqa) by which the mujtahid arrives at them is speculative (zanni). He illustrates with the analogy of a pilgrim heading to Mecca: the route chosen is uncertain, but reaching Mecca is certain. The passage then delineates two classical schools (madhab) on ijtihad: the Musawwiba (all mujtahids are correct) and the Ghayr Musawwiba (only one is correct). For each, the commentator presents an exposition (taqrir) of how a speculative ruling can be considered definitive. According to Musawwiba, consensus (ijma) and mass-transmitted meaning (mutawatir al-ma'na) ahad reports establish that when a mujtahid's dominant opinion (ghalabat al-zann) settles on a ruling, that ruling becomes definitively established in Allah's knowledge, and thus in reality (nafs al-amr). An objection arises: how can a maznun (speculative) become ma'lum (known) without contradiction? Two answers are given: (1) the speculation is in the path, not the ruling; (2) the temporal distinction — before the qiyas (analogical reasoning) it was speculative, after the qiyas it becomes known. A formal qiyas is provided to demonstrate this transformation. For the Ghayr Musawwiba, the exposition is different: the ruling is only definitively obligatory to act upon (wajib al-'amal qat'an) or definitively established with respect to evidence (bil-nazar ila al-dalil), not necessarily in reality. Objections are raised: (a) if fiqh is defined as knowledge of rulings, then this exposition would make it knowledge of obligation to act, rather than of the rulings themselves; (b) a ruling based on speculative evidence cannot be called definitive even relative to evidence. The responses clarify that 'knowledge' here is used in a broader sense, encompassing both definitive and speculative rulings, and that the mujtahid's certainty about the indication of his dominant opinion constitutes 'knowledge of rulings' because obligation and prohibition are part of the rulings. The passage underscores the subtle distinction between the epistemological status of the ruling in itself versus its practical obligation.

Four Syllogisms to Prove Certainty of Conjectural Rulings
Presenting four logical forms to demonstrate that qiyasi rulings are certain.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical structure of the syllogisms used to prove that a mujtahid's conjectured ruling is certain and evaluate the premises based on Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A mujtahid's conjectured ruling is certain because acting upon it is obligatory, proven by ijma and mutawatir hadith.

How can rulings derived through qiyas (analogy) be considered certain when they are based on conjecture? This passage introduces a logical approach using four syllogistic forms to establish that the mujtahid's maznun (conjectured ruling) is actually certain knowledge. By combining premises grounded in ijma and khabar ahad mutawatir fi al-ma'na, the argument aims to show that fiqh is a decisive science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
Recognize that the passage addresses an epistemological doubt: how can conjectural rulings (maznun) be certain? The answer uses logical syllogisms to transform conjecture into certainty.
This is a meta-argument about the epistemic status of fiqh rulings.
2
Parse the First Syllogism
The minor premise is: 'The conjectural ruling of the mujtahid is necessarily obligatory to act upon.' The major: 'Every ruling necessarily obligatory to act upon is known with certainty to be Allah's ruling.' The conclusion: 'The conjectured ruling is known with certainty as Allah's ruling.'
The minor is proven by ijma and khabar ahad mutawatir in meaning; the major by the fact that obligation comes only from the Lawgiver.
3
Trace the Second and Third Forms
The second form uses the major of the first as its minor, and adds a major: 'Everything known with certainty to be Allah's ruling is known with certainty.' The third form combines the minor of the first with the conclusion of the second to directly conclude the certainty of the conjectured ruling.
These forms reinforce the same conclusion using different arrangements of premises.
4
Evaluate the Overall Argument
Consider the strength of the premises: Are ijma and khabar ahad mutawatir fi al-ma'na sufficient to prove the obligation? Is the major premise self-evident or does it require proof? The argument's validity depends on accepting these premises.
This logical method is adopted from Aristotelian logic (qiyas mantiqi) into usul al-fiqh by later Hanafi scholars.

Qiyas reveals the ruling; it does not create it—the primary sources are the true establishers.

Commentary
This passage defines the four sources of fiqh: Kitab, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas. It explains that the first three are independent (usul mutlaqa) while Qiyas is both an origin and a branch. The commentator gives two reasons: first, the illa in qiyas is extracted from the primary sources; second, qiyas is merely a revealer (muzhir) of the ruling, not its establisher. Then examples are provided: from the Quran (analogy of liwat to hayd), from Sunnah (riba analogy with wheat and barley), and from Ijma (analogy of the mother of a slave woman to the mother of a free wife, extended to the mother of a woman fornicated with).

Key Terms
اصل (asl): A source or foundation of fiqh.
فرع (far'): A branch derived from an origin.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical deduction, a source of fiqh that is derivative.
علة (illa): The effective cause that grounds the analogy.
مظهر (muzhir): Revealer or manifestor of a ruling, not its originator.

Qiyas: A Derivative Principle, Not Absolute
Explaining that qiyas is subordinate to the three principles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why qiyas is considered a branch (far') of the three primary sources and identify the two proofs given for this subordination.

Core Idea
Qiyas reveals rulings already implicit in the Qur'an, Sunnah, or Ijma; it does not establish new rulings independently.

In usul al-fiqh, the Qur'an, Sunnah, and Ijma are absolute, independent sources. Qiyas, though a valid source, is not on par with them. This passage explains that qiyas is a relative principle: it is a source in one aspect but a branch in another. The author provides two proofs and illustrates with three concrete examples, showing how the effective cause ('illa) is always derived from the primary sources.

2

3

The second proof asserts that qiyas is a muzhir (revealer) of the ruling, not a muthbit (establisher). The real establisher is the primary evidence. This reinforces qiyas's subordinate role.

So their prohibition is established by ijma. The 'illa of this prohibition is juz'iyyah (part-whole relationship) and ba'diyyah (relationship of being a part), which is called hurmat al-musaharah (prohibition due to affinity). Then this same 'illa is found in the mother of a fornicated-with woman, so the prohibition is established: the fornicator cannot marry the mother of the fornicated-with woman. The author mentioned this under the title of comparing unlawful intercourse to lawful intercourse to establish prohibition due to affinity: unlawful intercourse means fornication with a stranger woman, lawful means intercourse with one's own slave-concubine. Now that lawful intercourse establishes prohibition due to affinity, so unlawful intercourse also establishes prohibition due to affinity because of the 'illa. He attributed this example to the usuliyyun because there is a problem in this example: someone could say that the prohibition of the mother of the slave-concubine is not from ijma but from dalalat al-nass (indication of the text), and similarly the prohibition of the mother of the fornicated-with woman is not from qiyas but from dalalat al-nass. To avoid this criticism, he attributed the example to the usuliyyun so that he would be free of responsibility.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion on the nature of qiyas in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author explains that qiyas is not an independent, absolute principle like the Qur'an, Sunnah, and Ijma. Rather, it is a principle in one sense (because it outwardly provides rulings) but a branch in another (because its effective cause, the 'illa, is derived from the primary sources). The passage provides two proofs: first, the 'illa in qiyas is taken from the three principles, so the ruling actually originates from them; second, qiyas is a 'muzhir' (revealer) of the ruling, not a 'muthbit' (establisher). The author then illustrates with three concrete examples: (1) from the Qur'an: analogy of sodomy to menstrual intercourse based on the harmful nature; (2) from the Sunnah: analogy of selling gypsum to selling wheat on the basis of genus and measure to prohibit riba; (3) from Ijma: analogy of the mother of a fornicated woman to the mother of a slave-concubine based on the principle of hurmat al-musaharah. Finally, the author addresses a potential objection regarding the third example, noting that the ruling might be derived from textual indication rather than consensus, but by attributing the example to the usul scholars, he avoids the controversy. This passage reinforces the Hanafi view that qiyas is a valid but subordinate source.

Key Terms
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning in which a ruling from an original case (asl) is extended to a new case (far') due to a shared effective cause ('illa).
اصل مطلق (asl mutlaq): An independent, complete principle that establishes rulings directly without reliance on another source.
علة ('illa): The effective cause or ratio legis that is the basis for a legal ruling and is used in qiyas.

The Hanafi Position on Qiyas: Absolute or Derived Source?
A dispute in usul al-fiqh: whether qiyas is an absolute source like the three primary sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on qiyas as a source of law and explain why it is not considered an absolute source (asl mutlaq), using the definition and the first objection-response.

Core Idea
Qiyas is a source in one respect but a branch of the three primary sources — not absolute because it depends on them for its authority and the 'illa.

In Hanafi usul, the sources of law are ranked. The Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma are absolute sources (usul mutlaqah) — independent and not derived from anything else. Qiyas, or analogical reasoning, is different: although rulings are often attributed to it, it ultimately depends on those three for its 'illa and the ruling itself. This lesson unpacks that distinction by examining the definition of an absolute source, the Hanafi position, and a key objection from those who argue qiyas should be considered absolute as well.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three absolute sources and their characteristics
From the passage, note that Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma are called 'absolute sources' because they are independent proofs (adillah mustaqillah) and are not branches of anything else. They establish rulings directly.
2
Define 'absolute source' (asl mutlaq) in Hanafi usul
Use the legal definition dissector: an absolute source is something that is not derived from another legal source; it is foundational. The passage contrasts this with qiyas, which is described as 'a source in one respect and a branch in another'.
The key condition is independence from other sources for authority.
3
State the Hanafi position on qiyas
According to Taftazani and the Hanafi school, qiyas is not an absolute source. It is a source because rulings are ostensibly attributed to it, but it is a branch because it depends on the three primary sources for the derivation of the 'illa and the ruling itself. Its effect is only to manifest a hidden ruling or extend it, not to create an original ruling.
4
Present the first objection and the Hanafi response
The objector argues that qiyas should be absolute because something (the ruling) is built upon it, and being a branch does not disqualify it, using the analogy of a father who is an absolute source for his son yet himself a branch of his own father. The Hanafi response: the analogy fails because in usul, an absolute source must be independent in authority; qiyas derives its authority from the primary sources, whereas a father's authority over his son is not derived from his own father in the same way.
The passage only contains the response to the first objection; the other four objections are listed but not answered here.
5
Recognize the practical implication of this classification
Understanding that qiyas is not an absolute source affects how rulings are prioritized. A ruling from qiyas cannot override a clear text from the primary sources. This hierarchy is crucial in ijtihad and fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute regarding the classification of qiyas (analogical reasoning) as a source of law in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The commentator, Taftazani, explains that the three primary sources—Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma—are absolute sources (usul mutlaqah) because they are independent and not derived from anything else. In contrast, qiyas is a derived source: it is a source in some respects (because rulings are ostensibly attributed to it) and a branch in others (because it depends on the primary sources for the 'illa and the ruling). He then presents five objections against this view, along with the Hanafi response to the first objection. 

The objections argue that qiyas should be considered an absolute source. The first objection claims that the definition of an absolute source does not require that it not be a branch; the example of a father being an absolute source for his son, yet himself a branch of his own father, is used. The Hanafi response, which begins at the end of the passage, refutes this by pointing out that the analogy is flawed. In usul, the primary sources are not dependent on anything else for their authority, whereas qiyas derives its authority and the ruling from them. The father example does not capture this dependency. The remaining objections (second through fifth) are presented but not answered in this passage; they involve arguments about proximate vs. remote causes, the necessity of separate mention, the role of qiyas in extending rulings, and the parallel with ijma. 

The passage underscores the Hanafi position that qiyas is not an independent source but a method of extending rulings from established sources. This distinction is crucial for understanding the hierarchy of evidence in Hanafi legal theory.

Key Terms
اصل مطلق (asl mutlaq): An absolute source of law that is independent and not a branch of another source.
قیاس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning that extends a ruling from an original case (asl) to a new case (far') based on a shared 'illa.
سبب قریب (sabab qarib): A proximate cause that directly leads to a ruling without any intermediary.

The Hanafi Classification of Qiyas: Absolute or Weak Principle?
Responds to five objections against qiyas being an absolute principle

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school classifies Qiyas as a weak principle (asl da'eef) and evaluate the five objections raised against this classification.

Core Idea
Qiyas is not an absolute principle; its principality (asaliyyah) is weak, dependent on the three sources.

In this passage, the commentator of Al-Tawdih wa Al-Talwih addresses five objections from opponents who argue that Qiyas should be considered an absolute principle, just like the Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma. The Hanafi position is that the term 'asl' applies to strong individuals (the three primary sources) by way of strength and to Qiyas by way of weakness. The distinction hinges on the strength of asaliyyah (principality), not on whether Qiyas is a branch or establishes rulings. We will unpack each objection and the Hanafi response, sharpening our understanding of source hierarchy in Usul al-Fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first objection and its basis
The first objection claims that Qiyas is an absolute principle because it is that upon which a ruling (the analogized case) is based. The opponent defines absolute principle as 'that upon which something else is based'.
This is a common-sense definition, but the Hanafis refine it.
2
Trace the Hanafi response to the first objection
The Hanafi response rejects the simplistic definition. It explains that 'asl' is a general term (kulli mushtarak) that applies to individuals with varying strength. The Quran is a strong individual; Qiyas is a weak individual. Weakness in asaliyyah is the criterion, not being a branch.
The passage emphasizes: 'مدار اصل کامل اور اصل ناقص ہونے کا فرعیت نہیں ہے بلکہ اصل کے کلی مسلک ہونے پر ہے'.
3
Analyse the father analogy (الاب)
The opponent analogizes Qiyas to a father: a father is both a branch of his own father and a principle for his son. Similarly, Qiyas can be a branch of the three principles yet still an absolute principle. The Hanafi response shows this analogy is invalid (qiyas ma'a al-fariq) because a father is absolute in his fatherhood (independent in that aspect) but a branch in his external existence. Qiyas, however, is dependent in its very principality.
This distinction is crucial: being a branch in existence does not affect being a principle in one's own right, but Qiyas is dependent in its principality.
4
Examine the objections about separate mention and change of ruling
The third objection says that even if Qiyas is weak, it is unnecessary to mention it separately. The Hanafi response argues that separate mention highlights its weakness. The fourth objection says Qiyas changes a ruling from particular to general, demonstrating its power to establish rulings, so it should be absolute. The Hanafi response counters that establishing a ruling does not imply strong asaliyyah.
The fourth objection is answered implicitly: the function of Qiyas is to extend rulings, but its principality remains weak.
5
Evaluate the fifth objection and the core criterion
The fifth objection says that if dependency disqualifies Qiyas, then Ijma (consensus) should also be disqualified because it often depends on Khabar Wahid. The Hanafi response implies that the criterion is not dependency alone but the strength of asaliyyah. Ijma, once formed, becomes a strong principle with qat'i authority, whereas Qiyas always remains zanni and dependent.
Thus, the core criterion is the strength of asaliyyah: strong individuals are absolute, weak individuals are not.

This is why qiyas is mentioned separately and distinctly, so that its weakness in principality becomes clear. And as for the father: Here begins the answer to the opponent's analogy. The opponent said that just as a father's being a branch of his own father does not prevent him from being a complete principle, similarly qiyas's being a branch of the three principles does not prevent it from being an absolute principle. The answer is that analogizing qiyas to a father is an analogy with a difference, because the father has two aspects: first, his external existence; second, his principality and fatherhood for the son. The famous notion that a father is a branch of his father applies only to the first aspect, i.e., his external existence, because the cause of his existence is his father; he was created from his sperm. As for his being a principle for his son, in terms of fatherhood and principality over the child, the father is not a branch of his father, because in fatherhood for the child, the father is independent without the intervention of his father. His father was a cause for him in terms of marriage with the wife, then intercourse with her, then the arrival of the sperm into the womb of his wife. Consider: in all these things—marriage, intercourse, and reaching the sperm to the womb—the father's father has no involvement. Therefore, the father in his fatherhood and principality over the child is an absolute and complete principle, not a branch of anyone. However, in his external existence, he is a branch of his own father. Our discussion is with respect to existence in the external.

Commentary
This passage is a Sharh (commentary) on Al-Tawdih wa Al-Talwih, addressing a series of objections raised against the Hanafi position that Qiyas (analogical reasoning) is not an absolute principle (Asl Mutlaq) but rather a weak principle. The discussion is part of the debate on the hierarchy of legal sources in Usul al-Fiqh. The author presents five objections (اعتراضات) from opponents who argue that Qiyas should be considered an absolute principle. The third objection: even if Qiyas is weak relative to the three principles (Kitab, Sunnah, Ijma), it is unnecessary to mention it separately; the author refutes by showing that in any classification, weaker types are not always isolated. The fourth objection: because Qiyas changes a ruling from particular to general, it is an absolute principle; the author counters that this change requires establishment of the ruling in the branch, which Qiyas does, but that does not make it absolute—only its function. The fifth objection: if dependency disqualifies Qiyas, then Ijma, which depends on Khabar Wahid, would also be disqualified; the author implies that the criterion is not dependency but strength of asaliyyah (principality). Then the author answers the first objection directly: the claim that 'absolute principle' means 'that upon which something is based' is rejected; rather, 'principle' (asl) is a general term (kulli mushtarak) that applies to strong individuals (like the three principles) by way of strength and to weak individuals (like Qiyas) by way of weakness. Thus, Qiyas is not excluded because it is a branch, but because it is weak in its asaliyyah. Finally, the analogy of 'father' (اب) is addressed: a father is a branch in his external existence but absolute in his fatherhood; thus the analogy with Qiyas is invalid (qiyas ma'a al-fariq). The entire passage demonstrates the Hanafi method of defending their hierarchy of sources through precise definition and logical argumentation.

Why Qiyas is Not an Absolute Principle (Asl Mutlaq)
Defending the weakness of qiyas in principality relative to Quran, Sunnah, Ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi criterion for an absolute principle and evaluate why qiyas does not meet it.

Core Idea
An absolute principle is independent in its asalah; qiyas is weak and dependent, so it is not an absolute principle.

In this passage, the Hanafi commentator defends the established position that qiyas (analogy) is not an absolute principle (asl mutlaq) like the Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma. The discussion centers on the concept of 'asalah' (principality) and the gradation (tashkik) in the application of the term 'asl'. The author refutes four objections that attempt to elevate qiyas to the status of an absolute principle, using rational arguments and careful distinctions.

Guided Path
1
Define 'absolute principle' (asl mutlaq)
Understand that an absolute principle is independent in its asalah — it does not rely on any other source for its authority. The three primary sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) are absolute; qiyas is not.
The term 'asl' is generic (kulli mushtakk) and applies by strength or weakness.
2
Explain gradation (tashkik) in 'asl'
Recognize that 'asl' is not univocal; some instances are strong (primary sources) and some weak (qiyas). The criterion is independence in the meaning of asalah, not merely being a branch in existence.
A father is independent in his fatherhood even though he is a son to his own father; qiyas is dependent in its very principality.
3
Examine the father analogy and its refutation
The objector analogizes qiyas to a father: a father is a perfect principle despite being a son. The Hanafi response shows the analogy fails because a father's fatherhood is independent, while qiyas's principality is not.
This is called qiyas ma'a al-fariq (analogy with a difference).
4
Analyze the proximate cause argument
The objector claims qiyas is a proximate cause (sabab qarib) for its ruling. The Hanafi response distinguishes between a cause that directly affects the ruling (primary sources) and a mere manifestation (qiyas). Qiyas does not establish the ruling; it only manifests it.
A proximate cause must influence the ruling directly; qiyas does not.
5
Synthesize the hierarchy of sources
Understand that in Hanafi usul, the primary sources are absolute and qiyas is secondary. Qiyas is mentioned separately to highlight its weakness. Changing a ruling from specific to general does not require qiyas; the primary sources can establish the ruling directly.
Taqrir (establishment) of a ruling does not depend on qiyas.

The answer is that we never claimed that being unconditional and perfect requires not being a branch. We did not say that an absolute principle is one that is not a branch of something else, nor did we exclude qiyas from being an absolute principle because it is a branch of the three principles. The summary is that being a branch or not has nothing to do with the concept of an absolute principle. Rather, we excluded qiyas from being an absolute principle because 'principle' is a generic term that applies to some individuals by strength and to others by weakness. One individual of 'principle' is that which is independent in its principality and also independent in building others upon it, not needing others, like the Book of Allah. Another individual is that which is not independent in its principality; in terms of meaning it needs something else, in the sense that the branch of the principle is actually based on that thing, not on itself. For example, qiyas in its principality needs the three principles, and its branch, i.e., the established ruling, actually depends on those three principles. Now it is clear that the first individual of 'principle' is strong and the second is weak. The word 'principle' applies to the first with strength and to the second with weakness. For this reason, we said that the three principles are absolute and perfect principles, meaning that the word 'principle' applies to them by strength, and qiyas is not a perfect principle, meaning that the word 'principle' applies to it by weakness. So the criterion for being a perfect or defective principle is not being a branch, but rather that the principle is a generic category. What is strong is called absolute principle, what is weak is not called absolute and perfect, hence it is not included in the absolute principle. That is why qiyas is mentioned separately and distinctly so that its weakness in principality is clear. As for the father, here begins the answer to the objector's analogy. The objector said that just as a son being a branch of his father does not prevent the father from being a perfect principle, similarly qiyas being a branch of the three principles does not prevent it from being an absolute principle. The answer is that comparing qiyas to the father is an analogy with a difference (qiyas ma'a al-fariq) because the father has two aspects: first, his external existence; second, his principality and fatherhood (ubuwah) for the son. It is well-known that a son is a branch of his father in terms of the first aspect, i.e., external existence, because his existence is caused by his father from his sperm. As for the father being a principle for his son, in terms of fatherhood and being the origin of the child, the father is not a branch of his own father because in fatherhood for the son, the father is independent without the input of his own father. Because he becomes a principle for his own father? Actually, the father is a principle for his son by means of marriage with the wife, then intercourse, then the arrival of the sperm into the womb of his wife. Now consider: in marriage, intercourse, and the arrival of the sperm into the womb, the grandfather has no input. Therefore, the father in his fatherhood and being the origin of the child is an absolute and perfect principle, not a branch of anyone. However, in his external existence, he is a branch of his own father. Our discussion is not about external existence but about fatherhood and principality. And qiyas is not a branch of the three principles in terms of its existence, but rather in terms of its principality, i.e., the meaning of being a principle. So comparing qiyas to the father is an analogy with a difference. Regarding the second objection: the proximate cause, etc. The answer to the second objection is that your comparing qiyas to a proximate cause is also an analogy with a difference. Your objection was based on the idea that qiyas is a proximate cause (sabab qarib) for its ruling, and a proximate cause is an absolute principle for the ruling, so qiyas would also be an absolute principle. We say that qiyas is not a proximate cause for its ruling because a proximate cause is that which directly and without intermediary affects and establishes the ruling (law/al-hukm). A remote cause is that which affects indirectly, i.e., its effect is through an intermediary and proximate cause, then through that to the branch. A remote cause does not directly affect the branch. So it is obvious that a proximate cause is more deserving in meaning of causality and principality, i.e., it is a principle and cause more deserving than a remote cause. But our discussion is about qiyas, and qiyas would be proven an absolute principle only if it is first proven to be a proximate cause, whereas qiyas is not a proximate cause for its ruling at all because a proximate cause affects the ruling, and qiyas does not affect or establish its ruling, let alone being an effective proximate cause. Rather, it is merely a manifestation (muzhir) of the ruling; the actual influence and establishment is from the three principles. Qiyas only manifests. Therefore, qiyas cannot be a proximate cause for its ruling. When it is not a proximate cause, how can it be an absolute principle? Regarding the third objection: we do not concede. However, sometimes the major premise or universal conditional is not mentioned in usul al-fiqh at all. For example: 'This ruling is obligatory because qiyas indicates its obligation, and every ruling that qiyas indicates its obligation is obligatory.' Now this major premise is not mentioned anywhere in usul al-fiqh. So how can it be called usul al-fiqh? And how can it be correct to say that this universal proposition (major premise) is prepared in usul al-fiqh? Answer! The author is responding that there are two ways in which these universal propositions are mentioned in usul al-fiqh: (1) Sometimes the universal proposition which serves as the major premise or the universal conditional is exactly mentioned in usul al-fiqh. (2) Sometimes it is not exactly mentioned in usul al-fiqh, but implicitly it enters into usul al-fiqh by being included under another universal proposition. Therefore, it is correct to say that it is included in usul al-fiqh and mentioned therein. For example, this universal proposition: 'Whatever qiyas indicates is obligatory in a case of dispute establishes obligation' (the restriction 'in a case of dispute is incidental; it has no relation to the purpose because qiyas occurs only in disputed cases). This universal conditional is not exactly present in usul al-fiqh. However, it is included in another universal proposition that is broader, and that is exactly mentioned in usul al-fiqh, which is: 'Whenever qiyas indicates the establishment of any ruling with this characteristic, the ruling is established' (with 'this characteristic' referring to the conditions of the ruling). Now the first universal conditional ('Whatever qiyas indicates obligation establishes obligation') is included in this universal proposition because this universal proposition contains the word 'every ruling', and this word 'ruling' includes all its types: obligation, recommendation, etc. So the first universal proposition which mentioned obligation becomes included in this universal 'every ruling'. As if instead of saying 'Whenever qiyas indicates the establishment of every ruling with this characteristic, the ruling is established', it is said: 'Whenever qiyas indicates obligation, obligation is established; whenever qiyas indicates recommendation, recommendation is established; whenever qiyas indicates prohibition, prohibition is established'. All these propositions are individuals of that universal proposition, so they are included in it. Hence it will be said that this qiyas (universal conditional) which is serving as the major premise in the two premises of the proof is mentioned in usul al-fiqh by way of implication. Thus the objection is repelled.

Commentary
This passage addresses an objection regarding the presence of universal propositions in the science of usul al-fiqh. The objection states that in qiyas (analogical reasoning), the major premise or conditional proposition is said to be part of usul al-fiqh, yet examples like 'every ruling indicated by qiyas is obligatory' are not explicitly found in usul books. The answer explains two possibilities: either the proposition is directly mentioned, or it is implicitly included under a broader universal proposition. The broader proposition 'كلما دل القياس على ثبوت كل حكم هذا شأنه يثبت الحكم' covers all rulings (obligation, recommendation, prohibition, etc.), so the specific one is included by implication (بطريق التضمن). Thus, the objection fails.

Core Idea
A single proposition has seven names based on its role in proof — understanding these is key to Istinbat.

The author's words: 'meaning by propositions.' Know that the complete composite (al-murakkab al-tamm) — the purpose of the commentator on Tawdih is the text; in the matn the word qadiyya was mentioned. Explaining it, he says: The composite utterance (murakkab khabari) that is subject to truth and falsehood has approximately seven names depending on different considerations and aspects. If the composite utterance is considered in terms of containing a judgment, it is called (1) qadiyya; if considered in terms of having potential for truth and falsehood, it is called (2) khabar; if considered as communicating a judgment, it is called (3) ikhbar; if considered that the composite utterance becomes a part of a proof, it is called (4) muqaddima; if considered that the complete composite is sought through a proof, it is called (5) matlub; if considered that it is obtained from a proof, it is called (6) natija; and if considered that this complete composite is questioned and occurs in teaching, it is called (7) mas'ala. So the essence is one (complete composite), but due to differing considerations, the expressions and names differ. And in a qadiyya there is a mahkum alayh (subject of judgment) and a hukm (judgment). The mahkum alayh is called mawdu (subject) and the mahkum bihi (predicate) is called mahmul (predicate). The subject of the matlub is called asghar (minor term) and the predicate is called akbar (major term). A proof (dalil) necessarily consists of two premises. In these two premises, one will contain the minor term and the other the major term. The one containing the minor is called sughra (minor premise) and the one containing the major is called kubra (major premise). In both premises, one thing is repeated; that thing is called hadd awsat (middle term). The form (hay'a) obtained from combining the two premises is called shakl (figure). Then according to the middle term, four figures arise; they are called ashkal arba'a (four figures). The commentator explains these four figures: (1) If the middle term is predicate in the minor premise and subject in the major, it is the first figure. (2) If the opposite — subject in minor and predicate in major — it is the fourth figure. (3) If the middle term is predicate in both, it is the third figure. (4) If the middle term is subject in both, it is the second figure. For example, 'the Hajj is obligatory, because it is commanded by the Lawgiver (minor premise), and everything that is commanded by the Lawgiver is obligatory (major premise).' In this example, 'the Hajj is obligatory' is the conclusion and the matlub. The two premises are: 'Because the Hajj is commanded by the Lawgiver' and 'Everything commanded by the Lawgiver is obligatory'. Commentary
This passage presents a critical debate between Taftazani (the commentator on Al-Tawdih) and the original author Sadr al-Shariah. The author had made a statement about the meaning of 'thubut al-hukm' and whether the proofs establish the ruling itself or our knowledge of it. Taftazani objects that the author's discussion presupposes that the shar'i proofs are muthbit (affirmative) of the ruling in reality, whereas the correct view is that they are merely mu'arrafat (definitions) and amarat (indicators) — they point to knowledge of the ruling, not its actual existence. Therefore, the author's elaborate interpretations (tawil) about thubut are useless. However, the text then provides a defense: even if we accept that the proofs are muthbit, the meaning of dalil is to give knowledge of thubut, not thubut itself. So the author's interpretation is still valid. The defense argues that the objection is invalid because the author's concern was about knowledge of the ruling, not its ontological establishment. The passage concludes that the author ultimately adopted the same position as the defense, generalizing 'ilm to cover certain and conjectural knowledge. The key Hanafi position is that the shar'i proofs are not creative causes (asbab) of rulings in reality; rather, the rulings are directly from Allah. The proofs are signs that lead us to knowledge of the rulings. This discussion exemplifies a common inter-school and intra-school debate about the nature of divine commands and their cognition.

Key Terms
ادلہ شرعیہ (adillah shar'iyyah): The proofs of Islamic law; according to this text, they are indicators (amarat) of rulings, not affirmative causes.
مثبت (muthbit): That which establishes the actual existence of a ruling in reality.
معرف (mu'arraf): That which defines or indicates something, leading to knowledge of it.
علم ثبوت الحكم (ilm thubut al-hukm): Knowledge of the establishment of a ruling, which is what the proofs provide.

Key Takeaway
Shari' proofs are mu'arrafat, not muthbitat; they give knowledge of the ruling, not its existence.
