Defending 'bi al-istidlal' in the Definition of Fiqh
Refuting the claim that 'bi al-istidlal' is redundant in fiqh definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Ibn al-Hajib's addition of 'bi al-istidlal' is not redundant and identify the type of knowledge it excludes.

Core Idea
The term 'bi al-istidlal' excludes knowledge gained without human inference, such as angelic inspiration.

In defining fiqh, scholars debated the necessity of the phrase 'bi al-istidlal' (by inference). While some argued it was redundant given the earlier restriction 'min adillatiha al-tafṣiliyya', Taftazani clarifies that it serves a distinct purpose: to ensure that only knowledge derived through human inferential reasoning qualifies as fiqh, excluding non-inferential knowledge like that of angels.

Guided Path
1
State the issue
Identify the contested point: whether 'bi al-istidlal' is redundant in the definition of fiqh.
2
Present the opposing view
Understand the author of al-Tawdih's argument: he thinks 'bi al-istidlal' is redundant because 'min adillatiha' already excludes the muqallid.
3
Explain the Hanafi defense
Taftazani argues that 'bi al-istidlal' excludes a different category: non-inferential knowledge (e.g., angelic knowledge), not the muqallid who is already excluded.
4
Determine the practical effect
Without 'bi al-istidlal', the definition would include knowledge obtained through direct inspiration, diluting the concept of fiqh as human reasoning.
5
Conclude the tarjih
Taftazani's view is relied upon, affirming that 'bi al-istidlal' is necessary and not redundant.

The Generic Definition of Usul al-Fiqh
Definition of usul al-fiqh as a specific science

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of usul al-fiqh as a specific science and explain the meaning of each component.

Core Idea
Usul al-fiqh is the science of rules that guide the verified derivation of fiqh — it is the method, not the product.

After introducing usul al-fiqh as a relational term (the proofs of fiqh), the author of al-Tawdih now defines it as a proper science. This generic definition is foundational: it tells us what usul al-fiqh is as a discipline — the knowledge of the qawa'id (rules) by which fiqh is derived with verification (tahqiq). Understanding this definition is the first step in grasping the entire subject.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Identify the exact Arabic sentence that defines usul al-fiqh: 'علم اصول الفقه هو العلم بالقواعد التي يتوصل بها الى الفقه على وجه التحقيق'.
This is the generic definition, as opposed to the relational one.
2
Break down the components
Recognize the four key elements: 'علم' (knowledge), 'بالقواعد' (the rules), 'يتوصل بها الى الفقه' (by which one reaches fiqh), and 'على وجه التحقيق' (with verification).
Each element has a technical meaning in Hanafi usul.
3
Understand 'qawa'id' and 'tahqiq'
Note that 'qawa'id' are general propositions (qadaya) that serve as tools, and 'tahqiq' indicates that the derivation is sound and certain — not mere guesswork.
Hanafis emphasize tahqiq to ensure rulings are firmly grounded.
4
Distinguish from fiqh
Realize that usul al-fiqh is the methodology (knowledge of rules), while fiqh is the product (the specific rulings).
A common confusion is to treat usul as a branch of fiqh.

The Need for Fiqh and Usul al-Fiqh
Explains the necessity for fiqh and usul al-fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of fiqh and usul al-fiqh, explain the need for each, and distinguish usul al-fiqh from dialectics.

Core Idea
Every action has a ruling; usul al-fiqh provides the principles to derive it.

This passage explains the foundational need for both fiqh (knowledge of detailed rulings) and usul al-fiqh (the methodology to derive them). The commentator clarifies that man is not created without purpose; each act is linked to a divine ruling. Since we cannot know every particular ruling directly, we rely on general principles that guide the inference from evidence. This sets the stage for understanding the role of usul al-fiqh as the tool for jurists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the purpose of creation
According to the passage, man is not created in vain; every action has a ruling from the Lawgiver.
This establishes the fundamental need for knowing rulings.
2
Recognize the limitation of direct knowledge
Encompassing all particular rulings is impossible (متعذر). Therefore, we need a method to derive rulings when needed.
3
Understand fiqh as the solution
Fiqh is the knowledge of detailed rulings (ahkam tafsiliyya) derived from evidence. It answers 'what is the ruling?' for each action.
Fiqh is the product, not the method.
4
See how usul al-fiqh emerges
Jurists classified evidence into four types and rulings into six, then formulated general principles about how to infer rulings from evidence in a general way (استدلال إجمالي). These principles, with conditions and methods, were codified as usul al-fiqh.
Usul al-fiqh provides the tools (qawa'id) for deriving fiqh.
5
Note the boundary with dialectics
The author added the condition 'ala wajh al-tahqiq' to exclude the science of dialectics (ilm al-jadal) because it does not directly lead to fiqh.
This demarcation ensures usul al-fiqh remains a practical methodology.

Analysis of 'Tawassul' in the Definition of Usul al-Fiqh
Analyzing the two possibilities of 'mutawassil' in the definition of usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two interpretations of 'tawassul' in the Hanafi definition of usul al-fiqh and explain the implications for the scope of the discipline.

Core Idea
The term 'tawassul' in usul al-fiqh can refer either exclusively to the mujtahid or inclusively to both mujtahid and muqallid, affecting the definition and content of the discipline.

This passage delves into the subtle yet significant word 'tawassul' (the means of reaching) in the classical definition of usul al-fiqh. Who exactly is the one who 'reaches' fiqh through these principles? The answer determines whether usul al-fiqh is exclusively for mujtahids or also serves muqallids in their understanding of rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two interpretations
Recognize that the first interpretation restricts 'mutawassil' to the mujtahid, while the second expands it to both mujtahid and muqallid.
2
Examine reasoning behind first interpretation
Understand that fiqh is defined as knowing rulings from the four proofs, which only a mujtahid can do.
The muqallid obtains rulings from the mujtahid's statement, not the four proofs.
3
Examine reasoning behind second interpretation
Note that the muqallid also uses a proof (the statement of a mujtahid), which can be considered part of usul.
This requires generalizing 'adilla' beyond the four proofs.
4
Identify required modifications
Under the second view, 'adilla' must be generalized and 'masa'il' added before 'fiqh' in the definition.
The definition becomes: 'yutawassalu biha ila masa'il al-fiqh'.
5
Consider practical consequence
The second view justifies including topics like taqlid and istifta in usul al-fiqh books.
This explains why some later scholars included such chapters.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Tawdih examines the word 'tawassul' (the means of reaching) in the definition of usul al-fiqh. After explaining the qawa'id (rules), the text investigates the meaning of 'yutawassalu biha' (by which one reaches). The author presents two probabilities regarding who is the 'mutawassil' (the one who reaches fiqh). The first view restricts it to the mujtahid only. This is because usul al-fiqh deals with universal rules that allow the mujtahid to derive rulings directly from the four proofs (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas). The muqallid does not use these proofs; he obtains his rulings from the statements of a mujtahid. Therefore, discussions of taqlid and istifta are not included in the standard Hanafi texts of usul al-fiqh. The second view, introduced by 'wa la yub'adu an yuqala' (it is not far-fetched), broadens the meaning to include both the mujtahid and the muqallid. In this case, two modifications to the definition are necessary: (a) 'adilla' (proofs) should not be restricted to the four proofs but should include any source from which one obtains knowledge of rulings, such as the statement of a mujtahid for the muqallid. (b) The word 'masa'il' (issues) should be added before 'fiqh', so the definition becomes 'yutawassalu biha ila masa'il al-fiqh' (by which one reaches the issues of fiqh), to accommodate both the mujtahid deriving general rulings and the muqallid learning specific issues. The passage provides an example of a muqallid who says, 'This ruling is established in my view because the opinion of Abu Hanifa has been conveyed to me, and everything conveyed from Abu Hanifa is established for me.' The second proposition (the general rule about relying on Abu Hanifa's opinion) is considered part of usul al-fiqh. Some scholars, therefore, include taqlid and ifta topics in usul al-fiqh works.

Why Sources Precede Rulings in Usul al-Fiqh: Methodological Priority
Justifying the order of topics in usul al-fiqh with proofs and an analogy from logic.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two reasons given by Taftazani for placing source discussions before ruling discussions in usul al-fiqh and apply the analogy from logic to understand the primary and secondary subjects of usul.

Core Idea
Sources have intrinsic priority and greater importance; usul al-fiqh is primarily about sources; rulings are secondary corollaries.

This passage from the commentary on al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih explains a foundational methodological choice in usul al-fiqh: why do we study the sources (adilla) before the rulings (ahkam)? The commentator Taftazani provides two proofs and an elegant analogy from logic to justify this ordering. Understanding this orientation is essential for grasping the structure of any classical usul text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
Recognize that the author states rulings are discussed after sources. This is the target of Taftazani's justification.
The claim is about the order of topics in the composition of usul works.
2
Examine Taftazani's two proofs
First proof: sources have intrinsic priority (taqaddum dhati) over rulings, so they should be placed first. Second proof: source discussions are more important (ahamm) than ruling discussions.
Intrinsic priority means sources are naturally prior; greater importance means they deserve more emphasis.
3
Understand the logic analogy
In logic, the primary subject is the 'conveyer' (muwassil) — concepts and affirmations that lead to knowledge. The 'conveyed-to' (muwassal ilayh) — the unknown concepts — is discussed only secondarily.
The analogy shows that the primary focus is on the tools of conveyance, not the final objects.
4
Apply the analogy to usul
In usul al-fiqh, the sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) are the conveyers; the rulings (ahkam) are the conveyed-to. Hence sources are the primary subject.
This mirrors the logic example: usul is primarily about how sources convey rulings, not an exhaustive list of rulings.
5
Recognize practical implication
Most discussions in usul focus on the sources; rulings are discussed only occasionally as corollaries. This helps set expectations when reading usul texts.
Students should not expect a fiqh manual; usul is about the methodology of deriving rulings.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion probe explaining why discussions of sources (adilla) precede discussions of rulings (ahkam) in usul al-fiqh. The commentator Taftazani provides two justifications: (1) the sources have intrinsic priority (taqaddum dhati) over rulings, so they should be placed first in composition, and (2) source discussions are more important. The author then draws an analogy with logic, where the primary subject is the 'conveyer' (muwassil) — i.e., concepts and affirmations that lead to knowledge — while the 'conveyed-to' (muwassal ilayh) — i.e., the unknown concepts — is discussed only secondarily. Similarly, in usul al-fiqh, the main focus is on the sources (the conveyers), and rulings (the conveyed-to) are discussed as corollaries. Taftazani elaborates that in logic, topics like definition, description, genus, species, properties, and argument forms are all studied in relation to their role in conveying. Occasionally, discussions about the conveyed-to (e.g., whether a concept is simple or composite) arise but are secondary. This clarifies the methodological structure of usul al-fiqh as a discipline primarily concerned with the sources of Islamic law.

In the first chapter, it will be explained how the Book conveys meaning. The second chapter is how the legal ruling is established from the Book. 'Know' – the author, clarifying his speech, says: The original purpose is the conveyance of effective meaning (muhkam) from the Book, but since the conveyance of a legal ruling depends on the conveyance of meaning, because first the meaning is known, then the legal ruling is derived from it, therefore it is necessary first to mention the discussions related to the conveyance of meaning from the Book of Allah, and afterwards the discussion will be on the conveyance of legal ruling. Hence, in the first chapter, discussions on specific ('amm), general ('amm), common (mushtarak), questioned (mas'ul), reality (haqiqah), metaphor (majaz), etc., will be conducted from the perspective that they convey meaning. And in the second chapter, the discussion will be on the conveyance of legal ruling, for example, the discussion on command (amr) that it establishes obligation, and on prohibition (nahy) that it establishes prohibition, etc.

Commentary
This passage is a front matter from the matn of al-Tawdih, outlining the structure of the upcoming discussion on the Qur'an. The author divides the discussion into two chapters because understanding legal rulings (ahkam shar'iyyah) depends on first understanding the meanings of the text. Therefore, Chapter 1 deals with how the Qur'an conveys meaning, covering topics like general and specific terms, common words, reality and metaphor. Chapter 2 deals with how legal rulings are derived, starting with commands and prohibitions. This two-step approach is fundamental in Hanafi usul: meaning extraction precedes legal derivation.

Key Terms
كتاب (kitab): The Book of Allah (the Qur'an).
باب (bab): A major section or chapter of a book.
افادہ معنى (ifada ma'na): The conveyance of meaning by the text.
افادہ حكم شرعى (ifada hukm shar'i): The conveyance or derivation of a legal ruling.

Key Takeaway
