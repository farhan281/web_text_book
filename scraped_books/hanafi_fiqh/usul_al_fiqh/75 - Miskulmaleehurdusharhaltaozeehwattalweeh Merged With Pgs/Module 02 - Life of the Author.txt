The Four-Book Chain and Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi
Author introduces the four-book structure and al-Bazdawi's biography.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four texts in the commentary chain and explain the role of Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Al-Talwih, Al-Tawdih, Al-Tanqih, and Usul al-Bazdawi form a layered commentary chain from the 11th/12th centuries.

This lesson unpacks the structure of the text you are studying: it is not a single work but a compilation of four linked books. You will learn the relationship between Usul al-Bazdawi, its abridgement Al-Tanqih, and the commentaries Al-Tawdih and Al-Talwih. We also meet the great Hanafi usuli Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi, whose biography anchors this entire tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four books
List the four works mentioned: Usul al-Bazdawi, Al-Tanqih, Al-Tawdih, Al-Talwih.
Remember: Al-Tanqih is an abridgement of Usul al-Bazdawi.
2
Understand the commentary chain
Al-Tawdih is a commentary on Al-Tanqih, and Al-Talwih is a super-commentary on Al-Tawdih. So the chain goes: Usul al-Bazdawi → Al-Tanqih (abridgement) → Al-Tawdih (commentary) → Al-Talwih (super-commentary).
3
Identify the authors
Usul al-Bazdawi by Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi; Al-Tanqih and Al-Tawdih by Sadr al-Shari'ah; Al-Talwih by al-Taftazani.
Note that Sadr al-Shari'ah authored both the abridgement and its commentary.
4
Summarize al-Bazdawi's biography
Born 400 AH in Bazda near Nasaf. He mastered usul, furu', hadith, fiqh, debate, and kalam. His kunya 'Abu al-'Usr' indicates his works are challenging, unlike his brother's easy works.
His full name: Fakhr al-Islam Abu al-'Usr 'Ali ibn Muhammad al-Bazdawi.
5
Assess the significance
Al-Bazdawi's Usul is a foundational Hanafi usul text, distinct from the Shafi'i tradition. Understanding this chain helps you locate authority in later commentaries.

Founder of Usul al-Fiqh as a Discipline
Presents two opinions on the founder of usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two scholarly opinions on who first compiled usul al-fiqh and identify the more widely accepted view.

Core Idea
The first to compile usul al-fiqh was Imam al-Shafi'i, though Imam Abu Hanifa's earlier principles are recognized.

Usul al-fiqh as a written discipline has a debated origin. This brief passage introduces two perspectives: the widely accepted view that Imam al-Shafi'i authored the first work (Al-Risalah), and an alternative claim that Imam Abu Hanifa preceded him. Understanding this helps contextualize the development of Hanafi methodology.

2

The dispute is not about who 'invented' usul al-fiqh, but who first compiled it in a written, systematic form.
Abu Hanifa's contributions are principles, not a dedicated usul work.
3
Recognize the historical significance
Imam al-Shafi'i's Al-Risalah is considered the first formal work on usul al-fiqh, while Hanafi methodology is traced to Abu Hanifa's earlier teachings.

Misk al-Malih… Explanation of al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih. He was a Hafiz. During his time there was a Shafi'i scholar who was extremely intelligent and would overcome whomever he debated; he converted many scholars to the Shafi'i school. Seeing this, the scholars of the time came to Allamah Fakhral-Islam and requested him to debate that scholar. After people insisted, he went to that scholar. The scholar praised Imam Shafi'i, saying that our Imam memorized the Quran in one month and would complete a recitation daily. He replied: Memorizing the Quran is easy for scholars. Bring me an official register containing two years' income and expenditure details and read it out. They did so. He sealed the register, locked it in a house, and went for Hajj. Six months later he returned, summoned the register, and in a public gathering recited it word for word. The Shafi'i scholar was shamed and everyone was astonished. Among his works, Usul al-Pazdawi is his masterpiece. He died in 482 AH and was buried in Samarkand. Author of Tanqih and Tawdih: name and lineage Abdullah ibn Mas'ud ibn Taj al-Shari'ah Mahmud ibn Ahmad Sadr al-Shari'ah al-Akbar. The author is called Sadr al-Shari'ah al-Asghar. His lineage connects to the Companion of the Messenger, Hadrat Ubadah ibn al-Samit. He was the imam of his time, encompassing rational and transmitted sciences, great muhaddith, jurist unparalleled in debate, hadith, fiqh, grammar, lexicography, literature, theology, logic, etc. He studied under his grandfather Taj al-Shari'ah and other scholars. His family was a scholarly family; excellence passed generation to generation. His grandfather was given the title Sadr al-Shari'ah al-Akbar, and he was given the title Sadr al-Shari'ah al-Asghar. Incident: Allamah Qutb al-Din al-Razi, his contemporary, excelled in rational sciences. He once wished to debate Sadr al-Shari'ah. Sadr al-Shari'ah sent his student Mulla Mubarak Shah to listen to his lesson. He found Qutb al-Din teaching Ibn Sina's al-Isharat in a unique way, following neither author nor commentator. So Mubarak Shah was impressed. Sadr al-Shari'ah wrote to Imam Razi: 'This man is not a scholar but a flame of fire; never face him or you will be shamed.' So Imam Razi desisted from debating. Works: Sharh al-Wiqayah, al-Tawdih, al-Tanqih, al-Muqaddimat al-Arba', Kitab al-Shurut, Kitab al-Muhadarat, etc. He died in 747 AH and his tomb is in Bukhara. Author of al-Talwih: name, lineage, title Sad al-Din, name Mas'ud ibn Umar al-Taftazani, born in Taftazan, a district of Khorasan, in Safar. He had a passion for learning from childhood. Some say he was intelligent but through exertion and study he was among the foremost. Dream: One night he dreamt an unfamiliar man saying: 'Sa'd al-Din, come for a walk.' He replied: 'I have no time for leisure; I am a seeking knowledge, and despite effort I cannot understand books.' The man left and returned, this happened thrice. Finally the man said: 'The Master is calling you.' He rushed out barefoot to a grove outside town where the Prophet was sitting with his Companions. Seeing him, the Prophet smiled and said: 'We repeatedly called you but you did not come.' He apologized. Then he complained of his dullness. The Prophet said: 'Open your mouth.' He opened it, the Prophet placed his saliva in his mouth and prayed, then said: 'Go.' Upon waking, he went to his teacher's lesson. During the lesson he raised such deep questions that his companions thought it was just nonsense, but the teacher understood and said: 'O Sa'd! Today you are not the same as before.' He studied various sciences under many scholars, including Allamah Adud al-Din al-Iji and Qutb al-Din al-Razi. Then he engaged in teaching, and in the prime of youth he was counted among great scholars. When he began teaching, hundreds of seekers quenched their thirst from his fountain. He also wrote extensively. He composed al-Tasrif al-Zanjani when only sixteen. He occasionally wrote poetry. Allamah Kafawi said: 'He was among the beauties of the age; eyes have not seen his like among scholars and notables.' His great ability is shown by the fact that even his rival Sayyid Sharif al-Jurjani benefited from his books. He had such awe that even tyrant kings feared him. Incident: One day Timur sent a messenger for an errand, ordering him to take any horse at a stage when his own tired. It happened that when the messenger needed a horse, he came upon Taftazani's tent with horses tied. He untied one. Taftazani, learning this, angrily beat the messenger and expelled him. The messenger complained at Timur's court. In a rage, Timur fell silent for a while, then said: 'If it had been someone else…'

Commentary
This passage presents biographical notices of three key figures in the Hanafi usul tradition: Fakhral-Islam (al-Pazdawi), Sadr al-Shari'ah al-Asghar (author of al-Tawdih and al-Tanqih), and Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani (author of al-Talwih). 

The passage type is a set of biographical entries (tarajim) characteristic of traditional Muslim learning, where the personal piety, intellectual excellence, and baraka of scholars are narrated to establish authority. 

For Fakhral-Islam, the text recounts a story of his remarkable memory as a proof of his God-given intellect, surpassing even a Shafi'i scholar's claim about Imam Shafi'i. His major work, Usul al-Pazdawi, is a foundational text of Hanafi usul.

For Sadr al-Shari'ah al-Asghar, the text emphasizes his expertise in both rational and transmitted sciences, his teaching style, and his decisive handling of Qutb al-Din al-Razi's challenge. His works al-Tawdih and al-Tanqih are core texts in the usul curriculum.

For Taftazani, the text narrates a dream where the Prophet granted him understanding, a key trope of sainthood. His teacher's comment 'Today you are not the same as before' signals a transformation. His teaching and writings, especially al-Talwih (a commentary on al-Tawdih), demonstrate his mastery. The anecdote with Timur shows his fearless independence.

These biographies establish the chain of transmission (sanad) of knowledge: Fakhral-Islam (early Hanafi usul) → Sadr al-Shari'ah (codifier of the matn) → Taftazani (critical commentator). Al-Talwih is a super-commentary that often refines or critiques al-Tawdih, forming the basis of the sadr al-shari'ah/taftazani dialectic studied in advanced Hanafi usul.

As a front-matter lesson, no rulings are derived; rather the authority of the authors is demonstrated for the reader.

Key Terms
فخر الإسلام (Fakhr al-Islam): Title of al-Pazdawi, a leading early Hanafi usul scholar (d. 482 AH), author of Usul al-Pazdawi.
صدر الشریعۃ الاصغر (Sadr al-Shari'ah al-Asghar): Abdullah ibn Mas'ud (d. 747 AH), Hanafi jurist and usuli, author of al-Tawdih and al-Tanqih.
سعد الدین التفتازانی (Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani): Mas'ud ibn Umar (d. 792 AH), polymath author of al-Talwih, a commentary on al-Tawdih often critical of the matn.
التوضیح (al-Tawdih): A Hanafi usul text by Sadr al-Shari'ah al-Asghar, forming the matn base for al-Talwih.
التلویح (al-Talwih): Taftazani's commentary on al-Tawdih, known for critical refinements and usul disputes.

Life and Legacy of al-Taftazani: The Scholar Behind Al-Talwih
Life story and scholarly career of al-Taftazani

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to recount key events in al-Taftazani's life and explain how his dream and his position as a Hanafi scholar shaped his contribution to usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
A dream turned Taftazani from a struggling student into a towering scholar; his pen conquered cities before Timur's sword.

This lesson introduces you to Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani, the prolific author of Al-Talwih. More than a biography, his life story reveals the interplay of divine inspiration, personal striving, and political patronage that produced one of the most influential works on Hanafi usul al-fiqh. Understanding his background helps you appreciate the authority of Al-Talwih in the Hanafi tradition.

He was born. From the beginning he had a passion for seeking knowledge. Some people say that Taftazani was very intelligent, but in striving, hard work, and study of books, he was foremost in achievements. Dream: One time he saw a dream that an unrecognizable person said: 'Sa'd al-Din, come, let's go for a walk.' I replied: 'Brother, I have no time for leisure. I am a poor student. Despite much effort, I cannot understand the book.' That person left and after a short while returned. In this manner, after three comings and goings, he said: 'Sir, the Prophet (peace be upon him) is calling you.' He got up worried and walked barefoot. Outside the city, there was a place with some trees. Under that tree, the Master of the Two Worlds was seated with his Companions. Seeing me, he said with a smiling tone: 'We called you repeatedly, you did not come.' I said: 'I did not know that you were calling me.' After that, I complained about my dullness. He said: 'Open your mouth.' I opened my mouth, he placed his saliva in my mouth, and after supplication said: 'Go.' After waking, he reached his teacher's lesson. During the lesson, many scholarly objections were raised. His companions thought that this is sleepiness, his objections are futile, but the teacher understood and said: 'Ya Sa'd, today you are other than what you were in the past: you are not the Mas'ud of the past, but someone else seems to be.' He studied various sciences and arts from many scholars, among them 'Allama 'Adud al-Din al-Iji and Qutb al-Din al-Razi are notable. Afterward, he engaged in teaching and lecturing, and in the prime of his youth, he came to be counted among the great scholars. When he ascended the seat of teaching, hundreds of thirsty seekers of knowledge quenched their thirst from his fountain of grace. Along with teaching, he also had a taste for authorship; he authored works in every science. When he authored 'Tasrif Zanjani', he was only sixteen years old. Sometimes he also composed poetry. 'Allama Kafawi said: 'He was one of the beauties of the age; the eyes have not seen his like among scholars and notables.' His ability and vastness of knowledge can be gauged from the fact that even his rival, Sayyid Sharif Jurjani, benefited from his books. He had such awe that even great tyrant kings feared him. Incident: One day, King Timur sent a messenger for some important work and ordered him: 'Wherever your horse gets tired, mount whatever horse you find.' By chance, where the messenger needed a mount, 'Allama Taftazani had pitched his tent, and his horses were tied. The messenger went and untied a horse. When 'Allama found out, he became furious and beat the messenger and expelled him. The messenger complained in Timur's court. Timur remained silent for a while due to anger, then said: 'If it had been anyone else, he would not have escaped punishment. I cannot say anything to that person whose pen has conquered every city before my sword.' King Timur was a tyrant; he used to kill people and sit on a throne placed on their skulls. 'Allama Taftazani was close to kings; he had great influence in the court of Shah Shuja' ibn Zafar. Later, he was appointed to the position of 'Chief Judge' under Shah Timur, who was his follower and greatly respected him. Initially, there was friendship between Mir Sayyid Sharif and 'Allama Taftazani. Taftazani himself brought Jurjani to Timur's court. After that, conflict arose between them. In Timur's court, debates often took place. Once a debate occurred on whether a metaphorical isti'ārah entails composition. 'Allama Taftazani was of the opinion of negation, while Sayyid Sharif held affirmation. Both sides piled up arguments; no decision could be reached. So Timur appointed Nu'man al-Mu'tazili as arbiter. Nu'man al-Mu'tazili gave the verdict in favor of Mir Sayyid Sharif, which severely pained 'Allama Taftazani because Taftazani was very popular among the public and elite; people thought no scholar was greater than him. Due to the shock, he fell ill and eventually died in Samarqand on Monday, 22 Muharram 792 AH. After that, on 9 Jumada al-Awwal, his body was transferred to the city of Sarakhs. Commentary: It is agreed that both Sayyid Sharif and Taftazani were among the famous scholars and were the sun and moon of knowledge of their time. However, in logic, literary sciences, and jurisprudential sciences, 'Allama Taftazani surpassed Mir Saheb. In investigations, verifications, intelligence, and acumen, Mir Saheb has no comparison to Taftazani. Two reasons are given for Nu'man al-Mu'tazili declaring Mir Saheb victorious: First, Mir Saheb was more eloquent than Taftazani; Taftazani had a stammer in his tongue, as it is well known that 'the tongue of Sayyid was more eloquent than his pen.' Second, Nu'man al-Mu'tazili had some resentment and anger towards Taftazani. The school of Mir Saheb was Hanafi; regarding Taftazani, there are two opinions: Hanafi and Shafi'i, but from Taftazani's expressions, being Hanafi seems more evident, as stated by Maulana 'Inayat Allah al-Lakhnawi. Some are of the opinion that 'Allama Taftazani was a Shi'a.

They produced extensive piles of proofs, but no decision could be reached. So Timur appointed Nu'man al-Mu'tazili as a referee. Nu'man al-Mu'tazili gave a verdict in favor of Mir Sayyid Sharif, which caused great distress to Taftazani because he was very popular among the elite and commoners. People thought there was no scholar greater than him. Due to the shock, he became bedridden until he passed away on Monday, 22 Muharram 792 AH in Samarqand. Afterwards, on 9 Jumada al-Awwal, his body was transferred to Sarakhs. Commentary: It is accepted that both Sayyid Sharif and Taftazani were among the eminent scholars and the sun and moon of knowledge of their time. However, in logic, literary sciences, and jurisprudence, Taftazani excelled over Mir Sahib. In research, precision, intelligence, and sagacity, Mir Sahib cannot compare with Taftazani. Two reasons are given for Nu'man al-Mu'tazili declaring Mir Sahib victorious: First, Mir Sahib was more eloquent than Taftazani, as it is well known that 'the tongue of Sayyid was more eloquent than his pen'; Second, Nu'man al-Mu'tazili had some grudge and displeasure with Taftazani. Mir Sahib's school was Hanafi. There are rumors about Taftazani being Hanafi or Shafi'i, but from his expressions it becomes apparent that he was Hanafi. Thus said Maulvi Inayatullah Lakhnawi. Some scholars hold the opinion that Allamah Taftazani was a Shi'a.

Core Idea
Hanafi scholarly titles denote distinct levels of analytical competence, not mere honorifics.

When studying Hanafi usul al-fiqh, you will encounter titles like al-Imam, al-Muhaqqiq, and al-Mudaqqiq. These are not random honorifics—they signal the scholar's methodological role. The passage from al-Tawdih explains each term's linguistic root and intellectual function. This lesson unpacks that vocabulary so you can recognize the authority and approach of the scholars you read.
