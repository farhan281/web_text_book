The Dual Nature of 'Awwal' and Its Usul Implications
Commentary on the grammatical rules of 'اول' in the matn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to determine whether 'awwal' in a legal text is used as a superlative adjective or as an adverb based on grammatical clues, and explain the usul consequence for the scope of the ruling.

Core Idea
Awwal with tanwin is an adverb meaning 'before'; without tanwin it is a superlative adjective meaning 'first'—this distinction changes legal scope.

This passage from Misk al-Malih Sharh al-Tawdih examines the word 'awwal' (first/before). The commentator resolves a grammatical objection about the tanwin on 'awwalan' by showing that 'awwal' has two functions: as a superlative adjective (ism tafdil) it is indeclinable, but as an adverb meaning 'before' it is declinable. This distinction is vital in Hanafi usul because when 'awwal' appears in legal texts (e.g., 'al-wajib al-awwal'), it may refer to a specific first obligation or a general prior obligation, affecting the ruling's scope.

Guided Path
1
Identify the grammatical puzzle
Notice that 'awwalan' in the matn has tanwin, but 'awwal' is typically ism tafdil (superlative) and should be ghayr munṣarif (indecilinable). The question is: why does it take tanwin?
This puzzle drives the entire discussion.
2
Distinguish the two functions of 'awwal'
'Awwal' can function as a superlative adjective meaning 'first' (e.g., 'al-sanah al-ula'), or as an adverb meaning 'before' (e.g., 'qabl'). When it is an adverb, it is declinable and takes tanwin.
Classify the usage by context: if it modifies a noun, it is an adjective; if it indicates time, it is an adverb.
3
Apply al-Jawhari's rule from al-Sihah
According to al-Jawhari: 'When you make it an adjective, you do not decline it (ghayr munṣarif); when you do not make it an adjective, you decline it (munṣarif).' This directly explains the tanwin on 'awwalan' in the matn.
Example: 'لقيتُه عامًا أولَ' (adjective) vs. 'لقيتُه عامًا أولاً' (adverb).
4
Connect to usul al-fiqh
In legal texts, the grammatical status of 'awwal' affects the interpretation. For instance, 'al-wajib al-awwal' as an adjective means 'the first obligation' (specific), while 'wajib awwalan' as an adverb means 'a prior obligation' (general). This determines whether the ruling is limited to a specific instance or applies to any prior case.
Hanafi usul relies on such linguistic precision to derive rules from texts.
5
Assess the Kufi-Basri debate
The Kufi school denies that 'awwal' is ism tafdil; the Basri school affirms it based on feminine 'ula' and plural 'awa'il'. The commentator follows the Basri view, which is the standard in Hanafi usul, and explains the tanwin via the adverbial usage.
This debate shows that even morphological classification can be contested, but the usul principle remains valid.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a line from Al-Tawdih that contains the words 'أوّلاً وثانياً' (firstly and secondly). The commentator first addresses a potential misinterpretation: if the matn intended restriction (hasr), it would be inappropriate because the author is not speaking with a speaker who doubts. Then he clarifies that 'أوّلاً' and 'ثانياً' are in the accusative case as adverbs of time (zarf). He anticipates an objection: 'أوّل' is a superlative form (ism tafdil), which is normally indeclinable (ghayr munṣarif), so why does it take tanwin? He resolves this by citing al-Jawhari's Sahah: when 'awwal' is used as a descriptor (sifah), it is indeclinable; when used as an adverb meaning 'before', it is declinable and takes tanwin. He then presents the Kufi and Basri grammatical debate on whether 'awwal' is truly a superlative, and the Basri response. Finally, he discusses the three opinions on the root letters of 'awwal' (d-w-l, w-d-l, w-y-l). This is significant for Hanafi usul al-fiqh because the grammatical status of 'awwal' affects the interpretation of legal texts where it appears, determining whether it refers to a specific first instance or a general anterior time.

Key Terms
أول (awwal): A word that can function either as a superlative adjective (ism tafdil) meaning 'first' or as an adverb (zarf) meaning 'before'.
اسم تفضيل (ism tafdil): A comparative/superlative noun form (af‘alu tafdil) indicating priority or superiority.
تنوين (tanwin): The vowel-ending with nunation indicating indefiniteness and declinability.
غير منصرف (ghayr munṣarif): A noun that does not take tanwin or kasrah for the genitive due to having two reasons for non-declension.

Understanding Hasr and Continuity in 'Ilayhi Yas'adu'
Explaining restriction and continuous renewal in 'ilayhi yas'adu'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the usul principles of taqdim ma haqqahu al-ta'khir (advancing what should be delayed) and fi'l mudari' (present tense) in a Quranic phrase, and explain how they indicate restriction (hasr) and continuous renewal, respectively.

Core Idea
Advancing what should be delayed indicates restriction; present tense indicates continuity.

In this lesson, we examine a brief but profound passage from the commentary on Al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih. The author teases out two subtle indicators from the Quranic phrase 'ilayhi yas'adu' (to Him ascends): word order and verb tense. Understanding how these linguistic features convey legal and theological meaning is essential in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and its components
Locate the Arabic phrase 'ilayhi yas'adu' and note its two parts: the prepositional phrase 'ilayhi' (to Him) and the verb 'yas'adu' (ascends).
The full verse is from Surah Fatir 35:10.
2
Analyze the word order
Observe that 'ilayhi' comes before the verb, whereas the typical order in Arabic is verb first. This departure is called taqdim ma haqqahu al-ta'khir.
This is a deliberate rhetorical device.
3
Apply the usul principle of restriction
In Hanafi usul, taqdim ma haqqahu al-ta'khir indicates hasr (restriction). Therefore, the phrase means that ascension is exclusive to Allah alone.
This principle is universally applied by Hanafi scholars.
4
Examine the verb tense
The verb 'yas'adu' is in the present tense (fi'l mudari'). In Arabic, present tense often implies ongoing or repeated action.
Here it indicates continuity and renewal (istimrar tajaddudi).
5
Synthesize the derived meanings
Combine the two findings: the word order shows exclusivity, the verb tense shows continuous renewal. Both are essential for a complete understanding of the verse.
This derivation is a standard Hanafi textual analysis.

This passage explains rhetorical nuances in the Quranic phrase 'ilayhi yas'adu' (to Him ascends). The author notes that the phrase is arranged with 'ilayhi' (to Him) placed before the verb, which is a departure from the usual order. This advance indicates hasr (restriction) — meaning that ascension is only to Allah. Additionally, the use of the present tense 'yas'adu' rather than the past tense indicates that this ascension is continuous and renewed, not a single past event. This is a standard usul point about the significance of word order and verb tense in the Quran.

The Four Levels of Textual Clarity: The Palace Analogy
Explaining the tashbih of ahkam to a qasr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four levels of textual clarity in the Quran (muhkam, zahir/nass, mutashabih, mujmal/mushkil) and explain how they correspond to the analogy of a palace.

Core Idea
Quranic rulings have four degrees of clarity, just like objects in a palace: from fully visible to completely hidden.

This passage reveals the commentator's explanation of Al-Tawdih's analogy between the rulings of the Quran and a palace. Just as a palace has objects at different levels of visibility, so do the verses of the Quran have varying degrees of clarity. Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for recognizing which verses require interpretation and which are beyond human comprehension.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four levels in the physical palace analogy
List the four levels of visibility of objects in a palace as described in the passage: utmost manifestation, less manifestation, extreme hiddenness (known only to the master), and hiddenness that can be overcome with effort.
This sets the base for the comparison.
2
Match each level to its corresponding Quranic category
Associate each physical level with a Quranic term: utmost manifestation = muhkam; less manifestation = zahir and nass; extreme hiddenness = mutashabih; hiddenness removable with effort = mujmal and mushkil.
The passage explicitly draws these parallels.
3
Discuss the purpose of the analogy
Explain that the author of the matn used this tashbih to illustrate the varying degrees of clarity in Quranic rulings, perfecting the analogy by showing four distinct levels.
This highlights the skill of the author.
4
Note the Hanafi position on mutashabihat
From the analogy, the third category (mutashabih) is known only to Allah, which aligns with the Hanafi madhhab that لا يعلم تأويله إلا الله.
This prepares for the next lesson on muqassarat.

Muqassarat: The Concealed Verses and the Hanafi Position
Defining muqassarat and Hanafi view on mutashabihat

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define muqassarat, explain the Hanafi position on the interpretation (ta'wil) of mutashabihat, and state the wisdom behind their revelation.

Core Idea
Muqassarat: verses whose meaning is confined, like tents of concealment over the mutashabih.

The commentator now defines the term 'muqassarat' as confined verses, specifically the mutashabihat. He explains that Allah has placed a veil over these verses so that their true meaning remains known only to Him. This serves as a test for scholars, preventing them from excessive speculation.

Guided Path
1
Define muqassarat literally and technically
Muqassarat literally means 'confined ones' or 'enclosed'. Technically, it refers to verses whose meaning is veiled and inaccessible, like objects in a locked chamber. The commentator says tents of concealment are pitched over them.
The term is built from the root ق-ص-ر meaning to confine.
2
Identify the Hanafi position on who knows the ta'wil of mutashabihat
The passage explicitly states that according to the Hanafi madhhab, no one knows the interpretation of mutashabihat except Allah. This is based on the stopping at 'إلا الله' in Quran 3:7.
This contrasts with the view that al-rasikhun also know.
3
Explain the wisdom (hikmah) of revealing such verses
The passage states that the benefit of revealing mutashabihat is to test the firmly grounded in knowledge (الراسخون في العلم) by preventing them from reflecting and reaching the ultimate goal of knowing the secrets. Just as the ignorant are tested by seeking what is not desired, scholars are tested by being prevented from what they desire.
This turns a potential difficulty into a divine trial.

The Maqsūrāt: Defining Mutashābihāt and Their Purpose as a Test in Hanafi Usul
Explaining maqsūrāt and the analogy with qasr, ending with the purpose of mutashābihāt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define maqsūrāt (mutashābihāt) according to the Hanafi school, explain why they were revealed as a test for scholars, and distinguish the Hanafi position on the interpretation of Quran 3:7 from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
Mutashābihāt are confined by Allah as a test: scholars must refrain from interpreting them, proving their submission.

This lesson unpacks the Hanafi usul concept of maqsūrāt—the 'confined' verses of the Quran. The commentator explains how these verses are like hidden objects in a qasr (enclosure) and why Allah revealed them. We will also examine the famous ikhtilaf with the Shafi'i school over who knows the true interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its lexical meaning
The term maqsūrāt is derived from qasr, meaning confinement. The commentator defines it as 'محبوسات' (confined things). This sets the foundation that these verses are deliberately kept from human knowledge.
2
Understand the analogy with the four levels of qasr
Just as a physical enclosure (qasr) has objects of varying visibility—some clear from afar, some visible with effort, some hidden, some completely concealed—the Quran has rulings (ahkām) of varying clarity. The four levels are: muhkamat (clear), zahir/nass (less clear), mutashabihat (hidden), and mujmal/mushkil (can be uncovered with effort). The maqsūrāt are the highest level of concealment.
3
Grasp the specific definition of maqsūrāt as mutashābihāt
Maqsūrāt refer specifically to the mutashābihāt—verses that are ambiguous and whose apparent meaning is not intended. According to the Hanafi school, no one knows their ta'wil (true interpretation) except Allah.
4
Analyze the purpose of revelation: a test for the rāsikhūn
The benefit of revealing mutashābihāt is iblā’ (test) for those firmly grounded in knowledge ('al-rasikhoona). Scholars naturally desire to uncover all secrets, but Allah prevents them from delving into these verses. Obedience requires them to refrain, proving their submission over their desires.
5
Contrast the Hanafi and Shafi'i readings of Quran 3:7
The ikhtilaf centers on the grammatical stop in Quran 3:7. Hanafis stop at 'illa Allah', making 'al-rasikhoona' a separate sentence: 'they say, we believe in it'. Shafi'is make 'al-rasikhoona' a conjunction to Allah, implying they also know the ta'wil. The Hanafi response is that the verse praises the rasikhoon for their declaration of faith, not for knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on the matn of al-Tawdih (by Sadr al-Shariah) and al-Talwih (by al-Taftazani). The author, Misk al-Malih, explains the classification of ahkam (rulings) into degrees of clarity, using the analogy of a physical enclosure (qasr) with four levels of visibility. The author then highlights the highest level of khafa (hiddenness): the mutashabihat, which according to the Hanafis are known only to Allah. The commentator defends the Hanafi view that the purpose of revealing mutashabihat is to test the 'rasikhoon' (those firmly grounded in knowledge) by preventing them from achieving their desire to uncover all secrets. This is a common test: every person is tested by being prevented from what they love. The passage also addresses the well-known ikhtilaf (disagreement) between the Hanafis and Shafi'is regarding the interpretation of Quran 3:7. The Hanafis stop at 'illa Allah', while the Shafi'is connect 'al-rasikhoon' to the subject of knowing the ta'wil. The commentator al-Taftazani, reputedly a Shafi'i, deliberately avoids specifying the school in his note, saying only 'according to the school' without naming it, to avoid being labeled. This shows scholarly etiquette. The passage concludes with a linguistic note on 'kabha' meaning to restrain.

Key Terms
مقصورات (maqsūrāt): Confined things; here referring to mutashabihat which are restricted from understanding.
متشابہات (mutashābihāt): Verses of the Quran that are ambiguous and whose apparent meaning is not intended.
تأويل (ta'wīl): The true interpretation or final meaning of a verse, often referring to the ultimate reality behind mutashābihāt.

Pronoun Agreement for Usul al-Fiqh: Idafi vs Laqabi
Resolving an apparent pronoun gender contradiction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when 'usul al-fiqh' functions as a plural compound (idafi) vs a singular title (laqabi) and apply the correct Arabic pronoun agreement.

Core Idea
Usul al-fiqh is plural in its added meaning and singular as a title — both pronouns are correct depending on context.

In the study of usul al-fiqh, precise language is paramount. This passage from Sharh al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih addresses an apparent inconsistency: the author uses a feminine pronoun when referring to 'usul al-fiqh' in one place and a masculine pronoun in another. The commentator, Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani, resolves this by revealing that 'usul al-fiqh' carries two distinct meanings—one additive (idafi) and one titular (laqabi). Understanding this duality not only clarifies the grammar but deepens our grasp of how the discipline is conceptualized.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent contradiction
The author uses 'arrafaha' (feminine) in the added definition and 'na'rifuhu' (masculine) in the laqabi definition, both referring to 'usul al-fiqh'.
The contradiction is only apparent; it arises from a lack of nuance.
2
Distinguish the two aspects of usul al-fiqh
Realize that 'usul al-fiqh' can be understood as an idafi compound (mudaf + mudaf ilayh) or as a title (laqab) for a science.
As an idafi compound, it is grammatically plural; as a laqab, it is singular.
3
Apply Arabic pronoun rules
In Arabic, non-rational plurals (like the idafi sense) take feminine singular pronouns. Laqabs are treated as singular proper nouns, taking masculine pronouns.
Thus the feminine pronoun suits the plural sense, the masculine pronoun suits the singular sense.
4
Confirm no contradiction
Both pronouns are correct because each refers to a different aspect of the same phrase.
The commentator's answer is that there is no contradiction (la tadad wa la ta'arud).

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis where the commentator (Sharh al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih) addresses an apparent contradiction: the author uses a feminine pronoun ('arrafaha) when discussing the added definition of usul al-fiqh, but a masculine pronoun (na'rifuhu) when discussing the laqabi definition. In its added meaning (as mudaf and mudaf ilayh), it is considered a plural compound, so a feminine pronoun is appropriate (since non-rational plurals take feminine singular pronouns in Arabic). In its laqabi meaning, as a title of a science, it is considered a singular proper noun, so a masculine pronoun is used. The text then further explains how usul al-fiqh is plural when considered as added: either as a terminological plural (usul being the plural of asl) or as a linguistic/customary plural (multiple elements: mudaf, mudaf ilayh, idafah). This careful grammatical analysis shows the precision expected in usul al-fiqh methodology.

Definition of Asl and the Necessity of Qayd al-Haythiyya
Expansion on the definition of asl and the necessity of qayd al-haythiyya.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the lexical definition of 'asl' with the qualification of perspective and explain why this qualification resolves the paradox of the four proofs being both usul and furu.

Core Idea
Asl is that upon which something is built, from that specific perspective, not absolutely.

In usul al-fiqh, the term 'asl' is foundational, but its definition must carefully handle relational concepts. The commentator al-Taftazani expands on the author's definition by adding 'min haythu innahu yubna 'alayhi' to prevent a contradiction: the four proofs are themselves based on kalam, yet they serve as usul for fiqh. This lesson unpacks that subtlety.

Commentary
This passage is an extensive commentary on the definition of 'asl' (root/origin) in the context of usul al-fiqh. The commentator, al-Taftazani, is explaining and expanding upon the definition given by the author of the matn (Sadr al-Shari'ah). The main issues addressed are: (1) the relationship between the singular 'asl' and the plural 'usul' in the definition, (2) the necessity of adding the qualification 'from the perspective of being built upon' (qayd al-haythiyya) to resolve a potential contradiction regarding the four proofs (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas) being themselves based on kalam, and (3) the various lexical and technical meanings of 'asl'. The commentary proceeds by raising implicit questions and answering them. First, it explains that defining the singular 'asl' suffices for defining the plural 'usul' because the plural derives from the singular. Then it addresses why Taftazani added the qualification 'min haythu innahu yubna 'alayhi' to the definition: without this, the four proofs would not qualify as 'usul' since they are themselves built upon kalam. With the qualification, it becomes clear they are usul only in relation to fiqh, not absolutely. This leads to a discussion of 'al-idafiyyat' (relational things) and the necessity of including the qualification of perspective in their definitions, though it may be omitted due to its fame. Finally, Taftazani lists multiple technical meanings of 'asl' (eleven are mentioned in the text) and then presents two views on which meaning is intended in 'usul al-fiqh': some say the technical meaning (dalil/proof), while the author of the matn indicates the lexical meaning (ma yabni 'alayhi ghayruhu). The passage thus provides a thorough usul-al-fiqh foundation for understanding the key term 'asl'.

الإضافيات (al-idafiyyat): Relational matters; concepts whose meaning inherently involves reference to another (e.g., father-son).

Two Meanings of Haythiyya in Defining Subjects

Core Idea
Haythiyya can be part of the subject or a mere limit; this changes what a science investigates.

In defining the subject (mawdu') of a science, a qualification (haythiyya) is often added to specify the perspective. The commentator on al-Tawdih explains that this qualification has two distinct meanings. The first meaning treats the qualification as an integral part of the subject itself, so the science only discusses the essential accidents that follow from the subject in that qualified sense. The second meaning treats it as an external restriction. Understanding this distinction is foundational for delineating the scope of any science, including usul al-fiqh.

When haythiyya is part of the subject, the science cannot discuss the subject itself or its parts. The nature of the haythiyya determines which essential accidents are considered and what topics fall within the science. For usul al-fiqh, the subject is 'the proofs insofar as they lead to rulings'—the 'insofar as' is part, so we study the essential accidents of proofs like generality, particularity, etc.
This is key for distinguishing usul al-fiqh from other disciplines.

Resolving the Circularity Objection: Wasita and Dhi Wasita
Resolving the circularity objection in defining subject with qualification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi usul distinction between wasita (potentiality) and dhi wasita (actuality) resolves the circularity objection (تقدم الشیء على نفسه) in defining the subject of a science with a qualification, and apply this distinction to any scientific subject.

Core Idea
The subject is defined by potentiality, not actuality; the qualification is for discussion, not inherence.

This passage deals with a common logical objection: when we define the subject of a science with a qualification (e.g., 'the human body from the perspective of health and illness'), it seems that the accidents (health/illness) would both be the intermediary and the result, causing circularity. The Hanafi answer distinguishes between the intermediary (wasita) which is the mere potentiality (امکان) of the accidents, and the mediated (dhi wasita) which are the actual accidents themselves. Furthermore, the qualification is only for the purpose of discussion, not for the actual inherence of accidents. Understanding this distinction is crucial for properly delimiting the scope of any science without falling into logical fallacies.

Guided Path
1
State the objection
State the circularity objection: if the subject of a science is defined by a qualification (e.g., body from the perspective of health and illness), then health and illness appear both as the defining perspective and as the accidents discussed, leading to تقدم الشیء على نفسه (a thing preceding itself).
The objection arises because the same thing (health) seems to be both the medium of definition and the accident.
2
Distinguish wasita and dhi wasita
Identify that the passage introduces a crucial difference: wasita (the intermediary, i.e., the qualification in the definition) is the potentiality of the accidents (e.g., possibility of health), while dhi wasita (the mediated) are the actual accidents themselves (e.g., health itself). The two are not identical.
The wasita is امكان (possibility); the dhi wasita is نفس الشیء (the essence of the thing).
3
Apply to examples
Work through the examples: In medicine, the subject 'human body from the perspective of health and illness' uses 'health' as potentiality (wasita), but the accidents discussed are actual health and illness (dhi wasita). Similarly in natural science: 'body from the perspective of motion and rest' uses potential motion/rest as wasita, but actual motion/rest as dhi wasita.
This distinction prevents the circularity because the subject is defined by potentiality, not actuality.
4
Analyze Taftazani's research answer
Understand Taftazani's further refined answer: the qualification (من حیث الصحة والمرض) is not a condition for the inherence of accidents but for the discussion of them. At the time of discussion, we must consider the perspective, but the inherence itself does not require the perspective as a cause.
The perspective is a 'qualification of discussion', not of the accidents themselves.
5
Recognize the author's critique
Note the author's observation that Taftazani's position (that the perspective must be considered during discussion) is exactly the 'expressive perspective' (حیثیت بیانیہ) he had earlier denied, implying an implicit acceptance of the Hanafi position.
This shows that the Hanafi usul principle is eventually unavoidable.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed response to the objection that defining the subject of a science with a qualification (e.g., 'body from the perspective of health and illness') leads to the fallacy of the thing preceding itself (تقدم الشیء على نفسه). The commentator clarifies that the intermediary (واسطہ) in the definition — the perspective — is not the same as the mediated (ذی واسطہ), i.e., the essential accidents themselves. The perspective indicates potentiality (امکان), whereas the accidents discussed are actualities (نفس الشیء). Thus, the subject of medicine is the human body insofar as it can be healthy or ill; the accidents are health and illness themselves. Taftazani's research answer further distinguishes between the discussion of accidents in a science (which requires considering the perspective) and the actual inherence of accidents in the subject (which does not require that perspective as an intermediary). The qualification is a restriction of discussion, not of inherence. The author then observes that Taftazani's admitted position — that the perspective must be considered during discussion — amounts to the 'expressive perspective' (حیثیت بیانیہ) that he had earlier rejected, implying an implicit adoption of the author's view.

Key Terms
واسطہ (wasita): The intermediary (here: the qualification of perspective) in the definition of the subject, indicating potentiality (امکان)
ذی واسطہ (dhi wasita): The mediated (here: the essential accidents themselves in actuality)
تقدم الشیء على نفسه (taqaddum al-shay' 'ala nafsih): The logical fallacy of a thing preceding itself, which would occur if the same entity is both subject and accident without distinction

Reconciling the Four Divisions of Nazm and Ma'na
Resolving contradictions between Fakhr al-Islam and the author on divisions of nazm and ma'na

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Taftazani's method of reconciling contradictory expressions regarding the four divisions of textual indication by identifying the common underlying principle that the divisions are of nazm (wording) relative to ma'na (meaning).

Core Idea
All four textual divisions relate to nazm relative to ma'na — wording is the direct subject, meaning is the indirect.

This passage from the commentary on al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih tackles apparent contradictions between the Hanafi master Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi and the author Sadr al-Shariah regarding the four-fold classification of how a text indicates its meaning. Taftazani demonstrates that both scholars ultimately converge: the divisions are of the wording (nazm) considered in relation to their meanings (ma'na). By analyzing their varying expressions, he extracts a unifying methodological principle — that attention should be paid to the intended meaning, not the literal wording.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent contradiction
Compare Fakhr al-Islam's statement that the fourth division (dalalah, iqtida') is a division of meaning with Sadr al-Shariah's statement that all four divisions are of 'ilm (knowledge) relative to meaning. Determine the conflict.
The contradiction is between treating the fourth division as 'ma'na' versus treating all divisions as 'nazm relative to ma'na'.
2
Analyze Taftazani's first answer
Notice that Taftazani argues that Fakhr al-Islam's various expressions for the fourth division (e.g., 'istidlal', 'thabit', 'wuquf') all amount to 'al-dal bi tariq...' — the indicator (dal) is always the word. Therefore, these divisions relate to nazm as well.
The indicator (dal) is the wording itself; hence the division is of nazm.
3
Analyze Taftazani's second answer
Examine how Taftazani reconciles Sadr al-Shariah's definition of the Quran as 'nazm dal 'ala al-ma'na' with the mashayikh's statement that the divisions are 'of nazm and ma'na'. He shows that both intend 'al-nazm al-dal 'ala al-ma'na' — the wording indicating meaning.
The mashayikh's 'nazm wa ma'na jami'an' means the nazm that is indicative of meaning, not two separate entities.
4
Evaluate the proof
Assess Taftazani's dalil: the Quran's attributes (Arabic, written in mushafs, mutawatir transmission) are attributes of the wording itself, not of its meaning. Similarly, i'jaz depends on balaghah, which is an attribute of wording in consideration of conveying meaning. This proves that the Quran is primarily nazm relative to ma'na.
This proof establishes that the divisions, which are derived from the Quran, also follow this pattern.
5
Synthesize the principle
Conclude that the underlying Hanafi usul principle is 'al-nazm bi al-nisba ila al-ma'na': all textual indications are analyzed from the wording but with constant reference to meaning. This principle resolves the apparent contradictions.
This exemplifies the Hanafi approach of focusing on intended meaning over literal wording (dab al-mashayikh).

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis reconciling different scholarly expressions regarding the four-fold classification of textual indications in Hanafi usul. The author of al-Tawdih, Sadr al-Shariah, and the earlier Hanafi master Fakhr al-Islam al-Bazdawi (d. 482H) are the key figures. The core issue is whether the four divisions (taqasim arba'a) – which categorize how a text indicates its meaning – are divisions of the wording (nazm) itself or of the meaning (ma'na). The passage opens by stating that indication to meaning is necessary, and if the division is based on linguistic assignment (wad'), it is the first division; if based on usage, the second; if based on indication with consideration of clarity (dhuhur) and completeness (wafa'), then it is the third or the prevalent division. Then it explains that Fakhr al-Islam treated all four divisions as divisions of nazm and ma'na, making the first three attributes of the word. He expressed the first division as 'with regard to aspects of the composition, the form and the language'; the second as 'with regard to aspects of the expression by that composition'; the third as 'with regard to the usage of that composition'. For the fourth division, Fakhr al-Islam used multiple expressions: sometimes 'istidlal bi al-'ibarah' etc., sometimes 'istidlal' for the first two and 'thabit' for the last two, sometimes 'wuquf' for all four. In his definitions, he used terms that are attributes of meaning (e.g., 'al-thabit bi al-nazm'), creating ambiguity. Later scholars disagreed on which divisions pertain to nazm and which to ma'na. Taftazani clarifies that the author's (Sadr al-Shariah) approach is excellent: all divisions relate to nazm relative to ma'na (bi al-nisba ila al-ma'na). Directly they are about the wording, but meaning is considered. This harmonizes the various expressions. The passage then addresses two presumed objections (sual muqaddar). The first objection: the author said all divisions are of 'ilm (knowledge) relative to meaning, while Fakhr al-Islam made the fourth division a division of meaning. Taftazani answers: no contradiction, since Fakhr al-Islam's various expressions all amount to 'the indicator by way of expression, indication, connotation, and requirement', and the indicator is always the wording, so these divisions also relate to nazm. The second objection: the author defines Quran as 'nazm dal 'ala al-ma'na' (composition indicating meaning), while the mashayikh (earlier scholars) say divisions are of nazm and ma'na, implying direct relation to both. Taftazani answers: no contradiction; the mashayikh's intent is exactly the same – 'al-nazm al-dal 'ala al-ma'na'. He then provides a proof (dalil): the Quran's attributes (being Arabic, written in mushafs, transmitted by tawatur) are attributes of the nazm relative to meaning, not of both nazm and ma'na. Similarly, i'jaz (inimitability) relates to balaghah (eloquence), which is an attribute of the wording in consideration of conveying meaning. Therefore, the definition of Quran as nazm relative to meaning is sound. This entire discussion exemplifies the Hanafi usul methodology of carefully analyzing the relationship between wording and meaning, and reconciling seemingly contradictory statements from authoritative scholars. It underscores the principle that attention should be given to the intended meaning rather than literal wording (dab al-mashayikh).

معنى (ma'na): The meaning or concept conveyed by the wording.
دلالة (dalalah): The indication or connotation of a word; here, specifically the secondary indication that is not directly from the wording but from its meaning.
تقسيمات اربع (taqasim arba'a): The four-fold classification of how a text indicates its meaning: by explicit expression ('ibarah), by allusion (isharah), by connotation (dalalah), and by requirement (iqtida').
نظم دال على المعنى (nazm dal 'ala al-ma'na): Wording that indicates meaning; the definition of the Quran emphasized by Sadr al-Shariah.

The Hierarchy of Textual Clarity: Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, Muhkam and Their Opposites
Defines the eight categories of textual clarity via a binary tree.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify scriptural texts according to the Hanafi hierarchy of clarity (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam, khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih) and assess the epistemological weight of each category.

Core Idea
A text's clarity determines whether it is open to ta'wil or abrogation—master this tree for sound istinbat.

This passage lays out the foundational classification of textual clarity in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It presents a logical binary tree: first, whether the meaning is apparent or hidden; then, within apparent, whether it admits ta'wil; within hidden, the cause and resolvability. By internalizing these eight terms and their relationships, you will be able to accurately gauge the strength of any scriptural proof.

Guided Path
1
1. Distinguish Apparent vs. Hidden Meaning
Begin by deciding whether the word's meaning is clear from its form (zahir) or hidden (khafi). This is the first binary split.
Apparent means the meaning suggests itself; hidden means it requires external investigation.
2
2. For Apparent: Check Openness to Ta'wil
If the meaning is apparent, ask: can it be interpreted away (ta'wil)? If yes, then determine if clarity comes from the word's form (zahir) or from context/usage (nass). If no, move to step 3.
Zahir and nass both accept ta'wil, but zahir's clarity is intrinsic, nass's is extrinsic.
3
3. For Apparent and Not Open to Ta'wil: Check Abrogation
If the meaning is clear and not open to ta'wil, ask: can it be abrogated (naskh)? If yes, it is mufassar; if no, it is muhkam.
Muhkam is the highest level of clarity and permanence.
4
4. For Hidden: Diagnose the Cause
If the meaning is hidden, determine whether the hiding is due to something external to the word (khafi) or due to the word itself. If internal, check if reflection removes the confusion (mushkil) or if clarification is still awaited (mujmal) or impossible (mutashabih).
Mujmal requires external bayan; mutashabih is inherently unknowable.
5
5. Apply the Tree to a Sample Text
Take a Quranic verse or hadith and trace through the hierarchy, justifying each decision with evidence from the text's wording and context.
This cements the classification through real examples.

Commentary
This passage introduces the first classification of textual clarity in Hanafi usul. It presents a binary tree: first, the meaning is either apparent (zahir) or hidden (khafi). For apparent meanings, they are either open to ta'wil (allegorical interpretation) or not. If open, and clarity comes from the form itself, it is zahir; if clarity is from context, it is nass. If not open to ta'wil, and the text can be abrogated, it is mufassar; if not abrogated, it is muhkam (most clear). For hidden meanings, if the hiddenness is due to external factors, it is khafi; if due to the word itself, then if the hiddenness can be removed by reflection, it is mushkil; if it awaits clarification, it is mujmal; if inherently unclear, it is mutashabih. This classification helps us understand the relative clarity of scriptural texts and which can be interpreted freely.

Key Terms
ظاہر (zahir): A word whose meaning is apparent from its form but open to ta'wil
نص (nass): A word whose meaning is apparent not from its form but from context or usage, and it is more clear than zahir
مفسر (mufassar): A word whose meaning is clear and not open to ta'wil, but it can be abrogated
محکم (muhkam): A word that is clear and cannot be abrogated
خفی (khafi): A word whose meaning is hidden due to something outside the word itself
مشکل (mushkil): A word whose meaning is hidden due to the word itself, but can be resolved through reflection and ta'wil
مجمل (mujmal): A word that is ambiguous and requires external clarification (bayan)
متشابہ (mutashabih): A word that is inherently unclear and cannot be fully understood

Definition of Mu'awwal and Its Objections
Defines mu'awwal and addresses objections from the commentator.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mu'awwal according to Hanafi usul, explain the two objections raised to its definition, and identify the necessary conditions for mu'awwal to be a division of nazm.

Core Idea
Mu'awwal: a mushtarak word whose intended meaning is preferred by dominant conjecture through reflection on the sighah itself, making it a division of nazm.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the classification of lafz (text) is foundational. One important category is mu'awwal (the interpreted), which sits between the clear and the completely ambiguous. This lesson unpacks the definition given by Imam Fakhr al-Islam, the two objections raised by the commentator, and the responses that refine our understanding of when a word qualifies as mu'awwal and why it remains a division of nazm.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition from the matn
The matn defines mu'awwal as 'selecting from the mushtarak one of its meanings by some aspects, by dominant opinion.' Note the key components: mushtarak, ghalib al-ra'i, and selection.
The definition is from Fakhr al-Islam's classification.
2
Understand the first objection
The commentator objects that restricting mu'awwal to mushtarak is wrong, because mu'awwal is also found in khafi, mushkil, and mujmal when resolved by a zanni dalil like khabar wahid or qiyas.
This objection broadens the typical scope of mu'awwal.
3
Grasp the response to the first objection
The response clarifies that the definition is not for absolute mu'awwal but specifically for mu'awwal as a division of nazm min hayth al-lughah wa al-sighah. For that taxonomic category, only mushtarak qualifies because its ambiguity stems from wad'.
The response shows that the definition is context-specific.
4
Understand the second objection and response
The second objection is that 'bi-ghalib al-ra'i' seems to imply preference always comes from ijtihad, but it can come from qiyas or khabar wahid. The response explains that 'ra'i' here means 'zann', so 'ghalib al-ra'i' means 'dominant conjecture' from any source, not just ijtihad.
The phrase is redefined to avoid misinterpretation.
5
Synthesize the conditions for mu'awwal as a division of nazm
From Taftazani's combined answer, two restrictions are essential: (1) the word must be mushtarak (ambiguity from wad'), and (2) the preference must be by ghalib al-zann through reflection on the sighah itself (not by qat'i dalil, which would be taghayyur, nor by external zanni dalil alone, which would remove it from nazm).
These conditions ensure the interpretation remains attributed to the text itself.

ماؤل نظم کے اقسام میں سے تو نہیں بلکہ یہ تو مجتہد کے اجتھاد متامل سے حاصل ہوتا ہے تو اس کو نظم کے اقسام صیغہ ولغتہ سے کیسے شمار کیا گیا تو شارح جواب دے رہے ہیں کہ تاویل کے بعد بھی چونکہ حکم مضاف و منسوب ہوتا ہے نظم کی طرف اس لیے اس کو اقسام اعظم سے شمار کیا گیا ہے وقيل المراد بغالب الراءى التامل والاجتهاد فى نفس الصیغہ یہاں سے تفتازانی دونوں اشکالوں کا مشترکہ جواب دے رہے ہیں کہ یہاں ماؤل کی تعریف میں من المشترکہ اور بغالب الرائی کی دو قید میں اس لیے لگائی گئی ہیں کہ یہاں اس ماؤل کی تعریف کی جارہی ہے جو نظم کے اقسام میں سے ہے صیغہ ولغتہ اور اس کی تعریف میں یہ قیدیں لگانا از حد ضروری ہے۔ فان المشترک موضوع سے قید اشتراک کے ضروری ہونے کی وجہ بیان کر رہے ہیں کہ مشترک متعدد معانی کیلئے وضع کیا جاتا ہے اور اس میں ہر معنی کا احتمال ہوتا ہے علی سبیل البدل تو جب صیغہ میں غور وفکر کر کے اس کو کسی ایک معنی پر محمول کر لیا جائے گا تو تب بھی وہ نظم کے اقسام میں سے ہی شمار ہوگا بخلاف اس صورت کے اگر اس کے معانی میں نے کسی ایک کو نفس صیغہ میں غور و فکر کر کے ترجیح نہ دی جائے بلکہ کسی دلیل قطعی کے ساتھ ترجیح دی جائے تو اس کو تاویل نہیں کہا جائے گا بلکہ تغیر کہا جائے گا اسی طرح اگر قیاس یا خبر واحد کے ساتھ ترجیح دی جائے اس کو بھی ماؤل نہ کہا جائے گا اسی طرح اگر مشترک نہ ہو بلکہ خفی مشکل مجمل ہو اور ان کا خفاء دلیل قطعی یا ظنی کے ساتھ زائل کیا جائے اس کو بھی ماؤل نہیں کہا جاتا اور نہ ہی یہ اقسام نظم میں سے ہوگا کیونکہ اس میں وضع کا کوئی دخل نہیں ہے تو ماؤل کو اقسام نظم صیغہ ولغتہ میں شامل کرنے کیلئے من المشترک اور بغالب الراءی دونوں قیدوں کا ہونا ضروری ہے۔
And besides, the purpose of the explanation of the text: Imam Fakhr al-Islam mentioned four divisions of lafz from the perspective of wad': khas, 'amm, mushtarak, and mu'awwal. He defined mu'awwal as: 'Selecting from the mushtarak one of its meanings by some aspects, by dominant opinion.' That is, mu'awwal is that one meaning is preferred over other meanings by reflection and thought. And from 'wa dalla 'ala an al-ma'wal' the commentator is objecting to the definition of mu'awwal. (1) The first objection: Placing the restriction of mushtarak in the definition of mu'awwal is wrong, because it suggests that mu'awwal is specific to mushtarak, whereas it is not correct; rather mu'awwal is also found in khafi, mushkil, mujmal, as mentioned in al-Mizan: if the ambiguity of khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mushtarak is removed by a qat'i dalil, it is called 'asr; if their ambiguity is removed by a zanni dalil which contains doubt, it is called mu'awwal, like khabar wahid and qiyas—these are zanni dalils. Therefore, restricting mu'awwal to mushtarak in the definition is not correct. The commentator answered in 'wa ujiba 'an al-awwal' that here the definition is not of absolute mu'awwal, but of that mu'awwal which is related to mushtarak, because this is the mu'awwal that belongs to the divisions of nazm min hayth al-lughah wa al-sighah. (2) The second objection: Placing the restriction of 'bi-ghalib al-ra'i' in the definition is not correct, because it implies that the preference of one meaning in mu'awwal always occurs through opinion and ijtihad, whereas this is wrong; sometimes preference occurs through qiyas, sometimes through khabar wahid, sometimes through reflection in the sighah itself. And from 'wa 'an al-thani' the commentator answers the second objection: Here 'ra'i' means 'zann' not 'ta'ammul wa ijtihad'; so 'ghalib al-ra'i' means 'ghalib al-zann', whether that ghalib al-zann is obtained from khabar wahid, or qiyas, or reflection in the sighah itself, like thalaathatu quru'. And the meaning of 'being from the divisions of nazm' — there is an implied question: mu'awwal is not among the divisions of nazm, rather it is obtained from the ijtihad and reflection of the mujtahid, so how is it counted among the divisions of nazm min hayth al-sighah wa al-lughah? The commentator answers: because after ta'wil, the ruling is attributed and related to the nazm, therefore it is counted among its divisions. And it is said: the meaning of 'bi-ghalib al-ra'i' is reflection and ijtihad in the sighah itself. From here, Taftazani gives a combined answer to both objections: two restrictions – 'min al-mushtarak' and 'bi-ghalib al-ra'i' – are placed in the definition of mu'awwal because here the definition is of that mu'awwal which is among the divisions of nazm min hayth al-sighah wa al-lughah, and placing these restrictions is extremely necessary. Then he explains the necessity of the restriction of mushtarak: mushtarak is placed for multiple meanings and each meaning is possible on a substitution basis. So when by reflecting in the sighah it is interpreted to one meaning, then it will still be counted among the divisions of nazm. Contrast the case where preference is not given by reflection in the sighah itself, but by a qat'i dalil, then it is not called ta'wil but taghayyur. Similarly, if preference is given by qiyas or khabar wahid, it is also not called mu'awwal. Also, if the word is not mushtarak but is khafi, mushkil, or mujmal, and its ambiguity is removed by a qat'i or zanni dalil, then that is not called mu'awwal, nor is it from the divisions of nazm because there is no role of wad' in it. So for mu'awwal to be included in the divisions of nazm min hayth al-sighah wa al-lughah, both restrictions 'min al-mushtarak' and 'bi-ghalib al-ra'i' are necessary.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary expansion on the definition of mu'awwal (the interpreted) as a division of lafz in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The matn of Fakhr al-Islam defined mu'awwal as 'selecting from the mushtarak one of its meanings by some aspects, by dominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'i).' The commentator (sharih) raises two objections to this definition. First objection: restricting mu'awwal to mushtarak is incorrect because mu'awwal also applies to khafi, mushkil, and mujmal when their ambiguity is removed by a zanni dalil (like khabar wahid or qiyas). The sharh responds that the definition here is not for absolute mu'awwal but specifically for the mu'awwal that is a division of nazm (text) from the perspective of lughah and sighah; that type of mu'awwal is only related to mushtarak because its ambiguity is due to wad' (linguistic coinage) and is resolved by reflection on the sighah itself. Second objection: the phrase 'bi-ghalib al-ra'i' implies ijtihad, whereas preference can also come from qiyas or khabar wahid. The sharh clarifies that 'ra'i' here means 'zann' (conjecture) not ijtihad, so 'ghalib al-ra'i' means 'dominant conjecture' from any source. Then a combined answer from Taftazani: both restrictions are necessary for mu'awwal to be a division of nazm because mushtarak's ambiguity is from wad' and its resolution through reflective preference (not qat'i dalil) keeps it within nazm; if resolved by qat'i dalil, it becomes taghayyur (alteration), not ta'wil. Similarly, if the word is not mushtarak but khafi etc., its resolution is not through wad' and thus not mu'awwal as a division of nazm.

Key Terms
ماؤل (mu'awwal): A lafz that is mushtarak and its intended meaning is preferred over others by dominant conjecture (ghalib al-zann) through reflection on the sighah itself, making it a division of nazm.
مشترک (mushtarak): A lafz that is linguistically coined (wad') for multiple meanings, each meaning possible on a substitution basis.
بغالب الرای (bi-ghalib al-ra'i): By dominant opinion/conjecture (ghalib al-zann), as opposed to certain knowledge. 'Ra'i' here means zann, not ijtihad.
