Linguistic vs Legal Meaning of Hamd
Resolving an objection via semantic shift

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the linguistic and legal definitions of 'hamd' and apply the legal definition to resolve an apparent contradiction in a Quranic analogy.

Core Idea
Hamd in the legal sense includes belief and action, not just speech—making the tree simile valid.

This passage addresses a subtle objection: how can 'hamd' (praise) be likened to a tree when a tree is composite and 'hamd' is simple verbal praise? The commentator resolves this by shifting from the linguistic meaning of 'hamd' to its legal meaning, which encompasses internal faith (the root) and external deeds (the branches). This lesson demonstrates how usul al-fiqh uses semantic analysis to reconcile apparent contradictions in textual analogies.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
Recognize the apparent contradiction: 'hamd' as a simple verbal act vs. a tree as a composite entity (root, branches, crown).
The objection arises if we take 'hamd' only linguistically.
2
Locate the Quranic Proof
Find the verse (Quran 14:24) that likens a 'goodly word' to a goodly tree. This is the dalil being interpreted.
The verse does not explicitly define 'kalima tayyiba'.
3
Analyze the Commentator's Resolution
Observe how the commentator shifts from linguistic meaning (verbal) to legal meaning (belief, speech, action) to make the simile coherent.
This shift is a common usul technique called 'takhsis al-urf al-lughawi bi al-urf al-shar'i'.
4
Classify the Text
Recognize that 'kalima tayyiba' in the verse is mujmal (ambiguous) and requires bayan (explanation). The bayan is that it refers to 'hamd' in its legal sense.
This demonstrates the usul principle of 'al-mujmal yubayyan'.
5
Derive the Ruling
Conclude that in Hanafi usul, 'hamd' in the legal sense includes faith (root) and good deeds (branches), so the tree analogy is valid.
The ruling is not a fiqh hukm but a classification in usul: the scope of a term.

Primary vs Secondary Rulings from Texts: The Minassa Metaphor
Explaining that texts yield primary direct rulings and secondary rulings through ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish primary concepts (mufahimat awwaliyya) from secondary concepts (mufahimat thaniyya) and explain how secondary rulings are derived through ijtihad while still being divine.

Core Idea
Texts are the platform for both direct rulings and derived rulings through mujtahids' ijtihad.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies a metaphor used by the author of al-Tawdih: texts are like a platform (minassa) on which a bride (arus) is displayed. An objection arises: aren't some rulings directly from God without human reasoning? The commentator resolves by distinguishing primary concepts, directly understood from language, from secondary concepts, which are derived through ijtihad but still reflect divine will.

Guided Path
1
Understand the metaphor
Recognize the author's image: minassa (platform) for arus (bride). What do the texts and derived rulings correspond to?
Texts = platform; rulings = brides displayed.
2
Identify the objection
What is the apparent problem with the metaphor? It seems to reduce all rulings to human thought.
Objection: if texts are a platform for thinkers' thoughts, that denies direct divine rulings.
3
Locate the resolution
How does the commentator respond? He distinguishes two types of concepts.
Answer: secondary concepts are derived, primary are direct.
4
Define primary concepts
What are primary concepts? Give example from the text: 'aqimu al-salah'.
Primary = known from language alone, no ijtihad needed.
5
Define secondary concepts
What are secondary concepts? How are they related to qiyas? Why are they still divine?
Secondary = derived after reflection; qiyas manifests, not creates; both are divine.

The plural of feminine is 'ara'is' and of masculine is 'urus'. And in this speech there is a kind of unease... The purpose of objection and answer: Objection: The author made the texts a display place for the pure thoughts of the thinkers. This indicates that the meanings and concepts derived from texts are all a result of the thinkers' thoughts, which is contrary to reality because the meanings and concepts apparent to people are not a result of thinkers' thoughts but directly the rulings of the King, the Truth, the Manifest, which appear from the texts whether thinkers think or not. So making texts a result of thinkers' thoughts is incorrect. From 'fa ka'annahu' he gives the answer. Summary of answer: What the author intends is not those concepts and rulings that appear from the texts, but rather the derived intellectual rulings that are obtained after the mujtahids' reflection and deep thought. Such rulings are a result of the thinkers' thoughts, which are like brides, and the texts for them are like the minassa for the bride. And under another heading, the concepts and rulings derived from texts are of two types: (1) primary concepts which are known from knowledge of language and composition, like 'wa aqimu al-salah' (establish prayer): the ruling of obligation of prayer does not require any reasoning; these are not results of thinkers' thoughts but are obtained from texts. (2) secondary concepts, called secondary rulings, which are obtained after reflection, for which mujtahids exert thought and reflection, derive causes, extract realities and subtleties. These secondary rulings are undoubtedly results of thinkers' thoughts, and here by 'brides' these secondary concepts and subsidiary rulings are meant. And just as texts are a minassa for primary and original concepts, they are also a minassa for secondary (subsidiary) concepts. And both types are divine rulings, because analogy (qiyas) is not a positiver of rulings but a manifestation of rulings, and the place of manifestation is the texts. By his saying 'they come to know meanings and subtleties' the commentator points to these secondary concepts. Under another heading: The concepts and meanings apparent from texts have two aspects: (1) the actual reality that these rulings are from the King, the Truth, not that they are results of thinkers' thoughts; (2) the second aspect is to consider these concepts in terms of their manifestation to people and explanation to people. In this respect, these concepts and meanings are a result of thinkers' thoughts and the texts are a minassa for them, because when the mujtahid manifests and explains the meanings and concepts to people, it is correct to say that the texts are the place and minassa for the thoughts of the mujtahids. His saying 'and His decisive speech' i.e. His speech that separates between truth and falsehood, and His speech that is decisive, which the one addressed understands and does not confuse. 'Al-fasl' is a verbal noun in the sense of active or passive participle. This is a conjunction of the specific to the general, indicating the greatness of its matter and the magnificence of its status, since the Sunnah is of two types: speech and action, and speech is the subject for explaining the laws, upon which most rulings are based, and its authority is agreed upon among people.

Commentary
This passage is a lexical and conceptual expansion by the commentator (sharh) on the metaphor of 'minassa' (platform) and 'arus' (bride) used by the author (likely the matn of al-Tawdih). The commentator first explains the literal and terminological meaning of 'minassa', addressing a grammatical objection about the feminine ta' on a container word. Then he discusses the word 'arus', noting its morphological form and usage. He then points out that the author's metaphor might be problematic: it seems to suggest that rulings from texts are products of thinkers' thoughts, which is contrary to the belief that they are direct divine commands. The commentator resolves this by distinguishing between primary concepts/meanings (mufahimat awwaliyya) that are directly understood from language and composition without need for ijtihad, and secondary concepts (mufahimat thaniyya) that are derived through the ijtihad of mujtahids. The primary concepts are directly from God, while secondary concepts, though also ultimately divine because qiyas manifests rather than creates rulings, are products of human reasoning in terms of their exposition to people. Thus, the metaphor holds: texts are the platform (minassa) for the rulings (brides) derived by the mujtahids. This is a key usul al-fiqh point about the nature of istinbat and the role of ijtihad. The commentator also briefly discusses 'fasl al-khitab' as a characteristic of the Qur'an/sunnah. Throughout, the Hanafi perspective is that qiyas is a legitimate and necessary tool, but it does not create rulings; it merely uncovers what is already in the text or established by divine will.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion on the definition of the term 'asl' (root/principle) as used in the compound 'usul al-fiqh'. The commentator, Sa'd al-Din al-Taftazani, is expanding upon the text of al-Tawdih by Sadr al-Shari'ah. The core issue is whether 'asl' in 'usul al-fiqh' is to be understood in its lexical sense (ma yubna 'alayhi ghayruh – that upon which something is built) or in its technical sense (dalil – evidence). The passage first lists eleven technical meanings of 'asl', noting that Taftazani only mentions the last three: universal rule, evidence, and preponderant. Then it presents two views: some scholars hold that the technical meaning (evidence) is intended, but the author (presumably Sadr al-Shari'ah) indicates that the lexical meaning is intended, because resorting to metaphorical transfer (naql) without necessity is contrary to the principle of staying with the literal meaning. The linguistic meaning is further divided into physical building (ibna' hissi) and intellectual building (ibna' 'aqli). Through the addition 'usul al-fiqh', where 'fiqh' is a science, it becomes clear that intellectual building is meant, specifically the building of rulings upon evidence. Therefore, even though the end result is that 'usul' means 'evidences', this is reached via the lexical meaning without metaphorical transfer, unlike the opposing view which takes it as a metaphor. The commentator then refutes an objection that the lexical meaning is ambiguous while the technical meaning is clear, by pointing out that the context (idafah to fiqh) removes the ambiguity, and that the technical meaning would require unjustified metaphorical transfer.

Haqiqi and Ismi Definitions in Hanafi Usul
Defining and distinguishing definitions with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish haqiqi and ismi definitions, identify which is appropriate for a given term, and apply the Hanafi condition that haqiqi requires a real essence.

Core Idea
Haqiqi defines essences; ismi defines words; non-existents get only ismi.

Definitions in usul al-fiqh are not all the same. Some definitions capture the very essence of a thing (haqiqi), while others merely clarify the meaning of a word (ismi). The Hanafi school insists that haqiqi definitions can only be given to things that have a real essence in the external world; mere mental concepts do not qualify. This passage from Misk al-Maleeh explains the distinction, the sub-types of haqiqi definitions, and why non-existent entities like the mythical 'anqa can only be defined nominally. Understanding this classification is crucial when defining legal terms, because the type of definition affects how we derive and explain rulings.

Guided Path
1

Identify the two categories: haqiqi (real) and ismi (nominal). What is the key difference? Haqiqi relies on a real essence, ismi only on linguistic meaning.

2
Analyze the conditions for haqiqi definition
List the conditions: the thing must have a real essence (mahiyya haqiqiyya nafs al-amriyya), and the coiner's conceptualization must capture the essence itself (tasawwur bi'l-kunh).
If the conceptualization is only of aspects (tasawwur bi'l-wajh), the definition cannot be haqiqi.
3
Examine the sub-types of haqiqi definition
Identify the four sub-types mentioned: hadd tamm (complete by essential properties), hadd naqis (partial essential), rasm tamm (complete by accidents), rasm naqis (mixed).
These are inherited from Aristotelian logic but applied to Islamic legal concepts.
4

Commentary
This passage from Misk al-Maleeh (a commentary on al-Tawdih and al-Talwih) discusses the classification of definitions (ta'rifat) into two kinds: haqiqi (real) and ismi (nominal). The author begins by explaining the process of coining words (wad'): the coiner first conceptualizes a meaning, then assigns a word to it. The conceptualized meaning may either have a real essence (mahiyya haqiqiyya) that exists in the external world, or it may be a mere mental concept without external reality. If it has a real essence and the coiner's conceptualization captures that essence fully (tasawwur bi'l-kunh), then the definition that expresses that essence is called haqiqi. Haqiqi definitions are further subdivided into complete/incomplete by essence (hadd tamm/naqis) and by accidents (rasm tamm/naqis). On the other hand, if the definition merely clarifies the meaning of a word without delving into its essence — either by using a more famous synonym or by providing a descriptive phrase — it is called ismi. The key rule: non-existent things (like the mythical bird 'anqa) can only be defined ismi because they lack a real essence. Existent things can have both types depending on the purpose: if we aim to clarify the linguistic meaning, it is ismi; if we aim to capture the essence, it is haqiqi. The commentator then addresses an apparent contradiction in the author's words: the author seemed to imply that real essences only have haqiqi definitions, while the commentator argued they can also have ismi definitions. Taftazani provides three reconciliations: (1) the author's statement can be understood with a qualification — definition of real essences qua essences is haqiqi, but they can also be defined ismi when considered as linguistic concepts; (2) the author's statement may be interpreted as 'usually' (fi'l-akthar), not always; (3) the author's example does not imply exclusivity. Ultimately, the correct position (tahqiq) is that real essences can be defined both ways because they have two aspects: as realities in the external world (giving haqiqi definition) and as concepts in the mind (giving ismi definition). This distinction is fundamental in Hanafi usul al-fiqh for understanding how terms like 'wajib' or 'haram' are defined — whether by their essential nature or by their linguistic usage.

Key Terms
تعریف حقیقی (ta'rif haqiqi): Definition that depicts the essence (mahiyya) of a thing through its essential or accidental properties, based on a real essence in the external world.
تعریف اسمی (ta'rif ismi): Definition that clarifies the meaning of a word without capturing its essence, either by a synonym or a descriptive phrase.
تصور بالکنہ (tasawwur bi'l-kunh): Conceptualization that captures the very essence of a thing, as opposed to considering its aspects.
حد تام (hadd tamm): Complete definition by all essential properties (jins and fasl).

Distinguishing Haqiqi and Ismi Definitions in Usul al-Fiqh
Explains the distinction between haqiqi and ismi definitions in usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between haqiqi and ismi definitions, apply the majority Hanafi position that ismi is a type of haqiqi, and evaluate Taftazani's contrary view.

Core Idea
A definition's type depends on the question; haqiqi seeks essence, ismi recovers meaning.

In usul al-fiqh, definitions (ta'rifat) are tools for precision. The type of definition you need depends on what you are trying to know: the reality of something or its lexical meaning. This passage from Sharh al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih clarifies the difference between haqiqi (real) and ismi (conceptual) definitions, showing how they relate and where scholars differ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definition questions
Find in the text the two interrogative particles: 'ma sharhah' (asking about the meaning/concept) and 'ma haqiqiyyah' (asking about the essence/reality). Note that the answer to the first is an ismi definition, to the second a haqiqi definition.
These are known as ma sharhah ismiyyah and ma haqiqiyyah.
2
Understand the coinage (wad') context
The text explains that before coinsining a name, the coiner (wadi') conceptualizes the meaning. This conceptualization is the basis for ismi definition. Haqiqi definition, however, does not require coinage in itself, though it is mentioned for teaching purposes.
The debate on necessity of wad' for haqiqi is addressed in the benefits section.

The text gives the example of defining a triangle as 'a figure enclosed by three sides'. Before knowing whether triangles exist, this is an ismi definition; after proving their existence, the same words become a haqiqi definition.
This illustrates that the same verbal expression can serve both roles depending on epistemic context.
4
Review the four types of haqiqi definition
The text lists: hadd tam (complete definition), hadd naqis (incomplete definition), rasm tam (complete description), rasm naqis (incomplete description). Identify the essential vs. accidental attributes.
These are standard categories in Hanafi logic (mantiq) and usul.
5
Examine the dispute with Taftazani
The passage criticizes Taftazani for equating ismi and lafzi definitions. According to the majority (jomhur), ismi is a subtype of haqiqi. The practical consequence: in usul works, definitions are either haqiqi or lafzi, not three co-equal types.
This ikhtilaf affects how one reads and applies definitions in fiqh.

The first thing is that with 'ma sharhah' the explanation of a name and its meaning and concept are determined. Then with 'hal basitah' the existence of the named thing, meaning and concept of that name is sought. Then with 'ma haqiqiyyah' the reality and essence of that named thing is sought. This detail is for the case where real essences are taken as being named by names. (2) The second case is that real essences are taken as being the meaning and concept of the name and the object of the coiner's intention at the time he was coining them. So from this perspective, the definition of the real essence will be an ismi definition because it occurs as an answer to 'ma sharhah' which is used to seek the concept of the name or the object of the coiner's intention. From this investigation it is established that the definition of real essences is sometimes haqiqi and sometimes ismi. If the question was about the reality, the answer will be a haqiqi definition; if the question was about the lexical meaning and concept, the answer will be an ismi definition. And this definition may be the very essence of that thing, etc. Here the commentator Taftazani states that sometimes haqiqi and ismi definitions become identical, and an ismi definition is sometimes the very essence and reality of that thing, in the sense that the coiner's intended object is the very reality and essence, and sometimes it is not the essence but the meaning and concept. For example, if the coiner's intended object is the very reality of a human, then mentioning it as that it is the meaning and concept for which it was coined is an ismi definition, and mentioning it as that it is human reality is a haqiqi definition. On this basis, the verifying scholars have declared that one and the same thing can be both an ismi definition and a haqiqi definition, and both can be combined in one expression. For example, if a thing is defined before knowledge and affirmation of its existence, that definition is ismi, and after knowledge of the thing's existence, that same ismi definition becomes haqiqi. For instance, in the principles of geometry, if a triangle is defined as 'a figure enclosed by three sides (i.e., three lines)', that is an ismi definition; then when the existence of a triangle is known by proof, this same ismi definition becomes haqiqi. First is that the coiner, before coinage, conceptualizes in his mind what is to be coined (i.e., he brings its meaning and concept to mind; these are called secondary concepts) so that he may coin a name for it. (2) Then he coins a name for it. (3) The named thing is also called the concept of the name and the coined thing. (4) There is a reality and essence of the named thing. So the definition of the named thing from the perspective that it is something coined for and a concept for it, cutting off consideration of the real essence, is an ismi definition. In this, the lexical meaning and concept are explained. Lexicons have been authored for this purpose; this is why ismi definitions are given in lexicons. But if the reality of the named thing is defined, that is a haqiqi definition. Commentary
This passage from Sharh al-Tawdih wa al-Talwih is an extensive discussion differentiating two types of definition (ta'rif) in the terminology of usul al-fiqh: haqiqi (real) and ismi (conceptual/lexical). The author begins by explaining that the type of definition depends on the question. The question 'ma sharhah' seeks the meaning and concept of a name, while 'ma haqiqiyyah' seeks the essence and reality. Thus, if the query is about the essence, the response is a haqiqi definition; if about the lexical meaning, the response is an ismi definition. The commentator Taftazani then elaborates that sometimes an ismi definition can coincide with the very essence of the thing if the coiner's intention was that essence. The example of a triangle illustrates that a definition can transition from ismi to haqiqi once the existence of the thing is established. The text then presents several 'fawa'id' (benefits) clarifying further: the process of coinage by the wadi' (coiner), the four types of haqiqi definition (hadd tam, hadd naqis, rasm tam, rasm naqis), and multiple distinctions (furuq) between haqiqi and ismi definitions, such as: haqiqi provides a new mental image while ismi recovers an existing one; haqiqi applies only to real essences whereas ismi applies also to mental concepts and non-existent things; ismi definitions are lexical and precede haqiqi definitions. A notable critical refinement appears at the end where the author corrects Taftazani: Taftazani equates ismi and lafzi definitions, but according to the majority (jomhur), ismi definition is a subtype of haqiqi, not opposed to it; the true opposite of haqiqi is lafzi (verbal) definition. Taftazani's example 'al-ghudanfar al-asad' is actually lafzi, not ismi. This reflects the Hanafi usul hierarchy where definitions are categorized by their object and function.

Key Terms
التعريف الحقيقي (al-ta'rif al-haqiqi): A definition that conveys the essence (mahiyyah) of a thing, providing a new mental image, answering the question 'ma haqiqiyyah'.
التعريف الاسمي (al-ta'rif al-ismi): A definition that explains the lexical meaning and concept of a name, recovering an image already in the mind, answering the question 'ma sharhah'.
ما شارحة (ma sharhah): A question seeking the explanation of a name's meaning and concept (the ma used for ismi definition).
ما حقيقية (ma haqiqiyyah): A question seeking the reality and essence of a thing (the ma used for haqiqi definition).
وضع (wad'): The act of coining a word for a meaning; a necessary condition for ismi definition, but not for haqiqi definition according to the majority, though mentioned for teaching purposes.

The Meaning of Ma laha wa Ma alayha in the Definition of Fiqh: Tasawwur or Tasdiq?
Analyzing whether 'ma laha wa ma alayha' refers to tasawwur or tasdiq in the definition of fiqh.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the Hanafi definition of fiqh by distinguishing between tasawwur and tasdiq, and apply the concept of wijdaniyat to determine what is excluded by the requirement of proof.

Core Idea
Fiqh is affirmation of rulings (ahkam) through proof, not mere conceptualization or affirmation of existence.

In this passage, the commentator examines a subtle but critical point in the classical Hanafi definition of fiqh: what exactly does 'ma laha wa ma alayha' (what is for it and against it) refer to? Two interpretations are tested: tasawwur (mere conceptualization) and tasdiq bi al-thubut (affirmation of existence). Both are rejected, leading to the correct understanding that fiqh is the affirmation of the rulings themselves. We'll also see how the requirement of 'proof' in the definition excludes merely sensory knowledge but not the rulings of sensory matters.

Identify the two proposed meanings
The passage states two possibilities for what 'ma laha wa ma alayha' could mean: (1) tasawwur (conceptualization) or (2) tasdiq bi al-thubut (affirmation of existence).
2
Evaluate the first possibility (tasawwur)
The passage rejects tasawwur because fiqh is not mere conceptualization; it deals with issues (masail) and aims for affirmation. Moreover, it would make scholar and layman equal.
Conceptualization alone does not distinguish the scholar from the layman.
3
Evaluate the second possibility (tasdiq bi al-thubut)
The passage rejects tasdiq bi al-thubut as absurd: affirming the existence of duties is trivial and not what fiqh means.
Fiqh is about rulings, not mere existence.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of the fourth probability (ihtimal) regarding the definition of fiqh as 'ma'rifat al-nafs ma laha wa ma alayha'. The commentator (al-Taftazani or the author of the sharh) discusses what is meant by 'ma'rifah'. Two possibilities are examined: first, that it means mere conceptualization (tasawwur) of one's duties; second, that it means affirmation of their existence (tasdiq bi al-thubut). Both are rejected. Fiqh is not mere conceptualization, because that would make a scholar and a layman equal (both can conceptualize prayer). It is also not affirmation of existence, because that is trivial. Rather, fiqh is the affirmation of the rulings (ahkam) of those duties, e.g., that prayer is obligatory, wine is forbidden. The commentator then addresses an objection: if 'ma'rifah' is defined as 'acquiring particulars through proof', then found things (wijdaniyat)—things known by direct sensation, like the obligation of justice?—would be excluded. The objection is that the matn (presumably Sadr al-Shariah) thought a special restriction 'practical' is needed, but actually the word 'ma'rifah' itself excludes them. The response: found things have two aspects—their existence (known by sensation) and their rulings (known by proof). Since the definition refers to knowledge of rulings, found things are not excluded; their rulings are derived by proof. The Hanafi position is that fiqh is knowledge of rulings derived from detailed proofs.

Key Terms
معرفة (ma'rifah): Acquiring particulars through proof (كتساب الجزئيات عن دليل)
تصور (tasawwur): Mere conceptualization without judgment
تصديق (tasdiq): Affirmation with judgment (e.g., that prayer is obligatory)
وجدانيات (wijdaniyat): Things known by direct sensation or inner experience

Defining Quran: Whole and Part Without Commonality of Sharing
Response to objection about Quran's meaning covering whole and part

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi position that Quran applies to both whole and part by a single coinage avoids the prohibition of umum al-ishtirak.

Core Idea
The Quran is one reality; its coinage covers both the entire revelation and any portion without needing separate meanings.

In this passage, the commentator addresses an important objection: if the term 'Quran' refers to both the entire revelation and any part of it, it would seem to have two meanings, leading to a prohibited commonality of sharing (umum al-ishtirak). The Hanafi response clarifies that Quran has a single coinage that encompasses both the whole and the part, thus no commonality arises. This lesson unpacks that logical move.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection states that if Quran means both the whole and a part, it would have two meanings, causing commonality of sharing (umum al-ishtirak) which is prohibited in Hanafi usul.
2
Understand the Hanafi response
The response argues that Quran has a single coinage (wad') that covers both the whole and part; it is not two separate coinages.
Key: 'وضع واحد' (single coinage)
3
Distinguish coinage types
Having two coinages would cause comum al-ishtirak; one coinage covering both does not. The part is included in the whole's coinage.
4
Apply to the definition
The definition 'what has been transmitted to us' (ma nqila ilayna) captures both the entire Quran and any portion, as it is a single description.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary (sharh) on Al-Tawdih deals with the definition of 'Quran' and 'Mushaf' and the logical problem of circularity (dawr) in definitions. The commentator is addressing objections raised against the definition of Quran as 'what has been transmitted to us' (ma nqila ilayna) and the definition of Mushaf as 'what contains the Quran' (ma jumi'a fihi al-Quran). The core issue is that if one defines both terms in relation to each other, a vicious circle arises: to know Quran one needs to know Mushaf, and to know Mushaf one needs to know Quran. The passage explores several attempts to avoid this circularity:

First, it is suggested that the definition of Quran is not intended to define its essence but merely to distinguish the word from other books or from eternal speech; in that case, knowledge of Mushaf can be taken from common custom, so no circularity. However, if one intends a true definition of essence (mahiyyah), circularity returns.

Second, some argued that circularity is avoided because the definition of Mushaf mentions Quran, not Kitab, and the definition of Quran is for Kitab. But the commentator refutes this by asserting the synonymy (taraduf) of Kitab and Quran—they are two names for the same reality. Therefore, whichever term is used, the circularity remains.

Third, others proposed redefining Mushaf as 'what contains the revealed revelation' (ma jumi'a fihi al-wahy al-munazzal) or 'what contains pages' (ma jumi'a fihi al-suhuf), which would avoid mentioning Quran. The commentator rejects these as well because 'revealed revelation' is synonymous with Quran, and defining Mushaf by 'pages' is a deviation from its well-established customary meaning (both linguistic and Sharia custom) which has become a reality (haqiqah) for the meaning 'what contains the Quran'. In usul, definitions should avoid such deviation from the apparent and from reality to metaphor.

Throughout, the commentator uses rational arguments (dalil aqli) to critique the proposed solutions, emphasizing the logical requirements of definitions and the principle that in circularity the aspect of dependence must be identical. The passage concludes with a new objection and the beginning of its response, but the full response is not given here.

This analysis demonstrates the rigorous logical methodology of Hanafi usul al-fiqh in defining foundational terms and the importance of avoiding circular definitions.

Avoiding Circularity in Defining Quran and Mushaf
Analysis of circularity (dawr) in definitions and attempted solutions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the logical problem of dawr (circularity) between Quran and Mushaf and evaluate the Hanafi refutations of proposed solutions.

Core Idea
Defining Quran by Mushaf and Mushaf by Quran creates a vicious circle unless one definition is not essential but merely identificatory.

The commentator now addresses the central issue: if we define Quran as 'what is contained in the Mushaf' and Mushaf as 'what contains the Quran', we fall into circularity. The passage examines several attempts to break this circle and explains why they fail according to Hanafi usul. This lesson walks through those attempts and the logical principles used to refute them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core circularity
If we define Quran as 'what is written in the Mushaf' and Mushaf as 'what contains the Quran', then to know Quran we need Mushaf, and to know Mushaf we need Quran. This is circular (dawr).
Circularity requires unity of aspect: if the dependency is on different aspects, it may not be circular.
2
Examine the first attempted solution
Some argue that the definition of Quran is for Kitab (the Book), not Quran itself, so no circularity. The commentator refutes by stating that Kitab and Quran are synonymous.
Synonymy (taraduf) means that Kitab and Quran refer to the same reality.
3
Examine the second attempted solution
Others propose defining Mushaf as 'what contains the revealed revelation' (al-wahy al-munazzal) instead of 'what contains the Quran'. This also fails because 'revealed revelation' is synonymous with Quran.
4
Examine the third attempted solution
Another proposal: define Mushaf as 'what contains pages' (ma jumi'a fihi al-suhuf). The commentator rejects this because it deviates from the apparent (zahir) and from the reality (haqiqah) in Sharia custom.
In definitions, deviation from the apparent or from reality to metaphor is not commendable.
5
Conclude the principle
To avoid circularity, the aspect of dependence must be different; if the defined term appears in the definition directly or through synonyms, the circle remains intact.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary (sharh) on Al-Tawdih deals with the definition of 'Quran' and 'Mushaf' and the logical problem of circularity (dawr) in definitions. The commentator is addressing objections raised against the definition of Quran as 'what has been transmitted to us' (ma nqila ilayna) and the definition of Mushaf as 'what contains the Quran' (ma jumi'a fihi al-Quran). The core issue is that if one defines both terms in relation to each other, a vicious circle arises: to know Quran one needs to know Mushaf, and to know Mushaf one needs to know Quran. The passage explores several attempts to avoid this circularity:

First, it is suggested that the definition of Quran is not intended to define its essence but merely to distinguish the word from other books or from eternal speech; in that case, knowledge of Mushaf can be taken from common custom, so no circularity. However, if one intends a true definition of essence (mahiyyah), circularity returns.

Second, some argued that circularity is avoided because the definition of Mushaf mentions Quran, not Kitab, and the definition of Quran is for Kitab. But the commentator refutes this by asserting the synonymy (taraduf) of Kitab and Quran—they are two names for the same reality. Therefore, whichever term is used, the circularity remains.

Third, others proposed redefining Mushaf as 'what contains the revealed revelation' (ma jumi'a fihi al-wahy al-munazzal) or 'what contains pages' (ma jumi'a fihi al-suhuf), which would avoid mentioning Quran. The commentator rejects these as well because 'revealed revelation' is synonymous with Quran, and defining Mushaf by 'pages' is a deviation from its well-established customary meaning (both linguistic and Sharia custom) which has become a reality (haqiqah) for the meaning 'what contains the Quran'. In usul, definitions should avoid such deviation from the apparent and from reality to metaphor.

Throughout, the commentator uses rational arguments (dalil aqli) to critique the proposed solutions, emphasizing the logical requirements of definitions and the principle that in circularity the aspect of dependence must be identical. The passage concludes with a new objection and the beginning of its response, but the full response is not given here.

This analysis demonstrates the rigorous logical methodology of Hanafi usul al-fiqh in defining foundational terms and the importance of avoiding circular definitions.

Key Terms
القرآن (al-Quran): The revealed speech of Allah transmitted to us, which is both whole (entire Quran) and part (any portion).
المصحف (al-Mushaf): The physical codex containing the written Quran.
الدور (al-dawr): Circularity in definition where term A depends on term B and term B depends on term A.
الترادف (al-taraduf): Synonymy: two different words referring to the same reality.

Scientific and Practical Derivation (Ishtiqaq 'Ilmi and 'Amali)
Definition and classification of ishtiqaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between scientific and practical derivation, state their definitions, and identify their relevance in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Scientific derivation is theoretical word relation; practical derivation is actual coinage for action.

The commentary opens with a classification of derivation (ishtiqaq) into two types: scientific and practical. Understanding this distinction is foundational because it affects how we treat derived words in legal interpretation—whether mere linguistic knowledge or actual legal effect. The definitions hinge on the presence or absence of 'action' (amal).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of ishtiqaq
The commentator states that derivation is explained sometimes by considering knowledge. There are two divisions: scientific derivation and practical derivation.
2
Define scientific derivation
Scientific derivation is only to the extent of knowledge; from it only the knowledge of derivation is obtained, no disposal or action. The definition: 'you find between two words a proportional relationship in original meaning and compositional letters, and in the scientific level one is referred to the other.'
This is a theoretical linguistic relationship.
3
Define practical derivation
Practical derivation involves action and disposal. The definition: 'you take from a word what is appropriate to it... and make this word indicative of a meaning that has proportion with the meaning of the first word.'
This results in actual legal usage and rulings.
4
Contrast the two
Scientific derivation is purely academic; practical derivation has operational consequences in fiqh (e.g., deriving rulings through qiyas based on word relationships).
The Hanafi school emphasizes practical derivation for istinbat.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on Al-Tawdih by Sadr al-Shariah, with critical remarks from Taftazani. It covers several key usul al-fiqh concepts. First, it distinguishes between scientific derivation (ishtiqaq 'ilmi) and practical derivation (ishtiqaq 'amali). Scientific derivation is a theoretical understanding of the relationship between words, while practical derivation involves actually coining a new word from another. The passage then tackles the definition of 'mutlaq' (absolute). Taftazani objects to the author's view that mutlaq is placed for the essence/named itself; Taftazani argues it is placed for an unspecified individual, citing Qur'anic evidence (e.g., 'tahrīr raqaba' meaning an unspecified slave). The author's defender replies that the original placement is for the essence, but context (qarā'in) indicates an individual. Next, the passage discusses the definitions of nakirah (indefinite) and ma'rifah (definite). The author initially gave a division based on intention of specific or shared individuals, but that was incomplete. So the author provided general definitions: nakirah is 'what is placed for a thing not specified' (mā wuḍi'a li shay' lā bi'aynihi), and ma'rifah is 'what is placed for a thing specified' (mā wuḍi'a li shay' bi'aynihi). Taftazani criticizes the additional conditions 'at the time of absolute usage' ('ind al-iṭlāq) and 'for the listener' (li al-sāmi'), arguing that specification should be based on the coinage (waḍ') and indication, not on usage context. He gives an example: 'jā'ani rajul' (a man came to me) – the word 'rajul' may be specified in context but is still indefinite by coinage. The response is that the author's conditions are meant to reflect the coiners' consideration of the listener's state. Finally, the passage discusses the distinction between types by aspects (ḥaythiyyāt) and not by essence. It notes that aspects can be compatible or contradictory. For example, a word can be both common (mushtarak) and general ('āmm) from different aspects, but general and specific (khāṣṣ) cannot combine in one word. The doubt about 'nakirah mawsufa' (indefinite qualified) being both general and specific is acknowledged and deferred. The passage ends expressing difficulty in fully clarifying the author's intent.

The Definition of Mutlaq: Dispute between Author and Taftazani
Dispute on the definition of mutlaq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two positions on the definition of mutlaq, evaluate the Hanafi response, and apply the practical consequence to interpreting absolute commands.

Core Idea
Mutlaq is placed for the essence without restriction, but context (qarīna) restricts it to an individual.

The commentator presents a dispute between the author (Sadr al-Shariah) and Taftazani over the definition of 'mutlaq' (absolute). Taftazani objects that mutlaq is not placed for the named essence but for an unspecified individual, citing Quranic 'tahrir raqaba'. The Hanafi response defends the author: original placement is for the essence, but contextual clues indicate the individual. This dispute has practical implications for how absolute commands in the Quran are fulfilled.

Guided Path
1
State the author's definition of mutlaq
From the author's expression, mutlaq is taken to mean the named without restriction, i.e., the concept itself, not an individual.
2
Understand Taftazani's objection
Taftazani argues that mutlaq is placed for an unspecified individual (fard ghayr mu'ayyan), not for the concept. He adduces 'tahrir raqaba' from the Quran, arguing it means emancipation of an unspecified slave, not the concept of 'slave'.
This is a key example in usul.
3
Examine the Hanafi response
The defender of the author says: the original placement of mutlaq is for the named essence, but then from context (qara'in) it becomes clear that a specific individual is intended. Thus the author's definition is correct, and the example aligns with it because context shows an individual is meant.
4
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The dispute matters when encountering an absolute command (amr mutlaq): does one have to fulfill it by any individual instance or by the essence itself? According to the author, one must act on the essence, but context may limit. According to Taftazani, the individual is directly intended without context.
This affects how we understand 'aqimu al-salah' – does it command the concept of prayer or the actual performance?

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on Al-Tawdih by Sadr al-Shariah, with critical remarks from Taftazani. It covers several key usul al-fiqh concepts. First, it distinguishes between scientific derivation (ishtiqaq 'ilmi) and practical derivation (ishtiqaq 'amali). Scientific derivation is a theoretical understanding of the relationship between words, while practical derivation involves actually coining a new word from another. The passage then tackles the definition of 'mutlaq' (absolute). Taftazani objects to the author's view that mutlaq is placed for the essence/named itself; Taftazani argues it is placed for an unspecified individual, citing Qur'anic evidence (e.g., 'tahrīr raqaba' meaning an unspecified slave). The author's defender replies that the original placement is for the essence, but context (qarā'in) indicates an individual. Next, the passage discusses the definitions of nakirah (indefinite) and ma'rifah (definite). The author initially gave a division based on intention of specific or shared individuals, but that was incomplete. So the author provided general definitions: nakirah is 'what is placed for a thing not specified' (mā wuḍi'a li shay' lā bi'aynihi), and ma'rifah is 'what is placed for a thing specified' (mā wuḍi'a li shay' bi'aynihi). Taftazani criticizes the additional conditions 'at the time of absolute usage' ('ind al-iṭlāq) and 'for the listener' (li al-sāmi'), arguing that specification should be based on the coinage (waḍ') and indication, not on usage context. He gives an example: 'jā'ani rajul' (a man came to me) – the word 'rajul' may be specified in context but is still indefinite by coinage. The response is that the author's conditions are meant to reflect the coiners' consideration of the listener's state. Finally, the passage discusses the distinction between types by aspects (ḥaythiyyāt) and not by essence. It notes that aspects can be compatible or contradictory. For example, a word can be both common (mushtarak) and general ('āmm) from different aspects, but general and specific (khāṣṣ) cannot combine in one word. The doubt about 'nakirah mawsufa' (indefinite qualified) being both general and specific is acknowledged and deferred. The passage ends expressing difficulty in fully clarifying the author's intent.

Definitions of Nakirah and Ma'rifah: Conditions and Dispute
Refinement of definitions and dispute over conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi definitions of nakirah and ma'rifah, critique Taftazani's objection regarding additional conditions, and justify the inclusion of 'ind al-iṭlāq' and 'li al-sāmi'.'

Core Idea
Nakirah is placed for unspecified thing; ma'rifah for specified, with the coiner's consideration of the listener's state in absolute usage.

The commentator refines the definitions of indefinite (nakirah) and definite (ma'rifah) after an initial incomplete division. The author provides general definitions: nakirah is 'what is placed for a thing not specified' and ma'rifah is 'what is placed for a thing specified'. Taftazani objects to the added conditions 'at the time of absolute usage' ('ind al-iṭlāq) and 'for the listener' (li al-sāmi'), arguing that definiteness depends only on wad'. The Hanafi response defends these conditions as reflecting the coiner's intention.

1
Identify the initial incomplete division
The author initially divided nouns based on whether the speaker intends specific individuals (ma'hood) or shared individuals (mushakkara). This division covers only some types of nakirah and ma'rifah.
2
Grasp the general definitions
To include all types, the author defined nakirah as 'what is placed for a thing not specified' (mā wuḍi'a li shay' lā bi'aynihi) and ma'rifah as 'what is placed for a thing specified'. These cover both individuals and concepts.
3
Understand Taftazani's objection

The author initially gave a division based on intention of specific or shared individuals, but that was incomplete. So the author provided general definitions: nakirah is 'what is placed for a thing not specified' (mā wuḍi'a li shay' lā bi'aynihi), and ma'rifah is 'what is placed for a thing specified' (mā wuḍi'a li shay' bi'aynihi). For example, a word can be both common (mushtarak) and general ('āmm) from different aspects, but general and specific (khāṣṣ) cannot combine in one word. Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on Al-Tawdih by Sadr al-Shariah, with critical remarks from Taftazani. It covers several key usul al-fiqh concepts. First, it distinguishes between scientific derivation (ishtiqaq 'ilmi) and practical derivation (ishtiqaq 'amali). Scientific derivation is a theoretical understanding of the relationship between words, while practical derivation involves actually coining a new word from another. The passage then tackles the definition of 'mutlaq' (absolute). Taftazani objects to the author's view that mutlaq is placed for the essence/named itself; Taftazani argues it is placed for an unspecified individual, citing Qur'anic evidence (e.g., 'tahrīr raqaba' meaning an unspecified slave). The author's defender replies that the original placement is for the essence, but context (qarā'in) indicates an individual. Next, the passage discusses the definitions of nakirah (indefinite) and ma'rifah (definite). The author initially gave a division based on intention of specific or shared individuals, but that was incomplete. So the author provided general definitions: nakirah is 'what is placed for a thing not specified' (mā wuḍi'a li shay' lā bi'aynihi), and ma'rifah is 'what is placed for a thing specified' (mā wuḍi'a li shay' bi'aynihi). Taftazani criticizes the additional conditions 'at the time of absolute usage' ('ind al-iṭlāq) and 'for the listener' (li al-sāmi'), arguing that specification should be based on the coinage (waḍ') and indication, not on usage context. He gives an example: 'jā'ani rajul' (a man came to me) – the word 'rajul' may be specified in context but is still indefinite by coinage. The response is that the author's conditions are meant to reflect the coiners' consideration of the listener's state. Finally, the passage discusses the distinction between types by aspects (ḥaythiyyāt) and not by essence. It notes that aspects can be compatible or contradictory. For example, a word can be both common (mushtarak) and general ('āmm) from different aspects, but general and specific (khāṣṣ) cannot combine in one word. The doubt about 'nakirah mawsufa' (indefinite qualified) being both general and specific is acknowledged and deferred. The passage ends expressing difficulty in fully clarifying the author's intent.

Key Takeaway
Classification of words in usul depends on aspects (haythiyyāt); some aspects are compatible (e.g., 'amm and mushtarak), others contradictory (e.g., 'amm and khāṣṣ), and apparent contradictions like nakirah mawsufa are resolved by analyzing which aspect applies.
