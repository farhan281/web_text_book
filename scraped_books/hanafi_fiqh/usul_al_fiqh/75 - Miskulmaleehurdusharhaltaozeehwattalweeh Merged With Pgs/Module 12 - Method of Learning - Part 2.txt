The Objection of Self-Priority and the Famous Answer
Resolving the logical paradox of haythiyya as both intermediary and accident via the famous answer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the objection of taqaddum al-shay' 'ala nafsihi against the restrictive aspect and explain how the famous answer resolves it by distinguishing possibility from actuality.

Core Idea
The restrictive aspect is potential, the accident is actual – no self-priority.

In this passage, Taftazani presents a famous logical objection against making the restrictive aspect (ḥaythiyya taqyīdiyya) a genuine part of the subject definition. The objection is that if the aspect is both the intermediary and the accident, self-priority results. The first answer avoids this by separating possibility from actuality, preserving the coherence of the subject definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection's core paradox
The objection states that if the restrictive aspect is made a qayd, it must serve as intermediary for all essential accidents. But if it is also itself an essential accident, then it would be both prior (as cause) and posterior (as effect). This is 'priority of a thing over itself' (taqaddum al-shay' 'ala nafsihi), a logical absurdity.
This objection arises only if the aspect is seen as the actual cause (wāsiṭa) of occurrence.
2
Understand the famous answer's key distinction
The famous answer differentiates between the intermediary as 'possibility' (imkān/istiʿdād) and the accident as 'actuality' (nafs al-shay'). For example, 'from the aspect of health' in the subject of medicine means possibility of health, while the essential accident 'health' is health itself. Since possibility and actuality are different, the intermediary and mediated are not identical, avoiding self-priority.
This distinction allows the aspect to be both a restrictive qayd (as possibility) and part of the essential accidents (as actuality) without contradiction.
3
Apply the distinction to the examples
In medicine, the subject is 'the human body from the aspect of health and sickness'. The aspect refers to possibility of health/sickness. The accidents investigated are actual health and sickness, which occur to the body through the possibility. In physics, 'body from the aspect of motion and stillness' similarly distinguishes possibility of motion from actual motion.
Thus the objection is resolved: the intermediary (possibility) is not the same as the accident (actuality).

So the subject becomes the locus, and the essential accidents become the accidents, and the aspect becomes the intermediary between them. When the aspect is an intermediary between the essential accidents and the subject, then this intermediary itself, i.e., the aspect, will not be included among those essential accidents, because it is an intermediary for the accidents. So how can it be included among the accidents, which are the mediated ones? There is a distinction between the intermediary and the mediated. For example, a collector says: 'I will accept those applications that reach me through Allah Bakhsh the patwari.' So Allah Bakhsh is the intermediary; now he himself will not be included among the applicants. Also, if this restrictive aspect is made from the essential accidents, a great flaw follows: the restrictive aspect serves as an intermediary for the accidents, and those accidents become mediated. The rule is that the intermediary precedes the mediated, because the intermediary is the cause for the mediated, and the cause precedes the effect. Therefore this restrictive aspect will be prior to the essential accidents. Then this aspect will be both the intermediary and the essential accident. In terms of being an intermediary, this aspect is prior; in terms of being an accident, it is posterior. Commentary
This passage is a detailed resolution of a logical objection in defining the subject-matter (mawḍūʿ) of a science. The commentator Taftazānī (d. 792/1390) addresses a well-known problem that arises when the 'aspect' (ḥaythiyya) is taken as a restrictive qualifier (qayd) of the subject, rather than as merely explanatory. The objection is that if the aspect (e.g., 'from the aspect of health and sickness') is supposed to be the intermediary (wāsiṭa) through which all essential accidents (aʿrāḍ dhātiyya) occur, then the aspect itself cannot simultaneously be one of those accidents, because that would require the aspect to be both prior (as intermediary) and posterior (as accident) — an absurdity called 'priority of a thing over itself' (taqaddum al-shayʾ ʿalā nafsihi). Taftazānī states that this objection arises if we view the aspect as an intermediary for actual occurrence. He then gives two responses. The first response (the 'famous' answer) distinguishes between 'possibility' (imkān/istiʿdād) and 'actuality' (nafs al-shayʾ). The restrictive aspect refers to the potentiality, e.g., 'from the aspect of health' means possibility of health, while the essential accident is the actual health itself. Thus the intermediary (potential) and the mediated (actual) are different, avoiding self-priority. The second response (the 'research-based' answer) reinterprets the function of the restrictive aspect: its purpose is not to be the actual cause of occurrence, but merely to specify the perspective from which the subject is investigated in the science. When investigating essential accidents, one must keep this aspect in view, regardless of whether the accidents actually occur through that aspect or not. This reframes the role of the aspect from ontological intermediacy to methodological specification. Both answers preserve the coherence of the definition of a science's subject.

Key Terms
حيثية تقيدية (ḥaythiyya taqyīdiyya): The aspect that restricts the subject; it is the perspective that determines which essential accidents are investigated in a science.
عارض ذاتي (ʿāraḍ dhātī): An essential accident that belongs to the subject by definition and is investigated in the science.
واسطة (wāsiṭa): Intermediary; here, the means by which essential accidents occur to the subject. The passage debates whether the ḥaythiyya is always the intermediary.
تقدم الشيء على نفسه (taqaddum al-shayʾ ʿalā nafsihi): The absurd situation where one and the same thing is both prior and posterior to itself; the objection raised against the initial understanding of ḥaythiyya taqyidiyya.

The Research-Based Answer: Aspect as Methodological Focus
Resolving the logical paradox of haythiyya as both intermediary and accident via the research answer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the research-based answer reinterprets the restrictive aspect as a methodological focus rather than an actual intermediary for accident occurrence.

Core Idea
The qayd is for investigation focus, not for causing occurrence – no self-priority.

Taftazani's second, research-based answer offers a more profound solution: the restrictive aspect (ḥaythiyya taqyīdiyya) does not function as the cause of accident occurrence at all. Instead, it simply indicates the perspective one must maintain when investigating the essential accidents. This reframes the purpose of the qayd from ontological intermediacy to methodological specification.

Guided Path
1
Redefine the purpose of the restrictive qayd
The research answer states that the definition of a subject is 'that regarding whose essential accidents investigation takes place'. Adding a restrictive aspect (e.g., 'from the aspect of health') does not mean the aspect is the cause of occurrence. Rather, it means that when investigating those accidents, one must keep that aspect in view as a focusing lens.
This avoids assuming any causal role for the aspect.
2
Distinguish investigation from occurrence
The answer draws a crucial distinction: the occurrence (ʿurūḍ/liḥāq) of essential accidents to the subject is a specific ontological matter, while the investigation (baḥth) of those accidents in the science is a general epistemological matter. Some accidents may occur without being directly related to the aspect; but the aspect still governs how they are studied.
For example, medicine may investigate the effects of exercise on the body, which are not directly about health/sickness, but the health aspect still frames the study.
3
Apply the distinction to resolve the paradox
Since the aspect is only about investigation, it does not claim to be the intermediary for actual occurrence. Therefore, even if the aspect itself is also studied as an accident (e.g., health itself), there is no self-priority because the aspect as investigation focus is not prior to itself in the causal sense.
The aspect as methodological focus is not the same as the aspect as accident.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed resolution of a logical objection in defining the subject-matter (mawḍūʿ) of a science. The commentator Taftazānī (d. 792/1390) addresses a well-known problem that arises when the 'aspect' (ḥaythiyya) is taken as a restrictive qualifier (qayd) of the subject, rather than as merely explanatory. The objection is that if the aspect (e.g., 'from the aspect of health and sickness') is supposed to be the intermediary (wāsiṭa) through which all essential accidents (aʿrāḍ dhātiyya) occur, then the aspect itself cannot simultaneously be one of those accidents, because that would require the aspect to be both prior (as intermediary) and posterior (as accident) — an absurdity called 'priority of a thing over itself' (taqaddum al-shayʾ ʿalā nafsihi). Taftazānī states that this objection arises if we view the aspect as an intermediary for actual occurrence. He then gives two responses. The first response (the 'famous' answer) distinguishes between 'possibility' (imkān/istiʿdād) and 'actuality' (nafs al-shayʾ). The restrictive aspect refers to the potentiality, e.g., 'from the aspect of health' means possibility of health, while the essential accident is the actual health itself. Thus the intermediary (potential) and the mediated (actual) are different, avoiding self-priority. The second response (the 'research-based' answer) reinterprets the function of the restrictive aspect: its purpose is not to be the actual cause of occurrence, but merely to specify the perspective from which the subject is investigated in the science. When investigating essential accidents, one must keep this aspect in view, regardless of whether the accidents actually occur through that aspect or not. This reframes the role of the aspect from ontological intermediacy to methodological specification. Both answers preserve the coherence of the definition of a science's subject.

Example: The science of health has as its subject celestial bodies insofar as they have composition, and the science of heaven and the world has as its subject celestial bodies insofar as they have nature; thus they are two sciences with one subject, and the distinction is by essential accidents. The aspect here is declarative, external to the subject. And the investigators have mentioned this. There is no dispute in terminology: we have our terminology and you have yours. And God knows best.

Commentary
This passage is a clarification (expansion) of the principle that sciences are distinguished by their predicates (essential accidents) and not solely by their subjects. The author responds to a potential objection: if a single entity can serve as the subject for multiple sciences, then how do those sciences differ? He answers that the differentiation arises from the different essential accidents studied in each science. He provides an example from the physical sciences: the science of health (medicine?) and the science of astronomy both study celestial bodies, but from different aspects (composition vs. nature). The 'aspect' (haythiyya) is considered declarative (bayaniyya), meaning it does not form part of the subject but merely indicates which accidents are investigated. This aligns with the Hanafi usul position that the identity of a science is determined by its objectives and investigating principles, not solely by its subject matter. The author concludes by acknowledging that this is a matter of terminological convention (istilah), and there is no forceful disagreement.

Can Multiple Sciences Share One Subject? The Hanafi Position on Tashāruk al-ʿUlūm
Usul dispute: can one subject serve multiple sciences?

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on a single subject serving multiple sciences, explain the proof using essential accidents, and evaluate Taftazānī's objections.

Core Idea
A single entity can be the subject of multiple sciences if each science studies different essential accidents of that entity.

In Usul al-Fiqh, the definition of a science often hinges on its subject. The majority of scholars held that each science must have a unique subject, but the Hanafi school (following Ṣadr al-Sharīʿa) argued that a single entity can serve as the subject of multiple sciences, provided each investigates a different set of essential accidents. This lesson unpacks the proof, the example of geometry and celestial science, and the subsequent objections raised by Taftazānī.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
The issue is whether a single entity (e.g., celestial bodies) can be the subject of more than one science. The majority deny it; the Hanafi author affirms both permissibility and actuality.
This is an ikhtilāf about the definition of a science and the role of its subject.
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
The author argues that a single thing can have multiple types of essential accidents (aʿrāḍ dhātiyya). Each science can focus on one type, making them distinct despite sharing the same subject.
The predicates (maḥmūlāt) are the essential accidents; the unity of a science is based on its predicates, not merely the subject.
3
Examine the example: geometry and celestial science
Both sciences study celestial bodies. Geometry (ʿilm al-hayʾa) studies their shapes, while celestial science (ʿilm al-samāʾ wa al-ʿālam) studies their natures. The subject (celestial bodies) is identical; the aspects (ḥaythiyya) are merely conditions for the accidents, not parts of the subject.
This example is used to show actual occurrence (wuqūʿ) in reality.
4
Analyze Taftazānī's first objection
Taftazānī objects to the division of aspects into 'subject-part' and 'explanatory' categories, claiming that all aspects are merely conditions. This challenges the author's reliance on the aspect being external to the subject.
The text notes that the author has already responded, but the objection forces a refinement of the position.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
This dispute affects how we define and separate Islamic sciences. For example, Fiqh and Usul al-Fiqh both study the actions of a mukallaf, but Fiqh focuses on specific rulings (e.g., obligatory, prohibited) while Usul investigates the proofs and methodology. They share a subject but differ in predicates.
Understanding this helps in correctly classifying disciplines and avoiding confusion.

So the conclusion is that sometimes a science differs due to the difference of essential accidents, even if the subject is unified. Commentary
This passage is a dispute in Usul al-Fiqh concerning whether a single entity can serve as the subject (mawḍūʿ) of multiple sciences. The text is from a commentary on Al-Tawdīḥ and Al-Talwīḥ, two core works in the Hanafi usul tradition. The author (Ṣadr al-Sharīʿa) argues against the majority (jumhūr) that it is both logically possible and actually occurs for multiple sciences to share one subject. His proof: a single object can possess different types of essential accidents (aʿrāḍ dhātiyya); each science investigates a distinct type. For example, the science of geometry studies celestial bodies in terms of shape, while the science of celestial nature studies them in terms of their physical properties. The aspect (ḥaythiyya) under which the subject is considered is not part of the subject itself, but a condition for the accidents. The commentator Taftazānī then raises objections, arguing that the distinction between a subject-part aspect and an explanatory aspect is invalid; both are merely qualifying conditions. The passage ends mid-objection, leaving the discussion open. This is a nuanced debate that touches on the definition of a science, the role of the subject, and the nature of essential accidents. It is important for students to understand that in the Hanafi usul framework, the unity of a science is determined not only by its subject but also by the predicates (maḥmūlāt) discussed.

Key Terms
عوارض ذاتیہ (aʿrāḍ dhātiyya): Essential accidents: those attributes that are intrinsically related to the subject and are studied in a science.
موضوع (mawḍūʿ): The subject of a science: the entity whose states and accidents are investigated.
محمولات (maḥmūlāt): Predicates: the attributes or accidents attributed to the subject in the propositions of a science.
حیثیت (ḥaythiyya): The aspect or consideration under which the subject is viewed; it qualifies how the subject is studied.

Sciences are distinguished by their subject matter, not by predicates; predicates only cause fragmentation when lacking a common genus.

In this lesson, we explore a foundational methodological question in usul al-fiqh: what defines the boundaries of a science? The author argues, following the Ḥanafī tradition, that the subject matter (mawḍūʿ) is the sole basis for unity and distinction. He then refutes objections from al-Taftāzānī, who suggests that predicates (essential accidents) also play a role. Understanding this debate clarifies why fiqh remains one science despite its diverse rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position and its foundation
Identify the claim: sciences are distinguished by their subjects, not by predicates. Note the supporting reasoning: the subject is known (maʿlūm) while predicates are sought (maṭlūb).
Focus on the phrase 'subject is known, predicate is unknown'.
2
Analyze Taftāzānī's first objection and the Hanafi response
Taftāzānī argues that the predicate itself (as an essential accident) is also known in essence, and both subject and predicate have unknown details. The author counters by emphasizing that the predicate's knownness does not make it the primary basis.
Look for the statement: 'the predicate itself, which is an essential accident, is also known like the subject'.
3
Examine the third objection and the answer
Taftāzānī warns that if a single subject can host multiple sciences, fiqh could be split into separate sciences for wujūb, nadb, etc. The answer: fragmentation does not occur when all essential accidents share a common genus (jins).
The example of naḥw (grammar) with rafʿ, naṣb, jarr all under iʿrāb illustrates the point.
4
Extract the principle of generic unity
The lesson concludes that predicates cause division only if they lack a common genus. In fiqh, all essential accidents (wujūb, nadb, etc.) fall under the genus 'sharʿī ḥukm', preserving the unity of the science.
Relate to the Hanafi view that the subject (act of mukallaf) plus the generic unity of its accidents keeps fiqh as one science.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute about the basis for distinguishing different sciences, particularly in the context of defining fiqh. The author argues that sciences are distinguished by their subject matter (mawḍūʿ), not by their predicates (maḥmūlāt) or essential accidents. He first outlines the established position: scholars determine the subject of a science and then investigate all its essential accidents. The unity of a science is due to the unity of its subject, even if the predicates (the accidents) are diverse. The commentator (likely referring to Taftāzānī) objects that predicates cannot be the basis because they are unknown, while the subject is known. The author refutes this, stating that the predicate itself (the essential accident) is also known in its essence, and both subject and predicate have unknown details. Then a third objection is raised: if a single subject could belong to multiple sciences based on different predicates, then fiqh—which deals with the act of the mukallaf—could be split into separate sciences for wujūb, nadb, ibāḥa, etc. The author answers that this fragmentation does not occur if all the essential accidents share a common genus, as in naḥw where rafʿ, naṣb, and jarr all share the genus iʿrāb. Thus, the unity of science is preserved by the generic unity of its essential accidents, even if they differ in species. The passage concludes with the commentator's remark that the verification of these investigations is as stated.

Key Terms
موضوع (mawḍūʿ): The subject matter of a science, the thing whose essential accidents are investigated.
محمول (maḥmūl): A predicate, an attribute (ʿaraḍ) attributed to the subject. In the context of sciences, it refers to the essential accidents discussed in a science.
عرض ذاتی (ʿaraḍ dhātī): An essential accident, a property that necessarily follows from the nature of the subject and is not separable from it.

Two Types of Definition: Essential and Descriptive (Had and Rasm)
Explaining types of definition to resolve circularity in Quran definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between essential (had) and descriptive (rasm) definitions and apply this distinction to resolve objections of circularity in defining the Quran.

Core Idea
Circularity invalidates essential definitions but is harmless in descriptive definitions.

This lesson explores a key distinction in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the difference between essential definitions that capture the quiddity of a thing and descriptive definitions that merely identify it. The commentator uses this distinction to refute an objection of circularity in the definition of the Quran, showing that the definition is descriptive, not essential.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two types of definition
Grasp that had (essential definition) expresses real quiddity via genus and differentia, while rasm (descriptive definition) identifies the defined by its distinctive properties without capturing essence.
The text lists five differences between them.
2
Identify the key differences
Review the five differences: (i) had requires genus and differentia, rasm does not; (ii) had answers 'what is reality?', rasm answers 'which one?'; (iii) had cannot rely on pointing, rasm can; (iv) circularity invalidates had but not rasm; (v) had requires knowledge of all parts, rasm does not.
Focus on difference (iv) for circularity.
3
Recognize the circularity problem
If the definition of the Quran were had, it would require prior knowledge of the Quran's quiddity, creating a vicious circle since the definition itself is supposed to provide that knowledge.
4
Apply the distinction to the Quran definition
The definition 'what has been transmitted to us' is a rasm because its purpose is to identify which meaning of Quran (recited vs. eternal) is intended. It uses transmission as a distinguishing property, not a genus-differentia analysis.
The text says it answers 'which Quran?' not 'what is the reality of Quran?'
5
Conclude that circularity is avoided
Since the definition is descriptive (rasm), circularity does not occur because identification does not presuppose knowledge of the defined quiddity. The objection is thus resolved.
The commentator calls this a thorough answer.

I want to know which type of definition this definition of al-Kitab or al-Quran is, because the completion of the first answer regarding circularity depends on this investigation. The investigation is that definition is of two types: (1) Sometimes the purpose of definition is to express the real essential quiddity of the defined; this is called had (essential definition). (2) Sometimes the purpose of definition is not to express the quiddity and reality, but rather to distinguish the defined from others and to determine and identify it; this is called rasm (descriptive definition). For example, when asked 'Which Zaid?' not intending to give specific characteristics distinguishing him from others, the answer is 'He is such-and-such name, resident of such place, from such tribe, such appearance' — these are specifics; mentioning them distinguishes Zaid from others. There are several differences between the two definitions: (1) In essential definition, mentioning genus and differentia is necessary, while in determination and identification it is not necessary; mere mention of properties and specifics suffices. (2) If the purpose is to express quiddity, the question is 'what is its reality?'; if determination and identification of the defined is intended, the question is 'which one?'. (3) If expressing quiddity is intended, pointing or reputation in custom is not sufficient; definition is necessary. If determination and identification of the defined is intended, that can be achieved by pointing and custom; definition is not necessary. (4) If the purpose is to express quiddity and reality, circularity is harmful in such definition; if the purpose is determination and identification of the defined, circularity is not harmful and is permissible. (5) In essential definition, knowledge of all parts of the definition is necessary to clarify the defined; in identification, knowledge of all parts is not necessary; pointing etc. suffices. Now we must see which of these two types the mentioned definition of al-Kitab or al-Quran is: is it an expression of the quiddity of the Noble Quran or its determination and identification (i.e., rasm)? If it is a definition of the quiddity of Kitab or Quran, then circularity is necessary; if it is determination and identification of Kitab and Quran, then circularity does not occur because circularity does not occur in determination and identification. So the author says: The definition given by our scholars (what has been transmitted to us), whether it is a definition of Kitab or Quran, in both cases it is not a definition of the quiddity of Kitab or the quiddity of Quran; rather, it is their determination and identification. This is why this definition occurs in answer to 'Which Kitab? or Which Quran?', e.g., 'Which book do you intend?' The answer comes: 'What has been transmitted to us...' because the word Quran applies to two meanings: (1) Sometimes al-Quran applies to pre-eternal speech and internal speech, which is an attribute of Allah Almighty. (2) Sometimes Quran applies to verbal speech, which indicates the pre-eternal speech and is recited by the servants. When the Noble Quran applies to two meanings, the question arises: 'Which meaning do you intend?' The answer given is: 'What has been transmitted to us...' meaning our intention is that Quran which is recited on the tongues of the servants, not the pre-eternal speech. Now by this definition, the defined (Quran) is determined, identified, and distinguished from pre-eternal speech. So this is personal determination, not an expression of quiddity. When it is established that this definition is determination and identification, then circularity does not arise because circularity does not occur in determination and identification. Circularity occurs only in essential definition of quiddity. If here too the purpose was a definition of the quiddity of Kitab or the quiddity of Quran, then circularity would arise because in that case knowledge of the quiddity of the Mushaf is necessary, and knowledge of the Mushaf is not sufficient by pointing etc., and knowledge of the Mushaf is dependent on knowledge of the quiddity of Quran, so circularity occurs. Now that it is determination and identification, no circularity occurs, thus the objection is removed. This answer to the circularity objection is investigative, while the first answer was cursory; a final coercive answer will come.

The commentator distinguishes between essential definition (had) and descriptive definition (rasm). Descriptive definition only aims to identify and distinguish the defined from others, using specific properties. The commentator then applies this distinction to the definition of the Quran: the definition 'what has been transmitted to us' (manqul ilayna) is not meant to express the essential nature of the Quran (which is the eternal speech of Allah), but rather to identify which of the two meanings of Quran (eternal speech vs. recited speech) is intended. Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of the definition of two key terms in usul al-fiqh: 'al-Kitab' and 'al-Quran'. The commentator (sharih) is expanding on the matn (base text), which likely said 'al-Kitab ay al-Quran' (the Book, meaning the Quran). The discussion proceeds in several steps:

1. Linguistic and conventional meanings: 'Kitab' literally means 'written', and in Shari'ah convention it refers specifically to the Book of Allah inscribed in the copies (masahif). 'Quran' literally means 'recitation', and in convention it refers to the specific collection of Allah's speech recited by the people. The commentator notes that 'Quran' is more specific and famous than 'Kitab' because 'Kitab' can be used for other books, whereas 'Quran' is exclusively for the divine book.

2. The phrase 'al-Kitab ay al-Quran' in the matn is a verbal definition (tafsir), not a real definition (ta'rif haqiqi). The real definition must distinguish the defined from everything else; it begins with 'al-Munazzal ala al-Rasul' (revealed to the Messenger). The word 'al-Quran' here is merely explanatory, not part of the definition.

3. The commentator refutes two groups who mistakenly include 'al-Quran' in the definition of 'al-Kitab':
   - First group: They start the definition with 'al-Quran'. The commentator argues this leads to circularity (dawr) because 'Kitab' and 'Quran' are synonyms; defining one by the other is like defining a thing by itself. To avoid this, 'al-Quran' must be excluded from the definition and treated only as an explanation.
   - Second group: They argue that 'al-Quran' in the definition is taken linguistically as 'al-maqru' (the recited), which is a general term covering both Quran and other recited texts, thus avoiding circularity because the defined ('Kitab Allah') is a subset of 'al-maqru'. The commentator refutes this by stating that it goes against convention ('urf'); in common usage, 'Quran' unequivocally means the specific Book of Allah. Interpreting it otherwise would be a departure from the apparent meaning (khilaf al-zahir) when there is a perfectly valid alternative: making it an explanation.

4. The passage then addresses a presumed question: Why does the definition of 'Kitab Allah' apply to the whole collection but not to individual verses, when usuliyun need a definition applicable to each verse as evidence? The commentator (Taftazani) answers that according to usuliyun, the term 'Quran' is common between the whole and every part. Therefore, the definition they provide uses attributes common to both: being a miracle, revealed to the Messenger, written in copies, and transmitted by mutawatir. Some scholars include all four attributes; some include only revelation and inimitability (arguing writing and transmission are not essential, as the Quran existed before writing); some include three (revelation, writing, transmission) because later generations only know the Quran through these.

This passage exemplifies the Hanafi usul method of precise definition, logical consistency (avoiding circularity), and deference to established convention ('urf) over purely linguistic possibilities.

Key Terms
الكتاب (al-kitab): In Shari'ah convention, it means the specific collection of Allah's speech established in the copies (masahif).
القرآن (al-quran): In common convention, it is the specific collection of Allah's speech recited on the tongues of the servants, revealed to Prophet Muhammad, written in copies, and transmitted mutawatir.
التعريف اللفظي (al-ta'rif al-lafzi): A definition that explains the word itself using a more famous equivalent, not providing essential attributes. Here, 'al-Kitab ay al-Quran' is a verbal definition.
الدور (al-dawr): Circularity in definition: defining a term by itself or its synonym, which is a logical fallacy. Occurs if 'al-Quran' is included as part of the definition of 'al-Kitab'.

Circularity in Definitions: Tafsir vs. Mahiyya
Resolving circularity in definitions of Quran and Mushaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between an identificatory definition (tafsir) and an essential definition (mahiyya), and apply the Hanafi usul rule that circularity (dawr) invalidates only essential definitions, not identificatory ones.

Core Idea
Circularity in definition is only fatal when defining essence (mahiyya); identificatory definitions can rely on custom without circularity.

This passage from Taftazani's commentary addresses a classic usul issue: can we define one thing in terms of another, and then define the second in terms of the first? Taftazani explains that it depends on the purpose of the definition. If we are merely identifying (tafsir) the Quran as 'what has been transmitted to us,' we can rely on customary knowledge of the Mushaf to avoid circularity. But if we are defining the essence (mahiyya) of the Quran, then defining it by Mushaf and Mushaf by Quran would be circular and invalid. This distinction is crucial for Hanafi usul methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of definition
Recognize the difference between tafsir (identificatory definition) and hadd (essential definition). Tafsir aims to distinguish an object from others; hadd aims to capture its essence.
Hanafis often call tafsir 'ta'rif lafzi' and hadd 'ta'rif haqiqi'.
2
Understand circularity (dawr)
Circularity occurs when the definition of A depends on the definition of B, and the definition of B depends on A. This is logically invalid for essential definitions.
In usul, such definitions are called 'ta'rif bi al-'ahnaf'.
3
Apply the distinction to the Quran-Mushaf case
If defining 'Quran' as 'what is transmitted to us' (tafsir), knowledge of 'Mushaf' is taken from custom (urf), breaking the dependency cycle. If defining the essence (mahiyya) of Quran, it would depend on Mushaf, and Mushaf's essence depends on Quran, creating circularity.
Custom (urf) provides knowledge without needing a separate definition.
4
Evaluate the rebuttal
Some argued no circularity because the defined term in 'ma nuqila ilayna' is Kitab, not Quran. Taftazani rejects this because the overall dependency chain (Kitab depends on Mushaf, Mushaf depends on Quran) remains circular.

Resolving Circularity in the Definition of the Qur'an
Responding to the objection of circularity in the definition of the Qur'an

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the objection of circularity (dawr) in the definition of the Qur'an and analyze the Hanafi response using the distinction between conceptual meaning and specific collective.

Core Idea
Defining the Qur'an by the Mushaf is not circular if the definiendum is conceptual and the definiens is a specific collective known by direct acquaintance.

When the Qur'an is defined as 'the book between two covers' (Mushaf), an opponent may object that this creates a circle: the Qur'an is defined by the Mushaf, and the Mushaf is defined by containing the Qur'an. In this passage, the commentator addresses this objection by distinguishing the conceptual meaning of the Qur'an from the specific collective (the physical Mushaf). You will learn how this distinction breaks the circularity and why the definition is logically sound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection is that defining the Qur'an by the Mushaf leads to circularity (dawr) because the Mushaf itself is defined in terms of the Qur'an. Write down the two definitions that form the circle.
Circle: Qur'an → Mushaf → Qur'an.
2
Understand the first response (adam al-ta'limi)
The first response argues that the definiendum (the Qur'an as defined by usul scholars) is a conceptual meaning (ma'na kulli) that applies to both the whole and its parts. The definiens (Mushaf) refers not to this conceptual meaning but to a specific collective (majmu' shakhsi). Knowledge of the specific collective does not depend on the conceptual meaning because people know the Mushaf directly. Therefore, no circle.
Key: different aspects (jihaat) break the circle.
3
Analyze the rebuttal to the first response
The commentator then rebuts this response: the specific collective actually depends on the conceptual meaning as its genus. The quiddity (mahiyyah) of the Mushaf depends on the specific collective, which in turn depends on the conceptual meaning, recreating the circle. Recognize this as a deeper logical analysis.
Counter-rebuttal: dependence on conceptual meaning is inevitable.
4
Examine the second response (jawaab ta'limi)
Even if we concede that the specific collective is independent, the definition of the Qur'an as 'the book between two covers' is still a definition of the specific collective (Mushaf). But the definition of the Mushaf also uses 'al-Qur'an' in the same specific sense, so the circle remains. The commentator implies that the definition is actually an identification (tashkhis), not a definition of quiddity, which avoids the circle.
Resolution: the definition is not a real definition but a descriptive identification.

The Qur'an's Indefinability and Refuting Ibn al-Hajib
Analyzing the impossibility of defining the Qur'an and critiquing Ibn al-Hajib's definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi claim that the Qur'an is a personal entity (shakhsi) and apply the logical argument to critique opposing definitions.

Core Idea
The Qur'an cannot be defined by a logical hadd; it is a unique personal entity known only by pointing.

In this lesson, we delve into a central usul dispute: can the Qur'an be defined by a logical definition (hadd)? The Hanafi position, as articulated by the author of this text, is that the Qur'an is a personal entity (shakhsi) and thus indefinable. We will walk through the syllogism, examine two interpretations of 'shakhsi', and see how the author turns the tables on Ibn al-Hajib's own definition by exposing a circularity dilemma.

Guided Path
1
State the syllogism
Identify the two premises: Major: 'Every personal entity (shakhsi) cannot be defined.' Minor: 'The Qur'an is a personal entity.' Conclusion: 'The Qur'an cannot be defined.' This is the foundation of the Hanafi argument.
The proof is aqli (rational), based on the nature of definition and personal entities.
2
Understand why a shakhsi is indefinable
Analyze the definition of hadd: a statement that gives knowledge by comprising parts and being predicated. Since a personal entity (like a unique individual) has no parts and is known only by pointing (isharah), it cannot satisfy the conditions of a hadd.
This is the major premise's proof.
3
Examine the two interpretations of shakhsi
The first (invalid) would make the Qur'an only the words as recited by Jibril; the second (valid) sees the Qur'an as specific words with a unique composition, irrespective of reciter. The author commits to the valid interpretation to avoid denying the present Mushaf.
Understand why the first interpretation is rejected and how the second avoids the problem of multiplicity.
4
Analyze Ibn al-Hajib's definition
Ibn al-Hajib defined the Qur'an as 'speech sent down for miraculousness with a surah from it.' The author forces a dilemma: if Ibn al-Hajib means a definition of essence (mahiyyah), his definition is circular because surah is defined as a portion of the Qur'an. If he means mere identification (ta'yin), then no circularity, but his definition is not a real hadd.
This refutes Ibn al-Hajib's objection of circularity against the Hanafi position.
5
Evaluate the outcome
The Hanafi position stands: the Qur'an is shakhsi and indefinable. Ibn al-Hajib's own definition fails either as a real definition or as a non‑circular identification. Thus the author’s conclusion that the Qur'an is not subject to hadd is validated.
The thamarah al‑khilaf: the Hanafi view refrains from offering a logical definition of the Qur'an, while opponents who attempt one face logical flaws.

(The author intended to explain that the Qur'an is not capable of definition...). In the previous passage, the author stated that what has been transmitted to us is not the definition (essence) of the Noble Qur'an, but rather its identification and specification. Now the author is presenting this investigation: whether the Pure Qur'an can be defined or not. He says that the Pure Qur'an is not at all capable of definition; its definition is simply impossible. Thus, from the author's side comes the claim: 'The Qur'an is not definable.' Its proof in the form of a minor and major premise would be: 'The Qur'an is a personal entity (shakhsi), and every personal entity is indefinable.' The author proves both the minor and major premises with evidence. The statement 'Indeed, definition (hadd) is a statement that gives knowledge of a thing, comprises the parts of that thing, and is predicated on it' is proof of the major premise: that a personal entity cannot be defined because a definition is a statement that gives knowledge of a thing, comprises its parts, and is predicated on it. From such a definition, knowledge of a personal entity cannot be obtained; rather, for knowledge of a personal entity, knowledge of its specific characteristics (mushakhkhasat) is necessary, and knowledge of its specific characteristics comes by pointing (isharah), not by definition. This is the proof of the major premise. Next, the proof of the minor premise—'the Qur'an is a personal entity'—is stated from 'and when you know that.' There are two sayings regarding the 'personality' (being a person) of the Noble Qur'an: one is unreliable and the other reliable. The unreliable one is that the Noble Qur'an is a personal entity because Gabriel brought it and recited it; now only those words that subsist with the tongue of Gabriel are called Qur'an, because the words of the Qur'an subsist with a single locus and person. In this case, the Noble Qur'an would be a real personal entity that absolutely does not admit multiplicity. When its being personal is established, the major premise is applied: 'A personal entity cannot be defined.' Therefore, the Pure Qur'an also cannot be defined. Thus the claim is proven. But this meaning of 'personal' (shakhsi) is unreliable because if that were the case, it would necessitate that the Noble Qur'an is only that which subsists with the tongue of Gabriel, and the present Qur'an that Zayd, 'Amr, Bakr, etc. recite is not that Qur'an but rather a likeness of the Qur'an—and the matter is not so. (2) The reliable saying regarding the definition of 'personal' (shakhsi) is that by 'being personal' is meant that the Noble Qur'an is the name of specific words that are composed with a specific composition; no other book has such a composition. The point is that it is personal (shakhsi) whether Zayd recites it, 'Amr recites it, or Khalid recites it. This is the reliable and true saying. When its being personal is established, by applying the major premise the result is obtained: 'Every personal entity is indefinable; therefore, the Qur'an is indefinable.' So our statement that the personal entity is not definable has two interpretations. From here, the author is answering an objection. The objection is: 'You have given a second meaning of shakhsi and deemed it true, but that is not correct because a personal entity does not apply to many and is not subject to multiplicity. You made the meaning of shakhsi as specific words with a specific composition; with this meaning, the Noble Qur'an applies to many: one is the Qur'an that Gabriel recited, one is that which the Master of the Universe (peace be upon him) recited, likewise the Companions recited, Zayd recited, etc. So there would be multiple Qur'ans; how would it be specific?' Answer: The author replies that according to this meaning, the Qur'an being personal has two interpretations: (1) The first interpretation is that by 'being personal' it is meant that the Qur'an is not literally a person (shakhs bi-'aynihi), but it resembles a person (shabih bi-shakhs). The resemblance is in the sense that just as knowledge of a personal entity is not obtained by definition but depends on pointing etc., similarly knowledge of the Pure Qur'an cannot be obtained by definition but comes by pointing etc., such as reciting from beginning to end and saying 'This is the Qur'an.' So when it resembles a personal entity, it is correct to say that just as a personal entity cannot be defined, likewise the Noble Qur'an cannot be defined. (2) The second interpretation is that although the Qur'an in this meaning is subject to multiplicity and applies to many, we call those words that are considered with a specific composition and with these specific characteristics 'personal' (shakhsi). Our terminology is that specific words that are considered together with their characteristics become a person (shakhs) by combining their characteristics. Because words are accidents (a'rad), and accidents, together with their specific characteristics, reach such a limit that they become personal and do not admit multiplicity; there is no multiplicity in their essence, but multiplicity only occurs with respect to the locus (mahall). For example, a specific poem is a specific person; there is no multiplicity in its essence. So this person exists by virtue of these accidents and characteristics; multiplicity only occurs due to the locus. Similarly, the Qur'an is a collection of specific, specified words; there is no multiplicity in its essence. And when we have established the terminology to call these specific words 'personal,' then proving that a personal entity is not definable is correct for this. Thus, when asked about the Qur'an, in response it will not be defined, but from beginning to end it will be recited and said: 'It is these specific words,' because without that, knowledge of it is simply not possible. And Ibn al-Hajib defined (the Qur'an). The author had given a definition; in it, 'Allamah Ibn al-Hajib had objected to circularity (luzum al-dawr). Now, as a response to that, the author is objecting to Ibn al-Hajib's definition. Ibn al-Hajib defined: 'The Qur'an is the speech sent down for miraculousness (i'jaz) with a surah from it.' The author asks: 'O Ibn al-Hajib! Are you defining the essence of the Qur'an or its identification and specification? If you are defining the essence, then the objection of circularity also applies to your definition because the essence of the Qur'an depends on the essence of a surah. Then when asked about the surah, 'What is the surah?', it is defined as 'a portion of the Qur'an,' so the essence of the surah depends on the Qur'an, thus circularity (dawr) occurs. And if you are doing identification and specification, and by 'surah' you intend the well-known and understood among people, then the objection of circularity does not apply to you nor to us. In summary: if you adopt the first alternative, the objection of circularity applies to both us and you; if you adopt the second alternative, then neither you nor we face the objection.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul discussion on whether the Qur'an can be defined by a logical definition (hadd). The author sets out to prove that the Qur'an is indefinable because it is a personal entity (shakhsi). He first establishes the major premise: a personal entity cannot be defined, because a definition is a statement that gives knowledge of a thing by enumerating its parts and is predicated on it, but personal entities are known only by pointing (isharah), not by such definitions. Then he establishes the minor premise: the Qur'an is a personal entity. He presents two interpretations of 'shakhsi': the first (invalid) would mean that the Qur'an is only the words as recited by Gabriel, which would deny that the recited Qur'an by Muslims is the actual Qur'an—this is unacceptable. The second (valid) interpretation is that the Qur'an is the specific words with a unique composition that cannot be found elsewhere; even if recited by different people, it remains the same personal entity. The author then addresses an objection: if shakhsi means specific words, then multiple recitations would imply multiple Qur'ans. He responds with two ta'wils: either the Qur'an is 'like' a personal entity in that it is known only by pointing, or in our terminology, such specific words, being accidents, acquire a kind of personal identity through their specific characteristics, and multiplicity is only in the locus (e.g., the reciter), not in the essence. Finally, the author turns to a critique of Ibn al-Hajib's definition of the Qur'an as 'speech sent down for miraculousness with a surah from it.' He forces a dilemma: if Ibn al-Hajib means to define the essence (mahiyyah), then circularity (dawr) arises because the Qur'an is defined by surah, and surah is defined as a portion of the Qur'an. If instead he intends mere identification (ta'yin), using 'surah' as a known reference among people, then the definition avoids circularity—but it also means his definition is not a real definition. Thus the author defends his own position that the Qur'an is not subject to hadd and that Ibn al-Hajib's objection of circularity against his view fails.

Key Terms
الحد (al-hadd): A statement that gives knowledge of a thing, comprises the parts of that thing, and is predicated on it (al-qawl al-mufid li-ma'rifat al-shay' al-mushtamil 'ala ajza' dhalika al-shay' al-mahmul 'alayhi).
شخصی (shakhsi): A personal entity that is unique and cannot be known by definition but only by pointing (isharah); in the context of the Qur'an, it refers to the specific words with a unique composition that are not subject to multiplicity in essence.
دور (dawr): Circularity in definition, where the definition of term A depends on term B, and the definition of term B depends on term A.

Key Takeaway
The Qur'an's uniqueness as a personal entity makes it indefinable by logical definition; any attempt to define it leads to circularity or mere identification.

The Qur'an as an Indefinable Shakhs: Two Interpretations
Defining the Qur'an and critiquing Ibn al-Hajib

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that the Qur'an is not subject to a real definition (hadd haqiqi) and explain the two interpretations that support its status as a shakhs.

Core Idea
The Qur'an is a shakhs: it cannot be defined, only identified by recitation.

This lesson examines why the Hanafi school holds that the Qur'an cannot be defined by a logical definition (hadd). The author provides two interpretations for the Qur'an being considered a shakhs, and then critiques Ibn al-Hajib's definition, exposing a circularity problem. Understanding this helps us grasp the nature of divine speech in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Understand the two interpretations of shakhs
Identify the first interpretation: the Qur'an resembles a shakhs in requiring indication, not definition. The second: in Hanafi terminology, specific words with particular composition become a shakhs.
Note the key difference: resemblance vs terminological designation.
2
Analyze the critique of Ibn al-Hajib's definition
Read the author's objection: if Ibn al-Hajib's definition is of essence (mahiyyah), it leads to circularity because 'surah' depends on 'Qur'an' and vice versa. If it is identification (tashkhis), it is acceptable.
This shows the importance of distinguishing between real definition and specification.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) expanding on the matn of Al-Tawdih. It addresses a core usul al-fiqh issue: whether the Qur'an can be defined by a logical definition (hadd). The author provides two interpretations for why the Qur'an is considered a shakhs (individual). First, the Qur'an resembles a shakhs in that it cannot be known through a definition but only through direct indication (isharah) or recitation. Second, in Hanafi terminology, specific words with particular composition and attributes are called shakhs because they become indivisible individuals, similar to a poem. The author then critiques Ibn al-Hajib's definition of the Qur'an as 'speech sent down for inimitability, smallest part a surah.' The author argues that if Ibn al-Hajib intends a definition of essence (mahiyyah), then circularity arises because 'surah' is defined in terms of 'Qur'an'. If Ibn al-Hajib intends identification (tashkhis), then the definition is acceptable and no circularity. The passage then explains why a shakhs cannot be defined: definition provides knowledge by genus and differentia, but a shakhs is known only through its specifics (mushakhkhisat), which are indicated by pointing or a proper name. The objection that a shakhs is a composite (murakkab i'tibari) of essence and individuation and thus definable is addressed. The author, following Taftazani, rejects the answer that composite entities have only verbal definitions, because if that were the case, the Qur'an itself would be a composite entity, and the author's elaborate arguments for its individuality would be unnecessary. Finally, two possibilities for defining a shakhs are examined: (1) using only essentials fails to distinguish among individuals; (2) including accidents fails because accidents change, so the definition would not be constant. Thus, a shakhs truly cannot be defined by a real definition.

Key Terms
شخصی (shakhs): An individual entity that cannot be defined by a logical definition because its specifics are not captured by genus and differentia
حد (hadd): A definition that comprises the essential components of a thing, providing knowledge of its essence
ماہیت (mahiyyah): Essence; what a thing is in itself
تشخیص (tashkhis): Identification or specification of an individual

Why a Shakhs Cannot Be Defined: The Composite Entity Objection and Its Refutation
Logical argument for indefinability of shakhs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a shakhs (individual) cannot be defined by a real definition, and evaluate the objection that a shakhs is a composite entity (murakkab i'tibari) and therefore definable.

Core Idea
A shakhs cannot be defined because its specifics are not captured by genus and differentia; including accidents makes the definition impermanent.

This lesson delves into the logical arguments for the indefinability of a shakhs. The commentator explains why definition fails for individuals, and then addresses the objection that a shakhs is a composite entity of essence and individuation, which might make it definable. Through a series of answers and refinements, the Hanafi position is defended.

Understand the proof that a shakhs cannot be defined
The commentator argues: definition aims for knowledge of the defined; knowledge of a shakhs requires determination of its specifics; specifics are indicated by pointing or proper names, not by genus and differentia; hence definition is impossible.
This is the main argument establishing the major premise.
2
Examine the objection from composite entity
An objector claims that a shakhs is a composite of essence and individuation, so it can be defined by including both in the definition.
This challenges the major premise by suggesting a way to include specifics.

Evaluate the Hanafi response via Taftazani

This shows the weakness of the verbal definition answer.

This conclusively proves the point.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) expanding on the matn of Al-Tawdih. It addresses a core usul al-fiqh issue: whether the Qur'an can be defined by a logical definition (hadd). The author provides two interpretations for why the Qur'an is considered a shakhs (individual). First, the Qur'an resembles a shakhs in that it cannot be known through a definition but only through direct indication (isharah) or recitation. Second, in Hanafi terminology, specific words with particular composition and attributes are called shakhs because they become indivisible individuals, similar to a poem. The author then critiques Ibn al-Hajib's definition of the Qur'an as 'speech sent down for inimitability, smallest part a surah.' The author argues that if Ibn al-Hajib intends a definition of essence (mahiyyah), then circularity arises because 'surah' is defined in terms of 'Qur'an'. If Ibn al-Hajib intends identification (tashkhis), then the definition is acceptable and no circularity. The passage then explains why a shakhs cannot be defined: definition provides knowledge by genus and differentia, but a shakhs is known only through its specifics (mushakhkhisat), which are indicated by pointing or a proper name. The objection that a shakhs is a composite (murakkab i'tibari) of essence and individuation and thus definable is addressed. The author, following Taftazani, rejects the answer that composite entities have only verbal definitions, because if that were the case, the Qur'an itself would be a composite entity, and the author's elaborate arguments for its individuality would be unnecessary. Finally, two possibilities for defining a shakhs are examined: (1) using only essentials fails to distinguish among individuals; (2) including accidents fails because accidents change, so the definition would not be constant. Thus, a shakhs truly cannot be defined by a real definition.

Key Terms
شخصی (shakhs): An individual entity that cannot be defined by a logical definition because its specifics are not captured by genus and differentia
حد (hadd): A definition that comprises the essential components of a thing, providing knowledge of its essence
مرکب اعتباری (murakkab i'tibari): A composite entity formed by combining essence and individuation, considered as a unity by convention
عرضیات (aradiyyat): Accidental properties that are not essential to the essence of a thing

Two Chapters of Usul: Conveyance of Meaning and Ruling
Announcement of the two-chapter structure and justification for excluding Arabic sciences.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two-chapter structure of Hanafi usul al-fiqh regarding the Qur'an, and justify why topics like definition and Arabic grammar are excluded from the first chapter using the criteria provided by the commentators.

Core Idea
Conveyance of meaning is prior to conveyance of ruling; thus two distinct chapters.

Al-Tawdih and Al-Talwih introduce the foundational division of topics in the study of the Qur'an as a source of law. The first chapter covers how the Qur'an communicates its meanings—through categories like general ('amm), specific (khass), literal (haqiqah), and metaphorical (majaz). The second chapter addresses how those meanings yield legal rulings, such as obligation from commands and prohibition from prohibitions. This lesson clarifies the rationale for this structure and the precise scope of each chapter, particularly why certain linguistic sciences are excluded.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two chapters and their subjects
From the author's statement, note that the first chapter handles how the Qur'an conveys meaning (ifadat al-ma'na), and the second how it conveys a legal ruling (ifadat al-hukm al-shar'i). List the example topics given: 'amm, khass, mushtarak, haqiqah, majaz for chapter one; amr (command) and nahy (prohibition) for chapter two.
The distinction is between semantic and normative dimensions.
2
Understand the exclusion of the definition of the Qur'an
The commentator anticipates the objection: why is the definition (ta'rif) not included in chapter one? The answer: definition is not directly about conveying meaning or establishing rulings; it is a preliminary matter.
Definition is a meta-level discussion, not part of the internal mechanisms of meaning conveyance.
3
Understand the exclusion of Arabic grammatical topics
A second objection: Arabic grammar (nahw, sarf) also conveys meaning, so why exclude it? The commentator gives two conditions: the topic must have a deep connection to conveying meaning and must not be independently detailed in another science. Arabic grammar is already a separate discipline with its own detailed coverage, so it is excluded.
The key is that usul al-fiqh focuses on those semantic tools that are not fully treated elsewhere and are specific to the Qur'an's structure.
4
Analyze al-Taftazani's refutation of the idhafa argument
An objector suggests that the idhafa in 'abhathuhu' (its topics) automatically excludes Arabic sciences because it implies specification. Al-Taftazani refutes this: if that were true, then many topics in chapter one like 'amm and khass, which are shared with the Sunnah, would also be excluded. Therefore, the idhafa is not for takhsis (specification) but merely indicates relation. The real justification is the connection to nazm (structure) of the Qur'an, which is mutawatir and preserved.
This shows the importance of precise usul reasoning and avoiding overgeneralization from linguistic constructions.
5
Appreciate the role of nazm in Hanafi usul
The commentator states that topics are included in chapter one because they pertain to the nazm of the Qur'an. Nazm refers to the composition and structure that is unique to the Qur'an and its miraculous inimitability. This is a distinctive Hanafi emphasis.
The Qur'an's nazm is the basis for discussing 'umum and khusus, as these are features of the text's composition.

Text of al-Tawdih: He [the author] says: We will present the topics of the Book of Allah in two chapters. In the first chapter, it will be explained how the Book conveys meaning. The second chapter is how a legal ruling is established from the Book. He says, using 'i'lam', clarifying his speech: The real purpose is the effective conveyance from the Book, but since the conveyance of a legal ruling depends on the conveyance of meaning, because first the meaning is known, then the benefit of the legal ruling is obtained from it, therefore it is necessary that first the topics related to the conveyance of meaning from the Book of Allah be mentioned, then the topics of conveyance of legal rulings will be discussed. For this reason, in the first chapter, the discussion of specific, general, common, questioned, reality, metaphor, etc. will be conducted, from the perspective that they convey meaning. And in the second chapter, the discussion related to the conveyance of legal rulings will be, for example, the discussion of command that it establishes obligation, and prohibition that it establishes prohibition; obligation and prohibition are among the legal rulings. Commentary of al-Talwih: He said: 'we will present its topics' i.e., explanation of its divisions, as a response to an estimated question. Question: You said that in the first chapter we will mention the topics of the Book of Allah that convey meaning. One of those topics is definition; it should also be mentioned in the first chapter? Response: Our intended meaning is those topics that are related to conveying meanings and establishing rulings. Since definition is outside that, it was not mentioned in the first chapter. And by 'conveyance' (ijab) [the question continues]. Question: You said that in the first chapter, those states and topics will be explained that are related to conveying meaning. Then the subjects of nahw, sarf, etc. should also be included in the first chapter because they also convey meaning. The commentator responds: Our intended meaning is those topics that have a deep connection with conveying meaning and that have not been independently mentioned in another science. These will be mentioned in the first chapter. Those topics that have a great involvement in conveying meaning or that have already been detailed in another science, like i'rab, bina', etc., will not be mentioned in this chapter. Objection: It is said that you have imposed two restrictions: one that the first chapter includes topics that have a strong connection to conveying meaning, and second that these topics have not been detailed in another chapter. This is done so that the Arabic sciences are excluded from the first chapter. However, the Arabic sciences are excluded even without these two restrictions because 'its topics' (abhathuhu) has an idhafa that indicates specification. The meaning would be: in the first chapter we will mention the topics that are specific to the Book. Since the topics of Arabic sciences are not specific to the Book, they are excluded. Response: The commentator al-Taftazani responds that this answer is not correct because if the idhafa in 'abhathuhu' is for specification and refers to topics that are specific to the Book of Allah alone, then many topics from the first and second chapters would be excluded, because the first chapter contains topics that are not specific to it, like general, specific, common, etc., because they also occur in the Sunnah. This is why scholars said that the correct approach would have been to mention these topics after both the Book and the Sunnah, because the topics are shared between both. By itself, the commentator [al-Taftazani] gives the reason for mentioning these topics in the Book of Allah: because they are related to the structure (nazm), and the structure of the Qur'an is mutawatir and preserved, so these topics are in the Book of Allah.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Tawdih and its commentary (Al-Talwih) outlines the two-chapter structure of the book. The first chapter deals with how the Qur'an conveys meaning (al-‘umum, al-khusus, al-mushtarak, al-haqiqah, al-majaz, etc.), and the second deals with how legal rulings (ahkam shar'iyyah) are derived (e.g., commands indicate obligation, prohibitions indicate prohibition). The commentator anticipates two objections: (1) why is the definition of the Qur'an not included in the first chapter? Answer: definition is not directly about conveying meaning or establishing rulings. (2) why are Arabic grammar topics not included? Answer: they are not specific enough; the first chapter covers topics that are deeply tied to conveying meaning and are not fully covered in other sciences. The commentator al-Taftazani then refutes the alternative justification that the idhafa 'abhathuhu' (its topics) automatically excludes Arabic sciences because it implies specificity. He shows that that would also exclude many topics that are shared with the Sunnah. Instead, the real reason is that these topics are discussed here because they pertain to the structure (nazm) of the Qur'an, which is preserved and mutawatir, so the Qur'an is the primary source for them.

Key Terms
افادہ معنی (ifadat al-ma'na): The conveyance of meaning; how the text reveals its semantic content.
افادہ حکم شرعی (ifadat al-hukm al-shar'i): The conveyance of a legal ruling; how the text establishes obligation, prohibition, etc.
ابحاثہ (abhathuhu): Its topics (i.e., topics of the Book of Allah).
نظم (nazm): The structure/composition of the Qur'anic text.

Criteria for Chapter Scope in Usul al-Fiqh
Explaining the scope of the first chapter through question-answer and objection-response.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A chapter's scope is defined by deep connection to meaning, relation to rulings, and independence from other sciences.

In this passage, the commentator clarifies the boundaries of the first chapter of Al-Tawdih, which deals with discussions related to conveying meaning from the Book of Allah. Through a series of objections and answers, we learn the precise criteria that guide the inclusion or exclusion of topics. Lexical and Technical Meanings of Nazm and Lafz
Defining nazm and lafz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define nazm and lafz in both lexical and technical senses, and distinguish between the two technical meanings of nazm.

Core Idea
Nazm is structured speech requiring arrangement; lafz is any utterance without conditions.

In Hanafi usul, precise terminology is key to analyzing divine speech. This lesson establishes the foundational definitions of nazm and lafz, showing that they differ both lexically and technically. Understanding these definitions will help you classify expressions correctly and avoid conceptual confusion.

Guided Path
1
Identify lexical meanings
Note that nazm lexically means 'stringing pearls', while lafz means 'to throw'. This already suggests a difference: one implies order, the other does not.
Lexical meanings often hint at technical usage.
2
Distinguish technical meanings
Nazm has two technical meanings: (1) as a masdar: the act of arranging words with coordinated meanings; (2) as a noun: the arranged words themselves. Lafz technically means any utterance, regardless of arrangement.
Both technical meanings of nazm serve the same purpose.
3
Compare the two technical meanings of nazm
Though expressed differently, both emphasize arrangement, meaningful order, and proportionality. The first is an action, the second is the product.
The difference is only verbal; the core concept is identical.
4
Contrast with lafz
Lafz has no conditions: it includes singular and composite, arranged or disordered. Thus lafz is broader than nazm.
This contrast is the basis for the distinction in later lessons.

Key Terms
نظم (nazm): Technically, the arrangement of words with coordinated meanings and proportionate indications; also, the arranged words themselves. It is specific to composite speech and requires order and meaningful coherence.
لفظ (lafz): Any utterance that a person pronounces, whether arranged or unarranged, singular or compound. It has no conditions of order or meaning.

The Two Differences Between Nazm and Lafz
Explaining the two differences between nazm and lafz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the two differences between nazm and lafz, and apply them to classify expressions.

Core Idea
Nazm only for composite speech with order; lafz includes any utterance, ordered or not.

Building on the definitions, this lesson isolates the two concrete differences that separate nazm from lafz. Understanding these will sharpen your ability to categorize Quranic and prophetic expressions correctly.

Guided Path
1
Recall the definitions
Nazm is specific to composite speech with arranged meanings and proportionate indications. Lafz is any utterance, singular or composite, without conditions.
Keep these definitions in mind as we derive the differences.
2
Derive difference one: scope
Nazm applies only to composite speech (kalam murakkab). Lafz applies to both singular and composite. Thus a single word is lafz but not nazm.
This is the most straightforward distinction.
3
Derive difference two: conditions
Nazm requires arrangement of words, arrangement of meanings, and proportionate indications. Lafz has no such requirements.
This shows nazm is a more restrictive category.
4
Apply to an example
Take the Quranic verse 'الحمد لله رب العالمين'. It is composite and meets the conditions of nazm. The single word 'الحمد' in isolation is lafz.
Practice this with other phrases.

They are not the same; rather there is a difference in their lexical meanings and also in their technical meanings. (1) The lexical difference is: nazm lexically means stringing pearls onto a thread, and lafz lexically means to throw. (2) As for technical meaning, two technical meanings of nazm are mentioned: (a) as a masdar: the arrangement of words with arranged meanings and proportionate indications, in accordance with what reason requires; (b) another technical meaning: 'words with arranged meanings and proportionate indications' - this is not a masdar meaning. In both meanings there is only a verbal difference in expression, the purpose is one. Meanwhile the technical meaning of lafz is: 'what a person utters' - whether arranged or unarranged, singular or compound. From their technical meanings it is established that there are two differences between nazm and lafz: (1) Nazm pertains to composite speech, not to singular words, while lafz is general; it can be a description of composite speech and also of singular words. (2) The second difference is that in nazm, the arrangement of words, arrangement of meanings, and proportionality of indications in accordance with reason are conditions, whereas in lafz none of these are conditions; rather however words come out, that is called lafz. Hence the author's statement that here nazm is intended to mean lafz and both are synonyms is incorrect. Moreover, the types that come later - khas, 'amm, mushtarak, etc. - these are divisions of singular words; thus they can be divisions of lafz but not of nazm, because nazm is specific to composite speech. Therefore the author's calling them 'divisions of nazm' is incorrect. From 'lana taqul', Allamah Taftazani gives two answers to this objection: (1) The first answer is that even though there is a difference in meaning between nazm and lafz, here nazm is used metaphorically in the sense of 'lafz', i.e., absolute speech, whether arranged or unarranged, singular or compound; this is of the category of mentioning the restricted and intending the absolute. (2) The second answer is that here the real meaning of nazm is intended, and 'divisions of nazm' means 'divisions related to nazm', i.e., the divisions that have a connection with nazm, in the sense that the singular words that occur in the speech of nazm become attributes and divisions of those words, not of the speech of nazm itself. Because in common usage, khas, 'amm, etc. are called attributes and divisions of lafz, not of nazm. Therefore in 'divisions of nazm', the idafah (construct) of 'divisions' to 'nazm' is due to a minimal connection. 'Fa'in qila' - an objection is raised: You used the term nazm for the words of the Quran because using lafz would be disrespectful, but there is a doubt that using nazm also involves disrespect, because one meaning of nazm is poetry, and the Quran and hadith condemn poetry and poets. Answer: The commentator replied that nazm in the sense of poetry is a metaphorical meaning, while the real meaning of nazm is 'stringing pearls', which is a very fine meaning. Here it is used in that lexical and real sense. Meanwhile, the lexical meaning of lafz itself involves disrespect because it means 'to throw from the mouth'. Thus the objection is repelled.
