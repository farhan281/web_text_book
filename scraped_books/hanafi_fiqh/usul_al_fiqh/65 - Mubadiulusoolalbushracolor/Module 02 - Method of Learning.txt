Definition of Usul al-Fiqh
Defines usul al-fiqh and its components

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define usul al-fiqh, distinguish it from fiqh, and identify its subject and purpose.

Core Idea
Usul al-Fiqh is the methodology for deriving practical rulings from the four sources.

Every science has a subject and a goal. Usul al-Fiqh is the science that equips the mujtahid with the rules to extract practical rulings from the Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. This lesson breaks down the definition so that you can understand what this discipline is and what it is not.

Guided Path
1
Isolate the defined term
The passage defines 'Usul al-Fiqh'. Start by identifying the two words: 'Usul' (plural of asl) and 'Fiqh'.
2
Parse the technical definition
The definition says: 'a science in which are investigated the rules by which one arrives at deriving practical rulings from the legal evidences.' Break this into components: science (ilm), rules (qawaid), deriving (istinbat), practical rulings (ahkam amaliyya), legal evidences (adilla shar'iyya).
3
Identify the subject and goal
The subject (mawdu') is the legal evidences in terms of leading to rulings. The goal (ghaya) is both knowledge of the rulings and ability to derive them.
4
Understand the linguistic roots
'Usul' linguistically means foundation; terminologically it refers to the four evidences. 'Fiqh' linguistically means understanding; terminologically it is knowledge of practical rulings.
5
Distinguish practical from creedal rulings
Fiqh deals with 'amaliyya (practical) rulings, not i'tiqadiyya (creedal). This separation shows that usul al-fiqh is about actions, not beliefs.
This distinction prevents confusing fiqh with kalam.

4
Definition of Usul al-Fiqh
In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful.
Praise be to Allah, Lord of the worlds, and peace and blessings be upon the master of the messengers, and upon his family and all his companions.
To proceed:
Definition of Usul al-Fiqh] So Usul al-Fiqh is a science in which are investigated the rules by which one arrives at deriving practical rulings from the legal evidences.
And the legal evidences are the Book, the Sunnah, consensus, and analogy.
Its subject is the legal evidences in terms of their leading to practical rulings. And its purpose is knowledge of practical rulings from the legal evidences, and the ability to derive them from them.
And since the legal evidences are four, it is necessary to investigate them, so that by it the method of extracting rulings may be known.
So Usul al-Fiqh: al-Usul is the plural of asl, which linguistically means that upon which something is built, like the foundation of a wall or the root of a tree; and terminologically, it means the legal evidences. And al-Fiqh linguistically means understanding; and terminologically, it is the knowledge of the practical legal rulings. [Interlinear Urdu glosses: 'by which one arrives' etc.] Practical rulings: subsidiary rulings. And original rulings: they are theological creedal rulings. And al-tamakkun: ability over something: being able to extract rulings from evidences. [End]

Commentary
This passage is a definition of usul al-fiqh. It begins with a standard Islamic opening. The author then defines usul al-fiqh as a science that investigates the rules (qawa'id) that enable a mujtahid to derive practical rulings (ahkam 'amaliyya) from the four primary sources (adilla shar'iyya): Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. This is the standard Hanafi definition. The subject (mawdu') of usul al-fiqh is the evidences themselves, and its goal (ghaya) is both knowing the rulings and acquiring the ability to derive them. The passage also gives linguistic and terminological definitions of 'usul' and 'fiqh'. 'Usul' is the plural of 'asl' meaning foundation or root; terminologically it refers to the four sources. 'Fiqh' linguistically means understanding, but technically it is knowledge of practical rulings. The author distinguishes between practical rulings (ahkam 'amaliyya) which are the subject of fiqh, and original rulings (ahkam asliyya) which are creedal (i'tiqadiyya/kalamiyya). The Urdu glosses clarify the Arabic terms. This definition sets the stage for the entire study: it is a methodological science for deriving fiqh.

Key Terms
أصول (usul): Plural of asl; linguistically foundation, terminologically the four sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas).
فقه (fiqh): Linguistically understanding; terminologically knowledge of practical Shari'a rulings derived from detailed evidences.
أحكام عملية (ahkam 'amaliyya): Practical rulings concerning actions of the mukallaf (e.g., wajib, haram).

Two Types of Connection: 'Illa vs. Sabab in Figurative Speech
Defines two types of connection between literal and figurative meanings in legal rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'illa (cause) and sabab (reason) in legal connections and identify in which direction figurative speech is permissible for each type.

Core Idea
The cause ('illa) directly produces the ruling; the reason (sabab) merely leads to it—figurative speech works both ways only with the cause.

In Hanafi usul, understanding how literal and figurative meanings interact is crucial for correctly interpreting legal texts. This passage introduces two types of legal connections—between cause and ruling, and between reason and ruling—and explains when substituting one for the other is valid. By mastering this distinction, you will avoid misapplying figurative interpretations in oaths, contracts, and other legal speech.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of connection
Read the passage and identify the two types: the connection between 'illa and hukm, and the connection between sabab and hukm. Note the examples given: purchase-ownership (first) and body-ownership to enjoyment-ownership (second).
The first type is reversible in figurative speech; the second is one-way.
2
Define 'illa and sabab as per the text
Extract the definitions: 'illa is the description upon which the ruling depends—ruling exists with it and ceases without it. Sabab is what leads to the ruling without having a direct effect. Write these definitions in your own words.
These definitions are foundational for qiyas (analogy) and understanding causality in fiqh.
3
Analyze the example of owning a slave
Consider the example: 'If I own a slave, he is free' where 'ownership' is used figuratively to mean 'purchase'. The passage says this is valid because purchase is the 'illa of ownership, and figurative speech works both ways. Verify the logic.
The key is that 'illa and ruling can substitute each other.
4
Analyze the example of wife and female slave
Examine the second set: saying to a wife 'I set you free' intending divorce is valid; but saying to a female slave 'I divorce you' intending manumission is not. Trace the causal chain: manumission -> removal of ownership of body -> removal of ownership of sexual enjoyment (divorce). Manumission is a 'pure reason' (sabab mahd) for divorce, but divorce is not a reason for manumission.
This illustrates the one-way nature of the sabab-hukm connection.
5
Understand the judicial implication
The passage ends with a note about judicial acceptance: if someone intends 'purchase' by 'ownership', it is valid but not accepted in court due to ease and suspicion. This shows that intention vs. outward expression can differ, but courts follow the apparent meaning.
This connects usul to practical furu' and fatwa.

Types of Literal Meaning
And the connection in legal rulings between the literal and figurative meanings is of two types:
First: The connection between the cause (علة) and the ruling, like the connection between purchase and ownership.
Second: The connection between the reason (سبب) and the ruling, like the connection between ownership of the body (raqabah) and ownership of enjoyment (mut'ah).
Its ruling: mentioning the reason and intending the ruling. Figurative speech in the first type is from both sides, and in the second type from one side.
Examples: If he says, 'If I own a slave, he is free,' and he meant by ownership the purchase, it is valid. And if he says, 'If I purchase a slave, he is free,' and he meant by purchase ownership, it is also valid.
And if he says to his wife, 'I have set you free,' and intended divorce, it is valid. And if he says to his female slave, 'I have divorced you,' and intended manumission, it is not valid.
The cause (علة) is the description upon which the legal ruling is dependent; the ruling exists with its existence and ceases with its non-existence, as will come in the chapter of analogy. Purchase is the cause of ownership. The reason (سبب) is that which leads to something without having an effect in it. Ownership of the body is the reason for ownership of enjoyment in the case of a female slave. 
If one owns half a slave, then sells him, then owns the other half, the slave is not emancipated because he did not have complete ownership of the slave all at once. The term 'owner' in common usage is applied to one who has complete ownership. If he intended by ownership the purchase, he will be emancipated because it is not necessary for a person to have something completely in his ownership to be considered a purchaser. Similarly its opposite: if he meant by purchase ownership, his intention is valid but it is not accepted judicially due to ease and suspicion. 
The intention of divorce through manumission is valid because manumission is the cause of the removal of ownership of the body, and removal of ownership of the body is the reason for removal of ownership of the vagina (buda'). Thus manumission is a pure reason for the removal of ownership of enjoyment. Its opposite is not valid in the case of a female slave because divorce is not a reason for manumission.

Commentary
This passage defines two types of relationships between literal and figurative meanings in legal texts. It distinguishes between 'illa (cause) and sabab (reason). The author gives examples from oaths and marital speech. Key point: In the first type (illa-hukm), figurative usage is possible from both sides (using cause to mean effect and vice versa). In the second type (sabab-hukm), only from one side (using reason to mean effect). The examples illustrate when metaphorical intent is valid and when it isn't, particularly in the context of emancipating a slave and divorce.

Key Terms
حقيقة (haqiqah): Literal meaning of a word as originally and commonly understood.
مجاز (majaz): Figurative meaning used due to a contextual indicator.
علة (illa): A description upon which a ruling is dependent; the ruling exists with it and ceases without it.
سبب (sabab): That which leads to a ruling without having a direct effect.
