Command Implies One Performance; Types of Wajib: Ada and Qada
Explaining that a command does not imply repetition, and detailing the types of wajib: ada and qada, and types of ada: kamil and qasir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rule that a command does not entail repetition, distinguish between ada and qada, and identify complete vs. deficient ada with their rulings.

Core Idea
A single command is fulfilled once; worship repeats due to recurring causes, not the command itself.

This passage introduces two foundational usul principles: first, a command (like 'pray') is satisfied by one performance; second, obligations arising from commands are classified into delivering the exact wajib (ada) or its substitute (qada). Within ada, performance can be complete or deficient, each with distinct legal consequences. Understanding these categories is essential for analyzing acts of worship in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Extract the rule on repetition
Read the first rule: 'al-amr bil fi'l la yqtaḍī al-takrār'. Understand that a command like 'ṣallū' means perform the act once. The repetition of worship (e.g., daily prayers) is due to repetition of their causes (e.g., entry of prayer times).
This contrasts with some schools that interpret a general command as requiring repetition.
2
Define the two types of wajib
Locate the definitions: ada is delivering the very wajib (ayn al-wājib) by command; qada is delivering the like of the wajib (mithl al-wājib) by command. Note that qada typically applies when the original time has passed.
The term 'wajib' here refers to the obligated act, not the level of obligation.
3
Distinguish complete and deficient ada
Identify the two subtypes of ada: kāmil (complete) delivers the wajib with all its attributes; qāṣir (deficient) delivers it with some deficiency. Study the examples: prayer in congregation is complete; prayer without reciting al-Fatiha or without ta'dīl al-arkān is deficient.
The deficiency may be compensated if a legal equivalent exists; otherwise the act is valid but sinful.
4
Analyze the rulings for deficient ada
Note the two cases: if deficiency has a legal equivalent (e.g., forgetting Fatiha → sujūd al-sahw), it is compensated. If no equivalent (e.g., missing ta'dīl al-arkān), the ruling of deficiency is lifted except for sin (atham). Understand that this affects the discharge of responsibility.
The phrase 'illā fī al-ithm' means sin remains; the act is still valid but disliked (makrūh).
5
Apply to the Zuhr prayer example
Read the example: the wajib at Zuhr time is the Zuhr prayer; the command directs to perform that specific wajib. When the time repeats, the obligation repeats due to the recurring cause, not the command being repeated. This illustrates the rule of takrār.
This reinforces that the command itself is not repeated; the cause (time) creates a new obligation.

Commentary
This passage outlines important usul rules regarding commands. First, a command to perform an act (e.g., 'pray') is fulfilled by doing it once; it does not require repetition. The repetition of acts of worship (like daily prayers) is due to the repetition of their causes (e.g., entry of prayer times). Second, the obligation (wajib) that arises from a command can be fulfilled either by performing the exact obligation (ada) or by performing a substitute (qada) when the original is missed. Within ada, there is complete performance (kamil) which discharges the duty fully, and deficient performance (qasir) which lacks some attribute. Deficient ada may be compensated by an equivalent if possible (e.g., prostration of forgetfulness for missing fatiha inadvertently) or else the deficiency is overlooked except that sin remains (e.g., missing the straightening of postures cannot be compensated by analogy because no legal equivalent exists). The example of zuhr prayer illustrates how the command relates to the obligation at a specific time. This shows the Hanafi approach to analyzing commands and their fulfillment.

Implications of Command: Repetition, Adā', Qadā', and Ma'mūr Types
Expounding rules on command's implications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a single command does not imply repetition, distinguish between adā' and qadā' (each with complete and deficient subtypes), and classify the one commanded as muṭlaq 'an al-waqt.

Core Idea
A command does not demand repetition; repeated worship is due to repeated causes.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text clarifies foundational implications of a command (amr). Understanding these implications is crucial for deriving rulings: once a command is issued, it is fulfilled by one performance; any repetition must stem from a recurring cause. The text also categorizes the obligatory act (wajib) into performance (adā') and substitute (qadā'), with further subtypes, and discusses obligations not tied to a specific time. These concepts are essential for applying commands correctly.

Guided Path
1
State the rule on repetition
Identify the first principle: 'al-amr bil-fi'l la yaqtadī al-takrār.' The meaning of 'pray' is to perform the prayer once. Understand that repeated worship is due to repeated causes (asbāb), not the command itself.
Example: daily prayers repeat because their times (causes) repeat.
2
Define adā' and qadā'
Parse the two types of wajib: adā' is delivering the very wajib as commanded; qadā' is delivering a legal substitute (mithl) for the wajib when the original is missed.
Adā' is the default; qadā' is only when adā' is impossible.
3
Classify the subtypes of adā'
Adā' is either complete (kāmil) – performing with all perfect attributes (e.g., prayer in time with congregation) – or deficient (qāṣir) – performing with some deficiency (e.g., prayer without reciting al-Fātiḥa). Note the ruling: complete discharges responsibility; deficient may be compensated if a legal equivalent exists, otherwise sin remains.
Compensation example: prostration of forgetfulness for missed Fātiḥa.
4
Classify the subtypes of qadā'
Qadā' is either complete – delivering a substitute matching form and meaning (e.g., making up a prayer) – or deficient – delivering a substitute in meaning only (e.g., ransom after death).
A thing with no substitute in form or meaning cannot have qadā' (e.g., benefits of usurped property).
5
Identify the two types of al-ma'mūr
The one commanded is either absolute regarding time (muṭlaq 'an al-waqt), like zakāh and hajj, or time-bound. The ruling: performance is obligatory with delay, as long as it does not expire before death. Hastening is recommended.
Delay is permitted but not encouraged without reason.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul al-fiqh exposition on the implications of a command (amr) from the section 'Divisions of the Specific' (aqsām al-khāṣ). It opens with a fundamental rule: a command to perform an action does not demand repetition. Thus, 'pray' (ṣallū) means perform the prayer once; any repetition of worship is due to the repetition of its cause (e.g., the time for prayer). Next, the text classifies the wajib (obligatory) resulting from a command into two types: adā' (performance) – delivering the exact wajib as commanded; and qadā' (make-up) – delivering a legal substitute (mithl) of the wajib. Adā' is further divided into complete (kāmil) – fulfilling the wajib with all its perfect attributes (e.g., prayer in time with congregation), and deficient (qāṣir) – fulfilling the wajib with some deficiency (e.g., prayer without reciting al-Fātiḥa or without proper posture adjustments). The complete adā' discharges the obligation entirely; the deficient adā' may be remedied if the deficiency has a legal equivalent (e.g., forgetting al-Fātiḥa is compensated by prostration of forgetfulness), otherwise the deficiency remains but the obligation is still discharged, though with sin. The text explains that repetition of worship stems from repetition of its cause, using the daily prayers as an example. Qadā' is also of two types: complete – delivering a substitute that matches the original in form and meaning (e.g., making up a missed prayer), and deficient – delivering a substitute in meaning only (e.g., ransom (fidyah) for prayer after death). Four benefits (fā'idah) follow: (1) The default is adā', whether complete or deficient; recourse to qadā' is only when adā' is impossible. (2) The default in qadā' is complete; deficient is only when unable to do complete. (3) Acts without any substitute in form or meaning cannot have qadā'; their ruling transfers to the Hereafter (e.g., the benefit of using a usurped slave is not compensable because there is no equivalent). (4) If Islamic law prescribes a substitute that is not a true equivalent, it is considered a legal equivalent (e.g., fidyah for the elderly replaces fasting). Finally, the one commanded (al-ma'mūr) – the person obligated by the command – is of two types: (1) absolute concerning time (muṭlaq 'an al-waqt) – obligations like zakāh, hajj, and ṣadaqat al-fiṭr that can be performed at any time, with the ruling that performance is obligatory with delay, as long as it is possible before death. Hastening is recommended but not obligatory. A note explains that benefits (manāfi') from usurped property are not compensable because they have no equivalent.

Key Terms
أمر (amr): A verbal demand for an action from a superior (shaari') that implies the obligation of that action.
تكرار (takrār): Performing the same action more than once as a result of the same command; the rule states that a single command does not require repetition.
أداء (adā'): Delivering the very wajib (the exact act) as commanded, whether complete or deficient.
قضاء (qadā'): Delivering a legal substitute (mithl) for the wajib when the original act is missed or cannot be performed.
مطلق عن الوقت (muṭlaq 'an al-waqt): An obligation that is not restricted to a specific time, like zakāh, hajj, and sadaqat al-fitr; performance can be delayed but must be done before death.

Types of Qada: Complete and Deficient
Definition and classification of qada with foundational principles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between complete qada (matching form and meaning) and deficient qada (meaning only), and apply the Hanafi principle that qada is only required when the missed act has a 'like' in form or meaning.

Core Idea
Qada is either complete (form and meaning) or deficient (meaning only); only acts with a 'like' can be made up.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi classification of qada (making up missed obligations) into two fundamental types. Understanding these types is essential for determining whether a missed act must be repeated exactly or can be compensated by an alternative like fidya. The text also lays out key principles that govern when qada becomes necessary and how the Lawgiver may designate a substitute as a 'legal like' even without resemblance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of qada
Locate the binary classification: qada kamil (complete) and qada qasir (deficient). Note the definitions: complete qada matches the wajib in both form (surah) and meaning (ma'na); deficient qada matches only in meaning.
Example given: making up prayer is complete qada; fidya after death is deficient.
2
Understand the primary and secondary rules
Read the first two fawaid: the default is ada' (timely performance); qada is only resorted to when ada' is impossible. Within qada, the complete form is primary; deficient is a fallback for inability.
This establishes a hierarchy: ada' > qada kamil > qada qasir.
3
Grasp the limit of qada: acts without a 'like'
The third fa'ida states that acts which have no like in form or meaning cannot have qada obligated. Their compensation is deferred to the Hereafter.
Usufructs (manafi') are provided as an example—they are not compensable in this world.
4
Recognize the concept of a 'legal like'
The fourth fa'ida explains that when the Lawgiver designates something as a 'like' despite no real resemblance, it is treated as a legal equivalent and becomes binding.
Example: fidya for the decrepit old person is a legal like for fasting, even though money does not resemble fasting in form.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Sabab may be identical to 'illa, substitute for it, or be a metaphor—the relationship determines how the ruling is attributed.

This passage from Mubadi' al-Usool clarifies the terminological relationship between sabab (cause) and 'illa (effective cause) in Hanafi usul. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly attributing rulings in qiyas and for identifying the occasioning causes of obligations. The passage presents three functional relationships and then applies them to specific acts of worship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three relationships
The passage presents three ways sabab relates to 'illa: equivalency (sabab carries the meaning of 'illa), substitution (sabab stands in place of 'illa when 'illa is inaccessible), and metaphorical extension (non-cause called sabab metaphorically).
Memorize these three categories as they recur in usul discussions.
2
Examine the equivalency case
When the sabab itself embodies the 'illa, the ruling is attributed to the sabab. Example: driving an animal that causes damage—the driving is sabab, the animal's action is 'illa, but liability goes to the driver because the sabab carries the meaning of 'illa.
This is the default relationship when 'illa is directly observable through the sabab.
3
Examine the substitution case
When the 'illa is not easily accessible, the sabab stands in its place to ease the obligation. Examples: deep sleep for hadath, seclusion for intercourse, travel for hardship.
This substitution is a mercy (taysir) for the mukallaf.
4
Examine the metaphorical case
Sometimes a non-cause is metaphorically called a sabab. Example: an oath is called the cause for kaffarah, but the actual cause is the violation (hanth).
Be careful not to confuse metaphorical usage with actual causation.

The passage lists the actual sabab for each wajib: time for prayer, sighting for fasting, growing nisab for zakat, the Kaaba for hajj, a dependant for fitr, productive land for ushr, arable land for kharaj. Fard and haram require definitive proof; wajib and makruh tahriman rely on probable evidence—affecting belief and punishment.

This passage from a classical usul text lays out the foundational taxonomy of Islamic legal rulings according to the Hanafi school. It divides prescriptive acts into four obligatory-to-voluntary categories and prohibitions into three degrees. The key insight is the epistemological basis: definitive (qat'i) vs. probable (zanni) evidence, which determines whether denial constitutes disbelief or merely error.

For type 1: if one endures until death, he is rewarded; the act is permissible but the prohibition is still present. For type 2: if one refuses and dies, he is sinful because the act became lawful. The rulings follow from the nature of the concession.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) lies in whether refusal is sinful or meritorious.

Two Types of Permissible Acts Under Duress
Definition of two types of mustabihat with rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of acts made permissible under duress (ikrah/darura) and state the ruling for each type.

Core Idea
Under duress, some acts become obligatory to perform; others are optional but reward endurance.

This short concluding passage from a Hanafi usul text explains a critical distinction: not all permissible acts under duress are the same. One type rewards patient endurance, while another type makes it sinful to refrain. Understanding this distinction clarifies when the Shari'ah requires you to take a normally forbidden action to save your life.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
The passage divides 'mustabihat' (acts made permissible) into two categories. Read carefully: the first type is conditional on the heart being at peace with faith; the second type is triggered by the existence of a valid reason like coercion or extreme hunger.
First type: inner condition. Second type: external cause.
2
Analyze the condition for each type
For the first type, the condition is 'heart at peace with faith' – the person chooses endurance out of reverence for Allah. For the second type, the condition is simply the existence of the reason (e.g., coercion). No additional inner state is required.
First type requires active faith; second type only requires the objective situation.
3
Determine the ruling for each type
If the person in the first type endures and is killed, he is rewarded. If the person in the second type refrains and dies, he is sinful. This implies the first type's act is permissible (mubah) with reward for endurance; the second type's act is obligatory (wajib) – refraining is a sin.
Contrast: first type = optional but meritorious; second type = obligatory.
