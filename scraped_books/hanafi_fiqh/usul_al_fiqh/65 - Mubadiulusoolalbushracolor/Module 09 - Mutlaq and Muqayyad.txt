Absolute and Restricted Terms in Hanafi Usul
Defines and gives rulings for absolute and restricted terms

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mutlaq and al-muqayyad, identify them in Quranic texts, and apply the Hanafi rule that the absolute operates without restriction unless clear evidence restricts.

Core Idea
The absolute term applies generally; the restricted term carries its qualification, and only strong evidence can restrict an absolute.

In this lesson, we will examine how the Hanafi school classifies words in the revealed sources as absolute (mutlaq) or restricted (muqayyad). This distinction is crucial for correct istinbat: an absolute command must be applied without adding conditions, while a restricted command requires its specified qualification. The passage first defines each term and then gives a Quranic example illustrating the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitions
Read the definitions: al-mutlaq indicates the essence without attributes, al-muqayyad indicates essence with attributes. Note the phrase 'duna khusus sifatiha' (without specification of its attributes).
2
Examine the examples
Contrast the two Quranic verses: 'raqabah' (absolute) vs 'raqabah mu'minah' (restricted). Observe how the addition of 'mu'minah' qualifies the neck.
3
Understand the ruling
Each operates according to its nature: the absolute applies absolutely (yajri 'ala itlaqihi); the restricted applies with its restriction (yajri 'ala taqyidihi).
4
Note the caveat about restriction
The marginal note clarifies that the absolute cannot be restricted by a solitary report (khabar al-wahid) or analogy (qiyas); only clear evidence (like a Quranic verse or mutawatir hadith) can do so.
This is a distinctive Hanafi position.

Commentary

The restricted term includes a qualifying attribute, e.g., 'believing neck' in the verse about accidental killing. Each operates according to its own nature: the absolute is applied without adding restrictions, and the restricted requires its qualification. The Three Meanings of Hatta in Hanafi Usul
Rule for interpreting three meanings of 'hatta'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify 'hatta' in a legal conditional statement as ghayah, mujazah, or mahd atf and apply the Hanafi rule of priority.

Core Idea
Hatta is always a limit first; if impossible, it becomes a purpose; if still impossible, a pure conjunction.

The particle 'hatta' (until) appears frequently in oaths and legal conditions, but its meaning shifts based on context. The Hanafi school provides a clear hierarchy: begin with the literal meaning of 'limit' if the action before and after support it. Only if that is impossible do we move to 'purpose', and finally to 'pure conjunction'. This lesson will teach you to parse such clauses correctly.

Guided Path
1
Check extensibility of the preceding action
Ask: can the action before 'hatta' be performed continuously or gradually? If yes (e.g., striking), proceed to step 2. If no (e.g., coming – a punctual action), move to step 4.
Actions like 'strike' are extendable; 'come' is not.
2
Check suitability of the following action as a limit
Ask: can the action after 'hatta' serve as an endpoint or boundary? If yes (e.g., intercession), then 'hatta' is ghayah (limit). If no (e.g., 'you feed me' – it's not a natural limit), proceed to step 3.
The limit must be a plausible natural end.
3
If both conditions met: interpret as ghayah
The condition is fulfilled when the action reaches that limit. If the limit is not reached, the condition triggers (e.g., oath broken). Apply the example of striking until intercession.
4
If ghayah impossible: test for mujazah (purpose)
Check if 'hatta' can mean 'so that' (kai). This is possible when the first action is not extendable and the second is not a limit but a purpose. Also ensure the second event can be a consequence of the first. If yes, interpret as mujazah.
Example: 'I did not come until you feed me' – here 'hatta' means 'so that'.
5
If even mujazah impossible: interpret as mahd atf
If neither ghayah nor mujazah fits, 'hatta' becomes a pure conjunction like 'fa' (then). The two events are simply linked in sequence without limit. The condition is triggered if the first event occurs without the second immediately following.
Example: 'I did not come until I eat lunch at your place' – here 'hatta' means 'and then'.

'Hatta' (until) is for the limit (ghayah) in its original usage, and this is when what precedes it is capable of extension and what follows it is fit to be a limit, like: 'My slave is free if I do not strike you until so-and-so intercedes.' Then if he did not strike at all, or he left striking before the intercession of so-and-so, he breaks his oath. If it is not suitable for the limit, then it is for reward (mujazah) in the sense of 'kai' (so that), and this is when what precedes it is not capable of extension and what follows it is not fit to be a limit, and it is possible to interpret it as a consequence, like his saying: 'My slave is free if I do not come to you until you feed me.' Then he came to him but he did not feed him, he does not break his oath. If this is not possible, then it is made for pure conjunction (atf mahd) metaphorically in the sense of 'fa' (then), and the meaning of limit is nullified, like his saying: 'My slave is free if I do not come to you until I eat lunch at your place today.' Then he came to him but did not eat lunch with him immediately on that day, he breaks his oath.

Commentary
This passage presents the three meanings of the particle 'hatta' (until) in order of priority. The primary meaning is 'limit' (ghayah): the action before 'hatta' must be extendable, and the action after must be a valid endpoint. Example: striking until intercession. Second meaning: if limit is impossible, 'hatta' can mean 'so that' (mujazah) indicating purpose or consequence. Example: coming so that you feed me. Third meaning: if even that is not possible, 'hatta' becomes a pure conjunction like 'faa' (then), linking two actions without any limit. Example: coming and then eating lunch. The Hanafi rules for legal interpretation prioritize the literal meaning unless it is impossible, then move to metaphorical meanings.

Key Terms
حتى (hatta): Particle with three meanings: primary ghayah (limit), secondary mujazah (purpose), tertiary mahd atf (pure conjunction). Priority is fixed.
الغاية (al-ghayah): The limit or endpoint; the primary meaning of 'hatta' when conditions are met.
المجازاة (al-mujazah): The meaning of 'so that' (kai); indicating purpose or consequence.
العطف المحض (al-atf al-mahd): Pure conjunction in the sense of 'then' (fa); linking two actions without limit.

The Particle Ila and Inclusion of the Limit
Defining the particle 'ila' and the rule for inclusion of the limit

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the limit (ghaya) expressed by 'ila' and apply the Hanafi rule to determine whether the limit is included or excluded in the ruling.

Core Idea
Self-standing limits are excluded; non-self-standing limits depend on the encompassing command.

In usul al-fiqh, the particle 'ila' (to/until) sets a limit. But does the limit itself fall under the ruling? The Hanafi school has a nuanced rule based on whether the limit is an independent entity and whether the initial command encompasses it. This lesson unpacks that rule with examples from sales, wudu, and fasting.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two categories of limits
Identify whether the limit (ghaya) is self-standing (qa'imah bi-nafsiha) or not. Self-standing means it is an independent entity; otherwise it is part of a continuum (e.g., body parts).
Example: a wall is self-standing; the elbow is not.
2
Apply the first rule: self-standing excluded
If the limit is self-standing, it is automatically not included in the limited matter (al-mughayya).
Example: 'I bought this wall up to this wall' – the second wall is not bought.
3
Apply the second rule: non-self-standing depends on encompassing
If the limit is not self-standing, check whether the initial wording (sadr al-kalam) encompasses the limit. If it does, the limit is included; if not (or there is doubt), it is excluded.
Example: 'Wash your hands to the elbows' – the command 'wash' encompasses the elbows, so elbows are included. In 'fast until night', 'fast' does not encompass night (since fasting is only in daytime), so night is excluded.
4
Confirm with the Quranic example
The verse 'Then complete the fast until the night' (al-Baqarah 2:187) illustrates the rule: night is not self-standing but the command 'fast' does not encompass it (doubt), so night is excluded.

Commentary
This passage explains the particle 'ila' which indicates the endpoint of a limit. The key question is whether the limit itself is included in the ruling. If the limit is a self-standing entity (e.g., a specific wall), it is not included in the purchase. But if the limit is not self-standing (e.g., joints in the body), then the inclusion depends on the initial wording: if the verb or command naturally encompasses the limit (like 'wash your faces hands to the elbows' includes elbows), it is included; if there is doubt or it does not encompass, it is not included (like 'fast until night' — night is not included in the fast). The Quranic example illustrates that night is excluded from the fasting period.

Key Terms
الغاية (al-ghaya): The limit or endpoint expressed by 'ila'
المغيا (al-mughayya): The thing limited (the matter that has a limit)
قائمة بنفسها (qa'imah bi-nafsiha): Something that exists independently, not merely a part or attribute of another thing
