Author's Preface: Purpose and Sources
Author explains need for introductory work to Usul al-Shashi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the author's motivation for writing Mabadi' al-Usul and identify the primary sources he used.

Core Idea
This book is a gateway to Usul al-Shashi — a simplified introduction, not a replacement.

In this preface, the author, Sa'eed Ahmad al-Balanboori, sets the stage for his work. He identifies a common problem: the classic text Usul al-Shashi, though valuable, is too difficult for beginners. His solution is Mabadi' al-Usul, a preparatory book that simplifies and paves the way for studying the original. He also acknowledges the key sources that helped him shape this work. Understanding this context is essential for appreciating the role of the book in the Hanafi usul curriculum.

Defining Usul al-Fiqh: Science of Deriving Rulings
Defines Usul al-Fiqh and its components

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Usul al-Fiqh, identify its subject and goal, and list the four primary evidences.

Core Idea
Usul al-Fiqh is the science of how to derive practical rulings from the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas.

Welcome to the foundation of Hanafi legal theory. This passage from a classical text gives us the precise definition of Usul al-Fiqh, clarifying what it studies, its purpose, and the sources it relies on. Understanding this definition is the first step toward systematic derivation of rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the full definition
The author defines Usul al-Fiqh as a science that investigates rules (qawa'id) by which one arrives at deriving practical rulings from legal evidences. Note the key components: science, rules, derivation, practical rulings, legal evidences.
This definition distinguishes Usul from Fiqh itself – Fiqh is the rulings, Usul is the method.
2
Distinguish subject and goal
The subject (mawdu') is the legal evidences themselves – the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas. The goal (ghayah) is twofold: knowledge of the practical rulings and the ability to derive them from the evidences.
The text explicitly states: 'Wa mawdu'uhu al-adillah al-shar'iyyah' and 'ghayatuhu ma'rifat al-ahkam al-'amaliyyah wa al-tamakkun min istinbatiha.'
3
Break down the terms 'usul' and 'fiqh'
Linguistically, 'usul' (plural of 'asl') means that on which something is built, like a foundation or root. Terminologically, it refers to the legal evidences. 'Fiqh' linguistically means understanding, and terminologically it is knowledge of practical legal rulings.
This linguistic analysis helps solidify the conceptual boundaries of the discipline.
4
List the four evidences
The passage explicitly lists the four primary sources: the Book (Quran), the Sunnah, consensus (Ijma), and analogy (Qiyas). Hanafis also use istihsan, but these four are the agreed-upon core.
Memorize these four: they are the foundational sources from which all rulings are derived.
5
Recognize the scope limitation
Usul al-Fiqh deals only with practical rulings ('amaliyyah), not creedal or theological rulings (asliyyah). This limitation is crucial – Usul is for actions, not beliefs.
The text contrasts 'al-ahkam al-'amaliyyah' with 'al-ahkam al-asliyyah' to clarify the domain.

Commentary
This passage opens the introduction to Usul al-Fiqh by giving its definition. The author defines Usul al-Fiqh as a science that studies the universal principles (qawa'id) through which a mujtahid can derive practical rulings (al-ahkam al-'amaliyyah) from the four primary sources (adillah): Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas. He then clarifies the subject matter (mawdu')—the adillah themselves—and the goal (ghayah)—both knowledge of rulings and the ability to derive them. The author also provides the linguistic and terminological meanings of 'usul' (foundations) and 'fiqh' (understanding). Notably, he distinguishes 'ahkam 'amaliyyah' (practical rulings) from 'ahkam asliyyah' (creedal rulings), showing that Usul al-Fiqh deals only with practical law, not theology. The Urdu explanatory notes embedded in the text further clarify that 'yutawassal biha' means to approach or draw near through rules, and that 'tamakkun' means having the ability to extract rulings.

Key Terms
أصول الفقه (usul al-fiqh): A science studying the principles by which practical Sharia rulings are derived from their evidence.
أدلة شرعية (adillah shar'iyyah): The four sources: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas.

Commentary
This passage is the table of contents of the book Mubadiulusoolalbushracolor, a text on Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh. It provides a structured overview of the topics covered, beginning with an introduction defining Usul al-Fiqh itself. The first major section (Research) is on the Book of Allah (Qur'an), where it defines key terms: Khass (specific), 'Amm (general), Mushtarak (homonym), Mu'awwal (interpreted), and then introduces the divisions of textual clarity: Haqiqah (literal), Majaz (metaphorical), Sarih (explicit), Kinayah (implicit), and the hierarchy of textual strength: Zahir (apparent), Nass (explicit), Mufassar (detailed), Muhkam (decisive). The second research is on the Sunnah, covering types of reports (Mutawatir, Mashhur, Khabar al-Wahid) and conditions for their authority. The third research is on Ijma (consensus), its definition and divisions. The fourth research is on Qiyas (analogy), its pillars, types, and conditions, including the concept of 'Illah (cause) and Sabab (reason), as well as preventers and rebuttals. A separate section covers the categories of legal rulings: Fard (obligatory), Wajib (necessary), Sunnah (recommended), Nafl (supererogatory), Haram (unlawful), Makruh (reprehensible), 'Azimah (primary ruling), and Rukhsah (concession). Also included are discussions on Bayan (clarification). This table of contents gives a roadmap for a comprehensive study of Hanafi Usul methodology.

Key Terms
الخاص (al-khass): A term with a specific meaning, as opposed to general (amm). Used in Qur'an interpretation.
العام (al-amm): A general term that includes all its individuals unless specified otherwise.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning to extend a ruling from an original case to a new case based on shared cause ('illah).
