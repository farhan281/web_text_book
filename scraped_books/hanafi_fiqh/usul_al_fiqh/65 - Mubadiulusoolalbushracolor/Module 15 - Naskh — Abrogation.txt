Bayan al-Tabdil: The Definition of Abrogation (Naskh)
Defines 'Bayan al-Tabdil' as naskh and establishes its fundamental ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of naskh and identify its key element: that only the Lawgiver can abrogate a legal ruling.

Core Idea
Abrogation lifts a prior ruling by a later text — only the Lawgiver can do this.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational concept of naskh (abrogation) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author defines 'Bayan al-Tabdil' as the lifting of an earlier ruling by a later textual proof from the Lawgiver. This clarifies that abrogation is not a human privilege but a divine prerogative, as demonstrated by the Prophet's hadith on visiting graves.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
The term 'Bayan al-Tabdil' is introduced. Notice that it is a type of bayan (clarification) that involves changing a ruling.
Bayan al-Tabdil is synonymous with naskh in this context.
2
State the definition of naskh
The text defines naskh as 'lifting the first ruling by a later legal text'. Focus on the two key elements: (1) a prior ruling exists, and (2) a later text from the Lawgiver cancels it.
The later text must be authentic and of equal or higher authority.
3
Examine the prophetic example
The hadith 'I used to forbid you from visiting graves, so visit them' illustrates naskh. The earlier prohibition is lifted by the later permission. Identify the change in ruling.
The conjunction 'fa' (so) indicates the shift.
4
Apply the ruling (hukm)
The text states that naskh is permissible from the Lawgiver but not from servants (humans). This establishes a core principle: only Allah or His Prophet can abrogate a divine ruling.
This distinguishes naskh from human amendment or ijtihad.

Commentary
This passage defines Bayan al-Tabdil as naskh (abrogation). In Hanafi usul, naskh is the lifting of a previously established ruling by a later revelation from the Lawgiver. The example is the Prophet's statement: 'I used to forbid you from visiting graves; now visit them' – this abrogates the earlier prohibition. The general ruling (hukm) is that abrogation can only be done by the Lawgiver (Allah or the Prophet), not by humans. This is a key principle in Islamic jurisprudence, ensuring that only the original lawgiver can change divine rulings. The Hanafi school accepts naskh of the Quran by the Quran and by mutawatir hadith, and of hadith by hadith, with specific conditions.

Key Terms
النسخ (al-naskh): Lifting a previous legal ruling by a later textual proof from the Lawgiver.
