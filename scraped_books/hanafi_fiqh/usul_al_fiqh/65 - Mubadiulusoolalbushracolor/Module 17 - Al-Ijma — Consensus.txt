Definition and Authority of Ijma
Defines ijma linguistically and technically, and proves its authority as a hujja

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ijma linguistically and technically, and identify its proof from the Quran and Sunnah as a decisive hujja.

Core Idea
Ijma is the agreement of all mujtahids in an age and is a decisive proof (hujja) like hadith.

In this lesson, we examine the third source of Islamic law: ijma (consensus). Understanding its definition and authority is crucial because it is a primary tool for deriving rulings when the Quran and Sunnah are not explicit. We will see how the Hanafi school establishes its binding nature using a clear Quranic verse and a well-known prophetic tradition.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic and technical definition
Ijma in language means agreement. In Shari'a, it is the agreement of all mujtahids (qualified jurists) of the ummah of Muhammad (peace be upon him) in a single era on a legal ruling. Note that the definition requires unanimity and the qualification of mujtahid.
The Hanafi definition is similar to other schools but emphasizes the agreement of all mujtahids in one age.
2
Identify the Quranic proof and its wajh al-dalalah
The passage cites Quran 4:115, which warns against following 'other than the way of the believers'. The wajh al-dalalah: by equating such deviation with opposing the Messenger, the verse implies that the consensus of the believers is obligatory to follow, making it a binding proof.
The thubut of the verse is qat'i (certain transmission), but its dalala is zanni (the exact implication requires interpretation).
3
Identify the hadith proof
The passage also references the hadith 'Allah will not gather my ummah upon error ever' (narrated by al-Hakim). This hadith directly assures that the collective agreement of the ummah is preserved from error.
This hadith is used to affirm that ijma is an infallible source.
4
Conclude the authority of ijma
Based on these proofs, ijma is considered a decisive proof (hujja qat'iyya) equivalent to the hadith. This means that any ruling established by ijma is binding and cannot be contradicted by individual opinion or weak evidence.
This does not diminish the Quran and Sunnah; rather, ijma confirms and explicates them.

Definition and Proof of Ijma'
Defining Ijma' and establishing its authority from Quran and Sunnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of Ijma', identify its proof from the Quran and Sunnah, and explain why it is considered a definitive proof (hujjah qat'iyyah).

Core Idea
Ijma' is the agreement of all mujtahids; it is a definitive proof like the Quran.

In this lesson, we explore the foundational concept of Ijma' (consensus) as a source of Islamic law according to the Hanafi school. Defining it precisely and establishing its authority is crucial for understanding how later rulings are derived. We begin with a necessary classification of narrators (rawi) to understand the context, then proceed to the definition and the textual proofs that make Ijma' binding.

Guided Path
1
Classify the narrators
Identify the two types of narrators: those known for knowledge and ijtihad (like the four caliphs and 'Abadilah) and those known for memory and reliability (like Abu Hurairah). Note the difference in how their narrations are treated relative to qiyas.
This classification sets the stage for understanding when consensus of scholars is authoritative.
2
Define Ijma' linguistically and terminologically
State the linguistic meaning (agreement) and the technical Hanafi definition: agreement of the mujtahids of the ummah of Muhammad (ﷺ) in an era on a matter.
Note the restriction to mujtahids, excluding commoners and non-jurists.
3
Identify the Quranic proof (4:115)
Analyze the verse: 'And whoever opposes the Messenger after the guidance has become clear to him and follows other than the way of the believers...'. Recognize that 'other than the way of the believers' refers to Ijma'.
The verse equates following other than the believers' way with opposing the Messenger.
4
Identify the Prophetic proof
Consider the hadith: 'Allah will never unite this ummah upon misguidance forever.' This indicates that the consensus of the ummah cannot be on error.
Both proofs are cited to establish Ijma' as a definitive proof.
5
Conclude the authority of Ijma'
Combine the proofs to derive that Ijma' is a hujjah qat'iyyah (definitive proof) like the report of the Messenger, requiring action and establishing certainty.
This ruling applies only to consensus of qualified mujtahids.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul text deals primarily with Ijma' (consensus) as a source of law. It begins with a preliminary classification of narrators (rawi) into two types: those known for knowledge and ijtihad (like the four caliphs and the 'Abadilah—Ibn Mas'ud, Ibn Umar, Ibn Abbas, and either Ibn 'Amr or Ibn az-Zubayr), and those known for memory and reliability (like Abu Hurairah and Anas). The ruling for the first type is that their narration is given precedence over qiyas (analogy). For the second, if their narration agrees with qiyas it is accepted; if it contradicts, it is not abandoned except for necessity (darurah). The necessity is explained: if acting on the hadith would completely close the door of opinion (ra'y), and the narrator was not a jurist and transmission by meaning was rampant, then qiyas is preferred to avoid error. This is not disrespect but a legal ruling. The passage then transitions to Ijma', defining it as the agreement of the mujtahids of the ummah of Muhammad (peace be upon him) in any era on a matter. Its authority is established by the Quranic verse (4:115) which equates following other than the way of the believers with opposing the Messenger, and by the hadith 'Allah will never unite this ummah upon misguidance forever' (narrated by al-Hakim). Thus Ijma' is a definitive proof (hujjah qat'iyyah). The text clarifies that only the consensus of those qualified for ijtihad (ahl al-ra'y wa al-ijtihad) is considered, not that of commoners, theologians (mutakallim), or hadith scholars (muhaddith) who lack insight into usul. Ijma' is divided into four types: (1) Clear consensus of the Companions on a ruling (e.g., caliphate of Abu Bakr) – definitive, rejecting it is kufr. (2) Silent consensus (consensus by silence) – also definitive but denial is not kufr. (3) Consensus of later generations on a matter with no prior opinion – like a well-known report (khabar mashhur) giving reassurance but not certainty. (4) Consensus on a particular opinion of the predecessors – like a solitary report (khabar wahid) requiring action but not certainty, and it takes precedence over qiyas. This classification shows the Hanafi concern for gradations of authority in consensus, corresponding to the strength of evidence.

الراوي (ar-Rawi): The narrator of a hadith, classified into two types: those known for knowledge and ijtihad, and those known for memory and reliability.

Types of Ijma' and Their Legal Authority
Classifying the four types of Ijma' with their legal authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four types of Ijma', state their respective degrees of authority, and explain which type entails kufr upon denial.

Core Idea
Ijma' varies in strength: some types are like a Quran verse, others like well-known or solitary reports.

In this lesson, we detail the four types of Ijma' according to the Hanafi school. Each type has a different legal weight, from definitive (denial equals kufr) to merely probable (like a solitary report). Understanding this hierarchy is essential for knowing how to argue using consensus and for correctly applying the rules of rejection.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four types
List the four types as given: (1) Clear consensus of the Companions (Ijma' al-sahabah bil-nass). (2) Silent consensus (Ijma' sukuti) where some Companions speak and others remain silent. (3) Consensus of later generations on a matter with no prior opinion from the Salaf. (4) Consensus of later generations on a particular opinion of the Salaf.
Each type corresponds to a different level of certainty and consequence.
2
Determine the ruling for each type
For type 1: It is definitive like the Quran; denying it is kufr. For type 2: Also definitive but denial is not kufr. For type 3: Like a well-known report (khabar mashhur), giving tranquility but not certainty. For type 4: Like a solitary report (khabar wahid), requiring action but not certainty, and it takes precedence over qiyas.
The gradation reflects the strength of evidence and the degree of consensus.
3
Draw examples from the text
The text gives examples: type 1 is the consensus on the caliphate of Abu Bakr. Type 2 is the consensus on fighting the zakat refusers. For types 3 and 4, no specific examples are given, but they are analogized to hadith types.
These examples ground the abstract types in historical events.
4
Understand the reasoning behind the gradation
Type 1 is strongest because all Companions explicitly agreed. Type 2 is still strong because silence implies consent, but the lack of explicit statement reduces the certainty of intention. Types 3 and 4 involve later scholars and are weaker due to the absence of direct Prophetic contact and the possibility of disagreement.
This reasoning aligns with Hanafi usul's emphasis on the authority of the Companions.
5
Recognize the scope of consideration
Only the consensus of 'ahl al-ra'y wa al-ijtihad' (people of opinion and ijtihad) is considered. Commoners, theologians (mutakallim), and hadith narrators (muhaddith) without juristic insight are excluded.
This reinforces the Hanafi requirement of juristic qualification for consensus.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul text deals primarily with Ijma' (consensus) as a source of law. It begins with a preliminary classification of narrators (rawi) into two types: those known for knowledge and ijtihad (like the four caliphs and the 'Abadilah—Ibn Mas'ud, Ibn Umar, Ibn Abbas, and either Ibn 'Amr or Ibn az-Zubayr), and those known for memory and reliability (like Abu Hurairah and Anas). The ruling for the first type is that their narration is given precedence over qiyas (analogy). For the second, if their narration agrees with qiyas it is accepted; if it contradicts, it is not abandoned except for necessity (darurah). The necessity is explained: if acting on the hadith would completely close the door of opinion (ra'y), and the narrator was not a jurist and transmission by meaning was rampant, then qiyas is preferred to avoid error. This is not disrespect but a legal ruling. The passage then transitions to Ijma', defining it as the agreement of the mujtahids of the ummah of Muhammad (peace be upon him) in any era on a matter. Its authority is established by the Quranic verse (4:115) which equates following other than the way of the believers with opposing the Messenger, and by the hadith 'Allah will never unite this ummah upon misguidance forever' (narrated by al-Hakim). Thus Ijma' is a definitive proof (hujjah qat'iyyah). The text clarifies that only the consensus of those qualified for ijtihad (ahl al-ra'y wa al-ijtihad) is considered, not that of commoners, theologians (mutakallim), or hadith scholars (muhaddith) who lack insight into usul. Ijma' is divided into four types: (1) Clear consensus of the Companions on a ruling (e.g., caliphate of Abu Bakr) – definitive, rejecting it is kufr. (2) Silent consensus (consensus by silence) – also definitive but denial is not kufr. (3) Consensus of later generations on a matter with no prior opinion – like a well-known report (khabar mashhur) giving reassurance but not certainty. (4) Consensus on a particular opinion of the predecessors – like a solitary report (khabar wahid) requiring action but not certainty, and it takes precedence over qiyas. This classification shows the Hanafi concern for gradations of authority in consensus, corresponding to the strength of evidence.

إجماع سكوتي (Ijma' Sukuti): Consensus formed by the explicit statement of some companions and the silence of the rest, indicating tacit agreement.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Only the explicit consensus of the Companions is like the Quran; other types have lower epistemological force.

Ijma (consensus) is the third primary source of Islamic law. This passage from the Hanafi usul text defines Ijma, establishes its authority through a hadith, and then breaks it down into four distinct types each with a different legal weight. Understanding these gradations is essential because not all consensus carries the same force — a point often misunderstood.

Commentary
This passage defines Ijma (consensus) as a source of Islamic law in the Hanafi usul tradition. It begins by citing the well-known hadith of Ibn Mas'ud, which grounds the authority of communal agreement. The author then states that Ijma of the ummah after the Prophet's death on subsidiary matters is a definitive proof (hujja qat'iyya) that obligates action. Importantly, only the consensus of scholars of opinion and ijtihad is considered; laymen, orators, and innovators lack the insight into usul al-din to contribute. The passage then classifies Ijma into four types with distinct legal weights: (1) explicit consensus of the Companions — definitive, denial amounts to disbelief; (2) silent consensus of the Companions — also definitive but denial does not entail disbelief; (3) consensus of later generations on a matter where no predecessor's opinion exists — equivalent to khabar mashhur, giving satisfaction but not certainty; (4) consensus on one of the predecessor's opinions — equivalent to a solitary report, binding in action but not knowledge, and preferred over Qiyas.
