The Dispute Over Thumma: Delay in Utterance and Ruling
Usul dispute on thumma with thamarah al-khilaf and figurative note

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute over the particle thumma and apply its practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) in conditional divorce scenarios.

Core Idea
Thumma indicates delay, but whether in utterance and ruling or only in ruling changes legal outcomes.

The particle 'thumma' (then) is a common Arabic word, yet its precise legal meaning was debated among the early Hanafi masters. Imam Abu Hanifa understood it to imply a delay both in speech and in legal effect, while his two chief disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, held that the delay applies only to the ruling, with the utterance remaining connected. This seemingly technical disagreement has a direct impact on how conditional divorces are adjudicated, as the source passage illustrates. Let us examine the arguments and the practical fruit of this ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary meaning of thumma
The passage states that thumma indicates tarakhi (delay). Recognize that this is the default linguistic meaning in Hanafi usul.
The delay can be in time or in order of events.
2
Contrast the two positions
Abu Hanifa says delay is in both lafz (utterance) and hukm (ruling). The Sahibayn say delay is only in hukm, while the utterance is connected (wasl). Understand the reasoning: Abu Hanifa sees tarakhi as requiring separation in speech; the Sahibayn argue that the word itself is one utterance, so connection in speech is the default.
This is the core theoretical difference.
3
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf in a conditional divorce
Work through the example: an unconsummated wife is told 'You are divorced, then divorced, then divorced, if you enter the house.' Under Abu Hanifa, only the first divorce is effective (the rest are لغو) because the utterance is disconnected. Under the Sahibayn, all three are attached to the condition and occur in order, but since the first divorce makes her ba'in, the second and third are void.
Note the difference: Abu Hanifa's ruling depends on the placement of the condition.
4
Note the figurative usage of thumma as waw
The passage cites Quran 90:17 where thumma is interpreted as 'and' (waw) figuratively. This shows that thumma can be used beyond its literal meaning, but the primary usul meaning is tarakhi.
This does not affect the dispute but broadens understanding.

Thumma (then) indicates delay (tarakhi). However, according to Abu Hanifa, it indicates delay in both wording and ruling. Commentary
This passage explains thumma (then). Its primary meaning is delay (tarakhi). The disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn is about the nature of this delay: Imam Abu Hanifa holds that the delay applies to both the utterance (lafz) and the ruling (hukm). That is, when one says 'then', it is as if there is a pause in speech and a pause in the legal effect. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say the delay is only in the ruling, while the speech is connected. The practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) is in the example of conditional divorces: If an unconsummated wife is told 'You are divorced, then divorced, then divorced, if you enter the house', then according to Abu Hanifa, the first divorce takes effect immediately, and the rest are invalid because the speech is disconnected. If the condition is mentioned first, then the first divorce is attached to the condition, the second occurs, and the third is invalid. According to the Sahibayn, all three are attached to the condition and occur in order. For an unconsummated wife, the first divorce effects a ba'in separation, so the second and third are invalid (since she is no longer his wife). The passage also notes that thumma can be used figuratively like waw, citing the Quranic verse.

Key Terms
ثم (thumma): A particle indicating delay (tarakhi) in time or order.
التراخي (al-tarakhi): Delay; a gap in time between two events.
ثمرة الاختلاف (thamarah al-khilaf): The practical consequence of a legal disagreement.
