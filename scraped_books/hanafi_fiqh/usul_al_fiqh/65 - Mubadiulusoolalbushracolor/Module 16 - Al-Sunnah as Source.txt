Definition of Sunnah in Hanafi Usul
Defines Sunnah linguistically and technically, including Companions' statements.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of Sunnah linguistically and technically, and explain why the Hanafi school includes the statements and actions of the Companions in this definition.

Core Idea
Sunnah is the Prophet's way; Hanafi usul extends it to include the practices of the Companions as a source of law.

This passage introduces the concept of Sunnah, a foundational source of Islamic law after the Quran. While Sunnah commonly refers to the Prophet's statements, actions, and approvals, the Hanafi school gives it a broader scope, incorporating the statements and actions of the Companions. Understanding this definition is crucial for grasping how Hanafi jurists derive rulings from transmitted precedents.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning
What does 'sunnah' mean in Arabic language? It means 'way' or 'method' (al-tariqah).
This is the literal sense, not the technical.
2
Identify the technical meaning for the Prophet
What falls under the Prophet's Sunnah? Three categories: statements (qawl), actions (fi'l), and tacit approvals (taqrir).
These are the standard elements of the Prophetic Sunnah.
3
Note the Hanafi extension
What additional element does the author include in the intended meaning here? The statements and actions of the Companions (aqwal wa af'al al-sahaba).
This distinguishes Hanafi usul from other schools.
4
Understand the significance
Why is this inclusion important for Hanafi legal theory? It elevates the Companions' practices to a source of law, reflecting the principle that their actions were often known and approved by the Prophet.
This is related to the concept of qawl al-sahabi (the saying of a Companion) as a proof.

The Three Types of Legal Explanation (Bayan) in Hanafi Usul
Defines three types of legal explanation (bayan) in Hanafi usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and explain the three types of bayan (bayan al-hal, bayan al-'atf, bayan al-tabdeel) with examples from Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Bayan is the Lawgiver's clarification — silence can speak, conjunction can specify, and abrogation can replace.

In Hanafi usul, the Sunnah is conveyed through various modes of explanation (bayan). This lesson introduces three key types: explanation by implication (bayan al-hal), explanation by conjunction (bayan al-'atf), and explanation by substitution (bayan al-tabdeel), which is abrogation (naskh). Understanding these patterns helps you recognize how rulings are derived from the Prophet's practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of Sunnah
Locate the opening line: 'Sunnah of the Messenger of Allah'. The text does not expand the definition here, but in Hanafi usul, Sunnah includes the Prophet's sayings, actions, and tacit approvals.
The key terms table provides the full definition.
2
Analyze bayan al-hal (explanation by implication)
Read the description: when the Lawgiver sees an action and remains silent, that silence serves as an implicit approval (ibahah). The example of a master's silence when his slave trades illustrates that silence in a context of need functions as permission.
This type of bayan is based on the maxim: 'al-sukut fi ma'rid al-hajah yanuzilu manzilat al-bayan'.
3
Analyze bayan al-'atf (explanation by conjunction)
When a specific item (e.g., a dirham) is conjoined to a general term (e.g., one hundred), the conjunction clarifies that the general term includes that specific type. The example 'one hundred and a dirham' shows that the hundred are also dirhams.
This is a linguistic tool to remove ambiguity from general statements.
4
Analyze bayan al-tabdeel (explanation by substitution / naskh)
This is abrogation: a later legal text lifts an earlier ruling. The example of visiting graves — initially forbidden, then permitted — shows how the Lawgiver can replace a ruling. The author notes that naskh is permissible only from the Lawgiver, not from humans.
Hanafis accept naskh as a valid form of bayan, but it must come from divine revelation.
5
Apply the legal definition dissector to 'bayan'
Use the provided toolbox: bayan is a legitimate tool (hukm) that applies to legal rulings (mahall), triggered by the need to clarify (sabab), must come from the Lawgiver (shart), with no preventer (mani), and its effect is clarifying the ruling (athar).
This framework helps you evaluate whether a given clarification qualifies as bayan.

Classification of Sunnah: Mutawatir and Mashhur
Defines Sunnah and classifies three transmission types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between mutawatir and mashhur hadith according to Hanafi usul, and state the level of knowledge each yields.

Core Idea
Mutawatir yields certain knowledge; mashhur also yields certain knowledge though originally ahad; rejecting mutawatir is disbelief.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi classification of the Sunnah based on transmission. Understanding these categories is essential for evaluating the authority of hadith and the level of knowledge they produce. The Sunnah, like the Quran, is an authoritative source, but doubt can arise regarding its connection to the Prophet. The classification resolves this.

Guided Path
1
Define Sunnah
Identify the linguistic and technical definitions of Sunnah, noting that Hanafis include the practices of the Companions.
Sunnah linguistically means 'way' and technically includes sayings, actions, and tacit approvals of the Prophet, plus Companion practices.
2
Understand the doubt in transmission
Recognize that while Sunnah is authoritative, its transmission may raise doubts. The focus is on how reports reach us (connection to the Prophet) and the number of narrators.
The doubt is in thubut (establishment) and ittisal (connection).
3
Define mutawatir
State the definition: a report narrated by an uncountable number at every level, impossible to collude on a lie. Give examples (Quran, five prayers) and note its ruling: certain knowledge, rejection is kufr.
The knowledge is daruri (intuitive), not inferential.
4
Define mashhur
State the definition: a report originally transmitted by few (ahad) but becomes widespread in the second generation, transmitted by a group that cannot collude, and accepted by the ummah. Give example (wiping over leather socks).
Mashhur also yields certain (daruri) knowledge, but rejection is not kufr.
5
Distinguish mutawatir from mashhur
Compare: both yield certain knowledge, but mutawatir is known from the beginning of transmission, while mashhur starts as ahad and gains certainty through later widespread acceptance. Rejecting mutawatir is kufr; rejecting mashhur is not.
The third category, khabar al-wahid, yields zanni (speculative) knowledge.

Definition and classification of Sunnah transmission types.

Mutawatir gives certainty; mashhur gives tranquility — rejecting mutawatir is kufr, rejecting mashhur is bid'ah.

The Prophet's Sunnah is a primary source of law, but not all hadith have the same level of authenticity. Hanafi scholars classify hadith by transmission strength: mutawatir (mass-transmitted) yields necessary knowledge, while mashhur (widely-accepted after initial rarity) yields tranquility. Khabar Wahid: Definition and Conditions
Defining khabar wahid and listing four narrator conditions for its authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khabar wahid, state its ruling, and identify the four narrator conditions required for its authority in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Khabar wahid is binding for action but not certain; its authority depends on the narrator's reason, precision, integrity, and Islam.

Definition and Conditions of Khabar Wahid
Defines khabar wahid, its hukm, and conditions for narrator acceptance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khabar wahid, state its hukm, list the four conditions for narrator acceptance, and distinguish between the two types of narrators.

Core Idea
Khabar wahid obligates action without certainty; its authority depends on narrator intellect, precision, justice, and Islam.

Khabar wahid (solitary report) forms the bulk of hadith material. Unlike mutawatir reports, it does not yield certain knowledge ('ilm al-yaqin) but still obligates action in practical matters. Understanding its conditions is essential for properly weighing transmitted evidence in Hanafi fiqh. This lesson unpacks its definition, evidentiary value, and the criteria that make a narrator reliable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three-part structure
Recognize that the passage addresses: (1) definition of khabar wahid, (2) its hukm, and (3) conditions for its proof. Also note the appendix on narrator types.
The text flows from definition to hukm to conditions.
2
Define khabar wahid and its key feature
Note that it is a report from one or two or more persons that has not reached the level of shuhra (fame) or tawatur. The number of narrators does not matter as long as the threshold of widespread transmission is not met.
The definition emphasizes the absence of shuhra, a Hanafi nuance.
3
State the hukm of khabar wahid
The passage explicitly says: 'يوجب العمل دون علم اليقين' – it obligates action but does not produce certain knowledge. This means it must be followed in practice but leaves room for other evidence.
This distinguishes it from mutawatir, which yields certainty.
4
List and explain the four narrator conditions
The narrator must possess: (1) complete intellect (aql) of an adult, (2) precision (dhabt) in hearing, understanding, memorizing, and reviewing, (3) justice (adala) free from major sins and persistence in minor sins, and (4) Islam. Reports from women, slaves, and the blind are accepted if these conditions are met.
The conditions are cumulative; missing one invalidates the report.
5
Distinguish the two narrator types
Narrators known for knowledge and ijtihad (e.g., four Caliphs, 'Abadilah) have their narration preferred over qiyas. Narrators known for memory and justice (e.g., Abu Hurairah, Anas) have their narration accepted if it agrees with qiyas, but if it differs, it is not abandoned except for necessity.
This classification reflects Hanafi caution when textual transmission contradicts reasoning.

Sabab, Shart, and Mani' in Hanafi Usul
Defining three key legal terms: sabab, shart, mani'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define sabab, shart, and mani', and distinguish them from illah and each other.

Core Idea
Sabab leads to the ruling without causing it; shart completes it; mani' blocks it.

Understanding the declaratory rulings (al-ahkam al-wad'iyyah) is essential for deriving fiqh correctly. In this lesson, we unpack three foundational terms—sabab, shart, and mani'—as defined in the Hanafi tradition. These concepts govern how a ruling is triggered, conditioned, or prevented.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overall framework
Notice the opening sentence: the ruling is connected to its sabab, exists at its shart, and is prevented by its mani'. This tells you that sabab, shart, and mani' are three distinct legal operators for any hukm.
2
Analyze the definition of sabab
Sabab is defined as 'that which leads to a thing without having an effect in it.' The example of a road leading to a destination shows that sabab is a non‑causative means. It does not produce the ruling; it merely provides the occasion for it.
Contrast with illah, which is the effective cause.
3
Analyze the definition of shart
Shart is 'that without which the thing is not complete, and which is not part of its essence.' Wudu is not part of prayer but is required for its validity. The condition is external to the act.
Shurut affect existence (wujud) or validity (sihhah).
4
Analyze the definition of mani'
Mani' is 'that which prevents the occurrence of the ruling despite the existence of the sabab.' Murder prevents inheritance even though the sabab (blood relationship) exists.
The mani' overrides the sabab, blocking the ruling's effect.
5
Apply the distinction
Test your understanding by constructing new examples from fiqh: e.g., the adhan is a sabab for Friday prayer, being a resident is a shart for Jumu'ah, and travel is a mani' for its obligation.

The Four Preventers (Mawani') of the 'Illa
Listing and explaining the four types of preventers of the effective cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four preventers (mawani') of the 'illa, and apply each type to given examples from fiqh.

Core Idea
The four preventers block the cause at different stages: formation, completion, initiation, or continuation.

In Hanafi legal theory, the effectiveness of a cause ('illa) can be blocked by a preventer (mani'). Understanding these four categories is essential for correctly deriving rulings in cases where the cause appears present but the ruling is absent. This lesson lists and explains each type with concrete fiqh examples.

Guided Path
1
Understand the function of a mani'
A mani' is not a condition (shart) that must be present for the cause to be effective; rather, it is an external factor that negates the ruling despite the cause existing. In Hanafi usul, mawani' block the effect of the 'illa at four possible stages.
2
Memorize the four types
The four preventers are: (1) preventer of formation (in'aqad) of the 'illa, (2) preventer of completion (tamam) of the 'illa, (3) preventer of initiation (ibtida) of the ruling, (4) preventer of continuation (dawam) of the ruling. Each corresponds to a different moment in the causal chain.
Use the mnemonics: formation, completion, initiation, continuation.
3
Associate each type with its example from the text
For type 1: selling a free person (lack of mahalliyya). Type 2: destruction of nisab before end of year. Type 3: sale with khiyar (option) delaying ownership. Type 4: khiyar al-bulugh (option of puberty) terminating marriage. Study the reasoning behind each.
The example for type 4 shows that the ruling (marriage) continues until the option is exercised, but the preventer blocks its continuation.
4
Apply the classification to new scenarios
Given a case where a cause is present but the ruling is absent, identify which preventer is at work. For instance, if a person owns nisab but loses it before the year ends, the preventer is of completion. If a contract lacks legal capacity of the object, it's a preventer of formation.
Practice with other examples from fiqh.
