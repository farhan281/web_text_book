Two Types of Connection Between Haqiqah and Majaz
Explaining two types of connection between literal and figurative meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the illa-hukm connection and the sabab-hukm connection, and state the ruling on using majaz in each type of connection.

Core Idea
Majaz works both ways in illa-hukm, but only one way in sabab-hukm: you may mention the sabab intending the hukm, not vice versa.

In Hanafi usul, the relationship between a word's literal meaning (haqiqah) and its intended figurative meaning (majaz) is not arbitrary. The Shariah recognizes specific patterns of connection (ittisal). This lesson unpacks two fundamental types: when the connection is via the effective cause (illa) and when it is via a mere reason (sabab). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly interpreting legal statements and oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of connections
Read the passage and note the two categories: (1) connection between illa (cause) and hukm (ruling), (2) connection between sabab (reason) and hukm.
The passage calls them 'نوعان'.
2
Define illa and sabab as used here
Understand that 'illa' is an attribute that directly brings about the ruling (e.g., purchase causes ownership), while 'sabab' is a precursor that leads to the ruling without causal effect (e.g., ownership of a slave is a reason for the right to enjoy her).
The passage provides explicit definitions.
3
Apply the rule on majaz directionality
For illa-hukm, majaz is valid in both directions: mentioning the illa intending the hukm, or mentioning the hukm intending the illa. For sabab-hukm, majaz is valid only from the sabab side: mentioning the sabab intending the hukm, but not the opposite.
The examples illustrate this asymmetry.
4
Analyze the examples provided
Go through each example: 'If I own a slave, he is free' (both directions ok), 'If I purchase a slave, he is free' (both directions ok), 'I freed you' to wife intending divorce (valid), 'I divorced you' to female slave intending emancipation (invalid).
Trace how each example maps to illa-hukm or sabab-hukm.
5
Consider the legal consequences of honest vs dishonest intent
Note that when the speaker's intention contradicts the literal meaning, the judiciary does not accept it if it would lead to leniency or suspicion, even if the intention is valid in principle.
This reflects the adab of litigation.

Commentary
This passage explains the two types of connection (ittisal) between haqiqah and majaz in Shariah rulings. The first type is the connection between the cause (illa) and the legal ruling (hukm). The illa is the effective cause that directly brings about the ruling. For example, purchasing is the cause of ownership. In such a connection, majaz can be used in both directions: one can mention the cause and intend the ruling, or mention the ruling and intend the cause. Both are valid. The text gives the example: saying 'if I own a slave, he is free' intending by 'ownership' the act of purchasing is valid; and saying 'if I purchase a slave, he is free' intending by 'purchasing' the resultant ownership is also valid. The second type is the connection between the reason (sabab) and the ruling. The sabab is a precursor that leads to the ruling without having causal effect. For instance, full ownership of a slave (milk al-raqaba) is the reason for the right to enjoy her sexually (milk al-mut'a). In this type, majaz is only valid from one side: one may mention the reason and intend the ruling, but not the opposite. The example: saying to one's wife 'I have set you free' intending divorce is valid because setting free (emancipation) is a cause of removal of ownership, which in turn is a reason for removal of marital rights. But saying to one's female slave 'I have divorced you' intending emancipation is invalid because divorce has no causative link to emancipation. The passage also clarifies definitions of illa and sabab, and provides additional examples about oaths and qasd. It emphasizes the distinction between the two types of connections and the legal consequences of using figurative speech in each.

Key Terms
اتصال (ittisal): Connection between the literal and figurative meanings in a legal context
علة (illa): Effective cause (ratio legis) upon which the ruling depends; the ruling exists with it and is absent with its absence
سبب (sabab): A reason or means that leads to something without having a direct effect on it
ملك الرقبة (milk al-raqaba): Full ownership of something (e.g., a slave), as opposed to mere right of use
ملك المتعة (milk al-mut'a): Right of sexual enjoyment, especially with a female slave or wife

Definition, Authority, and First Condition of Qiyas
Defines Qiyas, states its authority, and lists conditions for validity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of Qiyas, explain its authority as a proof (hujja) and revealer (muzhir), and apply the condition that it must not oppose an explicit text (nass), citing the Quranic proof.

Core Idea
Qiyas reveals the law by analogy but is invalid if it contradicts a clear text.

Welcome to the basics of Qiyas, the fourth source of Islamic law in the Hanafi tradition. This lesson unpacks its definition, its authority as a proof, and the first critical condition: it must not oppose a nass. We'll see how the Quran itself commands us to use analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic and technical definitions
Extract the literal meaning ('estimation') and the technical definition: extending the ruling from the original (asl) to the new case (far') due to a shared effective cause (illa).
2
Understand the authority (hukm) of Qiyas
Note that Qiyas is a proof (hujja) by both transmission and reason, and it is a 'revealer' (muzhir) of the ruling, not a creator (muthbit).
This distinguishes it from primary sources.
3
List the five conditions and examine the first
The author states there are five conditions, then elaborates only the first: Qiyas must not be in opposition to a text (nass). Understand that this is the most fundamental condition.
The other four are implied in later sections.
4
Analyze the proof from the Quran
Study the verse (al-Hashr 59:2) and the interpretation of 'i'tibar' as analogical reasoning. Understand how the Hanafis derive the validity of Qiyas from this command.
The word 'fa'tabiru' is the operative term.
5
Apply the condition to the example of laughter in prayer
Examine the flawed analogy: if slander does not break wudu, then loud laughter should also not break wudu. Recognize why it fails: it contradicts the explicit hadith that laughter breaks wudu.
This is a classic example of qiyas muqabil al-nass.

Qiyas does not create new rulings; it manifests what the sources already imply.

This lesson introduces the fourth source of Islamic law in the Hanafi usul: Qiyas (analogical reasoning). You will learn its linguistic and technical meanings, and understand why the Hanafi school considers it a proof that reveals, not creates, legal rulings.

Five Conditions for the Validity of Qiyas
Enumerates and explains five conditions that must be met for qiyas to be valid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the five conditions for the validity of qiyas according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Qiyas is valid only when it does not contradict a text, does not alter a text's ruling, uses a rationally understood cause, aims at a legal ruling, and does not duplicate an explicit ruling.

Qiyas is a vital tool for deriving rulings on new cases, but it is not unrestricted. The Hanafi school sets five conditions to ensure that analogy remains grounded in revelation. In this lesson, we explore each condition with examples and the proof for qiyas itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dalil for qiyas
Recognize that qiyas is established by the Quranic verse 'Fa'tabiru ya uli al-absar' (59:2) and the hadith of Mu'adh. The Hanafi position is that qiyas is a valid hujja (proof) for deriving rulings.
The verse uses 'i'tibar', meaning comparison, as the basis for analogical reasoning.
2
Learn the first condition: qiyas must not oppose a text
Understand the example given: comparing loud laughter in prayer to slander of a chaste woman to argue that laughter does not break wudu is invalid because it contradicts an explicit hadith commanding repetition of wudu for laughter.
The condition is 'la yakunu al-qiyas fi muqabilat al-nass'.
3
Learn the second condition: qiyas must not change a text's ruling
See the example: requiring intention (niyyah) in wudu by analogy to tayammum would change the absolute command of the wudu verse (which does not mention intention) to a restricted one, which is impermissible.
Qiyas cannot alter a hukm that is established by a clear text.
4
Learn the third to fifth conditions
Third: The asl ruling must be rationally understandable (ma'qul al-ma'na); purely devotional rulings (ta'abbudi) cannot be extended. Fourth: Qiyas must aim at a legal ruling, not a linguistic definition. Fifth: The branch (far') must not have an explicit ruling in the texts.
Examples: date-nabidh (ta'abbudi), linguistic definition of khamr, and explicit rulings on freeing slaves for expiations.
5
Apply the conditions
Test a proposed qiyas against each condition. For example, consider whether beating parents is prohibited by qiyas on the prohibition of saying 'uff' (Quran 17:23). The asl (saying 'uff') has a rationally understood cause (disrespect), so qiyas on beating is valid provided no text contradicts.
Practice with the examples from the lesson to solidify understanding.

Commentary
This passage lists the five conditions for a valid qiyas according to Hanafi usul. The first condition: Qiyas must not contradict a clear text (nass). An example is comparing loud laughter (qahqaha) in prayer to slander (qadhf) in terms of breaking wudu; this analogy is invalid because a hadith from the Bedouin shows that laughter breaks wudu. The text then provides the proof for qiyas itself (the Quranic verse and hadith of Mu'adh) suggesting that qiyas is a valid hujja, but the first condition limits its scope. The second condition: Qiyas must not alter a ruling of the text. For instance, using analogy to require intention (niyyah) in wudu based on tayammum would change the absolute command of the wudu verse to a conditional one, which is impermissible. The third condition: The ruling in the original case must be rationally understandable (ma'qul al-ma'na). If the ruling is purely devotional (ta'abbudi), like wudu with date-nabidh, analogy cannot extend it because the cause is unknown. The fourth condition: Qiyas must aim at a legal ruling, not a linguistic definition. For example, calling musaffa (grape juice cooked until half evaporates) wine based on linguistic meaning is not valid qiyas. The fifth condition: The branch must not have an explicit ruling already. If both asl and far' are directly covered by texts, no analogy is needed. These conditions ensure that qiyas remains a disciplined method of legal derivation within the boundaries of revelation.

Four Rules on Sabab and Illah
Stating four principles governing the relationship between sabab and illah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sabab and illah and apply the four Hanafi rules that govern their relationship.

Core Idea
Sabab is the means, illah is the cause. The ruling follows the illah, not the sabab, unless the sabab itself becomes the cause.

In Hanafi usul, understanding the difference between sabab (non-causative means) and illah (effective cause) is essential for correctly attributing rulings. This lesson presents four key principles that clarify how these two interact, ensuring that qiyas is applied consistently and that concessions are made when needed.

Guided Path
1
Define sabab and illah
Clarify the core distinction: sabab is a means that does not directly produce the ruling; illah is the effective cause that directly produces it. The first rule sets this baseline: when both are present, the ruling goes to the illah.
2
Examine the first rule – ruling attributed to illah
Study the example of the person who directs a thief but is not liable. The director is a sabab; the thief's act is the illah. The ruling (non-liability) follows the illah's absence of direct causation.
This rule prevents misattribution of liability to indirect participants.
3
Examine the second rule – sabab as illah
Analyze the animal-driver example: the driver's action is a sabab that directly causes the animal's behavior (the illah). Since the animal has no choice, the driver's sabab is deemed the effective cause in meaning.
This applies when the sabab is the sole cause of the illah's occurrence.
4
Examine the third rule – sabab substitutes for hidden illah
Identify the three examples: deep sleep for hadath, seclusion (khalwa) for intercourse, travel for hardship. The illah is hidden or difficult to ascertain, so the sabab is pragmatically substituted to ease the obligation.
This is an application of istihsan (juristic preference) for facilitation.
5
Examine the fourth rule – metaphorical sabab
Understand that an oath is called a sabab for expiation, but the real sabab is breaking the oath (hinth). This shows caution: terminology may be used loosely, but the actual cause must be identified.
This prevents literal confusion and keeps the focus on the effective cause.

Commentary
This passage enumerates four important principles in Hanafi usul regarding the interplay between sabab (non-causative means) and illah (effective cause). The first rule states that when both exist, the ruling is attributed to the illah, not the sabab—illustrated by the example of a person who directs a thief; the director is not liable because his role is merely sabab while the thief's act is the illah. The second rule covers cases where sabab functions effectively like an illah because it is the cause of the illah itself, e.g., a driver of an animal is liable for damage it causes because the driver's action is the real effective cause. The third rule deals with situations where we substitute sabab for illah as a practical concession when the illah is hidden, e.g., deep sleep substitutes for hadath (impurity) in nullifying wudu, seclusion (khalwa) substitutes for actual intercourse in establishing certain rulings, and travel substitutes for hardship in granting concessions. The fourth rule clarifies that sometimes a term is used metaphorically, like calling an oath a 'sabab' for expiation when the actual sabab is breaking the oath. These nuances are crucial for applying qiyas correctly in the Hanafi school.

السبب (al-sabab): A non-causative means or occasion that sets the stage for the illah but does not directly produce the ruling.

Al-Mumana'a: Denial as a Method of Refuting Qiyas
Explaining the first method of refuting qiyas: denial of the cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of mumana'a and apply denial of the cause (man' al-illa) to refute a qiyas argument in a Hanafi usul debate.

Core Idea
Mumana'a is not mere rejection; it is a structured denial of the opponent's proof, most commonly the cause in a qiyas argument.

In Hanafi usul, rebutting an opponent's qiyas is a disciplined art. The first of eight methods is al-mumana'a – a focused denial of the opponent's evidence. This passage lays out the definition and a powerful subtype: denial of the claimed effective cause ('illa). Through the example of sadaqat al-fitr, you will see how the Hanafi school uses man' al-illa to preserve its own position against a Shafi'i analogy.

Guided Path
1
Define al-Mumana'a
Understand that mumana'a means not accepting the opponent's proof (dalil) wholly or partially. It is derived from 'man'' (prevention) and is the first of eight ways to rebut qiyas.
Mumana'a is a dialectical tool, not a simple rejection.
2
Identify the two subtypes
The text mentions two types: (1) denial of the cause (man' al-illa) and (2) denial of something else (implied – likely denial of the ruling or of the textual basis). Focus on man' al-illa for this lesson.
Other subtypes (e.g., denial of the asl or hukm) appear in later discussions.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i qiyas
Al-Shafi'i argues that sadaqat al-fitr is wajib due to iftar (breaking the fast). Therefore, the obligation attaches at sunset on the last day of Ramadan, so one who dies after sunset owes it, but a newborn or new Muslim after sunset does not.
The Shafi'i 'illa is 'iftar'; the asl is probably the general obligation of zakat connected to a cause.
4
Apply the Hanafi man' al-illa
The Hanafi response: 'We do not accept that the cause is iftar; rather it is the head (dependant) whom one supports.' This denies the Shafi'i 'illa directly, breaking the qiyas.
Denying the cause does not require disproving the asl or hukm; it only challenges the link.
5
Extract the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafi—obligation at fajr of Eid day; thus the newborn/new Muslim after fajr owes, the deceased before fajr does not. Shafi'i—obligation at maghrib; thus the deceased after maghrib owes, the newborn/new Muslim after maghrib does not.
This difference stems from conflicting 'illas, not from different texts.

The Four Preventers of the 'Illah (Mawani' al-'Illah)
A rule listing the four types of preventers of the effective cause in qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish and apply the four categories of preventers (mawani') that impede the effective cause in Hanafi qiyas.

Core Idea
Four types of preventers block the 'illah: at inception, completion, ruling start, or ruling continuance.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the effective cause ('illah) may be prevented from producing its ruling by various obstacles. This lesson categorises these obstacles into four distinct types, each illustrated with concrete fiqh examples. Understanding these categories helps the faqih diagnose why a qiyas may fail to apply.

Guided Path
1
Identify the preventer that blocks the inception of the 'illah
Look for a scenario where the 'illah cannot even begin because a foundational element is missing—like selling a free person (lack of mahall).
This is the most fundamental block: the cause never comes into existence.
2
Identify the preventer that blocks the completion of the 'illah
Here the 'illah started but was interrupted before full realization—like loss of nisab during the year, which prevents the full cause of zakat.
The cause was partially formed but not completed.
3
Identify the preventer that blocks the beginning of the ruling
The cause is complete, but the ruling is delayed from commencing—like a sale with khiyar al-shart, where ownership is deferred.
The ruling attaches but does not take immediate effect.
4
Identify the preventer that blocks the continuation of the ruling
The ruling had begun but is cut short—like khiyar al-bulugh which allows termination of a marriage contract.
The ruling is temporary and can be undone.

The Five Objections to the 'Illah in Qiyas
Five 'illah objections with Hanafi-Shafi'i examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the five types of objections (mawaani') to an 'illah in qiyas, and apply each to a Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute example.

Core Idea
Five objections—'aks, fasad al-wad', farq, naqd, mu'aradah—safeguard the validity of analogical reasoning.

In Hanafi usul, qiyas is a central method of deriving rulings. However, the strength of an analogy depends on the soundness of the 'illah (cause). Opponents may raise objections to challenge the 'illah, and the Hanafi school has systematized five such objections. This lesson unpacks each objection with concrete examples from Hanafi-Shafi'i debates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the five objections
List the five terms: al-'aks (inversion), fasad al-wad' (invalid postulation), al-farq (distinction), al-naqd (breach), al-mu'aradah (counter-argument). For each, note the definition given in the passage.
These are the classical categories of 'illah critique.
2
Understand the logical structure
For each objection, analyze how it attacks the 'illah. For example, 'aks shows the 'illah leads to an opposite ruling, naqd shows the 'illah exists without the ruling.
Each objection targets a different aspect of the causal link.
3
Study the Hanafi response example
For each objection, the passage provides a Hanafi reply to a Shafi'i argument. Read the example and identify which part of the Shafi'i qiyas is being challenged.

4
Apply to a new scenario
Given a hypothetical qiyas, determine which objection(s) might apply. For instance, if an opponent argues that wine is prohibited because it intoxicates, you might raise al-farq: intoxication is not the sole cause.
This builds practical critical thinking in usul.
