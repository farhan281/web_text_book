The Book of Allah and the Four Divisions of the Word
Defining the Quran and presenting the four categories of lafz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of the Quran (Kitab Allah) as a mutawatir source, and identify and define the four categories of lafz by coinage (wad'): khas, amm, mushtarak, and muawwal.

Core Idea
The word (lafz) is divided into four types by coinage: specific, general, polysemous, and interpreted — the foundation of understanding Quranic and Sunnah texts.

This passage opens the study of the Quran as a primary source of law. It first defines the Quran itself as the mutawatir revelation transmitted without doubt. It then introduces the first of four divisions that usul scholars apply to both Quran and Sunnah: the division of words by their linguistic coinage (wad'). You will learn to distinguish four types: specific (khas), general (amm), polysemous (mushtarak), and interpreted (muawwal). This classification is essential for determining how texts yield legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Define the Quran (Kitab Allah)
Extract the three attributes given: (1) revealed to the Prophet, (2) written in the mus-hafs, (3) transmitted mutawatir without doubt.
2
List the four categories of lafz (by coinage)
From the text: khas, amm, mushtarak, muawwal. This is the first division; three more divisions exist (see later passages).
3
Define each category
Khas: coined for a known meaning or named thing exclusively (single meaning). Amm: indicates with sharing among individuals (multiple instances). Mushtarak: coined for more than one meaning without preference. Muawwal: a polysemous word where one meaning is outweighed by interpretation — it is a subset of mushtarak.
4
Understand the subcategories of khas
The text notes that 'exclusivity' means the word indicates one meaning, whether by person (e.g., Zayd), type (e.g., man), genus (e.g., animal), real concept (e.g., knowledge), or conventional meaning (e.g., number words).
5
Locate muawwal within mushtarak
The text explicitly states: 'So the interpreted in reality is a type of the polysemous.' Thus, muawwal is not a separate primary category but arises when interpretation resolves ambiguity.

Definition and Ruling of the Specific Word (Khas)
Defining the specific word and its ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the specific word (khas) in Hanafi usul and apply its ruling as a decisive proof (qat'i).

Core Idea
The specific word (khas) is a decisive proof; it must be acted upon because it indicates its meaning unequivocally.

In this lesson, we explore the definition of the specific word (al-khas) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author clarifies what makes a word 'specific' and why it carries decisive legal authority. Using a Quranic example, we see how a specific word like 'three' determines the waiting period for divorced women, illustrating its binding force.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of khas
Extract the precise definition from the text: 'a word coined for a known meaning or a known named thing exclusively.' Note the key condition of exclusivity (infirad).
The definition emphasizes that the word is placed for a single meaning, whether that meaning is a person, type, or genus.
2
Understand the condition of infirad (exclusivity)
Infirad means the word indicates one meaning, cutting off any sharing with other meanings. This condition distinguishes a specific word from a general or polysemous word.
The text clarifies that 'cutting off participation' means the word singles out one meaning, even if it is a conceptual meaning like numbers.
3
Examine the Quranic example
Analyze the verse about the waiting period. Identify the specific word 'thalathah' (three) and the term 'quru' interpreted as menstruation. See how the specific word decisively determines the waiting period as three complete menstruations.
The example shows how a specific number applies to a specific meaning.
4
Derive the ruling for khas
The text states: 'Its ruling: the specific is a decisive proof, it must be acted upon.' Understand why: because it encompasses its meaning decisively, leaving no ambiguity.
This ruling distinguishes khas from general or interpreted words.
5
Note the mention of subdivisions
The passage notes that command, prohibition, absolute, and restricted are divisions of the specific word, to be covered later. Recognize that these are subtypes of khas.
Do not confuse these subtypes as separate categories; they are all specific in their indication.

Defining al-amm and its linguistic scope

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of al-amm and distinguish between generality in wording and generality in meaning.

Core Idea
Al-amm is a word that covers a collectivity of individuals sharing a common description.

This lesson establishes the foundational definition of al-amm (general word) as used in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. Mastering this definition is essential because it determines the scope of legal rulings before any specification (takhsis) is applied.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Read the definition: 'al-amm: lafz yashmal jam'an min al-afrad'. What does 'jam'an min al-afrad' mean?
It means a collectivity of individuals.
2
Distinguish wording vs meaning
The definition says 'imma lafzan aw ma'nan'. Examples: 'Muslimin' is wording; 'man' is meaning. Identify which is which.
Words like 'kull' and 'shay' are general in meaning.
3
Confirm the condition of consistent definitions
Note the condition: individuals must share consistent definitions (muttafiqat al-hudud). Contrast with mushtarak.
This distinguishes amm from mushtarak.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'amm, state its ruling as qat'i, explain the effect of takhsis, and apply the Hanafi position to Quranic examples.

Core Idea
A general text is decisive until specified; after specification it becomes speculative for the remainder.

In Hanafi usul, distinguishing between general ('amm) and specific (khass) is crucial for deriving rulings. This lesson defines 'amm, establishes its original certainty (qat'i), and explains how takhsis (specification) reduces that certainty to speculation (zanni) while preserving obligation. Using the Quranic example of trade and riba, you will see how a general word is specified and how the remaining scope is treated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of 'amm
Recognize that 'amm is a word covering all individuals of its class without restriction. The example 'al-bay' (trade) includes every type of sale.

State the original ruling of 'amm

A specifier may be unknown (majhool) like the exact riba transactions, requiring reliance on the Lawgiver's explanation, or known (ma'loom) like the prince's order excluding ahl al-dhimmah.
This distinction affects how much we need additional evidence.

Classification of words by usage and clarity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define haqiqah, majaz, sarih, and kinayah, and state the ruling of each in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Words are classified by literal vs. figurative and clarity: sarih needs no intention; kinayah requires context.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, understanding how words convey meaning is crucial. This lesson introduces a foundational classification: words are either haqiqah (literal) or majaz (figurative), and either sarih (clear) or kinayah (implicit). The ruling of each determines whether intention (niyyah) or context (qarinah) is needed for legal effect.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish haqiqah from majaz

2
Distinguish sarih from kinayah
Determine if the intended meaning is clear without any additional indication (sarih) or requires a qarinah (context or intention) to be understood (kinayah).
Sarih: 'You are divorced' (clear). Kinayah: 'You are ba'in' (ambiguous).
3
Memorize the rulings
Learn the specific hukm for each: haqiqah necessitates its literal meaning; majaz necessitates the figurative meaning; sarih does not require intention; kinayah requires intention or contextual clue.
These rulings have direct fiqh applications, e.g., in divorce and contracts.

Apply to examples
Given a new example (e.g., 'I sold' in a contract, or 'You are free' for a slave), classify it as haqiqah/majaz and sarih/kinayah, then state the ruling.
Practice with Hanafi fiqh scenarios to internalize the concepts.

Types of Haqiqah and the Priority of Literal Meaning
Explains the priority of literal meaning and types of haqiqah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify haqiqah into muta'addirah, mahjurah, and musta'malah, and apply the Hanafi rule of preferring literal meaning over figurative meaning in the context of oaths.

Core Idea
Literal meaning (haqiqah) prevails unless it is unattainable or abandoned; then figurative (majaz) is used by agreement.

In Hanafi usul, the hierarchy between literal and figurative meanings is foundational for interpreting legal texts, oaths, and contracts. This lesson unpacks the rule that the literal meaning takes precedence as long as it is possible, introduces the three types of haqiqah, and explains when and why scholars shift to figurative meaning.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the foundational rule
The literal meaning (haqiqah) is always preferred over figurative (majaz) as long as acting upon it is possible. Figurative is like a borrowed usage that cannot compete with the original.
This principle is derived from the example of the Qur'anic verse on oaths (5:89).
2
Identify the three types of haqiqah
Haqiqah is divided into: (1) muta'addirah (unattainable except with difficulty), (2) mahjurah (abandoned by custom), and (3) musta'malah (currently used).
The first two types are rare; the third is the default.
3
Apply the rule to unattainable and abandoned types
For haqiqah muta'addirah and mahjurah, all scholars agree to resort to the figurative meaning. E.g., 'eating from the tree' figuratively means its fruit or price; 'placing foot' figuratively means entering.
The literal meaning is either physically impossible or socially abandoned.
4
Apply the rule to current haqiqah
If the literal meaning is current and there is no conventional figurative meaning, the literal is taken by consensus. If a conventional figurative meaning exists, Imam Abu Hanifa still holds that the literal meaning is preferred.
This distinguishes Abu Hanifa from some other scholars who might take the figurative when it is common.

Practice identifying the type of haqiqah and the resulting ruling in the given scenarios about trees, pots, and foot placement.
Pay attention to the condition of possibility and custom.

The Particle 'Ila' and Inclusion of the Limit in Hanafi Usul
Rule for 'ila' and limit inclusion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for 'ila' to determine whether a limit is included in a legal ruling, distinguishing between self-standing and non-self-standing limits.

Core Idea
'Ila' excludes the limit unless the limit is non-self-standing and the speech scope covers it.

This lesson focuses on a key principle in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the interpretation of the particle 'ila' (to/until) when it marks the end of a limit. Understanding when the limit itself is included or excluded is crucial for deriving accurate rulings from Quranic verses and legal texts. The rule is demonstrated with examples from everyday language and the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the particle and its function
Locate 'ila' in the text and recognize it as indicating the termination of a limit (ghayah). It marks the endpoint of an action or state.
Example: 'I travelled from Deoband to Delhi' — 'ila Delhi' marks the endpoint.
2
Determine if the limit is self-standing
Check whether the limit (the word after 'ila') refers to an independent entity or a part of the preceding item. If it is self-standing, it is not included.
Self-standing example: 'I bought this wall to this wall' — the second wall is a separate object.
3
Check if the speech scope covers the limit
If the limit is not self-standing, examine whether the initial part of the statement (sadr al-kalam) logically encompasses the limit. If it does, the limit is included; otherwise, it is excluded.
Non-self-standing example: 'Wash your hands to the elbows' — the elbows are part of the arm and the command 'wash' includes them.
4
Apply the rule to a Quranic example
Apply the rule to the verse 'Then complete the fast until the night' (Al-Baqarah 2:187). The night is a self-standing period separate from the day of fasting, so it is not included.

Core Idea
Bayan al-Tafsir clears ambiguity from mujmal or mushtarak words.

In this lesson, we explore a key linguistic tool in usul al-fiqh: istithna' (exception). Bayan al-Darurah and Its Three Types

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Bayan al-darurah: implicit clarifications that carry the same legal weight as explicit statements.

In Hanafi usul, not all legal rulings are derived from explicit wording. Sometimes the necessity of understanding forces us to infer a ruling from context, silence, or grammatical structure. This lesson defines bayan al-darurah (clarification by necessity) and breaks down its three distinct forms, each with a Quranic or practical example.

Guided Path
1
Define bayan al-darurah
Grasp the core concept: a clarification arising from necessity, not from explicit wording. It is a type of bayan that is binding in understanding the law.
Focus on the 'necessity' element – the ruling would be incomplete without this inference.
2
Identify the three subtypes
List and differentiate: (A) in ruling of the explicit statement, (B) by state (hal), (C) by conjunction ('atf). Each has a distinct source of implication.
Type A is text-based; Type B is context/behavior-based; Type C is grammar-based.
3
Analyze the examples for each type
For Type A: how the father's share (remainder) is derived from the verse giving the mother one-third. For Type B: how the Lawgiver's silence or master's silence implies permission. For Type C: how the conjunction 'and a dirham' specifies the genus.
Notice that in each case, the implication is necessary for complete understanding.
4
Apply the concept to new scenarios
Use the definition and examples to recognize when an implicit meaning is binding. Ask: Is the implication necessary? Does the context demand it?
This helps distinguish legitimate bayan al-darurah from arbitrary inference.
