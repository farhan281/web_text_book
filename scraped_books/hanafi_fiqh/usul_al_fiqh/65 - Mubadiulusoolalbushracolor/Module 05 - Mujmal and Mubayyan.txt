Categories of Word Usage: Haqiqah, Majaz, Sarih, and Kinayah
Defining the four categories of word usage: literal, metaphorical, explicit, and implicit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define haqiqah, majaz, sarih, and kinayah, and distinguish between them based on usage (literal vs metaphorical) and clarity (explicit vs implicit).

Core Idea
Words are classified by two axes: usage (literal vs metaphorical) and clarity (explicit vs implicit), yielding four cross-cutting categories.

In the previous division, we examined words that are clear or ambiguous in themselves (muawwal, etc.). Now we turn to a second division based on usage: whether a word is used in its original meaning (haqiqah) or another meaning (majaz), and whether its meaning is directly disclosed (sarih) or requires a clue (kinayah). These two criteria combine to give four categories that are fundamental for understanding how legal texts convey rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two criteria
The passage classifies words by (1) usage in the original meaning or not, and (2) disclosure or concealment of meaning. Recognize that these are independent axes.
2
Define each term
Using the definitions: haqiqah = used in its coined meaning; majaz = used in other than its coined meaning; sarih = meaning disclosed without a clue; kinayah = meaning requires a clue.
Memorize these definitions — they are axiomatic in Hanafi usul.
3
Apply to examples
Test the definitions with examples from the text: 'asad' for lion (haqiqah), 'salat' for prayer (legal haqiqah). Consider how a word could be both haqiqah and sarih, or majaz and sarih, etc.
4
Recognize cross-cutting nature
Understand that sarih/kinayah are not separate from haqiqah/majaz; they are additional attributes. A word can be haqiqah-sarih (e.g., 'asad' for lion) or haqiqah-kinayah (e.g., a word used literally but indirectly).
This is a common point of confusion — the categories overlap.

Commentary

مجاز (majaz): A word used in a meaning other than its original coinage.
صريح (sarih): A word whose meaning is disclosed directly without need for a clue.
كناية (kinayah): A word whose meaning is concealed and requires a clue to be understood.
وضع (wad'): The assignment of a word to a meaning such that it indicates that meaning without a clue.

Definition and Ruling of Al-Muawwal
Defines al-muawwal and states its ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-muawwal, identify its ruling, and apply the Hanafi usul framework to classify a word as muawwal.

Core Idea
Al-muawwal: a word whose meaning is preponderated by ijtihad; acting upon it is obligatory but allows for error.

This lesson introduces a key term in Hanafi usul: al-muawwal. Unlike definitive texts, muawwal words yield probable (zanni) rulings. Understanding this classification is essential for distinguishing between clear and ambiguous texts in legal derivation.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the Definition
Read the Arabic definition: 'لفظ ترجح بعض معانيه بغالب الرأي' – a word whose meaning is preponderated by predominant opinion. Identify that 'ترجح' indicates a weighting process, not certainty.
2
Analyze the Example
The example is 'قرء' (quru). It can mean either menstruation (حيض) or purity (طهر). The Hanafis, through evidence, preponderated menstruation. Note that the preponderance is based on ghalib al-ray (predominant opinion), not definitive proof.
This shows how ijtihad operates on mushtarak (homonymous) words.
3
Internalize the Ruling
The ruling is 'وجوب العمل به مع احتمال الخطأ' – acting upon it is obligatory (wajib) while acknowledging the possibility of error. This contrasts with definitive texts (muhkam/mufassar) where error is impossible.
The binding force comes from the strength of preponderant evidence, but the zanni nature always leaves room for alternative interpretations.

The ruling is that acting upon the preponderant meaning is obligatory, but there remains a possibility of error, unlike definitive texts. This term is part of the Hanafi classification of words by clarity.

Key Terms

Definition of Mujmal and Its Ruling
Defining mujmal and explaining its ruling via example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mujmal, identify its characteristics, and apply the ruling that it requires the speaker's bayan (clarification).

Core Idea
Mujmal text is so ambiguous that only the speaker's own clarification can resolve it — independent reasoning is insufficient.

In Usul al-Fiqh, texts vary in clarity. The most ambiguous category is al-mujmal, where the wording admits multiple interpretations and the intended meaning cannot be determined without a statement from the original speaker. This passage defines mujmal and illustrates it with the Quranic prohibition of riba, which was clarified by the Prophet's hadith. Understanding mujmal is crucial because such texts cannot be acted upon until the speaker provides a bayan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and its genus
The text defines mujmal as something that has increased in hiddenness over mushkil. So mujmal is a type of ambiguous text, more severe than mushkil.
The term 'mushkil' refers to a text that is difficult but can be resolved through ijtihad. Mujmal cannot.
2
State the cause of ambiguity
Because it admits of many possible interpretations (يحتمل وجوها). This multiplicity makes it impossible to know which meaning is intended without external clarification.
This is the key characteristic that distinguishes mujmal from other ambiguous categories.
3
Determine the remedy: need for speaker's bayan
The text explicitly states that the intended meaning can only be known 'ببيان من قبل المتكلم' – by a statement from the speaker. This means the speaker (God or His Messenger) must clarify.
The phrase 'قبل المتكلم' emphasizes that the clarification must come from the source, not from independent reasoning.
4
Examine the example given – riba
The Quranic verse 'وَحَرَّمَ الرِّبَا' (and He has forbidden usury) uses the word 'riba' which literally means 'increase'. But not all increase is prohibited; otherwise every sale with profit would be forbidden. The Prophet (peace be upon him) clarified in the hadith of six commodities that the prohibited riba is a specific increase without countervalue in the exchange of like-measured items.
This example shows how a single word can be mujmal due to its general literal meaning, requiring a specific bayan.
5
Derive the ruling
The ruling on mujmal is that it cannot be acted upon until the speaker provides a bayan. Mere tahlil (analysis) or talab (seeking) is insufficient. One must wait for the clarification.
This ruling is crucial for deriving fiqh rulings from such texts; without bayan, the ruling remains suspended.

Definition and Ruling of Mutashabih
Defines mutashabih as the most ambiguous text and its ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mutashabih, differentiate its two types, and apply the ruling of tawaqquf to ambiguous Quranic texts according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Mutashabih is text so obscure its meaning is completely unknown; the Hanafi ruling is to pause (tawaqquf) and await clarification.

In the Hanafi classification of unclear texts, mutashabih sits at the far end of ambiguity. Understanding its definition and ruling is essential for knowing how to approach certain Quranic verses and hadith. This lesson unpacks the concept from a foundational Hanafi text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Category
Locate mutashabih as the fourth division of unclear texts in the Hanafi hierarchy (after khafi, mushkil, mujmal). Recognize that it is the most obscure.
The text begins by referencing the previous division (mujmal) and then introduces mutashabih as more obscure.
2
Parse the Definition
Break down the Arabic definition: 'ma izdada khafa’an ‘ala al-mujmal hatta la yu’lam al-murad minhu aslan.' Identify the key components: increased obscurity, comparison to mujmal, and total ignorance of intended meaning.
The phrase 'la yu’lam al-murad minhu aslan' is crucial – it indicates complete lack of knowledge.
3
Examine the Examples
Study the two examples: al-huruf al-muqatta’at (disconnected letters) and the ambiguous divine attributes. Classify each according to the two subtypes mentioned at the end.
First type: meaning totally unknown (disconnected letters). Second type: lexical meaning known but divine intent hidden (attributes like yad, wajh).
4
Apply the Ruling
The ruling is tawaqquf – to pause, refrain from interpretation, and affirm the reality of the meaning while waiting for clarification from the Speaker (Allah). Understand that this prevents both literal anthropomorphism and arbitrary ta’wil.
Tawaqquf is the standard Hanafi position for mutashabih, distinguishing it from mujmal which may be clarified by bayan.

Definition of Bayan and Its First Type: Bayan al-Taqrir
Defines bayan and explains bayan al-taqrir.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define bayan linguistically and technically, and explain how bayan al-taqrir eliminates metaphorical or specific interpretations of a text.

Core Idea
Bayan al-taqrir reinforces the literal meaning, cutting off metaphor or specification.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, understanding how Allah and the Prophet (peace be upon him) clarify legal texts is essential. This lesson defines the term 'bayan' (clarification) and dives into its first type, bayan al-taqrir, which is used to confirm the obvious meaning and rule out any figurative or restricted readings. The Quranic example of birds flying 'with their wings' perfectly illustrates this concept.

Guided Path
1
Define bayan linguistically and technically
Look at the Arabic text: لغۃً: الإظهار (manifestation) and اصطلاحاً: إظهار المراد للمخاطب (making the intended meaning clear to the addressee). Note the Quranic evidence: 'عَلَّمَهُ الْبَيَانَ' (He taught him the bayan).
The linguistic sense is broader; the technical sense is specific to legal interpretation.
2
Identify the five types of bayan
The text states that bayan has five aspects (وجوه). Focus on the first: bayan al-taqrir. Understand that the other four are covered in subsequent lessons.
The fivefold classification is distinctive to Hanafi usul.
3
Explain the function of bayan al-taqrir
It reinforces (توكيد) the speech to cut off possibility of metaphor or specification. The word 'تقرير' means confirmation or establishment. The goal is to preserve the apparent meaning (zahir).
This type assumes the text is already clear but preemptively blocks alternative readings.
4
Analyze the Quranic example
Consider Q. 6:38: 'وَلَا طَائِرٍ يَطِيرُ بِجَنَاحَيْهِ' (And no bird flies with its two wings). The phrase 'بِجَنَاحَيْهِ' (with its two wings) emphasizes literal flight, excluding metaphorical usage like 'flying with ambition'. The bayan here cuts off that possibility.
The example shows how an extra qualifier can serve as taqrir.
5
Connect to earlier usul concepts
The text notes that bayan is needed for mujmal (ambiguous), mushtarak (homonym), ‘amm (general), and khass (specific) texts. Bayan al-taqrir mainly applies to texts where the literal meaning is apparent but still open to figurative interpretation.
This links bayan to the broader discussion of textual categories.

Bayan: Definition and Types in Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh
Defining bayan linguistically and terminologically, then listing three types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define bayan terminologically and distinguish its three types (taqrir, tafsir, taghyeer) by identifying their functions and examples.

Core Idea
Bayan clarifies texts: confirming literal meanings, explaining the ambiguous, or altering rulings through conditions.

In this lesson, we explore the concept of bayan (clarification), a foundational tool in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. You will learn its linguistic and terminological definition, then examine the first three types with Quranic and hadith examples. Understanding bayan is essential for interpreting texts that are ambiguous, general, or potentially figurative.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the twofold definition
Read the linguistic definition (al-izhar, manifestation) and the terminological definition (izhar al-murad lil-mukhatib, manifesting the intended meaning to the addressee). Note how they build on each other.
The terminological definition is the one used in usul.
2
Memorise the three types and their core functions
Learn bayan al-taqrir (confirmatory – reinforces literal meaning, cuts off metaphor/specification), bayan al-tafsir (interpretive – explains ambiguous or equivocal terms), and bayan al-taghyeer (altering – changes meaning through condition or exception).
The text mentions five types but only details three; focus on these.
3
Analyse the Quranic examples for each type
For taqrir: 'And no bird flies with its two wings' (6:38) – 'flies' could be metaphorical but the verse confirms literal flight. For tafsir: 'And divorced women shall wait for three quru'' (2:228) – 'quru'' is equivocal, the Prophet clarified it means menstruation. For taghyeer: 'You are divorced if you enter the house' – condition changes the ruling.
Notice how each type resolves a different kind of ambiguity.
4
Note the ruling on connected vs. disconnected bayan
Each type is valid whether the bayan is expressed in the same utterance (connected) or in a separate statement (disconnected). This affects how we interpret later clarifications.
Disconnected bayan is common in Sunnah explaining Quran.
5
Reflect on why bayan is needed
Bayan is required because texts may be general (amm), specific (khass), ambiguous (mujmal), or equivocal (mushtarak). Without bayan, the intended ruling may be misunderstood.
This connects to earlier discussions on amm, khass, etc.

Commentary
This passage is from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh text defining the concept of 'bayan' (clarification). The author first gives a linguistic and terminological definition of bayan, then states that it has five types, though only three are detailed here. The first type, bayan al-taqrir (confirmatory clarification), reinforces a statement by eliminating the possibility of metaphorical or specific interpretation, as in the Quranic verse about birds flying with their wings—where 'flying' could be metaphorical but is clarified as literal. The second type, bayan al-tafsir (interpretive clarification), explains an ambiguous or equivocal term, as when the Prophet explained the ambiguous 'quru'' as menstruation. The third type, bayan al-taghyeer (altering clarification), changes the meaning of a statement through conditions or exceptions. Each type has a ruling that it can be expressed either connectedly (within the same utterance) or disconnectedly (in a separate statement). The examples are drawn from Quran and hadith, consistent with Hanafi emphasis on textual proof.
