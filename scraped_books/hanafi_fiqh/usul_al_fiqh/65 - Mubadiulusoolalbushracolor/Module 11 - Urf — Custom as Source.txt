Five Contextual Indicators that Abandon the Literal Meaning
List of five contextual indicators

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the five indicators (qara'in) that cause the literal meaning to be abandoned, and apply them to a legal text to determine intended meaning.

Core Idea
Literal meaning is abandoned when custom, wording, context, speaker, or place indicate otherwise — five distinct contextual indicators.

In usul al-fiqh, words are presumed to carry their literal meaning, but that presumption can be overridden by contextual indicators. This lesson presents five such indicators: custom, wording itself, context of speech, speaker's intention, and place of speech. Each indicator is illustrated with classic Hanafi examples. Mastering these will sharpen your ability to interpret legal texts accurately.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the five indicators
List them in Arabic and English: دلالة العرف (custom), دلالة نفس الكلام (wording), دلالة سياق الكلام (context), دلالة من قبل المتكلم (speaker), دلالة محل الكلام (place).
Use the Arabic terms to anchor your recall.
2
Understand each example
For each indicator, explain how the literal meaning is abandoned. For custom: 'head' means livestock. For wording: 'every slave I own' excludes mukatab. For context: 'descend' grants safety unless conditional. For speaker: swift oath indicates immediacy. For place: marriage cannot be a sale.
Connect each example to its indicator type.
3
Distinguish indicators
Notice that each indicator has a different basis: custom relies on social usage, wording on lexical completeness, context on surrounding speech, speaker on internal intent, place on the subject matter's nature.
This helps avoid mixing them up.
4
Apply to new texts
Given a legal statement, determine which indicator might cause abandonment. For example, if a hadith says 'do not buy a head', custom applies. If a contract says 'I sell you this slave' but the slave is actually free, place indicates invalidity.
Practice with various cases to internalize the rule.

Statement of Particles of Meaning
6. What causes the literal meaning to be abandoned: five types:
1- Indicator of custom (dalalat al-'urf): If the figurative meaning is commonly known among people, the literal meaning is abandoned. For example, if someone swears he will not buy a 'head', it is interpreted as heads of cattle and sheep, not heads of sparrows and pigeons.
2- Indicator of the wording itself (dalalat nafs al-kalam): If someone says, 'Every slave I own is free,' the mukatab (slave under contract of manumission) is not freed because the word 'slave' (mamluk) refers to a completely owned slave.
3- Indicator of the context of speech (dalalat siyaq al-kalam): If a Muslim says to a harbi (non-Muslim from enemy territory), 'Descend,' and he descends, he is safe. But if he says, 'Descend if you are a man,' and he descends, he is not safe.
4- Indicator from the speaker (dalalat min qibal al-mutakallim): Like a swift oath (yamin al-fawr).
5- Indicator of the place of speech (dalalat mahal al-kalam): Where the place of speech does not accept the literal meaning, like marrying a free woman using the words of sale, gift, charity, or transfer of ownership.

Commentary
This passage enumerates five contextual indicators (qara'in) that cause the literal meaning to be abandoned in favor of figurative meaning. Each type is illustrated with a fiqh example: custom (head meaning livestock), wording (complete ownership), context (safe conduct), speaker's intention (swift oath), and place (marriage of free woman cannot be literal sale). These indicators are crucial for understanding legal texts and assessing intent.

Key Terms
دلالة العرف (dalalat al-urf): The indicator of common custom which makes the figurative meaning prevail over the literal.
دلالة نفس الكلام (dalalat nafs al-kalam): The indicator from the wording itself that restricts the general literal meaning.
دلالة سياق الكلام (dalalat siyaq al-kalam): The indicator from the context of speech that determines the intended meaning.
دلالة من قبل المتكلم (dalalat min qibal al-mutakallim): The indicator from the speaker's intention or state, such as a swift oath.
دلالة محل الكلام (dalalat mahal al-kalam): The indicator from the place of speech that does not accept the literal meaning.

The Principle of Contextual Specificity in Figurative Speech
Explains when intention is not needed for figurative speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when the locus (mahl) is specific to a type of figurative usage, intention (niyyah) is not required to determine the figurative meaning.

Core Idea
When context specifies the figurative type, no intention is needed.

In Hanafi usul, figurative speech (majaz) normally requires the speaker's intention. However, this lesson presents an important exception: when the context or locus (mahl) itself points exclusively to a certain figurative meaning, the need for intention drops away. This principle simplifies interpretation of many legal texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Recall that determining whether a word is used figuratively typically requires the speaker's intention (niyyah) or contextual clues.
This is standard in Hanafi balaghah.
2
Recognize the exception
The text states: when the mahl (locus/subject) is specific to a type of majaz, intention is not needed. The mahl itself determines the figurative usage.
3
Apply to the swift oath example
When someone says to a woman about to leave 'If you go out, you are divorced,' the context (immediate departure) and custom ('urf) make it clear that the condition applies instantly. No need to ask for intention.
The particle 'in' (if) is used conditionally, but context specifies the time.
4
Apply to the marriage contract example
When a marriage contract uses words of ownership (like 'I give you my daughter'), the free woman cannot be owned literally, so the context (marriage) forces a figurative reading of transfer of rights. Intention is not needed.
5
Differentiate ambiguous cases
If the mahl is not specific to a single figurative type, intention or additional context is required.
This is the default rule.

Benefit: Every place where the locus (mahl) is specific to a type of figurative speech, there is no need for intention (niyyah).
Statement of Particles of Meaning
And connected to literal and figurative meanings is a statement of particles that have meanings, among them particles of conjunction (huruf al-'ataf), like the swift oath (yamin al-fawr): every oath where contextual evidence indicates that the immediate present is intended rather than the future. For example, if someone says to a woman about to leave, 'If you go out, you are divorced,' the condition is immediately binding because his intent is to prevent that action customarily.
As for the case of marrying a free woman etc.: A free woman does not accept being owned in any way, so the literal meaning is abandoned and from those words the transfer of her pudendum is intended, which can only be through a marriage contract.
And connected to literal and figurative meanings: For example, the particle 'fi' (in) if it is used for containment (zarfiyyah) is literal, and if it is used in the sense of 'ala (upon) it is figurative.

Commentary
This passage provides additional notes: first, when the context makes figurative meaning obvious, intention is not needed. Then it discusses particles like conjunctions, giving the example of a swift oath. Finally, it notes that particles themselves can have literal and figurative meanings, as in the case of 'fi'. This helps in interpreting legal texts where particles can shift meaning.

Key Terms
محل (mahl): The locus or subject of speech which may determine figurative usage.
نية (niyyah): Intention of the speaker to use a word figuratively.
حروف المعاني (huruf al-ma'ani): Particles that carry meaning, such as conjunctions and prepositions.
ظرفية (zarfiyyah): The literal meaning of 'fi' as a container/preposition of place.

Literal and Figurative Meanings of Particles (Huruf al-Ma'ani)
Explains literal and figurative uses of particles like 'fi'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the literal and figurative uses of particles like 'fi' and apply this categorization to interpret legal texts.

Core Idea
Particles like 'fi' can be literal or figurative depending on context.

This lesson expands on the haqiqah/majaz framework by applying it to particles (huruf al-ma'ani). Just as nouns and verbs can be literal or figurative, particles too carry different meanings. The example of 'fi' (in) shows that when used for containment it is literal, but when implying 'upon' it becomes figurative. Recognizing this helps in precise interpretation of legal documents such as oaths and contracts.

Guided Path
1
Recall the haqiqah/majaz distinction
Literal (haqiqah) is the original meaning; figurative (majaz) is a transferred meaning requiring a clue (qarinah).
Applicable to all word types.
2
Focus on particles
Particles like 'fi', 'min', 'ila' have core literal meanings but can be used figuratively.
Common in legal language.
3
Analyze the example of 'fi'
'Fi' literally indicates containment (zarfiyyah). When used in the sense of 'ala (upon), it is figurative because the spatial relationship changes.
E.g., 'fi al-masjid' = literal; 'fi al-dabbah' = 'upon the animal' (figurative).
4
Apply to legal texts
In oaths or wills, the meaning of 'fi' can alter obligations. Identify whether the context demands literal or figurative reading.
Example: 'I swear I will not speak fi al-safar' may mean 'during travel' (literal containment) or 'about travel' (figurative).
