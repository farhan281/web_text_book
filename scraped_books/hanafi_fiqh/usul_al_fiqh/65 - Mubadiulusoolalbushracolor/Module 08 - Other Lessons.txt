The Prohibition Implies Ugliness: Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic
Definition and classification of prohibition's ugliness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify prohibited acts as qabīḥ li-ʿaynihi (intrinsically ugly) or qabīḥ li-ghayrihi (extrinsically ugly), and identify the subcategories based on the source of ugliness (innate rational judgment, revelation, inseparable attribute, or incidental circumstance).

Core Idea
Prohibition signals ugliness; some acts are ugly in themselves, others become ugly by circumstance.

This passage establishes the foundational principle that a prohibition from a wise legislator necessarily implies the ugliness (qubh) of the prohibited act. It then divides ugliness into two main types: intrinsic (qabīḥ li-ʿaynihi) and extrinsic (qabīḥ li-ghayrihi). Each type is further subdivided based on whether the ugliness arises from innate rational judgment (wadʿan) or revelation (sharʿan) for the intrinsic, and whether it is due to an inseparable attribute (waṣfan) or an incidental circumstance (mujāwiran) for the extrinsic. Understanding this classification is essential for correctly determining the legal effect of prohibitions.

Guided Path
1
State the basic principle
Begin by noting that the passage asserts that a prohibition from a wise prohibitor implies the quality of ugliness in the prohibited act. This is the starting point.
2
Identify the two main categories
The passage then says 'either it is ugly in itself... or ugly due to something else.' Identify these as qabīḥ li-ʿaynihi and qabīḥ li-ghayrihi.
3
Analyze intrinsic ugliness (qabīḥ li-ʿaynihi)
Intrinsic ugliness is further divided: wadʿan (by innate disposition) – acts that reason declares ugly even without revelation, like disbelief (kufr); and sharʿan (by revelation) – acts that are not rationally ugly but become so due to a legal definition, like selling a free person (bayʿ al-ḥurr).
Note: The passage gives kufr as example of wadʿan and bayʿ al-ḥurr as sharʿan.
4
Analyze extrinsic ugliness (qabīḥ li-ghayrihi)
Extrinsic ugliness is divided into waṣfan (due to an inseparable attribute) – e.g., fasting on the day of sacrifice (ṣawm yawm al-naḥr), where the act itself is good but becomes ugly because it conflicts with the command to celebrate; and mujāwiran (due to an incidental circumstance) – e.g., selling at the time of the Friday call to prayer (bayʿ waqt al-nidāʾ), where the sale is lawful but becomes prohibited due to distraction from obligation.
Note: The passage explains that the ugliness of fasting on the day of sacrifice arises from an attribute inseparable from the act itself.
5
Apply the classification to new examples
Given a new prohibited act, determine whether its ugliness is intrinsic or extrinsic, and then identify the specific subcategory. For example, drinking wine is intrinsically ugly by revelation (sharʿan) because it is prohibited in the Quran and its harm is apparent. Usury (ribā) is also intrinsically ugly by revelation.
This step trains the student to use the classification tool.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional analysis from a Hanafi usul work. It opens by stating that a prohibition from a wise commander necessarily implies that the prohibited act has the quality of ugliness (qubh). It then divides ugliness into two types: (1) intrinsic ugliness (qabih li 'aynihi), which can be either innate (wad'an) – such as disbelief, which reason deems ugly even without revelation – or by revelation (shar'an), such as selling a free person, which is not rationally ugly but becomes so due to a legal definition. The commentary then illustrates that obligations like prayer can fall due to performance or certain legal impediments (insanity, menses, postnatal bleeding) but not due to constriction of time or lack of means. It also explains that commendable acts (husn) like striving for Friday prayer and performing ablution are only required when their objectives (Friday prayer, prayer) are obligatory. This passage is a typical Hanafi usul exposition focusing on the conceptual framework of prohibition and the nature of ugliness in legal acts.

The Waiving of Obligations and the Conditionality of Means
Analysis of how obligations are waived and the conditionality of means on ends

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how obligations can be waived (by performance or by legal impediments) and apply the principle that commendable acts (like striving for Friday prayer) are only required when their objectives are obligatory.

Core Idea
The obligation of a means depends on the obligation of its end; when the end falls, the means falls.

This passage illustrates the mechanism by which obligations can be removed: either by performance (ada') or by the occurrence of legal impediments (a'warid) such as insanity, menstruation, or postnatal bleeding. It then links this to the concept that commendable acts (husn) like striving for Friday prayer (sa'y) and performing ablution (wudu) are only obligatory if their ultimate objectives (Friday prayer, prayer) are obligatory. This reflects the broader usul principle that the means takes the ruling of the end.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ways an obligation can be waived
The passage mentions two ways: performance (ada') and legal impediments (like insanity, menses, etc.). Note that constriction of time or lack of resources (water, clothing) do not waive the obligation.
2
Understand the conditionality of commendable acts
The passage says that striving for Friday prayer is good (hasan) because it leads to performing Friday prayer, but it is not obligatory for those who do not have to perform Friday prayer. Similarly, ablution is good because it is a key to prayer, but it is not obligatory for those who do not have to pray.
3
Derive the general principle
From these examples, derive the principle that the obligation of a means (wasila) is conditional on the obligation of its end (ghaya). If the end is not obligatory, the means is not obligatory, even if it is commendable.
4
Apply the principle to new scenarios
Give examples like: covering the 'awrah for prayer is obligatory only for those who must pray; if a person is excused from prayer (due to menses), covering is not obligatory. But note: some means may be obligatory even if the end is not, if they are independently required.

Two Types of Prohibition: Physical Acts vs. Legal Acts
Division of prohibitions into physical and legal acts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between prohibition of physical acts (al-afʿāl al-ḥissiyya) and prohibition of legal acts (al-afʿāl al-sharʿiyya), state the ruling for each, and apply the principle that prohibition does not negate legal effect.

Core Idea
Physical acts are prohibited in themselves; legal acts are prohibited due to external circumstances, yet prohibition does not void legal consequences.

In usul al-fiqh, not all prohibitions are the same. The Hanafi tradition distinguishes between acts that are evil by their very nature and acts that become prohibited due to context. This lesson unpacks that distinction using clear examples and introduces a key principle: a prohibited act can still produce valid legal effects. Understanding this helps avoid simplistic rulings and sharpens your ability to analyze legal norms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of prohibition
The passage states that prohibitions fall on two types of acts: physical acts (al-afʿāl al-ḥissiyya) and legal acts (al-afʿāl al-sharʿiyya). List the examples given for each.
Physical acts: adultery, wine, lying, injustice. Legal acts: fasting on the day of sacrifice, prayer at disliked times.
2
Understand the ruling for each type
For physical acts, the prohibition targets the act itself; it is always ḥarām with no possibility of being good. For legal acts, the act is good in itself (ḥasan li-nafsihi) but becomes prohibited due to an external factor (qabīḥ li-ghayrihi). Explain why.
The sin is due to the circumstance, not the act's essence.
3
Analyze the fā'ida (benefit): prohibition does not negate legal effect
The passage gives the divorce of a menstruating woman (ṭalāq al-ḥāʼiḍ) as an example. Even though it is prohibited, the divorce is valid and effects the separation. This shows that ḥurma (prohibition) does not conflict with tartīb al-ḥukm (attachment of a legal ruling).
Similarly, selling at the time of the Friday call is prohibited but the sale may be valid in some views.
4
Recognize the transformation of meaning in legal acts
Legal acts (like fasting and prayer) had original meanings (abstention, supplication) that were expanded by revelation. Their essences changed, so prohibition affects only the additional circumstances, not the core act.
This contrasts with physical acts whose meanings remain unchanged.

Commentary
This passage continues the usul al-fiqh discussion on prohibition, now dividing it into two categories based on the nature of the act prohibited. The first type is prohibition of physical acts (al-afʿāl al-ḥissiyya), such as adultery, drinking wine, lying, and injustice. For these, the prohibited thing is exactly the act itself; the act has no intrinsic goodness and is never permissible. The second type is prohibition of legal acts (al-afʿāl al-sharʿiyya), such as fasting on the day of sacrifice and praying at disliked times. For these, the act itself (e.g., fasting) is good, but it becomes prohibited due to an external factor (the day). The doer commits a sin because of the external factor, not because the act is evil in itself. The passage then provides a 'fā'ida' (benefit): the prohibition of an act does not prevent that act from being the subject of a legal ruling, e.g., the divorce of a menstruating woman is valid (so it effects divorce) even though it is prohibited. The text further explains the distinction by noting that physical acts retain their original meanings and essences after revelation (e.g., killing is still killing), whereas legal acts undergo transformation in meaning: fasting is not just abstention but includes shariʿ conditions; prayer is not just supplication but a structured act. This classification is important in Hanafi usul for understanding how prohibition interacts with the essential nature of acts.

Key Terms
الأفعال الحسية (al-afʿāl al-ḥissiyya): Physical acts whose original meanings and essences are unchanged by revelation; their prohibition is categorical.
الأفعال الشرعية (al-afʿāl al-sharʿiyya): Legal acts whose meanings are transformed by revelation; they are good in themselves but may become prohibited due to external circumstances.

Types of Haqiqah and Priority between Haqiqah and Majaz
Classifies haqiqah into three types and discusses priority between literal and figurative meanings, with intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify haqiqah into three types and apply the Hanafi rules for preferring haqiqah over majaz, including the ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the two companions.

Core Idea
When the literal meaning is possible and customary, Abu Hanifa still prefers it over the figurative; the two companions prefer the general figurative if more common.

This passage from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh text introduces the three categories of literal meaning (haqiqah) and the circumstances under which we resort to figurative meaning (majaz). It then presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute: whether the literal or figurative is preferred when both are in common usage. This classification is essential for interpreting legal texts, especially in oaths and contracts.

Guided Path
1

This passage from a Hanafi usul text discusses the types of haqiqah (literal meaning) and the rules for preferring between haqiqah and majaz (figurative meaning). Three Rules of Majaz in Hanafi Usul
Explaining three rules of majaz and a dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three rules of majaz in Hanafi usul, explain the dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on imkan al-haqiqah, and apply the rules to examples.

Core Idea
Majaz is a substitute for haqiqah, but its validity depends on the nature of substitution and the possibility of literal meaning.

This passage from Mubadi Ul Usool presents three fundamental rules regarding majaz (figurative speech) according to the Hanafi school. You will learn how majaz functions as a substitute for haqiqah, the limitation that both meanings cannot be simultaneously intended, and the requirement of suitability (munasaba). Additionally, you will encounter an important intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn about whether majaz is valid when the literal meaning is impossible.

Guided Path
1
Define haqiqah and majaz
Identify the literal (haqiqah) and figurative (majaz) meanings of words. Understand that majaz is a secondary usage.
2
Identify the first rule: majaz as substitute
Abu Hanifa holds majaz substitutes in utterance; Sahibayn hold it substitutes in ruling. This affects when majaz can be used.
Note the difference in the concept of 'substitute' (khalaf).
3
Examine the dispute on imkan al-haqiqah
Determine when majaz is allowed. Abu Hanifa permits majaz even if the literal meaning is impossible; Sahibayn require possibility of literal meaning.
The example of the master calling his older slave 'my son' illustrates the practical consequence.
4

A single word cannot carry both literal and figurative meanings in one context. Quran 4:43 is cited as evidence.
If the figurative meaning is intended, the literal meaning is automatically excluded.
5

For majaz to be valid, there must be a suitability or connection between the literal and figurative meanings, as in 'lion' for a brave man.
This prevents arbitrary figurative usage.

The Particle Fa in Hanafi Usul: Ta'qib and 'Illa
Defines the particle fa and its uses for 'illa and ta'qib

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two primary functions of the particle fa in legal texts and apply the Hanafi principle to determine the ruling based on whether the cause is continuous.

Core Idea
Fa indicates immediate sequentiality (ta'qib) or causality ('illa) if the cause is continuous.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the particle fa carries specific legal weight. It is not merely a conjunction; it can signal that the preceding action is the cause for the subsequent ruling, provided the cause persists. Mastering this particle helps you interpret contractual and conditional statements correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the apparent function
Look at the sentence containing fa. Does it appear to indicate sequence or cause? Note that fa usually indicates ta'qib, but context may modify.
The default meaning is immediate sequentiality; causality requires a continuous cause.
2
Check for continuity of the cause
If fa is used to indicate causality, verify that the cause ('illa) is continuous, i.e., it remains present after the ruling. The text gives the example of a master saying 'Pay one thousand and you are free' – the payment cause continues as a debt.
Continuous means the cause exists both before and after the ruling.
3
Distinguish literal vs figurative use
If fa appears to mean 'and' (waw), determine if it is used figuratively. The text states that fa sometimes means waw figuratively, as in 'a dirham and a dirham' implying two dirhams.
This figurative use is an exception; fa usually retains its sequential/causal force.
4
Apply the Hanafi principle
Based on the function determined, derive the ruling. For ta'qib, the second event occurs immediately after the first. For 'illa, the ruling is effective immediately but the cause remains binding.
In the example, the slave is free immediately, but the debt remains.

Al-'Aks: The Reversal Objection in Hanafi Jadal

Core Idea
Al-'aks exposes inconsistency by extending the opponent's analogy to a case they cannot accept.

In Hanafi jadal (dialectical reasoning), scholars often refute an opponent's qiyas by showing that the same reasoning leads to an absurd or unacceptable conclusion when applied to a similar case. This technique is called al-'aks (reversal). In this lesson, you will learn to recognize the 'aks structure and see it in action through a classical example involving zakat on jewelry.

Extract the illa (effective cause)
Determine the illa that the opponent is implicitly using. Here, the illa is something like 'item of personal adornment for women' or 'non-invested property' that is exempt.
Hanafis might argue the illa is broader.
3
Apply the same illa to a parallel case
The Hanafi response applies the same reasoning to men's jewelry: if the illa exempts women's jewelry, it should also exempt men's jewelry, because both are similar in being personal adornment.

The opponent (Shafi'i) does not hold that men's jewelry is exempt; thus, the analogy is inconsistent. This is the moment of 'aks: the ruling is reversed on the opponent by their own method.
The 'aks shows that the original qiyas fails because it leads to an unacceptable consequence (luzum ma la yaltazim).

Fasad al-Wad': The Logical Obstacle of an Inherently Flawed Cause
Defines fasad al-wad' with example from marriage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when an 'illa is corrupted in basis (fasad al-wad') and distinguish it from other qiyas objections.

Core Idea
An 'illa that contradicts the purpose of the ruling is inherently unfit — fasad al-wad'.

This lesson introduces the fifth obstacle preventing valid qiyas: fasad al-wad' (corruption of the basis). Unlike other objections that challenge the proof or presence of the 'illa, fasad al-wad' attacks the very suitability of the proposed cause. Through the example of marriage and the effect of Islam, we will see how Hanafi usul rejects an analogy when the 'illa's nature is fundamentally opposite to the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of fasad al-wad'
Recognize that fasad al-wad' means the 'illa (cause) is intrinsically unsuitable for the ruling. It is not about missing conditions but about a logical mismatch.
This is a core objection in Hanafi usul against qiyas.
2
Analyze the example: Islam and marriage
Read the passage: Shafi'i argues that Islam of one spouse invalidates marriage, analogizing to apostasy. Identify the claimed 'illa and ruling.
The 'illa is the change of religion; the ruling is invalidation of marriage.
3
Trace the Hanafi response
The Hanafis reply that Islam is a preserver of rights, not a remover. Thus the 'illa cannot produce the ruling. This is fasad al-wad' — the 'illa is inherently contradictory to the purpose.
The ruling aims to protect marriage, not destroy it.
4
Contrast with other qiyas objections
Compare fasad al-wad' with objections like qat' al-qiyas (break in analogy) or farq (difference).
Fasad al-wad' is unique because it targets the core suitability of the cause.
5
Apply to new scenarios
Given a proposed 'illa, test if it aligns with the purpose of the ruling. If it undermines the purpose, it is fasad al-wad'.
Practice with examples from other chapters.

The Obstacle of Farq (Distinction) in Qiyas
Explains farq as showing a key difference between the two cases in qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a farq (legally relevant distinction) that prevents qiyas and apply the Hanafi criteria for evaluating such distinctions.

Core Idea
A mere difference between two cases does not block qiyas; only a difference in the effective cause (illa) matters.

In this lesson, we examine the sixth obstacle to qiyas: farq, or distinction. The Shafi'i school argues that zakat is obligatory on a minor's wealth by analogy to an adult's wealth, because both enrich the poor. The Hanafi school rejects this analogy by identifying a key difference: the primary 'illa for zakat on adults is purification from sins, which does not apply to minors. This distinction (farq) invalidates the qiyas. Understanding when a difference is legally relevant is essential for sound istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claimed Analogy
Read the Shafi'i argument: zakat on a minor's property is analogous to zakat on an adult's property because both aim to enrich the poor. State the original (asl), the branch (far'), and the claimed 'illa (enriching the poor).
The asl is an adult with wealth; the far' is a minor with wealth.
2
Locate the Hanafi Rebuttal (Farq)
The Hanafi response points out a difference: the 'illa for zakat on adults is tathir al-dhunub (purification from sins), not merely enriching the poor. Identify this distinction in the text.
The key phrase is 'تطهير الذنوب'.
3
Verify Relevance of the Distinction
Determine whether the distinction is legally relevant. For Hanafis, tathir is a necessary element of the 'illa for adults, and it is absent in minors. Thus, the qiyas fails because the 'illa is not shared.
If the distinction does not affect the 'illa, qiyas would still be valid.
4
State the Legal Consequence
Because the qiyas is invalid, the Hanafi ruling is that zakat is not wajib on a minor's property. Contrast with the Shafi'i position that it is wajib.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical result of the disagreement).
5
Generalize the Principle
Farq is a valid obstacle only when it identifies a difference in the 'illa. Not every difference suffices. Formulate the principle: 'Al-farq al-mu'tabar huwa al-farq fi al-illa.'
This principle is used across all chapters of fiqh where qiyas is attempted.

Thus the cases are distinct and the analogy is invalid.

Key Terms
الفرق (al-farq): Showing a legally relevant difference between the original and the branch that prevents qiyas
تطهير الذنوب (tathir al-dhunub): Purification from sins, the primary wisdom behind zakat for adults

Sixth and Seventh Obstacles: Naqd and Mu'arada
Defines naqd and mu'arada as objections to the 'illa with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define naqd and mu'arada, identify their function as objections to the 'illa, and apply them to evaluate analogies in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Naqd exposes inconsistency in a claimed 'illa; mu'arada counters with a stronger analogy.

In Hanafi jadal, when an opponent proposes an 'illa (effective cause) to support a ruling, the Hanafi can object by showing that the 'illa does not hold consistently (naqd) or by presenting a counter-analogy that yields a different ruling (mu'arada). This lesson explains these two powerful tools using classic examples from the wudu disputes between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two objections
Read the passage and note the definitions of naqd (inconsistency) and mu'arada (opposition).
Both are rebuttals to a claimed 'illa.
2
Analyze the naqd example
The Shafi'i claims that wudu requires intention because it is purification like tayammum. The Hanafi counters: if purification required intention, then washing clothes would also require it. This shows the 'illa is inconsistent.
The Hanafi uses a counterexample where the 'illa fails.
3
Analyze the mu'arada example
The Shafi'i argues that wiping (masah) in wudu should be done three times because it is a pillar like ghusl. The Hanafi counters: other pillars like wiping the khuff and tayammum are not done thrice, so the analogy fails.
The Hanafi provides a conflicting analogy that leads to a different ruling.
4
Understand the function in jadal
Naqd and mu'arada are used to demonstrate that the opponent's 'illa is not valid. They are part of the Hanafi methodology of rebuttal.
They do not necessarily prove the Hanafi position, but they destroy the opponent's proof.
5
Apply to new case
Given a new analogy, practice identifying how a Hanafi might use naqd or mu'arada to object.
This builds skill in usul argumentation.

The final two obstacles: naqd and mu'arada. Naqd shows that the claimed 'illa does not always produce the ruling. The Shafi'i argues that wudu is purification (tahara) and thus requires niyya, like tayammum. النقض (al-naqd): Showing that the claimed 'illa does not produce the ruling in all relevant cases (inconsistency)
