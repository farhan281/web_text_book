Defining Al-Muawwal: The Interpreted Text in Hanafi Usul
Definition and ruling of al-muawwal with example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-muawwal, distinguish it from mufassar, and state its ruling along with its epistemological weight.

Core Idea
Muawwal is an obligatory ruling based on preponderant opinion, but never absolute certainty.

After studying clear, definitive texts (mufassar), we now encounter texts that require interpretation. This lesson defines al-muawwal—a word with multiple possible meanings whose intended sense is determined by preponderant evidence. You will learn why the Hanafi school acts upon such interpretations as binding, yet acknowledges the possibility of error.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its need for interpretation
Muawwal applies to a word that is homonymous (mushtarak), having multiple possible meanings. Because the Lawgiver did not specify which meaning is intended, a mujtahid must use evidence to prefer one meaning.
This contrasts with mufassar, where the Lawgiver clarifies the intended meaning directly.
2
Recognize the role of preponderant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y)
The interpreter uses ghalib al-ra'y—a level of certainty based on evidence that outweighs other possibilities. This is not mere speculation; it is a reasoned preference.
The Hanafi school uses this method for many legal terms.
3
Apply the example: qur'
The word 'qur' (قرء) in the Quran (e.g., 2:228) can mean menstruation or purity. The Hanafis prefer the meaning of menstruation based on preponderant evidence (e.g., the hadith and linguistic usage).
This is a classic example of muawwal.
4
State the ruling and its epistemological weight
Acting upon a muawwal text is wajib (obligatory), but the ruling carries a possibility of error because it is based on human reasoning (ijtihad).
This distinguishes it from qat'i rulings of mufassar.

Third Division: Classification by Clarity and Obscurity
Introduces the four-level clarity hierarchy of words (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four levels of clarity (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) in the Hanafi hierarchy and explain their relative epistemic strength.

Core Idea
A word's clarity determines its legal force: from open to interpretation (zahir) to fully explicit (muhkam).

The third division in usul al-fiqh classifies words by how clearly they convey meaning. This hierarchy is foundational for determining whether a text can be interpreted (ta'wil) or abrogated. The Hanafi school identifies four levels: zahir, nass, mufassar, and muhkam. This lesson introduces the first three as mentioned in the source.

Guided Path
1
Identify the classification basis
Words are divided according to clarity and obscurity of meaning. This is the third overarching division in usul.
The opposite would be obscure words (mutashabih, mujmal) which are covered separately.
2
List the four levels in ascending clarity
The Hanafi school recognizes four levels: zahir (apparent), nass (explicit), mufassar (detailed), and muhkam (firm). Memorize this order.
The source text only lists three; muhkam is understood from later discussions.
3
Define each level
Zahir: meaning is apparent but open to ta'wil. Nass: explicit and not open to ta'wil. Mufassar: explained and clarified by the text itself. Muhkam: most definitive, cannot be abrogated except by similar.
Each higher level has stronger dalalah (evidentiary force).
4
Understand epistemic weight
Zahir is zanni (probable) in dalalah; nass, mufassar, and muhkam are qat'i (definitive). This affects whether the text can be reinterpreted or overridden.
Hanafi: even zahir is binding until a valid ta'wil is established.
5
Apply the hierarchy
When encountering a text, first determine its level. If zahir, note that it may be qualified. If nass or above, treat as definitive unless abrogated.
Practice with Quranic verses and hadith to internalize the categories.

The Hanafi Classification of Words by Clarity and Obscurity
Defines haqiqah, majaz, sarih, kinayah, and classifies words by clarity/obscurity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define each of the eight categories of word meaning in Hanafi usul (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam, khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih) and apply the principle that zahir is definitive (qat'i) for establishing rulings despite theoretical possibility of contrary intention.

Core Idea
The apparent meaning (zahir) is binding and definitive in Hanafi usul, even though a different intention is theoretically possible—because that possibility lacks evidence.

This lesson introduces the foundational Hanafi classification of words based on how clearly their meaning is conveyed. You will learn the four degrees of clarity—zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam—and their four opposites of obscurity—khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih. Understanding this hierarchy is essential for proper istinbat (derivation) of rulings from the Quran and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Classify words by clarity
Understand the four levels: zahir (apparent from wording), nass (explicit intent), mufassar (no interpretation possible, abrogable), muhkam (no interpretation, non-abrogable).
These form a hierarchy from lowest to highest clarity.
2
Classify words by obscurity
The four opposite categories: khafi (hidden due to external factor), mushkil (hidden due to wording but solvable), mujmal (ambiguous, clarification needed), mutashabih (equivocal, no clarification possible).
These are the opposites of the clear categories.
3
Trace the logical basis
The classification depends on two criteria: does the meaning appear? If so, does it admit interpretation? If not, is it abrogable? For obscure words: is the hiddenness due to wording or external? Is resolution possible? This gives the eight categories.
Refer to the passage's rationale paragraph.
4
Apply the Hanafi rule on zahir
Even though zahir admits the theoretical possibility of a different intention (ihtimal), that possibility is not based on evidence, so it is disregarded. Hence, zahir is definitive (qat'i) and can be used to establish hadd punishments.
This is a key difference from the Shafi'i view.

Hanafi Hierarchy of Textual Clarity: From Zahir to Muhkam
Defines Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity and their rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four levels of textual clarity in Hanafi usul – zahir, nass, mufassar, and muhkam – and state the ruling for each.

Core Idea
A text's clarity determines its legal force: from probable (zahir) to immutable (muhkam).

This passage from a Hanafi usul text presents the foundational classification of texts by clarity of meaning. Understanding these categories is essential for determining how binding a textual ruling is and whether it can be interpreted, specified, or abrogated. The author lays out a systematic hierarchy that governs how jurists derive rulings from the Qur'an and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main families: clear and unclear
The passage divides texts into those whose meaning is apparent (clear) and those whose meaning is hidden (unclear). Each family has four subcategories: clear: zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam; unclear: khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih.
Remember that each unclear category is the opposite of a clear category.
2
Learn the four clear categories in ascending clarity
Start from zahir (meaning apparent on mere hearing, but open to ta'wil/takhsis) to nass (more explicit, intended for a specific ruling), then mufassar (clarified by the speaker, no possibility of ta'wil/takhsis, but can be abrogated), and finally muhkam (absolutely clear and immutable).
The examples given in the passage illustrate each category.
3
Understand the ruling attached to each category
For zahir and nass, the ruling is 'wujub al-amal' (obligation to act) on the apparent meaning, while acknowledging the theoretical possibility of ta'wil. For mufassar, the ruling is definitive without ta'wil, but abrogation is possible. For muhkam, it is necessary to act upon and believe the text without any possibility of change.
Despite the possibility of ta'wil, Hanafis treat zahir as qat'i (definitive) for legal application.
4
Map the four unclear categories to their opposites
The passage explicitly pairs each clear category with its opposite: khafi (hidden due to external cause) opposite zahir, mushkil (hidden due to wording but resolvable) opposite nass, mujmal (ambiguous, needs explanation) opposite mufassar, and mutashabih (unclear and irresolvable) opposite muhkam. Note the cause of hiddenness.
Understanding the opposites helps in classification.
5
Apply the classification to a given text
Given a Quranic verse or hadith, determine its category by assessing its clarity, the possibility of ta'wil/takhsis, and susceptibility to abrogation. For example, 'Allah has permitted trade' is zahir; 'So the angels prostrated, all of them, together' is mufassar.
Practice with the examples in the passage.

Al-Mufassar: The Level of Absolute Clarity in the Hanafi Text Hierarchy

Core Idea
Mufassar is a text so clarified by the speaker that no interpretation or specification remains — its meaning is certain until abrogated.

In the Hanafi usul tradition, texts are classified by clarity. The mufassar is the highest level short of the muhkam, where the speaker himself removes all ambiguity. In this lesson, you will parse the definition, understand its proof from the Quran, and see how it differs from nass and zahir.

After the revelation era, no new abrogation occurs.

Muhkam is even higher, but not discussed here.

Definition of Khafi and Its Ruling
Defines khafi with example of thief and pickpocket.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a text as khafi based on the definition, distinguish the external factor from wording-based ambiguity, and apply the Hanafi ruling of wajib al-talab to resolve its hiddenness through ijtihad and qiyas.

Core Idea
Khafi hides its intended scope due to an external factor; the jurist must seek clarity by analogizing cases of increase or decrease.

In this passage, we encounter the category of khafi (the hidden text). Unlike mujmal where the wording itself is ambiguous, khafi has a clear wording but its application to certain cases is unclear due to external factors like the introduction of new sub-cases. The ruling obligates the jurist to investigate (طلب) until the hiddenness is resolved. Understanding khafi is crucial for correctly applying qiyas within the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of khafi
Locate the definition: 'ما خفي مراده بعارض غير الصيغة'. Note that the hiddenness is due to an external factor ('عارض'), not the wording ('الصيغة').
The term 'عارض' means a temporary or incidental factor.
2
Analyze the example: the thief verse
The verse (5:38) is cited. The word 'السارق' is clear for the common thief but hidden for the pickpocket (الطار) and grave robber (النباش). Identify the external factor: the pickpocket adds an element (cutting pockets), while the grave robber lacks an element (stealing from a living person's possession in secret).
This external variation makes the text khafi, not the wording itself.
3
Understand the ruling: وجوب الطلب
The hukm is that it is obligatory to seek clarity until the hiddenness is removed. This is done through ijtihad and qiyas.
The jurist is not permitted to abandon the text due to hiddenness.
4

If the external factor adds meaning (zayid), the new case is attached to the original ruling (qiyas jali). If it subtracts (naqis), it is not attached. The pickpocket is attached (hand-cutting applies), the grave robber is not.
This distinction is the practical outcome of resolving khafi.

The hukm of a mushkil text is that its intended meaning cannot be attained 'illa bi al-talab, thumma al-ta'ammul'—only through seeking (research) and then deep reflection. This is an active intellectual process.
Talab includes consulting linguistic sources, custom, and context. Ta'ammul involves weighing possible meanings.
4
Work through the example
Consider the oath: 'I will not eat idam.' The word 'idam' is mushkil: it is commonly used for vinegar and date syrup (clear), but is ambiguous regarding meat, eggs, and cheese. The jurist must research whether these items are considered 'idam' in the speaker's custom.
The resolution depends on 'urf (custom) and linguistic usage.
5
Distinguish from other categories
Contrast mushkil with khafi: for khafi, the ambiguity is removed by seeking external clarification (e.g., the case of the thief). For mushkil, the word itself is ambiguous and requires linguistic analysis.
A Shafi'i reading may treat such texts differently, often classifying them as mujmal.

Zahir and Khafi: Clear and Hidden Texts
Definition of zahir and khafi with example and ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish a zahir text from a khafi text and apply the Hanafi method of analogy (qiyas) to determine whether a specific case is annexed to the general ruling.

Core Idea
Zahir is clear in wording; khafi is hidden in application, resolved by analogy.

In this lesson, we explore the first two levels of textual clarity: the clear (zahir) and the hidden (khafi). Using the Quranic verse on theft, the author shows how a general term like 'thief' is zahir for the typical thief but khafi when applied to subtypes like pickpockets and grave robbers. The Hanafi approach uses a specific criterion—increase or decrease in meaning—to decide whether the hidden case is included in the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the textual level
Read the passage and determine whether the text is classified as zahir or khafi. The verse 'al-sariq' is zahir for the common thief, but khafi for pickpocket and grave robber.
Zahir: clear from the wording alone. Khafi: requires external reasoning to apply.
2
Locate the source of hiddenness
For khafi texts, identify why the application is hidden. The pickpocket (tarrar) involves an increase in meaning (stealth and skill), while the grave robber (nabbash) involves a decrease (lack of secrecy).
Hiddenness arises from a specific characteristic not present in the general term.
3
Apply the Hanafi criterion
If the specific case has an additional meaning (ziyada) over the general term, it is annexed (mulhaq) to the ruling. If it has a deficiency (nuqsan), it is not annexed. Here, pickpocket is annexed; grave robber is not.
This is based on the principle: 'al-ziyada fi al-ma'na tujib al-ilhaq'.
4
Derive the ruling
Conclude: the pickpocket's hand is cut as per the verse; the grave robber does not receive the same punishment. This illustrates how khafi is resolved through ijtihad.
The ruling for khafi is: 'wujub al-talab hatta yazul 'anhu al-khafa'.

Mushkil, Mujmal, and Mutashabih: Deeper Ambiguity
Definitions of mushkil, mujmal, and mutashabih with examples and rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a text as mushkil, mujmal, or mutashabih and state the required method for resolving each: contemplation, seeking clarification from the Lawgiver, or stopping (tawaqquf).

Core Idea
Mushkil requires contemplation; mujmal needs the Lawgiver's clarification; mutashabih is known only to Allah.

This lesson moves deeper into textual ambiguity. The third level, mushkil, is hidden due to the wording itself, requiring reflection. The fourth, mujmal, is so ambiguous that only the Lawgiver's explanation can clarify it. The fifth, mutashabih, is beyond human understanding entirely—we affirm its truth but stop speculation. These categories refine the Hanafi method of textual interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Diagnose the source of ambiguity
For each text, determine if the hiddenness is due to the wording (mushkil), multiple interpretations (mujmal), or unknowable meaning (mutashabih). For example, 'idam' is mushkil; 'riba' is mujmal; 'alif lam mim' is mutashabih.
The source of ambiguity determines the method of resolution.
2
Determine the required method
Mushkil: seek and contemplate (talab and ta'ammul). Mujmal: need bayan from the speaker (the Lawgiver). Mutashabih: stop (tawaqquf) while affirming real meaning.
Each level has a distinct ruling.
3
Apply the method to an example
For the oath 'I will not eat idam', contemplate whether meat is included. For 'riba', the Prophet's hadith on six items clarifies the specific prohibited exchange. For 'yad Allah', we accept the literal meaning but do not seek further detail.
The examples illustrate how each category is handled.

The mujmal 'riba' is increase in an absolute sense, but that is not intended because every sale involves increase; rather, the intended is the specific increase which is the excess in exchange in the sale of homogeneous measured items. So the Prophet (peace be upon him) clarified it in the hadith of the six items.

Commentary
This passage from the third division of the text introduces the classification of legal texts according to their clarity. It begins with an example from the Qur'an (the verse on theft) to illustrate the difference between clear (zahir) and hidden (khafi) texts. The word 'al-sariq' (thief) is clear in its general meaning, but when applied to a pickpocket (al-tarrar) or grave robber (al-nabbash), its application becomes hidden because these are specific types. The ruling is that one must seek clarification to remove the hiddenness, and the method is analogy (qiyas): if the specific case has an additional meaning (like the pickpocket's stealth and skill), it is included; if it has a deficiency (like the grave robber's lack of secrecy in the grave), it is not included. 

Next, the definitions of al-mushkil and al-mujmal are given. Al-mushkil is one step more hidden than al-khafi, requiring both seeking and contemplation. The example of swearing not to eat 'idam' is used: 'idam' is clear for common condiments like vinegar and date syrup, but problematic for items like meat, eggs, and cheese because it is not immediately clear if they are included. Al-mujmal is even more hidden, requiring a clarification from the speaker (the Lawgiver). The example is 'riba' (usury/interest), which is linguistically ambiguous and needs prophetic clarification.

The commentary clarifies the terms and includes a Hanafi perspective on how to resolve hiddenness through qiyas. The authority weight of this text is from later Hanafi usul works, not necessarily from Zahir al-Riwayah, but it is based on established Hanafi principles.

Key Terms
مشكل (mushkil): A text whose meaning is hidden due to the wording itself, requiring contemplation (ta'ammul).
مجمل (mujmal): A text that is ambiguous and cannot be understood without a clarification from the speaker (e.g., riba).
متشابه (mutashabih): A text whose meaning cannot be known at all by human intellect, e.g., the disconnected letters at the start of some surahs.

Types of Nass: Ishara, Dalala, and Iqtida'
Defines three types of textual indication beyond literal wording

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between isharat al-nass, dalalat al-nass, and iqtida' al-nass, and apply the Hanafi rules regarding their authority and hierarchy.

Core Idea
Beyond literal wording, texts carry implied meanings: ishara, dalala, and iqtida' — each with distinct proofs and limits.

The Hanafi school recognizes that a text (nass) can convey rulings not only through its explicit wording (ibara) but also through implied indications. This lesson introduces three such types: isharat al-nass (textual pointer), dalalat al-nass (textual indication via linguistic cause), and iqtida' al-nass (textual requirement). Understanding these tools is essential for deriving rulings that are not directly stated but are nonetheless binding.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types
Name each type from the passage: isharat al-nass, dalalat al-nass, iqtida' al-nass.
These are categories of textual implication beyond explicit wording.
2
Distinguish ishara from ibara
Isharat al-nass is a secondary meaning not intended by the primary speech. Example: Qur'an 2:233 implies lineage to fathers via the lam of specification, though the primary ruling is financial responsibility.
In case of conflict, ibara takes precedence over ishara.
3
Understand dalala and its 'illa
Dalalat al-nass derives a ruling from the linguistic 'illa (cause) of the text, not from ijtihad. Example: Qur'an 17:23 forbids hitting parents because the 'illa of forbidding 'uff' is causing harm.
The 'illa is understood directly from language, distinguishing it from qiyas.
4
Recognize iqtida' as a necessary supplement
Iqtida' al-nass requires an implied supplement for the text to be meaningful. Example: 'You are divorced' implies actual divorce; 'raised from my community error and forgetfulness' refers to the ruling.
The muqtada (implied thing) is estimated by necessity and cannot exceed what is required.
5
Assess the hierarchy and ruling strength
Ishara's ruling is definitive (qat'i) but yields to ibara in conflict. Dalala's ruling is definitive and general due to the general 'illa. Iqtida' establishes the muqtada necessarily, limited to its need.
All three are binding proofs within Hanafi usul.

Definition of Isharat al-Nass

It takes precedence in case of conflict because of its specificity to the speech, like his statement (peace be upon him): 'She sits half of her lifetime not fasting and not praying.' 'Not by ijtihad': This is the difference between Dalalat al-Nass and Qiyas: that the 'illa in the former is linguistic, and in the latter is ijtihadi. Commentary
This passage defines three types of textual indication beyond the literal wording (ibara). Isharat al-Nass is an implied pointer from the wording, not the primary intent, e.g., Qur'an 2:233 indicates lineage to fathers via the lam of specification. Its ruling is definitive but yields to ibara when conflicts arise. Dalalat al-Nass is derived from the linguistic 'illa of the text, not personal reasoning, e.g., Qur'an 17:23 forbids hitting and reviling because the 'illa of forbidding 'uff' is causing harm. This 'illa is linguistic, making it distinct from qiyas (which uses ijtihadi 'illa). Its ruling is definitive and applies generally due to the general 'illa. Iqtida' al-Nass requires a supplementary implied element for the text's meaning to hold, e.g., 'You are divorced' implies actual divorce; 'My ummah is relieved' refers to the ruling. The implied element is necessary and limited to its need, so intending triple divorce with one utterance is invalid in such implied contexts.

Key Terms
إشارة النص (ishārat al-naṣṣ): An implied meaning from the text that is not the primary purpose of the speech.
دلالة النص (dalālat al-naṣṣ): A ruling derived from the linguistic 'illa (cause) of the text, not ijtihad.
اقتضاء النص (iqtiḍāʾ al-naṣṣ): An implied supplement necessary for the text's meaning to be valid.
