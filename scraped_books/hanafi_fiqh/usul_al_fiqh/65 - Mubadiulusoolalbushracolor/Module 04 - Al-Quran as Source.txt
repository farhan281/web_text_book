Definition of the Quran and Four Word Types
Defines the Quran and classifies words into four types by designation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the Quran as a source of law and classify words into khass, aam, mushtarak, and mu'awwal based on their designation.

Core Idea
Words in the Quran are classified by designation: particular, general, shared, or interpreted.

This lesson introduces the foundational division of words in the Quran and Sunnah according to linguistic designation (wad'). The usul scholars have derived twenty sections from four main divisions. We focus on the first division, which categorizes words into four types: khass, aam, mushtarak, and mu'awwal. Understanding these categories is essential for correctly deriving rulings from sacred texts.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the Authority of the Quran
The Quran is defined as the mutawatir revelation transmitted from the Prophet, written in mushafs. This establishes its definitive textual authority.
Tawatur ensures certainty in transmission.
2
Understand the First Division
Words are divided based on their placement for meaning. The four categories are khass (particular), aam (general), mushtarak (shared), and mu'awwal (interpreted).
This division is by wad' (linguistic designation).
3
Define Khass
A word placed for a single known meaning individually, cutting off sharing. It does not matter if the usage is literal or figurative, clear or obscure.
Infrad means cutting off sharing.
4
Distinguish the Categories
If a word indicates one meaning and is exclusive to that meaning, it is khass. If it indicates a meaning shared among individuals, it is aam. If it indicates multiple meanings, and one is preponderated by interpretation, it is mu'awwal; otherwise it is mushtarak.
Mu'awwal is a subtype of mushtarak.
5
Note the Relationship
Mu'awwal is in reality a type of mushtarak, as it requires preponderance of one meaning over others.
This clarifies the hierarchy among the four types.

The Book of Allah Almighty. The First Division. The First Research is on the Book of Allah Almighty. Definition of the Book: The Book is the Quran sent down to the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, written in the mushafs, transmitted from him via tawatur with no doubt about it. The usuliyyun conducted regarding the Book of Allah Almighty and similarly the Sunnah of the Messenger of Allah, peace be upon him, four divisions, from which twenty sections result. The First Division: The first division, regarding designation (wad'). A word, with respect to its designation for meaning, is of four types: al-khass (particular), al-aam (general), al-mushtarak (shared/homonym), and al-mu'awwal (interpreted). Definition of al-khass: (1) Al-khass: a word placed for a known meaning or a known named entity individually, whether its usage is by way of designation, i.e., from where it was placed for one meaning or more, disregarding whether in its real or metaphorical meaning, and disregarding the clarity or obscurity of the meaning. Al-mu'awwal: a word either indicates one meaning or more; if the first, then either it indicates individually, so it is khass, or it indicates shared among individuals, so it is aam; if the second, then either one of its meanings is given preponderance by interpretation (ta'wil), so it is mu'awwal, otherwise it is mushtarak. So mu'awwal is in reality a type of mushtarak. (From Nur al-Anwar: al-infrad means cutting off sharing, i.e., the word indicates one meaning, whether that meaning is singular in person, type, genus, or in the real sense, like knowledge and ignorance, or in the conventional sense like numbers.)

Commentary
This passage begins the first research on the Quran as a source of law. First, it defines the Quran as the revelation sent to the Prophet, transmitted mutawatir, written in the mushafs. Then it states that usul scholars have classified the Quran (and Sunnah) into four divisions, yielding twenty sections. The first division is according to wad' (linguistic designation). Under this, words are categorized into four types: khass (particular), aam (general), mushtarak (homonym/shared), and mu'awwal (interpreted). The author then defines khass as a word placed for a single known meaning individually, regardless of whether it is used literally or figuratively, or whether its meaning is clear or obscure. He then clarifies the relationship between these categories: a word may indicate one meaning or multiple. If one meaning, and it is individual, it's khass; if shared among individuals (i.e., applies to a class), it's aam. If multiple meanings, and one is preponderated by interpretation, it's mu'awwal; if not, it's mushtarak. Thus, mu'awwal is essentially a subcategory of mushtarak. The commentary from Nur al-Anwar explains 'infrad' as cutting off sharing, meaning the word indicates a single meaning, whether that meaning is a specific person, a species, a genus, or an abstract concept.

Key Terms
الكتاب (al-kitab): The Quran revealed to Prophet Muhammad, transmitted mutawatir, written in mushafs.
الخاص (al-khass): A word placed for a known meaning individually, cutting off sharing.
المؤول (al-mu'awwal): A homonym (mushtarak) where one meaning is given preponderance by interpretation (ta'wil).

The Causes of Obligations (Asbab al-Wujub) in Hanafi Fiqh
Listing of asbab for seven obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the sabab (cause) for each major Islamic obligation as listed in the Hanafi source and apply the distinction between actual and legal growth to determine zakat liability.

Core Idea
Every Islamic obligation is triggered by a specific cause - knowing it is essential for timing and validity.

This lesson presents a foundational list from Hanafi usul: the asbab (causes) that trigger various acts of worship and financial obligations. Understanding these causes is crucial because obligations do not become due without their specific sabab. For example, prayer is not obligatory simply because one is a Muslim, but only when its time enters. We will explore each sabab and the important distinction between real and legal growth for zakat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligations listed
Read the passage and list each obligation (salah, sawm, zakat, etc.) along with its assigned sabab. Note that each obligation has exactly one cause mentioned.
2
Distinguish real vs. legal growth
Focus on the condition for zakat: the nisab must be 'naami' (growing). The passage explains two types: real growth (actual increase through breeding or trade) and legal growth (potential to increase, e.g., money in hand or with an agent). Understand why the Hanafi school requires this.
This is a uniquely Hanafi condition; other schools may not require growth.
3
Understand the sabab for fitr
Sadaqat al-fitr is obligatory for every person whom one supports and has authority over (oneself, minor children, slaves). The passage clarifies 'ras yamoonahu' means those you provide for and manage.
In modern context, this includes dependent family members.
4
Differentiate ushr and kharaj
Ushr is due on land that actually produces (irrigated by rain), while kharaj is due on land suitable for cultivation (even if not currently farmed). Both are triggered by the land itself, but the tax type differs.
This distinction is important in land taxation rulings.
5
Memorize the sabab list
Create a mental table: Obligation → Sabab. Test yourself repeatedly. This list is a cornerstone for Hanafi fiqh understanding.
Many later rulings (like when zakat is due) depend on these causes.

Commentary
This passage is a practical enumeration of the sabab (cause) for each major Islamic obligation according to the Hanafi school. The author provides a concise list: prayer is obligated by the entry of its time; fasting by sighting the month of Ramadan; zakat by ownership of a nisab that is 'naami' (productive) either actually (e.g., livestock breeding, trade profit) or legally (e.g., money in hand that can be invested); hajj by the Kaaba (i.e., ability to reach it); sadaqat al-fitr by the head of a person whom one supports and has authority over (oneself, minor children, and slaves); ushr (tithe) by land that actually produces; kharaj (land tax) by land suitable for cultivation. The text also clarifies the distinction between real growth (actual increase) and legal growth (the potential for increase). The Urdu commentary further explains 'ras yamoonahu' as referring to oneself, one's minor children, and one's slaves. This list is foundational for understanding when these obligations become due in Hanafi fiqh.

Key Terms
النصاب النامي (al-nisab al-naami): The minimum threshold of wealth that is productive (growing) either in reality (actual increase) or legally (potential to increase).
شهود الشهر (shuhood al-shahr): Sighting the new moon of Ramadan; the sabab for the obligation of fasting.
سبب (ج: أسباب) (sabab (pl. asbab)): The cause or trigger that makes an obligation due. In usul al-fiqh, the sabab is that which the Lawgiver has made the sign for the obligation.
