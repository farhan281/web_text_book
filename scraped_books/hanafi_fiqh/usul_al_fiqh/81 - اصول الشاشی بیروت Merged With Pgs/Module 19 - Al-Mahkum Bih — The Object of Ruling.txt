Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic Goodness in Commanded Acts
Classifies commanded acts into two types of goodness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between intrinsic good (حسن بنفسه) and extrinsic good (حسن لغيره) in commanded acts, and apply the Hanafi ruling for each type.

Core Idea
An act's goodness can be intrinsic or derivative; the ruling for each type differs in how obligation is lifted.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces a fundamental distinction: not all commanded acts are good in the same way. Understanding whether an act is good in itself (like faith) or good because it leads to something else (like ablution for prayer) is essential for determining how its obligation can be fulfilled or dropped. The classification affects the nature of the ruling and the role of intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Start with the premise: a command from a wise commander indicates the goodness of the commanded act. This is the basis for classifying acts.
The command itself is evidence of desirability.
2
Classify the act into one of two types
Determine whether the act is good in itself (حسن بنفسه) or good for something else (حسن لغيره). Intrinsically good acts are desirable for their own sake; extrinsically good acts are desirable as means to another good.
Examples of intrinsic: faith, prayer, truthfulness. Extrinsic: going to Friday prayer, performing ablution.
3
Apply the ruling for intrinsic good
If the act is intrinsically good and becomes obligatory, it is not fulfilled except by actual performance. For some obligations that cannot be dropped (like faith), performance is the only way. For others that can be dropped (like prayer), performance or a legal impediment (e.g., insanity, menstruation) lifts the obligation.
The passing of time or lack of means does not drop the obligation.
4
Apply the ruling for extrinsic good
The goodness of such acts depends on their leading to the intended good. The reward is based on intention and outcome.
Ablution is good only if it enables prayer; going to Friday prayer is good because it leads to the prayer.
5
Test with examples
Given a scenario, identify the act type and its ruling. For instance, is the act good in itself or as a means? What happens if the means is performed without the intended end?
This step reinforces the practical application.

فصل في حسن المأمور به
الأمر بالشيء يدل على حسن المأمور به إذا كان الأمر حكيماً، لأن الأمر لبيان أن المأمور به مما ينبغي أن يوجد فاقتضى ذلك حسنه .
ثم المأمور به في حق الحسن نوعان:
۱- حسن بنفسه
۲- حسن لغيره.
فالحسن بنفسه مثل الإيمان بالله تعالى وشكر المنعم، والصدق والعدل والصلاة ونحوها من العبادات الخالصة .
فحكم هذا النوع أنه إذا وجب على العبد أداؤه لا يسقط إلا بالأداء، وهذا في ما لا يحتمل السقوط، مثل الإيمان بالله تعالى.
وأما ما يحتمل السقوط فهو يسقط بالأداء، أو بإسقاط الآمر .
وعلى هذا إذا وجبت الصلاة في أول الوقت، سقط الواجب بالأداء، أو باعتراض الجنون والحيض والنفاس في آخر الوقت باعتبار أن الشرع أسقطها عنه عند هذه العوارض ولا يسقط بضيق الوقت وعدم الماء واللباس ونحوه .
النوع الثاني ما يكون حسناً بواسطة.
وذلك مثل السعي إلى الجمعة والوضوء للصلاة، فإن السعي حسن بواسطة كونه مفضياً إلى أداء الجمعة والوضوء حسن بواسطة كونه مفتاحاً للصلاة .
Section on the goodness of the commanded act. Commanding something indicates the goodness of the commanded act if the commander is wise, because the command is to clarify that the commanded act is something that should exist, so that necessitates its goodness. Then the commanded act, concerning goodness, is of two types: 1) Good in itself, 2) Good for something else. Good in itself is like faith in God Almighty, gratitude to the Benefactor, truthfulness, justice, prayer, and similar pure acts of worship. The ruling of this type: when it becomes obligatory upon the servant to perform it, it does not fall except by performance, and this is regarding what cannot be dropped, like faith in God Almighty. As for what can be dropped, it falls by performance or by the commander's dropping it. On this basis, if prayer becomes obligatory at the beginning of its time, the obligation falls by performance or by the occurrence of insanity, menstruation, or postnatal bleeding at the end of the time, because the Lawgiver drops it due to these impediments; it does not fall by the expiry of time, lack of water, clothing, and the like. The second type is what is good as a means. That is like going to Friday prayer and ablution for prayer, for going is good as a means leading to performing Friday prayer, and ablution is good as a means being a key to prayer.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi defines two fundamental categories of commanded acts based on how they are good. The author first establishes a principle: a command from a wise commander implies that the act commanded is good (حسن), because the very act of commanding indicates that the act should exist. Then he divides commanded acts into two types. Type one: حسن بنفسه (good in itself). These are acts that are intrinsically good, such as faith, gratitude, truthfulness, justice, and worship like prayer. Their goodness is not dependent on anything else. The ruling for this type is that if it becomes obligatory, it can only be fulfilled by actual performance. For some of these acts, like faith, they cannot be dropped or replaced; they must be performed. For others that can be dropped (like prayer under certain conditions), they can be dropped by performance or by the command-giver (Allah) explicitly dropping them through a legal ruling. For example, prayer becomes obligatory at the start of its time; it is fulfilled by performing it, or it is dropped if a legal impediment like insanity or menstruation occurs before the time ends. However, it is not dropped merely by the passing of time or lack of means like water or clothing. Type two: حسن لغيره (good for something else). These acts are not good in themselves but become good because they lead to or enable another good act. Examples: going to Friday prayer is good because it leads to attending the prayer; ablution is good because it is the key to prayer. This classification is important in Hanafi usul for understanding the nature of obligations and recommended acts.

Key Terms
حسن بنفسه (ḥasan bi-nafsih): An act that is good in itself, such as faith and prayer, whose goodness is intrinsic and not dependent on anything else.
حسن لغيره (ḥasan li-ghayrih): An act that is good because it leads to or enables another good act, e.g., going to Friday prayer or performing ablution.

Key Takeaway
A commanded act's goodness is either intrinsic (حسن بنفسه) or extrinsic (حسن لغيره), and this distinction determines how its obligation is fulfilled or dropped.
