Understanding Khaas and Aam in Hanafi Usul
Defining the terms khaas and aam with examples of specification

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of khaas, identify its three levels of specification, and distinguish it from aam.

Core Idea
Khaas picks out one; aam covers all until proven otherwise.

This lesson introduces two foundational terms in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: khaas (specific) and aam (general). The passage from al-Shashi defines khaas as a word placed for a known meaning or object individually, with examples spanning individuals, types, and genera. Understanding these categories is essential for correctly interpreting Quranic verses and deriving rulings with precision.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the definition of khaas
Recall that khaas is 'a word placed for a known meaning or object individually'. This means it singles out one specific referent, whether an individual, a class, or a genus.
The key is 'individually' (على الانفراد) — it does not apply to multiple items simultaneously.
2
Identify the three levels of specification
The passage gives three examples: Zaid (individual), man (type/naw'), and human (genus/jins). Each exemplifies khaas at a different hierarchical level.
Even 'man' is considered khaas here because it specifies the type as opposed to all living beings.
3
Understand the incomplete definition of aam
The source cuts off with 'every word...' (كل لفظ). In Hanafi usul, aam is a term that applies to all members of a category without limit. It is qat'i al-dalala (definitive in meaning) until a specific exclusion (takhsis) is established.
This contrasts with the Shafi'i view that aam is zanni al-dalala. The practical impact: in Hanafi usul, a general command obligates all instances unless a specific proof narrows it.

Definitions of al-Khas and al-Amm and the Dispute on Istighraq
Defines khas and amm, records dispute on istighraq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definitions of al-Khas and al-Amm and identify the dispute regarding the condition of istighraq in the definition of al-Amm.

Core Idea
Al-Khas specifies; al-Amm encompasses a collection, but Hanafis of Transoxiana do not require comprehensive inclusion.

In this lesson, you will learn the foundational usul terms 'khas' (specific) and 'amm' (general). The passage provides a crisp definition of khas with examples of individual, type, and genus. For amm, it then presents a key intra-madhhab and inter-madhhab dispute: whether istighraq (comprehensive inclusion) is required for a word to be considered 'amm. Understanding this dispute is critical because it affects how general verses and hadiths are interpreted in istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the definition of khas
Khas is a word placed for a known meaning or named entity. It can specify an individual (Zayd), a type (man), or a genus (human). No dispute among Hanafis on this definition.
2
Memorize the definition of amm
Amm is every word that encompasses a collection of individuals, either in wording (e.g., plural 'Muslimun') or in meaning (e.g., the particles 'man' and 'ma').
Note the two types: lafzi (word-based) and ma'nawi (meaning-based).
3
Understand the dispute on istighraq
The author and Transoxianan Hanafis (al-Bazdawi, al-Nasafi) do not require istighraq. The Iraqi Hanafis and Shafi'is require it. Compare the two positions.
Imam al-Razi's definition exemplifies the condition of istighraq.
4
Apply to the indefinite plural example
The indefinite plural (e.g., 'Muslimun') is not considered 'amm according to those who require istighraq, because it does not cover all Muslims by itself. For Transoxianan Hanafis, it can still be considered 'amm due to its potential.
This has practical fiqh implications for interpreting Qur'anic plurals.

The passage then records a significant intra-Hanafi and inter-madhhab dispute: the Hanafis of Transoxiana (including the author, al-Bazdawi, and al-Nasafi) do not require istighraq (comprehensive inclusion of all individuals) for a word to be considered 'amm — rather, a word that can apply to multiple individuals is 'amm even if it does not include every single one. استغراق (istighraq): Comprehensive inclusion of all individuals that the word can apply to.

Precedence of the Specific Quranic Ruling (Khas min al-Kitab)
States the binding nature and priority of the specific Quranic text.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule when a specific Quranic ruling is opposed by a solitary hadith or analogy, and apply the reconciliation principle correctly.

Core Idea
A specific Quranic ruling is qat'i; it prevails over zanni evidences unless reconciliation is possible.

In the Hanafi usul, a specific Quranic text (khas min al-kitab) carries full certainty. This lesson examines the rule that governs such a text when it faces a solitary hadith (khabar wahid) or analogical reasoning (qiyas). The text presents a clear hierarchy: first seek reconciliation; if that changes the Quranic ruling, the Quran is followed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the khas ruling
Pinpoint the specific (non-general) Quranic command or prohibition. Recognize its qat'i status in thubut and dalala.
Khas applies to a defined category; it is not an general 'amm' text.
2
Recognize the opposing evidence
Determine whether the opposing evidence is khabar wahid (zanni in thubut) or qiyas (zanni in dalala). Note that both are subordinate to the Quranic nass.
If the opponent is a mutawatir or Quranic verse, different rules apply.
3
Attempt reconciliation
Check if both evidences can be harmonized without altering the specific ruling of the Quranic text. For example, one may qualify the other's scope.
Reconciliation must preserve the khas ruling unchanged; otherwise it is invalid.
4
Apply the priority
If reconciliation is impossible, act on the Quranic specific text and abandon the opposing evidence. The Quran's qat'i authority overrides zanni sources.
This reflects the Hanafi principle that a qat'i text cannot be abrogated by a zanni one.

Commentary
This passage presents a key Hanafi usul rule regarding the legal force of a specific (khas) text from the Quran. The rule is straightforward: a specific Quranic ruling is definitively binding (wajib al-'amal bihi la mahalah). When such a text is confronted by a khabar al-wahid (a solitary hadith) or by qiyas (analogy), the scholar must first attempt to reconcile the two without altering the specific Quranic ruling. If reconciliation is possible, both are applied. For example, a specific Quranic ruling and a hadith may each be applied to different contexts or one may qualify the other. If reconciliation is impossible, the Quranic text takes precedence and the opposing evidence (khabar wahid or qiyas) is abandoned. This is because the Quran's thubut (transmission) is qat'i (definitive), while khabar wahid is zanni (speculative), and qiyas is subordinate. This principle is fundamental in Hanafi usul, ensuring that clear scriptural rulings are not easily overridden by weaker evidence.

خبر الواحد (khabar al-wahid): A solitary hadith that does not reach the level of tawatur.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning to derive a ruling from a primary source.

The Khass Number in Quran 2:228: Literal Adherence vs. Linguistic Interpretation
Analyzing the word 'three' as khass requiring action and refuting Shafi'i interpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that khass is qat'i by analyzing the word 'three' in Quran 2:228 and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument based on linguistic inference.

Core Idea
The word 'three' is specific (khass) and must be implemented literally; interpreting it as part of a purity period violates its specificity.

This passage explores a key usul al-fiqh application: when a text contains a specific number, does it require literal adherence, or can it be reinterpreted based on other linguistic clues? The Hanafi school insists that the word 'three' in Quran 2:228 is khass (specific) and must be followed exactly. The Shafi'i school, relying on the feminine plural verb, interprets 'quru' as purity periods, leading to a count that only reaches two complete purities and part of a third. This lesson will guide you through the Hanafi argument and its refutation of the Shafi'i position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the operative word
Locate the word 'ثلاثة' (three) in the verse. It is a number specifying the quantity of 'قروء'.
The verse is from Surah Al-Baqarah 2:228.
2
Classify the word as khass
In Hanafi usul, a number is a type of khass (specific) because it denotes a precise quantity. Unlike general words (amm), khass is not open to interpretation beyond its literal meaning.
Numbers are unambiguous in their specification.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle
The principle is 'al-khass yufidu al-wujub wa huwa qat'i' — a specific text indicates obligation and is definitive in meaning. Therefore, 'three' must be implemented as exactly three complete units.
This principle is foundational in Hanafi istinbat.
4
Analyze the Shafi'i counter-argument
The Shafi'i school argues that the feminine plural verb 'يتربصن' indicates the subject is feminine, so 'قروء' must be plural of a feminine word, i.e., purity periods (tuhr), not menstruations (hayd).
They rely on a linguistic inference (qarina) to override the apparent meaning.
5
Refute the counter-argument
The Hanafi response shows that interpreting as purity periods leads to only two complete purities and part of a third, which contradicts the explicit number 'three'. Since khass is qat'i, it cannot be overridden by a zanni linguistic inference. Therefore, the Hanafi interpretation (three menstruations) is correct.
This demonstrates that primary textual evidence (khass) takes precedence over secondary linguistic clues.

Mahr: Fixed by Sharia or by Agreement? — A Usul Analysis
Presenting Shafi'i view that mahr is not fixed, using qiyas to financial contracts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the usul principle (khass vs qiyas) underlying the Shafi'i view on mahr and evaluate the Hanafi counter-argument.

Core Idea
Marriage is an act of worship, not a mere financial contract — so the sharia can fix mahr.

The amount of mahr (dower) in marriage: is it legally determined by the Quran and Sunnah, or left to the spouses? In this passage, we see the Shafi'i school using qiyas (analogy) to financial contracts to argue that the mahr is not fixed. But the Hanafi school holds that the verse from Surah Al-Ahzab is specific and thus binding, overriding any analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested ruling
Determine exactly what is in dispute: whether the mahr amount is fixed by sharia or left to mutual agreement.
2
Parse the Shafi'i argument
Identify the Shafi'i reasoning: marriage is a financial contract, so by qiyas to other financial contracts, the amount is left to the spouses.
The Shafi'i rely on the general category of financial contracts.
3
Identify the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that the verse is specific (khass fi al-taqdir al-shar'i), meaning the sharia has fixed the mahr, so qiyas does not apply.
The phrase 'خاص فى التقدير الشرعي' is key.
4
Analyze the usul principle
Recognize that the dispute is about the nature of the text: is it khass (specific) or 'amm (general)? The Hanafi see it as khass, blocking qiyas; the Shafi'i see the verse as not specifying an amount, allowing qiyas.
This is a classic usul al-fiqh disagreement about textual categories.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: the Hanafi school requires a minimum mahr (e.g., 10 dirhams), while the Shafi'i school allows any amount agreed upon.
This affects the validity of marriages with very low mahr.

Ruling on Shared Terms When One Meaning is Determined
Explains the rule for shared terms with examples and proof from consensus and Quran

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a mushtarak term in a legal text and apply the rule that once one meaning is determined as intended, the other meanings are no longer considered.

Core Idea
A shared term (mushtarak) has no generality; once one meaning is intended, the other is dropped.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi states a fundamental principle: when a homonymous word (mushtarak) appears and one meaning is clearly intended, the other possible meanings fall away. The author illustrates this rule with several examples from the Quran and juristic reasoning, including the famous ikhtilaf over the meaning of 'al-qur' in the 'iddah verse and the penalty for hunting. Understanding this rule is essential for correctly deriving rulings from texts that contain ambiguous terms.

Core Idea
Metaphor in contracts: the original literal effect must produce a component of the target; reverse is invalid.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi establishes a key principle for metaphorical usage in contracts: only contracts valid in their literal sense can be borrowed as metaphors for other contracts. Through the example of using 'hibah' (gift) to effect nikah (marriage), we see how the Hanafi school derives this rule and handles the condition of literal possibility.

Zahir, Nass, and Mufassar: Hierarchy of Textual Clarity
Hierarchy of textual clarity with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a legal phrase as Zahir, Nass, or Mufassar and apply the Hanafi rule that Mufassar overrides Nass.

Core Idea
A Mufassar (specific explanation) outweighs even a Nass (explicit text) when it clarifies the intended meaning.

Legal texts vary in clarity. The Hanafi school distinguishes between Zahir (apparent), Nass (explicit), and Mufassar (specific/clarified). This lesson teaches you to identify each category and understand that a Mufassar can override a Nass—a principle often misunderstood by beginners. We'll work through examples from marriage, debt, and currency.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
Learn the definitions of Zahir (text with possible interpretation), Nass (explicit text with no interpretation, but open to specification), and Mufassar (text clarified by an additional statement that determines the exact intended meaning).
Analogous to degrees of certainty: Zahir is 80%, Nass is 90%, Mufassar is 95%.
2
Understand the overriding rule
Grasp the principle: when a Nass and a Mufassar conflict, the Mufassar takes precedence because it pinpoints the intended meaning, making the Nass bound by that clarification.
This does not contradict the fact that Nass is explicit; rather, the Mufassar specifies the scope.
3
Analyze the marriage example
The phrase 'I married you' (تزوجت) is Zahir for permanent marriage, but the addition 'for a month' (شهراً) is Mufassar specifying temporary marriage (mut'a). The Mufassar overrides the Zahir, so the contract is mut'a, not proper marriage.
This example shows that a Mufassar can outrank a Zahir as well as a Nass.
4
Analyze the debt example
The phrase 'I owe a thousand' (علي ألف) is Nass for obligation and Zahir for local currency. Adding 'from the price of a slave' or 'from the currency of X country' is Mufassar. The Mufassar overrides the Nass, so payment is conditional on delivery and in the specified currency.
This illustrates the hierarchy: Mufassar > Nass > Zahir.
5
Apply the rule to new scenarios
Generalize the principle: any time a general explicit statement is later clarified by a specifying phrase, the clarifying phrase (Mufassar) governs the ruling.
This is a key tool for interpreting contracts and legal documents.

Core Idea

Definition of Muqtaḍā and Its Application in Divorce and Sale
Definition of muqtaḍā with concrete examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define muqtaḍā, identify its operation in legal statements, and apply the Hanafi rule that only pillars (arkan) are implied, not conditions.

Core Idea
Muqtaḍā makes the unstated legally binding when it is necessary for the truth of the statement.

In Hanafi usul, not everything that is implied by a statement is automatically legally effective. This lesson examines the concept of muqtaḍā (implication) and distinguishes between what is necessarily implied (pillars) and what is not (conditions). We will explore two classic examples: the divorce pronouncement and the command to free a slave for a price. The passage also presents a dispute with Abu Yusuf regarding whether possession is implied in a gift by implication.

Guided Path
1
Understand muqtaḍā as linguistic necessity
Muqtaḍā is that unstated meaning which is indispensable for the truth of the explicit statement. If the explicit statement can be true without it, it is not muqtaḍā. Identify the core linguistic operation: the word 'ṭalāq' is literally a description, but it cannot be true unless the act of divorce occurs.
2
Analyze the divorce example
When someone says 'You are divorced' (anti ṭāliq), the word 'ṭāliq' is a description of the woman. A description requires a source action (the divorce). Therefore, the divorce is deemed to have occurred by implication. The implication is necessary for the truth of the statement.
This shows that muqtaḍā is not an extra element; it is the essence of the utterance.
3
Analyze the sale/gift example
When someone commands 'Free your slave for me for one thousand dirhams,' mentioning a price shifts the meaning from pure emancipation to a sale. The implication includes the sale and the obligation to pay. The emancipation takes effect for the commander.
This demonstrates how context (mention of price) changes the implication.
4
Evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute with Abu Yusuf
Abu Yusuf argued that if someone says 'Free your slave for me for nothing,' this implies a gift and agency, and no possession is needed because it is like acceptance in sale. The majority Hanafi response distinguishes: acceptance is a pillar of sale, so it is necessarily implied; possession is only a condition of gift, so it is not implied.
The key takeaway: only pillars (arkan) are necessarily implied, not conditions (shurūṭ).

The Principle of Muqtada and Its Limitation to Necessity
Explaining legal implication (muqtada) in sale, gift, divorce, and eating

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle of muqtada (implication) and apply it to distinguish between when an implied element is limited to necessity versus when it is linguistically explicit, allowing broader scope.

Core Idea
Muqtada is limited to necessity; implied divorces are only one, but linguistically explicit words allow three.

In this passage from Usul al-Shashi, the author explains a key Hanafi usul principle: when a legal effect is established by implication (muqtada), it is strictly limited to the minimum necessary to validate the speech or action. This principle is then applied to various cases: sale, gift, divorce, and even eating. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly deriving rulings from implicit language in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concept of muqtada
From the examples (sale, gift, divorce, eating), identify what is being implied (muqtada) and what triggers the implication (iqtida). Note that the implied element is subsidiary to the explicit statement.
Muqtada is not explicitly stated but is necessary for the legal validity of the explicit act.
2
Differentiate rukn from shart in implication
Observe that in sale, acceptance (qabul) is a rukn, so it is automatically implied when sale is established by muqtada. In gift, possession (qabd) is a shart (condition), not a rukn, so it is not automatically implied. Understand the rationale: the implied thing follows the implying thing; a condition is not of the same kind as speech and cannot be dropped by implication.
Rukn is integral to the act; shart is external. The distinction determines what can be implied.
3
Apply the principle 'al-muqtada yuqaddar bi qadr al-darura'
When an element is established by implication, it is limited to the minimum necessary. In divorce, saying 'You are divorced' implies only one divorce because necessity is removed by one. In eating, saying 'If I eat' implies any food, not a specific one, because that suffices for the speech to be meaningful.
The implication cannot exceed the necessity that gave rise to it.
4
Recognize the exception for linguistically explicit elements
When the implied element is not merely inferred by necessity but is linguistically present (e.g., a verbal noun in 'Divorce yourself' or 'You are bain'), the intention of the speaker can be broader. Here, the word is not established by muqtada but by language, so it carries its full linguistic scope (e.g., three divorces are possible).
Linguistically explicit elements are not constrained by the necessity principle.

The Command Implies Obligation: The Hanafi Rule and Its Rational Proof
Stating the usul rule that command implies obligation with a qiyas proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the imperative implies obligation (wujub) and explain the rational proof (qiyas) from the authority of the commander.

Core Idea
A command obligates compliance because the commander's authority determines the necessity of obedience.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi establishes a foundational principle of Hanafi usul: the imperative form (amr) inherently indicates obligation (wujub). The author supports this with a rational analogy: just as a human master's command obligates his slave, God's absolute ownership and authority make His commands even more binding. We will unpack this proof and understand the scope of the rule.

Guided Path
1
State the rule and its exception
Identify the rule: the imperative implies obligation (wujub) as the default. Note the exception: unless evidence indicates otherwise.
The Arabic: والصحيح من المذهب أن موجبه الوجوب إلا إذا قام الدليل على خلافه.
2
Understand the underlying rationale
Leaving a command is disobedience (معصية), and compliance is obedience (طاعة). The necessity of compliance depends on the commander's authority over the addressee.
The author uses the phrase 'لزوم الايتمار إنما يكون بقدر ولاية الأمر على المخاطب'.
3
Analyze the qiyas (analogy)
The asl: a human master with limited ownership (ملك قاصر) over a slave commands; the slave must obey or deserve punishment. The far': God commands His servants. The illa (common cause): the authority of the commander.
The proof is a qiyas jali (clear analogy) from limited human authority to absolute divine authority.
4
Apply the rule to the divine context
Since God has complete ownership (ملك كامل) over every part of the universe and absolute disposal, His commands obligate without exception. Disobedience merits punishment.
The text uses 'فما ظنك' to emphasize the greater degree of obligation.
5
Note the exception and its implications
The rule is default but not absolute. If there is evidence (dalil) that a particular command is not obligatory (e.g., a command interpreted as recommendation or permission), then the obligation is overridden.
This preserves flexibility in interpreting commands from the Quran and Sunnah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that an absolute command does not imply repetition and distinguish the three views on this usul dispute.

Core Idea
A command is fulfilled once; repetition requires evidence.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi presents a foundational Hanafi principle: an absolute command does not imply repetition. The author states the rule, gives practical examples from fiqh (divorce, marriage), and then surveys the dispute among scholars. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly deriving rulings from the primary sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
The passage opens with a clear statement: 'al-amr bil fi'l la yaqtadi al-takrar' – the command to act does not imply repetition. This is the core Hanafi position.
2

Three fiqh examples are given: (1) a divorce command executed once cannot be re-executed after remarriage; (2) a command to arrange one marriage does not permit multiple marriages; (3) a command to a slave to marry is satisfied with one marriage. These illustrate that a single performance fulfills the command.
3
Understand the dispute
The text reports three positions: (a) some say an absolute command necessitates continuous repetition throughout life unless evidence prevents it; (b) some say it permits repetition but does not necessitate it; (c) some (including the Hanafis) deny even the possibility of repetition without evidence.
The key distinction is between 'mujib' (necessitator, no context needed) and 'muhtamal' (possible, context needed).
4
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof is based on the nature of a command: a command is an address to perform an action, and an action is a single occurrence. Repetition would require an additional indicator. The examples from divorce and marriage support this.
5
Apply the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: for a general command like 'Establish prayer', Hanafis say one performance fulfills the obligation unless there is evidence requiring repetition (like the five daily prayers). The opposing view would require continuous prayer unless restricted.

Core Idea
A command covers the lowest individual; intention can raise it to the whole genus.

In Hanafi usul, when Allah or the Prophet commands an act, the command generally refers to the genus of that act. But what counts as fulfilling the command? Understanding this rule is essential for interpreting oaths, divorce, and marriage commands correctly.

Guided Path

Understand the concept of genus (jins) in commands

Core Idea
Intention can expand a command to the full genre, but only within the permissible range.

When a command like 'Divorce!' or 'Marry!' is given, its default scope is the minimal complete unit. But what if the commander intended more? Two Types of Prohibited Acts and Their Rulings
Explains two categories of prohibited acts and the ruling after prohibition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between qabih li-aynihi and qabih li-ghayrihi, and apply the Hanafi principle that a prohibited shar'i transaction remains legitimate in form.

Core Idea
A prohibition does not necessarily void the act; it may only attach an external illegality while preserving the essential form.

In Hanafi usul, not all prohibitions are equal. Some acts are inherently evil (qabih li-aynihi) and thus never legislated. Others are good in themselves but prohibited due to an external factor (qabih li-ghayrihi). The Hanafi school uniquely holds that such prohibited transactions remain shar'i in form; otherwise, the believer would be incapable of performing a legitimate act. This principle has practical implications for invalid sales and other contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of prohibited acts
Read the passage and note the distinction between an act that is inherently evil (qabih li-aynihi) and one that is evil due to something else (qabih li-ghayrihi). The examples: fasting on Eid, prayer at disliked times, selling one dirham for two.
The first type is not legislated at all; the second type remains legislated in form.
2
Understand the ruling for each type
For qabih li-aynihi, the act is completely void (batil) and not shar'i. For qabih li-ghayrihi, the act is shar'i in essence but unlawful to perform due to the external factor.
The doer commits haram due to the circumstance, not the act itself.
3
Grasp the Hanafi rational proof (istihsan)
The Hanafi argue that if the prohibited act became non-shar'i, the servant would be incapable of performing a legitimate act, which is impossible from the Lawgiver. Thus, the act must remain shar'i in form.
This is a rational proof (istihsan) unique to Hanafi usul.
4
Apply the principle to examples
Consider a fasid sale: it is a prohibited transaction (due to riba or other defect) but remains a valid contract in form, so it confers ownership upon possession. Similarly, a vow to fast on Eid is a valid vow but sinful to fulfill.
This distinguishes Hanafi fiqh from other schools that may treat such acts as void.
5
Distinguish from physical acts
Physical acts (af'al hissiyya) like hitting or killing are different. If they are inherently evil, the servant is not incapable of performing them; they are simply forbidden. No rational necessity to keep them shar'i.
This contrast reinforces the principle for shar'i transactions.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary explains two categories of prohibited acts and the principle that a transaction remains legitimate in form even after a prohibition. The author first gives examples: fasting on the day of sacrifice (Eid al-Adha), prayer at disliked times, and selling one dirham for two dirhams (riba). He then distinguishes two types: (1) The prohibited act is inherently evil (qabih li-aynihi) – it is not legislated at all; e.g., selling a free person. (2) The prohibited act is good in itself but becomes evil due to an external factor (qabih li-ghayrihi) – e.g., fasting on Eid is good devotion but prohibited because it is the day of sacrifice. In such cases, the underlying act remains shar'i (valid in principle), but the prohibition makes it unlawful to perform. The Hanafi scholars (Ashabuna) state that the prohibition of shar'i transactions (like sale, lease, vow) actually affirms their legitimacy; otherwise, if the act became void, the person would be incapable of performing a legitimate act, and the Lawgiver does not prohibit that which is impossible. This is contrasted with physical acts (af'al hissiyya) which, if inherently evil, do not lead to such impossibility. The passage then applies this to invalid contracts: a fasid sale confers ownership upon possession because the contract form is valid (nazaran li-annahu bay'), but the ownership is defective due to the prohibition (li-annahu haram li-ghayrihi). This is a key Hanafi position distinguishing between fasid (invalid) and batil (void) transactions.

Key Terms
قبيح لعينه (qabīḥ li-ʿaynihi): An act that is inherently evil, not legislated at all, e.g., selling a free person.
قبيح لغيره (qabīḥ li-ghayrihi): An act that is good in itself but becomes evil due to an external factor, e.g., fasting on Eid.
شرعية التصرف بعد النهي (sharʿiyyat al-taṣarruf baʿda al-nahy): The principle that a shar'i transaction remains legitimate in form after a prohibition, because otherwise it would be a prohibition of the incapable.

Principle of Mudaddah: When Prohibition Nullifies a Contract (Marriage vs. Sale)
Principle of mudaddah applied to marriage and sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when a prohibition nullifies a contract (marriage) versus when it merely prohibits disposal (sale) by applying the Hanafi principle of mudaddah.

Core Idea
If the purpose of a contract directly opposes the prohibition, the contract is void; if they can coexist, the contract remains valid but the action is unlawful.

In Hanafi usul, not every prohibition makes the contract void. The key lies in whether the primary effect of the contract contradicts the prohibition. This lesson unpacks the distinction between marriage contracts (where prohibition nullifies) and sale contracts (where prohibition only forbids disposal), using the principle of mudaddah (contradiction).

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary legal effect of the contract
For marriage, the primary effect is permissibility of disposal (istiḥlāl al-taṣarruf). For sale, it is establishing ownership (thubūt al-milk).
2
Identify the effect of the prohibition
The prohibition imposes ḥurmat al-taṣarruf (prohibition of disposal).
3
Check for contradiction (mudāddah)
If the contract's effect and prohibition's effect cannot coexist (e.g., permissibility vs. prohibition of the same thing), then the prohibition nullifies the contract. If they can coexist (e.g., ownership vs. prohibition of disposal), the contract remains valid but disposal is unlawful.
4
Apply to marriage contracts
Marriage aims at permissibility of sexual relations; prohibition forbids them. They directly oppose, so prohibition is interpreted as negation (nafy) and the contract is void.
Examples: marrying polytheist women, father's wife, woman in iddah, incestuous marriages, marriage without witnesses.
5
Apply to sale contracts
Sale establishes ownership; prohibition only forbids disposal. Ownership can remain while disposal is prohibited (like fermented grape juice). So the sale is valid but the act of taking possession or using the item may be unlawful.
This distinction shows that prohibition does not automatically void a contract; the nature of the contract's effect matters.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi explains the usul principle of how prohibition (nahy) interacts with different types of contracts. The author distinguishes between two scenarios. First, when the purpose of the contract is directly opposed to the prohibition (contradiction), the prohibition renders the contract void and is interpreted as negation of validity. This applies to invalid marriages: marrying polytheist women, one's father's wife, a woman in iddah, incestuous marriages, and marriage without witnesses, because marriage aims at permissibility of sexual relations, and the prohibition aims at forbidding it—they cannot coexist. Second, when the purpose of the contract (ownership) is not directly opposed to the prohibition (unlawful disposal), the contract remains valid but the action is unlawful. The analogy of grape juice fermenting in a Muslim's property illustrates: ownership remains, but drinking is prohibited. The author then gives practical furu: vows to fast on forbidden days (Nahr, Tashriq) are valid because the act of fasting itself is permissible, though the time is disliked—the prohibition does not remove the intrinsic permissibility. Similarly, praying at disliked times (makruh) is valid; if one starts a nafl prayer at such a time, it becomes binding to complete, because completion without the disliked element is possible (e.g., waiting until the sun rises). This differs from fasting on Eid, where completing the fast inevitably involves a forbidden act, hence Abu Hanifa and Muhammad rule that starting such a fast is not binding. Finally, intercourse with a menstruating woman: the prohibition is due to harm (adha), so it does not negate the legal effects—ihsan (stoning obligation), dissolution of marriage for the first husband, dower, and iddah are established. This demonstrates that the type of 'illa (reason) behind the prohibition determines whether it nullifies the act or merely prohibits it while leaving other effects intact.

Key Terms
المضادة (al-muḍāddah): Contradiction between the purpose of an act and the prohibition, such that they cannot coexist; leads to the act being void.
موجب النهي (mūjibu al-nahy): The legal effect of a prohibition; here, it is the prohibition of disposal (ḥurmat al-taṣarruf).
النفي (al-nafy): Negation; when the prohibition is interpreted as negating the validity of the act (i.e., the act is not considered legally effective).

Key Takeaway
