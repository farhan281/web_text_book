Mutlaq and Muqayyad: The Hanafi Rule on Adding Conditions via Khabar al-Wahid
Defines mutlaq and muqayyad, states Hanafi position on adding conditions via khabar al-wahid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule regarding mutlaq and muqayyad, define both terms, and explain why khabar al-wahid cannot add conditions to absolute Quranic commands.

Core Idea
The absolute Quranic command stands alone; no zanni source can add conditions.

In this lesson, we explore a foundational usul al-fiqh rule: the absolute (mutlaq) text from the Quran cannot be restricted (muqayyad) by a solitary hadith (khabar al-wahid) or analogy (qiyas). This principle has profound implications for acts like wudu, where Hanafis do not require intention, order, or continuity based on ahad reports. We will define the key terms, examine the proof, and understand the disagreement with other schools.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Hanafi Rule
Read the first sentence: 'ذهب أصحابنا ... لايجوز'. The Hanafi position is that an absolute command from the Quran, if it can be acted upon absolutely, cannot have conditions added by khabar al-wahid or qiyas.
This is a rule about authority hierarchy: qat'i (certain) text cannot be restricted by zanni (speculative) evidence.
2
Examine the Example: Wudu Verse
The verse 'Wash your faces' is absolute. Hanafis say no conditions like niyyah, tartib, muwalat, or tasmiyah are wajib from ahad hadith.
The example shows the practical impact: Hanafi wudu does not require niyyah as a fard.
3
Define Al-Mutlaq and Al-Muqayyad
Identify the definitions: al-mutlaq is a term indicating a common individual without restriction; al-muqayyad is restricted by a description. Note the examples from kaffarah verses.
Memorize these definitions; they are standard in usul.
4
Analyze the Madhhab Dispute
The text mentions that Shafi'i, Maliki, and others allow adding conditions via khabar al-wahid. Their proof is the hadith 'innama al-a'malu binniyat'. Understand the Hanafi response: the hadith is general, not specifically imposing niyyah for wudu.
The root of the dispute is whether a zanni source can specify a qat'i absolute command.
5
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafis do not require niyyah for wudu; Shafi'is do. Also, tartib and muwalat are not fard in Hanafi school.
This shows why usul matters for fiqh rulings.

The Hanafi Rule on Mutlaq: No Obligatory Additions from Khabar al-Wahid
Hanafi rule on mutlaq from Quran and its restriction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when a Quranic mutlaq is operable, additions from khabar al-wahid or qiyas are not obligatory, and classify the status of such additions as sunnah.

Core Idea
The Quran's absolute expression is legally complete; no solitary hadith can make a condition obligatory.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi establishes a foundational Hanafi principle: the Quranic mutlaq (absolute) stands on its own. If the command can be applied in its literal sense, neither a solitary hadith nor analogy can add a binding condition. Instead, such additions are treated as non-obligatory (sunnah). We will explore this rule through definitions, examples, and the practical difference it makes in fiqh.

1
Define Mutlaq and Muqayyad

Focus on the presence or absence of an attribute.
2
State the Hanafi Rule
Identify the rule: if a Quranic mutlaq can be practiced in its absoluteness, adding an obligatory condition via khabar al-wahid or qiyas is not permissible.
This is the core memory anchor.
3
Apply to Wudu Example
'Wash your faces' is mutlaq. The passage states that conditions like intention and order are not obligatory via hadith; they are sunnah.
See how the rule preserves the Quran's obligation while accommodating the hadith.
4
Apply to Zina Hadd Example
The Quran commands 100 lashes (mutlaq). The hadith adding exile is interpreted as non-obligatory (recommended or discretionary).
The Quranic fixed punishment is complete; addition cannot be obligatory.
5

Other schools (Shafi'i, etc.) use khabar al-wahid to add obligatory conditions. The thamarah al-khilaf: e.g., niyyah in wudu is fard for Shafi'i, sunnah for Hanafi.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi is a foundational Hanafi rule regarding the interaction between the Quran and other sources. It defines two key terms: mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (qualified). The absolute is a word indicating a category without restriction, while the qualified adds a condition. The core Hanafi position stated here is that when the Quran uses an absolute expression that can be applied in its literal sense, neither a solitary hadith (khabar al-wahid) nor qiyas can impose additional conditions as obligatory. The first example is wudu: the verse commands washing, so conditions like intention (niyyah), order (tartib), continuity (muwalat), and pronouncing Bismillah (tasmiyah) are not made obligatory by hadith. Instead, the Hanafis treat these as sunnah, preserving the Quran's obligatory scope. The second example is the hadd punishment for zina: the Quran specifies one hundred lashes, but the hadith adds exile. The Hanafis argue that since the Quran's absolute punishment is complete, the exile from the hadith is not an obligatory addition but interpreted as a recommendation or a discretionary punishment. This contrasts with other schools like the Shafi'is, who use hadith to add conditions, citing 'Innama al-a'mal bi-niyyat.' The Hanafi methodology preserves the primacy of the Quran's absolutes and confines the role of lesser sources to non-obligatory matters unless the absolute cannot be applied.

Key Terms
المطلق (al-mutlaq): A word indicating a common individual or individuals without any restrictive attribute.
المقيد (al-muqayyad): A word indicating a common individual restricted by an attribute.
خبر الواحد (khabar al-wahid): A report transmitted by one or a limited number of chains not reaching tawatur.

Harmonizing Khabar with Quranic Absolute Commands
Principle of acting on khabar without altering Quranic ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a khabar adds a separate wajib without altering the Quranic mutlaq fard, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A khabar does not change the Quranic fard; it creates an independent wajib — the absolute remains intact.

When a hadith seems to add a condition to an absolute Quranic command, how does the Hanafi school reconcile the two without contradicting the Quran? This lesson explains the principle that the Quranic command remains fard, and the condition becomes a separate wajib. The passage uses examples from tawaf, ruku', and water purity to illustrate how this principle preserves the hierarchy of sources.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic mutlaq command
Locate the absolute (mutlaq) command in the passage (e.g., 'circumambulate the House', 'bow'). Note that it is unrestricted.
The command is fard by Quranic proof.
2
Identify the khabar adding a condition
Find the hadith mentioned that imposes an additional requirement (e.g., wudu for tawaf, tranquillity in ruku').
The khabar is zanni in transmission (usually ahad).
3
Apply the harmonization principle
State that the khabar does not change the Quranic mutlaq into a restricted (muqayyad) condition. Instead, the condition becomes a separate wajib obligation, while the original act remains fard.
This avoids making the hadith abrogate or specify the Quran.
4
Identify the practical consequence
Determine the effect of omitting the wajib condition: compensation (dam) is required, but the original act remains valid. Contrast with the opposing view where omission invalidates the act.

Commentary
This passage explains a key Hanafi usul principle: when a khabar (hadith) seems to add a condition to an absolute (mutlaq) command in the Quran, the khabar does not change the Quranic ruling (hukm). Instead, the Quranic command remains fard (obligatory in itself), and the additional condition becomes wajib (obligatory via the khabar). This avoids contradicting the Quran. The text gives three examples: (1) The Quranic verse about flogging for zina (Surah Nur:2) is absolute; the hadith adding exile (ghurb) does not make exile part of the hadd but a separate political/disciplinary measure. (2) The verse commanding tawaf is absolute; the hadith requiring wudu for tawaf does not make wudu a condition of the tawaf's validity as fard, but wudu becomes wajib separately—if omitted, one must sacrifice a sheep (dam) to compensate. (3) The verse commanding ruku' is absolute; the hadith requiring tranquillity (itminan) does not make itminan part of the fard ruku', but it becomes wajib. The author then applies this reasoning to water: Water mixed with a pure substance that changes one of its properties (like saffron) remains 'absolute water' (maa' mutlaq) because the additive did not remove the name 'water'. Therefore, wudu with such water is valid, as it falls under the absolute command to use water. The condition for tayammum is the absolute absence of water, so this water's existence prevents tayammum.

Key Terms
خبر (khabar): A prophetic hadith (sunna) that provides additional ruling or detail.
مطلق (mutlaq): An unqualified term in the Quran that includes any instance of the named concept without restriction.
فرض (fard): A ruling proven by a qat'i (definitive) text, such as the Quran or mutawatir sunna.
واجب (wajib): A ruling proven by a zanni (probable) source, e.g., a khabar ahad; its omission is sinful but can be compensated with dam.

The Hanafi Principle of Mutlaq and Muqayyad: The Quran Cannot Be Restricted by Ahad Hadith
Explains that mutlaq Quranic ruling is not restricted by ahad hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a mutlaq Quranic ruling is not restricted by an ahad hadith, and distinguish between a fard (from Quran) and a wajib (from ahad hadith) in the examples of ruku', wudu water, and zihar.

Core Idea
The Quranic mutlaq remains binding as is; an ahad hadith adds an independent obligation without modifying the Quranic ruling.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi usul rule: an absolute (mutlaq) text from the Quran cannot be qualified or restricted by a single-transmitter (ahad) hadith. Instead, the hadith creates a separate, additional obligation (wajib) that does not alter the original Quranic command. The passage from Usul al-Shashi uses the example of ruku' and tranquility, then applies the principle to water for wudu and the case of zihar. Understanding this rule is essential for deriving rulings in the Hanafi school and appreciating the distinct roles of the Quran and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mutlaq in the source text
Locate the act or command mentioned in the Quran that is stated without any qualification. In the passage, ruku' is the mutlaq act, and the Quran makes it obligatory (fard) without mention of tranquility.
The mutlaq here is 'ruku'', not 'ruku' with tranquility'.
2
Identify the ahad hadith and its added ruling
The hadith of the man who prayed incorrectly (narrated by Abu Hurairah and reported by Bukhari) commands tranquility (tuma'ninah) in the prayer postures. This is an ahad hadith, so its transmission is zanni.
The hadith adds the requirement of tranquility, but does not state that tranquility is a condition (shart) for the validity of ruku'.
3
Apply the Hanafi usul principle
According to Hanafi usul, the mutlaq from the Quran (ruku') remains a fard (obligation) as it is. The ahad hadith cannot restrict the Quran; instead, tranquility becomes a wajib (obligation of a slightly lower level) by the hadith. Thus, the act of ruku' has two components: the original fard and the added wajib.
This is the key distinction: fard from Quran, wajib from ahad hadith.
4
Extend the principle to the water and zihar examples
The passage applies the same logic to water mixed with pure substances: the Quran's mutlaq 'water' (used for wudu) is not restricted by the 'water sent down from the sky' (a muqayyad); the mixed water remains absolute water for wudu. For zihar, the Quran's mutlaq command to feed sixty poor is not restricted by analogy to fasting (which requires refraining from intercourse); so if the husband has intercourse during feeding, he does not restart.
In each case, the mutlaq runs on its absoluteness, and any restriction from a lesser source becomes an independent rule.
5
Note the cross-madhhab difference
Contrast the Hanafi view with the Shafi'i view: Shafi'is allow an ahad hadith to restrict the Quranic mutlaq, making tranquility a condition (shart) of ruku' rather than an additional wajib. This leads to different rulings on the validity of prayer if tranquility is omitted.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces a foundational rule: when a meaning is derived from a text by way of legal implication (iqtida'), it is confined to the minimum required by necessity. The Default and Potential Scope of Genus Nouns in Hanafi Usul
Defining the ruling of genus nouns in commands and oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a genus noun (ism al-jins) in a legal text, apply the Hanafi rule that it defaults to the lowest individual, and explain when intention can expand its scope to the entire genus.

Core Idea
A genus noun refers to the smallest instance when unqualified; the entire genus requires explicit intention.

This lesson clarifies a foundational usul principle: what does a genus noun like 'water' or 'strike' mean when used in a command or oath? Without qualification, the Hanafi school holds that it refers to the minimal manifestation. Yet the wording can also probabilify the entire genus—but only if intended. Understanding this rule is essential for deriving precise rulings from texts and avoiding misinterpretation in practical cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus noun
Locate the noun of genus (ism al-jins) in the passage, such as 'water' or 'strike'. Recognize that it denotes a category of things sharing an essence.
In this passage, the example is 'ضرب' (strike) and 'ماء' (water).
2
State the default ruling
When the genus noun is used absolutely (mutlaq, without qualifiers), its default reference is the lowest individual (adna al-jins). Memorize this as the Hanafi position.
Example: 'water' in an oath means any droplet.
3
Distinguish the two types of individuals
Recognize the real individual (al-fard al-ḥaqīqī) as the minimal instantiation, and the conceptual individual (al-fard al-iʿtibārī) as the entire genus. Intermediate amounts are not individuals of the genus.
Conceptual individual is not the default; it requires intention.
4
Apply to an oath scenario
If someone swears 'I will not drink water,' drinking a single drop breaks the oath because the genus noun defaults to the lowest individual.
Intention to swear off all water is valid, but does not change the default break.
5
Understand the role of intention (niyya)
Intention can specify the conceptual individual (entire genus) because the wording probabilifies both the lowest and the whole. But intention cannot create probabilities for intermediate amounts.
Intention clarifies an existing probability, not invents a new one.

Core Idea
In conditional expressions, the particle ب attaches the condition to the object, while أن attaches it to the act of informing.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi presents a classic example of how subtle differences in Arabic phrasing can produce opposite legal outcomes. By examining two nearly identical conditional statements about freeing a slave, we learn to identify when truthfulness is implicitly required and when it is not.
