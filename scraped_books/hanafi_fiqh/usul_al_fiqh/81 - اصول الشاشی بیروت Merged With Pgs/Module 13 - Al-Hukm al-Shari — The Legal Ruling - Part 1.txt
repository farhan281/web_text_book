The Principle of Mahjura (Abandoned Usage) in Hanafi Usul
Explains effect of abandoned usage on oaths and agency

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a term or usage as mahjura and apply the Hanafi rule that it is interpreted according to prevailing custom (urf), not its original literal meaning.

Core Idea
An abandoned legal term is interpreted by custom, not its obsolete literal sense.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, words and phrases that have fallen out of customary or legal use do not retain their original precise meanings. This lesson explores the principle of mahjura (abandoned usage) through two examples: oaths and agency. The source passage demonstrates how custom overrides literal interpretation, ensuring that legal intent aligns with common understanding.

Guided Path
1
Define Mahjura
State that mahjura refers to a word or usage that has been abandoned by common custom or legal practice.
It is not merely obsolete language; it includes any expression whose literal sense is no longer operative.
2
Identify the Two Examples
Locate the oath example: 'not to place his foot' in a house. Then locate the agency example: 'agency for the lawsuit itself'.
Both terms are considered mahjura in their respective contexts.
3
Apply the Rule to the Oath
Since 'placing the foot' is mahjura, the oath is interpreted according to custom: not to enter the house. Literal foot placement does not breach the oath.
This is agreed upon by the scholars.
4
Apply the Rule to Agency
Since 'agency for the lawsuit itself' is mahjura both legally and customarily, it is interpreted broadly as agency for any response, allowing the agent to answer 'yes' or 'no'.
The precise technical meaning is disregarded.

The Rule: Hudud Not Established by Kinayah
States a usul rule with illustrative examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that hudud are not established by kinayah and apply it to identify whether a given expression suffices for hadd.

Core Idea
Ambiguity in expression blocks hudud — only explicit words (sarih) trigger fixed punishments.

In Hanafi usul, fixed penalties (hudud) require absolute clarity. This lesson presents a fundamental rule: indirect or ambiguous expressions (kinayah) cannot serve as the basis for a hadd punishment. The reasoning is the presence of taraddud (ambiguity), which undermines legal certainty. The passage illustrates this with three concrete scenarios — confession, mute's gesture, and response to accusation — each showing that without an explicit sarih term, the hadd is not applied.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Read the first phrase: 'because of the presence of the meaning of ambiguity in kinayah, punishments are not established by it.' This is the rule: hudud require clarity; kinayah is not enough.
Kinayah = indirect expression that hints but does not state explicitly.
2
Analyze the first example: confession
If someone confesses to zina or theft using kinayah, the hadd is not applied unless 'he mentions the explicit word' (al-lafz al-sarih). Recognize that the condition for hadd is verbal sarih.
The explicit word for zina would be 'zina' itself or a clear equivalent.
3
Analyze the second example: mute's gesture
The rule is extended: a mute's gesture, being a form of kinayah, cannot establish hadd. This reinforces the principle that ambiguous indication is insufficient.
Gestures are considered kinayah because they are indirect.
4
Analyze the third example: response 'Sadaqta'
When accused of zina, if the accused says 'You have spoken the truth' (Sadaqta), the hadd is not imposed because the statement could be confirming something else (e.g., the accuser's general honesty). The ambiguity prevents legal certainty.
The response is kinayah; it does not explicitly admit guilt.
5
Extract the underlying principle (talil)
The common thread is ambiguity (taraddud). The illah (cause) is the presence of doubt in the expression. Hudud are ward off by doubts (shubuhat), so kinayah triggers a doubt.
This aligns with the broader Hanafi maxim: 'Hudud are repelled by doubts.'

Freeing Mukatab vs Mudabbar for Kaffarah: The Role of Complete Manumission
Deriving a fiqh ruling from the completeness of slavery.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the concept of complete (kāmil) vs deficient (nāqiṣ) riqq to determine whether freeing a slave fulfills the requirement of tahrir in kaffarah, and derive the ruling using Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Freeing a slave for expiation requires complete removal of slavery; deficient slavery fails the requirement.

This passage from Uṣūl al-Shāshī demonstrates how a single concept—completeness of slavery—determines the validity of a legal act. The author explains why freeing a mukatab counts for expiation but freeing a mudabbar or umm walad does not, despite all being slaves. Understanding this distinction requires grasping the legal effect of a contingent right to freedom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation in kaffarah
The text states that the obligation (wājib) is tahrīr—complete manumission. Recognize that not every freeing counts; only that which fully removes slavery.
Tahrir is defined as 'Ithbat al-hurriyya bi-izalat al-riqq'.
2
Define the condition for valid tahrir
For tahrir to be complete, the slave's riqq must be complete (kāmil). If riqq is deficient (nāqiṣ), freeing does not remove slavery from every aspect.
Complete riqq means the slave is fully owned with no automatic termination of slavery.
3
Apply the condition to mukatab and mudabbar
A mukatab has complete riqq despite his contract; his freeing is valid. A mudabbar has deficient riqq because his freedom is contingent on the master's death; his freeing is invalid for kaffarah.
The same applies to umm walad: her riqq is deficient due to automatic freedom at master's death.
4
Derive the fiqh ruling
From the condition, conclude: freeing a mukatab fulfills kaffarah; freeing a mudabbar or umm walad does not.
This is a clear example of istinbat from a legal definition.

Freeing Slaves for Expiation: Complete vs. Incomplete Riqq
Ruling with talil on manumission for expiation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on freeing a mukatab versus a mudabbir or umm walad for expiation and explain the underlying legal reasoning (talil) based on the concept of complete vs. incomplete slavery.

Core Idea
Only a slave with complete riqq qualifies for expiation tahrir; mudabbir and umm walad have incomplete riqq.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi presents a clear ruling on which types of slaves can be freed for expiation (kaffarah). The Hanafi school distinguishes between slaves with complete slavery (mukatab) and those with incomplete slavery (mudabbir, umm walad). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying the rules of tahrir in expiation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its ruling
The act is manumission (itq) for the purpose of expiating an oath or zihar. The ruling: valid for mukatab, invalid for mudabbir and umm walad.
2
Identify the wajib (obligatory act) and its definition
The wajib is tahrir (liberation), which is defined as establishing freedom by completely removing slavery (izalat al-riqq).
Tahrir is the complete removal, not a partial one.
3
Analyze the condition of completeness of slavery
For tahrir to be valid, the slave must have full riqq at the time of manumission. Mukatab has full riqq (even though he is working towards freedom), while mudabbir and umm walad have incomplete riqq (their freedom is already anticipated).
The key is whether the slave is fully owned at the moment of manumission.
4
Apply the condition to the three categories
Mukatab: his slavery is complete until he pays off his kitabah, so freeing him removes slavery completely. Mudabbir: his freedom is contingent on the master's death, so his slavery is partial; freeing him does not achieve complete removal. Umm walad: similarly, she becomes free upon the master's death, so her slavery is also incomplete.
5
Conclude the ruling and its talil
Therefore, only freeing a mukatab fulfills the requirement of tahrir, while freeing a mudabbir or umm walad does not, because their slavery is incomplete.
This is based on the principle that the wajib must be discharged in full.

Context Overrides Default Obligation in Commands
Stating the rule that context determines whether a command is obligatory, with examples from wakalah and the fly hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that context (siyāq al-kalām) can override the literal implication of an imperative command, and identify when a command is for worldly benefit rather than religious obligation.

Core Idea
Context determines whether a command is obligatory: an imperative for worldly benefit is not binding.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the imperative form (amr) is not automatically obligatory. This lesson examines how context (siyāq al-kalām) reveals the true intent behind a command, using the well-known fly hadith and examples from the law of agency (wakāla). Understanding this principle is essential to avoid misinterpreting the Shari'a.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Imperative and Its Context
Locate the imperative verb in the source text (e.g., 'dip it' in the fly hadith; 'buy' in the wakalah examples). Then note any accompanying phrases that provide context—here, the purpose ('to serve', 'to have intercourse', or the mention of disease and cure).
2
Classify the Textual Feature
Recognize that the operative phrase is an imperative (ṣīghat al-amr). In Hanafi usul, the default ruling of an imperative is obligation (wujūb), but this default can be overridden by context.
3
Apply the Hanafi Principle on Context
Apply the principle: 'Al-amr yufīdu al-wujūb illā bi qarīna' – the imperative indicates obligation unless there is a contextual indicator (qarīna). Determine whether the surrounding text provides such an indicator.
In the fly hadith, the reason given (disease and cure) reveals a worldly purpose, so the qarīna is present.
4
Derive the Ruling
Based on the presence or absence of a qarīna, derive the ruling: if context shows worldly benefit, the command is not obligatory (e.g., recommended or permissible). If no such context, the obligation stands.
5
Test with Alternate Reading
Consider how a different usul methodology (e.g., Shafi'i) might treat the same text. This highlights the Hanafi reliance on even implicit contextual clues.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A wise speaker's command is interpreted by His wisdom, not by naked literalism.

In Hanafi usul, the literal meaning of a word is primary, but it is not absolute. When the speaker's known attributes or context clearly indicate that a literal reading leads to an absurd or improper conclusion, the literal meaning is abandoned. This lesson introduces the fourth rule: abandoning al-ḥaqīqa due to an indication from the speaker (dalāla min qibal al-mutakallim). We will examine the classic Quranic example and see how this principle governs real-world agency transactions.

This is a core principle in Hanafi usul.

The imperative form 'falyakfur' is literal, but it conflicts with Allah's wisdom.
3
Understand the reasoning
Allah is wise and does not command evil. Therefore, the literal meaning is abandoned and the verse is interpreted as a threat (tahdīd) or permission.
This reasoning uses ḥikmat al-āmir (the commander's wisdom) as a qarīna.

The principal's circumstances indicate his real intent, overriding the literal command.
5
Generalize the principle
This principle extends to any case where the speaker's known attributes or situation contradict a literal reading. The interpreter must give priority to the evident purpose (maqsad) over the literal wording.

Command and Repetition of Obligation
Explaining how command applies to the genus and repetition arises from cause

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Repetition of obligation arises from the cause, not from the command itself.

In this passage from Usul al-Shashi, the author clarifies a foundational Hanafi principle: a single command does not demand repeated performance. Instead, repetition is triggered by the recurrence of the cause (sabab). Understanding this distinction is key to grasping how obligations like the daily prayers are structured.

1
State the Author's Thesis
Identify the main claim: command applies to the genus of an act, not to each individual instance. Repetition of the obligation follows from repetition of its cause.

2

The author compares the shari'ah command to everyday commands like 'pay the price' or 'pay maintenance'. Consider how these commands are understood: they demand fulfillment of a specific duty that arises from a particular cause (e.g., sale, marriage).
This analogy grounds the abstract usul rule in tangible examples.

Absolute Commands: Delay or Immediacy?
Classifying commands as time-bound or absolute with rulings and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a command as absolute (mutlaq) or time-bound (muqayyad) and apply the Hanafi rule that absolute commands permit delay (tarakhi) as long as the act can be performed within one's lifetime.

Core Idea
An absolute command does not require immediate action; delay is permitted until the end of one's life.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces a fundamental distinction in Hanafi usul: commands can be either bound to a specific time or left absolute. Understanding this distinction is crucial because it determines whether delaying an obligation incurs sin. The relied-upon Hanafi position is that absolute commands may be delayed (tarakhi), and the passage provides practical examples from vows, zakat, and make-up prayers to illustrate the principle. It also reports a minority view from al-Karkhi that demands immediacy (fawr).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of commands
The passage divides al-ma'mur bihi into mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (time-bound). An absolute command has no specified time; a time-bound command does.
2
State the Hanafi ruling for an absolute command
Performance is wajib but with delay (ala al-tarakhi) on condition that it is not missed within one's lifetime. If the act becomes impossible (e.g., death, destruction of nisab), the obligation lapses.
3
Examine the practical examples
Imam Muhammad's examples: vows of i'tikaf or fasting may be performed in any month; zakat delayed does not make one negligent; an oath-breaker who becomes poor may expiate by fasting; make-up prayers are not allowed in disliked times because the original performance was time-bound.
These examples demonstrate that delay is permissible without sin.
4
Locate the ikhtilaf
Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi held that an absolute command requires immediacy (fawr). His basis is the literal meaning of the imperative (amr).
Al-Karkhi is a major Hanafi figure, but his view is not the relied-upon position.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: delaying a vow for years is not sinful according to the Hanafi position; al-Karkhi would consider it sinful. The mufta bihi is tarakhi.
This affects fatwa on delayed obligations.

Two Types of Time-Bound Obligations: Container vs Measure

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish between the container (al-zarf) and measure (al-mi'yar) types of time-bound obligations in Hanafi usul, and apply their respective rulings to new scenarios.

Core Idea
Time-bound obligations are either a container (capacity for multiple acts) or a measure (defines the act itself).

In Hanafi usul, obligations that are tied to a specific time (al-muwaqqat) are not all the same. The author of Usul al-Shashi divides them into two fundamental types based on how time relates to the act. Understanding this division is key to deriving rulings about when multiple similar acts can be performed in the same time slot, when intention is required, and how the act's performance is affected by time expansion or contraction. Let us dissect the passage step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the overarching division
The text states that time-bound obligations (al-muwaqqat) are of two types. Read the first sentence after 'وأما الموقت فنوعان' to note the two categories: 'الظرف' (container) and 'المعيار' (measure).
These are technical terms in Hanafi usul; memorize their definitions.
2
Define the container type (al-zarf)
The container type means time is like a vessel: the act occurs in it but is not measured by it. Look for the description: 'الوقت ظرفاً للفعل'. This type can accommodate multiple acts of the same type (e.g., multiple prayers in Zuhr time).
Also called 'musa'' (expansive).
3
Define the measure type (al-mi'yar)
The measure type means time defines the act itself: the act is measured by the time. Look for 'الوقت معياراً له'. This type cannot accommodate another act of the same type (e.g., fasting: one fast per day).
Also called 'mudayyiq' (restrictive).
4

For the container, note: (a) obligation does not prevent another obligation of the same type; (b) validity of another similar act is not prevented; (c) a specific intention is required even if time is narrow. For the measure, note: (a) the act increases or decreases with time; (b) it cannot accommodate another act of its type.
These rulings follow directly from the definition.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi discusses the classification of time-bound obligations (al-muwaqqat). The author divides them into two types: the container (al-zarf) and the measure (al-mi'yar). The container type, exemplified by prayer, means that the time is like a container that can hold multiple acts. Core Idea
A restricted obligation demands specific intention because time is a standard that accommodates only one act, yet competition remains even when time narrows.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, obligations are classified based on the time available for their performance. Some acts are restricted (mudayyaq) to a time that cannot accommodate another act of the same nature. This passage from Usul al-Shashi defines this concept and divides it into two types: designated and non-designated. Understanding this classification is crucial for determining the role of intention (niyya) and the validity of performing multiple acts in the same time window.

Types of Time-Bound Obligations and the Role of Intention
Explaining two types of time-bound wajib and the effect of intention when obligations conflict.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-wajib al-mudiq al-mubayyan and al-wajib al-mudiq ghayr al-mu'ayyan, and apply the Hanafi rule regarding intention when obligations conflict.

Core Idea
When Shari'ah fixes a time for an obligation, that time overrides the servant's intention; absent a fixed time, the servant's specification does not bind.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi clarifies a subtle but critical distinction in Hanafi usul: what happens when a time-bound obligation has a fixed time versus when it does not? Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the rules of intention (niyya) when multiple obligations coincide. Let's explore the two types and the role of ta'yin al-niyyah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Recognize the two types of time-bound wajib mentioned: al-wajib al-mudiq al-mubayyan (fixed time, e.g., Ramadan fast) and al-wajib al-mudiq ghayr al-mu'ayyan (non-fixed time, e.g., qada' of Ramadan).
The key is whether Shari'ah assigned a specific time or left it open.
2
Understand the ruling for fixed-time
For fixed-time wajib: if a healthy resident performs any other wajib during that time, it automatically counts for the fixed-time wajib, not what he intended. The requirement of specification (ta'yin) drops.
Example: fasting during Ramadan for a vowed fast counts for Ramadan.
3
Understand the ruling for non-fixed-time
For non-fixed-time wajib: the servant's specification of a time does not make that time exclusive. He can perform other wajib (e.g., kaffarah, nafl) during those days, and can fulfill the original obligation on other days.
Example: specifying days for qada' of Ramadan does not bind those days exclusively.
4
Analyze the role of niyya vs ta'yin
In both cases, original intention (niyya) is required for the act to be valid. However, ta'yin (specifying which obligation) is only needed when there is a conflict (muzahama). The fixed time removes the need for ta'yin.
The servant cannot change a hukm shar'i by his own specification.
5
Apply the principle to vows and khul'
If someone vows to fast a specific day and fasts it for another wajib (qada', kaffarah), it counts for that other wajib because the Shari'ah's ruling (absolute qada') prevails. For nafl, it counts for the vow because nafl is the servant's right. Similarly, in khul', the stipulation can drop financial rights but not relational ones like housing.
This shows consistency: servant's actions affect his own rights, not fixed Shari'ah rulings.

The Subject Cannot Change a Shariah Ruling
Explains the principle and tests it with examples

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a mukallaf cannot alter an established Shariah ruling, and distinguish between rights of Allah (huquq Allah) and rights of individuals (huquq al-'ibad).

Core Idea
A legal subject can bind himself but cannot override a divine ruling.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces a foundational principle: human beings can create obligations upon themselves through vows or contracts, but they cannot modify or nullify any ruling that Allah has established. The key distinction is between rights that belong to the Shariah (huquq Allah) and rights that belong to individuals (huquq al-'ibad). Learning Objective

Core Idea
An obligation good in itself drops only by performance or by the Lawgiver's decree — not by hardship alone.

Compensation by the Like (Jabr bi al-Mithl) and Its Limitations
States the rule of jabr bi al-mithl and applies it to various cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of jabr bi al-mithl to identify which deficiencies in ibadat and muamalat can be compensated and which cannot, and explain the intra-madhhab dispute regarding the compensation of quality.

Core Idea
A deficiency is compensable only by its legal like; if no like exists, the deficiency remains except for sin.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces the foundational Hanafi principle of compensation by the like (jabr bi al-mithl). It governs how deficiencies in acts of worship and transactions are resolved. Understanding this principle is crucial for distinguishing between irreparable defects and those that can be remedied by a substitute, as well as for grasping the limits of legal equivalence in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the general rule
The passage opens with the rule: if it is possible to compensate a deficiency (naqs) by its like (mithl), then it is compensated; otherwise, the ruling of deficiency falls except for sin (ithm).
This is the core principle; note that 'sin' refers to any remaining moral culpability if the act was prohibited.
2
Identify the condition for compensation
Compensation by the like requires that a legal equivalent exists in the same genus or category. The passage illustrates this by contrasting cases where a like exists (e.g., dam for tawaf, sajdat al-sahw for wajibat) and where it does not (e.g., leaving a rukn in salah, quality of a coin).
The existence of a like is not physical but legal; it is established by Shari'ah or custom.
3
Apply to acts of worship
Work through each example: adjusting pillars (arkan) has no like → prayer becomes invalid; leaving wajibat (Fatiha, Qunut, Tashahhud, Eid takbirs) is compensable by sujud al-sahw; tawaf without wudu is compensable by dam.
Note the distinction: rukn vs wajib. A missing rukn destroys the act; a missing wajib can be repaired.
4
Apply to transactions and note the dispute
In the bad coin case: if a bad coin is lost at the creditor, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say no claim because quality (joodah) has no independent like. Abu Yusuf holds that the creditor can return a similar coin and demand good ones. The passage presents Abu Hanifa's view as the relied-upon position.
This dispute hinges on whether quality is a compensable attribute. The Hanafi rule limits compensation to what has a recognized mithl.

The Principle of Ada' vs Qada' in Ghasb: Hanafi and Shafi'i Views
States the usul principle and contrasts Shafi'i and Hanafi rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi distinction between ada' and qada' in cases of usurpation and compare it with the Shafi'i position, identifying the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
Primary obligation is returning the specific object; only when impossible does compensation apply.

This passage introduces a foundational usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: what is the obligation of a usurper when the original object has been fundamentally transformed? Both schools agree on the principle that the primary duty (ada') is to return the specific object (ayn) itself. However, they diverge on when this duty transforms into compensation (qada'). Let us trace the reasoning of each school through the text and see how the Hanafi position incorporates istihsan.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Initial Principle
Read the first sentence: 'الأصل هو الأداء'. Recognize that both schools begin from the same usul axiom: the primary legal duty is performance (ada') by returning the specific object. Understand that recourse to compensation (qada') is only permissible when ada' is impossible.
This principle governs not only ghasb but also wadi'ah and wakalah.
2
Trace the Shafi'i Derivation
Examine the section 'تفريع الإمام الشافعي'. Al-Shafi'i applies the principle strictly: even extreme transformations (grinding, building, slaughtering) do not extinguish the owner's right in the specific object. The usurper must return the transformed object plus compensation for deficiency (arsh).
For al-Shafi'i, the obligation remains ada' in all circumstances; qada' has no role here.
3
Contrast the Hanafi Response
Now look at 'مذهبنا'. The Hanafi school agrees on the principle but introduces a qualification: when transformation is so extreme that the object's identity is destroyed (e.g., wheat into flour), the obligation shifts from ada' to qada'. The usurper becomes owner of the transformed object and pays its value.
This shift is based on istihsan and practical impossibility of reversal.
4
Distinguish Two Categories within Hanafi
Note the examples in the Hanafi text: silver struck into coins, bullion into dinars, sheep slaughtered – in these, the owner's right is severed (zahir al-riwaya). But for cotton spun into yarn or yarn woven into cloth, the owner's right is not severed. Recognize the subtlety: not all transformations are treated equally.
The distinction may relate to whether the transformation is a change of genus or merely a change of form.
5
Consider the Thamarah al-Khilaf
Finally, reflect on the practical consequence (thamarah) of this dispute. For the same case (e.g., usurped wheat ground into flour), the Hanafi school gives the flour to the usurper with payment of value, while the Shafi'i school gives the flour to the owner with compensation for deficiency.
This contrast highlights how a difference in usul reasoning directly affects judicial rulings.

Core Idea
Compensation is either matching in form and meaning (kamil) or only in meaning (qasir); the type depends on whether the item is fungible (mithli) or unique (qimi).

Understanding these definitions is essential for determining how liability is calculated in property disputes.

Guided Path

The standard is market availability at the time of obligation, not future possibility.

The sheep example shows that even if sheep exist in markets, they vary in quality, so they are qimi.

Litigation and the Non-Compensability of Usufructs in Hanafi Law
Rule connecting litigation to compensation, and the Hanafi position on usufructs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafi law does not require compensation for usufructs destroyed through use of misappropriated property, and contrast this with the Shafi'i position, identifying the practical consequence of the difference.

Core Idea
Usufructs have no equivalent in form or meaning, so they cannot be compensated; liability for value arises only when the exact equivalent is unavailable at litigation.

This passage builds on the definitions of compensation to address a key controversy: are usufructs (manafi') compensable? The Hanafi school, based on reasoning from the impossibility of finding an equivalent for usufruct, holds that no damages are owed. The Shafi'i school disagrees. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to apply usul al-fiqh principles to practical cases.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule regarding litigation
Read the sentence: 'al-asl fi al-qada al-kamil al-khusumah.' This establishes that the basis for requiring complete compensation is the existence of a legal dispute (khusumah). Then focus on Abu Hanifa's statement: liability for value is assessed on the day of litigation because only then does the inability to deliver the exact equivalent become apparent.
This is a procedural rule: valuation is not fixed at the time of misappropriation but at litigation.
2
Analyze the reasoning for usufructs
Examine the sentence: 'fa amma ma la mithl lahu surah wa la ma'nan la yumkin ijab al-qada fih.' This is a general principle. It is applied to usufructs: since usufruct has no equivalent in form (it is intangible) and no equivalent in meaning (the usufruct of a slave is not the same as the usufruct of a house), compensation is impossible. The Hanafi conclusion: 'al-manafi' la tudman bi al-itlaf.'
Two reasons: (1) no mithl possible because usufructs lack material form; (2) no 'ayn possible because the object itself is not the usufruct.
3
Examine the example
The example clarifies: using a misappropriated slave (employing him), riding an animal, or living in a house are acts that destroy usufruct but do not require compensation in Hanafi law. Understand that the usufruct is considered a non-entity in terms of liability.
This is not about damaging the item itself – that would require compensation.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
The passage notes 'wa 'inda al-Shafi'i tudman al-manafi' bi al-itlaf.' This is the opposing view. Reflect on why Shafi'is may consider usufructs compensable – they likely value the benefit that was lost.
The Shafi'i view is presented as a counterpoint; the text does not provide their full proof.
5
Identify the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Consider a real scenario: if someone lives in your house without permission for a month, Hanafi says no payment for the use; Shafi'i requires payment of market rent for that month. This is the practical impact of the differing positions.
This difference is significant in property disputes.

Classification of Prohibition: Sensory vs. Shar'i Acts
Defining two types of prohibition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sensory and shar'i prohibitions and classify a given prohibition into one of the two types.

Core Idea
Some prohibitions are known by intellect, others only by revelation.

Not all prohibitions are the same. The Hanafi usul divides al-nahy into two categories based on the nature of the act: sensory acts, which are inherently evil and recognized universally, and shar'i acts, whose ruling depends entirely on divine revelation. This classification helps us understand the epistemological basis of prohibitions—whether they derive from reason or from revealed law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Read the passage and note that the author divides prohibition into two types: prohibition of sensory acts (al-af‘āl al-ḥissiyya) and prohibition of shar‘i acts (al-taṣarrufāt al-shar‘iyya).
2
Understand the basis of sensory prohibitions
Sensory acts are those whose evil is known by sense perception or universal reason, independent of revelation. Examples: adultery, murder, lying.
These are ma‘qūl al-ma‘nā (rationally intelligible).
3
Understand the basis of shar‘i prohibitions
Shar‘i acts are those whose moral status depends entirely on revelation. Their prohibition cannot be known by intellect alone. Example: fasting on the Day of Sacrifice.
These are ta‘abbudī (purely devotional).
4
Apply the classification
Given a new act, determine if its evil is known by intellect before revelation or only after. This determines whether the prohibition is sensory or shar‘i.
Use the text's examples as a guide.
5
Recognize the usul significance
This classification affects how we derive rulings: sensory prohibitions may have a rational basis for qiyas, while shar‘i prohibitions are limited to the text.
Important for later usul discussions.

Muḍāddah vs External Prohibition in Contracts
Rational proof and examples distinguishing muḍāddah from external prohibition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether a prohibition nullifies a contract or renders it fasid, and apply the rational proof that the act remains legitimate after prohibition.

Core Idea
A prohibition only voids a contract if it opposes its essential nature; otherwise the act remains valid but defective.

In Hanafi usul, not every prohibition renders a contract void. Some prohibitions target the essence of the contract, making it impossible to combine them, while others are external, leaving the contract essentially valid but defective. This passage from Imam al-Shashi's Usul provides the rational basis for this distinction and illustrates it with examples from sales, marriages, and vows. Understanding this principle is crucial for correct derivation of rulings in contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rational argument
The text argues: if a prohibition entirely nullified the legitimacy of an act, the servant would be commanded to do something impossible (since the act is required for some purpose), which is impossible from the Lawgiver. Therefore, the act remains legitimate despite the prohibition.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) that sets the general principle.
2
Distinguish muḍāddah from external prohibition
The text differentiates between prohibitions that oppose the essence of the contract (e.g., marriage prohibitions) and those that are external (e.g., a prohibited condition in a sale). In muḍāddah, the combination of the contract's essence and the prohibition is impossible, so the contract is void. In external prohibitions, the essence can coexist with the prohibition, so the contract is merely defective (fasid).
Look at the examples: marriage of mahram vs. defective sale.
3
Analyze the defective sale example
The text states that a defective sale (bayʿ fāsid) confers ownership upon possession because it is still a sale in essence, even though the act of possession is prohibited due to an external cause. The opposing factor is the prohibition of handling the item, but ownership can coexist with that prohibition (like fermented grape juice).
This shows that the essence of sale (ownership transfer) is retained.

Core Idea
A prohibited act can still be legally effective; sin does not nullify legal consequences.

In Hanafi usul, an act may be forbidden (ḥarām) yet still produce its intended legal result. This principle distinguishes between the moral wrongness and the legal validity of an action. The passage from Usul al-Shashi clarifies that rulings such as divorce, prayer, or sale remain intact even when performed under prohibition, challenging the assumption that prohibition automatically voids the act.

Guided Path
1

Read the central statement: 'Wa ḥurmat al-fiʿl lā tunāfī tarattub al-aḥkām.' Understand that this is the rule: prohibition does not prevent the attachment of legal rulings.
The principle is absolute in Hanafi thought; exceptions exist where prohibition invalidates (e.g., lack of ownership).

List the six examples given: divorce during menstruation, ablution with usurped water, hunting/ slaughter with usurped tools, prayer on usurped land, and sale during the call to prayer. For each, note that the act is prohibited yet legally effective.

Focus on the istinbāṭ from Quran 24:4. The verse says 'do not accept their testimony' (lā taqbalū shahādatahum). Learning Objective

Core Idea
When fa indicates cause, the legal effect is immediate—the action is not required.

In Hanafi usul, the Arabic letter 'fa' can serve more than a sequential function. It may indicate that the first part of a statement is the cause of the second part's legal effect. Same principle: cause is the utterance, not the action.
4
State the Legal Outcome
In both cases, the legal effect (freedom / aman) occurs at the moment of utterance, not upon performance. The 'fa' indicates that the preceding statement is the effective cause.
This is the Hanafi position; some other schools may interpret differently.
5

If 'fa' were merely sequential, the effect would depend on the action. For example, 'He arrived then I honored him' indicates sequence: honor follows arrival. But in ta'lil, the cause is the statement itself.
Context and linguistic clues help determine which function is intended.

Hanafi Principles of Conditional Phrases in Manumission and Divorce
Examples of conditional statements and their legal outcomes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that certain conditional promises (e.g., 'give a thousand then you are free') take immediate effect, and differentiate the legal outcomes of various wordings in divorce delegation based on the examples given.

Core Idea
Conditional phrases may be immediately fulfilled; divorce delegation wording precisely determines ba'in, raj'i, or multiple divorces.

This passage from al-Jami' presents several key examples from Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The first two show that a condition attached to a promise of freedom or safe conduct is treated as fulfilled at the moment of utterance. The remaining examples demonstrate that in divorce delegation, the exact sequence and phrasing of words dictate whether the divorce is ba'in (irrevocable), raj'i (revocable), or multiple. Understanding these distinctions is essential for properly applying Hanafi legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional structure in each example
For the slave, the phrase 'if you give a thousand then you are free' makes freedom conditional on payment. For the harbi, 'descend and you are safe' makes safety conditional on descending. In the divorce cases, the husband places the wife's affair in the agent's hand with various wordings.
Notice the syntactic patterns: 'if... then...' and the use of 'and' (واو) or 'فاء'.
2
Apply the Hanafi principle of immediate effect for certain conditions
The Hanafi school rules that in manumission and aman, such conditions are considered fulfilled immediately. The master's utterance itself creates the freedom, regardless of actual payment or descent.
This differs from conditions in other contracts where performance is required.
3
Distinguish the divorce delegation outcomes based on wording
The passage lists three wording variants: (1) 'Place her affair in your hand and divorce' → one ba'in divorce. (2) 'Divorce and place her affair in your hand' → one raj'i divorce. (3) 'Divorce and I have placed her affair in your hand' → two divorces. Also 'I divorced her and made her ban' yields two.
The sequence and tense alter the number and type of divorces.
4
Extract the underlying usul rule
The examples illustrate that in Hanafi law, the literal wording of conditional phrases directly determines legal consequences. This reflects the principle 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' but with strict adherence to linguistic form.
This is a memorization anchor for the rule that some conditions are deemed fulfilled instantly.

The Three Categories of Legal Reports and Their Conditions
Defining conditions for accepting reports in three legal areas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three categories of reports (akhbar) in Hanafi usul and state the condition for each: number, justice, or both.

Core Idea
Reports in law are not all equal: some require multiple just witnesses, others accept a single sinful reporter.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi lays out a fundamental classification in Hanafi evidence law: the conditions for accepting reports (akhbar) vary by legal context. Understanding this classification is crucial for a faqih to correctly assess testimony and reports in different cases, from disputes to everyday transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories
The passage explicitly names the second, third, and fourth categories (the first is presumably covered earlier). For each, note the condition: (2) number and justice, (3) report of a single person regardless of justice, (4) either number or justice.
Memorize the condition for each: 2=both, 3=neither (just one), 4=either/or.
2
Associate each category with an example
Each category has a typical application: second (disputes/munaza'at), third (transactions/mu'amalat), fourth (disqualification/'azl and interdiction/hajr). Connect the condition with the legal area's needs.
Transactions are informal, so a single report suffices; disputes require heavier proof.
3
Understand the Hanafi nuance in the fourth category
Imam Abu Hanifa requires either number OR justice for the fourth category. This means that either multiple witnesses (even if not all just) or a single just witness is acceptable. Contrast with the second category requiring both.
This shows the principle of flexibility in Hanafi usul.

Cessation of Cause: Purification by Removing the Substance
Ruling on purification using vinegar and cause-based reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that removing the cause (ʿillah) of impurity restores original purity (tubriyah asliyyah).

Core Idea
Impurity is caused by presence of najis; remove it, original purity returns.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi usul rule: the impurity of an object is not inherent but due to the presence of a najis substance. Once that substance is removed, original purity is restored—even if removed with vinegar, not water. This principle also explains why water is uniquely required for hadath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule
The author states: if a garment washed with vinegar and impurity is removed, it is ruled pure because the cause of impurity has ceased.
The rule is directly stated in the passage.
2
Analyze the Cause (ʿIllah)
The cause of impurity is the presence of the najis substance in the object, not that the object itself is impure.
This is clarified by the gloss: 'ʿillat kawnihi najisan qiyām al-najis fī al-maḥall.'
3
Apply the Rule
When the cause is removed, original purity returns. Vinegar removes the substance, so the garment becomes pure.
No need for water; any substance that eliminates the najis works for khabath.
4
Contrast with Hadath
Hadath is a legal state, not physical; vinegar cannot remove it because its cause is not a substance. Water is uniquely the mutahhir for hadath.
This distinction is key to understanding Hanafi purifications.

Distinguishing Hadath and Khabath: The Role of the Cause
Distinction between hadath and khabath using the cause-based approach

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the difference between hadath and khabath in Hanafi fiqh and state why vinegar can remove one but not the other.

Core Idea
Hadath is a legal state; khabath is a physical substance. Only water removes hadath.

The passage draws a critical distinction between two types of impurity: hadath (ritual impurity, like after using the bathroom or after sexual relations) and khabath (physical impurity, like urine or blood). Vinegar can remove the physical substance (khabath) but cannot remove the legal state (hadath). This lesson explores the underlying usul principle that makes this distinction necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Types of Impurity
The text explicitly names hadath and khabath as distinct categories. Hadath is a legal state; khabath is a physical substance.
Hadath requires wudu or ghusl; khabath requires washing the affected area.
2
Understand the Role of the Cause
For khabath, the cause (ʿillah) is the presence of the najis substance. For hadath, the cause is not a substance but a legal description (e.g., breaking wudu).
Thus, removing the substance removes khabath; only water (al-mutahhir) removes hadath.
3
Apply the Distinction to Vinegar
Vinegar can remove the substance (khabath) but cannot remove the legal state (hadath). Therefore, vinegar-purified garment is pure from khabath, but a person in hadath cannot substitute vinegar for water.
This is the practical thamarah al-khilaf.
4
Recognize the Usul Principle
The principle: 'al-ʿillah idha zālat zāla al-hukm' applies to khabath but not to hadath, because hadath's 'cause' is not a physical entity that can be removed by vinegar.
This shows how usul principles are applied differently based on the nature of the hukm.

The Effect of Personal Opinion on Hadd and Shubha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that personal opinion (ra'y) can create a valid doubt (shubha) that averts hadd when no textual proof exists, and identify the conditions under which lineage is not established.

Core Idea
Personal opinion creates a legally operative doubt in the absence of text, averting hadd but not establishing lineage.

In this lesson, we examine a short passage from Usul al-Shashi that illustrates a critical principle: when a textual proof is absent, a person's sincere opinion (ra'y) can generate a doubt (shubha) that prevents the application of a fixed punishment (hadd). However, this same doubt does not extend to establishing lineage. This teaches us the precise boundaries of operative personal judgment in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path

Identify the Two Scenarios
Read the passage and note the two statements: 'I thought she was unlawful' and 'I thought she was lawful.' Observe the different rulings on hadd for each.
The first scenario leads to hadd, the second to no hadd.
2
Analyze the Reasoning (Ta'lil)
The passage explains why: 'because the doubt of ownership in the father's property is not established by text, so his opinion is considered.' This shows that in the absence of a textual proof (nass), personal opinion can be a valid basis for doubt.
Shubhat al-milk is not textually established here, so ra'y fills the gap.
3
Understand the Scope
Note that even if the man claims the child, lineage is not established. This demonstrates that the doubt created by opinion has limits: it can avert punishment but cannot create a positive legal status like lineage.
The doubt is sufficient to negate hadd but insufficient to prove lineage.

Generalize the Principle
Extract the usul rule: In the absence of a clear textual prohibition or permission, a person's sincere personal opinion can create a shubha that prevents the application of hadd. This is based on the maxim 'Drive away hadd by doubts.'
