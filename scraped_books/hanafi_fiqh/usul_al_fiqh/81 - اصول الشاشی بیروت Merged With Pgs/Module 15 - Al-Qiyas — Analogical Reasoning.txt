Two Paths of Isti'ara in Hanafi Usul
Definition of isti'ara and its two types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two types of isti'ara in legal rulings and distinguish between them based on the connection of 'illah and sabab mahd.

Core Idea
Isti'ara extends rulings via two connections: cause ('illah) or pure occasion (sabab mahd).

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the term isti'ara (metaphorical extension) refers to the analogical transfer of a ruling from a known case to a new case. This passage introduces two distinct paths by which such extension is valid: one relying on the effective cause ('illah) and the other on a mere occasioning cause (sabab mahd). Understanding these types is foundational for recognizing how rulings are derived beyond literal texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two paths of isti'ara
Read the passage and list the two bases: connection between 'illah and hukm, and connection between sabab mahd and hukm.
The text says 'bi-tariqayn' (through two paths).
2
Define 'illah and sabab mahd
Recall that 'illah is the efficient cause that necessitates a ruling, while sabab mahd is a mere occasion that triggers the ruling without being the ratio legis.
Hanafi usul distinguishes these two concepts; this distinction drives the two types.
3
Understand the ruling of the first type
The passage states the first type 'yujibu sihhat al-isti'ara min al-tarafayn' – it validates extension from both sides.
Both the 'illah and the hukm can be used as the source for analogy.
4
Consider the second type (implied)
Though the text only gives the ruling of the first type, reflect that the second type involves extension from the sabab mahd to the hukm, typically with more restrictions.
The chapter likely continues with details on the second type.

Isti'ara in Hanafi Usul: Two Types of Metaphorical Extension
Defining and dividing isti'ara into two types based on connection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two types of isti'ara in Hanafi usul and apply them to conditional statements in legal texts.

Core Idea
Isti'ara is valid both ways between cause and ruling, but only one way from pure cause to ruling.

The Hanafi usul scholars employ the concept of isti'ara (metaphorical extension) to interpret legal language when a term is used figuratively. This lesson examines the classification of isti'ara into two types based on the nature of the connection: the connection between the effective cause (illa) and the ruling (hukm), and the connection between the pure cause (sabab) and the ruling. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying legal conditions in contracts and oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of connection
The passage divides isti'ara based on whether the connection is between the illa (effective cause) and the hukm (ruling), or between the sabab (pure cause) and the hukm. Note that sabab is a direct cause not subject to qiyas.
In Hanafi usul, illa is the ratio legis in qiyas, while sabab is a direct cause like purchase triggering ownership.
2
Understand the ruling for each type
For the first type (illa-hukm), metaphorical extension is valid from both sides: either term can stand for the other. For the second type (sabab-hukm), it is valid only from the original (cause) to the branch (ruling).
The second type is asymmetrical: the ruling cannot be used to refer to the cause.
3
Examine the example
Consider two conditions: 'If I own a slave, he is free' vs 'If I buy a slave, he is free'. In the first, owning half then later the other half does not trigger freedom because the condition requires complete ownership at the moment of the first half. In the second, buying half then later the other half triggers freedom on the second purchase because 'purchase' (cause) can metaphorically extend to 'owning' (ruling) from the cause side.
The first condition is about pure ownership (sabab-like? Actually ownership is a state, not a cause; the passage uses it as a counterexample to show the difference).
4
Note the court exception
If someone uses isti'ara to claim a leniency (e.g., calling ownership 'purchase' to fulfill a condition), his intention is valid in reality but not accepted in court due to suspicion of cheating. This does not invalidate the isti'ara itself.
The exception is procedural, not substantive.
5
Apply the rule to new scenarios
Given a conditional statement, determine whether the terms used are connected as illa-hukm or sabab-hukm, and then decide if metaphorical extension is valid bilaterally or unilaterally.
This helps in interpreting oaths, vows, and contractual conditions.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi defines the concept of isti'ara (metaphorical extension) as applied in Hanafi legal theory. The author distinguishes two ways in which isti'ara operates in the rulings of Sharia: the first is based on the connection between the illa (effective cause) and the hukm (ruling); the second is based on the connection between the sabab (pure cause) and the hukm. The first type allows isti'ara to be valid from both directions—i.e., the illa can stand for the hukm and vice versa. The second type only allows validity from one direction: the original (the cause) can stand for the branch (the ruling), but not the reverse. The example clarifies: in the 'ownership' condition, owning half the slave does not trigger freedom because the condition requires complete ownership; when he later owns the other half, the condition was not met at the moment of the first half, so the slave is not freed. In contrast, the 'purchase' condition triggers freedom for the second half because purchase is the cause and ownership is its effect; the isti'ara between cause and effect is valid from both sides, so the second purchase fulfills the condition. However, if someone tries to use isti'ara to claim a legal benefit (e.g., calling ownership 'purchase' to fulfill a condition), his intention is valid in reality but not accepted in court due to suspicion of cheating, not because the metaphorical extension itself is invalid.

Key Terms
الاستعارة (isti'ara): Metaphorical extension in legal reasoning, where one term is used for another due to a connection (illa or sabab).
العلة (illa): The effective cause (ratio legis) in qiyas, which entails the ruling.
السبب (sabab): A pure cause that brings about the ruling without being the 'illa in qiyas; a direct connection.
الحكم (hukm): The ruling or legal effect that follows from the cause.

The Ruling Revolves Around the Effective Cause
Illustrates the principle that rulings revolve around effective causes through multiple legal examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that rulings are contingent upon their effective causes, using examples from oaths and worship to distinguish form from meaning.

Core Idea
Rulings depend on the effective cause, not the literal form.

This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary demonstrates a foundational principle: legal rulings revolve around their underlying causes ('illal). The author shows that even if the outward act is present, if the intended meaning is absent, the ruling does not apply. This is illustrated through examples from expiation, filial piety, Friday prayer, and oaths. Understanding this principle is essential for proper istinbat (derivation) and for distinguishing between form and substance in Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Locate the phrase 'يدار الحكم على تلك العلة' and note that the author establishes that rulings revolve around the effective cause. This is the core thesis of the passage.
The principle is explicitly stated after the kaffarah example.
2
Analyze the Kaffarah Example
Examine how expiation is established for intercourse by explicit text (nass) and for eating/drinking by indication of text (dalalat al-nass) because they share the same 'illah: violating the fast.
This shows extension based on cause, not literal wording.
3
Parse the Hypothetical about 'Uff'
Consider the case where people consider 'uff' as respectful. The ruling (sin) is absent because the disrespectful meaning (the 'illah of 'uff') is absent. This demonstrates that the 'illah is the basis for the ruling.
Even if the word is 'uff', the ruling depends on intention/context.
4
Apply to Friday Sale
Analyze the Friday prayer example: a sale during Friday prayer is disliked only if it prevents attendance. If it does not (e.g., on a ship heading to the mosque), the 'illah (distraction) is absent, so the sale is not disliked.
The ruling (karahah) is tied to the cause (prevention from prayer).
5
Work through Oath Examples
Examine the hitting and speaking oaths. Hitting without pain or hitting a dead person does not break the oath because the 'illah (inflicting pain) is absent. Similarly, speaking to a dead person does not break an oath because the 'illah (conveying understanding) is absent.
This illustrates that the literal act alone is insufficient; the cause must be present.

The Exceptional Use of Faa to Indicate Cause in Hanafi Usul
Explains the exceptional use of faa to indicate cause with condition of continuity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when the letter faa is exceptionally used to indicate a cause and apply the condition of continuity (dawam) to validate such usage in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Faa normally cannot precede a cause, but it is allowed if the cause is continuous — because continuity makes it coexist with the effect.

In Arabic grammar and usul al-fiqh, the letter faa is primarily used to connect rulings, not causes, because a cause cannot be subsequent to its effect. However, Hanafi scholars recognized an exception: when the cause is ongoing (has dawam), it is permissible for faa to precede it. This lesson unpacks that exception, its justification, and its application in legal reasoning. This linguistic insight helps you understand how Hanafi usul handles apparent contradictions between language and logic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original rule
State the baseline: faa enters on rulings, not causes, because a cause cannot be subsequent to its effect. This is based on logical priority.
The Arabic phrase 'لأنها مترتبة على العلم' indicates rulings depend on knowledge, hence faa is appropriate.
2
Recognize the exception
Note that the passage says 'إلا أنها قد تدخل على العلل على خلاف الأصل' — except that it may enter on causes contrary to the original rule. This establishes an exception.
The exception is not absolute; it depends on a condition.
3
Specify the condition
The condition is dawam (continuity/duration). Explain why: if the cause is continuous, it can be considered to overlap with the effect, so faa is not strictly indicating temporal posteriority.
The text says 'بشرط أن يكون لها دوام' — on condition that it has continuity.
4
Analyze the example
The text gives an example: to a person in distress, one says 'أبشر فقد أتاك الغوث' (Rejoice, for help has come). Here 'help' (the cause) is continuous after the rejoicing begins? Actually, the help is ongoing, so it is not entirely prior.
The help remains present, so faa is permissible because the cause has dawam.
5
Apply the rule in usul
Understand that this rule is used to justify certain formulations in legal texts where faa appears before a cause. It confirms that the Hanafi school is flexible in linguistic interpretation with rational justification.
This rule is used when analyzing texts for causality in legal rulings.

Wording Determines Contract Type: Sale vs Salam
Example of how wording defines contract type

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify, based on the wording of a contract, whether it constitutes a sale (bay‘) or a salam contract, and state the corresponding ruling on substitution of the price before possession.

Core Idea
The placement of words determines contract type: what comes first after 'I sold you' is the sold item; the rest is the price.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi presents a concrete example that reveals a key principle of Hanafi contract law: the wording of a contract is not merely formal; it actively determines the legal classification and attendant rulings. By examining two parallel statements, we learn how the same items (a slave and a quantity of wheat) can produce either a regular sale or a salam contract depending on which is mentioned first. Understanding this parsing is essential for correctly applying the rules of substitution and deferment in transactions.

Guided Path
1
Parse the first wording
Identify the sentence: 'I sold you this slave for a kurr of wheat, describing it.' Determine which item is the subject of sale (mabī‘) and which is the price (thaman). The Hanafi rule is that the first definite item after the verb 'bā‘tuka' is the sold object.
Here 'this slave' (اِت هٰذَا الْعَبْد) is definite and comes first, so it is the mabī‘; the wheat (كُرّاً) is the thaman.
2
Apply the ruling to the first wording
Since the contract is a regular sale (bay‘), the price (wheat) is a debt that can be substituted before possession. The text explicitly states: 'it is permissible to substitute it before taking possession.'
Substitution is allowed because the price is a dain (debt) and can be replaced by mutual consent.
3
Parse the second wording
Now analyze the second sentence: 'I sold you a kurr of wheat, describing it, for this slave.' Here, the first indefinite noun after the verb is 'a kurr of wheat' (كُرّاً مِنَ الْحِنْطَةِ), which makes it the sold item. The slave becomes the price.
Indefinite noun (nakirah) as first indicates salam, because the sold item must be described and deferred.
4
Apply the ruling to the second wording
The contract is now classified as salam (forward sale). The wheat is the subject of sale, and the slave is the spot price. Salam requires the delivery of the wheat to be deferred (mu’ajjal). Immediate delivery would invalidate it.
Salam is valid only with a specified future delivery date; immediate delivery turns it into a prohibited sale of something not in hand.
5
Extract the legal principle
The general principle: In a contract of exchange, the first object mentioned after the verb 'bā‘tuka' is the sold item; the second is the price. This determines whether the contract is bay‘ or salam, with corresponding rules for substitution and deferment.
This principle is based on Hanafi usul: the wording of the contract (al-lafẓ) governs the nature of the contract.

When Qiyas Prevails over a Hadith: The Hanafi Principle
A usul rule stating the preference for qiyas over a contradictory hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for preferring qiyas over a contradictory hadith and apply it to the example of wudu from cooked food.

Core Idea
A hadith that contradicts qiyas is set aside — qiyas takes precedence.

This lesson addresses a key Hanafi usul principle: when a single report (khabar) from a non-mujtahid narrator contradicts an analogical deduction (qiyas), the qiyas is given preference. The rule is not anti-hadith; it is a methodological caution. The example of the hadith 'Perform wudu from what is touched by fire' and Ibn Abbas's rebuttal by qiyas illustrates how the principle works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule

The rule applies to a specific category of narrators.
2

'Mithlihima' refers to two previously defined categories: the mujtahid and the reliable non-mujtahid. The rule primarily concerns the latter.
The reliability of the narrator affects the strength of the hadith.

The qiyas is based on the principle that heat itself does not cause impurity.

The preference for qiyas is not arbitrary. It is based on the fact that a non-mujtahid narrator's hadith, when contradicting a well-established qiyas, indicates the hadith's intended meaning is different or it is weak. Qiyas is based on universal principles.
This principle only applies when the hadith is ahad and zanni.
5

This rule does not apply to mutawatir hadith or to hadith that are clear and supported by other evidence. It is a guideline for conflicting evidences.
The rule prioritizes stronger evidence.

Core Idea
A valid branch proves its own origin only – it cannot validate a different origin.

In Hanafi usul, qiyas depends on a common illah between the asl and far'. The Hanafi school insists that shar‘i causes are specified by the Lawgiver, not by language. Using the example of the grave robber (nabbash) being compared to a thief (sariq), we will see why such analogy fails and how it would destroy the integrity of shar‘i rulings.

Invalidity of Qiyas in Linguistic Names
Refuting qiyas based on linguistic 'illa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when qiyas is invalid due to reliance on a linguistic 'illa and explain why the Hanafi school rejects it.

Core Idea
Linguistic names are conventional; qiyas cannot extend them based on shared attributes.

This passage tackles a common methodological error: using qiyas to extend linguistic names. Some scholars argued that if a thing shares the defining attribute (illa) of a name, it should be called by that name. For example, they claimed that cooked date juice should be called 'khamr' because it covers the intellect, just like wine. The Hanafi response, grounded in both linguistic convention and Shar'i reasoning, shows why such qiyas is invalid. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correct istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and examples
Read the first part of the passage where the opposing argument is presented: cooked juice is khamr because it shares the 'illa of covering the intellect; grave robber is a thief because of secret taking. Identify these as qiyas based on linguistic 'illa.
These examples illustrate the mistaken method.
2
Understand the refutation from linguistic convention
The author gives the example of Arab naming: a horse is called 'adham' for blackness, but a black man is not. This shows names are conventional, not transferred by qiyas. Note the inductive proof (istiqra).
This is the core rational proof (dalil aqli).
3
Recognize the consequence for Shar'i causes
If we extend names by qiyas, we also extend Shar'i causes (asbab). For example, theft is a cause for amputation; if we call grave-robbing 'theft', we change the cause. The author argues this invalidates the original Shar'i designation.
Shar'i causes are specific; widening them destroys their legal effect.
4
Formulate the rule
From the passage, derive the rule: Qiyas is invalid when based on a linguistic 'illa (al-'illa al-lughawiyya) because linguistic names are conventional (tawqifiyya) and Shar'i causes are fixed. Qiyas is only valid for Shar'i 'illa that are not purely nominal.
This rule is a cornerstone of Hanafi usul.

Fifth Condition of Qiyas: Far' Not Textually Stipulated
Fifth condition of qiyas exemplified with mutlaq/muqayyad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a branch ruling is textually stipulated and apply the rule that qiyas is invalid if the branch is already covered by nass.

Core Idea
Qiyas is invalid if the branch ruling is already in the text – the nass governs directly.

This lesson focuses on the fifth condition of valid qiyas: the branch (far') must not have its ruling explicitly stated in the primary sources. The author of Usul al-Shashi gives two examples to illustrate when a proposed qiyas is invalid because the text already covers the branch. Understanding this condition prevents misuse of analogy and ensures that textual rulings are not bypassed.

Guided Path
1
Understand the Condition
Grasp the rule: qiyas is invalid if the ruling of the branch is already mentioned in the Quran or Sunnah. The text directly governs the branch, so no analogy is needed.
This condition ensures that we do not derive rulings by analogy when the Lawgiver has already spoken on the matter.
2
Analyse Example 1: Kaffarah of Yamin/Dhihar vs. Qatl
The text for yamin and dhihar says 'free a slave' without qualification (mutlaq). The text for qatl says 'free a believing slave' (muqayyad). The ruling for the branch (freeing a slave in yamin) is already given in the absolute text; qiyas to qatl's restricted ruling is invalid because the branch is already stipulated.
The absolute text covers all slaves, so the branch is textually stipulated.
3
Analyse Example 2: Restarting Feeding in Dhihar
If a person who has made dhihar engages in intercourse during the feeding period, can he restart feeding by analogy to fasting? The text for feeding is absolute (just 'feeding sixty'), while fasting requires consecutive days. Qiyas is invalid because the feeding text already gives the ruling absolutely; no need for analogy.
Again, the absolute text stipulates the branch ruling.
4
Synthesise the Principle
In both examples, the texts for the branch are absolute (mutlaq); the analogized texts are restricted (muqayyad). The condition is that if the branch ruling appears in the nass in any form (here as mutlaq), qiyas is prevented. This applies even if the illah seems to match.
Always check if the branch is textually stipulated before attempting qiyas.

Core Idea

Two Types of Qiyas and the Need for Tajnis al-Illa
Explaining the two types of qiyas with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two types of qiyas (by species and by genus) and apply the requirement of tajnis al-illa when extending a ruling to a new case.

Core Idea
Qiyas by genus demands homogenizing the cause to a common genus; qiyas by species does not.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces a key classification in Hanafi legal theory: qiyas can transfer a ruling that is either of the same species (nau') as the original or of the same genus (jins). The distinction matters because the second type requires an extra step — tajnis al-illa, i.e., identifying the shared genus-level attribute. We will unpack the definitions, work through the examples provided, and practice applying this rule to new cases.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between nau' and jins
Define 'nau'' (species) and 'jins' (genus) in the context of rulings. Nau' is a more specific category sharing identical attributes; jins is a broader category encompassing multiple species that share a common attribute. In qiyas, if the transferred ruling shares the same species as the original, no extra step is needed. If it only shares the same genus, the cause must be homogenized.
In Hanafi usul, rulings are classified hierarchically: genus (jins) → species (nau') → individual (shakhs).
2
Trace the illa in qiyas by species
For the first type, take the example of minority (sighar) as a cause for guardianship of marriage (wilayat al-inkah). The original ruling applies to a minor boy. Find the same cause in a minor girl, so the ruling transfers by species — both are 'guardianship of marriage' of the same type.
Other examples: cat's lapping as cause for dropping impurity of leftover water, transferred to domestic animals; puberty with intellect as cause for removing guardianship of marriage.
3
Trace the illa in qiyas by genus
For the second type, consider the example of frequent lapping as a cause for dropping the difficulty of seeking permission regarding slaves. The transferred ruling is about dropping the difficulty of impurity of leftover water. These are different species but both are 'difficulty' (haraj) — the same genus. Thus, we need to explicitly articulate the genus-level cause.
Similarly, minority is a cause for guardianship over wealth (species: financial disposition). It transfers to guardianship over the person (species: personal disposition) because both are under the genus 'guardianship' due to incapacity.
4
Perform tajnis al-illa
To apply tajnis al-illa, identify the underlying common attribute (jins) that allows the analogy. In the minority example: the genus is 'incapacity to act independently', which triggers guardianship for both wealth and person. Articulate this in a sentence: 'The father's guardianship over the minor's wealth is established because she is incapable of disposition; she is also incapable of disposition over herself, so guardianship over her person is necessary.'
This step is mandatory for the second type; without it, the qiyas is invalid because the cause (illa) must be unified at the genus level.
5
Apply to a new case
Given a hypothetical scenario, determine if the qiyas is by species or genus. If by species, simply check for the same cause. If by genus, perform tajnis al-illa by clarifying the genus-level attribute that connects the original and new case.
For example: if a cause (e.g., intoxication) exists in new substance, the ruling of prohibition of khamr may transfer by species (if same genus of intoxication).

Qiyas with an Appropriate Description (Al-Wasf Al-Munasib)
Explains the fourth method of knowing the 'illa via an appropriate description deduced by ijtihad.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for using an appropriate description (al-wasf al-munasib) as an 'illa and apply it to derive a ruling via qiyas.

Core Idea
An appropriate description naturally requiring the ruling, found in a consensus case, establishes high probability that it is the 'illa.

In the previous methods, the 'illa was known through explicit text or consensus. Here, the mujtahid uses ijtihad to identify a description that is inherently appropriate to the ruling, relying on its natural connection. This method is valid in Hanafi usul when no stronger evidence exists, and it requires the ruling to be associated with the description in a case of consensus to establish high probability.

Guided Path
1
Define al-wasf al-munasib
Identify what an 'appropriate description' is: a description that naturally necessitates the ruling by its own consideration.
2
Identify the conditions
Note two conditions: (1) the description must be appropriate for the ruling, and (2) the ruling must be found with that description in a case of consensus (ijma).
The consensus case provides the association that generates high probability.
3
Understand the role of ghalabat al-zann
Recognize that this method establishes a high probability (ghalabat al-zann) that the description is the 'illa, which obligates action in the absence of stronger evidence.
The analogy of the traveler who strongly believes water is nearby shows that high probability can prevent tayammum.
4
Recognize what invalidates it
The qiyas is invalidated by an appropriate difference (al-farq al-munasib) between the original and branch that undermines the appropriateness of the 'illa.
A mere difference is not enough; the difference must be relevant to the ruling.
5
Apply the example
Analyze the example: giving a dirham to a poor person. The description 'relieving need' is appropriate for the ruling of reward, and it is found in consensus cases (e.g., zakah). This shows how the method works.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi discusses the fourth method of knowing the 'illa (ratio legis) for qiyas: qiyas based on a description deduced by the mujtahid's opinion and ijtihad, known as al-wasf al-munasib (the appropriate description). The author first clarifies that when the original and branch share the same 'illa, they must share the ruling even if they differ in other aspects. The second type of qiyas (i.e., qiyas with a specific difference) is invalid because it prevents proper analogy. Then, the author explains the fourth method: if a mujtahid finds a description that is appropriate to a ruling such that it naturally necessitates and requires the ruling, and the ruling is found with that description in a case of consensus (e.g., a known ruling without dispute), the ruling is attributed to that description due to the appropriateness (munasaba), even if no explicit text (nass) or consensus (ijma) declares it as the 'illa. The example is of a person giving money to a poor person; it appears that the giving is to relieve need and gain reward. This establishes that such a description (relieving need) is appropriate for the ruling of giving. The author then compares this to the case of a traveler who strongly believes water is nearby: he cannot perform tayammum because the high probability (ghalabat al-zann) of finding water invalidates the excuse. Similarly, a high probability that a description is the 'illa obligates action. The ruling of this type of qiyas is that it is invalidated by an appropriate difference (al-farq al-munasib), meaning that if someone points out a relevant difference between the original and branch that undermines the appropriateness, the analogy fails.

غلبة الظن (ghalabat al-zann): A high probability that obligates action in the absence of a stronger evidence.
الفرق المناسب (al-farq al-munasib): A relevant difference between original and branch that undermines the appropriateness of the 'illa, invalidating the qiyas.

The Principle of Ghalabat al-Zann in Qiyas
Explanation of the role of strong presumption and suitable difference in qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a strong presumption (ghalabat al-zann) suffices for legal action in qiyas when no stronger proof exists, and identify when a suitable difference (farq munasib) invalidates the analogy.

Core Idea
Strong presumption of a cause obligates action unless a suitable difference breaks the analogy.

In Hanafi usul, identifying the effective cause for qiyas often relies on strong presumption rather than certainty. This passage explains that when a suitable description (wasf munasib) is consistently associated with a ruling in cases of consensus, we presume it is the cause. However, this presumption is rebuttable if a suitable difference exists between the original case and new case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the wasf munasib
Locate the description that appears suitable to be the cause (illah) for the ruling because it aligns with the wisdom or purpose of the law.
Suitability is key in Hanafi usul.
2
Check for consensus association
Determine if the ruling has been associated with this description in a case that is agreed upon by consensus (ijma).
Consensus strengthens the presumption.
3
Apply ghalabat al-zann
If the ruling is consistently attached to that description in consensus, then a strong presumption arises that the ruling is caused by that description. In the absence of a stronger proof, this presumption obligates acting upon it.
Example: traveler who strongly presumes water is near cannot do tayammum.
4
Test for farq munasib
Check whether there exists a suitable difference between the original case (asl) and the new case (far‘) that would prevent the analogy. If such a difference exists, the presumption is broken and the qiyas is invalid.
The farq must be suitable (munasib) to the ruling.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of the strength of the ‘illah (cause) in qiyas (analogical reasoning). The author begins by establishing a principle: when we see a suitable attribute (wasf munasib) accompanying a ruling in a case where consensus exists that the ruling is attached to that attribute, our presumption (zann) is strengthened that the ruling is indeed caused by that attribute. This strong presumption carries legal weight—it obligates action unless a stronger proof overrides it. An example is given: a traveler who strongly presumes water is nearby cannot perform tayammum; the presumption of water suffices to deny the concession. Similarly, in issues of taharri (seeking the correct direction), the strong presumption guides action.

However, this analogy is invalidated if there exists a suitable difference (farq munasib) between the original case and the new case. That is, if in the new case there is another equally suitable attribute that could be the cause, the presumption weakens and the analogy fails. The author then categorizes the ‘illah into three types based on how it is known:
1. Known by explicit text (nass): This is the strongest, akin to a witness who has been declared upright (tazkiyah).
2. Known by consensus (ijma‘): Medium strength, like a witness whose uprightness is apparent but not yet formally declared.
3. Known by ijtihad: Weakest, like the testimony of a mastur (witness whose character is unknown).

The commentary (marked with ش) expands on examples: giving money to the poor is presumed to be for need and reward; punishing a slave after a crime is presumed to be for that crime. The overall lesson: the strength of a qiyas depends on how the ‘illah is established, and any suitable difference undermines the analogy.

الوصف المناسب (al-waṣf al-munāsib): A description that is suitable to be the cause (illah) for a ruling because it aligns with the wisdom or purpose of the law.
غلبة الظن (ghalabat al-ẓann): Strong presumption (overwhelming likelihood) that a ruling is attached to a particular cause, which legally suffices for action in the absence of certainty.
الفرق المناسب (al-farq al-munāsib): A suitable difference between the original case and the new case that is relevant to the ruling, which invalidates the analogy because the cause may be different.

The Hierarchy of the Illah: Text, Ijma, Ijtihad
Categorization of the cause according to its source of knowledge.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of 'illah based on how they are known (by text, ijma, ijtihad) and rank their evidentiary strength using the testimony analogy from the passage.

Core Idea
The cause known by text is strongest, by ijma is medium, and by ijtihad is weakest.

This passage further categorizes the 'illah (cause) according to the certainty of its source. Three levels are identified: explicit text (nass), consensus (ijma'), and independent reasoning (ijtihad). The author compares them to the levels of testimony: a witness who has been formally declared upright (tazkiyah), a witness whose uprightness is apparent, and a witness of unknown character (mastur). This hierarchy determines the strength of a qiyas built on that cause.

Identify the three types of 'illah
List the three sources: text, consensus, and ijtihad. Note that they are ordered by certainty.
From the passage: 'حكم الوصف المعلوم بالنص ... بالإجماع ... بالاجتهاد'.
2
Understand the testimony analogy
The author compares each type to a level of witness credibility. First type: witness after formal tazkiyah. Second: witness with apparent uprightness. Third: witness of unknown status.
This analogy shows how strongly we rely on each cause.
3
Rank the types by strength
Text (nass): highest, like tazkiyah. Ijma: medium, like apparent uprightness. Ijtihad: weakest, like unknown witness.
The ranking determines the force of qiyas built on that cause.
4
Apply the hierarchy
When evaluating a qiyas, first check if the cause is explicitly stated in Quran or Sunnah (strongest). If not, check if there is consensus on the cause (medium). If neither, resort to ijtihad (weakest).

Ruling of the Description Known by Text, Consensus, and Ijtihad
Clarifying the ruling strength of different methods of knowing the 'illah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three levels of certainty in knowing the 'illah (text, ijma, ijtihad) and apply the Hanafi analogy of witness testimony to classify the strength of a qiyas argument.

Core Idea
The certainty of a qiyas ruling depends on how the 'illah is known: text > ijma > ijtihad, like verified witnesses.

In Hanafi usul, the strength of a qiyas argument hinges on how the 'illah (legal cause) is established. This passage uses a powerful analogy from court testimony to rank 'illah certainty: text is like a fully accredited witness, ijma is like a witness of apparent integrity, and ijtihad is like an unknown witness. Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for evaluating qiyas-derived rulings in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path

Identify the three methods of knowing the 'illah
List the three methods mentioned: text (nass), consensus (ijma), and ijtihad. Note that each corresponds to a different level of certainty.

Understand the witness analogy
Map each method to its witness counterpart: text = witness after tazkiyah (formal accreditation); ijma = witness when uprightness is apparent before tazkiyah; ijtihad = mastur (witness of unknown character).

3
Apply the analogy to classify an 'illah
Given a hypothetical 'illah, determine its method of knowledge and then assign it the corresponding witness status. This helps evaluate the strength of the qiyas derived from it.

4
Consider the initial condition
The passage begins with a condition: if there exists another suitable cause, the presumption of attributing the ruling to the first 'illah is invalidated. This condition interacts with the strength of the 'illah.
This condition is more likely to affect weaker 'illahs (e.g., ijtihad-based).
5
Synthesize the epistemological weight
Recognize that the ruling of qiyas is only as strong as the method of knowing the 'illah. A fatwa based on an ijtihad-derived 'illah is less certain than one based on text.
This is a key principle in Hanafi usul for evaluating evidence.

Understanding al-Mumana'ah: The First Objection to Qiyas
Defines mumana'ah and splits into two types with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mumana'ah, distinguish its two types (man' al-wasf and man' al-hukm), and apply them to a qiyas argument.

Core Idea
Mumana'ah is a structured denial that forces the proponent of qiyas to prove their case.

In Hanafi usul, a qiyas argument can be challenged through eight standard objections. The first and most fundamental is al-mumana'ah—a denial that either the claimed attribute (wasf) or the ruling (hukm) is valid. This lesson breaks down the definition and types of mumana'ah, using concrete fiqh examples to show how it operates.

Guided Path
1
Identify the place of mumana'ah among the eight objections
The passage lists eight objections to qiyas; mumana'ah is the first. Recognize that it is the most basic challenge.
The other seven are: al-qawl bi mujib al-'illah, al-qalb, al-'aks, fasad al-wad', al-farq, al-naqd, al-mu'aradah.
2
Define mumana'ah and its two subtypes
Mumana'ah means denial. It targets either the attribute (wasf) used in the qiyas or the ruling (hukm) itself. Understand each subtype.
Man' al-wasf: denying that the claimed attribute exists in the original case or is valid as a cause. Man' al-hukm: denying the ruling even after accepting the attribute.
3
Analyze the first example (zakat al-fitr)
The opponent claims the cause is breaking the fast (al-fitr). The Hanafi response denies that cause. Identify this as man' al-wasf.
The Hanafi says 'We do not accept its obligation due to al-fitr'—denying the attribute, not the ruling.
4
Analyze the second example (general zakat)
The opponent claims the amount of zakat is a debt in the dhimmah. The Hanafi denies that the obligation is in the dhimmah; rather it is a payment obligation. Again, this is man' al-wasf—denying the nature of the obligation as a debt.
The Hanafi position is that zakat is an obligation of payment (ada'), not a debt (dayn) in the dhimmah.
5
Understand the legal effect of mumana'ah
Mumana'ah forces the proponent of qiyas to provide further evidence for the attributed cause or the ruling. If successful, the qiyas is weakened or rebutted.
It is not a final rejection but a procedural objection that shifts the burden of proof.

Core Idea
Objection to attribute denies the common cause; objection to ruling accepts the cause but denies the ruling.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, one of the key skills in jadal (dialectics) is raising valid objections (mumana'ah) against an opponent's qiyas. Al-Mu'allal: Objection in Qiyas by Showing Different Ma'lul
Definition of al-mu'allal with two examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the structure of a mu'allal objection and apply it to a qiyas argument by conceding the 'illah but refuting the claimed ma'lul.

Core Idea
In qiyas, conceding the cause ('illah) but showing its effect (ma'lul) differs breaks the analogy.

In usul al-fiqh, not every objection to qiyas requires denying the 'illah. A powerful rebuttal, called al-mu'allal, concedes the opponent's description as a valid cause but demonstrates that the resulting ruling (ma'lul) is not what the opponent claimed. This lesson unpacks the definition and two classic Hanafi examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the structure of al-mu'allal
Note that the objection concedes the wasf as 'illah but rejects the claimed ma'lul. This is a concessive rebuttal, not a denial.
2
Analyze the first example (wudu)
The opponent claims the elbow is a boundary (hadd) and thus not washed. The Hanafi concedes it is a boundary but shows it is the boundary of the forearm (sa'id), which falls under the ruling of washing. Thus the ma'lul (not washing) is incorrect.
Key: The 'illah 'being a boundary' is accepted, but its application differs.
3
Analyze the second example (Ramadan fasting)
The opponent claims fasting Ramadan requires the servant's specification (ta'yin) like qada'. The Hanafi concedes the need for specification but shows that in Ramadan it is provided by the Law itself, so the servant's specification is unnecessary. The ma'lul (need for servant's ta'yin) differs.
Note how the rebuttal reframes the ma'lul based on the source of specification.
4
Compare the two examples
Both follow the same pattern: accept the 'illah, then demonstrate that the effect (ma'lul) claimed by the opponent does not follow in the original case because of a contextual difference.

Definition and illustration of the first type of qalb

Learning Objective

Core Idea

This lesson introduces al-qalb, a powerful rebuttal technique in usul al-fiqh. When an opponent argues that a ruling applies in one direction due to an 'illah (cause), you reverse the relationship: the 'illah becomes the ma'lul, breaking the causal link. Understanding this tool sharpens your ability to critique and refine qiyas arguments.

Core Idea
Qalb inverts the 'illah to break the causal link; 'Aks forces the proponent to distinguish or contradict themselves.

In Hanafi usul, disputation (jadal) is not mere debate but a method to uncover the soundness of legal reasoning. The tools of qalb (inversion) and 'aks (reversal) allow the opponent to test the consistency of a claimed 'illah. This passage introduces two types of qalb and the technique of 'aks with concrete fiqh examples. Understanding these tools sharpens your ability to evaluate and construct sound analogical reasoning.

The proponent says: 'Riba in large quantities implies riba in small quantities, like currencies.' The opponent inverts: 'Riba in small quantities implies riba in large quantities.' See how this nullifies the original causal claim.

Core Idea
A legal cause that contradicts the nature of the ruling is void (fasad al-wad').

In this lesson, we examine a critical concept in Hanafi usul: corruption of the 'illah (fasad al-wad'). When a jurist proposes a cause for a ruling that is inherently incompatible with that ruling, the analogy based on it collapses. The author of Usul al-Shashi illustrates this with two well-known disputes between Hanafis and Shafi'is, showing how an apparently plausible cause can be fundamentally flawed.

Guided Path
1
Define fasad al-wad'
State the definition from the text: the 'illah is made a description that does not suit the ruling. Understand that this is a defect in the 'illah itself, not in the analogy's form.
The term 'wad'' here means 'occasioning factor' or 'cause' in qiyas.
2
Identify the 'illah in the first example
The Shafi'is claim that Islam of one spouse causes separation, analogous to apostasy. Identify the 'illah: 'Islam as a difference of religion' (ikhtilaf al-din).
The ruling (hukm) is invalidation of marriage (fasad al-nikah).
3
Analyze Hanafi rejection: why is this fasad al-wad'?
The Hanafis argue that Islam is a covenant that preserves ownership (ahd 'asim lil-milk), not one that destroys it. Therefore, describing Islam as a cause for invalidation is unsuitable—it contradicts the inherent nature of Islam.
This shows that the 'illah must be congruent with the hukm's purpose.
4

The Shafi'is analogize a free man marrying a slave woman to a man already married to a free woman. The 'illah is 'freedom and ability to marry,' which should permit marriage, not prohibit it. Hence, it too is fasad al-wad'.
The description 'free' actually supports permissibility, so it cannot justify prohibition.

Al-Naqd in Usul al-Fiqh: Refutation by Counterexample
Explaining the concept of naqd with an example from tayammum.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-naqd, identify its elements, and apply it to refute an invalid qiyas.

Core Idea
Naqd breaks an analogy by finding a counterexample where the 'illah exists but the ruling does not.

In usul al-fiqh, a mujtahid must ensure that an analogy (qiyas) is consistent. One way to challenge a qiyas is through naqd (refutation): showing that the claimed effective cause ('illah) is not exclusive to the original case. This lesson unpacks the concept using the classic example of intention in purification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's claim
The opponent asserts that all acts of purification require intention (niyyah). This is based on the analogy with tayammum, which requires intention.
The claim is implicitly: 'Wudu is purification, so it needs intention.'
2
Extract the hidden qiyas
The opponent's reasoning forms a qiyas: the original case (asl) is tayammum, which requires intention. The new case (far') is wudu (or any purification). The effective cause ('illah) is 'being an act of purification.'
The 'illah is not explicitly stated but is inferred.
3
Find the counterexample
The Hanafi response points to washing a garment and a vessel (ghasl al-thawb wa al-ina'). These are acts of purification (ritual cleaning) but do not require intention. Thus, the 'illah exists without the ruling.
This is the naqd: a case that disproves the claimed correlation.
4
Understand why this is naqd
Naqd shows that the opponent's 'illah is not exclusive (tard) or not consistent (aks). The opponent must either refine the 'illah or provide a distinguishing factor (farq) to salvage the analogy.
The burden shifts to the opponent to prove the 'illah is properly defined.
5
Recognize the consequence
Without a distinguishing factor, the qiyas is invalid. The ruling (wajib of intention) cannot be extended to wudu based solely on the shared property of 'purification.'
This illustrates the critical role of naqd in scholarly debate.

Examples of Naqd and Mu'arada in Qiyas
Providing illustrative examples of naqd and mu'arada in argumentation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between naqd and mu'arada in usul al-fiqh and apply them to examples of qiyas argumentation.

Core Idea
Naqd invalidates the original evidence by exposing inconsistency; mu'arada provides a competing evidence without undermining the original.

In this lesson, we examine two key types of objections used in analogical reasoning (qiyas): al-naqd (refutation) and al-mu'arada (counter-evidence). The passage from Usul al-Shashi provides concrete examples to illustrate how each works. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for analyzing and constructing valid qiyas arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of objections
Recognize that naqd aims to invalidate the original evidence by showing that the claimed 'illah exists without the ruling, while mu'arada offers an alternative evidence that counters the ruling without directly invalidating the original.
2
Analyze the naqd example
The opponent claims that wudu requires intention because it is a purification. The naqd response points out that washing a garment is also purification but does not require intention, thus breaking the link between the 'illah and the ruling.
This shows the 'illah is not consistently effective.
3
Analyze the mu'arada example
The opponent claims that wiping in wudu should be repeated three times because it is a pillar, like washing. The mu'arada counters by citing cases where wiping is a pillar but not repeated (khuff and tayammum), presenting a different analogy.
This offers a competing qiyas without denying that wiping is a pillar.
4
Distinguish the two
Naqd attacks the validity of the original dalil itself; mu'arada presents a different dalil that leads to a contrary ruling. The original dalil remains valid but is outweighed.
In mu'arada, the opponent's evidence is not rejected; it is opposed by another evidence.

Cause as Effective Cause in Liability
Usul rule: when a cause is treated as effective cause for liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a cause (sabab) is treated as an effective cause ('illa) for liability and apply the Hanafi rule to distinguish between liability and non-liability in similar scenarios.

Core Idea
A cause can be treated as an effective cause when harm is attributed to it in common understanding or legal reasoning.

In Hanafi usul, not every cause (sabab) triggers liability; only those that are treated as effective causes ('illa) do. This lesson explores the key principle from Usul al-Shāshī: when a cause is considered equivalent to an effective cause, the ruling (liability) is attached to it. We will examine several examples that illustrate this principle and its exceptions, sharpening your ability to distinguish between direct and indirect causation in legal contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Cause
First, pinpoint the act that is claimed to be a cause (sabab) in the scenario. For instance, placing a boy on an animal, driving an animal, giving testimony, or directing a thief.
The cause may be indirect.
2
Determine Attribution
Ask: is the resulting harm commonly attributed to this cause in everyday language or legal reasoning? If yes, the cause may be treated as an effective cause.
Consider how people describe the situation: 'he destroyed it by driving the animal'.
3
Check for Intervening Agents
If there is a direct agent (e.g., the boy driving the animal, the thief stealing), check whether that agent is independent. If the agent is independent and not compelled, the cause is not treated as effective.
The boy's choice breaks the attribution.
4
Apply Exceptions
Liability may arise even from a mere cause if the cause itself is a prohibited act (like a pilgrim directing hunting in ihram) or if the cause involves a breach of duty (like a trustee directing a thief).
These are not based on attribution but on the nature of the act.
5
Conclude the Ruling
Decide whether liability is imposed on the cause. Summarise the principle: a cause is treated as an effective cause when the ruling is attributed to it because the direct agent's act is considered a necessary consequence of the cause.
This is a rule of istinbāṭ, not a case-specific fatwa.

The Cause That Substitutes for the Effective Cause (Sabab Na'ib 'an al-'Illah)
Defining when a sabab replaces an 'illah for ease.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When the true effective cause is hidden, the Lawgiver substitutes a visible cause to ease the duty.

In Hanafi usul, the 'illah (effective cause) of a ruling is not always directly observable. To facilitate the believer, the Lawgiver sometimes designates a tangible sabab (cause) that stands in place of the hidden 'illah. This lesson explains this principle and illustrates it with three classic fiqh examples.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
Read the first sentence: the sabab may replace the 'illah when the latter is difficult to ascertain, for ease.
Key phrase: 'when ascertaining the reality of the effective cause is difficult'.
2

Once substitution occurs, the hidden 'illah is no longer considered; the ruling is directly attached to the sabab.
The author says: 'consideration of the effective cause is dropped, and the ruling is attached to the cause.'
3
Analyze the First Example
Complete sleep replaces hadath as the cause for nullifying wudu. Why? Because hadath is hidden; sleep is observable.
Sleep is easier to ascertain than actual impurity.
4

Valid seclusion (khalwah sahihah) replaces actual intercourse for the completion of dowry and obligation of 'iddah.
Seclusion is a clear event; actual intercourse is private and uncertain.
5

Travel replaces hardship for concessions such as breaking fast and shortening prayer. Even a sultan touring his kingdom qualifies if he covers the travel distance.
The concession is attached to travel itself, not the actual hardship.

The Borrowed Cause: Distinguishing Metaphor from Reality in Sabab
Defining borrowed cause and giving examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a borrowed cause (sabab musta'ar) and distinguish it from a real legal cause (sabab haqiqi) using the Hanafi criterion of consistency.

Core Idea
A borrowed cause contradicts its effect; a real cause does not.

In usul al-fiqh, the term 'sabab' can be used metaphorically. This lesson clarifies the distinction between a real cause and a borrowed cause (sabab musta'ar). Using two examples—the oath and conditional suspension—you will learn how to spot a borrowed cause and why it matters for correct legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Read the opening statement: 'And sometimes something other than a cause is metaphorically called a cause.' Recognize that the author is introducing a figurative usage of 'sabab'.
2
Understand the criterion
The key criterion: a real cause does not contradict the existence of its effect. If the supposed cause ceases when the effect occurs, it cannot be a real cause.
This is the litmus test for distinguishing real from borrowed causes.
3
Apply to the oath example
The oath is called a cause of expiation, but the real cause is breaking the oath (hanth). The oath ends when the effect (obligation of kaffarah) occurs, showing contradiction.
4
Apply to the conditional suspension example
Suspending a ruling (ta'liq) is called a cause, but the real cause is the occurrence of the condition. The suspension ends when the condition occurs, again showing contradiction.
5
Conclude the pattern
Both examples share the same pattern: the supposed cause ceases exactly when the effect takes place. Hence, they are borrowed causes, not real ones.
This pattern helps identify other potential borrowed causes in fiqh.

The Fourfold Classification of Preventers (Mawani') in Hanafi Usul
Classifying obstacles in legal causation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of preventers (mawani') in Hanafi usul and apply the classification to examples such as selling a free person and conditional suspension (ta'liq).

Core Idea
A preventer can block the inception, completion, perfection, or continuance of the legal cause or ruling.

In Hanafi legal theory, a preventer (mani') is not merely an absence of a condition, but an active obstacle that intercepts the causal chain between the 'illah (cause) and its legal effect. Imam Abu Zayd al-Dabusi provides a fourfold classification that sharpens our understanding of how and at what stage a preventer operates. This lesson unpacks each type and illustrates the first with tangible examples from sales and conditional suspensions.

Type 1 prevents the cause from existing at all; type 2 allows the cause to exist but not reach full effectiveness.

Divisions of al-Mani' in Relation to the 'Illa
Parses the types of preventer and the terminological dispute over takhsis al-'illa.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four types of preventer (mani') under the Hanafi view (permitting takhsis al-'illa) from the three types under the opposing view, and explain the terminological difference between them.

Core Idea
The dispute over takhsis al-'illa reduces to a terminological shift: what one school calls a preventer of the ruling, the other calls a preventer of the 'illa's completion.

In Hanafi usul, a preventer (mani') can block a legal cause ('illa) at different stages. This passage from Usul al-Shashi clarifies that the classification of preventers depends on whether one permits specification of the 'illa (takhsis). By parsing the examples and the two competing frameworks, you will grasp a foundational principle that governs qiyas (analogical reasoning) across schools.
