The Authority of Ijma and Its Four Categories
Establishing Ijma as a binding proof and classifying its ranks

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four categories of Ijma according to Hanafi usul and explain why the first category is equivalent to a Quranic verse.

Core Idea
Ijma is a binding proof, and its first category—explicit Companion consensus—carries the weight of a Quranic verse.

After dealing with the Quran and Sunnah, Usul al-Shashi now turns to Ijma (consensus) as the third source of law. This passage establishes Ijma as a binding proof (hujja) in subsidiary matters and then introduces a four-tier ranking that helps jurists weigh the authority of different types of consensus. The first category is particularly strong, equated to a verse from the Book of Allah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of Ijma
Read the first sentence: Ijma of the Ummah after the Prophet in furu' matters is a proof that obligates action. Note that it is a hujja and that it is specifically in subsidiary (furu') matters, not usul.
Ijma does not apply to matters of belief that require textual proof.
2
List the four categories of Ijma
The text enumerates four divisions. Write them down: 1) Companion Ijma by explicit statement, 2) by explicit statement of some and silence of others, 3) Ijma of later generations on a new matter, 4) Ijma on a previous opinion.
These categories differ in how consensus is formed and the strength of evidence.
3
Understand the ranking of the first category
The text states the first is 'like a verse from the Book of Allah.' This means it is qat'i (definitive) in authority, not open to ijtihad.
This ranking is unique to Hanafi usul.
4
Apply the categories to a hypothetical scenario
Consider a case: all Companions agree that a new transaction is permissible. That is category 1. If most agree and a few remain silent, it is category 2. Later Tabi'un agree on a new issue, category 3. They agree on a single Companion's opinion, category 4.
Practice classifying examples to internalize the hierarchy.
5
Assess the binding nature of each category
All four are hujja, but their authority differs. The first is like the Quran; others are still binding but with less epistemological weight.
This affects how they are used in tarjih (preference) when proofs conflict.

The Four Ranks of Ijma and Their Evidentiary Authority
Defines Ijma and its four ranks with analogies to textual proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four ranks of Ijma, identify their evidentiary weight, and distinguish which opinions are considered in Ijma according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Ijma has four ranks: the strongest equals Quran, the weakest equals a solitary hadith.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, Ijma (consensus) is a primary source of law, but not all consensus carries the same authority. This passage from Usul al-Shashi categorizes Ijma into four distinct ranks, each with a different evidentiary value. Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for correctly weighing sources when deriving rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four divisions
The passage lists four types of Ijma. Write them down in order.
Memorize the sequence: 1) explicit consensus of Companions, 2) consensus by explicit statement of some and silence of others, 3) consensus of later scholars on a new matter, 4) consensus on an earlier Companion's statement.
2
Associate each rank with its analogy
The author compares each rank to a type of text: Quran, mutawatir, mashhur, ahad. Memorize these pairings.
First rank → Quranic verse; second → mutawatir hadith; third → mashhur hadith; fourth → ahad hadith.
3
Understand the basis for the rank
Why is the first rank like Quran? Because it is explicit and certain. Why is the fourth like ahad? Because it is a later consensus on an earlier opinion.
The authority decreases as we move from the generation of the Companions to later ones, and from direct statements to secondary endorsements.
4
Restrict the valid participants
Only ahl al-ray wa al-ijtihad (mujtahids) count. Note who is excluded: al-awamm, the mutakallim or muhaddith without usul insight.
This exclusion ensures that only qualified scholars shape consensus.

Compound Ijma (Ijma Murakkab) – Definition and Authority
Defines compound Ijma and illustrates with a wudu nullification example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of compound Ijma, identify its example, and explain why it ceases to be a proof if the underlying cause is corrupted.

Core Idea
Ijma on a ruling is valid even if mujtahids differ on its cause, but it loses authority if the cause is proven unsound.

Ijma is a primary source of law in the Hanafi school. But not all Ijma is alike. This lesson introduces a classification of Ijma into compound (murakkab) and non-compound (ghayr murakkab). Compound Ijma occurs when all mujtahids agree on the ruling of a particular incident, yet differ on the effective cause ('illa) that justifies that ruling. Understanding this concept is crucial because it clarifies when consensus remains binding and when it may be invalidated. We will examine a classic example from wudu nullification and explore the condition that keeps this Ijma authoritative.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the division of Ijma
The passage states that Ijma is divided into two types: compound (murakkab) and non-compound (ghayr murakkab). Identify that this lesson focuses on the definition and ruling of the first type.
Memorize the two categories.
2
State the definition of compound Ijma
The definition: agreement on the ruling of a legal incident (haditha) despite disagreement on the underlying cause ('illa). The key is that the ruling is unanimously held, but the reasoning differs.
The 'illa may be different, but the ruling is the same.
3
Parse the example
The example is the consensus that both vomiting and touching a woman nullify wudu. Hanafis say the cause is vomiting, while the other school (e.g., Shafi'is) say it is touching. Yet all agree on the nullification ruling.
This shows that difference in 'illa does not prevent Ijma.
4
Understand the condition of validity
The passage states that this type of Ijma does not remain a proof (hujja) after the appearance of corruption (fasad) in the 'illa. That means if the 'illa is proven unsound, the consensus on the ruling becomes invalid.
The Ijma is contingent on the soundness of the reasoning.
5
Apply the implication
If a later scholar demonstrates that the 'illa is corrupt, the ruling can no longer be attributed to Ijma and must be derived from other sources. Thus, compound Ijma is a proof only as long as the underlying cause remains valid.
This protects the integrity of Ijma as a source.

Definition of Compound Ijma (الإجماع المركب)
Defining compound ijma with example

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Compound Ijma: consensus on the ruling, not the cause.

Classical usul scholars divided Ijma into two types: simple and compound. Compound Ijma occurs when scholars agree on a specific legal ruling for a case, but disagree on the underlying cause (illa). This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces this concept and provides a vivid example from the nullification of wudu.

Note that 'العلة' (cause) and 'المأخذ' (basis) are used interchangeably. The ruling is built on the cause.

Core Idea
Absence of a differentiation proponent is ijma only if the two cases share the same source of disagreement.

Scholars sometimes base rulings on the fact that no one has distinguished between two similar cases. In Hanafi usul, this 'absence of a proponent of differentiation' is considered a type of consensus (ijma), but only under specific conditions. Examples of Illah Known by Consensus
Providing examples of illah established by consensus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify examples of illah established by ijma' and apply the principle of extending rulings via qiyas using these causes.

Core Idea
Consensus on the cause permits analogical extension of the ruling to similar cases.

In Hanafi usul, the effective cause (illah) for a ruling can be established not only by explicit scriptural text but also by consensus (ijma). This lesson examines concrete examples from the Usul al-Shashi to illustrate how consensus-determined causes allow jurists to extend a known ruling to analogous cases through qiyas. Understanding these examples strengthens your ability to apply the mechanism of qiyas when the illah is known by ijma'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concept of illah bi al-ijma'
Understand that consensus on the cause itself is a valid method for establishing the illah. This is distinct from the illah being explicitly stated in the Qur'an or Sunnah.
Ijma' as a source for illah is accepted in Hanafi usul.
2
Study the first example: blood from a burst vein
Recognize that the bursting of blood (infijar al-dam) is taken as the cause for impurity. This cause is then used to extend the ruling of impurity to phlebotomy (fasd) and cupping (hijama) through qiyas.
The original case is a drop from a burst vein; the ruling of impurity extends to similar blood-letting practices.
3

Minority (sighar) is the cause for the father's guardianship over a minor boy. By consensus, this same cause extends the ruling of guardianship to a minor girl.
The extension is based on the common illah of vulnerability due to minority.

Puberty accompanied by reason (bulugh 'an 'aql) is the cause for removal of the father's guardianship over a boy. This cause extends to a girl, so guardianship ends for both when they reach rational puberty.
The cause is the attainment of capacity, regardless of gender.

For a woman with chronic bleeding (mustahada), the bursting of blood is the cause for invalidation of her state of purity (wudu). This cause extends to other individuals in similar conditions.
The cause is the actual bursting, not the mere presence of blood.

Understanding Mu‘āraḍah in Hanafi Usul
Explaining the concept of mu'arada with an example from masah in wudu.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mu‘āraḍah (objection) in Hanafi usul and apply it to identify when a counter-analogy blocks an opponent's qiyās.

Core Idea
Mu‘āraḍah defeats an analogy by presenting a stronger counter-analogy without attacking the original proof.

This lesson introduces the concept of mu‘āraḍah, a key method in Hanafi usul for responding to qiyas arguments. Unlike naqd, which attacks the reasoning, mu‘āraḍah presents an alternative analogy that leads to a different ruling, thereby blocking the opponent's conclusion. We will examine a classic example from wudu regarding the repetition of wiping (masah).

Guided Path
1
Define mu‘āraḍah
Understand the meaning of objection in usul and how it differs from naqd.
Mu‘āraḍah does not invalidate the opponent's proof; it blocks its application.
2
Identify the opponent's qiyās
Break down the analogy: masah is a pillar (rukun) in wudu, and since ghusl (washing) is also a pillar and is repeated thrice, masah should be repeated thrice.
The opponent uses the illa of 'being a pillar' to transfer the ruling of tathlith (thrice repetition) from ghusl to masah.
3
Identify the Hanafi response (mu‘āraḍah)
The Hanafi presents a counter-analogy: masah is a pillar, but like wiping over khuff and tayammum (both pillars with single wipe), it is not repeated. This counter-example shows that the opponent's analogy is invalid.
The Hanafi does not deny that ghusl is repeated; he offers a different case that contradicts the claimed ruling.
4
Classify the objection
Recognize that this is mu‘āraḍah because the Hanafi uses a conflicting analogy rather than attacking the opponent's reasoning.
In naqd, one would argue that 'being a pillar' does not entail tathlith; in muʿāraḍah, one proves that it does not by example.
5
Apply the concept to another scenario
Think of a similar case where mu‘āraḍah might be used, e.g., an opponent analogizing tayammum to wudu in all aspects. The Hanafi responds by citing differences in rulings.
Practice identifying the structure: opponent's qiyās → counter-analogy → ruling preserved.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why legal rulings are attached to causes and identify time as the cause of the obligation of prayer using the Hanafi definition of sabab.

Core Idea
Rulings are hidden; causes are visible signs appointed by Allah to indicate when a ruling applies.

This passage opens the chapter on causes (asbab) of legal rulings. The author establishes that obligation (wujub) is a hidden reality—we cannot perceive it directly. Therefore, Allah has attached rulings to perceptible causes so that the servant can know when a ruling becomes obligatory. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi conception of sabab and its role in legal reasoning.

Guided Path

Understand why rulings need causes

This analogy confirms that the sabab is the factor that makes the command operative.

Inferring Absence of Ruling from Absence of Cause: A Fallacy in Usul
Explains a flawed method of argumentation in usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fallacy of istidlal bi-‘adam al-‘illa ‘ala ‘adam al-hukm and explain why the Hanafi school rejects it.

Core Idea
Absence of one cause does not prove absence of the ruling — rulings may have multiple causes.

Knowing what is not evidence is as important as knowing what is. This lesson examines a common flawed argument: assuming that because a specific cause is absent, the ruling itself must be absent. The classical text warns against this error with vivid examples, showing that a ruling may stand on other proofs. Understanding this fallacy sharpens your ability to evaluate legal reasoning critically.

Guided Path
1
Name the Fallacy
Identify the Arabic term and its meaning: 'al-istidlal bi-‘adam al-‘illa ‘ala ‘adam al-hukm' — inferring absence of the ruling from absence of the cause.
This is one type of 'al-ihtijaj bi-la dalil' (argumentation without evidence).
2
Examine the Examples
Read the three examples: vomiting and wudu, brother and slavery, and the partner of a minor. In each case, someone claims the ruling is absent because a particular cause is absent.
The examples show that the claimed cause is not the only possible basis.
3
Identify the Logical Error
The error is assuming that the cause is exclusive (مُنْحَصِرَة). The absence of one cause does not logically entail the absence of the effect; other causes or evidence may exist.
Analogous to saying 'He did not die because he did not fall from the roof' — there are many ways to die.
4
Recognize the Hanafi Principle
The Hanafi school holds that such reasoning is invalid. The correct approach is to seek all possible evidence for a ruling, not to negate it based on the absence of one factor.
This principle protects against premature dismissal of valid rulings.
5
Apply the Principle to New Cases
When you encounter an argument that 'X is not required because the text does not mention it', check whether you are falling into this fallacy. The absence of explicit mention does not prove absence of obligation.
Always ask: could there be another cause or evidence?

The passage criticizes this reasoning as analogous to saying 'he did not die because he did not fall from a roof'—i.e., absence of one potential cause does not prove the absence of the effect. The references to al-Mughni and Kashf al-Asrar are for further reading.

Key Terms
الاستدلال بعدم العلة على عدم الحكم (al-istidlāl bi-ʿadam al-ʿilla ʿalā ʿadam al-ḥukm): A fallacious argument that if the cause is absent, the ruling must be absent.
الاحتجاج بلا دليل (al-iḥtijāj bi-lā dalīl): Arguing without proper evidence, i.e., using invalid reasoning.
