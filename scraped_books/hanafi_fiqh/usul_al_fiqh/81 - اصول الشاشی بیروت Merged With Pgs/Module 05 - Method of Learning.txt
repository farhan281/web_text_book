The Four Primary Sources of Usul al-Fiqh
Foundational statement listing the four sources.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four primary sources of Islamic jurisprudence according to the Hanafi school and explain the basic role of each.

Core Idea
The four sources are Quran, Sunnah, Ijma‘, and Qiyas — the foundation of Hanafi legal derivation.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi opens the study of legal theory by declaring that the principles of jurisprudence rest on four pillars. Understanding these sources is the first step in the method of deriving rulings (takhrij al-ahkam). The Hanafi school, while accepting these four, also recognizes additional sources like istihsan and ‘urf, but these four form the core.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the four sources in order
The passage lists them in a specific sequence: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma‘, Qiyas. Commit this order to memory as it reflects the hierarchy of authority.
2
Identify the primary source (Quran) and its authority
The Quran is the foundational source, revealed directly from Allah. All other sources derive their authority from or are subordinate to the Quran.
3
Distinguish between Sunnah and Quran
Sunnah includes the sayings, actions, and approvals of the Prophet (peace be upon him). It clarifies and complements the Quran but is secondary in authority.
Hanafi usul treats Sunnah mutawatira as qat‘i, but ahad as zanni.
4
Define Ijma‘ and Qiyas
Ijma‘ is the consensus of qualified mujtahids of the Muslim community on a legal ruling. Qiyas is analogical reasoning, extending a ruling from an original case to a new case due to a shared effective cause (illah).
Ijma‘ is a secondary source; Qiyas is a method of derivation.
5
Recognize that Qiyas is instrumental, not revelatory
Qiyas is a tool used by jurists to derive rulings when no explicit text or consensus exists. It does not create new law but applies existing principles.
Hanafi school requires the illah to be explicit or deducible from the text.

The first is the Book of Allah (the Quran), which is the primary and most authoritative source. The second is the Sunnah of the Prophet (peace be upon him), which includes his sayings, actions, and tacit approvals. The third is the consensus of the scholars of the Muslim community (ijma‘). The fourth is analogical reasoning (qiyas), which extends rulings from established principles to new cases based on shared effective cause. Definition and Ruling of al-Mu'awwal
Defines al-mu'awwal and states its ruling with an example from sales.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mu'awwal (interpreted meaning) and apply its ruling of obligatory action despite possibility of error to a new scenario.

Core Idea
Al-mu'awwal: a once-ambiguous word is given a preponderated meaning that must be acted upon, even though it could be wrong.

In this lesson, we examine a key term in Hanafi usul: المؤول (al-mu'awwal). When a word has multiple possible meanings, the jurist uses contextual clues to preponderate one meaning, but the possibility that the other meaning was intended remains. This tension—obligation to act while acknowledging fallibility—is at the heart of much ijtihad. The source text from Usul al-Shashi defines it clearly and gives a practical fiqh example from sales.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
Locate the word 'المؤول' in the Arabic text. It is the subject of the definition.
The definition begins with 'ثم إذا ترجّح بعض وجوه المشترك بغالب الرأي يصير مؤولاً'.
2
Parse the definition into its components
Break down the definition: (1) subject: a shared term (مشترك), (2) condition: one meaning is preponderated (ترجّح) by dominant opinion (غالب الرأي), (3) result: it becomes mu'awwal.
The word 'مؤول' is the passive participle of 'أوّل' meaning to interpret or prefer.
3
Understand the ruling (حكم)
The text states 'حكم المؤول: احتمال الخطأ مع وجوب العمل به'. Learn that the mu'awwal carries two attributes: possibility of error (not certainty) and obligation to act.
This distinguishes mu'awwal from nass (clear text) which carries certainty.
4
Apply the definition to the sales example
The example: when price is mentioned absolutely, it is interpreted as the dominant currency of that country. This is a ta'wil because 'thaman' is shared among currencies, and context preponderates one.
The preponderance is based on 'غالب نقد البلد' (dominant currency of the country).

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'المؤول' (the interpreted/preponderated) in Usul al-Fiqh. When a homonymous (مشترك) term has multiple possible meanings, and one meaning is stronger (raajih) based on dominant opinion or contextual clues, that meaning is called 'mu'awwal'. The ruling is twofold: (1) there remains a possibility of error (احتمال الخطأ) because the other meaning is not impossible, (2) despite that possibility, it is obligatory to act upon this preponderated meaning. The example given is from sales law: when someone mentions a price in absolute terms (e.g., 'I sell this for a thousand'), it is interpreted according to the prevalent currency in that country (e.g., if the common currency is dollars, then it's dollars). This interpretation is a form of ta'wil because the word 'thaman' (price) could refer to any currency, but the context of the country gives preponderance to the commonly used one.

Key Terms
المؤول (al-mu'awwal): A homonymous word whose meaning is determined by preponderance (ترجيح) based on dominant opinion; it carries the ruling of probable correctness with obligation to act.
غالب الرأي (ghalib al-ra'y): Dominant opinion or strong probability that tips the balance toward one meaning over others.

Definition of 'Ibarah al-Nass (Explicit Expression)
Definition of the explicit expression of the text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of 'ibārat al-naṣṣ and identify whether a given meaning is directly intended by the text.

Core Idea
Explicit expression: the meaning for which the words are directly formulated.

This lesson introduces the first category of textual implications: the explicit expression (عبارة النص). Understanding this concept is foundational for distinguishing between primary and secondary meanings derived from sacred texts. The definition is simple but essential for proper istinbāṭ.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Read the Arabic definition: 'فأما عبارة النص فهو ما سيق الكلام لأجله'. Identify each word.
2
Identify the key term
The term 'عبارة النص' means 'explicit expression of the text'. Note that it refers to the meaning the speech is intended to convey.
3
Analyze the condition
The definition includes 'سيق الكلام لأجله' – the speech is formulated for that meaning. This is the condition: the meaning must be the direct purpose of the utterance.
If a meaning is not the direct purpose, it falls under a different category.
4
Contrast with other categories
Keep in mind that 'ibārah is only one of four categories. The others (ishārah, dalālah, iqtiḍā') give meanings indirectly.

Contrast with dalālah which may require reasoning.

Apply an example
From the verse 'فلا تقل لهما أف' the explicit meaning is not to say 'uff'. The indicated meaning is the prohibition of hitting, because hitting is worse than saying 'uff'. This is derived from the construction, not additional words.
This example is from later Hanafi works; the text does not provide it, but it is standard.

The Principle of Adā' (Performance) as the Basis of Obligation
States a fundamental usul principle with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle of adā' and apply it to distinguish when performance of the exact duty is required versus when substitution is permitted.

Core Idea
Performance (adā') is the default; substitution (qadā') is only allowed when performance is impossible.

This lesson introduces a foundational principle of Hanafi uṣūl al-fiqh: al-aṣl huwa al-adā'. Understanding this principle is crucial because it shapes the default obligation in many areas of fiqh, from deposits and agency to liability for usurpation. The passage also shows how this principle is applied consistently, even when the subject matter has changed.

Guided Path
1
State the principle
Identify the core idea: the primary obligation is to perform the exact act (adā') or return the exact item. Only when that is impossible do you resort to substitution (qadā').
In Arabic: الأصل هو الأداء.
2
Identify the scope of application
The text gives specific categories where this principle operates: deposits (wadīʿa), agency (wakāla), and usurpation (ghaṣb). Recognize that in each, the specific item (ʿayn) is what must be returned, not an equivalent.
These are examples, but the principle is general.
3
Apply the principle to a new case
Consider the sale with a defect: the buyer has the option to return the exact item (radd) or keep it. The default is to return the specific item, not seek compensation.
This shows adā' even in contracts.
4
Contrast with Shafi'i's application
Imam al-Shafi'i applies the same principle to usurpation even after radical change (e.g., wheat ground, beam built into house). Note that he insists on returning the original item plus compensation (arsh).
This illustrates how the same principle can yield the same ruling in Hanafi? Actually, Hanafi may differ on radical change – but the passage does not state Hanafi's position, only Shafi'i's.

Commentary

Preference of Literal Meaning over Figurative (Al-Haqiqah Awla)
Stating a rule of interpretation: when a word has both literal and figurative meanings, the literal is preferred.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that literal meaning (haqiqah) is preferred over figurative (majaz) to interpret ambiguous texts and explain the practical consequence in the dispute over the daughter from zina.

Core Idea
Literal meaning is preferred over figurative unless a clear contextual indicator shifts to the figurative.

This passage introduces a core principle of Hanafi usul al-fiqh: when a word has both a literal and a figurative meaning, the literal is given precedence. This rule is not merely theoretical; it directly impacts fiqh rulings. The author illustrates this with the controversial case of a daughter born from adultery, where the Hanafi school applies the literal meaning of 'bint' to prohibit marriage, while the Shafi'i school adopts a figurative interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principal rule
The text states 'al-haqiqah awla' — the literal meaning is more worthy. Understand that this is a default presumption in Hanafi usul.
2
Analyze the underlying rationale
Why is haqiqah preferred? Because it is the original meaning; majaz requires a qarinah (contextual indicator) to be valid.
3
Examine the illustrative example
The daughter from zina: Hanafis take 'bint' literally as biological daughter, so marriage is forbidden. Shafi'is take it figuratively as legal daughter (from valid marriage), so marriage is permissible.
4
Trace the usul reasoning
Hanafi position relies on the rule of haqiqah awla; Shafi'i position relies on a qarinah (absence of valid marriage) that shifts to majaz.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical outcome: in Hanafi, the daughter is mahram; in Shafi'i, she is not, so marriage is allowed.

Imam al-Shafi'i, however, considered the relationship not to be one of legal fatherhood because the child is not born in wedlock, so the prohibition does not apply. The Hanafi argue that the word 'daughter' (bint) is literal in this context because she is the man's offspring, regardless of the circumstances of conception. Thus, by applying the rule that the literal meaning is preferred, the prohibition applies.

حقيقة (haqīqah): The literal meaning of a word, i.e., the meaning for which it was originally coined and is used without need for context.
مجاز (majāz): The figurative meaning of a word, used with a contextual indicator (qarinah) preventing the literal meaning.

When proofs conflict, follow the hierarchy: Quran → Sunnah → Athar → Qiyas; use opinion only when no substitute exists.

The second major topic in this passage from Usul al-Shashi is the method for resolving apparent conflicts between legal proofs. Arguments Without Evidence: Exclusion of Cause and Istishab
Explanation of two types of argument without evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that absence of a cause only negates a ruling when the cause is exclusive, and distinguish the Hanafi view of istishab as a proof only for defense from the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
Absence of cause does not prove absence of ruling unless the cause is exclusive; istishab defends but cannot impose.

In this lesson, we examine two categories of argument that lack direct textual evidence: inference from absence of cause (istidlal bi-‘adam al-‘illa) and presumption of continuity (istishab al-hal). The author, a Hanafi jurist, clarifies the limited conditions under which the first is valid and explains why the Hanafi school restricts istishab to defense only, contrasting with the Shafi'i position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of weak arguments
Recognize that the passage covers two distinct types: inference from absence of cause (istidlal bi-‘adam al-‘illa) and istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity). Both are considered arguments without direct evidence.
2
Understand the rule for inference from absence of cause
The default position is that absence of a cause does not prove absence of a ruling. The passage gives examples where this inference fails: vomiting not invalidating just because it didn't exit from the two passages, and a brother not being freed for another brother just because there is no parentage. The inference is only valid when the cause is exclusive (munhasira) to the ruling.
3
Work through the examples of exclusivity
When the cause is exclusive, the inference becomes sound. The passage provides examples: the offspring of a usurped item is not guaranteed because it is not itself usurped (usurpation is the exclusive cause of guarantee in that context); and a retracting witness in a qisas case is not liable because he is not the killer (killing is the exclusive cause of qisas).
The relationship between cause and ruling must be necessary and exclusive.
4
Understand istishab al-hal and the Hanafi position
Istishab presumes that a state of affairs continues until evidence proves otherwise. The Hanafi school holds that istishab is only a proof for defense (daf‘), i.e., to repel a claim, but not for imposition (ilzam) or establishing new rulings. The Shafi‘i school considers it a proof for both defense and imposition.
The key distinction is whether the presumption can be used to create new obligations.
5

Consider a dispute over whether a debt has been paid. Under the Hanafi view, the creditor can use istishab to defend the claim that the debt still exists (original state), but cannot impose a new obligation like interest. The debtor can use istishab to defend against payment if they claim the debt was paid, but cannot demand a new debt. The burden of proof lies on the party asserting a change.

Inference from Absence of the Cause (al-Istidlal bi-'Adam al-'Illa)
Defines a conditional method of inference using two examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the condition that absence of a ruling is inferred from absence of its cause only when the cause is exclusive and necessary.

Core Idea
Absence of a ruling is inferred from absence of its cause only when the cause is the sole necessary condition.

Legal rulings are intrinsically linked to their causes (عِلَل). When a cause is exclusive (munhasira) and necessary (lazim) for a ruling, its absence logically indicates the ruling's absence. Imam Muhammad's examples from liability and qisas illustrate this precise method of inference, distinguishing it from broader presumptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
The inference from absence of cause to absence of ruling is valid only when the cause is exclusive (munhasira) and necessary (lazim) for the ruling.
Exclusive means the ruling has no other cause; necessary means the ruling cannot exist without this cause.
2
Examine the first example: offspring of a stolen animal
Imam Muhammad states that the offspring of a stolen animal is not guaranteed (not liable) because it is not stolen. Here, the cause of liability (ghasb) does not apply to the offspring, so liability is absent.
The ghasb is the exclusive and necessary cause for the liability of the stolen item itself.
3
Examine the second example: retracting witnesses in qisas
Witnesses who retract their testimony in a qisas case are not subject to qisas because they are not the actual killers. The cause of qisas (being the killer) is absent.
Qisas requires direct causation; the witness's testimony, even if retracted, does not make them a killer.
4
Generalize the logical structure
In both examples, the absence of the cause (ghasb, qatl) leads to the absence of the ruling (daman, qisas) because the cause is lazim (necessary) for the ruling.
If the cause were not exclusive, the ruling might still exist from another cause.

Istishab al-Hal: Scope of Presumption of Continuity in Hanafi Usul
Defines istishab and elaborates its scope with fiqhi examples and a madhhab comparison.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on the scope of istishab and apply the Hanafi position to new cases involving continuity of a prior state.

Core Idea
Istishab is a proof for defense and preservation, not for imposing new obligations or annulling existing ones.

Istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) is a foundational principle in usul al-fiqh. This lesson presents its definition, the Hanafi position that it is valid only for defense (daf') and preservation (ibqa'), and contrasts it with the Shafi'i view allowing both defense and annulment. Examples from menstruation, lineage, and missing persons illustrate the practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of istishab
Istishab means ruling on a thing according to its previous state until evidence of change arises. It is a presumption based on absence of contrary evidence.
The previous state must have been established by proof (e.g., a person is alive until death is proven).
2
Identify the Hanafi restriction
Hanafis hold that istishab is a proof only for daf' (repelling a claim) and ibqa' (preserving the status quo), not for raf' (annulment) or ilzam (imposing new obligations).
The rationale: existence does not necessitate persistence; it only prevents unwarranted change.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Shafi'is consider istishab a proof for both daf' and raf'—i.e., it can also create new rulings (e.g., after a period, a missing person may be declared dead).
This difference leads to divergent rulings in cases like missing persons or continuous bleeding.
4
Analyze the example of a person of unknown lineage (majhul al-nasab)
If someone claims a majhul al-nasab is a slave and causes injury, the blood money for a free person is not due because such payment would be an imposition (ilzam) without evidence.
The original state of freedom is presumed unless overridden.
5
Examine the menstrual examples
A woman with a known habit: bleeding beyond her habit is istihadah (irregular) because it could be either menstrual or non-menstrual; istishab preserves her habit. A girl reaching puberty with istihadah: her period is set at ten days (the maximum) because fewer days would be uncertain; after ten days, evidence (that menstruation does not exceed ten) changes the ruling.
These examples show how istishab is used for preservation, not creation.
6
Note the missing person case
The case of the missing person (mafqud) is cited as evidence that absence of evidence is a proof for defense, not imposition. Hanafis do not declare death based on istishab alone.
This contrasts with the Shafi'i view where after a certain period, the missing person may be deemed dead.
