The Hanafi Principle of Reconciling Hadith with Quran
Stating the Hanafi principle of avoiding abrogation of Quran by hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a hadith cannot abrogate a Quranic ruling by reinterpreting the hadith's addition as a separate obligation that does not alter the original Quranic fard.

Core Idea
A hadith can never change a Quranic ruling; the Quranic fard remains intact, and the hadith's addition becomes a lesser duty.

This passage introduces a foundational Hanafi usul principle: when a hadith appears to add a requirement to a clear Quranic command, the Quranic ruling is preserved as an absolute obligation (fard), and the hadith's addition is assigned a lower legal rank—either sunnah, wajib, or a discretionary policy. This ensures that the Quran's explicit text is never overridden by a hadith, which the Hanafis consider zanni al-thubut (uncertain transmission) compared to the Quran's qat'i thubut. The passage illustrates this with four examples: ghusl and intention, the fornication penalty, tawaf and wudu, and ruku and intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic Ruling
Look for the clear Quranic command in each example. The Quran states an obligation in absolute (mutlaq) terms, e.g., 'flog them one hundred lashes' for fornication.
The Quranic text is considered qat'i (definitive) in both transmission and meaning.
2
Identify the Hadith Addition
Find the extra element introduced by a hadith, e.g., 'and exile for a year' added by a prophetic hadith.
The hadith is zanni (speculative) in transmission, not qat'i.
3
Apply the Hanafi Preservation Principle
State that the Quranic ruling remains unchanged (it is fard). The hadith's addition cannot abrogate or modify it. Instead, reinterpret the addition as a separate ruling with a lower status.
The principle: 'لا يزاد على حكم الكتاب بالخبر' - no addition to the Book's ruling by a hadith.
4
Reclassify the Hadith Addition
Assign a new legal category to the addition: either sunnah (recommended), wajib (obligatory but not fard), or siyasa (policy). For example, in fornication, exile becomes a political measure, not part of the hadd.
In tawaf, wudu becomes wajib compensated by dam; in ghusl, intention becomes sunnah.
5
State the Practical Consequence
Summarize the difference in practice: the Quranic act remains valid even if the hadith addition is omitted, but omission may incur a compensation (dam) or lose reward.
This preserves the Quranic text as the primary source of obligation.

Commentary
This passage presents a core Hanafi usul principle: a hadith (khabar) can never abrogate or change the fundamental ruling of the Quran. When a hadith appears to add an extra requirement, the Quranic ruling remains as a fard (obligation) in its absolute form, and the extra element is either interpreted as a sunnah (recommended) or as a wajib (obligatory) that is separate from the Quranic ruling, but does not alter the Quran's original obligation. The first example is ghusl (washing) and niyyah (intention). The Quran commands washing (ghusl) for ritual impurity absolutely; the hadith adds intention. The Hanafi ruling: the absolute washing remains fard from the Quran, and intention is sunnah from the hadith, not fard. The second example is the penalty for fornication. The Quran prescribes 100 lashes; a hadith adds exile (taghrib). The Hanafis hold that the 100 lashes remain the fixed hadd (divine penalty) from the Quran, and exile is a discretionary policy (siyasa) from the hadith, not part of the hadd. The third example is tawaf (circumambulation). The Quran says 'let them circumambulate' absolutely; a hadith says 'tawaf is prayer' implying wudu. The Hanafi ruling: absolute tawaf remains fard from the Quran, and wudu for tawaf is wajib (obligatory) from the hadith, but not a condition of validity—if wudu is omitted, a dam (sacrifice) is required to compensate. The fourth example is ruku (bowing). The Quran says 'bow with those who bow' absolutely; a hadith mentions intention. The Hanafis say absolute ruku is fard, intention is sunnah. The passage also mentions other views: Imam Malik's school holds that the Prophet consistently performed wudu in order, implying order is wajib; but the Hanafis do not make order a condition. The Zahiri school holds that basmala is wajib for wudu based on a hadith, but the Hanafis do not. Throughout, the Hanafi principle is to preserve the Quranic ruling as primary and relegate the hadith's addition to a lesser degree of obligation or recommendation.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): An unrestricted, absolute term that applies without qualification
فرض (fard): An obligation established by a definitive (qati) proof from Quran or mutawatir sunnah
سنة (sunnah): A recommended act that is not obligatory; established by a hadith that does not reach the level of qati
خبر (khabar): A hadith or report from the Prophet; may be ahad or mutawatir

Bayan al-Tabdil: Abrogation and Its Permissibility
Definition and ruling of Bayan al-Tabdil with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define bayan al-tabdil (abrogation), identify its exclusive source (the Lawgiver), and distinguish the Hanafi positions on whether a connected clarifying statement constitutes tabdil or taghyir.

Core Idea
Abrogation is a divine prerogative; humans cannot abrogate their own statements.

In this lesson, we explore the concept of Bayan al-Tabdil (abrogation) as presented in Al-Shashi's Usul. The passage establishes that abrogation is exclusive to the Lawgiver, then applies this principle to human transactions, showing that retractions of acknowledgments, divorces, and emancipations are impermissible. A key dispute between Abu Hanifa and his disciples on the classification of a connected clarification about counterfeit coins illustrates the practical application of this usul rule.

Guided Path
1
Define Bayan al-Tabdil
Identify the term and its synonym 'naskh'. Understand that it is a type of bayan that completely replaces a previous ruling.
2
Determine its hukm (ruling)
The passage states it is permissible only from the Lawgiver (Allah and His Prophet) and not from human beings. This is the core principle.
3
Apply the rule to examples
Analyze why istithna' al-kull 'an al-kull (excepting the whole from the whole) is invalid, and why retracting an iqrar, divorce, or emancipation is not allowed. Each involves an attempt to abrogate, which is forbidden for humans.
These examples show the principle in action.
4
Analyze the intra-Hanafi dispute
Examine the scenario: saying 'a thousand as loan or price' and then 'they are counterfeit'. Understand Abu Hanifa's view (it is tabdil, therefore invalid) and the disciples' view (it is taghyir, thus valid if connected).
The dispute hinges on whether the clarification changes the essence of the obligation or merely qualifies it.
5
Extract the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: if the statement is connected, according to Abu Hanifa the entire statement is invalid; according to the two, it is valid with the clarification. Identify which view is mufta bihi if any (the text does not give tarjih).

Section [on Bayan al-Tabdil]. The meaning of Bayan al-Tabdil and its ruling. As for Bayan al-Tabdil, which is abrogation (naskh), it is permissible from the Lawgiver and not permissible from the subjects. On this basis, the exception of the whole from the whole is invalid, because it is abrogation of the ruling, and retracting an acknowledgment, divorce, or emancipation is not permissible, because it is abrogation, and the slave does not have that. Commentary
This section defines 'Bayan al-Tabdil' (statement of alteration) as naskh (abrogation). It states that abrogation is only valid from the Lawgiver (Allah or the Prophet), not from human beings. Therefore, a person cannot retract an acknowledgment (iqrar) of a debt, a divorce, or an emancipation because that would be abrogation, which is not permissible for non-prophets. Similarly, 'exception of the whole from the whole' (istithna' al-kull 'an al-kull) is invalid because it effectively abrogates the original statement. The passage then gives a specific example: someone says 'I owe a thousand as a loan or as the price of a sold item' and then says 'and they are counterfeit.' According to the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad), this statement is a 'bayan of change' (bayan al-taghyir) and is valid if the clarification is connected (mawsul). According to Abu Hanifa, it is a 'bayan of abrogation' (bayan al-tabdil) and is not valid even if connected, because it changes the original obligation entirely.

Key Terms
بيان التبديل (bayan al-tabdil): A type of bayan that completely replaces a previous ruling with a new one; synonymous with naskh (abrogation).
استثناء الكل عن الكل (istithna' al-kull 'an al-kull): A statement that excepts the entirety from the entirety, e.g., 'I owe you something except that very something.'
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a legal ruling by a subsequent ruling from the Lawgiver.

Bayān al-Tabdīl: Definition and Ruling
Defines abrogative clarification and its applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define bayān al-tabdīl, state its ruling (permissible only for the Lawgiver), and apply it to distinguish valid clarifications from invalid abrogations in legal statements.

Core Idea
Bayān al-tabdīl is abrogation, permissible only for the Lawgiver; humans cannot abrogate their own statements.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi defines a crucial concept in Hanafi legal theory: bayān al-tabdīl (clarification of change), which is synonymous with naskh (abrogation). The text establishes the fundamental rule that only the Lawgiver may abrogate a ruling, then explores its implications in several fiqh examples, including an intra-Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Guided Path
1
Define bayān al-tabdīl
Identify the term and its equivalence to naskh (abrogation). Note that it is a type of bayan (clarification) that replaces a previous ruling.
The term contrasts with bayān al-taghayyur (clarification of change in attribute) used by the Sahibayn.
2
State the ruling
Who may perform bayān al-tabdīl? The text states: only the Lawgiver (صاحب الشرع). The subjects (العباد) cannot abrogate rulings.
This is a core principle in Hanafi usul: naskh is exclusive to the Lawgiver.
3
Apply to istithnā' al-kull 'an al-kull
Understand why this type of exception is invalid: it abrogates the original ruling (e.g., canceling an acknowledgment). Since humans cannot abrogate, such an exception is void.
Istithnā' al-kull 'an al-kull is equivalent to a complete revocation.
4
Analyze the dispute on counterfeit coins
Examine the example: someone acknowledges a debt then says the coins are counterfeit. Abu Hanifa sees this as bayān al-tabdīl (invalid), while the Sahibayn see it as bayān al-taghayyur (valid if connected). Identify the core usul difference: does the qualifying statement clarify the reality or change the legal effect?
This dispute shows how the definition of 'change' vs 'abrogation' impacts fiqh rulings.
5
Examine the slave girl example
Consider the scenario: a person acknowledges the price of a slave girl who perished before delivery. Abu Hanifa rules it as bayān al-tabdīl because the acknowledgment implies the sale was valid, but if the slave perished without delivery, the sale is void, so the acknowledgment contradicts the legal reality.
This reinforces the principle: the subject's statement cannot change the established legal effect.

Commentary
This passage defines a key usul concept: bayān al-tabdīl (clarification of change), which is synonymous with naskh (abrogation). The author states the fundamental ruling: only the Lawgiver (the Prophet ﷺ as a legislator, or Allah) may perform abrogation; the subjects (human beings) cannot abrogate rulings. This principle is then applied to several fiqh examples. First, 'exception of the whole from the whole' (istithnā' al-kull 'an al-kull) is invalid because it effectively abrogates the original ruling—for example, saying 'I owe you nothing' cancels the debt, which is like abrogation. Second, one cannot revoke an acknowledgment (iqrār), divorce (talāq), or emancipation (itq) because that would be abrogation, which is not within human power. Third, a dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Sahibayn) is presented: if someone acknowledges a debt of a thousand but then says the coins are counterfeit (zyūf), the two consider it bayān al-taghayyur (clarification of change) and valid if stated immediately in one breath, while Abu Hanifa considers it bayān al-tabdīl and therefore invalid even if connected. The reasoning is that the statement alters the nature of the debt (from real to fake coins), which Abu Hanifa sees as a change in the legal reality akin to abrogation. Fourth, in the case of acknowledging a price for a slave girl who has perished without delivery, Abu Hanifa rules it as bayān al-tabdīl because acknowledging the binding price implies the sale was valid, but if the slave girl perished before delivery, the sale is voided; thus the acknowledgment contradicts the legal effect, making it an invalid abrogation. This passage illustrates how usul principles directly determine fiqh rulings.

Key Terms
بيان التبديل (bayān al-tabdīl): Clarification through abrogation; a new ruling that replaces a previous one, permissible only from the Lawgiver.
بيان التغيير (bayān al-taghayyur): Clarification of a change in the attribute of an object (e.g., a debt being counterfeit) without abrogating the original legal effect.
استثناء الكل عن الكل (istithnā' al-kull 'an al-kull): An exception that excludes the entire subject matter, effectively cancelling the original ruling (e.g., saying 'I owe you nothing' after an acknowledgment).
