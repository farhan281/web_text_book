Distinguishing Sabab from 'Illah in Hanafi Usul
Defining sabab and distinguishing it from 'illah with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sabab and 'illah by identifying the intermediary in a given case, and explain the Hanafi usul relationship: ruling attached to sabab, established by 'illah, exists at shart.

Core Idea
Sabab is the occasion; 'illah is the intermediary that justifies the ruling.

This lesson unpacks the foundational Hanafi distinction between the cause (sabab) and the effective cause ('illah). While both terms are sometimes conflated, classical Hanafi usul treats them as separate: the sabab is the means, and the 'illah is the intermediary through which the ruling is established. Understanding this distinction is crucial for qiyas and for grasping how rulings attach to their occasions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the tripartite relationship
The author states that the ruling relates to three elements: sabab, 'illah, shart. Note the different verbs: 'yuta'allaqu bi sababihi, yathbutu bi 'illatihi, yujadu 'inda shartihi.' This is the skeleton of the relationship.
Each element has a distinct function: attachment, establishment, existence.
2
Define sabab
The text defines sabab as 'ma kana tariqan ila al-shay'i bi wasitah.' So sabab is the remote cause, leading to the ruling via an intermediary.
The intermediary is the 'illah.
3
Identify the intermediary (the 'illah)
In the examples, walking is the 'illah for reaching a destination via the road; lowering is the 'illah for reaching water via the rope. The 'illah is the direct means.
Without the 'illah, the sabab does not produce the ruling.
4
Formulate the legal relationship
For any ruling, the sabab is the circumstance or act that triggers the ruling, but the 'illah is the legally recognized effective cause that provides the rationale. The shart is a condition for the ruling to exist.
Example: travel (sabab) leads to qasr (ruling) via hardship ('illah), provided the condition of distance.
5
Apply the distinction to a fiqhi case
Consider the example of travel and shortening prayer. Identify each element: travel is the sabab; the journey's hardship or the distance is the 'illah; the ruling (qasr) exists when the shart (e.g., distance over 48 miles) is met.
This shows how the sabab-'illah distinction operates in practice.

The Pure Cause (Sabab Mahd) and Attribution of Rulings
Defining pure cause and its legal attribution rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define pure cause (sabab mahd), state the rule of attribution when cause and illa coexist, and apply the rule to example cases to determine liability.

Core Idea
A pure cause is a means that does not bear liability when an effective attribute (illa) of free choice intervenes.

In Islamic legal theory, not all causes are equal. When you set a chain of events, the first link may not be the one that determines the ruling. This lesson explores the concept of 'pure cause' (sabab mahd) – a cause that is merely a means, not the effective legal cause. We will see why giving a knife to a minor does not make you liable for his suicide, and why other situations differ.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of pure cause
Locate the term 'sabab mahd' in the source passage and note that it is a cause that is a means to a result but the ruling is not attributed to it because an intervening illa (choice) exists.
The passage opens with examples of causes (opening doors, releasing restraints) to illustrate the concept.
2
State the attribution rule
Extract the rule: when cause and illa coexist, the ruling is attributed to the illa, not the cause, unless attribution to the illa is impossible, then it goes to the cause.
The rule is explicit: 'والسبب مع العلة إذا اجتمعا يضاف الحكم إلى العلة دون السبب إلا إذا تعذرت الإضافة إلى العلة فيضاف إلى السبب'.
3
Apply to the minor-and-knife example
Analyze why the giver is not liable when the minor uses the knife to kill himself: the giving is a pure cause, the minor's voluntary act is the illa, so liability falls on the minor.
Contrast with the case where the knife falls and injures the minor: then the giver is liable because no illa intervened.
4
Apply to other examples
Consider the mounting a minor on an animal example and the directing-a-thief examples. Note that liability does not attach to the pure cause (mounting, directing) because the direct actor's choice is the illa.
The passage also discusses exceptions where a separate duty (depositary) or prohibition (muhrim) creates liability.
5
Distinguish from exceptions
Understand why the depositary and the muhrim are liable despite directing: for the depositary, liability is due to negligence in protective duty, not the direction; for the muhrim, the direction itself is prohibited, making it an illa.
This shows that the rule applies unless the cause itself carries a separate ruling.

The Cause of Obligation: Time as Sabab for Prayer
Explaining the concept of sabab (cause) and two analytical methods for temporal obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the concept of sabab in Hanafi usul and apply the two analytical methods (transfer of causality and consideration of conditions) to determine the temporal scope of an obligation.

Core Idea
Obligation is hidden; it becomes known through its cause, like prayer tied to the entry of time.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi explains a foundational Hanafi principle: rulings are attributed to their causes because obligation (wujub) is not directly observable. We need a sign (sabab) to know when a ruling applies. For prayer, the cause is time. This lesson will explore the reasoning behind this and the two methods scholars use to analyze how obligation extends across a time window.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sabab for a ruling
The passage uses prayer as an example. The cause (sabab) of its obligation is the entry of time. This is because the command to pray is only addressed after time enters, not before. Also, obligation applies even to those unconscious, so it must be based on time, not on hearing the command.
The sabab is an external sign, not dependent on the servant's awareness.
2
Trace the proof from the text
The author provides two proofs: (a) the khitab (command) to perform prayer is directed only after the entry of time, and (b) obligation is established upon those whom the khitab does not reach (sleeping, unconscious), so it must be based on the occurrence of time.
These proofs establish that the first part of time is the cause.
3
Apply the first method: transfer of causality
Once the first part of time is established as the cause, causality is transferred (naql) from the first part to the second, third, and so on until the end of the time window. This means the obligation becomes established for the entire duration.
This method shows how obligation spreads across time.
4
Apply the second method: consider the condition of the servant and the attribute of the part
The second method considers two factors: (1) the state of the servant (e.g., being a child at the beginning of time), and (2) the attribute of that part of time (e.g., early or late). These considerations affect whether the obligation is binding immediately or can be deferred.
The passage begins to explain this but cuts off; the idea is that the obligation adjusts based on these factors.

Obligation of Prayer Established by Entry of Time and Two Considerations
Establishes that prayer obligation is tied to time entry with two considerations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the obligation of prayer is established by the entry of time, explain the rational proof, and apply the two considerations (state of the person and attribute of the time part) to determine obligation.

Core Idea
The obligation of prayer does not exist before its time; the first part of the time is the cause, and both the person's capacity and the completeness of that part are considered.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi establishes a fundamental usul principle: time is the cause of obligation for prayers. The author argues rationally that obligation is established even for those not directly addressed (like a sleeper) once the time enters. He then introduces two important considerations that refine how the obligation is determined: (1) the state of the servant in that part of time, and (2) the attribute of that time part itself. Understanding these considerations is crucial for correctly applying time-based obligations in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its cause
The passage states that obligation (wujub) is established with the entry of time (يثبت بدخول الوقت). This is a general ruling that the prayer becomes obligatory upon the entry of its time. Identify that the cause (sabab) of this obligation is the time itself, specifically the first part of the time.
The author provides a rational proof: obligation is established even on those not addressed by the command, like a sleeper, once time enters.
2
Understand the rational proof (dalil aqli)
The proof is: 'ولأن الوجوب ثابت على من لا يتناوله الخطاب كالنائم والمغمى عليه' (obligation is established upon one who is not addressed by the command, like the sleeper and the unconscious). This shows that the cause is external (time entry), not the person's awareness. Trace how this proves that the first part of time is the sabab.
The proof is rational (dalil aqli) because it relies on logical necessity: obligation is not dependent on being addressed, so it must be tied to an external factor (time).
3
Distinguish the two ways after establishing the cause
The author mentions two ways after the cause: one is transferring causality from the first part to successive parts, and the other is the two considerations. For this lesson, focus on the two considerations: the state of the slave and the attribute of the part.
The 'first part' (الجزء الأول) is the initial segment that triggers obligation; it is not necessarily the very beginning of time but a part that can be considered the effective cause.
4
Apply the first consideration: state of the person
The first consideration evaluates the person's capacity (e.g., adulthood, Islam, purity) in that part of time. If a person gains capacity (like reaching puberty or converting to Islam) at that part, the prayer becomes obligatory. Conversely, if they lose capacity (like menstruation or insanity), the obligation drops. Also, travel/residence is considered at the end of time.
Examples: a child at the beginning but adult in that part -> wajib; a disbeliever at beginning but Muslim in that part -> wajib; a resident at beginning but traveler at end -> prays 2 rak'ahs.
5
Apply the second consideration: attribute of the time part
The second consideration looks at whether that part of time is 'complete'. If it is complete, the obligation is fully established and cannot be fulfilled by performing the prayer at a disliked time. The passage mentions that the end time for Fajr is complete, meaning it is a full part of time.
This consideration relates to the quality of the time segment. If the part is incomplete (e.g., due to being very short), the obligation might be affected.

[مثاله]: وذلك نحو إجراء كلمة الكفر على اللسان مع اطمئنان القلب عند الإكراه، وسب النبي صلى الله عليه وسلم وإتلاف مال المسلم، وقتل النفس ظلماً. [حكمه] أنه لوصبر حتى قتل يكون مأجوراً لامتناعه عن الحرام تعظيماً للشارع. والنوع الثاني: تغيير صفة الفعل بأن يصير مباحاً في حقه، قال الله تعالى: فمن اضطر في مخمصة. [مثاله]: وذلك نحو الإكراه على أكل الميتة وشرب الخمر. [حكمه] أنه لوامتنع عن تناوله حتى قتل يكون آثماً بامتناعه عن المباح وصار كقتل نفسه.
As for rukhsah (license), it is an expression of ease and facility. In the Shari'ah, it is diverting a command from difficulty to ease by means of an excuse in the legally responsible person. [Its types]: Its types are different due to the difference of their causes, and they are the excuses of servants, and ultimately they revert to two types, one of which: The license of the act while the prohibition remains, in the position of pardon in the chapter of transgression. [Its example]: That is like uttering the word of disbelief upon the tongue while the heart is at ease under coercion, and insulting the Prophet (peace be upon him), and destroying the property of a Muslim, and killing a person unjustly. [Its ruling]: That if he is patient until he is killed, he is rewarded for refraining from the prohibited out of reverence for the Lawgiver. The second type: Changing the description of the act so that it becomes permissible for him. Allah ta'ala says: 'But if one is forced by hunger.' [Its example]: That is like coercion to eat carrion and drink wine. [Its ruling]: That if he refrains from consuming it until he is killed, he is sinful for refraining from the permissible, and he becomes like one who kills himself.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Shashi's Usul defines the term 'rukhsah' (license/dispensation) in Islamic jurisprudence. Linguistically, it means ease and facility. Technically, it is a concession that changes a ruling from difficulty to ease due to an excuse (udhr) that applies to the mukallaf (legally responsible person). The author then divides rukhsah into two fundamental types based on the nature of the concession. The first type is 'rukhsat al-fi'l ma'a baqa' al-hurma' – a license to perform an act while its original prohibition remains intact. The act is still haram in itself, but due to necessity, the person is excused for committing it. This is analogous to 'afw' (pardon) in the chapter of jinayah (criminal transgression). Examples include uttering kufr under coercion while the heart remains firm in faith, or being forced to insult the Prophet (peace be upon him), destroy Muslim property, or kill an innocent person unjustly. The ruling: if the person patiently endures and is killed for refusing, he is rewarded for his forbearance and avoidance of the prohibited act out of reverence for the Shari'ah. The second type is 'taghyir sifah al-fi'l' – a change in the description of the act, making it actually permissible (mubah) for the person in that situation. The act becomes halal for him due to the excuse. The Quranic evidence is the verse 'fa-man-idturra fi makhmasatin' (whoever is forced by hunger, etc.). Examples include being coerced to eat carrion or drink wine. The ruling: if the person refrains and consequently dies, he is sinful because he rejected a permissible concession and caused his own death.

Key Terms
الرخصة (al-rukhsah): In Shari'ah, diverting a command from difficulty to ease due to an excuse in the mukallaf.
العذر (al-‘udhr): An excuse that lifts the normal obligation or prohibition, such as ikrah (coercion), illness, or travel.

The Two Types of Rukhsah: License with Remaining Prohibition and Permissibility
Classification of rukhsah into two types with examples and rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the two types of rukhsah, provide examples, and state the ruling for each type.

Core Idea
Rukhsah either allows a prohibited act while keeping it prohibited (type 1) or changes the act to permissible (type 2).

The author now classifies rukhsah into two fundamental types depending on the nature of the concession. Type 1 pardons the doer without lifting the prohibition; type 2 makes the act lawful. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying concessions correctly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
The passage describes type 1 as 'rukhsat al-fi'l ma'a baqa' al-hurma' and type 2 as 'taghyir sifah al-fi'l'. Note the key difference: the prohibition remains in type 1 but is lifted in type 2.
Type 1 is like pardon (afw) in criminal law.
2
Analyze the examples
For type 1: uttering kufr under coercion, insulting the Prophet, destroying property, killing unjustly. For type 2: eating carrion and drinking wine under coercion. Why are these different? Identify the common thread: in type 1, the acts are intrinsically harmful and prohibition is absolute; in type 2, the acts are normally prohibited due to impurity/harm but become permissible when necessary.
The excuse in both is coercion (ikrah).
3
Determine the rulings
Type 1: if the person refrains until killed, he is rewarded. Type 2: if he refrains until killed, he is sinful. Contrast the outcomes. The difference lies in whether the act is inherently evil (type 1) or only conditionally forbidden (type 2).
This shows that in type 2, the act actually becomes permissible; rejecting it is rejecting a mercy.
4
Note the Quranic evidence
The author cites the verse 'faman idturra fi makhmasatin' which is part of the verse permitting carrion in necessity. This supports type 2. No explicit dalil for type 1 is given; it is based on qiyas or general principles.
The verse is from Surah al-Ma'idah (5:3) or similar.
