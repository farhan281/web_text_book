Combining Literal and Metaphorical in Oaths and Wills: The Role of Urf
Rulings on interpreting oaths and wills based on urf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when customary usage (urf) shifts a word from its literal meaning to a metaphorical one and derive the correct legal ruling for oaths and wills in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
Custom determines meaning: in oaths and wills, words shift from literal to metaphorical based on urf, and both are combined for sound interpretation.

In Hanafi usul, words are presumed to carry their literal meanings, but custom (urf) can override this presumption and introduce a metaphorical reading. This lesson explores how such shifts are applied to oaths and wills, using concrete examples from the text to show when a literal reading leads to unintended consequences and how custom harmonizes the two.

Guided Path
1
Identify the word in context
First, locate the key word or phrase in the oath or will. For example, 'placing the foot' (وضع القدم) in an oath, or 'unmarried daughters' (أبكار) in a will. Note whether the context is an oath or a will, as the same principle applies to both.
The word may have a clear literal meaning but might be interpreted differently in custom.
2
Assess if literal meaning is intended or absurd
Ask: Would taking the literal meaning lead to a result that contradicts the speaker's apparent intent or common practice? For instance, if a man swears 'not to place his foot' but enters riding, the literal meaning is not met, yet custom treats any entry as breaking the oath.
The Hanafi school holds that the speaker's intent is understood through custom, not strict literalism.
3
Apply the principle: urf shifts haqiqah to majaz
Recognize that urf has turned the literal term into a metaphor for a broader meaning. In the text, 'placing the foot' becomes 'entering', and 'the house of Zayd' becomes 'any dwelling where Zayd resides', regardless of ownership.
This is called جمع الحقيقة والمجاز (combining literal and metaphorical) because both meanings are considered valid but the metaphorical takes precedence when indicated by custom.
4
Derive the legal consequence
Finally, apply this shifted meaning to the specific case. For the oath, entering any way breaks it. For the will, 'sons' excludes grandsons, and 'unmarried daughters' excludes those who have lost virginity through fornication, because custom no longer considers them 'unmarried'.
The consequence is consistent across different scenarios: custom unifies the interpretation.

Core Idea
When 'bal' corrects a divorce, the first statement takes effect if the wife is no longer a valid subject.

This lesson examines the Arabic particle 'bal' (بل) and its legal consequences when used in divorce utterances. The Hanafi school analyzes 'bal' as a particle of retraction (idrab) intended to correct a mistake by replacing the first statement with the second. However, in the context of talaq, the timing of the effect is critical: if the first pronouncement has already rendered the wife unavailable for further divorce, the retraction fails. Understanding this requires both linguistic precision and knowledge of Hanafi usul principles.

The Particle 'Lakin' for Istidrak in Usul
Defines the function and conditions of 'lakin' for correction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the function of the particle 'lakin' in legal texts, distinguish it from 'bal', and apply the coherence conditions to interpret statements containing 'lakin'.

Core Idea
Lakin corrects after negation—it affirms the second part, while the negation of the first stands by its own evidence.

In usul al-fiqh, precise understanding of particles like 'lakin' is crucial for interpreting legal texts. This lesson explores how 'lakin' functions as a tool for istidrak (correction) specifically after a negation, and the conditions under which it serves as a conjunction. We will contrast it with the particle 'bal' to sharpen your analytical skills.

Guided Path
1
Identify the presence of 'lakin'
Locate 'lakin' in the statement and determine whether it follows a negation or an affirmation.
This is the first clue to its function.
2
Check speech coherence
Ensure the speech is continuous (itasāq al-kalām) and that the scope of negation and affirmation are distinct. If not, 'lakin' starts a new sentence.
Coherence requires both connectedness and non-contradiction.
3
Apply coordination type
If coordinating single words, 'lakin' requires prior negation. If coordinating full sentences, it can follow affirmation as well.
Example: 'ما ضربت زيداً لكن عمرواً' vs. 'جاءني زيد لكن عمرو لم يأت'.
4
Distinguish from 'bal'
Note that 'bal' inherently negates the first part and affirms the second, while 'lakin' does not negate the first—it only affirms the second.
Also, 'bal' works after both affirmation and negation even for single words.

The preposition 'على' appears frequently in legal texts, but its meaning is not fixed. Hanafi usul scholars have categorized its usage into three distinct legal senses. Commentary
This passage is a definition and rule about the word 'على' (ala) in legal language, from the Hanafi usul al-fiqh text Usul al-Shashi. The author opens by stating that 'على' primarily signifies obligation (الإلزام) and its root conveys the meaning of superiority (التفوق) and connection (التعلي) — that is, a binding tie. The first example clarifies that if someone says 'فلان على ألف' (so-and-so has upon me a thousand), it is construed as a debt (dīn) because 'على' imposes an obligation. Conversely, if one uses 'عندي' (with me), 'معي' (with me), or 'قبلي' (in my possession), the meaning is merely possession, not a binding debt. The author then cites a case from al-Siyar al-Kabir (Imam Muhammad's work on international law) to illustrate: when a fortress commander says 'آمنونی على عشرة من أهل الحصن' (give me security for ten of the fortress people), the ten are separate from himself, and he has the right to choose who they are. But if he says 'وعشرة' (and ten) or 'فعشرة' (then ten) using 'و' or 'ف' (conjunctions), the ruling is the same. This shows how 'على' marks the object of the obligation. The passage then moves to two other meanings: 'على' can be metaphorical for the preposition 'باء' (ba) meaning 'with' or 'for' — especially in sale contracts, where 'بعتك هذا على ألف' means 'I sold you this for a thousand,' indicating exchange (معاوضة). Finally, 'على' can mean 'on condition that' (شرط), as in the Quranic verse about the pledge of women (Surah al-Mumtahina 60:12) and in the Hanafi ruling on divorce: if a wife says 'طلقني ثلاثاً على ألف' (divorce me three times for a thousand), it is a conditional statement. Thus, the passage systematically dissects the word 'على' into three distinct legal usages: obligation, exchange, and condition. The student should note that context — such as the nature of the transaction or the presence of a conditional clause — determines which meaning applies.

Key Terms
على (ala): A preposition in legal language primarily denoting obligation and superiority, but also used metaphorically to mean 'with' (باء) in exchange contracts and 'on condition' (شرط) in conditional undertakings.
الإلزام (al-ilzam): Obligation; the primary meaning of 'على' that imposes a binding duty.
التفوق والتعلي (al-tafa'uq wa al-ta'alluq): Superiority and connection; the root meaning of 'على' indicating a link of superiority or binding tie.
المعاوضة (al-mu'awada): Exchange; used in contracts of sale where 'على' takes the sense of 'for' (باء).
الشرط (al-shart): Condition; the usage of 'على' preceding a clause to indicate a stipulation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five key Companions foundational to Hanafi usul al-fiqh and explain their specific contributions to Islamic legal knowledge.

Core Idea
These five Companions are the pillars of knowledge transmission upon which Hanafi usul al-fiqh is built.

The Hanafi school traces its usul not only to the Quran and Sunnah but also to the Companions who were the primary transmitters and interpreters of that revelation. The passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces us to five of these foundational figures: Abdullah ibn Masud, Abdullah ibn Abbas, Abdullah ibn Umar, Zaid ibn Thabit, and Muadh ibn Jabal. Each had a unique expertise—tafsir, fiqh, Quranic recitation, inheritance, and practical judgment—that shaped the legal methodology later codified by Abu Hanifa and his students.

Guided Path
1
Identify each Companion and their epithet
Read the passage carefully and note the titles given to each Companion, such as 'hibr al-ummah' for Ibn Abbas or 'shaykh al-muqri'in' for Zaid ibn Thabit. These indicate their recognized expertise.
2
Understand their relationship to the Prophet and early caliphs

3

4

Consider that these Companions taught the tabi'un who later taught the founders of the Hanafi school. For instance, Ibn Masud's students in Kufa (Alqamah, al-Aswad) directly influenced Abu Hanifa.

He collected the Quran during the caliphate of Abu Bakr, then copied it during the caliphate of Uthman. Al-Sha'bi said: Zaid surpassed the people in two things: inheritance (fara'id) and the Quran. Sulayman ibn Yasar said: Umar and Uthman did not prefer anyone over Zaid in inheritance, fatwa, recitation, and judgment. Ibn Abbas said: The well-preserved people among the companions of Muhammad knew that Zaid ibn Thabit was among those firmly grounded in knowledge. Yahya ibn Sa'id said: When Zaid ibn Thabit died, Abu Hurairah said: The scholar of the Ummah has died; perhaps Allah will make Ibn Abbas his successor. He died in the year forty-five. See his biography in Siyar A'lam al-Nubala' 2/426-441, al-Isti'ab 2/537, Usd al-Ghabah 2/278, al-Isabah 4/041.

Muadh ibn Jabal ibn Amr, the leader, the imam, Abu Abd al-Rahman al-Ansari al-Khazraji al-Madani al-Badri. He attended the Pledge of Aqabah as a beardless youth. Abdullah ibn Amr said: Take the Quran from four: from Ibn Masud, Ubayy, Muadh ibn Jabal, and Salim the freedman of Abu Hudhayfah. Umar addressed the people at al-Jabiyyah and said: Whoever wants fiqh should go to Muadh ibn Jabal. Umar used to say when Muadh went to Syria: His departure has emptied Madinah and its people of fiqh and of what he used to give them fatwa upon. I spoke to Abu Bakr to detain him due to the people's need for him, but he refused, saying: A man who wants a cause—meaning martyrdom—I will not detain him. Muadh died in the year seventeen in the Ghawr of Khalid in Jordan.

Commentary
This passage is a biographical section from Usul al-Shashi, providing tarajim (biographies) of major Companions who are foundational sources of knowledge for the Hanafi school in usul al-fiqh. The text first lists a group of Companions and then gives detailed entries on Abdullah ibn Masud, Abdullah ibn Abbas, Abdullah ibn Umar, Zaid ibn Thabit, and Muadh ibn Jabal. These individuals are pivotal because they transmitted Quran, Sunnah, and legal rulings. The author cites statements from later scholars praising their knowledge, piety, and contributions. For instance, Ibn Masud is described by Hudhayfah as most resembling the Prophet in guidance, and Umar sent him to Kufa as a teacher. Ibn Abbas is called 'the scholar of the Ummah' and was prayed for by the Prophet to be taught the Quran's interpretation. Ibn Umar is noted for his strict adherence to the Prophet's practice and his long tenure as a mufti. Zaid ibn Thabit was the scribe of revelation and expert in inheritance calculations. Muadh ibn Jabal was sent to Yemen and was considered the gateway to fiqh. These biographies establish the chain of knowledge transmission upon which Hanafi usul relies. The author, likely Sadr al-Shari'ah (or the compiler), includes these to affirm the authority of these figures as primary sources of Islamic legal methodology.

Key Terms
حبر الأمة (hibr al-ummah): A title meaning 'the scholar of the Ummah', used for Ibn Abbas and also for Zaid ibn Thabit by Abu Hurairah.
الفرائض (al-fara'id): The Islamic science of inheritance shares; Zaid ibn Thabit was a master of this discipline.
المقرئين (al-muqri'in): Quran reciters; Zaid was their shaykh.
