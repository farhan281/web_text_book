Furu' of Mudaddah: Fasting, Prayer, and Menstruation
Practical applications of mudaddah: fasting, prayer, menstruation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate whether a prohibition attached to an act nullifies its validity based on the 'illa (harm vs. intrinsic prohibition) by analyzing the Hanafi examples of fasting on Nahr/Tashriq, prayer at disliked times, and intercourse with a menstruating woman.

Core Idea
A prohibition based on harm ('adha) does not nullify the act's legal effects, while a prohibition that cannot be separated from the act (like Eid fasting) nullifies commencement.

The previous lesson established the principle of mudaddah. Now we explore three practical examples (furu') from the same passage: vows to fast on forbidden days, prayer at disliked times, and intercourse with a menstruating woman. These demonstrate how the 'illa (reason) behind the prohibition determines whether the act remains valid and whether its legal effects follow.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and its prohibition
Act: fasting on day of Nahr/Tashriq, praying at disliked times, intercourse with menstruating woman. Prohibition: can be intrinsic (ḥarām) or due to harm (adha).
2
Determine the 'illa of the prohibition
For fasting and prayer, the prohibition is due to the time (Eid, disliked times). For menstruation, the prohibition is due to harm (adha) as per Quran 2:222.
3
Check if the act can be completed without committing a forbidden act
For prayer at disliked times, one can wait until the time passes to complete without dislike. For Eid fasting, completing the fast necessarily involves the forbidden act (fasting on a prohibited day). For menstruation, the act itself is allowed once the impurity ends.
4
Derive the ruling on validity and commitment
If completion is possible without the forbidden element, the act is valid and binding once started. If completion inevitably involves the forbidden, the act is not binding if started (or the start is discouraged).
5
Apply the Quranic evidence for menstruation
Verse 2:222 shows the prohibition is due to harm (adha), so legal effects like ihsan, mahr, and iddah are established because the act is not intrinsically void.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi explains the usul principle of how prohibition (nahy) interacts with different types of contracts. The author distinguishes between two scenarios. First, when the purpose of the contract is directly opposed to the prohibition (contradiction), the prohibition renders the contract void and is interpreted as negation of validity. This applies to invalid marriages: marrying polytheist women, one's father's wife, a woman in iddah, incestuous marriages, and marriage without witnesses, because marriage aims at permissibility of sexual relations, and the prohibition aims at forbidding it—they cannot coexist. Second, when the purpose of the contract (ownership) is not directly opposed to the prohibition (unlawful disposal), the contract remains valid but the action is unlawful. The analogy of grape juice fermenting in a Muslim's property illustrates: ownership remains, but drinking is prohibited. The author then gives practical furu: vows to fast on forbidden days (Nahr, Tashriq) are valid because the act of fasting itself is permissible, though the time is disliked—the prohibition does not remove the intrinsic permissibility. Similarly, praying at disliked times (makruh) is valid; if one starts a nafl prayer at such a time, it becomes binding to complete, because completion without the disliked element is possible (e.g., waiting until the sun rises). This differs from fasting on Eid, where completing the fast inevitably involves a forbidden act, hence Abu Hanifa and Muhammad rule that starting such a fast is not binding. Finally, intercourse with a menstruating woman: the prohibition is due to harm (adha), so it does not negate the legal effects—ihsan (stoning obligation), dissolution of marriage for the first husband, dower, and iddah are established. This demonstrates that the type of 'illa (reason) behind the prohibition determines whether it nullifies the act or merely prohibits it while leaving other effects intact.

Two Key Usul Rules: Preference for Literal Meaning and Avoidance of Unnecessary Takhsis
Defining two methods for understanding intended meaning from legal texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the two Hanafi usul principles—preferring haqiqa over majaz and avoiding unnecessary takhsis—by identifying the preferred interpretation in a given legal text and stating its fiqh consequences.

Core Idea
When interpreting legal texts, the Hanafi school gives priority to literal meaning and avoids unnecessary restrictions.

This passage introduces two foundational rules that guide how Hanafi usul scholars determine the intended meaning of a text. These rules help resolve ambiguities between literal and figurative meanings, and between interpretations that require exceptions versus those that don't. By mastering these rules, you will be able to follow the Hanafi method of istinbat (derivation) in practice.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two interpretative rules
First, note that the passage presents two distinct methods: (1) when a word has both a literal (haqiqa) and metaphorical (majaz) meaning, the literal is preferred; (2) when two interpretations are possible, the one that does not require an exception (takhsis) to the text's general application is preferred.
These rules are known as 'al-haqiqa muqaddamah ala al-majaz' and 'al-haml ala ma la yastalzim al-takhsis awla.'
2
Apply the first rule: preference for haqiqa
Consider the example of the daughter born from zina. The word 'daughter' in the Quranic prohibition is haqiqa (literal) for any biological daughter. The Hanafis argue that since she is a literal daughter, the prohibition applies. The Shafi'i view treats her as not a legal daughter, which would be a metaphorical interpretation. According to the rule, the literal meaning takes priority.
This rule prevents arbitrary metaphorical readings that would limit the text's application.
3
Apply the second rule: avoidance of takhsis
Now examine the word 'lamastum' in the wudu verse. It can be interpreted as intercourse (haqiqa) or as hand touch (majaz). If interpreted as intercourse, the ruling applies universally without exceptions. If interpreted as hand touch, many exceptions arise (e.g., touching mahrams does not break wudu). The rule says to choose the interpretation that avoids unnecessary takhsis.
Thus, the Hanafi preference for 'intercourse' is based on this rule, ensuring the text's general application is preserved.
4
Derive fiqh consequences from the chosen interpretation
Once the interpretation is chosen, derive the related fiqh rulings. For the daughter example: prohibition of marriage, obligation of dower, inheritance, etc. For the touch example: the ruling on wudu, permissibility of prayer, touching the Quran, etc. These consequences vary between schools based on the interpretive rule applied.
The passage lists these consequences to show the practical impact of the rule.

Commentary
This passage presents two fundamental rules of usul al-fiqh for interpreting legal texts. The author begins by stating that there are multiple methods to determine the intended meaning. The first rule: when a word has both a literal (haqiqa) and a metaphorical (majaz) meaning, the literal meaning is given priority. The example given is the famous dispute between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i regarding the daughter born from zina. The Hanafis hold that such a daughter is considered the literal daughter of the fornicator, and thus falls under the Quranic prohibition of marrying one's daughters, making her marriage forbidden. Al-Shafi'i considers her not a daughter in the legal sense, so marriage is permissible. The passage notes that various rulings branch out from this difference, including permissibility of intercourse, obligation of dower, maintenance, inheritance, and guardianship. The second rule: when there are two possible interpretations (mahmal) of a word or phrase, and one interpretation would require making an exception (takhsis) to the general application of the text while the other does not, the interpretation that avoids the exception is preferred. The example given is the word 'lamastum' (touch) in the verse about wudu. If interpreted as intercourse, the rule about wudu breaking applies universally; if interpreted as physical touch with the hand, then many exceptions arise (e.g., touching mahrams or a small child does not break wudu). The Hanafi position interprets it as intercourse (which is their view), while al-Shafi'i interprets it as physical touch, but even in his school there is a ruling that touching certain persons does not break wudu. The passage notes that various rulings, such as the permissibility of prayer, touching the Quran, entering the mosque, validity of imamate, and the need for tayammum, derive from this interpretive difference.

Key Terms
حقيقة (haqiqa): The literal meaning for which a word was originally coined; the primary, non-figurative sense.
مجاز (majaz): The figurative or metaphorical meaning that a word can carry beyond its literal sense, requiring a contextual indicator.
تخصيص (takhsis): Restricting the general application of a text (amm) to some of its individuals or cases, thereby creating an exception.
محمل (mahmal): One of the possible interpretations or meanings that a word or phrase can bear, especially in disputed contexts.

Preferring Interpretation Without Takhsis
States a principle and gives example on lamastum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that when a text admits two interpretations, the one that avoids takhsis is preponderant, using the example of 'lamastum' in Quran 5:6.

Core Idea
When a text can be interpreted two ways, prefer the one that leaves it unrestricted.

The Hanafi usul tradition values preserving the general applicability of a text. This lesson introduces a key principle: if one possible interpretation of a word or phrase would require specifying (takhsis) the text, while another would not, the latter is preferable. We explore this through the famous Quranic verse 'or you have touched women' and its implications for wudu rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the text and its two interpretations
The Quranic verse 'aw lamastum al-nisa' can be interpreted literally as 'touching with the hand' or figuratively as 'intercourse'. Recognize both possible meanings.
These are called mahmal (possible interpretations) in usul.
2
Assess each interpretation for takhsis
Determine whether each interpretation would require restricting the general scope of the command. The touching interpretation requires specifying that some instances (e.g., touching mahram, small child) do not break wudu, thus restricting the text. The intercourse interpretation applies uniformly to all cases of intercourse.
Takhsis is the specification of a general term to some of its instances.
3
Apply the principle
Since the interpretation without takhsis is preponderant, the preferred interpretation is intercourse. This means the verse is generally applied to all cases of intercourse without exception.
This is a preference, not an absolute rule; other evidence can override it.
4
Observe the derived rulings
From this principle, different fiqh rulings emerge: the Shafi'i school adopts intercourse as the nullifier of wudu; the Hanafi school holds to the literal touching interpretation based on other proofs.
The passage states that rulings on prayer, touching mushaf, entering mosque, etc. are derived according to the two schools.

Combining Multiple Qira'at or Riwayat
Introduces rule for handling multiple readings of a text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that when a text has multiple readings (qira'at) or versions (riwayat), the preferred method is to give effect to all by allocating each to a distinct context.

Core Idea
Two readings of the same verse are not contradictory; each applies to a different scenario, making both operative.

This lesson introduces a key hermeneutic principle in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: when a Quranic verse or hadith is transmitted in more than one recitation or narration, the ideal approach is to apply each reading to a distinct context so that both are given effect. This principle avoids rejecting any authentic transmission and demonstrates the depth of Hanafi textual analysis. The passage provides two detailed examples from the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Read the opening statement: if a text is recited in two ways or narrated in two versions, it is better to apply it in a way that gives effect to both.
2
Analyze the first example (wa arjulakum)
Note the two readings: nasb (accusative) and khafd (genitive). Understand the grammatical context: nasb indicates washing, khafd indicates wiping.
The verse is Quran 5:6.
3
Apply the principle to the example
Assign each reading to a different condition: nasb for when not wearing khuffs (washing), khafd for when wearing khuffs (wiping). Thus both readings are operative.
4
Analyze the second example (hatta yat-hur-na)
Note the two readings: tashdid (heavy) and takhfif (light). The heavy reading indicates thorough purification, the light reading indicates mere cessation of blood.
The verse is Quran 2:222.
5
Apply the principle to the second example
Assign each reading to a specific duration: takhfif applies when menstrual days are ten (maximum), tashdid applies when days are less than ten.

The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) hold that 'thumma' implies delay in the legal ruling (hukm), meaning that the three utterances are spoken together without temporal gap, but their legal effect is deferred until the condition (if any) is fulfilled, and then they apply in order, but only one divorce takes effect because the other two are considered redundant or invalid according to the rule that three divorces given at once count as three only in specific circumstances. The passage provides examples to illustrate the practical consequences. For a non-consummated woman (ghayr madkhul biha), where divorce is immediately effective, the difference leads to different outcomes. When the condition precedes the divorces, Abu Hanifa rules that the first divorce is tied to the condition, the second falls immediately, and the third is void; the companions rule that all are suspended until the condition, and only one divorce occurs. When the divorces precede the condition, Abu Hanifa rules the first occurs immediately and the others are void; the companions again tie all to the condition, resulting in one divorce. For a consummated woman (madkhul biha), the rulings are more complex: if the condition is stated first, Abu Hanifa ties the first to the condition and lets two fall immediately; if the condition is after, two fall immediately and the third is tied. The companions consistently tie all to the condition in both scenarios. This dispute stems from a different understanding of the linguistic function of 'thumma': whether it modifies the speech act itself or the legal consequence of that speech act.

Core Idea
Context determines whether 'ila' includes or excludes the limit – never assume one meaning.

The Arabic particle 'ila' (إلى) seems simple but carries a subtle distinction in legal reasoning. This lesson unpacks the Hanafi usul classification of 'ila' into two types: imtidad (extension) where the limit is excluded, and isqat (exclusion) where the limit is included. The particle 'ala shifts meaning: in exchange contexts it acts as 'with'; as a condition it triggers contingency.

This lesson explores a critical usul principle: interpreting common linguistic particles. The particle 'ala (upon/on) can function as 'ba' (with) in exchange contexts or as 'shart' (condition) in pledges. Understanding this distinction directly affects rulings in contracts such as khul'. By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the ikhtilaf between Imam Abu Hanifa and his companions on the legal effect of omitting or including the preposition 'fee' in divorce oaths, and identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).

Core Idea
The literal meaning of 'fee' indicates partial containment, altering the scope of temporal and spatial oaths.

In Hanafi usul, a single preposition can change the ruling of an oath. This passage presents a disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two main disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, regarding the word 'fee' (in) when used in divorce oaths linked to time or place. The dispute turns on whether 'fee' implies generality or partiality. By understanding this ikhtilaf, you will see how linguistic precision directly affects legal outcomes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ikhtilaf
Distinguish the two positions: the two companions hold that deleting or showing 'fee' is equivalent; Abu Hanifa differentiates. State each position clearly.
The companions: both forms mean divorce at the earliest moment. Abu Hanifa: without 'fee' it is immediate; with 'fee' it is vague.
2
Analyze the linguistic basis
Examine Abu Hanifa's reasoning: the word 'fee' indicates containment within a container (dharf), implying a part, not the whole. Without 'fee', there is no container, so the default is the first part.
This is based on the literal meaning of the preposition.
3
Apply to temporal examples
Work through the fasting example: 'If you fast the month' (no 'fee') means the entire month; 'If you fast in the month' (with 'fee') means any part, even one hour. See how the container limits the scope.
4
Apply to spatial examples and general principle
For place, the principle is extended: if the act is intransitive, the agent must be in the place; if transitive, the object must be in the place. Use the mosque examples to see the application.
Note that divorce itself is not spatially limited, so 'in the house' is interpreted metaphorically as 'upon entry'.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
In practice, Abu Hanifa allows the husband to delay the effect by intention when he says 'fee'; the companions do not allow such delay. This is the practical consequence.

The Letter Ba as an Indicator of Attachment in Contracts
Definition and legal application of the letter Ba

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic meaning of the letter Ba in Hanafi usul and apply it to identify the role of the substitute in a sale.

Core Idea
The letter Ba in contracts indicates attachment, making the substitute the price, not the sold item.

In this lesson from Usul al-Shashi, we explore the linguistic and legal function of the simple letter Ba. Understanding its meaning of attachment (ilṣāq) is crucial for interpreting contracts correctly. The author demonstrates that when Ba precedes the price in a sale, it attaches the price to the sold item, clarifying which party is principal and which is follower.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning of Ba
The letter Ba is originally for attachment (الإلصاق) in Arabic linguistic usage. This is the foundational meaning from which legal implications follow.
This is a memorization anchor: Ba = ilṣāq.
2
Distinguish the roles in a sale
In a sale, the sold item (المبيع) is the principal (الأصل), and the price (الثمن) is a condition (شرط) and a follower (التبع). The follower is attached to the principal.
Destruction of the sold item cancels the sale; destruction of the price does not.
3
Apply the rule: Ba on the substitute
When the letter Ba enters upon the substitute (البدل) in a sale, it indicates that the substitute is the follower attached to the principal, so it becomes the price (ثمن), not the sold item.
The Ba marks the substitute as the attached thing (الملصق به).
4
Understand the structure of attachment
Attachment involves two sides: the attached thing (الملصق) and the thing attached to (الملصق به). Ba enters upon the latter. Example: 'I wrote with the pen' – the writing is attached, the pen is the thing attached to.
The purpose is to connect an attribute to a name.
5
Note the legal consequence
Because the price is a follower attached to the sold item, its destruction does not terminate the sale, whereas destruction of the sold item (the principal) does.
This distinction is crucial in contractual disputes.

Permission for Leaving: Each Time vs Once
Parses two exception constructions to show different legal effects.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that the particle and construction in an exception determine whether permission is required each time or once, using the example of conditional divorce.

Core Idea
Wording matters: 'except with my permission' demands repetition; 'except that I permit you' suffices once.

This lesson from Usul al-Shashi demonstrates a core Hanafi linguistic principle: the same legal outcome—divorce conditional on leaving—can produce different results based solely on the phrasing of the exception. By parsing the constructions 'illā bi-idhnī' and 'illā an adhana laki', we learn how the attachment of permission to each act versus a single act of permission affects the ruling. Understanding this prepares you to analyze similar conditional statements in contracts, oaths, and family law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two wordings
Locate the two conditional statements from the passage: (1) 'except with my permission' (illā bi-idhnī) and (2) 'except that I permit you' (illā an adhana laki).
2
Parse the first wording
Analyze 'illā bi-idhnī': the particle 'bi' (with) attaches the permission directly to the act of leaving. This suggests that the permission is a condition for each individual instance of leaving.
3
Parse the second wording
Analyze 'illā an adhana laki': the subordinating conjunction 'an' (that) followed by the verb 'adhana' (I permit) indicates a single event of permission that removes the condition for all future leave-takings.
The structure 'an + verb' points to a one-time action.
4
Derive the ruling for each
Based on the parse: for the first, permission is required each time she leaves; for the second, once he permits her, she may leave any number of times without further permission.
5
State the usul principle
The general rule: the linguistic form of an exception determines the scope of the permission. A attached exception (bi- plus noun) implies repetition; a verbal exception (an + verb) implies a single occurrence.
This is an example of how amm (general) and khass (specific) interact in Hanafi usul.

Bayan al-Taqrir: Definition and Examples
Defining Bayan al-Taqrir and illustrating with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Bayan al-Taqrir, identify its key components (zahir, haqiqah, possibility), and apply it to examples.

Core Idea
Bayan al-Taqrir confirms the apparent meaning by explicitly stating it, cutting off any other possibility.

We often encounter expressions that have a clear apparent meaning but also allow for another interpretation. In Hanafi usul, when the speaker explicitly confirms the apparent meaning, this is called Bayan al-Taqrir. This lesson explains its definition and illustrates it with two practical examples from financial transactions.

1
Understand the definition
Break down the definition into three elements: (1) the word has an apparent meaning (zahir), (2) it allows another possible meaning, (3) the speaker clarifies that the intended meaning is the apparent one.
Zahir is the meaning that comes first to mind but still has another possibility.
2
Analyze the first example (qafiz of wheat)
Identify the apparent meaning (the local measure and currency), the alternative possibility (another measure/currency), and how the clarification confirms the apparent.
3
Analyze the second example (deposit)

4
Generalize the rule
The passage states this applies to every literal expression (haqiqah) that might be metaphorical (majaz) and every general term ('amm) that might be specified (takhsis). Recognize that Bayan al-Taqrir cuts off the possibility and settles the ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi theory of exception to derive the ruling in a given case and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Exception in Hanafi usul means the speaker never uttered the excepted part; the ruling only applies to the remainder.

Exception (istithna) is a key tool in usul al-fiqh for clarifying the scope of a general ruling. The Hanafi and Shafi'i schools differ fundamentally in how they conceive the effect of an exception: does it prevent the ruling from ever applying to the excepted case, or does it carve out a case from an otherwise established rule? This dispute has real consequences, as we will see in the well-known hadith about selling food for food. By the end of this lesson, you will understand the Hanafi position and its implications.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on istithna
Identify the core Hanafi claim: the speaker, when making an exception, is considered to have only spoken about the remainder. The excepted part is as if it was never mentioned.
This is a radical view – the exception does not ‘remove’ something from a ruling; it means the ruling never encompassed the excepted case at all.
2
State the Shafi'i position on istithna
Identify the Shafi'i claim: the initial general statement establishes a legal cause (illa) for the entire class, and the exception blocks that cause from applying to the excepted case.
For Imam al-Shafi'i, the exception operates like the absence of a condition in a conditional obligation.

Both schools cite the same hadith: 'Do not sell food for food except equal for equal.' Recognize that the dispute is not about the text but about its legal interpretation.

This difference arises from their respective theories of exception.

Apply the theories to the case of selling a handful of wheat for two handfuls. Hanafi: permitted (since equality cannot be determined). Shafi'i: prohibited (since it is unequal).

Clarification by Act in Ambiguous Statements
Expansion probe with intra-Hanafi dispute and consensus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Hanifa denies clarification through intercourse in ambiguous manumission and apply the principle to distinguish exclusive vs. non-exclusive acts.

Core Idea
An act clarifies an ambiguous statement only if the act's legal basis is exclusive to one specific option.

When a person makes an ambiguous declaration—like 'one of you is divorced' or 'one of you is free'—a subsequent act may resolve the ambiguity. But not every act suffices: the act must be legally exclusive to one of the possibilities. This lesson explores a classic dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two companions over whether intercourse clarifies ambiguous manumission, and identifies a consensus on other acts that do clarify.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule for ambiguous divorce
Read the first part of the passage: if a man says 'I divorce one of my two wives' and then has intercourse with one, that act clarifies that the other wife is divorced. Ask: why does intercourse work here? Because intercourse with a wife is exclusive to marriage—it can only happen with one's wife, so the act points uniquely to the woman not involved.
The exclusivity of the act's permissibility is key.
2
Contrast ambiguous manumission
Now consider the manumission case: 'One of you is free' to two slave women, followed by intercourse with one. According to Abu Hanifa, this does NOT clarify which slave is freed. Why? Because intercourse with a slave woman can be permissible via two different legal bases: ownership (the master can have intercourse with his slave) or marriage (if the master marries her). The act of intercourse is not exclusive to ownership, so it does not reveal which basis is operative.
The lack of exclusivity prevents the act from serving as bayan.
3
Analyze the opposing view of the two companions
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad analogize to the divorce case: just as intercourse clarifies divorce, so it should clarify manumission. But Abu Hanifa rejects this analogy because the illa (reason) differs: in divorce, intercourse is exclusive to marriage; in manumission, it is not exclusive to ownership. This shows the importance of precise causal analysis (tahqiq al-manat).
The disagreement is over the effective cause (illa) for bayan.
4
Apply the consensus on other acts
The passage ends with a consensus: if after ambiguous manumission, the master sells, pawns, makes a kitaba contract, designates as mudabbara, or kills one of the slaves, that act DOES clarify the manumission of the other. These acts are exclusive to ownership—they cannot be done to a free person or a wife. Thus they satisfy the exclusivity condition.
These acts all presuppose ownership of the acted-upon slave.

Conjunction as Clarification in Hanafi Usul
Rule on conjunction as clarification with three cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a conjunction clarifies a general term according to Hanafi usul, and apply the rule to given statements, distinguishing the majority view from that of Abu Yusuf.

Core Idea
Conjunction clarifies only when the conjoined item can be a debt in the dhimma (measured/weighed).

This lesson introduces a key rule in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: when a general term like 'one hundred' is followed by a conjunction (e.g., 'and a dirham'), does that conjunction specify the genus? The answer, as outlined by Imam al-Shashi, depends on the nature of the conjoined item. Understanding this rule helps you interpret legal statements accurately, especially in debts and contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general term and the conjoined item
Look for an expression like 'one hundred and X.' The general term is the number (e.g., one hundred), and the conjoined item is the noun after 'and.'
The rule applies when the general term is ambiguous (mujmal) and the conjunction provides clarification (bayan).
2
Classify the conjoined item
Determine whether the conjoined item is of a type that can be owed as a debt in the dhimma (ma yasluhu daynan fi al-dhimma). Typically, this includes measured/weighed items (makil/mawzun) like dirhams, wheat, etc. Items like a single garment or a sheep do not qualify.
Hanafi fiqh treats mithli (fungible) items as suitable for generic debts.
3
Apply the rule
If the conjoined item qualifies, the conjunction clarifies that the general term is of that same genus. If it does not, the conjunction does not clarify—the general term remains unspecified as to genus.
Exception: Abu Yusuf held that any conjunction clarifies, regardless of the nature of the item, based on the principle that all is of one genus (as in 'one hundred and three garments').
4
Check the three cases
The passage lists three cases: (1) conjoining a measured/weighed item → clarification applies. (2) Delayed explanation after two numbers (e.g., 'one hundred and three garments') → clarification applies because both numbers refer to one thing. (3) Conjoining an item not suitable as a debt → no clarification, unless it's a multi-number case as in (2).
Case (2) explains why 'one hundred and three garments' clarifies the hundred as garments, but 'one hundred and a garment' does not.

The author begins by stating the general rule: when a measured or weighable item (like a dirham or a qafiz of wheat) is conjoined to a general statement like 'one hundred,' the conjunction specifies that the hundred is of that same genus. For example, if someone says 'I owe one hundred and a dirham,' it means all one hundred are dirhams. Similarly, if he says 'one hundred and three garments,' the hundred is understood as garments. It does not apply to items like a single garment or a sheep, because such items are not typically owed as debts in their singular form (they are often specified individually). The Difference Between Sabab, 'Illa, and Shart
Defining and distinguishing sabab, 'illa, and shart in usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sabab, 'illa, and shart in Hanafi usul al-fiqh and apply the rule of attribution when both sabab and 'illa are present.

Core Idea
The ruling is attached to its sabab, established by its 'illa, and exists upon its shart.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi lays the foundation for understanding how legal rulings are occasioned. Without grasping the precise relationship between sabab, 'illa, and shart, a student cannot properly analyze the causal structure of any fiqh ruling. We will walk through the definitions and the critical example of the knife and the boy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three concepts
The passage introduces sabab (cause), 'illa (effective cause), and shart (condition). State each term and its role in relation to the ruling.
2
Understand the functional relationship
The ruling is attached to its sabab, established by its 'illa, and exists only when its shart is present. This is the core principle.
3
Define sabab and its examples
Sabab is defined as a path to something via an intermediary. Examples: the road (sabab) to reach a destination via walking (intermediary); the rope to reach water via lowering.

Sabab is the external occasioning factor; 'illa is the intermediary that directly brings about the ruling. Example: opening the stable door (sabab) leads to the animal escaping (intermediary/‘illa) which results in destruction (ruling).

When both sabab and 'illa are present, the ruling is attributed to the 'illa, not the sabab. If attribution to the 'illa is impossible, the ruling is attributed to the sabab. The knife example illustrates this.
