Definition and Ruling of Kinayah (Allusive Speech)
Defining kinayah and its legal effect requiring intention or contextual indication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define kinayah and distinguish it from sarih, applying the requirement of intention or contextual indication to determine its legal effect.

Core Idea
Kinayah is ambiguous speech; its legal effect requires niyyah or dalaalat al-haal — ambiguity alone does not trigger a ruling.

In Hanafi usul, not all speech is treated equally. Some words carry their meaning on the surface, while others are veiled. Kinayah — allusive speech — falls into the second category. Understanding its definition and legal effect is essential for correctly deriving rulings in areas like divorce and penal law, where the difference between explicit and implicit can change the entire outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition
The passage defines kinayah as 'what is hidden in meaning' (mustatir al-ma'na). Note that this is the Hanafi technical definition.
Contrast with sarih (explicit speech) which is clear on its own.
2
Recognize the requirement
Kinayah does not automatically establish a ruling; it requires niyyah (intention) or dalaalat al-haal (contextual indication) to remove hesitation and determine the intended meaning.
The text says 'there must be evidence to remove hesitation and prefer some aspects.'
3
Apply to the divorce example
Understand why expressions like 'you are forbidden' are kinayah in talaq: they do not effect divorce without intention or context, so the husband retains the right of revocation.
The wife is not immediately divorced; the matter remains revocable.
4
Apply to penal examples
See why kinayah does not trigger hudud punishments: a confession to zina or theft using allusive language is insufficient for the hadd penalty without explicit words.
Even if someone accuses another of zina and the accused says 'you have spoken the truth,' it is kinayah because it might affirm something else.
5
Extend to gestures
The mute's gesture is akin to kinayah; no hadd penalty is imposed because it lacks explicit speech, showing the same hesitation principle.
Gestures are not considered sarih in penal matters.

Commentary

The passage thus establishes that kinayah, due to its inherent ambiguity, does not trigger strict legal punishments or irrevocable actions without clear evidence of intent.

Understanding this principle is critical for correctly deriving rulings from ambiguous speech.

Understanding Dalala al-Nass: Definition, Example, and Legal Effect
Definition of dalala al-nass with example from Qur'an.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dalala al-nass, identify its linguistic cause from a given text, and apply its ruling to new cases based on the generality of the cause.

Core Idea
The cause of a ruling is understood directly from the wording (dalala al-nass), making the ruling binding for all cases sharing that cause.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, not all rulings are derived through explicit commands. Some are understood through the linguistic implication of the cause. This lesson introduces dalala al-nass, a type of textual indication where the reason for a ruling is immediately grasped from the language. Using the Qur'anic verse on honoring parents, we will see how the prohibition of saying 'uff' extends to all forms of harm, making the derived rulings as binding as the explicit text.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Read the definition: dalala al-nass is knowledge of the cause of a ruling from the text itself, linguistically, without ijtihad or deduction. Note the phrase 'la ijtihadan wa la istinbatan'.
2
Examine the example
Look at the verse 'Do not say to them 'uff'. The cause (avoiding harm) is immediately understood from the wording and context to any Arabic speaker.
The cause is not derived through qiyas.
3
Apply the ruling
The ruling (prohibition) applies to all cases sharing the same cause. The passage lists hitting, insulting, employing for hire, imprisoning for debt, and execution – all prohibited because they involve the same cause of harm.
This is the 'generality of the cause' principle.
4
Note the legal effect
The passage states that dalala al-nass is equivalent to the explicit text (nass) in authority, even for establishing hudud punishments. This is a strong claim in Hanafi usul.
It is binding proof (hujja) for rulings.

Oaths are interpreted by the first understanding of the listener, not by technical definitions.

In this passage, Imam al-Shashi illustrates a key Hanafi rule for interpreting oaths: the meaning that comes to mind at first hearing (awwal al-sama') governs. This principle reflects the school's reliance on customary ('urfi) meanings rather than literal linguistic definitions, especially in matters of intention. Understanding this helps us correctly apply the ruling in everyday oath scenarios.

The ruling is based on the listener's first understanding of the oath's purpose: to avoid consuming blood-originating flesh.

Understanding Iqtida al-Nass: The Necessary Implication
Definition and illustration of textual implication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define iqtida al-nass and identify the muqtada (implied element) in a legal text by applying the condition that the text's meaning is incomplete without it.

Core Idea
Iqtida al-nass supplies what the text must mean, not just what it suggests.

In usul al-fiqh, not every legal statement is fully self-contained. Sometimes the text itself demands an additional element for its meaning to be coherent. This is called iqtida al-nass (textual implication). The added element is called al-muqtada. In this lesson, you will learn how Hanafi usulis identify these necessary implications and how they differ from other forms of inference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the text that seems incomplete
Look for a statement that, if taken literally, would be meaningless or contradictory. For example, 'You are divorced (taliq)' uses an attribute without an explicit subject.
The attribute 'taliq' requires a source (the act of divorce) to be meaningful.
2
Determine if the text can be understood without an addition
Ask: Can this text be legally operative without adding something? If yes, no iqtida. If no, the addition is necessary.
The condition is that the text's meaning is not realized except through the addition.
3
Specify the implied element (muqtada)
Articulate exactly what must be added for the text to make sense. In the divorce example, the muqtada is the act of divorce (talaq).
The muqtada is treated as if explicitly stated for legal purposes.
4
Verify that the addition is essential for legal operation
Confirm that without the muqtada, the text would have no legal effect. For instance, without the implied act of divorce, the statement 'You are divorced' would be a mere predicate without a legal consequence.
This distinguishes iqtida from mafhum (implied meaning not essential to the text).

The Principle of Iqtida' and Its Application in Hanafi Usul
Ruling on iqtida' with example from divorce context

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule of iqtida' by necessity, apply it to the divorce example, and distinguish it from takhsis (specification).

Core Idea
Necessity only supplies the minimum for validity; no extra legal effects are implied.

This lesson examines the Hanafi usul principle of al-iqtida' (implied meaning). The passage emphasizes that iqtida' operates only to the extent of necessity—it supplies what is essential for a utterance to be valid, but no more. Using a divorce scenario, we see how this principle yields a revocable single divorce, not an irrevocable one. Understanding this boundary prevents overextension of implied meanings in legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
Read the opening: 'Necessity is removed to the extent of the absolute individual.' This sets the principle: iqtida' only supplies the minimum needed.
The phrase 'absolute individual' refers to a single, unrestricted instance.
2
Understand the necessity condition
The passage states that iqtida' cannot add unnecessary elements. It only supplies what is essential for the statement to be valid (e.g., to avoid falsehood).
3
Apply to the divorce example
The husband says 'i'taddi' (observe waiting period) intending divorce. The word 'i'taddi' implies a preceding divorce because waiting period follows divorce. Out of necessity, one divorce is deemed to have occurred.
The waiting period (iddah) is only relevant if divorce happened; thus iqtida' supplies the divorce.
4
Note the limitation on extra effects
The passage explains that irrevocability (baynuna) is not implied because it exceeds the necessity. Only revocable divorce is established, and only one divorce occurs.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle that iqtida' cannot be used to add extra legal consequences.

Coherence of Speech in Admissions with Exception
Applies the rule of ittisal al-kalam to iqrar examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of ittisal al-kalam to determine the legal effect of connected vs. disconnected negation and exception in admissions.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, an exception must be immediately connected to be valid; separation turns the negation into a rejection.

This passage applies a crucial linguistic principle from Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the coherence of speech (ittisal al-kalam). When someone makes a statement containing a negation followed by an exception or contrasting affirmation using 'but', the Hanafi school requires that the two parts be uttered without significant separation. If connected, the negation is interpreted as limited to the cause mentioned, not the underlying right. If disconnected, the negation stands alone as a rejection of the admission, and the subsequent statement is ineffective. Through concrete examples of admissions and denials, the text illustrates how this principle determines ownership and obligation.

Coherence in Ratification of Marriage by Master
Applies the same rule to ijaza of a slave-girl's marriage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of coherence (ittisaq) to evaluate the validity of a master's ratification (ijaza) of a slave's marriage and identify whether the contract is void or dissolved.

Core Idea
A ratification that contradicts itself is void; speech must be coherent to be effective.

This passage expands a key usul principle from the domain of istithna' (exception) to the domain of ijaza (ratification). The principle is that for a speech act to be legally effective, its parts must be logically consistent (ittisaq). When a master responds to a slave-girl's unauthorized marriage, his words must not contradict themselves or impose new conditions that alter the original contract. Contradiction voids the contract, and conditional statements dissolve it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial scenario
A slave-girl marries herself without permission for 100 dirhams. The master's response determines whether the marriage is ratified.
The master's ijaza is a form of speech act that must be coherent.
2
Analyze the first response
The master says: 'I do not ratify at 100, but I ratify at 150.' This is contradictory because he negates ratification at 100 but affirms it at 150, which is the same contract (only 100 existed). The speech is incoherent; therefore the original contract is void (batil).
Negation and affirmation of the same thing cannot coexist.
3
Analyze the second response
The master says: 'I do not ratify it, but I ratify it if you increase me fifty.' This is a conditional statement. A conditional ratification introduces a new term not in the original contract; it is not a clarification but a new proposal. The speech lacks coherence, so it constitutes a dissolution (faskh) of the marriage.
Conditional ratification fails because it does not simply affirm or deny the existing contract.
4
Extract the principle
The condition for valid ijaza is coherence (ittisaq). Any speech that contradicts itself or imposes new conditions without direct continuity is ineffective. The same principle applies to istithna' and other legal speech acts.
This principle is a qawida sughra (minor maxim) in Hanafi usul.
5
Apply to other scenarios
Use the same test: Is the master's response logically consistent with the original contract? If it negates and affirms the same thing, or adds new conditions that break continuity, the ratification is invalid.
Practice with hypothetical responses to master the application.

Bayan al-Taghyir and the Exception in the Hadith of Selling Food
Dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is over the effect of an exception in a hadith ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that an exception (istithna') can restrict the 'illah of a ruling, not just the ruling itself, and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i position in a given case.

Core Idea
The exception in 'except like for like' restricts the prohibition to cases where equality is demonstrable — preventing hardship.

This passage introduces the Hanafi concept of 'bayan al-taghyir' (explanation of change), where a subsequent statement qualifies a previous general ruling. The key example is the hadith 'Do not sell food for food except like for like.' The Hanafi and Shafi'i schools diverge on the scope of the exception. By analyzing this dispute, you will learn how Hanafi usul treats exceptions as potentially restricting the underlying cause ('illah) of the ruling, leading to a different practical outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the hadith and its structure
Read the hadith: 'Do not sell food for food except like for like.' Note that it contains a general prohibition followed by an exception. Recognize that the initial part establishes a general ruling ('illah for prohibition).
The exception uses the word 'illa' (except).
2
Understand the Shafi'i position
According to al-Shafi'i, the initial part creates a general prohibition on selling food for food. The exception only excludes the specific case of equality, so all other cases remain prohibited. This leads to prohibiting a handful for two handfuls, since a handful is not equal and not excepted.
3
Understand the Hanafi position
According to Abu Hanifa, the exception restricts the 'illah (cause) itself: the prohibition applies only when the servant can establish equality. Since a handful cannot be measured for equality, it is not covered by the ruling. The exception is treated like a condition that limits the scope of the general rule.
This prevents hardship (nahy al-ajiz) — not burdening people with impossible obligations.
4
Apply to the disputed case
Consider selling a handful of food for two handfuls. Hanafi: permissible (because equality cannot be measured). Shafi'i: prohibited (because it falls under the general prohibition). This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence).
The example illustrates how the same text yields different rulings based on usul principles.
