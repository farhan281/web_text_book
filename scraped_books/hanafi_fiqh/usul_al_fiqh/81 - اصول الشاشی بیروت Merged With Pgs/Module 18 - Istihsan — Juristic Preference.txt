Deriving Rulings from the Fasting Verse: The Hanafi Istinbat Method
Istinbat of various rulings from Quran 2:187 about fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of istinbat (derivation) from a Quranic verse to derive multiple rulings on fasting, including the permissibility of janaba, rinsing, tasting, and the timing of intention.

Core Idea
From the permission for intimacy until dawn, necessary implications yield rulings on janaba, rinsing, tasting, and intention timing.

The Quranic verse on fasting (2:187) is a rich source for legal derivation. In this lesson, we will follow the Hanafi method of istinbat as demonstrated by the author of Usul al-Shashi, showing how a single permission generates a chain of rulings through necessary implication (luzum), analogy (taqliyya), and inference from command structure (istidlal). You will see how the verse supports rulings that fasting with janaba is valid, that rinsing and tasting do not break the fast, and that intention need not be at the exact moment of dawn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source text and its explicit permission
Locate the operative phrase in the verse: 'Permitted to you on the night of fasting is intimacy with your wives... until dawn.' This is the textual anchor.
Focus on the permission being limited to the night, ending at dawn.
2
Apply luzum (necessary implication) to derive the ruling on janaba
If intimacy is permitted until dawn, then it is possible to be in a state of janaba at the moment dawn begins. Since the command to fast applies at dawn, the fast must be valid despite janaba, otherwise the permission would be futile.
This is a classic Hanafi use of luzum without requiring separate proof.
3
Extend by analogy to similar actions
Since janaba does not invalidate the fast, other actions that do not involve ingestion—like rinsing the mouth, sniffing water, tasting food, wet dreams, cupping, and oiling—also do not invalidate the fast.
The common factor is that none of these actions negate the essential refrainment from the three defined invalidators.
4
Derive the timing of intention from the command structure
The verse commands: 'Then complete the fast until night.' The command to complete implies that the fast is already validly commenced. Thus, the obligation to intend (niyyah) does not need to be at the very first moment of dawn; it can be made after, because the command targets the continuation, not the inception.
This supports the Hanafi view that intention for voluntary fasts can be made even during the day.
5
Apply the same method to kaffarah feeding
In expiation (kaffarah), the command is to feed. The purpose is to fulfill need. Therefore, feeding one poor person repeatedly for multiple days is valid if the need is renewed, because each feeding satisfies a separate need.
This shows how istinbat extends beyond the immediate context of fasting to related rulings.

The Hanafi View on Usufruct Compensation and the Concept of Legal Substitute
Explains Hanafi ruling on usufruct compensation and introduces legal substitute concept.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on compensation for usufruct in ghasb, identify the principle of al-mithl al-shar'i, and apply it to examples like fidya and diya.

Core Idea
Usufruct is not compensable in Hanafi fiqh except when the Lawgiver prescribes a legal substitute.

This passage presents a distinctive Hanafi principle: the usufruct of a usurped property is not subject to monetary compensation. Instead, the sin remains and its recompense is in the Hereafter. This is contrasted with the Shafi'i view. The passage then introduces the concept of the legal substitute (al-mithl al-shar'i), where the Lawgiver prescribes a substitute that does not resemble the original, yet is legally binding. Understanding this concept is key to grasping Hanafi istihsan in matters of compensation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the practical case
Consider the scenario of ghasb: someone usurps a slave and uses him for a month, or a house and lives in it. The Hanafi ruling is that no compensation for the usufruct (manāfi‘) is required upon return.
2
Recognize the madhhab difference
Note that the Shafi‘i school holds usufruct compensable. The Hanafi response: usufruct is not property (māl) with guaranteed value; the sin is sufficient and its recompense is in the Hereafter.
3
Extract additional examples of non-compensable usufruct
The text lists other instances: false testimony leading to divorce, killing a married woman, and intercourse with another's wife – none require compensation in Hanafi fiqh.
4
Define al-mithl al-shar‘ī
When the Lawgiver prescribes a substitute that does not resemble the original in form or meaning, that substitute becomes legally equivalent (mithl shar‘ī) and must be fulfilled.
This principle is an application of istihsān (juristic preference).
5
Apply the principle to examples
The text gives two examples: (1) fidya (ransom) for a decrepit elderly person who cannot fast – it is a substitute for fasting; (2) diya (blood money) for accidental killing – it is a substitute for the life of the victim. In both, the substitute is not similar but legally binding.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling and a rule. It first states the Hanafi position on compensation for usufruct (manāfi‘) in cases of ghasb (usurpation). According to Imam Abu Hanifa and his school, one who usurps an object and uses it is not liable to pay compensation for the benefit derived (e.g., living in a house or using a slave), even if the owner claims it. The sin of using the property without permission remains, but its punishment is deferred to the Hereafter. This is contrary to Imam al-Shafi‘i, who holds that usufruct is compensable. The text gives further examples: false testimony that leads to divorce, killing a married woman, or even intercourse with another man's wife—none of these require monetary compensation for the husband in Hanafi fiqh. Then it introduces the principle of al-mithl al-shar‘ī (legal substitute): if the Lawgiver explicitly prescribes a substitute for something that does not resemble it in form or meaning, that substitute is legally considered equivalent, and it must be rendered. Examples are the ransom (fidya) for an elderly person unable to fast—which is like fasting—and blood money (diya) for accidental manslaughter—which is like the life of the victim—even though there is no actual similarity. This rule is used in various legal compensations where strict analogy (qiyās) is not applied, and it is a form of istihsān.

Key Terms
المنافع (al-manāfi‘): Usufruct or benefits derived from using a property, such as living in a house or using a slave.
الغصب (al-ghaṣb): Unlawful taking and use of another's property without permission.
المثل الشرعي (al-mithl al-shar‘ī): A substitute prescribed by the Lawgiver that does not resemble the original in form or meaning but is legally equivalent.

Reconciling Multiple Qira'at and Riwayat in a Single Nass
Stating the principle of preferring to act on both recitations/transmissions when possible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of reconciling multiple recitations of a Quranic verse to derive distinct fiqh rulings for different contexts.

Core Idea
When a text has two recitations, act on both by applying each to a different context.

Usul al-Shashi presents a key Hanafi principle: when a single Quranic text is revealed with two different recitations (qira'at) or transmitted through two different narrations (riwayat), it is preferable to act on the text in a way that gives effect to both readings, provided that is possible. This approach respects the divine origin of both recitations and avoids abandoning any part of revelation. Two classic examples—the feet in wudu and menstrual purity—demonstrate how context-specific application harmonises what might appear contradictory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two recitations
Locate the exact Quranic word or phrase that is transmitted with two different recitations (qira'at). Note the grammatical or phonetic difference between them.
The differences may be in vowel endings (e.g., nasb/jarr) or in verb emphasis (tashdid/takhfif).
2
Determine the legal implication of each recitation
For each recitation, determine the literal or implied legal rule it suggests. For example, nasb on 'feet' implies washing; jarr implies wiping.
Each recitation is a valid revelation and thus carries a potential legal effect.
3
Find a contextual differentiation
Identify a circumstance or condition that allows each recitation to be applied without conflict. This often involves distinguishing between different situations (e.g., wearing khuffs vs. not; blood stopping at ten days vs. earlier).
The differentiation must be plausible within the framework of the Shari'ah.
4
Derive the resulting fiqh rulings
Apply each recitation to its respective context to produce distinct rulings. Both rulings are valid and operative in their own spheres.
This process preserves the authority of both recitations.
5
Verify consistency with other principles
Ensure that the reconciliation does not contradict other established usul principles or consensus (ijma').
The Hanafi school often uses istihsan to support such reconciliations.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi presents a key Hanafi usul principle: when a single Quranic text has two different recitations (qira'at) or two different narrations (riwayat), the preferred method is to act on the text in a way that gives effect to both readings, provided that is possible. This is based on the idea that both recitations are divinely revealed, so reconciling them is more comprehensive and respectful of revelation.

The first example is the famous verse of wudu (al-Ma'idah 5:6) regarding the feet. The word 'أرجلكم' has been recited in two ways: with nasb (accusative), grammatically attached to 'the washed' (i.e., the face and hands), implying washing; and with jarr (genitive), attached to 'the wiped' (i.e., the head), implying wiping. The Hanafi school reconciles by applying the genitive recitation to the situation of wearing khuffs (leather socks), where wiping over them suffices, and the accusative recitation to the situation without khuffs, where washing is required. Thus both recitations are upheld. The text notes that based on this, some scholars argued that the permissibility of wiping over khuffs is established by the Quran itself, though the dominant Hanafi view considers it established by the Sunnah.

The second example is from al-Baqarah 2:222 concerning menstrual purity. The phrase 'حتى يطهرن' has two recitations: one with tashdid (emphasized) and one with takhfif (light). The takhfif recitation (without emphasis) indicates that purity is achieved simply by the cessation of blood after ten days, while the tashdid recitation (with emphasis) indicates that the woman must actively purify herself (by ghusl) if the blood stops before ten days. The Hanafi jurists apply both: when the blood stops at exactly ten days, absolute purification is established by the mere cessation, so intercourse is permissible before ghusl and the prayer obligation begins even if no time remains for ghusl. When the blood stops before ten days, complete purification requires ghusl, so intercourse is impermissible until ghusl, and prayer obligation depends on whether sufficient time remains for ghusl. This application demonstrates the principle of reconciling multiple recitations.

Key Terms
قراءة (qira'ah): A variant recitation of the Quran, transmitted through authentic chains.
رواية (riwayah): A version of a hadith or legal ruling transmitted through a specific chain.

The Waw for حال as a Metaphorical Condition in Hanafi Usul
Discussion on the metaphorical use of waw for hal as a condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when the waw indicates a conditional state (hal) in legal language and apply the Hanafi rule that such usage is metaphorical, requiring contextual evidence.

Core Idea
The waw al-hal is metaphorical: it conveys a condition only when the wording admits it and context supports it.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi explores a subtle feature of Arabic legal language: the waw (and) that appears in conditional statements like "Pay a thousand and you are free." While it looks like a simple conjunction, Hanafi usul holds that it functions as a metaphorical indicator of a condition (hal). Understanding this helps us correctly interpret similar pronouncements in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the statement pattern
In each example (e.g., "Ad ilayka alfan wa anta hurr"), note that the waw connects an imperative clause with a nominal clause stating a result.
The waw is literally "and" but is being interpreted as "so that" or "then."
2
Recognize the metaphorical shift (majaz)
The waw is not literally indicating a condition; it is interpreted as such due to context. For example, in the slave case, freedom cannot occur before payment because the master doesn't owe money to his own slave.
Requires evidence (dalalah) to support the metaphorical reading.
3
Distinguish between immediate and conditional readings
When the husband says "You are divorced and you are sick," the default is immediate divorce unless he intended a condition. The waw alone is not enough to make it conditional.
The intent (niyyah) can override the default but is only valid between him and Allah.

Resolve the apparent contradiction of which element is conditioned

Transferability of Rulings: Changing Opinion in Takbirat al-'Id
Illustration of a usul rule via contrast and analogy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of transferability to cases where a jurist's opinion changes after action, and to distinguish such cases from those where the ruling is fixed.

Core Idea
If a ruling's subject admits of transfer, a change of opinion does not void prior valid acts—like naskh.

This passage explores a key usul concept: when does a change of juristic opinion after acting affect the validity of prior actions? The text contrasts two scenarios—the fixed ruling on prayer garments versus the transferable rulings on qiblah direction and Eid takbir counts. Understanding this distinction helps you appreciate how Hanafi jurists navigated differences among companion reports.

The Sabab as a Substitute for the 'Illa
Explaining how sabab replaces 'illa when the true 'illa is inaccessible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the concept of al-sabab al-na’ib ‘an al-‘illa and apply it to identify when a sabab replaces the ‘illa in a given scenario.

Core Idea
When the true cause is hidden, the law substitutes a measurable sabab for ease.

In Hanafi usul, the sabab (cause) sometimes stands in for the hidden 'illa (effective cause) to remove hardship. This lecture unpacks how the Lawgiver replaces the inaccessible 'illa with a tangible sabab, using three classic examples: sleep, seclusion, and travel.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Read the first sentence: 'Then the sabab may be set up in the place of the 'illa when it is impossible to ascertain the true 'illa, to make things easier.' Note the key phrases: 'yuqam maqam al-'illa' and 'taysiran'.
The substitution is a facility, not a change in the nature of the 'illa.
2
Understand the rationale
Why is substitution needed? Because the true 'illa (like hadath or actual hardship) may be hidden or difficult to verify. The sabab (sleep, travel) is observable.
This aligns with the broader Hanafi principle of taysir.
3
Parse the three examples
For each example (sleep, seclusion, travel), identify the sabab, the original 'illa, and the ruling that now attaches to the sabab.
Sleep → hadath: wudu breaks by sleep even without certainty of hadath. Seclusion → intercourse: full mahr and 'idda. Travel → hardship: rukhsas apply.
4
Distinguish sabab from 'illa
The sabab is not the true 'illa; it is a proxy. The ruling is 'idarat al-hukm 'ala al-sabab' – the ruling revolves around the sabab.
If the true 'illa becomes known, the sabab may cease to operate.
5

Consider a case where a person is in a state of doubt about hadath. How does this principle apply?
The sabab (sleep) removes the need to investigate the hidden 'illa.
