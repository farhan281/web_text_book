Priority of Text over Opinion in Ijtihad
States the obligation of a mujtahid to seek ruling from Quran then Sunnah before resorting to opinion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the obligation of the mujtahid to seek ruling from Quran and Sunnah first and apply the rule that opinion is impermissible when textual evidence is available.

Core Idea
When textual evidence exists, personal opinion is invalid; the mujtahid must exhaust nass before resorting to ra'y.

This passage establishes a fundamental hierarchy in Islamic legal methodology. The Quran and Sunnah are the primary sources. Only when these are silent may the mujtahid employ reasoned opinion. The text illustrates this with two concrete fiqhi examples that demonstrate the practical application of this rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the duty
Read the opening sentence: the mujtahid must seek the ruling from the Book of Allah, then from the Sunnah, by explicit text or its indication.
This is the primary obligation.
2
Understand the prohibition of opinion
The text states that there is no way to act upon opinion if it is possible to act upon the text. This is a strict rule.
Opinion (ra'y) is only allowed in the absence of nass.
3
Apply to the qibla example
When the qibla direction is ambiguous and a reliable person informs you of it, you must accept that report and not engage in taharrī (independent reasoning).
The informant's report is treated as a textual indication.
4
Apply to the water example
If a trustworthy person informs that water is impure, you must not use it for wudu; instead perform tayammum.
The report overrides your own assessment of purity.

Chapter on explaining the duty of the mujtahid. The duty of the mujtahid is to seek the ruling of an incident from the Book of Allah Almighty, then from the Sunnah of the Messenger of Allah, may Allah bless him and grant him peace, by the explicit text or its indication as has been mentioned before. For there is no way to act upon opinion while it is possible to act upon the text. For this reason, if the direction of the qibla is ambiguous to him and one person informs him of it, he is not allowed to exert his own judgment (taharrī). And if he finds water and a trustworthy person informs him that it is impure, he is not allowed to perform wudu with it, but rather he performs tayammum. And based on the consideration that acting upon opinion is not equal to acting upon the text, we say: That the doubt concerning the subject (shubha bi'l-mahall) is stronger than the doubt in speculation (shubha fi'l-zann), such that the consideration of the slave's speculation is dropped in the first case. And its example: If a man has intercourse with his son's slave-girl, he is not subject to the hadd punishment, even if he says 'I knew she was forbidden,' and the lineage of the child is established from him, because the doubt of ownership for him is established by text regarding the property of the son. The Prophet, peace be upon him, said: 'You and your property belong to your father.' So his speculation concerning its permissibility or prohibition is dropped in that case. But if the son has intercourse with his father's slave-girl, his speculation concerning permissibility and prohibition is considered.

Commentary
This passage is a clear statement of a fundamental usul principle: the mujtahid must first seek the ruling from primary textual sources (Quran and Sunnah) before resorting to personal opinion (ra'y). The author emphasizes that when textual evidence is available, there is no room for opinion. This is illustrated with two examples: (1) If the qibla direction is unclear and a reliable person informs of it, one must accept that report and not engage in independent reasoning (taharrī). (2) If water is found but a trustworthy person says it is impure, one cannot use it for wudu; instead, tayammum is performed. The underlying rationale is that doubt in the subject matter (shubha bi'l-mahall) is stronger than doubt in speculation (shubha fi'l-zann). Then a further example is given regarding hadd punishment for intercourse with a slave-girl: If a man has intercourse with his son's slave-girl, he is not punished because there is a textual proof (hadith 'You and your property belong to your father') that creates a doubt of ownership (shubhat al-milk), overriding his personal knowledge that she is forbidden. Conversely, if a son has intercourse with his father's slave-girl, his personal speculation (whether he thought it permissible or not) is considered, because there is no such textual support for the son. This shows the priority of nass over opinion even when doubt is present.

Key Terms
المجتهد (al-mujtahid): A jurist qualified to derive rulings from primary sources through ijtihad.
الرأي (al-ra'y): Personal opinion or reasoning, contrasted with textual evidence.
النص (al-nass): Textual evidence from Quran or Sunnah, either explicit (sarih) or by indication (dalala).

Shubha bi'l-Mahall: Textual Doubt Overrides Personal Knowledge
Explains shubha bi'l-mahall and its application in hadd cases with the father-son slave-girl example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between shubha bi'l-mahall and shubha fi'l-zann, and explain how a textual basis for doubt can avert hadd punishment even when the individual knows the act is forbidden.

Core Idea
A doubt rooted in textual authority (shubha bi'l-mahall) is stronger than mere speculative doubt and can override personal knowledge.

This lesson focuses on the second part of the passage, which introduces a key concept in Hanafi criminal law: when a textual proof creates a plausible doubt about the permissibility of an act, it prevents the application of hadd punishments, even if the individual personally knew the act was forbidden. The example of a father and son's slave-girls illustrates this principle vividly.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the two types of doubt
The passage distinguishes between 'shubha bi'l-mahall' (doubt concerning the subject itself) and 'shubha fi'l-zann' (doubt in one's personal speculation). The first is stronger because it is based on textual evidence.
Shubha bi'l-mahall arises from a legitimate textual basis that casts doubt on the very object of the act.
2
Understand the hadd example
If a man has intercourse with his son's slave-girl, he is not subject to hadd punishment, even if he declares he knew she was forbidden. The reason is the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father,' which creates a textual doubt of ownership (shubhat al-milk).
This textual doubt overrides his personal knowledge.
3
Contrast the opposite case
If the son has intercourse with his father's slave-girl, his personal speculation about permissibility/prohibition is considered. There is no textual support for the son claiming ownership over the father's property.
Thus, the son's own knowledge (that she is forbidden) is not overridden.
4
Extract the usul principle
The principle is that when a text creates a plausible doubt (shubha), it is given priority over individual certainty (qat' al-zann) in matters of hadd, because hadd punishments are averted by doubts (tudra'u al-hudud bi al-shubuhat).
This is a well-known rule in Hanafi fiqh.
