The Uniqueness and Development of Usul al-Fiqh
Prefatory remarks on usul al-fiqh and the author

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the unique Islamic origin of usul al-fiqh, identify the first independent author (al-Shafi'i), and distinguish between the mutakallimun and Hanafi methodologies.

Core Idea
Usul al-Fiqh is a uniquely Islamic science, invented by Muslims to regulate legal derivation from infallible sources.

This preface by Shaykh Yusuf al-Qaradawi opens the book Usul al-Shashi with a powerful claim: usul al-fiqh is a pure Islamic creation, unmatched in any other civilization. This lesson explores why usul al-fiqh is considered the true philosophy of Islam and traces its early history, from al-Shafi'i's pioneering work to the distinct Hanafi method of deriving principles from branch rulings.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the significance
Understand why usul al-fiqh is considered a glory of Islamic heritage and unique to the Muslim ummah.
The text emphasizes it is a pure Islamic science with no prior example.
2
Identify the function
Grasp the purpose: to regulate the evidences, establish rules of inference, and link subsidiary rulings to their sources.
It prevents arbitrary opinions and ensures discipline in derivation.
3
Trace the authorship
Note that Imam al-Shafi'i was the first to author an independent work on usul (al-Risalah), though Abu Yusuf may have written earlier but his work is lost.
The text credits al-Shafi'i for systematic, independent authorship.
4
Distinguish methodologies
Differentiate the mutakallimun method (starting from principles) from the Hanafi method (starting from branches to derive principles).
The mutakallimun approach is deductive; the Hanafi is inductive.
5
Locate the present work
Understand that Usul al-Shashi is a concise Hanafi usul work, and its author al-Shashi is from the city of Shash.
This sets the context for studying the text.

By: His Eminence Shaykh, the Scholar, Dr. Yusuf al-Qaradawi

To proceed:
Verily, the science of Usul al-Fiqh is considered – without doubt – among the glories of our Islamic scholarly heritage. It is a pure Islamic science, invented by Muslims without any prior example, and they excelled in it. No such science is known among any nation of the civilizations.
Through this unique science, Muslims intended to regulate the evidences from which the obligating and situational rulings are derived, and to establish the rules of inference from them, so that no one says whatever he wishes. Rather, subsidiary issues are traced back to their principles, and principles are traced back to their infallible sources that do not lead astray nor forget, and the texts of these sources are understood according to disciplined rules. No wonder that some professors of philosophy and historians of Islamic thought consider that this science is more indicative of the true philosophy of Muslims than the (official) Islamic philosophy known by this name, by which the well-known Islamic Peripatetic school is known in our history, represented by the great philosophers al-Kindi, al-Farabi, Ibn Sina, and those who followed their path.
The first to author in this science was Imam Muhammad ibn Idris al-Shafi'i (d. 204 AH); I mean, he was the one who authored in it in a scientific, systematic manner, as an independent composition, embodied in his remarkable book al-Risalah, although before him there were scattered ideas in each school that form much of the foundational material of this science. In fact, it was said that Abu Yusuf was the first to author in Usul al-Fiqh, but what he wrote has not reached us. Imam al-Shafi'i, may Allah be pleased with him, was followed by authors who took various paths, including from Ahl al-Sunnah and the Mu'tazila. Some took the path of the theologians (mutakallimun), which establishes the principles and then builds upon them the branches. Some took the path of the Hanafis, which proceeds from the branches to arrive at the principles, as it is said that application precedes theory. Some combined both paths, as is known in the history of this science.
Among those who authored in usul from the Hanafi scholars was the scholar al-Shashi, whose beneficial concise book we present, which has been verified by our son, the scholar, the focused researcher, Shaykh Muhammad Akram al-Nadawi, may Allah guide his steps.
Al-Shashi – the author of the book – is attributed to (Shash), which is a city.

Haḳīqah and Majāz: Definitions and the Rule of Non-Combination
Definitions of haqiqah and majaz and the rule of non-combination

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definitions of haqiqah and majaz, explain why they cannot be intended together in one utterance, and apply this rule to distinguish between literal and figurative interpretation in legal texts.

Core Idea
A word cannot simultaneously convey its literal and figurative meanings—the speaker's intention is singular.

Understanding the categories of haqiqah (literal) and majaz (figurative) is foundational for interpreting the Qur'an and Sunnah. This lesson introduces these terms as defined in Hanafi usul, and presents the key principle that they are mutually exclusive in a single act of intention. The passage also records a major school difference on this point, with practical examples from sales and tayammum.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitions
Read the first two sentences: haqiqah is a word placed by the language-setter for a specific thing; using it for something else makes it majaz. Note the authority of the wad' (linguistic convention).
2
State the rule of non-combination
The third sentence asserts that haqiqah and majaz cannot be intended (iradah) together from one word in one case. The qualifier 'iradah' excludes mere potential—the word may have both possibilities, but the speaker's intention picks one.
This is a Hanafi stance; the Shafi'i school allows joint intention.
3
Analyze the ikhtilaf
The passage lists opposing groups: Hanafis, most literary scholars, and verifiers among the Shafi'is hold impossibility; al-Shafi'i, most of his followers, hadith scholars, and some mutakallimin hold permissibility. No proofs are given here; focus on the camps.
This is a memorization anchor—remember the two sides.
4
Apply the rule to the examples
Work through the hadith of sa' (dry measure) and the verse of malamasa (touch). In each, the Hanafi view requires choosing either the literal or figurative meaning—the choice dictates the ruling. For the bequest example, 'mawali' is taken literally (one's own freed slaves), not figuratively (their freed slaves).
Notice how the same word yields different legal outcomes depending on which meaning is intended.
5
Connect to fiqh outcomes
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is that in the hadith, if 'sa'' is taken figuratively (what enters it), selling one sa' for two becomes permissible; if taken literally, it remains prohibited. This shows how usul principles directly affect fatwa.
This is the core of the dispute—the same text yields opposite rulings.

Chapter on Haqiqah and Majaz. Every word that the language-setter placed for a thing is haqiqah (literal) for it. And if it is used for other than it, it is majaz (figurative), not haqiqah. Then, haqiqah and majaz cannot be combined (intended) from one word in one case. He qualified with 'intended' as a precaution against the permissibility of their combination in the word's probability for both. The usul scholars differed regarding the permissibility of applying one word to its literal meaning and its figurative meaning at one time. The Hanafis, most literary scholars, the verifiers among the followers of al-Shafi'i, and most mutakallimin (theologians) held that it is impossible. And in al-Siyar al-Kabir: If the people of war seek aman for their fathers, the grandfathers are not included in the aman, and if they seek aman for their ancestors (annahatihim?), the aman is not established.

Commentary
This passage from al-Shashi's Usul defines the fundamental linguistic categories of haqiqah (literal) and majaz (figurative). The author defines haqiqah as a word placed by a language-setter for a specific meaning, and majaz as its use for another meaning. The key legal principle is that these two cannot be intended simultaneously from a single word in a single instance. The author qualifies this with 'intended' (iradah) to exclude cases where the word merely bears both meanings potentially. A significant ikhtilaf is then recorded: the Hanafi school (along with most literary scholars and verifiers) holds that simultaneous intention is impossible, while al-Shafi'i and many others permit it. The passage provides three examples: (1) the hadith 'Do not sell a dirham for two dirhams, nor a sa' for two sa'' – when the intended meaning is 'what enters the sa'' (figurative), the literal sa' is disregarded, allowing the sale of one sa' for two; (2) the verse of tayammum where 'touch' (al-malamasa) is interpreted figuratively as intercourse, dropping the literal sense; (3) Imam Muhammad's ruling on bequest to mawali – the word 'mawali' (clients) literally refers to one's own freed slaves, not their freed slaves, so a bequest to 'mawali' goes only to immediate ones. The example from al-Siyar al-Kabir further illustrates that when 'fathers' is used, it does not include grandfathers figuratively. All examples reinforce the principle that one intended meaning precludes the other.

الحقيقة (al-haqiqah): The word as placed by the language-setter for a specific thing
المجاز (al-majaz): The word used for other than what it was originally placed for
إرادة (iradah): The intention of the speaker; the qualifier in the rule that haqiqah and majaz cannot be intended together

The Second Type of Metaphor: Using Original for Branch
Explains the second type of metaphorical usage in legal statements with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a case of isti'arah (metaphorically using the original for the branch) in a legal statement and explain its validity conditions according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Metaphor is valid when the original is used for the branch, but judicial acceptance may be limited to prevent self-serving interpretations.

This lesson examines the second type of metaphorical usage recognized in Hanafi usul: using the original term (asl) to refer to the branch (far'). The passage from Usul al-Shashi clarifies when such usage is valid in legal speech and when it is accepted in court. Understanding this distinction is crucial for analyzing legal statements that employ metaphor.

Guided Path
1
Define the second type of isti'arah
Identify the concept: using the original term (e.g., 'ownership') metaphorically to denote the branch (e.g., 'purchase'), or vice versa. Recognize that the metaphor is bidirectional between cause and effect.
The passage states that isti'arah between cause and effect is 'encompassed from both sides'.
2
Examine the first example: ownership vs purchase
Analyze the conditional emancipation scenario: if a person says 'If I own a slave, he is free,' owning half then the other half does not trigger freedom. But if he says 'If I purchase a slave, he is free,' purchasing half then the other half does trigger freedom upon the second purchase, because 'purchase' (cause) is metaphorically used for 'ownership' (effect).
The metaphor is valid, but judicial acceptance may be withheld if it benefits the speaker (due to suspicion).
3
Understand the bidirectional validity
The passage clarifies that using the effect for the cause (e.g., 'ownership' for 'purchase') is also valid as isti'arah, but with the same judicial caveat.
The speaker's intention matters, but in court, self-serving metaphors are not accepted.
4
Examine the second example: freeing for divorce
When a husband tells his wife 'I have freed you' intending divorce, it is valid as a metaphor because freeing a slave removes ownership of the body, analogous to divorce removing marital authority. This produces an irrevocable divorce (bain), not revocable, because the metaphor is for the 'remover of enjoyment'.
The passage explicitly distinguishes between revocable and irrevocable divorce.
5
Analyze the reverse case and limits
The reverse (saying 'I have divorced you' to a slave-girl intending emancipation) is invalid because the branch (divorce) cannot establish the original (freeing). The metaphor only works from original to branch, not branch to original.
This asymmetry is based on the causal relationship between the terms.

Ruling of the second type: The second type requires validity from one side, and it is metaphorically using the original for the branch. Example of the first: When he says: 'If I own a slave, he is free' then he owns half the slave, sells it, then owns the other half, he does not become free, because the entire slave was not in his ownership. Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi discusses the second type of metaphorical usage in legal speech: using the original term for the branch. The author provides two examples. The first involves conditional emancipation linked to ownership or purchase. The key point is that when the cause (purchase) is used for the effect (ownership) or vice versa, the metaphor is valid from both sides, but in judicial proceedings it may not be accepted if it benefits the speaker (avoiding suspicion). The second example uses the word 'حررتك' (I freed you) for a wife, intending divorce. This works because freeing a slave removes ownership of the body, which is analogous to divorce removing marital authority. A question arises: why doesn't this make the divorce revocable? The answer: the metaphor is for the remover of enjoyment (irrevocable divorce), not divorce in general. Finally, the reverse does not hold: saying 'طلقتك' to a slave-girl intending emancipation is invalid because the branch (divorce) cannot establish the original (freeing).

Key Terms
استعارة (isti‘ārah): Metaphorical usage of a word, where the original meaning is used to denote a related branch meaning.
مجاز (majāz): Figurative speech, where a word is used in a non-literal meaning accepted by convention.
أصل (aṣl): The original meaning or the primary referent of a word.
فرع (far‘): The branch meaning or secondary referent derived from the original.

Waw does not automatically impose order; it can indicate mere togetherness, shifting the legal effect from suspension to immediacy.

The key is whether waw links elements within the protasis (condition) or the protasis to the apodosis (result).

This rule applies to divorce and can be analogized to other legal statements.

The Cause of Wudu: A Dispute in Hanafi Usul
A dispute about the cause of wudu

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two views on the cause of wudu, identify which view is attributed to Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, and explain the practical consequence of each view.

Core Idea
The cause of wudu is hadath, not the obligation of prayer—this distinction determines whether a non-Muslim must perform wudu.

In the previous lesson, we saw a list of causes for various obligations. The case of wudu, however, contains a notable dispute. Some scholars hold that the cause of wudu is the obligation of prayer (salah), while others—including Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani—hold that the cause is ritual impurity (hadath) and that the obligation of prayer is merely a condition. This subtle difference has practical consequences in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two views
The passage presents two opinions: (1) 'the cause is prayer' and (2) 'the cause is hadath, and the obligation of prayer is a condition.' The second view is attributed to Imam Muhammad explicitly.
Note the wording: 'wa qala al-ba'd: sabab wujubihi al-hadath, wa wujub al-salat shart'.
2
Understand the legal reasoning
The first view relies on the observation that wudu is only required when salah is obligatory; hence, they argue that salah's obligation is the cause. The second view relies on an explicit text (nass) from Imam Muhammad, which distinguishes between cause and condition.
In Hanafi usul, a cause (sabab) directly triggers the ruling, while a condition (shart) is external. Hadath is the direct cause of impurity requiring purification.
3
Analyze the practical consequence
Under the first view, a person who never has salah obligatory upon them (e.g., a non-Muslim) would not need wudu because the cause is absent. Under the second view, hadath itself necessitates wudu regardless of salah; thus, even a non-Muslim would need wudu to handle the Qur'an or perform other acts requiring purity.
This difference (thamarah al-khilaf) is the practical impact of the usul dispute.

The cause necessitates the ruling, while the condition is an external prerequisite. The passage demonstrates how the existence of the cause determines the obligation and the permissibility of early performance.

Key Terms
سبب (sabab): A cause that triggers a legal obligation; the occasioning factor for a ruling.
شرط (shart): A condition that must exist for the ruling to be applicable, but does not itself cause the ruling.
تعجيل (ta'jil): Performing an act before its due time, permitted if the cause already exists.

The Four Types of Obstacles in Hanafi Usul
Defining four types of obstacles that affect the legal cause and its ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of obstacles (mawani') according to the Hanafi school, classify given examples under each type, and explain the dispute regarding takhsis al-illah.

Core Idea
Obstacles block the cause or its ruling at four stages: inception, completion, full effect, or continuity.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, a legal cause (illa) does not always produce its ruling without hindrance. Certain obstacles (mawani') can prevent the cause from operating, or the ruling from taking effect, in different ways. This passage from Imam Abu Zayd al-Shashi's Usul al-Shashi breaks down these obstacles into four distinct categories, a division rooted in the Hanafi acceptance of takhsis al-illah. Mastering these categories will sharpen your ability to analyze why certain contracts or obligations are suspended, annulled, or modified.

Guided Path
1
State the four categories
Memorize the four Arabic phrases: (1) مانع يمنع انعقاد العلة – obstacle preventing inception of the cause. (2) مانع يمنع تمامها – obstacle preventing completion of the cause. (3) مانع يمنع تمام الحكم – obstacle preventing the full effect of the ruling. (4) مانع يمنع دوامه – obstacle preventing the continuity of the ruling.
These four divisions are exclusive to the Hanafi school and rely on the doctrine of takhsis al-illah.
2
Match each example to its category
For each example given in the text (sale of a free person, destruction of nisab, sale with option, option of puberty), identify which of the four categories it belongs to and explain why. Reference the specific Arabic terminology.
Example: Sale of a free person prevents inception (انعقاد) because the subject matter lacks legal capacity (محلية).
3
Trace the role of takhsis al-illah
Understand that the third category (preventing full effect) is based on the Hanafi principle that a cause can be specified to not produce its full effect in certain circumstances. Those who reject takhsis al-illah (e.g., some Mutakallimin) only have three categories: inception, completion, and continuity.
The third category is rejected by opponents because they argue that if a cause is valid, it must produce its ruling fully; an obstacle that only reduces the effect is arbitrary.
4
Apply to a new case
Consider a conditional gift (hiba mu'allaqa) that is suspended on a future condition. Determine which obstacle type applies before the condition occurs. Justify your answer using the text's reasoning about suspensions (ta'liq).
Answer: The suspension prevents inception (انعقاد) of the gift as a cause, similar to the oath example given.

Commentary
This passage is a classic Hanafi usul al-fiqh text defining the divisions of obstacles (mawani') that affect the operation of the legal cause (illa) and the resulting ruling (hukm). The author, Imam Abu Zayd al-Shashi (may Allah have mercy on him), states that obstacles are of four types according to the Hanafi school, but this division is based on the permissibility of specifying the cause (takhsis al-illah). The first obstacle prevents the inception of the cause itself: the cause cannot come into existence at all. Examples include the sale of a free person (a sale that is invalid because the subject matter is not fit for sale), carrion, and blood—since there is no legal capacity (mahalliyya) for such transactions, the act cannot even be considered a valid cause for producing a legal effect. Also included are all suspended contracts (ta'liq), because the effect is conditional upon a future event; until the condition occurs, the contract does not act as a cause. For instance, if a man swears he will not divorce his wife and then says, 'If you enter the house, you are divorced,' he does not break his oath because the suspension prevents the divorce from being a cause until entry occurs. The second obstacle prevents the completion of the cause after it has begun. Examples: the destruction of the nisab (minimum wealth threshold for zakat) during the year, which interrupts the completion of the cause for zakat obligation; the refusal of one of the two witnesses to testify in a case requiring two witnesses; or the revocation of part of a contract before it is finalized. The third obstacle prevents the full effect of the ruling from taking place, though the cause is complete. Examples: a sale with a stipulation of an option (khiyar al-shart) delays the effect of the sale; and for a person with an excuse (e.g., illness), the ruling of an act may be postponed or modified. The fourth obstacle prevents the continuity of the ruling after it has taken effect. Examples: the option of puberty (khiyar al-bulugh) and option of manumission (khiyar al-‘itq) allow annulment of a contract, thus ending its continuity; the option of inspection (khiyar al-ru'yah) if unseen defects appear; lack of equivalence (adam al-kafa'ah) in marriage; and healing (al-indimal) in the case of wounds, where the right to retaliation (qisas) may lapse if the wound heals. The passage then notes that for those who do not accept the concept of takhsis al-illah (specification of the cause to some cases while excluding others, which is a distinctive Hanafi position), obstacles are only three: preventing the beginning, preventing completion, and preventing continuity—because they reject the division that pertains to preventing the full effect of the ruling, which arises from the possibility of specification.

Key Terms
مانع (māni‘): An obstruction that prevents the legal cause from producing its full effect on the ruling, according to the categories listed in the text.
علة (‘illa): The legal cause that is the effective reason (sabab) for producing a ruling, capable of being blocked by obstacles.
تخصيص العلة (takhṣīṣ al-‘illa): The legal principle that a cause may be restricted to certain cases or circumstances, so its ruling does not apply universally even when the cause is present.
