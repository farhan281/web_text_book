Preference of Haqiqah over Majaz: Rule and Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Rule on preferring literal over figurative with intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to EXPLAIN the Hanafi rule that haqiqah is preferred over majaz when both exist, and IDENTIFY the intra-Hanafi dispute on the definition of muta'arif and its practical consequences.

Core Idea
Literal meaning is preferred unless a common figurative meaning exists; but 'common' is debated.

This passage from Usul al-Fiqh lays out the default rule for interpreting words when both a literal and a figurative meaning exist. While the principle that haqiqah is preferred is agreed upon when no common figurative meaning exists, a key intra-Hanafi disagreement arises over the definition of 'commonly recognized' (muta'arif) and how it affects the application of the rule.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default rule
Note that when a word has both literal and figurative meanings, but no figurative meaning is commonly recognized (muta'arif), the literal (haqiqah) is preferred without dispute.
This is the baseline; the dispute only arises when a common figurative meaning exists.
2
Locate the disagreement
When a common figurative meaning does exist, Abu Hanifa still prefers haqiqah, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) prefer acting on the general figurative meaning (majaz).
The Sahibayn give more weight to common usage.
3
Trace the source of the difference
The difference stems from two interpretations of 'muta'arif': the Balkh scholars (representing the Sahibayn) define it as recognition through customary practice (ta'amul), while the Iraqi scholars (representing Abu Hanifa) define it as recognition through common linguistic understanding (tafahum).
This is a classic example of how definitions drive rulings.
4
Analyze the central example
Consider the oath 'I will not eat meat.' Under Abu Hanifa, eating human or pig meat breaks the oath because linguistically it is called meat (tafahum). Under the Sahibayn, it does not break the oath because such meat is not customarily eaten (ta'amul).
The same example proves both positions.
5
Apply the pattern to other examples
Work through the wheat example (oath on wheat vs. bread) and the Euphrates example (drinking directly vs. any method). Observe how each school applies its definition consistently.
The practical differences (thamarah al-khilaf) are clear and testable.

Commentary
This passage presents a rule from Usul al-Fiqh on the preference between the literal meaning (haqiqah) and figurative meaning (majaz) when both are in use. The rule is divided into two scenarios: if there is no commonly recognized figurative meaning, literal is unanimously preferred. If there is a common figurative meaning, Abu Hanifa maintains the priority of the literal, while the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider acting upon the general figurative meaning as more appropriate. The difference stems from how they define 'commonly recognized' (muta'arif). The Hanafi jurists later split: the Balkh scholars (representing Abu Yusuf and Muhammad's view) define it as recognition through actual usage in transactions and customs (ta'amul). The Iraqi scholars (attributed to Abu Hanifa) define it as recognition through linguistic understanding (tafahum). The example of swearing not to eat meat illustrates this: under Abu Hanifa's view, eating any substance that is linguistically called meat (even human or pig) breaks the oath; under the Sahibayn's view, only meat that is customarily eaten as meat counts. The passage then provides two further examples: wheat and the Euphrates. In the wheat example, Abu Hanifa restricts the oath to the specific grain, so bread is not included; the Sahibayn extend it to all products by the common figurative extension of the term. Similarly, drinking from the Euphrates: Abu Hanifa limits it to direct sipping, while the Sahibayn include any method of drinking its water due to common figurative usage. Finally, the passage defines the nature of majaz: according to Abu Hanifa, it is a substitute for haqiqah in the word (lafz), meaning the word itself is transferred from its original meaning. According to the Sahibayn, it is a substitute in the ruling (hukm), meaning the word remains but its application is broadened. Both agree that if literal meaning is possible but something prevents acting on it, majaz is resorted to in order to avoid meaningless speech.

Key Terms
حقيقة (haqiqah): The literal, original meaning of a word as coined for it
مجاز (majaz): A word used in a meaning other than its original due to a relationship and context
متعارف (muta'arif): Commonly recognized, either through customary practice (ta'amul) or common understanding (tafahum)

The First Two Levels of Textual Clarity: Zahir and Nass
Definition of paired opposites and first two levels

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-zahir and al-nass according to the Hanafi usul classification and distinguish them from al-sarih.

Core Idea
Zahir is understood upon hearing; nass is the intended meaning; sarih has complete clarity.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi lays the foundation for understanding how Hanafi usul scholars categorize texts by clarity. You will learn the first two categories—zahir and nass—and how they differ from sarih, a term often confused with both. Mastering these definitions is essential for building the hierarchy of textual proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the paired opposites
List the four levels of clarity (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) and their four opposites (khafi, mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih). This gives you the full framework.
These eight terms are foundational in Hanafi usul for classifying textual evidence.
2
Define al-zahir precisely
From the passage: zahir is speech whose meaning becomes clear to the listener by the very act of hearing, without needing reflection. Memorize this definition word-for-word.
The key phrase: 'بنفس السَّماع من غير تأمل'.
3
Define al-nass precisely
Nass is speech that is specifically intended to convey that meaning. It is more explicit than zahir in terms of purpose.
The key phrase: 'ما سيق الكلام لأجله'.
4
Distinguish sarih from zahir and nass
Sarih requires complete clarity (zuhur tamm), while zahir has mere clarity. Sarih differs from nass and mufassar only by frequency of usage, not by hierarchy.
This distinction prevents mixing terms from different classification systems.

A single text can be nass in one aspect and zahir in another — acting on both is obligatory.

Core Idea
Khafi is clear in wording but hidden in application due to an external cause — its ruling demands investigation.

In the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity, khafi stands as the opposite of zahir. While a zahir text is immediately clear in both wording and intended application, a khafi text has clear wording but its intended application is hidden due to an external factor such as custom or context. Understanding khafi is essential for correctly applying general rulings to specific cases that may not be commonly included. This lesson will define khafi, illustrate it with examples from the Quran and oaths, and explain its ruling.

Guided Path
1
Locate khafi in the clarity hierarchy
Recall that the Hanafis classify texts into four levels of clarity: zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam. Each has an opposite: khafi (opposite of zahir), mushkil, mujmal, mutashabih. Recognize that khafi is at the first level of hiddenness, involving an external cause.
Zahir is clear in both wording and meaning; khafi is clear in wording but hidden in meaning due to something outside the wording.
2
Analyze the definition of khafi
The definition states: 'ما خفي المراد به بعارض لا من حيث الصيغة' — what is hidden in its intended meaning due to an external cause, not from the wording itself. Break this into two parts: (1) the wording itself is clear (لا من حيث الصيغة); (2) the hiddenness comes from an external cause (بعارض). This external cause could be custom, context, or uncommon applicability.
The wording itself is not ambiguous; the ambiguity arises from how the text applies to specific cases.
3
Work through the Quranic and oath examples
Example 1: The verse 'والسارق والسارقة فاقطعوا أيديهما' is zahir for a common thief but khafi for a pickpocket (الطرار) or grave robber (النباش) because custom does not typically include them under 'thief' in the immediate context. Example 2: 'الزانية والزاني' is zahir for a fornicator but khafi for a sodomite (اللوطي). Example 3: In an oath not to eat fruit, the word 'fruit' is zahir for commonly eaten fruits but khafi for grapes and pomegranates because local custom may not classify them as 'fruit' in oath contexts.
In each example, the wording is clear, but the external cause (custom or common usage) creates hiddenness for certain cases.
4
State the ruling of khafi
The ruling is: 'وجوب الطلب حتى يزول عنه الخفاء' — it is obligatory to seek [clarification] until the hiddenness is removed. This means the mujtahid must investigate the external cause (e.g., custom, context, or further evidence) to determine whether the text applies to the specific case. It is not permissible to simply ignore the text or apply it blindly.
Unlike a mujmal text which requires explanation, khafi requires investigation (talab) of the external cause.

Abandoning the Literal Meaning Due to Speaker's Wisdom
Stating a usul rule with Quranic and fiqhi examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a text where the literal meaning is abandoned due to evidence from the speaker's side and apply the Hanafi principle to interpret similar cases in the Qur'an and mu'āmalāt.

Core Idea
The literal meaning is abandoned when the speaker's wisdom contradicts the surface sense.

In Hanafi uṣūl al-fiqh, the literal meaning (ḥaqīqa) is the starting point for interpretation, but it is not absolute. The fourth principle teaches us that evidence from the speaker's own attributes or circumstances can override the literal meaning. This lesson examines the rule through two concrete examples: a challenging Qur'anic verse and a practical agency case. Understanding this principle sharpens your ability to derive rulings by considering the full context of revelation and human speech.

Guided Path
1
State the Rule
Read the Arabic text: 'قد يترك الحقيقة بدلالة من قبل المتكلم'. Note that the rule is about abandoning the literal meaning (ḥaqīqa) when there is evidence (dalāla) emanating from the speaker (min qibal al-mutakallim).
This is a distinct principle from abandoning ḥaqīqa due to verbal context (qarīna lafẓiyya).
2
Analyze the Qur'anic Example
Examine the verse 'فمن شاء فليؤمن ومن شاء فليكفر'. The literal form is an imperative (amr) commanding disbelief. However, Allah is Ḥakīm (Wise) and al-Kufr is evil. A wise speaker does not command evil. Therefore, the literal meaning is abandoned, and the imperative is interpreted as a threat or permission (tahdīd or takhyīr).
The evidence from the speaker's side (dalāla min qibal al-mutakallim) is Allah's attribute of wisdom.
3
Analyze the Agency Example
Consider the case of a principal (muwakkil) instructing an agent (wakīl) to buy 'meat' (laḥm). The ḥaqīqa of 'meat' is raw meat. But if the agent is a traveler on the road, it is impractical to cook raw meat. The principal's circumstances (being a traveler) provide evidence that the intended meaning is cooked or roasted meat. Conversely, if the principal is a house-owner, the literal meaning (raw) prevails.
The agent's situation is attributed to the speaker's implied intention through the principle of dalāla min qibal al-mutakallim.
4
Summarize the Principle
The rule is: when the literal meaning of a word conflicts with what is known about the speaker (such as wisdom, justice, or practical circumstances), the literal meaning may be set aside and a metaphorical or contextual meaning adopted. This is a core Hanafi tool for reconciling apparently problematic texts.
This principle is often invoked in interpreting problematic āyāt or aḥādīth that seem to contradict other established principles.

Deriving Secondary Rulings through Isharah (Implicit Indication)
Explanation of implicit indications in two Quranic verses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a Quranic text as nass and derive its secondary ruling through isharah, applying the Hanafi method of talil.

Core Idea
Isharah: a text's implicit indication yields a binding secondary ruling.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi explains how a single verse can contain two levels of legal meaning: an explicit primary ruling (nass) and an implicit secondary ruling (isharah). The author uses two Quranic examples to illustrate how the Hanafi school extracts additional fiqh rulings from the same wording. By mastering this tool, you will understand how seemingly simple verses generate multiple legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary ruling (nass)
Read the verse and determine its obvious, intended purpose. For example, in Surah al-Hashr 59:8, the verse is about entitlement to spoils of war.
The primary ruling is the reason the verse was revealed.
2
Locate the implicit indication (isharah)
Look at the wording that, while serving the primary purpose, also reveals a secondary fact. In the same verse, the description 'poor emigrants' indicates that they lost their property.
The indication is not the main point, but logically necessary.
3
Apply talil (reasoning by implication)
Reason that if the property remained in their ownership, they would not be poor. Therefore, ownership must have transferred to the disbelievers who seized it.
This establishes a cause (sabab) for ownership transfer.
4
Extend to analogous cases
From this cause, derive further rulings: trade with disbelievers, booty, etc. Similarly, from the fasting verse, derive that janaba does not break the fast, and that rinsing/tasting does not invalidate it.
The Hanafi school uses this method to expand rulings beyond the immediate text.

Iqtida' al-Nass: The Implied Requirement of the Text
Defines iqtida' al-nass and distinguishes it from mahdhuf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'muqtada' (iqtida' al-nass), distinguish it from 'mahdhuf', and apply the distinction to a legal example from the Hanafi usul tradition.

Core Idea
Iqtida' al-nass: the text's meaning cannot stand without this unstated element; it is treated as if stated.

In Hanafi usul, not every omitted word in a text is treated equally. Some omissions are merely linguistic shortcuts (mahdhuf), while others are legally necessary implications (muqtada) that the text cannot do without. This lesson unpacks the definition of muqtada and the critical distinction drawn by later Hanafi scholars, using classical examples to ground the concept.

Guided Path
1
Define muqtada
Identify the key phrase 'زيادة على النص لا يتحقق معنى النص إلا به' – an addition without which the text's meaning is incomplete. Understand that muqtada is a shar'i (legal) requirement, not a mere figure of speech.
The muqtada is subsidiary; the ruling of the text depends on it.
2
Contrast earlier and later Hanafi views
Note that early Hanafis and Shafi'is treated mahdhuf (omitted word) as part of muqtada. Later Hanafis separated them: mahdhuf is linguistic (فهم اللغة), muqtada is legal (أمر شرعي).
This is a key intra-school refinement.
3
Apply the distinguishing sign
The sign: if stating the omitted element explicitly would transfer the ruling to that element, it is mahdhuf; if the omitted element remains subsidiary to the text's ruling, it is muqtada.
Muqtada is 'تبع' (subordinate); mahdhuf becomes the primary subject when stated.
4

Examine 'Free your slave for me' – ownership of the slave is implied (muqtada) because the command to free presupposes ownership. In 'Ask the village', the omitted 'people' is mahdhuf – the ruling applies to them only if explicitly stated.
The examples clarify the practical difference between the two categories.

The Particle Hatta (حتى) in Hanafi Oath Jurisprudence
Defines 'hatta' as a limit and applies it to oath examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for 'hatta' to identify when it functions as a true limit in oath formulations and determine whether the oath is broken.

Core Idea
Hatta creates a condition: if the preceding action is extendable and the following event is suitable, the oath is broken if the limit is not reached.

In this lesson, we dissect the particle 'hatta' (until) as defined by the Hanafi school. The key is understanding that it only operates in its primary sense when the action before it can be extended and the event after it serves as a proper endpoint. Through examples from Imam Muhammad, we learn how this principle governs oath-breaking.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two components
Look at the structure: 'hatta' connects a preceding action and a following event. Determine if the action is capable of continuous extension (imtidad) and if the following event can be a natural endpoint (ghaya).
E.g., 'beating' is extendable; 'intercession' can be an endpoint.
2
Check the condition for haqiqa
Confirm that both conditions are met: (1) what precedes can be extended, (2) what follows is a suitable limit. If both hold, 'hatta' operates in its true meaning.
If not, 'hatta' might mean 'aw' (or) or be figurative.
3
Apply to oath formulations
Given an oath with 'hatta', verify the limit. If the person stops the action before reaching the limit, the oath is broken (hanith). If the limit is reached, the oath is fulfilled.
Example: Swearing not to beat until night enters; beating until night is fine; stopping before night breaks the oath.
4
Distinguish from other uses
If the action is instant or the limit is unsuitable, 'hatta' may not be literal. This distinction matters for the legal consequence.
E.g., 'until you die' for beating is not a suitable limit because death ends the action, but it may be too extreme.

Silence in Context of Need as Expression: The Principle of Bayan al-Hal
Principle statement with illustrative examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of bayan al-hal to identify when silence constitutes legal consent or permission in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Silence in a context requiring speech is legally equivalent to an explicit statement.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces a foundational Hanafi principle: bayan al-hal, or the statement of the situation. It teaches that when a person remains silent in a situation where they are expected to speak, their silence carries the same legal weight as an explicit declaration. This principle is applied across various domains—from the Lawgiver's tacit approval of actions to a pre-emptor's acceptance of a sale, and even to the formation of consensus (ijma' sukuti). Understanding this principle is crucial for deriving rulings from the sources where explicit texts are absent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
State the core rule: silence in a context of need (mawdi' al-haja ila al-bayan) is treated as a statement (bayan). This is a general usul principle.
The need for expression is key; not all silences are equal.
2
Examine the Examples
Go through each example given in the passage (Lawgiver, pre-emptor, virgin, master, defendant) and verify how the principle applies. For each, identify the context that created the need for speech.
The examples illustrate the breadth of the principle across different fields of fiqh.
3
Understand the Condition
The silence must occur in a situation where there is a clear need for the person to express their stance (e.g., seeing an action, learning of a contract). If the context does not require speech, silence is not binding.
Capacity to speak and awareness are also necessary.
4
Analyze the Ikhtilaf on Nukul
Note the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the two companions on whether a defendant's refusal to take an oath (nukūl) is an admission (iqrar) or a tender (badhl). Both views rely on the same principle of silence but interpret the underlying evidence differently.
