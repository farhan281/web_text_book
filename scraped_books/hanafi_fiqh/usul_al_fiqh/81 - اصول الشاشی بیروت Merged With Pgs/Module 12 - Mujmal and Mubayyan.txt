Definition and Ruling of Mufassar
Defining mufassar as clarified by the speaker and stating its binding ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mufassar and explain why its ruling is definitive action with certainty.

Core Idea
Mufassar is an ambiguous word made certain by the speaker's own clarification — and it must be acted on without interpretation.

In Hanafi usul, not every ambiguous word remains open to interpretation. When the speaker himself clarifies which meaning he intends, that clarification becomes binding. This lesson defines that category — al-mufassar — and establishes its legal weight.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its prerequisite
Recognize that mufassar applies to an originally ambiguous word (mushtarak) that has been clarified by the speaker.
Ambiguity here means the word has multiple possible meanings.
2
Recognize the cause (sabab) of mufassar
The cause is the speaker's explicit clarification (tafsir) that gives preponderance to one meaning over others.
This clarification must come from the speaker himself, not from external evidence.
3
State the ruling of mufassar
The ruling is that the clarified meaning must be acted upon with certainty (yaqinan); no further interpretation or inference is allowed.
Contrast with tawil, which is inferential interpretation based on evidence.
4
Work through the example
Examine the sentence 'I owe ten dirhams from the currency of Bukhara.' Without the specification, the default would be the country's prevalent currency via tawil. But the speaker's clarification (tafsir) creates a mufassar, so the specified currency must be used.
This shows how mufassar overrides default assumptions.

Al-Mujmal: Definition and Legal Significance
Definition of mujmal with a Quranic example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mujmal, distinguish it from mushkil, and identify when a text requires bayan from the speaker.

Core Idea
Mujmal is a text whose meaning is not known until the speaker clarifies it.

In Hanafi usul, texts are ranked by clarity. Beyond mushkil lies mujmal—a level where ambiguity cannot be resolved by reflection alone. The mujmal text requires an explicit explanation (bayan) from the Lawgiver. This lesson unpacks the definition and its practical implication through the Quranic example of riba.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition in the passage
Read the first sentence: 'Then above mushkil is mujmal. It is what has admitted multiple aspects, so it has become in a state where the intended meaning is not attained except by a clarification (bayan) from the speaker.' Identify the key condition: the need for bayan from the speaker.
2
Understand why the example of riba is mujmal
The word 'riba' literally means 'increase'—an absolute concept. However, the Shari'ah intends a specific type of increase. Since the word does not indicate this specificity, reflection alone cannot yield the intended ruling. External clarification (e.g., hadith) is necessary.
This shows the difference between mushkil (resolvable by thought) and mujmal (requires speaker's bayan).
3
Apply the definition to other texts
Identify Quranic verses or hadith that are mujmal because they contain a general word whose legal specification is not evident. For example, 'And establish prayer' (al-Baqarah 43) – the word 'prayer' is mujmal until the Prophet (ﷺ) explained its form and conditions.
The Hanafi school emphasizes that mujmal texts are not binding until bayan arrives.

Mujmal and Mutashabih in Hanafi Usul: Definitions and Rulings
Defining mujmal and mutashabih in the Hanafi text-clarity hierarchy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mujmal and al-mutashabih, distinguish them, and explain their legal effect (hukm) in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Mujmal requires clarification; mutashabih defies human understanding.

This lesson introduces two key terms in the Hanafi text-clarity hierarchy: mujmal and mutashabih. Understanding these categories is essential for proper istinbat (derivation of rulings), as they determine whether a text can be applied directly or must await explanation. We will examine definitions, examples, and the ruling (hukm) that applies to both.

Guided Path
1
Define al-mujmal
Al-mujmal is a text that admits multiple possible meanings, such that the intended meaning cannot be known without external clarification (bayan) from the speaker. Identify the key condition: the ambiguity cannot be resolved by mere linguistic reflection.
Hanafi usul places mujmal above mushkil in obscurity.
2
Analyze the example of riba
The verse 'wa harrama al-riba' is mujmal because the word 'riba' literally means 'increase' in general, but the shari' meaning is specific (increase without consideration in certain sales). The wording alone does not indicate this; the Prophet's explanation provided the bayan.
3
Define al-mutashabih
Al-mutashabih is a text from which hope of knowing the intended meaning is entirely cut off. It is more obscure than mujmal. The example is the disconnected letters (huruf muqatta'at) at the beginnings of surahs.
Shafi'i usul similarly recognizes this category.
4
Contrast mujmal and mutashabih
Mujmal can eventually be clarified (bayan is expected), while mutashabih cannot be fully understood by humans. Both require belief in the truth of their intended meaning.
5
State the hukm (ruling) for both
The ruling is: 'i'tiqad haqqiyyat al-murad bihi hatta ya'ti al-bayan' – believe that the intended meaning is true and await clarification. For mujmal, clarification is anticipated in this world; for mutashabih, it may be in the Hereafter or reserved for Allah's knowledge.

Time as a Container: The First Type of Muwaqqat
Classification of muwaqqat into zarf type with rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'al-muwaqqat' and distinguish its first type (zarf) by stating its two rulings and explaining why it is called an expanded container.

Core Idea
Time for prayer is a container that accommodates multiple acts, not a measure that requires full occupation.

In Usul al-Fiqh, time-bound obligations (muwaqqat) are divided into two categories. This lesson focuses on the first category, where time serves as a container (zarf) for the action. Understanding this classification clarifies why multiple prayers can be performed within the same time window, and why vowing extra prayers is binding. We will unpack the definition and its two key rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the classification
Recognize that muwaqqat is divided into two types. The passage discusses only the first type, where time is a container (zarf).
The second type (where time is a measure) is implied but not detailed here.
2
State the meaning of 'zarf'
Understand that 'zarf' means time is a container for the action; the act occurs within the time but does not require occupying the entire period.
The text says 'يكون الوقت ظرفاً للفعل'.
3
List the two rulings
First: the obligation of one act does not prevent another obligation of the same kind (e.g., a vowed prayer). Second: the obligation does not preclude the validity of another prayer (e.g., nafl).
Example: vowing extra rak'ahs in Zuhr time is binding.
4
Explain the term 'expanded container'
The container is called 'zarf muwassa'' because the time can accommodate the act and other acts of its kind; the act is 'within' time, not 'measured by' time.
Contrast with acts like fasting, where time is a measure.

Hatta as Purpose, Result, or Conjunction in Conditional Oaths
Explains three modes of interpreting 'hatta' in conditional oaths.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three interpretations of the particle 'hatta' (ghaya, jaza', atf) and apply the Hanafi contextual method to determine whether a conditional oath is broken.

Core Idea
Hatta is not always a limit; in Hanafi usul, it can be result or conjunction based on meaning, altering oath rulings.

In Hanafi usul, the particle 'hatta' in conditional oaths can be interpreted in multiple ways depending on context. This lesson explores three interpretations—limit (ghaya), result (jaza'), and pure conjunction (atf)—using examples from Imam Muhammad. You will learn how these interpretations affect the ruling on oath-breaking and why Hanafi scholars defend metaphorical usage against grammarians.

1
Identify the ambiguous particle

A single coordination of a measured or weighed item clarifies the genus of a general number.

When someone says 'I owe you a hundred and a dirham,' the phrase is ambiguous: is the hundred also dirhams?
