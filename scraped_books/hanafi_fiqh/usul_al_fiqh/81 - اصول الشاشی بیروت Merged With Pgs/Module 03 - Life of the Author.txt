Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key biographical details of Imam Abu Hanifa and explain his significance as the founder of the Hanafi school of law.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa, born in 80 AH, met a Companion, mastered fiqh, and was hailed by al-Shafi'i as the one on whom people depend in fiqh.

This passage opens a Hanafi usul text with a biographical tribute to Imam Abu Hanifa. It situates his authority by detailing his lineage, his encounter with the Companions, his scholarly breadth, and the acclaim he received from later masters. Understanding his life helps us appreciate why his school, the Hanafi madhhab, became so influential.

In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. Praise be to Allah who elevated the status of the believers by His generous address, and raised the ranks of the scholars by the meanings of His Book, and singled out the deductive scholars among them with increased correctness and reward. May blessings be upon the Prophet and his Companions, and peace be upon Abu Hanifa and his loved ones. To proceed: The Prophet Muhammad. Al-Nu'man ibn Thabit ibn Zuti, the Greatest Imam, the jurist of the nation, the Kufan. He was born in the year 80 AH during the lifetime of the younger Companions, and he saw Anas ibn Malik when he came to them in Kufa. He excelled in theology (kalam) as he excelled in grammar and literature, and he was concerned with seeking hadith traditions, and he traveled for that purpose. As for fiqh and precision in opinion and its subtleties, he is the ultimate authority, and people are dependent upon him in that regard. Imam al-Shafi'i said: 'People are dependent upon Abu Hanifa in fiqh.' He was offered the position of judge but refused, and the governorship of wealth but he rejected. From Sharik: Abu Hanifa was long of silence, great of intellect. Abu Asim al-Nabil said: Abu Hanifa was called 'the peg' due to his many prayers. Al-Khuraybi said: None criticizes Abu Hanifa except an envier or an ignorant. From Yahya ibn Sa'id al-Qattan: We do not lie against Allah - we have not heard anything better than the opinion of Abu Hanifa, and we have adopted most of his statements. From al-A'mash that he was asked about a matter and he said: Only al-Nu'man ibn Thabit al-Khazzaz can do this well, and I think he is blessed in his knowledge. Ibn al-Mubarak said: Abu Hanifa is the most knowledgeable of people. Al-Dhahabi said: His biography deserves to be compiled in two volumes. He died, may Allah have mercy on him, in the year... end. Commentary
This passage is a biographical introduction to Imam Abu Hanifa (d. 150 AH), the founder of the Hanafi school of law. It begins with the basmala and hamdala, situating the work within the tradition of Islamic scholarship. The author then notes that the Prophet Muhammad is the ultimate source of guidance. The biography details Abu Hanifa's lineage: al-Nu'man ibn Thabit ibn Zuti, born in Kufa in 80 AH, which allowed him to see some of the younger Companions, including Anas ibn Malik. He excelled in theology, grammar, and literature before focusing on hadith and fiqh. His mastery of fiqh and legal reasoning was unparalleled, leading Imam al-Shafi'i to declare that all people are dependent on Abu Hanifa in fiqh. He famously rejected offers of judgeship and financial governance, demonstrating his piety and independence. Numerous scholars praise his intellect, silence, prayer, and knowledge. Yahya ibn Sa'id al-Qattan and Ibn al-Mubarak among others commend his fiqh. The biography closes with a reference to al-Dhahabi's estimation that his life deserves a two-volume work, and a list of sources for further reading. This introductory material establishes the authority and prestige of the imam whose usul principles will be taught in the text.

الإمام الأعظم (al-Imam al-A'zam): The Greatest Imam, a title of veneration for Abu Hanifa, emphasizing his preeminence in fiqh.

Imam al-Shafi'i: Life and Legacy in Usul al-Fiqh
Biographical profile of Imam al-Shafi'i

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state key biographical facts about Imam al-Shafi'i and explain his principle of prioritizing authentic hadith over personal opinion.

Core Idea
Al-Shafi'i's rule 'authentic hadith overrides my opinion' defines his usul methodology and shapes his school's approach.

This lesson introduces you to the life and legacy of Imam al-Shafi'i, a pivotal figure whose methodological innovations in usul al-fiqh profoundly shaped Islamic legal theory. Understanding his biography provides context for the key differences between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools – differences that are rooted in each Imam's intellectual formation. As you read, note how al-Shafi'i's educational journey and interactions with Hanafi scholars like Muhammad al-Shaybani influenced his approach.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Imam and his lineage
Read the full name and lineage: Muhammad bin Idris bin al-Abbas bin Uthman bin Shafi' al-Qurashi al-Muttalibi. Note his title 'Nasser al-Hadith' (supporter of hadith).
His lineage traces back to the Prophet's tribe, Quraysh.
2
Trace his educational journey
Identify his birth (Gaza, 150 AH), early move to Mecca, studies under Muslim bin Khalid al-Zanji and Sufyan bin Uyaynah, then under Imam Malik in Medina. Note his stay with Muhammad al-Shaybani in Baghdad.
This shows he learned both the Maliki and Hanafi traditions.
3
Note his intellectual character
Read the praise from Ahmad bin Hanbal, Abu Ubayd, and others. Understand that he was considered highly intelligent and a master of both hadith and fiqh.
Ahmad's quote indicates al-Shafi'i's broad influence.
4
Extract his famous principle
Locate the statement: 'Whatever I say, if from the Messenger of Allah contrary to my saying is authentic, then it takes precedence.' This is a core usul principle of adherence to hadith.
This principle is foundational in the Shafi'i school.
5
Recognize his connection to Hanafi school
Note that he was a guest of Muhammad al-Shaybani in Baghdad, indicating scholarly exchange. His usul differs from Hanafi usul on points like definition of 'amm, but both are rooted in Quran and Sunnah.
Understanding this helps contextualize ikhtilaf.

Muhammad bin Idris bin al-Abbas bin Uthman bin Shafi' al-Qurashi al-Muttalibi, Abu Abdullah, the Imam, the scholar of the age, the supporter of hadith, the jurist of the religion (150-204 AH). He was born in Gaza in Palestine and was taken from there to Mecca when he was two years old. He visited Baghdad twice and went to Egypt in 199 AH and died there. He studied fiqh under Muslim bin Khalid al-Zanji and Sufyan bin Uyaynah in Mecca, and under Malik in Medina. During his stay in Baghdad, he was a guest of Muhammad bin al-Hasan al-Shaybani. Ahmad bin Hanbal said: 'No one touched an inkpot or a pen except that al-Shafi'i had a favor upon him.' He authored his famous treatise in usul al-fiqh. Abu Ubayd said: 'I have not seen anyone more intelligent than al-Shafi'i.' Yunus al-Sadafi said: 'I have not seen anyone more intelligent than al-Shafi'i. Al-Shafi'i said: 'Whatever I say, if from the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) contrary to my saying is authentic, then it takes precedence, and do not follow me blindly.' A man said to him: 'Do you adopt this hadith, O Abu Abdullah?' He said: 'When you narrate a sound hadith from the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) and I do not act upon it, then I bear witness that my mind has gone.' Qutaybah bin Sa'id said: 'Al-Shafi'i is an imam.' Al-Asma'i said: 'I took the poetry of Hudhayl from al-Shafi'i.' Ibrahim al-Harbi said: 'I asked Ahmad about al-Shafi'i, and he said: Sound hadith and sound opinion.'

Biography of Muhammad ibn al-Hasan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state key facts about Imam Muhammad ibn al-Hasan's life, his contributions to Hanafi fiqh and usul, and his scholarly relationships.

Core Idea
Muhammad ibn al-Hasan codified the Hanafi school's teachings and authored its foundational texts.

Imam Muhammad ibn al-Hasan al-Shaybani (d. 189 AH) is one of the three central pillars of the Hanafi madhhab, alongside Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf. This lesson introduces his biography, emphasizing his studies under Imam Malik, his prolific writings, and the high esteem in which he was held by contemporaries like Imam Ahmad and Imam al-Shafi'i. Understanding his role helps us appreciate the transmission and systematization of Hanafi law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the person and his era
Read the passage and note that Muhammad ibn al-Hasan lived in the 2nd century AH and died in 189 AH. He was a student of both Abu Hanifa and Imam Malik.
His death date is important for understanding the chronology of Hanafi school development.
2
Note his teachers and students
The passage mentions he stayed with Malik for three years and heard 700 hadiths. It also indicates he taught others, as his books were used by Imam Ahmad.
He studied under Abu Hanifa as well, though not mentioned here.
3
Recognize his literary contributions
The passage states he authored many books in fiqh and usul. These include the six works of Zahir al-Riwayah, which are authoritative in the Hanafi school.
His writings are primary sources for Hanafi jurisprudence.
4
Understand the significance of his works in the Hanafi school
Imam Ahmad and al-Shafi'i praised his knowledge. His books preserved and systematized Hanafi teachings, making them accessible to later generations.
The Hanafi school relies heavily on his formulations.
5
Reflect on the testimonies of other imams
The passage quotes Imam Ahmad saying he derived precise issues from Muhammad's books, and al-Shafi'i's statement about his study with Malik. These show his high scholarly stature.

Knowing the scholars who shaped the Hanafi school is essential for understanding its methodology. Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi stands out as the one who compiled the foundational principles (usul) that underpin the entire madhhab. His biography not only highlights his personal piety but also his intellectual legacy in systematizing Hanafi legal theory.

Biography of Imam Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl
Biographical note on Zufar, a major Hanafi scholar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the contributions of Imam Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl to Hanafi fiqh and usul, and recall his position as a major student of Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
Imam Zufar was the greatest student of Abu Hanifa, a mujtahid expert in nasikh wa mansukh, and his views are part of the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Imam Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl is a towering figure in the early Hanafi school. As the most senior student of Abu Hanifa, he is frequently cited in intra-madhhab differences. His expertise in distinguishing abrogating from abrogated hadith made him a key authority in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key biographical facts
Note Zufar's birth (110 AH), death (158 AH), and his teacher (Abu Hanifa). Recognize that he was considered the greatest of Abu Hanifa's students.
Memorize these details as they establish his chronological and scholarly position.
2
Recognize his scholarly qualifications
The text describes him as a 'faqih' (jurist), 'mujtahid' (qualified to perform ijtihad), and an expert in nasikh wa mansukh (abrogating and abrogated hadith). Understand that these titles reflect his high rank in fiqh and usul.
In the Hanafi context, being a mujtahid fi al-madhhab means he can derive rulings within the school's principles.
3
Understand his place in the Hanafi school
Zufar's opinions are often considered but may not be the mufta bihi (operative ruling) if they conflict with the majority of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad. Nevertheless, his views are part of the nawadir and are referenced in ikhtilaf.
This hierarchy is important for understanding how later Hanafi jurists weighed different opinions.

زفر بن الهذيل بن قيس بن مسلم العنبري، الفقيه المجتهد الرباني العلامة، ولد سنة عشر ومأة، وكان من بحور الفقه وأذكياء الأمة، تفقه بأبي حنيفة، وهو أكبر تلامذته، وكان ممن جمع بين العلم والعمل، وكان يدري الحديث ويتقنه، قال الحسن بن زياد اللؤلؤي: ما رأيت فقيهاً يناظر زفر إلا رحمته، قال أبو نعيم: كنت أعرض الأحاديث على زفر فيقول: هذا ناسخ وهذا منسوخ هذا يؤخذ به هذا يرفض، قال الذهبي: كان هذا الإمام منصفاً في الحديث، متبعاً مات سنة ثمان وخمسين ومأة.
Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl ibn Qays ibn Muslim al-Anbari, the jurist, mujtahid, godly, great scholar, was born in the year 110 AH. He was among the oceans of fiqh and the intellects of the ummah. He studied fiqh under Abu Hanifa, and he was the greatest of his students. He combined knowledge and action, and knew hadith and mastered it. Al-Hasan ibn Ziyad al-Lu'lu'i said: I never saw a jurist debating Zufar except that I felt pity for him. Abu Nu'aym said: I used to present hadith to Zufar, and he would say: this is abrogating, this is abrogated; this is to be acted upon, this is to be rejected. Al-Dhahabi said: This Imam was fair in hadith, a follower. He died in the year 158 AH.

Commentary
This passage is a brief biography (tarjama) of Imam Zufar ibn al-Hudhayl, a prominent figure in the Hanafi school. He was a student of Abu Hanifa and is often cited in ikhtilaf within the madhhab. He is renowned for his deep understanding of fiqh and hadith, particularly his ability to distinguish between abrogating and abrogated hadith. He was also known for his sharp intellect; al-Hasan ibn Ziyad said that anyone who debated Zufar was pitied. Zufar's opinions are often considered within the Hanafi school, though the relied-upon position (zahir al-riwaya) usually follows the majority of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad. His death in 158 AH places him in the early generation of Hanafi jurists.

Key Terms
مُجْتَهِد (mujtahid): A scholar qualified to perform ijtihad, deriving rulings from primary sources.
ناسخ ومنسوخ (nasikh wa mansukh): Abrogating and abrogated hadith; Zufar was an expert in this field.
