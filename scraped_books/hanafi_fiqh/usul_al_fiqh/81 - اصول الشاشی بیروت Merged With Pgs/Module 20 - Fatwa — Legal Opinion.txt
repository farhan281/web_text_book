Divorce Phrases with Divine Will: The Usul Principle of Directness (Ṣarāḥa)
Ruling from al-Ziyadat on ineffective divorce phrases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a divorce pronouncement must be direct and unconditional, and explain why attributing divorce to divine will nullifies its legal effect.

Core Idea
A divorce tied to Allah's will is not a divorce—only a direct, unconditional pronouncement effects talaq.

This lesson examines a ruling from Imam Muhammad's al-Ziyadat: a husband's statement 'You are divorced by the will of Allah' does not result in divorce. At first glance, one might think the presence of the word 'divorced' ('tāliq') makes it effective. However, the Hanafi school, based on the principle that a ṣarīḥ (explicit) divorce must be free of condition or attribution to divine mysteries, treats such phrases as non-binding. Understanding this rule illuminates how the Hanafi madhhab prioritises certainty and directness in marital dissolution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and its source
Read the Arabic text from al-Ziyadat. Note that the ruling is explicit: 'she is not divorced' (lam tutallaq). Recognise that al-Ziyadat is one of the six canonical books of the Hanafi Zahir al-Riwayah, carrying high authority.
The authority level is Zahir al-Riwayah, the core of Hanafi authoritative texts.
2
Extract the operative usul principle
Ask: why does attaching the phrase to divine will nullify the divorce? The underlying principle is that a valid departure from marriage requires an unambiguous, unconditional intent. Phrases like 'by the will of Allah' introduce either a condition (mashī'ah is unknown) or an attribution that undermines directness.
This relates to the Hanafi rule: ṣarīḥ (explicit) talaq needs no intention; kināyah (metaphorical) needs intention and context. Here, the phrase is neither ṣarīḥ nor intended as such.
3
Compare with similar phrases
Consider other formulations: 'If Allah wills, you are divorced' is a suspended talaq (ta'līq) – also ineffective unless the condition is known and actual. The ruling in al-Ziyadat extends this logic to attributing divorce to Allah's attributes.
The principle prevents divorces that are not fully voluntary and immediate.
4
Apply the principle to a new case
Suppose a husband says 'You are divorced by Allah's decree' (bi-ḥukm Allah). According to the same logic, this too does not effect divorce. Test whether the student can extrapolate the ruling.
The key is whether the speech act is a direct, unqualified pronouncement.
5
Confirm the correct usul maxim
The maxim 'Al-Ṣarīḥ yastaghniyya an al-niyyah' (an explicit term needs no intention) is inverted here: because the phrase is ambiguous, it does not work. The broader maxim is 'Mā tarakkaba min al-shart wa al-jazā' lā yufīd al-talaq fī al-ḥāl' (a compound of condition and result does not effect immediate divorce).
Memorise: divorce must be unconditional and direct to take effect.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling from Imam Muhammad's work al-Ziyadat (one of the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah). It clarifies that making divorce conditional upon or attributed to divine attributes (mashī'ah, irādah, hukm) does not effect a divorce because these phrases are not direct pronouncements of divorce; they are either suspended on an unknown condition (the will of Allah is hidden) or are not intended as actual divorce. This is a specific Hanafi position: divorce must be explicit (ṣarīḥ) or unambiguous in context; such phrases are ambiguous and thus not binding.

Key Terms
الزيادات (al-Ziyādāt): One of the six books of Zahir al-Riwayah by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, containing additional rulings.
بمشيئة الله (bi-mashī’at Allah): By the will of Allah; considered a conditional phrase that suspends the divorce.
صريح (ṣarīḥ): Explicit and unambiguous; a direct pronouncement that requires no intention to effect divorce.
كناية (kināyah): Metaphorical or ambiguous wording that may imply divorce but requires intention and context.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi usul, attributing divorce to divine will (mashī'ah, irādah, hukm) renders the pronouncement ineffective; only a direct, unconditional ṣarīḥ statement effects talaq.
