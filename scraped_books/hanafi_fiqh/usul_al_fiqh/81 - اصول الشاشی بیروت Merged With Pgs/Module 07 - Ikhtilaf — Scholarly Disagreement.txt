‘Āmm Specified by Khāṣṣ: The Marriage Guardian Example
Usul dispute with furu‘ expansion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a specific text (khāṣṣ) specifies a general text (ʿāmm) using the marriage without guardian hadith, and identify the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilāf) on related furūʿ.

Core Idea
Khāṣṣ specifies ʿāmm — the specific hadith overrides the general Quranic verse in Hanafi usul.

This passage presents a classic usul conflict: a general Quranic verse (2:230) implies that any subsequent marriage makes a divorced woman lawful, while a specific hadith requires a guardian for a valid marriage. The Hanafi school resolves the conflict by making the specific text (khāṣṣ) an exception to the general (ʿāmm). From this core ruling, numerous branches (furuʿ) follow, differing between valid and invalid marriages.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Texts
Locate the Quranic verse (2:230) and the hadith in the passage. Classify each: the verse is general (ʿāmm) regarding 'marriage' — it doesn't mention a guardian; the hadith is specific (khāṣṣ) because it explicitly states that marriage without a guardian is invalid.
The verse: '...until she marries another husband.' The hadith: 'Any woman who marries herself without permission of her guardian... bāṭil.'
2
Apply the Hanafi Usul Rule
In Hanafi usul, a khāṣṣ text overrides an ʿāmm text by way of takhsīṣ (specification). The hadith specifies the general ruling of the verse: not all marriages are valid — only those with a guardian. Thus, the marriage without a guardian is invalid (bāṭil).
The Ḥanafīs maintain that the ʿāmm is ḍaʿīf al-dalāla (probable in meaning) until specified, while khāṣṣ is qaṭʿī al-dalāla (decisive) within its scope.
3
Derive the Ruling
From the specification, the ruling is: marriage contracted without a guardian is bāṭil (void). This is the muftā bihī position in the Ḥanafī school.
Bāṭil means no legal effects arise unless certain conditions (like consummation) trigger specific obligations.
4
Expand to the Branches (Furūʿ)
The passage lists branches: lawfulness of intercourse, obligation of mahr, nafaqa, suknā, occurrence of divorce, and remarriage after three divorces. Since the marriage is bāṭil, intercourse is considered zinā (unless there is shubha), mahr may still be wajib if consummation occurred (mahr al-mithl), but talaq does not occur because there is no valid marriage.
The exact branching differs among schools; here the Ḥanafī position is stated.
5
Note the Intra-Madhhab Dispute
The passage mentions that early Shāfiʿī scholars (qudamāʾ aṣḥābihi) held different branches than later ones. This shows that even within a school, ikhtilāf can evolve, but the core usul principle remains.
The Ḥanafī position is consistent: the hadith is relied upon.

Majaz as Substitute: The Intra-Hanafi Dispute
Defines majaz as substitute and presents intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the two Hanafi definitions of majaz (khalaf in wording vs. khalaf in ruling), apply them to scenarios where literal meaning is impossible or prevented, and explain the exception when contradiction exists.

Core Idea
Majaz is a substitute for haqiqah—the dispute is whether the substitution occurs in the wording or the ruling.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi presents a foundational intra-Hanafi difference regarding the nature of majaz (metaphor). Is majaz a substitute in the wording itself or only in the ruling? The answer determines when we can resort to metaphorical interpretation. Understanding this distinction is key to Hanafi istinbat, as it directly affects rulings in contracts, oaths, and emancipation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two definitions of majaz
Locate the passage where Abu Hanifa defines majaz as 'khalaf 'an al-haqiqah fi haqq al-lafz' and the Sahibayn as 'khalaf 'an al-haqiqah fi haqq al-hukm'. Note that one focuses on wording, the other on ruling.
The Arabic terms: 'خلف عن الحقيقة في حق اللفظ' vs 'في حق الحكم'.
2
Determine the condition for applying majaz under each definition
For Abu Hanifa: majaz can be used even if the literal meaning is impossible in itself. For the Sahibayn: majaz is only used when the literal meaning is possible but acting on it is prevented by an impediment (mani').
The Sahibayn require that the literal meaning be possible; otherwise, there is no ruling to substitute.
3
Apply to the slave example
Consider the master's statement to an older slave: 'This is my son.' The literal meaning (biological sonship) is impossible. According to Abu Hanifa, majaz is applied, so the slave is freed. According to the Sahibayn, no majaz, the slave remains a slave.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) of the definitional difference.
4
Examine the exception: wife-daughter case
If a man says to his wife 'This is my daughter,' this is not taken as a metaphor for divorce, because the literal meaning (daughter) would contradict marriage itself. Metaphor cannot be used where literal meaning contradicts the context.
The principle: 'لا استعارة مع وجود التنافي' (no metaphorical usage with contradiction).
5
Generalise to other cases
The text mentions analogous cases: 'I owe a thousand on this wall,' 'My slave is free,' 'My donkey is free.' These follow the same rule: whether literal impossibility is sufficient (Abu Hanifa) or requires a prevention (Sahibayn).
The exception for contradiction only applies when there is inherent contradiction, not mere impossibility.

Commentary
This passage sets out a core Hanafi usul principle: the relationship between the literal (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz) meanings. It first presents the difference between Imam Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the nature of majaz as a substitute. For Abu Hanifa, majaz is a substitute in the very wording (lafz), meaning that when we use a word metaphorically, it is as if we are using a replacement word. For the Sahibayn, majaz is a substitute only in the ruling (hukm), meaning that the same word can carry both literal and metaphorical rulings. This difference leads to a practical divergence: if the literal meaning is impossible, the Sahibayn say no recourse to majaz because the substitute is only for the ruling, and if the literal is impossible, there is no ruling to substitute. But Abu Hanifa, since he sees the wording itself as a substitute, allows majaz even when the literal is impossible. The example: A master says to his older slave 'This is my son.' The literal meaning (biological sonship) is impossible because the slave is older and a slave. According to the Sahibayn, no metaphor is applied; the slave remains a slave. Then it addresses an objection: If a man says to his wife 'This is my daughter,' this is not taken as a metaphor for divorce, even if the wife is older or younger, because the literal meaning would contradict marriage, and metaphor cannot be used where contradiction exists. In contrast, the statement 'This is my son' to a slave does not contradict ownership; ownership is established and then emancipation occurs.

The Absolute Command: Obligation or Recommendation?
Presents the ikhtilaf on whether the imperative form indicates obligation or not

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on the meaning of the imperative form (siyaghat al-amr) and evaluate the opposing arguments using the rational proof provided.

Core Idea
The imperative form is haqiqah for obligation unless a qarinah diverts it.

In this passage from Usul al-Shashi, the author presents the famous dispute on what the imperative form (siyaghat al-amr) indicates when it appears without any contextual clue. This is a foundational issue in usul al-fiqh because it determines how we interpret commands in the Quran and Sunnah. The Hanafi school, along with the majority, holds that the default is obligation, but they acknowledge that evidence can shift the meaning to recommendation or permissibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central question
The dispute is about the meaning of the absolute command (al-amr al-mutlaq) – the imperative form free from any contextual indication (qarina) that specifies obligation, recommendation, or permissibility.
2
List the positions mentioned
The text lists five main positions: majority (obligation), Mu'tazila (recommendation), Ash'ari (suspension), homonymy (obligation, recommendation, permissibility), and the Hanafi position which aligns with the majority but is specifically stated as 'the correct position of the madhhab.'
3
State the Hanafi position and its proof
The Hanafi position: the imperative necessitates obligation (wujub) unless evidence indicates otherwise. The proof is rational: leaving a command is disobedience (ma'siya) and obeying is obedience (ta'a), which implies a presumption of obligation.
The couplet from Al-Hamas illustrates the linguistic usage that commands demand compliance.
4
Understand the role of qarina
The Hanafi position is contingent on the absence of a qarina. If a qarina is present (e.g., in the Quranic examples given), the meaning may shift. This is not an absolute rule but a default presumption.
5
Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
For example, the command 'Establish prayer' (aqim al-salah) is taken as obligatory by Hanafis unless a qarina suggests otherwise, while a Mu'tazili might consider it recommended in the absence of a qarina.
This shows how the usul difference translates into fiqh rulings.

Prohibition does not negate legal effects—an act being haram does not void its shar'i consequences.

In usul al-fiqh, it is common to encounter arguments that use a text prohibiting an act to argue that the act cannot produce any valid legal results. Core Idea
'لا بل' corrects mistakes — in reports it adds, in creative acts it replaces.

The phrase 'لا بل' ('no, rather') is used to correct a preceding statement, but its legal effect depends on whether the statement is a report (ikhbar) of a fact or a creative act (insha) that brings about a new legal state. The Hanafi proof is linguistic: 'لا بل' functions to correct a mistake by replacing the first statement with the second, but the first is not nullified. This nuance is derived from the broader fiqh rules on divorce counts.

If the divorce is phrased as a report (e.g., 'I divorced you yesterday once, no, twice'), only two divorces occur because it is treated like an acknowledgment: the second number adds to the first.
This shows that the operative factor is the nature of the speech act, not the topic.

Blocking the Ruling (Man' al-Hukm) with Examples
Illustrating man' al-hukm through three Hanafi debate examples.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Man' al-hukm denies that the claimed ruling applies to the specific case, without denying the ruling itself.

In legal debate, one common technique is to 'block' the opponent's ruling (man' al-hukm) by showing that the ruling they claim does not actually apply to the case at hand. This lesson presents three classic Hanafi examples that illustrate this method. By analyzing these examples, you will learn how to differentiate between the ruling itself and its application, a skill essential for constructing and critiquing legal arguments.

The Cause-Based Argument (al-Qawl al-Mujib al-'Illah)
Defining and illustrating the cause-based objection in Hanafi jadal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and apply the cause-based objection (al-qawl al-mujib al-'illah) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, distinguishing it from other types of objections.

Core Idea
Acknowledge the cause, but reframe its effect; the dispute shifts to what the cause actually produces.

In legal debates, sometimes you concede the opponent's cause but argue its consequence is different. This is no surrender—it's a strategic reframing. This lesson unpacks the 'cause-based argument' (al-qawl al-mujib al-'illah) and its application in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Define the concept
Recognize that the objector accepts the attribute as a cause but denies the claimed legal effect. The cause is not rejected; instead, its effect is reinterpreted.
Contrast with objections that reject the cause entirely.
2
Examine the elbow example
The opponent says the elbow is a boundary (cause), so it should not be washed (effect). The Hanafi response accepts 'boundary' as a cause but argues that the effect of a boundary is exclusion from the bounded, meaning the elbow itself is the boundary of the forearm, so it is not included in the washing ruling.
The cause is the same, but the effect changes from 'not washing the elbow' to 'the elbow is the boundary, so it is part of the bounded?' Actually, the Hanafi effect is that the elbow is not washed, but for a different reason: the boundary is not part of the bounded.
3
Examine the Ramadan fast example
The opponent analogizes Ramadan fast to make-up fasts: both are obligatory, so both require servant's specification (cause). The Hanafi response accepts that obligatory fasts require specification (cause), but shows that in Ramadan, specification is already provided by the Lawgiver through time, so the effect (servant's specification) is not required here.
The distinguishing feature is that specification exists from the Lawgiver, not that the cause differs.
4
Apply the pattern generically
In any legal debate, if you accept the opponent's cause, you can still contest the effect by showing that the claimed effect does not follow from that cause in this specific case due to a contextual factor.
This differs from simply rejecting the analogy; you concede the analogy but tweak the outcome.

Fasad al-Wad' (Corruption of Placement) in Jadal
Defines fasad al-wad' with an example from marriage conversion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a case of fasad al-wad' and explain why the illa is inappropriate for the ruling.

Core Idea
Fasad al-wad' exposes an illa that contradicts the nature of the ruling—protector cannot be destroyer.

In Hanafi jadal, not every arguable cause (illa) is valid. Fasad al-wad' is a potent critique: it shows that the opponent's proposed illa is logically incompatible with the ruling it purports to justify. Understanding this technique sharpens your ability to evaluate analogical reasoning in usul al-fiqh, especially when the nature of the illa conflicts with the nature of the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the opponent's illa
Locate the attribute (wasp) that the opponent claims is the cause (illa) for the ruling. In the example, the illa is 'conversion to Islam' (used to justify dissolution of marriage).

Identify the ruling and its nature
Determine the ruling the illa is meant to justify and consider the natural effect of that ruling. The ruling is 'dissolution of marriage'—a removal of ownership and rights.
3
Analyze the illa's inherent property
Consider what the illa is known to do in Shari'ah. Islam is a covenant that protects rights and ownership (as in marriage), not one that destroys them.
4
Compare illa property with ruling nature
Check if the illa's property logically leads to the ruling. A protector (illa) cannot be a remover (ruling). This mismatch is fasad al-wad'—the illa is placed in a position that does not suit it.
5
Conclude and apply

Lack of evidence repels claims but does not impose obligations; acting without proof is not allowed in establishing a ruling.

Commentary
This passage is a demonstration of a key usul principle: that a lack of evidence (la dalil) can only be used as proof to repel a claim (daf‘), not to impose an obligation (ilzam). The author gives three illustrative cases from Hanafi fiqh. First: a person of unknown lineage cannot be forced to pay the blood-money of a free man on a claim of slavery, because that would be an imposition without proof. Second: in menstruation rulings, if blood exceeds a woman's habitual days, the excess is considered istihadah (non-menstrual bleeding) because it is ambiguous; to change her habit would be acting without evidence. Similarly, a woman who begins istihadah at puberty has a maximum menstrual period of ten days, since anything less is ambiguous; to rule otherwise would require evidence. Third: the case of a missing person (mafqud) — neither do others inherit from him nor does he inherit from relatives, because in both cases the claim lacks evidence. The passage then addresses an objection: some might think Abu Hanifa rejected khums on ambergris based purely on absence of evidence. The author clarifies that Abu Hanifa simply gave his excuse; his actual reasoning was that ambergris resembles fish, which itself resembles water, and there is no khums on it. This reinforces the principle that absence of evidence is not itself a basis for a ruling, but only a justification for withholding a ruling.

لا دليل (la dalil): Lack of evidence or proof for a legal claim or obligation.

Is Absence of Evidence a Proof? The Ambergris Case
Defense of a Hanafi ruling against the charge of relying on lack of evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between using absence of evidence as a proof (invalid) and giving a personal excuse for not finding evidence, using the Hanafi example of ambergris.

Core Idea
Absence of a proof is not itself a proof; a ruling must have a positive basis, even if that basis is analogy.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, one cannot simply say 'there is no evidence, so the ruling is X.' That would be tamassuk bi 'adam al-dalil, which is invalid. But what if a scholar says 'I didn't find a tradition, so I rule accordingly'? Is that permissible? This passage defends Abu Hanifa's statement on ambergris from this charge, showing that his ruling was actually based on qiyas, not mere absence. Understanding this nuance is critical for proper istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the usul principle at stake
What is tamassuk bi 'adam al-dalil? Why is it invalid in Hanafi usul? Recognize that absence of evidence cannot be used as a positive proof.
This principle is agreed upon across madhahib.
2
Analyze the objection
What was the charge against Abu Hanifa? The passage mentions: 'It is narrated from Abu Hanifa that he said: There is no khums on ambergris, because the tradition did not come with it.' This appears to be clinging to lack of evidence.
The objector claims that Abu Hanifa used 'adam al-dalil as a hujja.
3
Examine the defense
How does the author refute the charge? He explains that Abu Hanifa's statement was an expression of his personal excuse (udhr), not a general proof. The real basis was qiyas: ambergris is like fish, fish is like water, and water has no khums.
The defense shows that there is a positive dalil (qiyas), not mere absence.
4
Apply the lesson
In your own usul analysis, when you encounter a ruling that seems based on 'adam al-dalil, always look for a hidden positive proof. Differentiate between 'I have no evidence' (personal incapacity) and 'absence of evidence is evidence of absence' (invalid).
This protects the integrity of istidlal.

Commentary
This passage addresses the usul principle that 'absence of evidence is not a proof' (tamassuk bi 'adam al-dalil). The first sentence is a ruling about a missing person (mafqud): he does not inherit from others nor they from him, establishing that entitlements require evidence. Then a question is posed: Abu Hanifa ruled no khums on ambergris because 'the tradition did not come with it'—could this be an invalid reliance on lack of evidence? The answer clarifies that Abu Hanifa was not positing a general principle; rather, he was explaining his personal excuse for not finding a tradition. He analogized ambergris to fish, and fish to water, which has no khums. Thus, the ruling has a positive basis (qiyas), not mere absence of evidence. This is a Hanafi defense against the charge of using 'adam al-dalil as a hujja.
