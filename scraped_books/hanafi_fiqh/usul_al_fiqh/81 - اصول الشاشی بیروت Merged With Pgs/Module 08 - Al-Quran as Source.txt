Setting Aside a Hadith When It Contradicts a General Quranic Verse
Illustrating the principle of setting aside a hadith when it contradicts a general Quranic ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a solitary hadith is set aside when it irreconcilably contradicts a general Quranic verse, and identify when reconciliation is impossible.

Core Idea
When a solitary hadith and a general Quranic text cannot be reconciled, the hadith is set aside — the Quranic generality remains binding.

This passage illustrates a foundational Hanafi usul principle: a general Quranic ruling (amm al-Quran) is definitive (qat'i) in its meaning, while a solitary hadith (khabar ahad) is speculative (zanni) in transmission. When they conflict irreconcilably, the hadith cannot override the Quranic general; instead, the hadith is set aside. The author gives two concrete examples to anchor this rule, which you will analyze step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two opposing texts
List the Quranic verse (amm) and the solitary hadith (khabar) that appear to contradict each other.
In the first example: Quran 6:121 prohibits eating without tasmiya; the hadith says eat.
2
Attempt reconciliation (tawfiq)
Try to interpret both texts harmoniously — for instance, by specifying or qualifying one with the other.
If reconciliation is possible, both texts are acted upon. If not, proceed to step 3.
3
Determine if reconciliation is possible
Assess whether accepting the hadith would negate the Quranic ruling entirely. Here, if deliberate omission were permissible, forgetting would also be permissible (by the same reasoning), which lifts the Quranic prohibition.
Reconciliation fails because the hadith, if applied, would abrogate the Quranic general, not merely specify it.
4
Set aside the hadith and keep the Quranic ruling
Since reconciliation is impossible, the hadith is set aside (yutrak al-khabar) and the Quranic generality remains operative.
This preserves the hierarchy of sources: Quran over khabar ahad when they irreconcilably conflict.

This passage demonstrates the Hanafi principle that when a general Quranic ruling and a specific hadith contradict each other and reconciliation (tawfiq) is impossible, the hadith is set aside (yutrak al-khabar) in favor of the Quranic generality. The Quranic Verse on Sadaqat: Mention of Categories is for Definition, Not for Obligation to Give to All
Deriving that the listing of charity recipients is to define where to spend, not to require giving to each.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of siyāq al-kalām to derive that the listing of zakāh recipients defines permissible channels, not mandatory distribution to all categories.

Core Idea
The verse lists charity recipients to define where to spend, not to require giving to every category.

This passage explores the legal implication (dalāla) of the Qur'anic verse on zakāh distribution. You will see how the context of a preceding criticism verse (9:58) shapes the meaning of the famous 'eight categories' verse (9:60). Understanding this context is crucial for correctly applying the Hanafi ruling on zakāh distribution.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two verses and their relationship
Locate the verse on charity recipients (9:60) and the verse that precedes it (9:58), which criticizes hypocrites who object to the Prophet's distribution. The juxtaposition is the key to interpretation.
The word 'عقيب' (following) indicates direct sequential context.
2
Apply siyāq al-kalām (contextual analysis)
Recognize that the criticism in 9:58 shows that the listing of categories in 9:60 is a response to those who found fault with the distribution. Therefore, the purpose is to cut off their greed (qaṭʿu ṭamaʿihim) and define where zakāh can be spent.
This is a Hanafi usul principle: context restricts or clarifies meaning.
3
Analyze the preposition 'li' (لِ) in the verse
The preposition 'li' in 'lil-fuqarā'i' indicates assignment for benefit (al-ikhtiṣāṣ). Combined with the context, it shows the categories are channels (maṣārif), not mandatory individual recipients.
The technical term for this type of assignment is 'mubayyan' (clarified).
4
Derive the ruling on discharging obligation
Since the categories are channels, the obligation (al-khurūj ʿan al-ʿuhda) is fulfilled by giving zakāh to any of the eight categories, not necessarily all. One may choose one or more categories as long as the amount is spent in these defined channels.
This is the mufta bihi Hanafi position.

Mantuq of Prohibition: Deriving Witness Capacity of the Fasiq
Deriving a ruling from Quranic prohibition via usul principles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of mantuq of prohibition and tahqiq al-manat to derive that a fasiq retains witness capacity, and state the resulting ruling on marriage witnesses.

Core Idea
A prohibition presupposes the existence of the prohibited thing—so the fasiq still has witness capacity.

How do we derive that a fasiq (sinner) can still be considered a witness when the Quran clearly says 'do not accept their testimony'? The answer lies in a fundamental usul principle: a prohibition logically implies that the thing prohibited exists. In this lesson, we will walk through the Hanafi reasoning that distinguishes between witness capacity (ahlīyah al-shahāda) and acceptance of testimony (qubūl), and see how this leads to the ruling that marriage witnessed by fasiqs is valid.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source text and its prohibition
The verse says 'Wa lā taqbalū lahum shahādatan abadā' – 'And never accept their testimony forever.' The prohibition is clear: do not accept testimony from sinners. First, note the language: it prohibits acceptance (qabūl), not existence of testimony.
The verse is in Surah An-Nur, 24:4, originally about those who accuse chaste women of adultery without four witnesses.
2
Apply the principle of mantuq of prohibition
The Hanafi usul principle: a prohibition (nahy) on an act logically entails that the act exists. Here, prohibiting 'acceptance of testimony' means there is testimony to accept. Therefore, the fasiq must have the capacity to give testimony; otherwise the prohibition would be meaningless.
This is based on the rational maxim: 'al-nahy 'an shay' yufīdu wujūdahu' – prohibition of something presupposes its existence.
3
Distinguish between capacity (ahlīyah) and acceptance (qubūl)
The passage clarifies: 'Their testimony is not accepted due to corruption in performance (fasad fī al-adā'), not because they lack testimony essentially (lā li-'adam al-shahāda aslan).' So the fasiq has the capacity to be a witness, but his testimony is rejected because sinfulness corrupts his faithful fulfillment of the testimony duty.
This distinction is a hallmark of Hanafi usul: ahlīyah is a precondition for valid testimony, but acceptance is a separate condition regarding reliability.
4
Derive the ruling on marriage witnesses
If the fasiq has witness capacity, then when he witnesses a marriage, the condition that 'two Muslim witnesses' is met (since capacity alone suffices for the validity of the contract). Therefore, a marriage contract witnessed by fasiqs is valid. Li'an is not required because li'an is a substitute for testimony, and the husband's testimony is not accepted due to his sin.
Li'an would be required if the husband had valid testimony but chose not to produce it; here, his testimony is inherently rejected.
5
Confirm the ruling through further application
The same principle applies to other acts that require witnesses: e.g., testimony in court, hudud cases. The fasiq can be a valid witness in terms of capacity, but his testimony is not accepted unless he repents and becomes 'adl (just).
This derivation shows how usul principles generate nuanced rulings.

Weak Evidentiary Clinging in Usul al-Fiqh: Two Examples
Identification of two flawed arguments from naqli evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and critique instances of weak tamassuk by distinguishing between what a text directly proves and what it does not.

Core Idea
A text's scope must match the ruling sought — stretching evidence is weak tamassuk.

In this lesson, we examine two examples of weak evidentiary clinging from a classical usul text. These examples reveal a common pitfall: using a text that proves one point to argue for another, unrelated point. Strengthening your ability to spot such misapplications is essential for sound legal derivation. The author warns us by highlighting the mismatch between the text and the claimed ruling.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of tamassuk da'if
Tamassuk da'if is clinging to evidence that does not actually support the claim. The text may be authentic but its scope is narrower than the ruling being derived.
The author says it is a warning about 'points of defect' in istinbat.
2
Analyze the first example: hadith on vomiting
Some argue that the Prophet's vomiting without performing wudu proves vomiting is not a nullifier. The hadith only shows he did not do wudu on that occasion, not that vomiting never nullifies wudu.
The dispute is about the nullifying effect, not about the immediate act.
3
Analyze the second example: verse on carrion
Citing 'Forbidden to you is carrion' to prove that water becomes spoiled by a dead fly. The verse forbids eating carrion, but says nothing about water impurity from a fly's death.
The text is about prohibition of consumption, not about taharah rulings.
4
Formulate the principle
The evidence must directly address the specific legal issue. If the text proves a different point or is silent on the contested ruling, the tamassuk is weak.

Core Idea

In the Hanafi school, the primary sources of law are Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas. But what happens when two verses, two hadiths, or two analogies appear to contradict each other? The Second Type of Coercion: When the Prohibited Becomes Permissible

Core Idea
Under coercion, a normally prohibited act becomes permissible; refusing it then becomes sinful if it leads to death.
