First Type: Customary Usage (Dalalat al-Urf)
Introduces custom as the first factor that abandons literal meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of customary usage (urf) to override the literal meaning of a word in oath contexts and identify the underlying rationale.

Core Idea
Customary meaning of a word overrides its literal meaning for legal rulings like oaths.

In this chapter, the author explains that the literal meaning of a word is not always applied. Five factors can cause it to be abandoned. The first and most common is custom (urf). When a word has a well-known customary meaning among people, that meaning is taken as the speaker's intended meaning, even if it differs from the original literal sense. This is especially important in oaths and contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning (haqiqah)
Start by recognizing the original, literal definition of the word in question, as it was coined in the language.
2
Check for a customary meaning (urf)
Determine whether there is a well-established customary usage among the people in that context that differs from the literal meaning.
Custom can be general (urf am) or specific (urf khas).
3
Apply the customary meaning as the apparent intent
If the word has a customary meaning, presume that the speaker intended that meaning, unless there is a contextual indicator (qarinah) showing otherwise.
The ruling (e.g., hanth in oaths) is then based on the customary meaning.
4
Derive the legal ruling
Apply the customary meaning to the specific case to reach the hukm. For oaths: if the customary meaning excludes something, the oath is not broken.
Example: 'head' customarily means head of a large animal, so buying a sparrow's head does not break the oath.

Commentary
This passage opens a chapter on the factors that override the literal (haqiqi) meanings of words. The first type is 'dalalah al-urf' (indication of custom). The reasoning is that legal rulings are established based on the speaker's intended meaning as conveyed by words. If a word has a customary usage among people, that customary meaning is taken as the apparent intended meaning. Examples from oaths: if someone swears not to buy a 'head', the custom determines that it means the head of a large animal, not a bird. Similarly for eggs. This is a key Hanafi principle where urf can specify or restrict the literal meaning.

Key Terms
دلالة العرف (dalalat al-urf): The indication of customary usage that overrides the literal meaning of a word.
الحقيقة (al-haqiqah): Literal meaning of a word as originally coined.

Basing Oaths on Intended Meaning (Ma'na)
Illustrates the principle of basing rulings on intended meaning in oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that oaths are interpreted according to customary intended meaning, not literal wording, and distinguish between cases where the oath is broken or not.

Core Idea
Oaths follow customary meaning, not literal wording: an oath to avoid 'meat' excludes fish but includes human flesh.

In Hanafi usul, legal rulings follow the intended meaning (ma'na) behind words, not just their literal form. This is especially important in oaths, where a person's motive and customary understanding determine whether an act breaks the oath. The passage from Usul al-Shashi illustrates this principle through a series of examples—from sales during Friday prayer to oaths about hitting and eating meat. By studying these examples, you will learn to identify the underlying rationale (illah) and apply it consistently.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its literal wording
Read each oath example and note the exact words used (e.g., 'I will not hit my wife', 'I will not eat meat').
Literal wording is the starting point, but not decisive.
2
Determine the customary meaning and likely motive
Consider what the swearer would customarily intend. For 'hit', the usual meaning is causing pain. For 'meat', the customary meaning in Arabic is flesh of land animals with blood.
Use the principle that the oath follows the intention and custom ('urf).
3
Apply the principle: does the act fall within that meaning?
Check if the act performed corresponds to the intended meaning. If the act matches the customary meaning, the oath is broken (hanth). If not, it is not broken.
For example, playful hitting without pain does not match the intended meaning of hitting.
4
State the ruling (hanth or no hanth)
Based on your analysis, conclude whether the oath is broken. Provide the ruling for each example from the passage.
Verify with the text: pulling hair with pain = hanth; play = no hanth; eating fish = no hanth; eating pork = hanth.
5
Identify the 'illah (effective cause) behind the ruling
Extract the common reason: all rulings depend on the presence or absence of the intended meaning (pain, conveyance of message, blood content).
This 'illah can be applied to new cases by analogy.

And if we suppose a sale that does not prevent the two contracting parties from going to Friday prayer, as if they are in a ship sailing to the mosque, the sale is not disliked. And on this we said: If someone swears not to hit his wife, then he pulls her hair, or bites her, or strangles her, he breaks his oath if it is with the intent of pain. And if the form of hitting and pulling hair occurs in play without pain, he does not break his oath. And (considering this meaning it is said: If someone swears not to eat meat, and eats fish or locust meat, he does not break his oath. Commentary
This passage illustrates the usul principle that legal rulings follow the intended meaning (ma'na) and customary understanding, not mere literal wording. It begins with an example: a sale during Friday prayer is not disliked if the transaction does not prevent attendance (e.g., the parties are on a ship heading to the mosque). This is because the prohibition is based on distraction, not the act of selling per se. Building on this, the author applies the same reasoning to oaths. If someone swears not to hit his wife, any act causing pain (pulling hair, biting, strangling) breaks the oath because the customary meaning of 'hitting' includes causing pain. However, playful actions without pain do not break the oath. Similarly, hitting or speaking to a dead person does not break the oath because the intended meanings (causing pain, conveying understanding) are absent. The key example: if someone swears not to eat 'meat' (lahm), the customary understanding excludes fish and locust (which are not typically called 'meat' in Arabic), but includes pork and human flesh because they are derived from blood-containing animals. The ruling is based on the widely understood motive of avoiding bloody substances. This demonstrates that oaths are interpreted according to custom and the likely intent of the swearer.

Key Terms
الحنث (al-hinth): Breaking an oath by committing the sworn act; incurring the legal consequence.
المعنى (al-ma'na): The intended meaning or customary understanding behind a legal statement.
الإيلام (al-ilam): The act of causing pain; the essential element in hitting that triggers oath violation.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi usul, oaths are interpreted according to the customary intended meaning (ma'na) and the likely motive, not the literal dictionary definition.

The Word 'على' Indicates Obligation
Explaining that 'على' conveys obligation and its application in debt and aman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the linguistic indication of the preposition 'على' in Hanafi usul and apply it to distinguish debt from mere possession.

Core Idea
The word 'على' imposes liability; 'عندي' only states custody.

In Hanafi usul, certain prepositions carry fixed legal meanings. This passage zeroes in on the word 'على' (upon), explaining that it conveys obligation (ilzam) rather than mere possession. The author contrasts this with words like 'عندي' and illustrates the rule with a practical case from Kitab al-Siyar al-Kabir involving aman (safe conduct).

Guided Path
1
Identify the key preposition
Locate the word 'على' in the source text and note its immediate context. This is the central term being analyzed.
The passage begins with 'فصل [في على]' – a dedicated section.
2
Understand its root meaning
The author explains that 'على' originally conveys superiority and elevation, which metaphorically extends to liability.
This linguistic origin supports the usul claim.
3
Contrast with other words
Compare 'على' with 'عندي', 'معي', and 'قبلي'. The author states that these do not impose obligation but merely indicate possession or custody.
This contrast sharpens the distinctive role of 'على'.
4
Apply to a case from al-Siyar al-Kabir
Examine the aman example: when the commander says 'آمنونی على عشرة', the word 'على' indicates that the ten are separate from him (exclusive) and he has the right to designate them. This shows how the rule is applied in a real legal scenario.
The same ruling applies if he uses 'و', 'فعشرة', or 'ثم عشرة'.

فصل [في على] كلمة على للإلزام، وأصله لإفادة معنى التفوق والتعلي. ولهذا لو قال : لفلان على ألف يُحمل على الدين، بخلاف ما لو قال: عندي أو معي أو قبلي. وعلى هذا قال في السير الكبير : إذا قال رأس الحصن: آمنونی على عشرة من أهل الحصن ففعلنا، فالعشرة سواه، وخيار التعيين له، ولو قال: آمنوني وعشرة، أو فعشرة أو ثم عشرة ففعلنا، فكذلك.
Section [on 'ala]. The word 'ala (upon) indicates obligation (ilzam), and its origin is to convey the meaning of superiority and elevation. Therefore, if someone says, 'So-and-so has upon me a thousand [dirhams]', it is understood as a debt, unlike if he says, 'with me' or 'in my possession' or 'before me'. And upon this, it is said in al-Siyar al-Kabir: If the commander of a fort says, 'Grant me aman (protection) upon ten people of the fort,' and we do so, then the ten are exclusive of him, and he has the option of designation. And if he says, 'Grant me aman and ten,' or 'Grant me aman ten,' or 'then ten,' and we do so, it is the same.
Source pages p. 174

Commentary
This passage is a separate section on the preposition 'على' (upon). In Hanafi usul, 'على' has the primary function of ilzam (obligation/imposition). Its root meaning involves superiority and elevation, which metaphorically implies liability. For example, if someone says 'لفلان على ألف' (so-and-so has a thousand upon me), this is interpreted as a debt due to the word 'على', because it conveys an obligation on the speaker. In contrast, if he uses words like 'عندي' (with me), 'معي' (with me), or 'قبلي' (in my possession), these only state possession, not liability. The author then cites a practical example from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani's Kitab al-Siyar al-Kabir (a Zahir al-Riwayah text) concerning aman (safe conduct) in warfare. If a fort commander says 'آمنونی على عشرة من أهل الحصن' (grant me aman upon ten people of the fort), meaning he requests protection for ten individuals, the word 'على' here indicates that the ten are additional to him (the commander is excluded) and he has the discretion to identify which ten. Similarly, if he uses 'و' (and), 'فعشرة' (so ten), or 'ثم عشرة' (then ten), the same ruling applies.

Key Terms
على (ʿalā): A preposition indicating obligation (ilzam) and liability; when used in the context of debts, it imposes a duty upon the speaker.
الإلزام (al-ilzām): The legal effect of imposing a binding obligation or liability.
