The Hierarchical Duty of the Mujtahid: Quran First, Then Sunnah
Hierarchy of sources for ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the hierarchical order of sources a mujtahid must follow when deriving a ruling, and explain why the Quran is given precedence over the Sunnah and other sources.

Core Idea
The mujtahid must first seek the ruling from the Quran, then the Sunnah, before qiyas or other sources.

This lesson presents a foundational rule in Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh: the sequential priority of sources for ijtihad. When a new incident arises, the mujtahid cannot resort to analogy or personal reasoning until he has thoroughly searched the Quran and the Sunnah. This hierarchy ensures that rulings are grounded in the strongest and most definitive evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary duty
Read the Arabic text carefully: the mujtahid is obligated (wajib) to seek (talab) the ruling (hukm) of the incident (al-ḥāditha) from the Book of Allah (Kitāb Allāh).
The term 'wajib' here indicates a binding duty, not just a recommendation.
2
Note the sequential order
The phrase 'thumma min sunnat Rasūl Allāh' means 'then from the Sunnah'. This 'thumma' (then) establishes a clear order: first Quran, then Sunnah. The rest of the commentary explains that if found in the Quran, no further search is needed.
In Hanafi usul, this order is strict: one cannot skip to Sunnah if a ruling is found in the Quran.
3
Understand the rationale for precedence
The Quran is given precedence because it is 'the strongest of proofs' (aqwā al-dalā'il) and 'definitive, divine speech' (qat'ī, kalām rabbānī) over the speculative (ẓannī) nature of many hadith.
This reflects the Hanafi epistemological hierarchy: qat'ī over ẓannī.
4
Recognize this as an introduction to qiyas
The passage ends by stating that this chapter is an 'introduction to the chapter on qiyas', explaining that qiyas is only valid after exhausting the primary sources.
Qiyas is a fallback, not a first resort.

Definition and Conditions of Ijtihad
Defines ijtihad and lists conditions for mujtahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ijtihad linguistically and technically and list the conditions required for a mujtahid.

Core Idea
Ijtihad is disciplined effort to derive rulings, not opinion; it requires mastery of Quran and Sunnah.

This lesson lays the groundwork for understanding who can perform ijtihad and what it entails. The text distinguishes the linguistic and technical meanings and sets the conditions that make a person a mujtahid. These conditions ensure that ijtihad is a rigorous, methodical process, not arbitrary speculation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic definition
Find the phrase 'بذل الوسع والطاقة في المقصود' and understand that linguistically ijtihad means exerting utmost effort towards a goal.
2
Identify the technical definition
Locate 'بذل الوسع والطاقة في طلب الحكم بطريقه' – i.e., expending effort to seek a ruling by its proper method, which distinguishes it from mere opinion.
3
List the conditions for a mujtahid
Extract the conditions from the passage: comprehensive knowledge of the Quran (including linguistic and legal meanings, general/specific, command/prohibition) and knowledge of the Sunnah (types: mashhur, mutawatir, ahad, and texts).
The passage references 'al-Fusul' and mentions that this knowledge includes methods of istidlal (inference) from the text.
4
Understand the scope of required knowledge
Note that the mujtahid must be familiar with the methods of deriving rulings from the Quran and Sunnah, including 'عبارة النص وإشارته ودلالته واقتضائه' (the expression, indication, implication, and requirement of the text).

Definition and Conditions of Ijtihad in Hanafi Usul
Defines ijtihad and states conditions for a mujtahid.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of ijtihad and list the minimum knowledge required for a mujtahid.

Core Idea
Ijtihad is systematic effort within sources; a mujtahid must master Quran and Sunnah linguistically and legally.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi provides a foundational definition of ijtihad and sets the bar for who may engage in it. The Hanafi school is particularly meticulous about the breadth of knowledge required, ensuring that ijtihad is anchored in textual evidence and sound method. Understanding these conditions is essential for distinguishing disciplined legal reasoning from arbitrary opinion.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two definitions
Locate the linguistic definition ('bazl al-wus‘ wa al-taqah fi al-maqsud') and the technical definition ('bazl al-wus‘ wa al-taqah fi talab al-hukm bi-tariqihi'). Note that the technical definition adds 'through its proper method' — this is the key constraint.
The proper method refers to the four types of textual inference: ibarah, isharah, dalalah, iqtida.
2
Identify the knowledge conditions
The text states: the mujtahid must encompass knowledge of the Quran with its linguistic and legal meanings, including the distinctions between 'amm and khass, commands and prohibitions, etc. He must also know the Sunnah with its transmission types (mashhoor, mutawatir, ahad) and its texts.
This knowledge ensures ijtihad is not based on personal whim but on comprehensive source mastery.
3
Connect to inference methods
The footnote clarifies that the 'proper method' refers to what was previously discussed: inference from the text's expression, indication, connotation, and implication. These are the tools of istidlal.
This links the condition to the broader usul framework.
4
Reflect on the purpose
The conditions serve to guarantee that ijtihad produces rulings that are defensible within the Hanafi tradition. A mujtahid without these qualifications cannot reliably derive rulings.

Shubha bi al-Mahal vs Shubha fi al-Zann
Defines and distinguishes two types of shubha with examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between shubha bi al-mahal and shubha fi al-zann, and apply the Hanafi rule that only shubha bi al-mahal drops the hadd regardless of personal belief.

Core Idea
Shubha arising from a legal proof overrides personal conjecture.

This passage from al-Shashi's Usul clarifies two types of shubha (ambiguity) that affect criminal liability. The first type, shubha bi al-mahal, arises from a textual proof and is objective; the second, shubha fi al-zann, is subjective. Understanding this distinction is crucial for applying hadd penalties correctly, as the Hanafi school gives priority to the legal proof over the individual's opinion.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of shubha
Define shubha bi al-mahal (shubha in the locus) and shubha fi al-zann (shubha in conjecture). Note the key difference: the former is based on a legal proof, the latter on personal guesswork.
The text gives three names for the first type: شبهة الاشتباه, شبهة الفعل, شبهة الظن.
2
Recognize the source of authority
Understand that shubha bi al-mahal is rooted in a text (like the hadith 'You and your wealth belong to your father'), making it stronger than personal opinion.
The hadith is cited as evidence for the father's right over his son's property.
3
Apply to the father-son slave-girl example
Analyze the case: a father has intercourse with his son's slave-girl. Even if he knew it was forbidden, he is not hadded because the hadith creates a shubha of ownership.
Lineage is also established due to this shubha.
4
Apply to the son-father slave-girl example
Contrast with the son having intercourse with his father's slave-girl. Here, the son's personal belief matters because no textual proof creates the shubha for him.
If the son thought it was permissible, no hadd; if he thought it was forbidden, hadd applies.
5
Note the cross-madhhab dispute
The Hanafi position is that shubha bi al-mahal always drops the hadd, while the Shafi'i school may consider personal certainty. Understand the thamarah al-khilaf.
This difference leads to divergent rulings in similar cases.

Commentary
This passage is a usul rule regarding two topics: the types of shubha (ambiguity) and how a mujtahid resolves conflicting evidences. The text first defines shubha bi al-mahal (shubha in the locus) and shubha fi al-zann (shubha in conjecture). Shubha bi al-mahal arises from a legal proof (like the hadith 'you and your wealth belong to your father') and is stronger, removing the hadd even if the perpetrator's personal judgment says otherwise. Shubha fi al-zann relies on personal opinion and does not remove hadd if the perpetrator admits knowledge of prohibition. An example: a father who has intercourse with his son's slave-girl is not hadded because the hadith creates a shubha of ownership, even if he knew it was forbidden; lineage is established. In contrast, a son who has intercourse with his father's slave-girl is judged by his own belief: if he thought it was permissible (due to the hadith), no hadd; if he thought it was forbidden (so he knew it was wrong), then hadd applies. The text then addresses a scenario with a brother's or sister's slave-girl: due to clear separation of ownership, no shubha exists, so hadd is not dropped by mere claim of ignorance. The second part covers ta'arud al-adillah (conflict of proofs). When two Quranic verses conflict, the mujtahid turns to the Sunnah because the Prophet's hadith explains the Quran. An example is the apparent conflict between the verse ordering recitation in prayer and the verse ordering listening (in prayer). The Sunnah clarifies that the follower's recitation is not required. When two hadiths conflict, he turns to the sayings of the Companions or sound analogy. The hierarchy is: Quran > Sunnah > Athar of Sahabah > Qiyas. If chronology of revelation is known, the later abrogates the earlier; otherwise, recourse to the next level.

Key Terms
الشبهة بالمحل (ash-shubha bil-mahal): Ambiguity that arises from a legal proof (like a hadith) that creates doubt about the permissibility of an act, making the act resemble a permissible one.
الشبهة في الظن (ash-shubha fiz-zann): Ambiguity that arises from the individual's own conjecture or ignorance, not from a textual proof.

Ta'arud al-Adillah: Resolving Conflicting Evidences
Outlines the hierarchy for resolving conflicting legal proofs.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi hierarchy for resolving conflicting proofs: Quran → Sunnah → Athar → Qiyas, and distinguish between abrogation (naskh) and preference (tarjih).

Core Idea
When proofs conflict, the strongest is the Quran, then Sunnah, then Companions' sayings, then analogy.

This part of the passage provides the mujtahid with a clear hierarchy for when two proofs (like two verses or two hadiths) appear to contradict. The author explains that if the chronology of revelation is unknown, one must revert to the next strongest source. An example is given of two verses that seem to conflict regarding recitation in prayer, resolved by the Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict scenario
Recognize that the text addresses ta'arud (conflict) between two proofs that cannot be reconciled by specification (takhsis) or abrogation (naskh).
The conflict must be genuine, not apparent.
2
Recall the hierarchy of sources
Memorize the order: Quran → Sunnah → Athar of Sahabah → Qiyas. This is the default preference when chronology is unknown.
If one proof is later in time, it abrogates the earlier.
3
Apply to the example of two verses
Analyze the verses: verse A (al-Muzzammil 20) seems to obligate recitation for the follower, verse B (al-A'raf 204) obligates listening. They conflict. Since chronology is unknown, the mujtahid turns to the Sunnah, which clarifies that the follower's recitation is not required.
The hadith 'Whoever has an Imam, the recitation of the Imam is sufficient for him' resolves the conflict.
4
Understand the role of naskh
If the dates of the proofs are known, the later one abrogates the earlier. Otherwise, the hierarchy applies.
This prevents arbitrary selection.
5
Note the scope of the rule
For conflict between two hadiths, one turns to the sayings of the Companions (athar) or qiyas. The text emphasizes that the Sunnah is the strongest after the Quran because it explains the Quran.
This rule is for the mujtahid, not the common person.

Commentary
This passage is a usul rule regarding two topics: the types of shubha (ambiguity) and how a mujtahid resolves conflicting evidences. The text first defines shubha bi al-mahal (shubha in the locus) and shubha fi al-zann (shubha in conjecture). Shubha bi al-mahal arises from a legal proof (like the hadith 'you and your wealth belong to your father') and is stronger, removing the hadd even if the perpetrator's personal judgment says otherwise. Shubha fi al-zann relies on personal opinion and does not remove hadd if the perpetrator admits knowledge of prohibition. An example: a father who has intercourse with his son's slave-girl is not hadded because the hadith creates a shubha of ownership, even if he knew it was forbidden; lineage is established. In contrast, a son who has intercourse with his father's slave-girl is judged by his own belief: if he thought it was permissible (due to the hadith), no hadd; if he thought it was forbidden (so he knew it was wrong), then hadd applies. The text then addresses a scenario with a brother's or sister's slave-girl: due to clear separation of ownership, no shubha exists, so hadd is not dropped by mere claim of ignorance. The second part covers ta'arud al-adillah (conflict of proofs). When two Quranic verses conflict, the mujtahid turns to the Sunnah because the Prophet's hadith explains the Quran. An example is the apparent conflict between the verse ordering recitation in prayer and the verse ordering listening (in prayer). The Sunnah clarifies that the follower's recitation is not required. When two hadiths conflict, he turns to the sayings of the Companions or sound analogy. The hierarchy is: Quran > Sunnah > Athar of Sahabah > Qiyas. If chronology of revelation is known, the later abrogates the earlier; otherwise, recourse to the next level.

Key Terms
تعارض الدليلين (ta'arud ad-dalilayn): Conflict between two legal proofs that cannot be reconciled by specification or abrogation, requiring the mujtahid to give preference to one.

Ruling on Son's Intercourse with Father's Slave-Girl
Ruling on the son's intercourse with father's slave-girl based on zann.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the concept of shubha to derive the ruling on hadd and lineage in cases of zann.

Core Idea
Shubha based on textual proof drops hadd but does not establish lineage.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi examines how a mujtahid's subjective belief (zann) interacts with a legal semblance (shubha) to determine the application of hadd and lineage. It highlights the Hanafi principle that a shubha arising from a textual proof can exempt from punishment even when the act is essentially unlawful. The key differentiator is whether the shubha is rooted in a recognized textual evidence or merely a mistaken personal belief.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the key variable
The act is intercourse (wat') by the son with his father's slave-girl. The variable is the son's subjective belief (zann) about its permissibility.
Focus on the two scenarios: belief of prohibition vs. belief of permissibility.
2
Analyze the two scenarios of zann
If the son says 'I thought she was forbidden,' hadd is obligatory because he admits wrongdoing. If he says 'I thought she was permissible,' hadd is not obligatory.
The outcome depends on the presence of a shubha, not just the belief itself.
3
Identify the shubha and its textual basis
The shubha arises from the hadith 'أنت ومالك لأبيك' (You and your wealth belong to your father), which creates a semblance of ownership for the son in the father's property.
This hadith is a textual proof (dalil) that makes the son's belief of permissibility legally significant.
4
Contrast with the brother/sister case
If the son has intercourse with his brother's or sister's slave-girl, no such textual proof exists, and customary distinctions prevent any shubha. Thus, hadd is not dropped by a mere claim of ignorance.
This contrast shows that shubha must be based on a recognized proof, not just any confusion.
5
Determine the effect on lineage
Even though hadd is dropped due to shubha, the child's lineage is not established because the act remains zina in essence. Lineage requires a valid basis of ownership or marriage, which is absent.
Shubha only affects punishment, not the underlying legal status of the act.

Commentary
This passage presents a ruling from the chapter on the obligations of a mujtahid in Usul al-Shashi. It addresses the case of a son who has intercourse with his father's slave-girl. The key factor is the son's subjective belief (zann) about the permissibility of the act. If he believes it is forbidden, hadd (punishment for zina) is imposed because he admits he knowingly did wrong. If he believes it is permissible, hadd is not imposed due to a semblance of ownership (shubhat al-milk) derived from the prophetic saying 'You and your wealth belong to your father' (attributed to Ali). This saying creates a legal semblance that the son might consider the slave-girl as part of his father's wealth to which he has some right. The contrast is made with the case of a brother's or sister's slave-girl, where no such textual proof exists and the customary distinction of property prevents any semblance, so hadd is not dropped by mere claim of ignorance. However, even though hadd is dropped due to the semblance, the child's lineage is not established because the act itself is still zina in essence, and lineage requires a valid basis of ownership or marriage, which is absent here. The passage emphasizes that the shubha in the place (the slave-girl) arises from a specific religious proof (the hadith), which is why it is considered a valid excuse regardless of the son's actual belief.

Key Terms
الظن (al-zann): Subjective belief or opinion that is not certain, which the mujtahid uses to determine a ruling.
شبهة (shubha): A legal semblance or doubt that arises from a textual or analogical proof, affecting the application of punishments.
