Override of Literal Meaning by Customary Usage ('Urf)
Explaining the first of five reasons to override literal meaning: customary usage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when customary usage overrides literal meaning in oaths and apply the Hanafi principle of haqiqah qasirah.

Core Idea
Custom overrides literal meaning in oaths, but without resorting to metaphor—only to a restricted literal meaning.

This passage introduces the first of five reasons to abandon literal meaning: the indication of customary usage ('urf). The author explains that legal rulings are based on the speaker's intent, and widespread custom reveals that intent. The key examples are oaths about buying a 'head' or eating 'eggs'. The important nuance is that abandoning literal meaning does not automatically mean adopting metaphorical meaning; instead, Hanafi usul recognizes an intermediate category called haqiqah qasirah (deficient literal meaning).

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
Recognize that customary usage ('urf) is one of five factors that cause abandonment of literal meaning.
This is the first factor mentioned by the author.
2
Understand the rationale
The rule exists because legal effect depends on speaker's intent, and custom indicates that intent.
The passage states: 'Thubut al-ahkam bi-al-alfaz innama kana li-dalalah al-lafz 'ala al-ma'na al-murad li-al-mutakallim.'
3
Apply the rule to examples
In the oath examples, custom restricts the general term to a specific subset: 'head' of large animals, 'eggs' of chickens.
The example: swearing not to buy a head – conventionally means cattle/sheep head, not sparrow.
4
Draw the usul principle
Abandoning literal meaning does not entail moving to majaz; there is haqiqah qasirah.
The author says: 'tark al-haqiqah la yujibu al-masir ila al-majaz'.
5
Distinguish haqiqah qasirah from majaz
Both depart from strict literal meaning, but haqiqah qasirah is still literal in that it refers to a real subset, whereas majaz is figurative.
The commentary explains haqiqah qasirah is intermediate between haqiqah kamilah and majaz.

Commentary
This passage introduces a new section on the five factors that lead to abandoning the literal meaning (haqiqah) of words in favor of another meaning. The first factor is 'dilalat al-'urf' (indication of customary usage). The reasoning is that legal rulings are attached to words based on what the speaker intends, and if there is an established custom in how people use a word, that custom reveals the intended meaning. Therefore, the ruling is based on the customary meaning, not the strict literal definition. Two examples are given: an oath not to buy a 'head' (ra's) – customarily this refers to the heads of larger animals like cattle or sheep, not tiny heads like sparrows; similarly, an oath not to eat 'eggs' – customarily this means chicken eggs, not tiny bird eggs. In both cases, breaking the oath by eating a sparrow egg or head does not count as a violation because the customary understanding is different. The passage then draws an important principle: abandoning the literal meaning does not automatically mean resorting to metaphor (majaz); rather, there is an intermediate category called 'haqiqah qasirah' (deficient literal meaning) which is still literal but more specific than the full literal meaning. The commentary elaborates that 'haqiqah qasirah' lies between complete literal meaning (haqiqah kamilah) and metaphor. This principle prevents the hasty conclusion that any deviation from literal meaning is metaphorical.

Key Terms
العرف (al-'urf): Customary usage in speech and understanding, not customary practice in transactions.
الحقيقة القاصرة (al-haqiqah al-qasirah): A literal meaning that is deficient or restricted compared to the full literal meaning, but is still literal, not metaphorical.
المجاز (al-majaz): Figurative or metaphorical usage of a word for a meaning other than its original sense.

Custom ('Urf) as a Ground for Leaving Literal Meaning and Haqiqah Qasirah
Explains how custom overrides literal meaning and introduces haqiqah qasirah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that custom (urf) overrides literal meaning in oaths and vows, and distinguish between metaphor (majaz) and defective literal meaning (haqiqah qasirah).

Core Idea
Custom determines the intended meaning; leaving literal meaning may yield haqiqah qasirah, not metaphor.

In Hanafi usul, the literal meaning of a word can be abandoned when custom indicates a different common usage. This lesson explores the first of five grounds: dalaalat al-urf (indication of custom). Through examples of oaths and vows, we see how custom shapes legal rulings. The passage also introduces a key Hanafi concept: haqiqah qasirah (defective literal meaning), which is an intermediate between complete literal meaning and metaphor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ground: custom (urf)
Recognize that dalaalat al-urf is the first of five grounds for leaving the literal meaning. Understand that rulings depend on the speaker's intention, and custom reveals that intention.
This is a foundational usul principle.
2
Analyze the oath examples
Examine the oaths on 'head' and 'eggs.' Note how custom restricts the literal meaning: 'head' means heads of large animals, and 'eggs' means chicken eggs. This shows that custom overrides the full literal scope.
These examples illustrate the practical effect of custom.
3
Derive the principle: no automatic shift to metaphor
From the examples, the author concludes that leaving literal meaning does not force metaphor. Instead, it may create haqiqah qasirah, where the word remains literal but applies only to some individuals.
Compare with the concept of takhsis (restriction of general terms).
4
Apply to vow (nadhr) examples
Consider vows to perform Hajj, walk to the Kaaba, or strike the Hatim. Custom determines that these actions commit one to the full Hajj rituals, even though the literal meanings are broader.
Custom here is 'shar'i' or 'general' custom.
5
Define haqiqah qasirah
Identify the conditions: the word must have a clear literal basis; the restricted meaning is a subset of the original individuals; it is not a full transfer as in metaphor. This is an intermediate category unique to Hanafi usul.
The footnote elaborates on the intermediate nature.

Commentary

The author begins by stating that rulings are attached to words based on their indication of the speaker's intention. The same applies to walking to the Kaaba or striking the Hatim with a garment – these expressions have become customary for the acts of Hajj. The commentary further defines al-haqiqah al-qasirah: it is when a word is used in its real sense but only for some of its individuals, like using 'head' for only the head of a cow or sheep. This is different from general usage because the absoluteness (covering all individuals) is lost. The passage emphasizes that such usage is still haqiqah, not majaz, because the word retains its relationship to the designated set, albeit a subset. The example of a vow shows that the legal effect (obligation of Hajj) is based on the customary understanding, not the literal linguistic meaning. Thus, custom can create a new reality that is deficient relative to the original but still authoritative in law.

Abandoning Literal Meaning Due to Context of Speech: Aman Examples
Usul rule illustrated by siyar examples of aman.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the contextual clues (qara'in) that override literal meaning in Hanafi usul and apply them to determine whether aman is granted in a given scenario.

Core Idea
Context can override literal meaning; a challenge or threat is not a grant of aman.

In Hanafi usul, the literal meaning of words is the default, but it may be abandoned when the context of speech (siyaq al-kalam) indicates a different intention. This lesson explores that principle through specific examples from the law of war: the granting of safe conduct (aman) by a Muslim soldier to an enemy combatant. By examining phrases like 'Descend if you are a man' and replies to 'Aman, aman', we see how custom (urf) and implied rebuke (tawbikh) shift the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal words
First, note the exact words spoken. For example, 'Descend' (inzil) literally means to come down, and 'Aman' literally means safety.
2
Examine the additional phrases
Look for any conditions, threats, or appended phrases that may alter the meaning. In 'Descend if you are a man', the phrase 'if you are a man' adds a condition. In the response 'Aman, you will soon know what you will receive tomorrow', the latter part changes the tone.
3
Determine the customary interpretation (urf)
In the context of war, a simple 'Descend' is understood as an invitation to surrender, implying safety. Adding 'if you are a man' turns it into a challenge (tawbikh). Similarly, repeating 'Aman' indicates acceptance, but adding a threat nullifies it.
4
Classify the speech act
Decide whether the words are used in their literal sense (haqiqah) or figuratively (majaz). The additional phrases act as qara'ina (contextual indicators) that override the literal meaning, making the speech a rebuke or threat.
Hanafi usul recognizes tawbikh as a valid qarina.
5
Derive the ruling on aman
Based on the classification, conclude whether aman was granted. If the context indicates a challenge or threat, no aman is established; if the context shows acceptance or permission, aman is granted.

Commentary
This passage presents the third reason for abandoning the literal meaning of words: the context of speech (سياق الكلام). The author cites examples from al-Siyar al-Kabir by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, a foundational work of Hanafi fiqh in the Zahir al-Riwayah corpus. In the context of a Muslim warrior giving security (aman) to a non-Muslim combatant (harbi), the literal words 'descend' or 'aman' are interpreted based on the speaker's tone and additional phrases. If the Muslim simply says 'Descend' (إنزل), it is taken as granting aman because in the common understanding of warfare, such a command implies safe passage. But if he says 'Descend if you are a man' (إنزل إن كنت رجلاً), the condition 'if you are a man' changes the context to one of taunt and challenge; it is a provocation, not an offer of safety, so descending does not confer aman. Similarly, if the Harbi cries 'Aman, aman' and the Muslim simply repeats 'Aman, aman', the repetition indicates acceptance and thus aman is granted. However, if the Muslim responds with 'You will soon know what you will face tomorrow' or 'Do not be hasty until you see', the context turns it into a threat or warning, and no aman is granted. The commentary explains that these phrases, by common usage (urf), indicate a rebuke and challenge, overriding the literal meaning of the words. The speakers' intention is understood from the context, not from the isolated dictionary meaning. This lesson demonstrates how usul al-fiqh derives rules from the full communicative situation, a key principle in Hanafi istinbat.
