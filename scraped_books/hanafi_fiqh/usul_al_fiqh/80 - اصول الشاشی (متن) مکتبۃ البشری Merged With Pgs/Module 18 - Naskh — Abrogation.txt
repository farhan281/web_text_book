Weakness of Using Prohibition of Fasting on Eid Days to Prove Invalidity
Critique of using a prohibition to prove invalidity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi argument that a prohibition (nahy) does not necessarily imply invalidity (butlan) and identify the epistemological weight of a hadith when used as proof.

Core Idea
A prohibition alone does not invalidate an act; validity depends on fulfilling conditions, not on permission.

This passage examines a common argument: the hadith 'Do not fast on these days' is used by some to claim that fasting on Eid is invalid. The author demonstrates the weakness of this argument by showing that the hadith only establishes sin, not invalidity. This lesson teaches you to distinguish between the categories of prohibition and validity in Hanafi usul, and to critically assess the proof value of ahad hadith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Opponent's Claim
Recognize that the opponent uses the prohibition in the hadith to argue that the fast is invalid (batil). Understand the logical leap from 'sinful' to 'invalid'.
2
Locate the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response is that the prohibition only establishes sin, not invalidity. The text is silent on validity. Identify the specific phrase 'لا ينافي الصحة' (does not contradict validity).
3
Analyze the Dalil
Examine the hadith's chain criticism to understand that even if the hadith is accepted, its explicit content is only a prohibition. The Hanafi principle is that validity is determined by the act's conditions and pillars, not by permissibility.
4
Distinguish Between Sin and Invalidity
Learn the Hanafi distinction: an act can be valid (sahih) yet forbidden (haram) if it fulfills its essential requirements. Example: prayer in a usurped garment is valid but sinful.
5
Recognize the Usul Principle
The underlying usul principle is that a prohibition (nahy) does not necessarily entail fasād (invalidity) or butlān. The ruling on validity depends on whether the conditions of the act are met.

Bayān al-Taghyīr: The Condition of Connection for Valid Modification
Defining and ruling on connected vs. disconnected modification of speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling on connected vs. disconnected modification and apply the condition of connection to identify valid modifications in legal speech.

Core Idea
A disconnected modification of speech is invalid because it constitutes naskh — only connected changes are permitted.

This lesson explores a key principle in Hanafi uṣūl: the requirement of immediate connection (waṣl) for valid modification (bayān al-taghyīr). When a speaker adds a condition or exception after a gap, the original statement's legal effect is already established, so a later change would be abrogation (naskh) — which is impermissible for ordinary people. We will examine the definition, examples, and the scholarly consensus that supports this rule.

Guided Path
1
Define Bayān al-Taghyīr
Identify what it is: a clarification that changes the apparent meaning of a prior statement, e.g., saying 'owes' then adding 'as a trust' shifts the obligation to guardianship.
2
Identify the key condition
Recognize that the modification must be connected (mawṣūlan) to the original statement to be valid; disconnected (manfūlan) modifications are invalid.
Connection means no significant gap or interruption between the original statement and the modifying phrase.
3
Understand the reasoning
A disconnected modification would be abrogation (naskh) because the original statement's legal effect is already established. Naskh is not permissible from ordinary people.
4
Note the ikhtilāf
Some Shāfi‘īs and Ibn ‘Abbās allowed disconnected exceptions, but the Hanafīs reject this citing weak transmission and distinguish between internal intention (diyānatan) and external expression.
5
Apply the rule to examples
Apply the rule: e.g., adding 'if Allāh wills' to a disconnected oath does not nullify the oath; adding it in the same breath does.

Commentary
This passage defines Bayān al-Taghyīr (modification) and its ruling in Hanafi usul. Bayān al-Taghyīr occurs when a speaker uses words that change the apparent meaning of an earlier expression, such as saying 'owes' (implies obligation) but then adding 'as a trust' (changes to guardianship). The key ruling is that such modification is valid only if it is connected (mawsūlan) to the original statement, not if it is disconnected (manfūlan) — i.e., separated by a gap. The reason given is that a disconnected condition or exception would effectively abrogate (naskh) the original ruling, which is not permissible for ordinary people. This ruling is supported by consensus (ijmā‘) of the fuqahā’. The passage also mentions a dispute: some Shāfi‘īs and Ibn ‘Abbās allowed disconnected exception, but the Hanafīs reject this, arguing that the report is weak or that it refers to internal intention, not external expression.

Key Terms
بيان التغيير (bayān al-taghyīr): A type of bayān (clarification) where the speaker uses words that change the apparent meaning of a previous statement, such as adding a condition or exception that alters the original implication.
موصولاً (mawṣūlan): When the modifying phrase is directly connected to the original statement without interruption.
شرط (sharṭ): A condition attached to a statement that alters its legal effect.
استثناء (istithnā’): An exception that excludes part of the general meaning of a statement.
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a previous ruling by a later one; here, disconnection is considered naskh because it changes an already established effect.

Clarification through Substitution (Naskh) is Exclusive to the Lawgiver
Rule statement with proof on the exclusivity of naskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of naskh (tabdeel) and apply the Hanafi rule that naskh is exclusive to the Lawgiver, identifying cases where human actions are invalid as abrogation.

Core Idea
Naskh (abrogation) is a divine act; humans cannot abrogate rulings, only the Lawgiver can.

This lesson from Usul al-Shashi introduces bayan al-tabdeel—clarification through substitution, which is naskh. The central principle is that abrogation of a ruling is only valid from the Lawgiver (Allah and His Messenger). This has practical applications in distinguishing between valid and invalid legal statements, such as retracting an acknowledgment or attempting to make an exception that nullifies the entire original ruling.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the definition
Identify that bayan al-tabdeel is synonymous with naskh: it is clarifying the end of a previous ruling by substituting it with another.
The text opens with 'فصل في بيان التبديل' and equates it to naskh.
2
Identify the authority
Understand that naskh is only permissible from the Lawgiver (sahib al-shar'), not from humans (al-ibad). This is a foundational rule in Hanafi usul.
The text explicitly states: 'يجوز ذلك من صاحب الشرع، ولا يجوز ذلك من العباد'.
3
Apply the principle to human actions
Recognize that humans cannot retract from acknowledgments (iqrar), divorce (talaq), or emancipation (itaq) because such retraction would be an act of naskh, which is exclusively divine.
The text gives these examples to illustrate the principle.
4
Analyze the proof (dalil)
The author cites Quran 16:101 as evidence that Allah substitutes verses, establishing that naskh is a divine act. Understand the wajh al-dalalah: the verse uses 'بدلنا' (We substitute), proving that only Allah performs substitution of rulings.
The verse is quoted partially: 'وَإِذَا بَدَّلْنَا آيَةً مَكَانَ آيَةٍ'.
5
Distinguish valid from invalid exceptions
Apply the rule to 'istithna al-kull an al-kull' (exception of the whole from the whole). Such an exception is invalid because it amounts to abrogation of the original ruling, which humans cannot do.
Example: saying 'I owe you one hundred except one hundred' is invalid.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi discusses bayan al-tabdeel, which is clarification through substitution, i.e., naskh (abrogation). The author establishes a fundamental rule: abrogation is only permissible from the Lawgiver (Allah or His Messenger), not from human beings. This is because naskh involves changing a divine ruling, which is not within human authority. The text then applies this principle to various fiqh issues. First, 'istithna' al-kull 'an al-kull' (exception of the whole from the whole) is invalid because it would effectively be abrogation of the original ruling. For example, if someone says 'I owe you one hundred except one hundred,' this is not a valid exception; it is an attempt to negate the debt entirely, which is not allowed. Similarly, a person cannot retract from an acknowledgment (iqrar) he made, nor from a divorce or emancipation he pronounced, because that would be like abrogating his own statement, which is not permissible for a human. The passage also includes extended commentary on the preceding section about 'atf, explaining the reasoning behind the distinction between measured/weighed items and others. The key point for this lesson is that naskh is a form of bayan that clarifies the duration of a ruling, and it is exclusive to the Lawgiver. The example of wine prohibition illustrates how a later ruling abrogates an earlier one, and this is considered a clarification from the Lawgiver that the earlier ruling was only temporary.

Learning Objective

Only the Lawgiver can abrogate; a servant cannot retract his own legal statements.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi defines naskh (abrogation) as a form of clarification (bayan) that replaces a previous ruling with a new one. It emphasizes that this power belongs exclusively to Allah and His Messenger. The author then applies this principle to show that a servant's attempt to retract an acknowledgment, divorce, or emancipation is invalid because it would constitute unauthorized abrogation.

It first establishes that abrogation is exclusively the right of the Lawgiver (Allah and His Messenger); servants cannot abrogate divine rulings. The commentary expands on a related principle about interpreting ambiguous numbers when connected by 'and', explaining the Hanafi rule that co-ordinated items are treated as a single unit if they fall in the category of measured/weighed goods due to common practice. However, this rule does not extend to garments because they are not typically owed in the dhimmah. The main focus returns to abrogation: it is defined as the termination of a legal ruling that was assumed to continue indefinitely, proven by the Quranic example of wine prohibition superseding earlier permission. The passage then presents a fiqh case: if someone says 'So-and-so owes me one thousand' and then adds 'and they are counterfeit' or 'from the price of a slave-girl I did not receive', Abu Hanifa considers this a form of abrogation (tabdil) of the original acknowledgment, and thus invalid whether the statement is connected or disconnected. Intra-Hanafi Dispute: Taghyir vs Tabdil in Acknowledging a Debt with a Defect
Application of tabdil/taghyir to a debt acknowledgment with a defect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the intra-Hanafi dispute over whether specifying a defect (e.g., 'counterfeit') after acknowledging a debt is a valid taghyir or an invalid tabdil, and apply the ruling to similar cases.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa: specifying a defect is an invalid retraction (naskh); Sahibayn: it is a valid clarification (taghyir) when connected.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi applies the concept of tabdil to a practical scenario: a person acknowledges a debt of one thousand and then says 'and they are counterfeit'. According to Abu Hanifa, this is an invalid abrogation (tabdil) because a contract implies freedom from defect. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) consider it a valid alteration (taghyir) when said in the same breath. Understanding this ikhtilaf clarifies how the principle of naskh operates in everyday transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario
The scenario: a person says 'I owe you one thousand' and then adds 'and they are counterfeit' (or 'from the price of a slave-girl I did not receive'). Identify the two statements.
The additional statement qualifies the original acknowledgment.
2
State Abu Hanifa's view
Abu Hanifa considers it tabdil (naskh), so the second statement invalidates the first, whether said connectedly or disconnectedly. The overall acknowledgment is nullified.
He views the contract as requiring good quality; counterfeitness is a defect, so it is a retraction.
3
State the Sahibayn's view
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider it taghyir (alteration), valid only when the second statement is connected (mawsulan). The acknowledgment stands as one thousand counterfeit dirhams.
They see the absolute number as prima facie good quality, but specifying counters clarifies the actual owed item.
4
Analyze the reasoning
Abu Hanifa: 'Aqd al-mu'awadah (contract of exchange) implies safety from defect; counterfeitness is a defect, so specifying it is a retraction (ruju'), which is invalid. Sahibayn: The absolute number does not necessarily imply good quality; it is only the default. Specifying counters is a permissible taqyid (qualification) when connected.
The difference hinges on whether the default is considered a binding part of the acknowledgment.
5
Determine the practical consequence
If Abu Hanifa's view applies, the entire acknowledgment is void. If the Sahibayn's view applies, the debtor owes one thousand counterfeit dirhams (i.e., the debt is for the specific items).
The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school typically follows the Sahibayn in this kind of taghyir, but the passage presents both views without explicit tarjih.

Chapter on Explanation of Alteration. Abu Yusuf: its place is a clarification in one hundred and a sheep, and one hundred and a garment on this principle. Chapter on Explanation of Alteration: As for the explanation of alteration: it is abrogation. That is permissible from the Lawgiver, and it is not permissible from the servants. i.e., alteration of the previous ruling and its abrogation. Abrogation of the ruling. And on this basis: invalidated is the exemption of the whole from the whole because it is abrogation of the ruling, and retraction from confession, divorce, and emancipation is not permissible because it is abrogation, and the servant does not have that. = 'Bayan etc.' because the speaker mentioned two indeterminate numbers and followed them with an interpretation from garments in the first, dirhams in the second, and slaves in the third, so they revert to them due to their equal need for interpretation. 'Like measured and weighed etc.' means that the practice of omitting the explanation of the co-ordinated item and being satisfied with mentioning the explanation in the co-ordinating item arose only due to the necessity of frequent use of the number, and that occurs in what is established as an obligation in the dhimmah in general transactions, and it is in measured and weighed items, unlike the garment, for it is not established in the dhimmah as a loan or sale except in salam specifically, so its obligation in the dhimmah is not broken, so the necessity is not realized, so the original remains, as in 'al-Ma'din'. On this principle: the co-ordinated and the co-ordinating item are like one thing because the conjunctive waw is for conjunction, as in his statement 'one hundred dirhams'. And for the two of them (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) there is a distinction between measured/weighed and others: the practice of omitting the interpreter in the co-ordinated item and being satisfied with mentioning the interpretation in the co-ordinating item arose only in measured and weighed items due to the necessity of frequent speech regarding what is frequently used, and its frequency is in what is established as a debt in the dhimmah in general transactions, and it is the estimated (items), unlike garments because they are not established in the dhimmah as a loan or sale except in salam specifically, so for this reason satisfaction is not found in them, as in 'al-Ma'din' and the commentaries of 'al-Bazdawi'. And as for the explanation of alteration: i.e., alteration of the previous ruling and its abrogation, and it is placing something in the place of something else. Allah Most High said: 'And when We substitute an ayah in place of an ayah...' And it is abrogation: it is the explanation of the termination of an absolute legal ruling that was established in our minds as continuing, in a delayed manner. Example: Allah Most High permitted wine in the beginning of Islam, so we assumed that the permission would remain until the Day of Judgment. Then when prohibition came after that, this was a clarification from the Lawgiver that the ruling of permission was until that time. 'And retraction is not permissible' because it is established with the usuliyyun that abrogation is not permissible from the servant; the author derived a branch from it and said: on this basis, the exemption of the whole from the whole is invalid because it is abrogation of the ruling, and the servant is not allowed to retract from a confession. ... And if someone says: 'So-and-so owes me...' the alteration is valid according to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) when connected, and according to Abu Hanifa it is explanation of alteration, so it is not valid, even if a loan or the price of goods, and says: 'and they are counterfeit' — that is an explanation. His statement: 'and they are counterfeit' — connected. And if someone says: 'So-and-so owes me one thousand from the price of a slave-girl whom he sold to me and I did not take possession of her,' and the slave-girl has no effect, that is explanation of alteration according to Abu Hanifa in its place because acknowledgment of the obligation of the price is an acknowledgment of possession upon destruction of the sold item, for if it perished before possession, the sale is rescinded and the price is not binding. 'According to them etc.' because one thousand is absolute without a condition of quality, but the apparent meaning in a loan, debt, price, and their likes is good quality, so it becomes abrogation and alteration, so this apparent meaning is not [the case]. According to Abu Hanifa: it is abrogation and not valid when connected because a contract of exchange requires the attribute of being free from defect and counterfeitness is a defect, so it is a retraction, and retraction is not considered whether connected or disconnected. So it is not valid even if connected because the contract of exchange necessitates the obligation of property with the attribute of being free from defect, and counterfeitness is a defect, so it is a retraction, and retraction is not effective whether connected or disconnected, and it becomes like claiming a term in a debt or claiming an option in a sale, as in 'al-Ma'din'. Acknowledgment of possession: so his statement 'I did not take possession of her' is retraction after acknowledgment of possession or obligation of the price, and retraction is not valid whether connected or disconnected.

Commentary
This passage defines 'bayan al-tabdil' (explanation of alteration), which is abrogation (naskh). It first establishes that abrogation is exclusively the right of the Lawgiver (Allah and His Messenger); servants cannot abrogate divine rulings. The text then derives a branch: if someone makes a general exception of the whole from the whole (e.g., 'I owe you everything except everything'), this is considered an abrogation of the original acknowledgment and is invalid because it is a retraction, which is not permitted for servants. The commentary expands on a related principle about interpreting ambiguous numbers when connected by 'and', explaining the Hanafi rule that co-ordinated items are treated as a single unit if they fall in the category of measured/weighed goods due to common practice. However, this rule does not extend to garments because they are not typically owed in the dhimmah. The main focus returns to abrogation: it is defined as the termination of a legal ruling that was assumed to continue indefinitely, proven by the Quranic example of wine prohibition superseding earlier permission. The passage then presents a fiqh case: if someone says 'So-and-so owes me one thousand' and then adds 'and they are counterfeit' or 'from the price of a slave-girl I did not receive', Abu Hanifa considers this a form of abrogation (tabdil) of the original acknowledgment, and thus invalid whether the statement is connected or disconnected. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider it a mere alteration (taghyir) valid when connected. The commentary explains the reasoning: Abu Hanifa sees the intrinsic requirement of a sale contract as freedom from defect, so claiming a defect is a retraction; the two companions see the absolute number as implying good quality only by default, so specifying counterfeit is not a retraction but a clarification.

Key Terms
التغيير (al-taghyir): Modification of a statement without nullifying the original; permissible when connected
الرجوع (al-ruju'): Retraction of a binding statement; considered invalid as it amounts to naskh

Qiyas Cannot Change a Revealed Text: The Rule of Naskh by Qiyas
Usul rule prohibiting qiyas that changes a revealed text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when qiyas constitutes naskh of a revealed text and apply the Hanafi rule that such qiyas is invalid by consensus.

Core Idea
Qiyas cannot abrogate a nass—changing a text's ruling by analogy is naskh, and naskh by qiyas is invalid by consensus.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi establishes a critical boundary: analogy (qiyas) must serve the revealed texts, not override them. You will learn why requiring intention for wudu by analogy on tayammum, or requiring purity for tawaf based on the hadith 'tawaf is prayer', are both invalid—they would change an absolute text into a restricted one, which is naskh (abrogation). The Hanafi school holds that naskh can only come from revelation or consensus, never from human reasoning. This lesson sharpens your ability to distinguish legitimate interpretation from prohibited alteration.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two example analogies
The passage gives two cases: deriving intention (niyyah) for wudu by qiyas on tayammum, and requiring purity and covering private parts for tawaf by qiyas on prayer (based on the hadith 'tawaf is prayer'). Identify which texts each analogy would change.
Both analogies aim to restrict an absolute (mutlaq) command.
2
Understand why they constitute naskh
Changing a mutlaq command to muqayyad (restricted) is considered naskh of the original ruling. The passage argues that such qiyas alters the text's ruling, not merely interprets it.
In Hanafi usul, taqyid (restriction) of a mutlaq is a form of naskh if it changes the ruling.
3
Trace the consensus (ijma) argument
The author states that naskh of a nass by qiyas is invalid by consensus (ijma). This is the core proof. The ijma establishes that qiyas cannot override or alter revealed texts.
Ikhtilaf exists on whether restriction is naskh, but the principle that qiyas cannot abrogate is agreed upon.
4
Apply the principle to the tawaf hadith
The hadith 'tawaf is prayer' equates tawaf and prayer in some rulings, but the analogical extension to require all conditions of prayer would change the absolute Qur'anic command 'let them circumambulate the ancient house'. This is not permissible because it alters the nass.
The hadith itself is not naskh; it's the analogical inference that causes the change.
5
Distinguish from valid qiyas
Not all qiyas is invalid; only qiyas that changes a nass ruling. Valid qiyas applies a ruling to a new case without altering the original text—e.g., qiyas of narcotics on wine for prohibition.
The rule prevents human reasoning from superseding revelation.

فصل في شروط صحة القياس. البحث الرابع في القياس. الوضوء بالقياس على التيمم، فإن هذا يوجب تغيير آية الوضوء من الإطلاق إلى التقييد. وكذلك إذا قلنا: الطواف بالبيت صلاة بالخبر، فيشترط له الطهارة، وستر العورة كالصلوة، كان هذا قياساً يوجب تغيير نصّ الطواف من الإطلاق إلى القيد. وقد مر البحث عنها في فصل المقيد والمطلق، وحاصله: أن إطلاق آية الوضوء يقتضي حصول الوضوء من النية، واشتراطها بعدمه، وهو حكم من أحكامه؛ فإنه لا يجوز؛ لأنه نسخ، ونسخ النص لا يجوز بالقياس إجماعاً كذا في بعض الشروح. بالخبر: أي الحديث قال رسول الله صلى الله عليه وسلم "الطواف بالبيت صلاة إلا أن الله أحل فيه المنطق، فمن نطق فيه فلا ينطق إلا بخير". أخرجه الترمذي في الحج برقم: ٩٦٠، والدارمي في المناسك برقم: ۱٧٧٦، واللفظ له. الشافي: ص ٢٥٩. يوجب تغيير نص إلخ: لأن قوله تعالى: وليص بالبيت العتيق الآية مطلق في الطواف، وهو اسم الدوران حول البيت، فاشتراط الطهارة وستر العورة يوجب تغيير النص من الإطلاق إلى التقييد، وأنه لا يجوز أصلاً كذا في بعض الشروح. إلى القيد: ومسألة الطواف والنية قد مر البحث عنهما في فصل المطلق والمقيد من هذا الكتاب.

Commentary
This passage is a usul rule: qiyas cannot change a revealed text. The author gives two examples. First: if one tries to derive the obligation of intention (niyyah) for wudu by analogy on tayammum (which requires niyyah), this would restrict the absolute command of wudu (which does not mention niyyah) — this is invalid because it abrogates the text. Second: using the hadith 'tawaf is prayer' to require purity and covering private parts (as in prayer) would restrict the absolute permission of tawaf (which only mentions circumambulation) — also invalid. The principle: changing a nass (whether from absolute to restricted or otherwise) via qiyas is naskh (abrogation), and naskh by qiyas is prohibited by consensus (ijma). The author then cites the hadith and explains the verse.

Key Terms
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a legal ruling of the text by a later text or by consensus; here, qiyas cannot cause naskh.
مطلق (mutlaq): An absolute term in a text that does not have any qualification; it remains general until restricted by a separate evidence.
مقيد (muqayyad): A restricted term that qualifies the absolute, limiting its scope.
