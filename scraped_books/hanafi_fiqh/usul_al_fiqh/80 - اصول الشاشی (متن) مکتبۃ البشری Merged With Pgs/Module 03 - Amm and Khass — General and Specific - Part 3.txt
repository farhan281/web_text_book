Types of Bayan and the Ta'liq Dispute
Defining bayan al-taqrir, al-tafsir, al-taghyir and the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute on ta'liq

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three types of bayan (taqrir, tafsir, taghyir) and apply the Hanafi position on ta'liq.

Core Idea
Bayan al-taghyir alters apparent meaning; Hanafis hold ta'liq is cause only upon condition.

This lesson introduces the three categories of bayan (clarification) in usul al-fiqh: confirmatory, explanatory, and altering. The most debated is bayan al-taghyir, especially the subcategory of ta'liq (suspension of a ruling on a condition). You will learn the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on when a conditional statement becomes a legal cause, and why this matters.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of bayan
Read the passage and distinguish bayan al-taqrir, bayan al-tafsir, and bayan al-taghyir based on their definitions and examples.
Each type acts differently: taqrir confirms, tafsir explains, taghyir alters.
2
Analyze the examples for each type
For each type, study the given example (e.g., 'عندي' + 'وديعة' for taqrir; 'شيء' + 'ثوب' for tafsir; ta'liq for taghyir) and understand how the clarification works.
Focus on the change from the original apparent meaning.
3
Understand the dispute on ta'liq
Compare the Hanafi and Shafi'i views on when a conditional statement becomes a cause. Hanafis: cause only at condition existence; Shafi'is: cause at utterance but condition prevents effect.
The key difference is the cause's timing.
4
Apply the Hanafi position to a scenario
Consider a conditional divorce or manumission and determine the ruling under Hanafi usul: no legal effect until the condition is met.
The condition defines when the cause operates.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi defines three types of bayan (clarification) in usul al-fiqh: bayan al-taqrir (confirmatory), bayan al-tafsir (explanatory), and bayan al-taghyir (altering). Bayan al-taqrir occurs when the speaker confirms the apparent meaning of his words, as in the example of saying 'trust' after saying 'with me'. Bayan al-tafsir clarifies a vague or ambiguous term, such as interpreting 'something' as a garment. The passage notes that both these types are valid whether the clarification is connected (muttasil) or disconnected (munfasil). Evidence for disconnection is the Qur'anic verse (75:19) which implies that Allah's clarification came after the revelation. Bayan al-taghyir alters the apparent meaning by directing a word to a less common interpretation, like using a metaphor or specifying a general term. The passage then gives two key examples of taghyir: ta'liq (suspension of a ruling on a condition) and istithna' (exception). A major dispute arises between the Hanafis and Shafi'is over ta'liq: the Hanafis (Abu Hanifa and his followers) hold that a conditional statement becomes a cause only when the condition occurs, so no ruling exists before that; the Shafi'is hold that the conditional statement is a cause immediately but the condition acts as a preventer (mani'), delaying the effect. This difference impacts the timing of legal effects like divorce or manumission. The passage presents the Hanafi view as the correct one, supported by the principle that the cause must reach its locus (mahall) to be effective.

Key Terms
بيان التقرير (bayan al-taqrir): Confirmatory clarification: confirming the apparent meaning of a statement by a subsequent expression.
بيان التفسير (bayan al-tafsir): Explanatory clarification: revealing the intended meaning of an ambiguous or vague term.
بيان التغيير (bayan al-taghyir): Altering clarification: changing the apparent meaning to a remote possibility, such as metaphor or specification.
التعليق (al-ta'liq): Suspension: linking a legal statement to a future condition, e.g., 'You are free if you enter the house.'
الاستثناء (al-istithna'): Exception: excluding part of a statement, e.g., 'I owe you a thousand except a hundred.'

Hanafi and Shafi'i Views on Exception (Istithna)
Compares Hanafi and Shafi'i interpretations of legal exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi and Shafi'i interpretations of legal exception (istithna) and apply them to derive rulings in debt statements.

Core Idea
Exception in a legal statement redefines the obligation: Hanafis treat it as speech about the remainder only.

Exception (istithna) is a fundamental usul tool that shapes how we interpret legal statements. The Hanafi and Shafi'i schools diverge on whether the exception negates the initial statement or merely blocks its application. This lesson unpacks that disagreement and its practical impact on obligations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two interpretations
State the Hanafi position: exception makes the speech about the remainder only, as if the excepted part was never spoken. State the Shafi'i position: the initial statement creates a cause for the whole, but the exception prevents that cause from applying to the excepted part.
The Hanafi view is based on linguistic equivalence; the Shafi'i view draws an analogy to conditional statements.
2
Analyze the underlying reasoning
Examine the Hanafi dalil: logical equivalence—'one thousand except one hundred' equals 'nine hundred'. Contrast with the Shafi'i dalil: the initial speech is a cause (illah) for the entire thousand, but the exception blocks it, like a condition preventing a suspended contract.
The difference stems from whether the exception is seen as part of the speech meaning or as an external preventer.
3
Apply the difference to a concrete example
Consider the statement: 'Zaid owes me one thousand except one hundred'. Under Hanafi usul, the obligation is nine hundred. Under Shafi'i usul, the obligation is a thousand with the hundred blocked by the exception.
This example is directly from the source text and illustrates the thamarah al-khilaf.
4
Extract the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: in a court, Hanafis would rule that nine hundred is owed immediately; Shafi'is would consider the thousand as the basis but the exception removes the hundred, possibly affecting how the debt is treated in case of ambiguity.
The disagreement affects how obligations are understood in contracts and oaths.
5
Connect to broader usul principles
Relate this dispute to the concepts of amm/khass and mafhum al-mukhalafah. The Hanafi view aligns with the idea that an exception is a form of takhsis (specification), while the Shafi'i view treats it as a preventer akin to a condition.
This connection shows how a single linguistic tool can be analyzed differently based on underlying usul principles.

Commentary
This passage discusses the usul rule of exception (istithna) and the differing interpretations between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. The Hanafi position is that an exception effectively restates the sentence to only include the remainder. For example, if someone says 'I owe you one thousand except one hundred,' the Hanafis consider it as if he said 'I owe you nine hundred.' The excepted part is not considered part of the obligation at all. The Shafi'i view is that the initial part ('one thousand') creates a cause (illah) for the entire thousand, but the exception ('except one hundred') prevents the cause from applying to the hundred, like a condition prevents a suspended contract. The practical difference is about how the debt is treated: Hanafis see it as immediately nine hundred, while Shafi'is see it as a conditional thousand with the exception blocking the hundred. The passage also links this to the earlier discussion of description, noting that the disagreement stems from the same root: the concept of mafhum al-mukhalafah. The example of 'one thousand except one hundred' is used to illustrate the divergent interpretations.

Hanafi vs Shafi'i on the Legal Effect of Exception (Istithna')
Explaining the Hanafi view that exception negates the initial generality entirely

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi and Shafi'i views on istithna' and apply each to a textual example, identifying the practical difference.

Core Idea
Exception cancels the initial generality in Hanafi; it merely blocks application in Shafi'i.

In this lesson, we examine a central usul dispute: how does an exception (istithna') interact with a general statement? The Hanafi school argues that the exception redefines the speaker's intent — the initial generality is as if never uttered for the excepted part. The Shafi'i school holds that the initial statement establishes a cause (illa) for the whole, and the exception only prevents its application to the excepted part. This difference has real fiqh consequences, as illustrated by the hadith on selling food for food.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi Position
The Hanafi view: an exception (istithna') redefines the speech act. The speaker is only speaking about what remains after the exception. Example: 'I owe ten except three' means 'I owe seven'.
This is based on linguistic custom and the principle that the Lawgiver does not command the impossible.
2
State the Shafi'i Position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the initial statement serves as a cause (illa) for the entire ruling, but the exception acts like a missing condition (ta'liq) that prevents the cause from operating on the excepted part.
The general remains operative for the non-excepted remainder.
3
Apply to the Food Hadith
Consider the hadith: 'Do not sell food for food except equal for equal.' According to Shafi'i, the general prohibition applies to all food sales, and the exception only allows equal exchanges; thus selling a handful for two handfuls is prohibited. According to Hanafi, the prohibition only applies to sales where equality can be measured; since a handful is not measurable, the sale is not covered.
The Hanafi rejects the general cause because it would lead to prohibiting the incapable.
4
Analyze the Proofs
The Hanafi relies on rational argument (impossibility), custom (food is measured), and a corroborating hadith. The Shafi'i relies on the apparent generality of the initial imperative.
Both use the same hadith but interpret the interaction of generality and exception differently.
5
Identify the Practical Difference (Thamarah)
In practice, the Hanafi school permits selling a small handful of dates for two handfuls (unequal but small), while the Shafi'i prohibits it. This showcases how usul principles directly affect fiqh rulings.
The dispute hinges on whether the general statement is a binding cause or is negated by the exception.

Commentary
This is an expanded commentary on the principle of modification (taghyeer) in legal texts, specifically focusing on exception (istithna). The passage first illustrates modification by restricting a general description: the Quranic text regarding marriage to slave women is restricted to believing slave women, so the ruling of permissibility does not apply when the description of faith is absent. Then it moves to exception as a form of clarification. The core dispute: The Hanafi school holds that an exception redefines the entire speech act—the speaker is effectively only talking about what remains after the exception. For example, 'I owe ten except three' means 'I owe seven' as if the ten was never said. In contrast, Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the initial statement establishes a cause (illa) for the whole, and the exception acts as a preventer (mani') similar to the absence of a condition in a suspended ruling. This difference is then applied to a real-world case: the hadith 'Do not sell food for food except equal for equal.' According to the Shafi'i view, the prohibition is general for all food sales, and the exception only allows equal exchanges; therefore, selling a handful of dates for two handfuls (unequal but small amounts) is prohibited because the general prohibition still applies beyond the exception. The Hanafi view, however, reasons that the prohibition only applies to sales where the servant can feasibly ensure equality, which is only possible when the amount is measurable by standard measures (kayl). Since a handful is not measurable, such a transaction is not covered by the prohibition and is permissible. This analysis draws on rational and textual proofs: the capacity of the servant (to avoid commanding the impossible), the agreed standard of measurement, and a corroborating hadith 'measure for measure.' The Hanafi position avoids prohibiting what cannot be avoided, reflecting the principle that the Lawgiver does not command the impossible.

Key Terms
استثناء (istithna'): A linguistic exception that excludes part of what is included in the initial general statement; according to Hanafis, it means the speaker only intended the remaining part from the start.
علة (illa): The effective cause or ratio legis that makes the ruling applicable; in this context, the initial statement is claimed to be an illa for the whole obligation.
تعليق (ta'liq): A suspended conditional ruling where the cause exists but the ruling is prevented by the absence of a condition, analogous to how Shafi'i views exception.

Restricting General Prohibitions: Hanafi Analysis of the Food Exchange Hadith
Textual analysis and madhhab dispute on hadith generality

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of takhsis by impossibility (nahy al-'ajiz) to a general textual prohibition, and derive the ruling for selling small food quantities.

Core Idea
A general prohibition is restricted to what the servant can perform; forbidding the incapable is repugnant.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi usul argument that restricts the scope of a general textual prohibition. The hadith 'لا تبيعوا الطعام بالطعام إلا سواء بسواء' appears to forbid all unequal exchanges of food. However, the Hanafis argue that the prohibition is limited to cases where equality can be objectively determined, using the rational principle that Allah does not command the impossible. Understanding this reasoning is key to mastering the Hanafi approach to reconciling textual generality with practical feasibility.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source text
Locate the hadith in the passage: 'لا تبيعوا الطعام بالطعام إلا سواء بسواء'. This is the starting point of analysis.
The hadith is a general command/prohibition shape: 'Do not sell... except equal for equal'.
2
Distinguish the two readings
Read how al-Shafi'i interprets the text: the exception only removes the equal case, leaving the remainder under the general prohibition. Then read the Hanafi view: the prohibition is inherently tied to the ability to determine equality.
Shafi'i: amm + istithna' leaves the rest under the general rule. Hanafi: the prohibition is restricted to cases where the servant can determine equality (kayl).
3
Locate the rational argument
Find the phrase 'نهي العاجز' (prohibition of the incapable). The Hanafis argue that if the prohibition included small quantities, it would require the servant to do something impossible (establish equality without a standard measure), which is repugnant (qabih).
This is a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) based on reason, overriding the apparent generality.
4
Examine the evidence for the standardizer (kayl)
Note the multiple proofs: consensus (ijma), the hadith 'كيلاً بكيل', custom (urf), and legal ruling on compensation (value vs. like). These establish that the standard for equality is measurement (kayl).
The standardizer (al-musawwi) is kayl; what cannot be measured falls outside the scope.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf
Apply the difference: for the Shafi'i, selling a handful of wheat for two handfuls is prohibited (unequal). For the Hanafi, it is permissible because a handful is not measurable. This is the practical consequence.
This step shows how usul principles directly impact fiqh rulings.

Understanding Bayān al-Taghyīr: Altering the Initial Import
Defining taghyīr with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Bayān al-Taghyīr and identify its examples in legal statements.

Core Idea
Connected alteration is valid; disconnected alteration is invalid abrogation.

Have you ever heard someone say 'I owe you a thousand' and then add 'as a deposit'? This simple addition can completely change the legal meaning. In this lesson, we explore the concept of Bayān al-Taghyīr—statements that modify the initial import of a declaration. Understanding when such modifications are accepted is crucial in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial statement and its legal import
Look at the first part of the declaration (e.g., 'alā alf' – upon me one thousand) and determine the apparent legal effect (here, obligation of debt).
The word 'alā' in acknowledgments indicates obligation by default.
2
Recognize the altering statement
Identify the subsequent words that modify the initial meaning (e.g., 'wadī‘a' changes obligation to safekeeping).
Alteration can be by specifying quality, condition, or clarification.
3
Determine if the alteration is connected or disconnected
Check if the altering statement is immediate (mawsūlan) or separated (mafsūlan). If connected, it is valid; if disconnected, it is invalid.
Connection means no intervening pause; the entire utterance is one speech act.

Bayan al-'Atf: Clarifying Ambiguous Numbers via Conjunction
Rule explanation with examples and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a conjunctive phrase ('atf) clarifies an ambiguous number only when the conjoined item is makīl or mawzūn and identify counterexamples.

Core Idea
A number plus 'and a dirham' means all dirhams—only if the item is debt-eligible and measurable.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, ambiguous numbers (mujmal) require clarification (bayān). One subtle mechanism is conjunction ('atf): when an ambiguous number like 'one hundred' is followed by 'and a dirham,' the conjunction itself serves as the clarification, specifying that the entire hundred refers to dirhams. This lesson unpacks the conditions and limits of this rule from Uṣūl al-Shāshī.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous number
Start by spotting a number like 'one hundred' that stands alone without a specific unit. This number is mujmal—it could mean dirhams, garments, wheat, etc.
In the example 'one hundred and a dirham,' the number is the first term.
2
Recognize the conjoined item
The conjunction 'and' (wāw) attaches a specific item, e.g., 'a dirham.' Check the nature of this item: is it makīl (measured by volume) or mawzūn (weighed)?
Only items that can be debts in the dhimma (like grains, metals) qualify.
3
Apply the rule of bayān
If the conjoined item is makīl/mawzūn, the conjunction clarifies that the ambiguous number refers to items of that same genus. The number is now mubayyan (specified).
This is the Hanafi position: the 'atf acts as a substitute for an omitted qualifier.
4
Test the exception
If the conjoined item is not of the makīl/mawzūn type (e.g., 'one hundred and a sheep'), the conjunction does NOT clarify. The number remains ambiguous.
A sheep is not a standard debt item; its genus differs.
5
Summarize the condition
The rule is specific: only makīl/mawzūn items trigger clarification. The rationale is customary practice (ʿurf) and suitability as a debt.
This distinguishes between 'one hundred and three dirhams' (clarified) and 'one hundred of a garment' (not clarified).

Distinguishing Bayan al-Taghayyur from Bayan al-Tabdil: The Case of Defective Coins
Disputing whether specifying defect in acknowledgment is a valid explanation or an invalid retraction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between bayan al-taghayyur and bayan al-tabdil in the Hanafi usul framework and apply the distinction to the case of acknowledging defective coins in a loan or sale.

Core Idea
A connected explanation that contradicts the essential requirement of a contract is a retraction (tabdil), not a valid clarification (taghyyur).

This passage from Usul al-Shashi presents a key usul distinction: when does a qualifying statement count as a valid explanation (bayan al-taghayyur) and when is it an invalid substitution (bayan al-tabdil)? The dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn over acknowledging defective coins illustrates how the nature of the underlying obligation determines the ruling. By analyzing their arguments, you will learn to apply this distinction in various transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial acknowledgment and its default implication
Recognize that when someone says 'I owe so-and-so a thousand,' the default understanding is that the coins are of good quality, especially in loans, debts, and sales.
The default (al-asl) in commutative contracts is freedom from defect.
2
Classify the explanatory statement as taghyyur or tabdil according to each imam
Sahibayn view the added phrase 'and they are defective' as clarifying an ambiguity (taghyyur), so it is valid when connected. Abu Hanifa views it as changing the obligation (tabdil), hence invalid.
The dispute hinges on whether the explanation is a clarification or a retraction.
3
Trace the underlying principle: commutative contracts require soundness
Abu Hanifa argues that a commutative contract (e.g., loan, sale) necessitates property free from defects; specifying defectiveness contradicts this and is a retraction.
This principle is a usul rule derived from the nature of exchange.
4
Identify exceptions: cases where there is no such presumption
In usurpation or deposit, there is no requirement of good quality, so specifying defect is valid whether connected or disconnected.
The usul rule is context-dependent.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
In a loan, Sahibayn accept the specification of defect (connected) as a valid explanation; Abu Hanifa rejects it entirely. This affects whether the debtor must pay good or defective coins.
The difference leads to different legal outcomes in fatwa.

Two Objections on Qiyas: The Limit and the Intention in Fasting
Answering two objections regarding the definition of qiyas and the need for intention in fasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that specification by the Sharia eliminates the need for the servant's specific intention, and evaluate the Shafi'i qiyas on intention in Ramadan fast.

Core Idea
When the Sharia designates an act, the servant's specific intention is not required—specification is already present.

This passage from Ash-Shashi's Usul addresses two objections against qiyas. The first concerns the definition of the limit (hadd) and whether it falls under the ruling of the limited. The second and more significant objection is the Shafi'i argument that Ramadan fasting requires specific intention (ta'yin) just like make-up fasting. The Hanafi response reveals a key usul principle: specification by the Sharia itself removes the need for the servant's specification. We will analyze the reasoning and the rebuttal.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two objections
The passage presents two separate objections directed at qiyas: (1) the limit (elbow) does not enter under the ruling of the limited, so qiyas on washing is flawed; (2) Ramadan fast requires specific intention like make-up fast, so qiyas should apply.
Both objections are from opponents of the Hanafi school, likely Shafi'i.
2
Analyze the first objection: the limit
The opponent claims that the elbow, as a limit of the omitted part (the arm not washed), cannot be included in the ruling of washing. The Hanafi responds that the limit belongs to the omitted part, not the washed part, so it is not covered by the washing ruling.
Key terms: 'hadd' (limit), 'mahdud' (limited). The limit is excluded from the limited.
3
Analyze the second objection: intention in fasting
The Shafi'i argues by qiyas: Ramadan fast is an obligatory fast like make-up fasting, so both require specific intention (ta'yin) from the servant. The Hanafi responds by denying the similarity: the root (make-up) lacks Sharia specification, while the branch (Ramadan) has it via the month itself.
This is a classic case of 'mumāna'a' (objection to the parity of the branch).
4
Identify the Hanafi dalil
The Hanafi proof is the hadith: 'When Sha'ban ends, then no fasting except for Ramadan.' This shows that the Sharia itself has designated Ramadan, making the servant's specific intention unnecessary.
Dalil type: Sunnah ahad (but considered sufficient for this principle).
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: In the Hanafi school, a person who intends to fast in Ramadan without explicitly saying 'Ramadan' has a valid fast. In the Shafi'i school, the fast would be invalid because specific intention is required.
This illustrates the importance of usul in deriving practical rulings.

Metaphorical Use of 'Sabab' (Cause) for Oaths and Conditional Clauses
Definition and illustration of metaphorical sabab

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why an oath is called a 'sabab' for expiation only metaphorically in Hanafi usul, and distinguish between true cause (haqiqah) and metaphorical cause (majaz).

Core Idea
A true cause does not contradict its effect; an oath contradicts expiation, so it is only metaphorically called a cause.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the term 'sabab' (cause) has a precise definition: it must lead to its effect without contradiction. However, certain things like oaths and conditional clauses are also called 'sabab' in common usage. This lesson unpacks the logical reasoning behind why this is only metaphorical, using the example of an oath (yamin) and its connection to expiation (kaffara). Understanding this distinction prevents confusion when deriving legal rulings from texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The passage states that non-causes are sometimes called 'sabab' metaphorically. Locate this claim in the text and note the examples given: oath for expiation and conditional clauses for divorce or manumission.
The claim is that these are not true causes in reality.
2
Examine the oath example
An oath is called a 'sabab' for expiation, but the passage argues it is not a true cause because the oath contradicts the obligation of expiation. Expiation becomes obligatory only upon breaking the oath (hanath), while the oath is intended to prevent breaking. Therefore, the oath is a preventer (mani') of its supposed effect.
True cause does not contradict its effect.
3
Understand the logical contradiction
The sabab (cause) must not contradict the existence of the musabbab (effect). Here, the oath is for obedience (birr), which necessitates avoiding breaking; breaking is required for expiation. Thus the oath is contradictory to expiation, so it cannot be a true sabab.
This is a key Hanafi usul principle.
4
Recognize the metaphorical basis
They are called 'sabab' only because of the potential outcome (ma' al-tawil): if the condition (breaking oath or fulfilling condition) occurs, the ruling (expiation or divorce) results. So they are named 'sabab' in view of that eventual consequence, not as actual causes.
The commentary explains 'they are called sabab in view of the outcome'.
5
Apply to conditional clauses
Similarly, conditional divorce (ta'liq al-talaq) is called a 'sabab' for divorce, but the conditional clause ends when the condition is fulfilled, so it cannot be a true cause. The ruling arises from the condition, not the ta'liq itself.
This analogizes to the oath case.

Commentary
This passage clarifies that the term 'sabab' is sometimes used metaphorically for things that are not true causes. The author gives two examples: First, an oath (yamin) is called a sabab for kaffara (expiation), but in reality the oath prevents kaffara because kaffara only becomes obligatory upon breaking the oath (al-hanath), and the oath is intended for obedience, so it actually contradicts the obligation. The commentary elaborates on the logical contradiction: the sabab should not contradict the effect, but the oath contradicts kaffara; however, it is called sabab due to its potential to lead to the effect. The Real Sabab: Definition and Application to Travel
Defining the real sabab and illustrating it with the travel example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the real sabab (cause) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh and apply the definition to determine whether a given travel scenario qualifies as a real sabab.

Core Idea
A real sabab leads to the ruling without contradiction.

This passage opens the chapter on sabab, 'illa and shart. The author first defines the real cause (sabab haqiqi) and immediately grounds it in a concrete example: the journey itself is the cause for the concessions of qasr and iftar. Understanding this definition is essential before exploring metaphorical extensions in the next lesson.

Guided Path
1
Read the definition
Identify the statement: 'The ruling is attached to the journey itself.' This tells us the sabab is the act itself, not the intention or the distance.
2
Extract the key condition
Note that the sultan intends the travel distance (three days/nights). Without this intention, the journey is not a sabab. Intention is a shart (condition), not the sabab.
The passage explicitly says: 'It is only restricted by intending the travel distance because if he traveled for years without intending three days and nights, he does not have the license.'
3
Distinguish sabab from shart
The sabab is the journey itself; the intention of distance is a condition for the sabab to operate. This distinction is fundamental in Hanafi usul.
4
Apply the definition
If a person travels without intending the distance, the journey is not a real sabab, so the ruling of qasr does not follow. This tests whether a candidate 'cause' truly leads to the ruling without contradiction.

Metaphorical Sabab: Oath and Conditional Suspension
Explaining metaphorical sabab using oath and conditional divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why an oath (yamin) and conditional divorce/emancipation are called sabab metaphorically, despite being contrary to the ruling, and apply the principle of naming by potential outcome.

Core Idea
Something that prevents the ruling may still be called sabab if it may eventually lead to the ruling.

Having defined the real sabab, the author now addresses a conceptual challenge: an oath is called a cause for expiation, yet it actually prevents expiation. Similarly, a conditional suspension of divorce or emancipation is called a cause but prevents the occurrence of the condition. This lesson unpacks the logical argument and the resolution: such terms are used metaphorically, based on what they may lead to, not what they actually do.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The author notes that an oath is commonly called a sabab for expiation (e.g., 'kaffarat al-yamin'), yet it contradicts the definition because the oath prevents expiation.
2
Understand the preventer argument
The oath is made for righteousness (birr), which negates breach (hanth); breach is necessary for expiation. So the oath is a preventer (mani') of expiation, not a cause.
The passage says: 'the oath was a preventer of breach, and breach is necessary for expiation.'
3
Analyze the resolution
The author answers that the oath is called sabab 'based on what it may lead to' (bi i'tibar ma ya'ulu ilayh): if the oath is broken, expiation becomes wajib. This is metaphorical extension.
4
Apply to conditional divorce
Similarly, a conditional divorce (e.g., 'If you enter the house, you are divorced') is intended to prevent the condition, yet it is called a cause for divorce. The same resolution applies: it is called sabab because it may lead to divorce if the condition occurs.
5
Connect to the Quranic example
The author cites the story of Yusuf (AS): grapes are called 'wine' because of their potential outcome. This illustrates the broader linguistic principle: naming something by its potential.
This is not a ruling but an analogy to show the permissibility of such naming.

Similarly, a conditional divorce aims to prevent the condition. However, they are called sabab because they may ultimately lead to the ruling (if the oath is broken or condition occurs). The example of grapes being called wine in the Qur'anic story shows that something can be named by its potential outcome. This is a classic Hanafi discussion on the semantics of sabab.

Key Terms
السبب المجازي (al-sabab al-majazi): A metaphorical cause: something that is not a real cause but is called a cause because it may lead to the ruling.
اليمين (al-yamin): An oath that is a preventer of expiation but metaphorically called a cause.

Commentary
This passage is a usul definition and classification of preventers (mawani') in qiyas. The author categorizes preventers into four types: (1) those that prevent the 'illa (cause) from being established at all; (2) those that prevent the 'illa from being completed; (3) those that prevent the ruling from coming into effect; (4) those that prevent the ruling from continuing. Each type is illustrated with fiqh examples from Hanafi law. The first type is exemplified by attempting to sell a free person, carrion, or blood—since these are not considered property, the transaction cannot serve as a cause for effecting ownership. Similarly, suspensive conditions (ta'liq) prevent the cause from becoming effective until the condition occurs; for instance, if a man swears he will not divorce his wife and then suspends divorce on entering a house, he does not break his oath because the cause (the pronouncement of divorce) does not meet its locus. The second type includes the destruction of the nisab during the haul, which prevents the cause for zakat from being completed, and the refusal of one witness, which prevents the completion of testimony. The third type is illustrated by a sale with a stipulation of option (khiyar al-shart): the offer and acceptance (the cause) exist, but the ruling of ownership does not immediately follow due to the option; similarly, the continuation of time for a person with a chronic excuse (sahib al-'udhr) prevents the nullification of wudu from taking effect immediately. The fourth type includes the option of puberty for minors married off by other than the father or grandfather, and the option of emancipation for an enslaved wife—these options prevent the ruling of marriage from becoming binding, i.e., they prevent its continuance or bindingness. The text notes some disagreement: the author of al-Manar divides preventers into five, distinguishing between a preventer of the ruling's continuance and a preventer of its bindingness, giving the option of inspection (khiyar al-ru'ya) as an example of the former and the option of defect (khiyar al-'ayb) as the latter. Finally, the passage discusses the application of the fourth type to indemnity for wounds: according to Abu Hanifa, once a wound heals (al-indimal) without effect, it no longer warrants indemnity, though it may still warrant discretionary punishment; Abu Yusuf requires fair compensation, and Muhammad requires the doctor's fee and medicine costs. This demonstrates how the concept of preventers allows for the specification of the 'illa (takhsis al-'illa), a principle accepted by the early Hanafi scholars from Iraq.

البحث الرابع في القياس
فصل في أقسام الحكم التكليفي
فأما على قول مَن لا يقول بجواز تخصيص العلة، فالمانع عنده ثلاثة أقسام: ۱- مانع يمنعُ ابتداء العلة ٢- ومانع يمنع تمامَها ٣ - ومانع يمنعُ دوام الحكم. أي العلة وأما عند تمام العلة فيثبت الحكم لا محالة.
وعلى هذا كل ما جعله الفريق الأول مانعاً لثبوت الحكم جعله الفريق الثاني مانعاً لتمام العلة، وعلى هذا الأصل يدور الكلام بين الفريقين.
[عمدة الحواشي: ص ۲۳۸] على هذا الأصل: أي بناء على القول بتخصيص العلة الشرعية والقول بالمانع أي مانع الثبوت، ومانع التمام تتفرع الفروع الفقهية. [الشافي: ص ٣٠٤]
The fourth research on analogy. Chapter on the divisions of the obligating ruling. As for the opinion of one who does not permit specification of the cause, then the preventer with him has three divisions: 1- A preventer that prevents the inception of the cause, 2- A preventer that prevents its completion, 3- A preventer that prevents the continuation of the ruling, i.e., the cause. And when the cause is complete, the ruling is established inevitably. According to this, everything that the first group made a preventer for the establishment of the ruling, the second group made a preventer for the completion of the cause, and upon this principle the discussion revolves between the two groups. [ʿUmdat al-Ḥawāshī: p. 238] Upon this principle: i.e., based on the statement that specification of the legal cause and the statement of the preventer, i.e., preventer of establishment and preventer of completion, the fiqh branches are derived. [al-Shāfī: p. 304]

Commentary
This passage is an exposition of a key usul principle regarding qiyas: the classification of preventers (mawāniʿ) that hinder the operation of the ʿilla (ratio legis) in extending a ruling. The author begins by setting out the position of those who do not permit takhṣīṣ of the ʿilla (i.e., specification of the cause). For them, a preventer can occur in three ways: (1) preventing the cause from coming into existence at all, (2) preventing its completion, or (3) preventing the continuation of the ruling even after the cause is fully present. However, they maintain that when the cause is complete, the ruling necessarily follows. The text then contrasts this with the view of a second group (those who allow takhṣīṣ of the ʿilla). According to the latter, what the first group calls a preventer of the ruling is actually a preventer of the completion of the cause itself. Therefore, the ruling does not apply not because of a preventer to the ruling but because the cause itself is incomplete. This disagreement underlies many fiqh disputes. The marginal note from ʿUmdat al-Ḥawāshī clarifies that this principle of tashkīk (specification) and māniʿ is the basis for deriving many branches. The passage is presented in a concise, rule-oriented manner typical of usul texts, focusing on the conceptual framework rather than specific examples.

Key Terms
العلة (al-ʿilla): The ratio legis or effective cause for a ruling in qiyas, upon which the ruling depends.
المائع (al-māniʿ): A preventer; a factor that blocks either the inception, completion, or effect of the ʿilla.
تخصيص العلة (takhṣīṣ al-ʿilla): Specification of the legal cause; the view that a general ʿilla may be restricted to some cases and not apply in others due to a preventer.
