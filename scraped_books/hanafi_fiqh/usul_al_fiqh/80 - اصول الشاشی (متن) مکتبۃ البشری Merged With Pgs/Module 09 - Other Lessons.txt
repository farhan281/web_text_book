The Principle that Haqiqa and Majaz Cannot Combine
Explaining the usul principle of non-combination with applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that haqiqa and majaz cannot combine in a single usage, apply it to interpret Quranic verses and legal documents (wills, oaths), and differentiate the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on inclusion of grandsons.

Core Idea
A word cannot simultaneously carry its literal and metaphorical meanings in one instance — only one governs.

This lesson explores one of the foundational principles of Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the impossibility of combining literal (haqiqa) and metaphorical (majaz) meanings in a single utterance. We will see how this principle is applied to interpret Quranic verses, wills, grants of aman, and oaths. We will also examine an internal Hanafi dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn regarding the scope of the word 'banun' (sons).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
State clearly: al-haqiqa wa al-majaz la yajtami'an – literal and metaphorical meanings cannot be intended together in one usage. The speaker intends either the haqiqa or the majaz, but not both.
This is a key Hanafi rule. The majaz can be intended only when the haqiqa is impossible or when a clear qarina (contextual indicator) points to it.
2
Apply to the Quranic Verse
In verse 5:6, the word 'lamastum' literally means 'touching with the hand'. However, because the context of janabah (major impurity) requires full intercourse to cause janabah, the intended meaning is intercourse (majaz). The literal 'touching' cannot be intended alongside the metaphorical intercourse; one must be chosen.
The Hanafi interpretation relies on the principle that haqiqa and majaz cannot combine, so the context forces the majaz.
3
Apply to Wills and Aman
In a will for 'bani fulan' (sons of so-and-so), the term 'ibn' is haqiqa for son and majaz for grandson. Since they cannot combine, only sons are meant. Similarly, in aman for 'fathers', grandfathers are excluded. The same logic applies to 'abkar' (virgins) in a will: a deflowered woman is not included because she is not a virgin in haqiqa.
Abu Hanifa restricts to the literal. The Sahibayn, however, include grandsons because the term 'banun' by custom includes them. This is a rare exception based on widespread 'urf, not a violation of the rule.
4
Examine the Defense Against Objections
Objections arise from oaths: e.g., 'I will not place my foot' – literal foot placement vs. entering; 'I will not marry' – contract vs. intercourse; 'day of arrival' – day vs. night. The response is that these are not actual combinations; custom ('urf) has made the metaphorical meaning so dominant that it becomes the effective reality. Thus, in oaths, the customary meaning prevails unless the swearer explicitly intends the literal.
This shows that the rule is preserved; the custom effectively selects one meaning (the majaz) as the operative one, and the haqiqa is dropped.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition of the Hanafi usul principle that al-haqiqa wa al-majaz la yajtami'an (literal and metaphorical meanings cannot be combined in a single utterance). The author begins by illustrating the principle with examples: in the case of measuring with a sa', the literal measure is disregarded when the metaphorical meaning (like selling one for two) is intended. Similarly, in the verse of mualamasa (touching), the intended meaning is intercourse (majaz) rather than literal hand-touch. Imam Muhammad then provides several rulings: in a will for mawali, only the testator's own mawali are meant; in granting aman to 'fathers', grandfathers are excluded, and similarly for 'mothers'. This is because the terms 'father' and 'mother' are literal for parents and metaphorical for grandparents. The principle is then stated explicitly. The discussion continues with the Quranic verse on tayammum, where 'lamastum' is interpreted as intercourse (majaz). The text then addresses a will for 'abkar' (virgins): a woman deflowered by zina is not included because she is no longer a virgin literally. The dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) is presented: in a will for 'bani fulan', Abu Hanifa says only sons are meant, while the two say grandsons also included due to common usage. On page 36, the author defends the principle against objections from oath examples. The objector claims that in oaths regarding marriage, placing the foot, residence, and day of arrival, the rulings seem to combine literal and metaphorical meanings (e.g., 'nikah' used literally as contract but oath not broken by fornication; 'wad' al-qadam' used literally as foot-placing but oath broken by any entry). The response (أجاب) is that these are not actual combinations; instead, due to 'urf (custom), the metaphorical meaning has become the practical reality. 'Wad' al-qadam' is commonly understood as 'entering', and 'nikah' in oaths is taken in its literal contract meaning because that is the default. The text also discusses the role of intention (niyya): if the oath-taker specifies a literal meaning, his intention is respected. The conclusion emphasizes that the 'combination' is only apparent; the true operation is the dominance of the metaphorical meaning due to customary usage, which overrides the literal in legal contexts. Throughout, the commentary highlights the Hanafi reliance on 'urf and qara'in (circumstantial evidence) to determine meaning, and shows how the rule functions in both Qur'anic interpretation and practical legal cases.

Key Terms
الحقيقة (al-haqiqa): The original meaning of a word as it was coined for its proper referent, e.g., 'ibn' for a direct male child.
المجاز (al-majaz): A word used for something other than its original referent due to a relationship (e.g., 'ibn' for grandson).
الجمع بين الحقيقة والمجاز (al-jam' bayn al-haqiqa wa al-majaz): The impossibility of simultaneously intending both the literal and metaphorical meanings of a word in a single utterance.
النكاح (al-nikah): In the context of oaths, the literal meaning is the marriage contract (haqiqa).
وضع القدم (wad' al-qadam): Literally 'placing the foot', used metaphorically (majaz) for entering a place due to 'urf.

The Dispute on the Condition of Possibility for Majaz: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Intra-Hanafi usul dispute on majaz condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition of possibility for majaz according to Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and apply the dispute to the example of freeing a slave.

Core Idea
Majaz is valid even if haqiqa is impossible if the utterance is linguistically sound — Abu Hanifa.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi presents a fundamental intra-Hanafi debate: does the use of metaphorical language (majaz) require that the literal meaning (haqiqa) be at least possible? Imam Abu Hanifa says no; his two disciples say yes. The difference leads to distinct rulings in cases like a master calling an older slave 'my son'.

Guided Path
1
Define haqiqa and majaz
Identify the definitions from the passage: haqiqa is the original meaning of a word; majaz is a substitute used due to a connection (al-ʿalāqah). Understand that majaz operates when acting upon haqiqa is prevented.
2
Identify the dispute
Locate the differing views: Abu Hanifa holds that majaz can be used even if haqiqa is impossible in itself; Sahibayn require that haqiqa be at least possible. Note the phrase 'عنده يُصار إلى المجاز وإن لم تكن الحقيقة ممكنة' and the contrasting 'عندهما لا يُصار'.
The pronoun 'هو' refers to Abu Hanifa; 'هما' refers to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
3
Analyze the example: 'this is my son'
Apply the dispute to the case: a master says to an older slave 'هذا ابني'. For Sahibayn, literal sonship is impossible (slave older), so no majaz — speech is meaningless. For Abu Hanifa, the utterance is grammatically sound (subject-predicate) and carries a necessary consequence (freedom), so majaz applies and the slave is freed.
The key distinction: Abu Hanifa views majaz as a substitute in speech (خلف في حق التكلم), while Sahibayn view it as a substitute in ruling (خلف في حق الحكم).
4
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Abu Hanifa frees the slave; Sahibayn do not. Similarly, in other cases like debt claims ('له علي ألف' or 'على هذا الجدار'), Abu Hanifa interprets majaz to assign a meaning, while Sahibayn reject it as meaningless.
5
Apply to the 'my daughter' case
Consider the counterexample: a husband says to his wife 'هذه ابنتي'. Sahibayn and Abu Hanifa agree this cannot be majaz for divorce because literal daughterhood contradicts marriage, and no necessary connection exists. This contrasts with sonship, which does not negate ownership.
This shows that majaz requires a plausible connection; mere impossibility is not enough for Abu Hanifa.

The Goodness of Hadd and Jihad through Their Ends: The Role of Wasita
Explaining why hadd and jihad are good through their ends

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that acts commanded despite apparent harm are justified by their beneficial ends (wasita), and distinguish between acts whose end is achieved directly versus through a separate act.

Core Idea
Hadd and jihad are not good in themselves – their goodness comes from the deterrence and defense of faith they achieve.

This lesson delves into a foundational usul al-fiqh principle: how can Allah command acts that involve harm, such as hadd punishments and jihad? The answer lies in the concept of 'wasita' (medium) – the beneficial outcome that makes the act good. We will explore the reasoning behind this and its implications for understanding divine commands.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
The passage begins by acknowledging a theological difficulty: hadd punishments and jihad involve pain, destruction, and harm. How can such acts be considered good and commanded by Allah?
This reflects the broader usul principle that acts are evaluated by their ends (maqasid).
2
State the Solution: Wasita
The author resolves this by stating that these acts are good 'by means of' (bi-wasita) their beneficial consequences: deterrence from crime (zajr) for hadd, and repelling evil and elevating the word of truth for jihad.
The goodness is not intrinsic but acquired through the medium (wasita).
3
Analyze the Hypothetical
The text uses a hypothetical (law faradna) to illustrate: if the medium were absent (e.g., no crime, no disbelief leading to war), the act would not be commanded. This shows the ruling is contingent on the presence of the medium.
This is a rational proof (dalil aqli) that the act's command depends on its purpose.
4
Distinguish Types of Acts
The commentary distinguishes between acts where the end is directly achieved by the act itself (hadd, qisas, jihad) versus acts where the end is separate (like sa'i and wudu' for prayer). In the first category, the act itself accomplishes the purpose; in the second, the purpose is attained through a separate act.
This distinction is crucial for understanding the nature of the wasita.
5
Reconcile with Revelation
The hypothetical that jihad would cease if all became Muslim is acknowledged to contradict explicit hadith that jihad continues until the Day of Resurrection. The author notes this, but does not resolve it within this passage, leaving it as an apparent tension.
Later Hanafi scholars reconcile this by noting that the hypothetical is purely theoretical and the hadith provides the actual ruling.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, delivering the exact obligation with a deficiency discharges liability but may require compensation or incur sin.

In Hanafi legal theory, fulfilling an obligation (ada') is not always a simple binary. The school distinguishes between complete performance—where the obligation is delivered with all its required attributes—and deficient performance, where the same thing is delivered but lacking some attribute. This distinction governs when compensation is needed and when sin remains. This lesson anchors those definitions and the key examples from the text.

Recognize that ada' is divided into complete (kamil) and deficient (qasir). Complete performance delivers the exact obligation with full attributes; deficient delivers the same thing but with a deficiency.

Complete performance occurs when the owner (or rightful recipient) receives what is owed, even if via a void contract. The examples show that the usurper's delivery to the owner via sale, pawn, or gift counts as ada'.
The contract is void, but the fulfillment is effective.

Two Uses of Fa: Immediate Succession and Causality in Hanafi Usul
Explaining conditional divorce and fa for causality with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule of immediate succession (fa' al-ta'lil) in conditional divorce and distinguish the conditions for fa to indicate causality according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Fa for condition demands immediate temporal succession; fa for causality requires the cause to be durative or final.

The Arabic letter fa carries distinct legal implications. In conditional divorce, it imposes a strict sequence: the second condition must follow the first without delay. When used to indicate causality, fa signals that the following phrase is the reason, with a nuanced condition debated among Hanafi jurists. Understanding these uses is essential for analyzing legal statements in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditional divorce statement

2
Apply the immediate succession rule
If the second condition occurs first or with a delay, the condition is not met and the divorce does not occur. Understand that the fa binds the two conditions temporally.
The rule is derived from the general linguistic principle that fa indicates immediate succession.
3
Recognize fa for causality
Identify when fa is used to show that the following phrase is the cause of the preceding statement. This is fa' al-ta'lil, often interchangeable with lam al-ta'lil.

The Particle 'Thumma' and Delay in Utterance and Ruling
Definition of 'thumma' and intra-Hanafi dispute with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi dispute concerning 'thumma' and apply it to conditional divorce scenarios, identifying whether the delay affects utterance and ruling or ruling alone.

Core Idea
‘Thumma’ means delay, but does it delay the words or just the effect? That changes the divorce count.

A single particle—‘thumma’ (then)—can change an entire legal outcome in divorce. The Hanafi school has an internal dispute on what exactly is delayed: the utterance (the actual speech) or only the legal ruling. Understanding this usul principle is crucial for correctly applying conditional divorce statements.

Guided Path
1
Define ‘thumma’ according to the Hanafi school
State that ‘thumma’ is a conjunction indicating delay (tarākhī). Identify the core issue: does the delay apply to the utterance (lafẓ) and the ruling (ḥukm) together, or to the ruling alone?
The literal meaning ‘then’ implies a gap in time.
2
Identify the positions of Abu Hanifa and the two companions
Abu Hanifa holds that ‘thumma’ indicates delay in both utterance and ruling. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) hold that it indicates delay only in ruling, not in utterance.
This is an intra-Hanafi dispute.
3
Map the reasoning (dalil) for each position
Abu Hanifa argues that the word is placed for delay, and the perfection of delay is in both utterance and ruling. The companions argue that conjunctions require continuity in speech; only the legal effect is delayed.

The Particle 'Lakin' and Its Legal Effect in Istidrak
Defines the usage of 'lakin' and its legal effect in statements after negation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the legal function of the particle 'lakin' after negation and apply the condition of coherence (ittisaq al-kalam) to determine the effect of statements in Hanafi legal analysis.

Core Idea
'Lakin' affirms what follows; it does not negate what precedes — that negation is from the negative particle itself.

This lesson explores the Hanafi usul rule governing the particle 'lakin' (but/however). Often confused with 'bal' (but rather), 'lakin' has a specific role: it only occurs after negation and serves to affirm a different concept, while the negation of the prior statement is already established by the negative word. Understanding this distinction is crucial when interpreting acknowledgments (iqrar) and other legal statements where error correction may occur.

Guided Path
1
Identify the particle 'lakin' in a statement
Recognize when 'lakin' appears after a negation, typically marked by 'la' or 'ma'.
The negation must be explicit; 'lakin' alone does not negate.
2
Determine the function of 'lakin'
State that 'lakin' is for istidrak (correction/exception). Its effect is to affirm what follows it. It does not negate the preceding part; that negation comes from the negative particle.
This is the core rule: 'lakin' does not negate the previous.
3
Check the condition of coherence (ittisaq al-kalam)
The statement must be coherent: (a) the speech is connected, not disjointed, and (b) the affirmed matter is different from the negated matter (different causes, types, etc.) so that there is no contradiction.
If coherence is absent, the statement may be treated as independent (musta'naf).
4
Apply the rule to a legal statement
Using an example from the text (e.g., acknowledgment of debt), trace how the negation applies only to the cause, not the essence, if the speech is coherent.
Example: 'No, but it is a usurpation' — negation on cause (loan), not on debt.
5
Distinguish 'lakin' from 'bal'
Note that 'bal' can be used after both negation and affirmation, and it voids the previous ruling, whereas 'lakin' does not void the previous ruling but only adds the subsequent affirmation.
This distinction is critical for correct legal inference.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Al-Shashi's Usul al-Fiqh deals with the legal-linguistic rule concerning the particle 'lakin' (but/however). It is an expansion (sharh) on the original matn's section on 'lakin'. The author first distinguishes between performative utterances (insha') and reports (khabar), noting that errors can be corrected in reports but not in performatives, using the examples of divorce (talaq) and acknowledgment (iqrar). Then the core rule is presented: 'lakin' is used for istidrak (correction/exception) after negation; its function is to affirm what follows it, while the negation of what precedes is established by the preceding negative word (e.g., 'la'). The condition for its proper use is 'ittisaq al-kalam' (coherence of speech), which requires two things: (1) the speech must be connected and not disjointed, and (2) the affirmed matter must be in a different category from the negated matter (e.g., a different cause), so that there is no contradiction. The commentary then distinguishes 'lakin' from 'bal' (but rather): 'bal' can be used after both affirmation and negation, and its effect is to negate the previous and affirm the next; 'lakin' after negation only affirms the next, and the previous remains negated by the original negation word. When conjoining sentences, 'lakin' can also be used after affirmation, but it never voids the previous ruling like 'bal' does. The passage provides examples from Imam Muhammad's Al-Jami' al-Kabir to illustrate the rule. For instance, if someone acknowledges a debt of a thousand as a loan, and the other responds 'No, but it is a usurpation,' the debt is still owed because the negation ('No') only denied the cause (loan), not the debt itself; the affirmation ('but it is a usurpation') affirms the debt with a different cause. Similarly for a slave claimed by two parties: the negation of ownership from oneself combined with 'but he is for another' establishes ownership for the other. This shows that 'lakin' links the negation with the subsequent affirmation, creating coherence. The commentary emphasizes that the key is whether the speech is consistent and the negation is on the cause, not the essence. This usul rule is crucial for understanding legal statements in court proceedings and acknowledgments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule for 'lākinna' to determine whether negation attaches to the cause or stands alone, and distinguish coherent from incoherent statements.

Core Idea
When 'lākinna' follows a negation, coherent speech links the negation to the cause, not the essence.

This lesson examines the particle 'lākinna' (but/however) in legal statements. In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the interpretation of 'lākinna' after a negation depends on coherence: if the overall statement is consistent, the negation applies only to the cause (sabab), preserving the affirmed essence. If incoherent, the clause after 'lākinna' is treated as an independent statement. We'll work through examples from the Hanafi tradition to master this principle.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi explains the rule governing the particle 'lākinna' (but/however) when it appears after a negation in legal statements. The core principle is that negation ('nafy') attaches to the affirmation ('ithbāt') that follows 'lākinna' if the overall speech is coherent ('mutasāq'); otherwise, the statement after 'lākinna' is treated as an independent new statement ('musta'naf'). Coherence here means that the negation and affirmation do not contradict each other; i.e., they apply to different aspects (such as the cause versus the essence). The text provides three examples from Hanafi fiqh. First: if a person acknowledges a loan of one thousand, and the claimant responds 'No, but it was a usurpation,' the money is still owed because both agree on the existence of the debt; the negation only concerned the cause (loan vs. usurpation). Second: if the acknowledgement is for the price of a slave-girl, and the claimant negates the slave-girl being his but affirms the debt, again the debt holds. Third: if someone says 'This slave belongs to so-and-so,' and the named person denies ownership but then attributes it to another, the outcome depends on whether the speech is connected or separated. If connected (using 'lākinna'), the slave goes to the second person; if separated (e.g., a pause), the first speaker's denial stands. The final example involves a slave-girl marrying herself without permission; the master rejects the marriage at one price but accepts at a higher price. The text rules that this is incoherent because it negates and affirms the same act (the marriage) – only the dowry differs, which is subsidiary. Thus the 'lākinna' is treated as a fresh start, voiding the original contract. The passage emphasizes that coherence requires both connection in speech and lack of contradiction in subject matter.

'Aw' in conflicting oath clauses means 'until', not 'or', and inclusion of the ghaya depends on homogeneity.

If the person enters the first house first, the condition is triggered and the oath is broken (حنث). If they enter the second house first, the oath is fulfilled (برّ).

The Particle 'Hatta' as Ghaya (Limit) in Hanafi Usul
Defining 'hatta' as ghaya with conditions and custom exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that 'hatta' indicates a limit (ghaya) to interpret oaths and identify when custom overrides the literal meaning.

Core Idea
Hatta primarily indicates the limit (ghaya), requiring extension of the prior action and a suitable endpoint.

In Hanafi usul, particles often carry legal weight. The particle 'hatta' (حتى) is central to oath interpretation. This lesson unpacks its primary function as a ghaya (limit), the conditions for its literal operation, and the role of custom in overriding it. You will learn to distinguish between literal and metaphorical applications based on the nature of the actions involved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the textual element
Locate the particle 'hatta' in the legal utterance. Note its position relative to the action and the event.
In oaths, 'hatta' often appears as 'until' or is implied by 'or' when conjunction is impossible.
2
Apply the usul rule: ghaya
Assume 'hatta' indicates a limit: the action before it extends until the event after it occurs. This is the default Hanafi position.
This rule is derived from linguistic analysis and Quranic usage (e.g., 'until the break of dawn').
3
Check conditions for literal operation
Verify that the preceding action can extend (imitidad) and the following event is a suitable endpoint (ghaya). If both conditions are met, 'hatta' operates literally.
Example: beating can extend; intercession is a suitable endpoint.
4
Consider custom (urf) as exception
If literal application leads to absurdity or against common practice (e.g., beating until death), custom overrides the literal meaning. The oath is then interpreted according to custom.
The passage states that swearing to beat until death is interpreted as severe beating, not literal death.
5
Derive the ruling
If the oath-taker stops before the endpoint (or before the customarily understood limit), they break the oath (hanth). If they reach the endpoint, the oath is fulfilled (birr).
The ruling depends on the order of events and the presence of custom.

The Particle 'Hatta': Three Meanings in Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh
Analysis of 'hatta' in legal texts and oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three possible meanings of 'hatta' (ghaya, sababiyya, atf) and apply the Hanafi priority rule to interpret oaths containing 'hatta'.

Core Idea
Hatta's primary meaning is limit (ghaya); only if impossible, it becomes causality (sababiyya) or conjunction (atf).

The Arabic particle 'hatta' (until/even) appears frequently in legal texts and oaths. Hanafi usul distinguishes three possible meanings: limit (ghaya), causality (sababiyya meaning 'so that'), and pure conjunction (atf). The primary meaning is limit, and it is applied whenever the first action can be extended and the second is a suitable endpoint. When custom or impossibility prevents that, the secondary meanings are used. This lesson examines the rules for determining which meaning applies, using examples from oaths.

Guided Path
1
Understand the primary meaning of 'hatta' as ghaya
Learn that 'hatta' primarily indicates a limit (ghaya) of an action. The condition is that the first part must be capable of extension and the second part must be a suitable endpoint.
Example: 'I will not separate until he pays' – non-separation can extend, payment is an endpoint.
2
Recognize when custom blocks the literal meaning
When literal interpretation is impossible or against custom (e.g., 'beat until death'), the meaning is shifted to severe beating; the oath is not broken by stopping before death.
Custom (urf) overrides the literal due to impossibility.
3
Identify the secondary meaning as sababiyya (causality)
If the first part cannot be extended but the second is a suitable recompense, 'hatta' means 'so that' (causality). The first part becomes a cause for the second.
Example: 'I will not come until you feed me' – coming is cause for feeding.
4
Recognize the tertiary meaning as pure conjunction (atf)
When neither limit nor causality is possible, 'hatta' is interpreted as mere conjunction (like 'and'). This is a juristic metaphor.
Rare in practice; used only when both prior conditions fail.
5
Apply the priority rule to oath examples
Given an oath with 'hatta', first check if ghaya is possible (extension + suitable limit). If custom forbids, move to sababiyya; if that fails, use atf.
The mufta bihi in Hanafi school is to follow this priority.

The silent person must not be under compulsion, insanity, or other impediment. They must be able to express objection if displeased.
A virgin's shyness does not prevent refusal; she can say 'no' but shyness hinders 'yes'.
3
Determine the default consequence
In the absence of prohibition, silence implies permission (ibaha) or consent. For the Prophet, his silence indicates that the action is legislated.
Not silence on everything; only when there is occasion to speak.
4
Consider the Quranic proof
The command to enjoin good and forbid evil obliges the Prophet to speak against wrong; his silence thus indicates the action is not wrong.
Quran 7:157 and 53:3-4 support this inference.
5
Examine the practical application to other cases
Apply the same reasoning to similar situations where a person with authority witnesses an action and remains silent.
Example: a master seeing his slave trade – silence grants permission.

Commentary
This passage explains the principle of 'bayan al-hal' (clarification through situation). The author illustrates that in certain circumstances, silence amounts to a clear declaration. The Hanafi Scope of Istishab: Defense Only
Discussion of Istishab as proof only for defense

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on istishab al-hal and distinguish between its use for defense (daf) and its prohibition for imposition (ilzam).

Core Idea
Istishab preserves existing states but cannot create new obligations.

This lesson explores a foundational rule of Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the presumption of continuity (istishab al-hal) is a proof, but only for defense, not for imposing new obligations. We will examine the rationale through concrete examples and contrast it with the Shafi'i school. By the end, you will understand when and how istishab may be invoked.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
Recognize that the core question is the scope of istishab as a legal proof: can it only maintain existing states (daf') or also create new rulings (ilzam)?
The text explicitly states it is for daf' only.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that istishab is valid for defending a previously established state and preserving it, but not for establishing a new ruling or imposing an obligation.
This is the key distinction from other schools.
3
Review the supporting examples
Analyze the three examples: unknown lineage (presumed free, so no full diyah claimed), menstruation (habit presumes non-menstrual after habit days), and missing person (no inheritance to or from him). In each, istishab repels a claim but does not create a new obligation.
Focus on how daf' operates in each case.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Note that al-Shafi'i allows istishab for both daf' and raf' (raising new rulings), leading to different outcomes. Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) in the missing person case.
The text prefers the Hanafi view.
5
Understand the epistemological weight
Recognize that the Hanafi restriction is based on the principle that imposing obligations without evidence is invalid. Istishab is weak (zanni) and cannot bear the burden of ilzam.
This aligns with the Hanafi cautious approach to sources.

Commentary
This passage from the Hanafi usul text establishes a key principle regarding istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity). The author begins by stating that istishab is valid only for defense (daf) and not for imposing a new obligation (ilzam). This is a characteristic Hanafi position. The first example is a person of unknown lineage: if someone claims him as a slave and then injures him, the full diyah for a free person is not obligatory because that would be an imposition, and istishab (here, the presumption of freedom) cannot be used to impose a new obligation. The second example concerns menstruation: a woman with a regular habit exceeds her habit but remains within ten days. The excess is considered istihadah (non-menstrual bleeding), because connecting it to the previous menstrual blood would be an imposition without evidence. Then the text contrasts the Hanafi and Shafi'i views: al-Shafi'i holds that istishab can be used both for defense and for establishing new rulings (raf), while the Hanafis restrict it to preservation and defense, not for creation of new obligations. The missing person case illustrates: during his absence, others do not inherit from him, and he does not inherit from them, because using istishab to grant inheritance would be an imposition. Finally, the narration from Abu Hanifah about ambergris exempting it from khums because no text (nass) exists is cited as an example where the lack of evidence prevents imposing an obligation.

Key Terms
استصحاب الحال (istishab al-hal): Presumption of continuity of a previously established state until evidence of change arises
دفع (daf'): Repelling a claim or obligation that contradicts the presumed state
إلزام (ilzam): Imposing a new obligation or legal effect that was not previously established
