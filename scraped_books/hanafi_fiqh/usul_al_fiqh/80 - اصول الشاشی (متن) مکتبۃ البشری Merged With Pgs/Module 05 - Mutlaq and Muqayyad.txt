The Absolute and the Restricted in Hanafi Usul
Defines absolute and restricted terms and states the Hanafi position with the Shafi'i counterposition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (restricted) terms and apply the Hanafi rule that the mutlaq is not carried to the muqayyad unless acting on both independently is impossible.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school holds that an absolute text is not automatically restricted by a restricted text; each applies in its own context.

This lesson introduces a foundational usul al-fiqh principle: the relationship between mutlaq (absolute) and muqayyad (restricted) texts. The Hanafi position is that the absolute remains operative unless it is impossible to act on both texts independently. This principle has significant practical implications for deriving rulings. We will explore the definitions, the Hanafi rule, the Shafi'i counterposition, and the fruit of the dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue and definitions
First, understand what is meant by mutlaq and muqayyad. The passage defines mutlaq as a term indicating an unrestricted common individual (e.g., 'raqaba' without qualification) and muqayyad as a term restricted by an attribute (e.g., 'raqaba mu'mina').
The definitions are standard across schools; the dispute is on how they interact.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi rule: 'المطلق يجري على إطلاقه' — the absolute runs on its absoluteness. It is not restricted by the mere existence of a muqayyad text. Restriction (taqyid) is applied only when it is impossible to act on both texts (تعذر الجمع).
This means each text is independently operative in its own context.
3
State the Shafi'i position
According to Imam al-Shafi'i, the mutlaq is automatically carried to the muqayyad because both come from the same Lawgiver. If one is restricted, the absolute is intended to be restricted in all cases to avoid contradiction.
This is a fundamental ikhtilaf between the two schools.
4
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafi response: there is no contradiction; each text applies in its own context. Only when acting on both is impossible (i.e., the same ruling cannot accommodate both) is restriction applied. This preserves the independent operation of each text.
The key is that the contexts are different; for example, the oath expiation verse and the accidental killing verse address different obligations.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: In the case of freeing a slave for expiation, Hanafis hold that freeing any slave suffices for an oath (mutlaq), while Shafi'is require a believing slave because the muqayyad verse in accidental killing restricts the absolute in all contexts.
This example illustrates how the dispute affects actual fiqh rulings.

This is a key Usul principle distinguishing the Hanafi school from the Shafi'i school, as later clarified: Shafi'i holds that if there is a restricted version elsewhere, the absolute is carried to the restricted. The passage then includes a lengthy marginal note (initiated by 'فإذا قام الدليلُ إلخ') that actually returns to the earlier discussion on riba, explaining how scholars derived different rulings on riba-based transactions through qiyas and ta'lil. This note serves as an example of the earlier principle of specification, not directly of mutlaq-muqayyad. The Hanafi rule is that the absolute is not restricted by mere existence of a restricted text; it is only restricted if acting on the absolute alone is impossible (ta'adhdhur al-jam'). Mutlaq and Muqayyad: Tawaf and Ruku as Examples
Explaining how mutlaq commands are not qualified by khabar al-wahid

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a mutlaq Quranic command is not altered by a khabar al-wahid, and determine the resulting fard and wajib obligations.

Core Idea
Absolute Quranic command remains fard; khabar al-wahid can only add wajib conditions.

In this passage, the author explains a fundamental Hanafi usul principle: when a khabar al-wahid (single-narrator hadith) appears to add a condition to a mutlaq (absolute) Quranic command, it does not change the original obligation. Instead, the command remains fard (obligatory by definitive proof) and the extra condition becomes wajib (obligatory by probable proof). This principle is illustrated with tawaf and ruku, then applied to the ruling on wudu water.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mutlaq command in the Quranic verses
Locate the Quranic commands: 'وَلْيَطُوفُوا بِالْبَيْتِ الْعَتِيقِ' (Surah al-Hajj) and 'وَارْكَعُوا مَعَ الرَّاكِعِينَ' (Surah al-Baqarah). These are absolute in meaning, covering any form of tawaf or ruku without conditions.
The Arabic word for tawaf is used in its unqualified sense.
2
Recognize the khabar al-wahid that seems to add a condition
The khabar states that tawaf is like prayer (requiring wudu) and that ruku requires i'tidal (straightening after bowing). These are not from mutawatir transmission.
The exact wording of the khabar is not quoted here, but it is known from hadith collections.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule: the khabar does not change the mutlaq ruling
Since the Quran is qat'i in transmission and meaning, and khabar al-wahid is zanni, the khabar cannot abrogate or restrict the absolute command. Instead, the absolute command remains fard, and the condition from the khabar becomes wajib.
This harmonizes the two evidences without downgrading the Quranic obligation.
4
Determine the practical consequences
For tawaf: the absolute tawaf is fard; wudu is wajib. If wudu is omitted, the tawaf is valid but requires damm (blood) as compensation. For ruku: absolute ruku is fard; i'tidal is wajib. The same principle applies to wudu water: water mixed with a pure substance remains mutlaq water, so wudu is valid; the condition for tayammum is total absence of mutlaq water, which is not met here.
The final line summarizes: the absolute operates according to its absoluteness.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi is a ruling-type passage demonstrating how mutlaq (absolute) commands in the Quran interact with ahad (single-narrator) hadith that appear to add conditions. The author gives two examples: the command in Surah al-Hajj (22:29) for tawaf, and in Surah al-Baqarah (2:43) for ruku. In both cases, the Quranic command is absolute, covering any instance of tawaf or ruku in its basic linguistic meaning. The hadith that 'tawaf is like prayer' or that one must straighten in ruku (i'tidal) do not change the essence of the command; rather, the absolute act remains fard (obligatory by definitive proof) from the Quran, while the additional condition becomes wajib (obligatory by probable proof) from the hadith. This is a critical Hanafi principle: khabar al-wahid cannot abrogate or alter the Quranic ruling; it can only impose additional obligations that are compensated by blood (damm) if omitted. The author then applies this principle to wudu: water mixed with a pure substance like saffron remains mutlaq water, so it is suitable for wudu; the condition for tayammum is the total absence of mutlaq water, and here mutlaq water exists. The final line summarizes: the absolute operates according to its absoluteness, meaning it is not restricted by external qualifications unless proven to be part of the original command.

The Hanafi Definition of Mutlaq Water and the Linguistic Test
Defining mutlaq water and distinguishing it from muqayyad water

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi linguistic test to determine whether a water is mutlaq or muqayyad, and evaluate the Shafi'i objection based on the Quranic condition.

Core Idea
Water remains mutlaq if the word 'water' still applies in common language, regardless of added pure substances.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi tackles a foundational question: what counts as 'water' for wudu? The Hanafi school uses a simple linguistic test: if a person asking for 'water' would not be mistaken if given that water, it remains mutlaq. This directly impacts daily practice, such as using saffron-scented water for wudu. We will walk through the definition, the dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i, and the Hanafi response, grounding every step in the source text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central definition
Find where the author defines mutlaq water. Look for the phrase 'mutlaq water is what comes to mind when the word water is uttered'.
The definition hinges on common linguistic understanding, not chemical composition.
2
Apply the linguistic test to examples
The author contrasts saffron water (mutlaq) with rose water (muqayyad). Ask: would a native speaker be mistaken if given that water?
Saffron water passes the test; rose water fails because it is typically called 'rose water', not just 'water'.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i objection
Imam al-Shafi'i argued that water must remain 'as sent down from the sky' (Quran 25:48), rejecting mutlaq status for water mixed with pure substances. Identify this claim in the text.
The opposing view is grounded in a literal reading of the Quranic attribute.
4
Examine the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response argues that the condition of being 'sent down' is a restriction on the Book that cannot be added. The text states this explicitly.
This is a usul principle: adding a condition to a text without evidence is not permissible.
5
Distinguish types of changes
The author notes that some additions remove the name entirely (e.g., rose water), while others do not (e.g., saffron). Classify each example given.
The key is whether the original name 'water' is replaced in common usage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify water as mutlaq or muqayyad according to Hanafi usul, apply the linguistic test, and evaluate the Hanafi–Shafi'i dispute on water mixed with pure substances.

Core Idea
Water remains absolute if it still bears the name 'water' in common usage; change in essence removes it, not mere addition.

This lesson examines the Hanafi definition of absolute water (maa' mutlaq) from Usul al-Shashi. The key is a linguistic criterion: if the word 'water' comes to mind when the term is used, it is absolute. This has direct implications for the validity of wudu with water mixed with substances like saffron. The text also engages with the Shafi'i school's stricter requirement that water must remain exactly as sent down from the sky, and provides a Hanafi rebuttal through a dalil from the Quran and considerations of linguistic usage.

Guided Path
1
Understand the linguistic test
Absolute water is what comes to mind when the word 'water' is uttered without qualification. If an additive does not remove the name 'water' in common speech, the water remains mutlaq.
This is a semantic criterion, not a chemical one.
2
Apply the test to examples
Saffron water: still called water, passes. Rose water: not called water in common usage, fails. Similarly, soup water and bean water fail.
Distinguish between additives that change the essence versus those that only alter properties.
3
Connect to the Quranic dalil
The verse 'And We sent down from the sky pure water' (al-Furqan 25:48) is absolute (mutlaq). It does not restrict the water to a specific form, so any water that is pure and called water is included.
The Hanafi principle: al-mutlaq yajri 'ala itlaqih (the absolute runs on its absoluteness).
4
Examine the Shafi'i objection
The Shafi'i school argues that water mixed with pure substances like saffron becomes muqayyad (qualified) and no longer counts as water as sent down. They require the water to remain in its original state.
Their proof: the verse says 'from the sky', implying the water should be unchanged.
5
Follow the Hanafi response
The Hanafi school responds that the linguistic usage proves the name remains, and the Shafi'i condition would be an impermissible restriction on the absolute text. Impure water is excluded not because of its name but because it does not effect purification.
Thus the thamarah al-khilaf is clear: Hanafi permits wudu with saffron water, Shafi'i does not.

The Hanafi proof is that the condition of 'not touching' is mentioned in the Quran for emancipation and fasting, but not for feeding. If it had been intended for feeding, it would have been repeated there too. The repetition in fasting (after emancipation) proves it was not intended for feeding.
The lack of repetition in feeding is decisive against the analogy.
5
Determine the Practical Difference (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
If a muzahir has intercourse during the feeding, Hanafi says the feeding already done counts and he continues; the other schools say the feeding is invalidated and he must restart. This difference stems directly from the usul principle.
Always trace back to the usul rule to see the root of the ikhtilaf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a mutlaq text is not restricted by qiyas, and differentiate between legitimate restriction by hadith and impermissible restriction by analogy.

Core Idea
A mutlaq text cannot be restricted by analogy (qiyas), only by clear textual evidence that itself constitutes naskh.

In this passage, the Hanafi school defends the absoluteness of the word 'raqaba' in the expiation verses for zihar and yamin against the Shafi'i attempt to restrict it by analogy to the qisas verse. The core argument is that restricting a mutlaq text through qiyas would amount to abrogation (naskh), which cannot be established by analogical reasoning alone. However, the Hanafis do accept restriction by hadith in other cases, such as the amount of masah (wiping) and the requirement of actual intercourse in the hadith of Rifa'ah. This lesson will guide you through the reasoning and the distinguishing features of each case.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed text and competing rulings
Note that the Quranic verses for zihar (58:3) and yamin (5:89) say 'freeing a slave' without the adjective 'believing', while the qisas verse (4:92) says 'freeing a believing slave'. The Hanafi school holds the first two are mutlaq; Shafi'i argues they should be restricted by qiyas to the qisas verse.
2
Examine the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi rely on the literal absoluteness of the text. The principle is that a mutlaq text runs upon its absoluteness until a clear textual evidence restricts it, and such restriction constitutes naskh, which cannot be done by qiyas.
This is a usul rule: 'al-mutlaq yajri 'ala itlaqihi'.
3
Examine the Shafi'i proof
Shafi'i argues that all expiations are of one type (jins wahid), so the condition of faith from the qisas verse should apply by analogy to zihar and yamin expiations.
Refer to the madhhab dispute map.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i objection
Hanafi response: Restricting by analogy would be an addition (ziyada) that abrogates the absoluteness, and naskh cannot be proven by qiyas because qiyas is zanni and naskh requires qat'i evidence.
The key point: 'al-ziyada naskh'.
5
Distinguish the two exceptions: masah and the woman of Rifa'ah
In those cases, the restriction is by a clear hadith, not by qiyas. The Hanafi argue that the text on wiping is not truly mutlaq because performing any indefinite part would not fulfill the command; similarly, the marriage verse is mutlaq only in appearance, as the condition of actual intercourse is derived from a hadith that clarifies the intended meaning.
This shows that restriction by hadith is permissible when the hadith itself is probative and clarifies the text, not adds a new condition.

Al-Mutlaq wa al-Muqayyad: Definition and the Rule against Taqyid by Qiyas
Defining mutlaq and arguing against taqyid without strong proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define a mutlaq term and explain why restricting it by analogy (qiyas) is invalid in the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A mutlaq term applies equally to all its individuals; restricting it by analogy is naskh, which requires definitive evidence.

The Hanafi school holds that an absolute (mutlaq) text remains on its absoluteness and cannot be restricted by analogy, because doing so would be an increase (ziyada) tantamount to abrogation (naskh). This lesson examines the sign of a mutlaq term, applies it to the examples of wiping the head and the termination of severe prohibition by marriage, and then enters into the classical dispute with Imam al-Shafi'i over the expiation (kaffarah) verses. By understanding this dispute, you will grasp a foundational usul principle that governs how Hanafis read the Qur'an and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the sign of a mutlaq term
The passage states that the sign (alama) of mutlaq is that the ruling applies equally to every individual of the category. For example, if a command says 'free a slave', freeing any slave fulfills the command. If the ruling does not apply to all individuals equally, the term is not mutlaq.
This distinguishes mutlaq from 'amm: 'amm applies to all individuals of a genus, but mutlaq applies to all individuals of the category described.
2
Analyze the masah (wiping the head) objection
Opponents object: The Qur'an commands wiping 'some part of the head' (mutlaq), yet Hanafis restrict it to a quarter (forelock) based on hadith. The Hanafi response: The term is not truly mutlaq because wiping half or two-thirds does not fulfill the obligation – only wiping the whole head or the specified quarter does. So the sign of mutlaq is missing.
This shows that the text in the wiping verse is mujmal (ambiguous), not mutlaq.
3
Analyze the marriage (termination of severe prohibition) objection
Opponents object: The Qur'an is absolute about marriage terminating the severe prohibition after three divorces, but Hanafis require consummation with a second husband. The Hanafi response: Some interpret 'marriage' in the verse as 'intercourse', while others say the condition is established by a famous hadith (khabar mashhur), not a solitary report, so it is permissible.
The dispute here involves the nature of the Qur'anic text and the authority of the hadith.
4
Evaluate the kaffarat dispute and the Hanafi proof
The Qur'an in zihar and oath expiations says 'free a slave' (mutlaq), while in killing expiation it says 'free a believing slave' (muqayyad). Al-Shafi'i analogizes all expiations as one genus and restricts the absolute verses to require a believing slave. Hanafis reject this: the absolute proceeds on its absoluteness; restricting it by qiyas would be an increase (ziyada) that amounts to naskh, which cannot be done by analogy.
This is the central ikhtilaf: can a mutlaq text be restricted by qiyas? Hanafis say no because it is naskh in effect.

Commentary
This passage is a definitional and disputational exposition on the concept of 'mutlaq' (absolute) and 'muqayyad' (qualified) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author begins by stating the chapter on mutlaq and muqayyad, then immediately addresses two objections raised by opponents (likely Shafi'is). The first objection concerns the wiping of the head (masah): the Quran commands wiping 'some part of the head' (mutlaq), yet Hanafis restrict it to a quarter (the forelock) based on hadith. The second objection is about the termination of severe prohibition after three divorces: the Quranic text is absolute regarding marriage, but Hanafis restrict it by requiring consummation with a second husband, based on the hadith of Rifa'a's wife. The Hanafi response: The term in the wiping verse is not truly absolute because a mutlaq term must apply uniformly to all individuals. For example, if wiping half or a third were enough, then each would be a complete fulfillment, but that is not the case; only wiping the entire head (or the specified quarter) fulfills the command. So the text is not absolute; it is ambiguous (mujmal). For the marriage case, some Hanafis argue that 'marriage' in the text actually means 'intercourse' (since the contract is understood from 'husband'), while others accept that the condition of consummation is established by a famous hadith (khabar mashhur), not a solitary one, so restricting the Quran by it is permissible. The passage then discusses the example of freeing a slave in expiations: the verses for zihar and oath are absolute without requiring faith, while the verse for killing requires a believing slave. Al-Shafi'i analogizes all expiations as one genus and thus applies the faith condition universally. The Hanafi position is that the absolute stands on its absoluteness; restricting it by analogy would be an increase (ziyada) tantamount to abrogation (naskh), which cannot be done via analogy. The author concludes that the sign of an absolute term is that its ruling applies equally to every individual; otherwise it is not truly absolute.

Buying a Slave Girl and the Hadith on the Fly: Context as a Specifier in Hanafi Usul
Examples of context specifying ambiguous commands in buying and in the fly hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi principle of siyaq (context) specifies absolute commands, using the examples of buying a slave girl and the fly hadith.

Core Idea
Context (siyaq) can override the literal obligation of a command, converting it to recommendation when the purpose is worldly benefit.

This passage from the Shashi text illustrates a key Hanafi usul principle: the literal meaning (haqiqah) of a word can be abandoned in favor of the context of speech (siyaq al-kalam). Two vivid examples are given: first, a principal's instructions in a wakalah (agency) contract, where the context restricts what slave girl can be bought; second, the well-known hadith about a fly falling into food, where the imperative to dip the fly is interpreted as recommended rather than obligatory due to the worldly benefit. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly deriving rulings from commands in the Quran and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle being demonstrated
The passage demonstrates that context (siyaq) can restrict an absolute term (mutlaq) or override the literal implication of a command. This is a fundamental Hanafi usul concept.
2
Analyze the wakalah example
Examine how the principal's stated purpose ('to serve me' or 'to have intercourse') restricts the absolute term 'slave girl' (jariyah). If the agent buys a blind or paralyzed girl when the purpose is service, the contract is not valid. Similarly, buying a foster sister for intercourse is invalid. The context specifies the intended meaning.
This shows that absolute words can be made restricted (muqayyad) by the context of the speaker's purpose.
3
Examine the fly hadith
Read the hadith: 'When a fly falls into the vessel of one of you, let him dip it completely...' Note the imperative 'falyaghmasuhu'. Literally, this indicates obligation. But the context that follows—mentioning disease and cure—indicates that the dipping is for a worldly benefit.
The hadith is authentic (zanni al-thubut), but the interpretation of its meaning (dalala) is affected by context.
4
Apply the Hanafi rule on commands (amr)
In Hanafi usul, a command (amr) indicates obligation (wujub) unless there is a contextual indicator (qarinah) that diverts it. Here, the context of worldly benefit serves as such a qarinah, transforming the ruling from wajib to nadb (recommended).
This is known as 'al-amr li-ghayrihi' or 'al-amr al-muta'allaq bi al-ghayr' – commands for worldly benefit.
5
Consider the objection and response
The passage addresses a potential objection: commands can be obligatory even if they involve benefit (e.g., Quranic verse 'do not throw yourselves into destruction' [2:195] is obligatory). The response is that in this hadith, the context specifically indicates the benefit is for the individual's worldly well-being, not an act of devotion. Thus, it is not obligatory.
This nuance distinguishes between commands that are purely devotional and those primarily intended for benefit.

Commentary
This passage continues the theme of abandoning literal meaning (haqeeqah) due to context. The first example involves agency (wakalah): if someone says 'buy me a slave girl to serve me', the agent cannot buy a blind or paralyzed one because the context specifies a girl capable of service. Similarly, if the principal says 'buy me a slave girl so that I may have intercourse with her', the agent cannot buy the principal's foster sister (by rada') because she is unlawful for intercourse; context restricts the absolute term 'slave girl'. The second, longer example is the well-known hadith about a fly falling into a drink or food. The hadith commands to dip the fly fully and then remove it. The literal implication of the imperative (amr) is obligation (wujub). However, the context of the hadith—mentioning that one wing has disease and the other cure—indicates that the dipping is for a worldly benefit (benefiting health), not a purely devotional act. Thus, the command is interpreted as recommendation (nadb) rather than obligation. The text also explains that this does not contradict the principle that commands can be obligatory even if they involve benefit; there is a discussion using the Quranic verse 'do not throw yourselves into destruction' which is obligatory. The unique aspect here is the context that makes the command for our benefit, not an act of worship, thus not obligatory. The commentary also discusses the impurity issue: according to the majority, the fly's fall does not make the food impure, but Imam al-Shafi'i had one opinion deeming it impure, though the majority of his followers reject that. The footnote provides the hadith references. This is an important usul principle: amr after prohibition or for worldly benefit indicates non-obligation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mutlaq and muqayyad commands and explain the Hanafi ruling that absolute commands permit deferment (tarakhi), citing the proofs and addressing the opposing fawr view.

Core Idea
Absolute commands are for ease: you may delay them as long as you act before death.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces a foundational classification: commanded acts are either absolute with respect to time or time-bound. According to the Hanafi school, absolute commands such as zakat, 'ushr, and expiations allow deferment (tarakhi) — a position rooted in the nature of command and the principle of ease. However, some scholars (including certain Hanafis) argue for immediate performance (fawr). This lesson unpacks the definitions, the dispute, and the Hanafi proofs that support tarakhi.

1
Identify the two types of commanded acts
The passage begins by stating that the commanded act is divided into two types: absolute (mutlaq 'an al-waqt) and time-bound (muqayyad bi al-waqt). Locate the definitions.
2
Understand the definition of mutlaq
A mutlaq command is not tied to a specific time; performance at any time fulfills the obligation. Examples include zakat, sadaqat al-fitr, and kaffarat. Note that promptness is recommended but not obligatory.
The key feature: the command does not expire with the passage of time.
3
Understand the definition of muqayyad
A muqayyad command has a specified time window; if that time passes, performance is no longer valid (except as qada'). Examples include daily prayers and Ramadan fasting.
Both schools agree on this classification.
4
Analyze the dispute: tarakhi vs fawr
The author reports that some Hanafis (e.g., al-Karkhi) and Shafi'is (e.g., al-Ghazali) hold that absolute commands must be performed immediately (fawr). The majority Hanafi position (tarakhi) is defended by the author.
The thamarah al-khilaf: whether one sins by delaying until near death.
5
Examine the Hanafi proofs for tarakhi
The passage provides rational arguments: (1) the command seeks future occurrence, not immediate; (2) specifying a time would destroy absoluteness; (3) absoluteness is for ease, so fawr would cause hardship; (4) fawr would entail qada' instead of ada', contrary to consensus. Also cited are scriptural commands (Quran 2:43, hadith on fitra and 'ushr) whose general form indicates lack of time specification.
The condition 'not missing in one's lifetime' is established by predominant probability, not certainty.

Chapter on the Commanded Act. The commanded act is of two types: absolute with respect to time and time-bound. [Commentary begins]... The absolute with respect to time is that which is not tied to a limited time such that the command expires with its expiration, like the command for zakat, 'ushr, sadaqat al-fitr, expiations, absolute vows, and the like. Each of these is not restricted to a time that expires with its expiration; rather, whenever one performs it, it is performance, although promptness is recommended. Some of our companions, like Abu al-Hasan al-Karkhi, and from the Shafi'is, Abu Bakr al-Sayrafi and Abu Hamid al-Ghazali, held that it is obligatory immediately as a precaution for the matter of worship, meaning that one sins by delaying. According to us, one does not sin except at the end of one's life or upon seeing signs of death without having performed it. The time-bound is that for which the Law has specified a time for its performance such that performance expires with its expiration, like prayer, fasting, and specified vows. The ruling of the absolute: performance is obligatory with deferment, on condition that one does not miss it in one's lifetime. Based on this, Imam Muhammad said in al-Jami' al-Kabir: If one vows to observe i'tikaf for a month, he may observe it in any month he wishes. Likewise, if he vows to fast a month, he may fast any month he wishes. In zakat, sadaqat al-fitr, and 'ushr, the known madhhab is that he does not become negligent by delaying. Then a response to an objection: The meaning of absolute is lack of specification by time, and muqayyad is specification. The ruling on tarakhi: it is permissible to delay to any time the commanded act is performed. This is the madhhab of the majority of our companions, while the general body of hadith scholars and some Mu'tazila hold it is immediate. Abu Sahl al-Zajjaji reported that according to Abu Yusuf it is immediate, while according to Muhammad and al-Shafi'i it is on tarakhi. It is also narrated from Abu Hanifa that it is immediate. The correct view is what we have stated, because the command is for seeking the occurrence of the masdar in the future, and the specification of time is only due to the specification of matter, like 'buy meat' based on the assumption that he needs to eat it today. The command seeks the occurrence of an absolute act, so compliance is achieved by performing it in any future part. If it were immediate, it would be time-bound and not absolute, which is the assumption. Also, the absoluteness of the command from time restriction is for ease and facility, so if it were interpreted as immediate, it would contradict its purpose, become harder than the time-bound, and remove ease. Also, if the command were immediate, the act after the immediate time would no longer be commanded, and performing it would be a make-up (qada') instead of performance (ada'), contrary to consensus. Regarding the condition that it not be missed: a person does not know his lifespan, so conditioning permission to delay on not missing it is conditioning on something unknowable. The response: This is known by predominant probability (ghalabat al-zann). Sudden death is rare and does not form the basis for rulings. The absence of negligence is due to the absoluteness of the command for zakat (Quran 2:43), sadaqat al-fitr (hadith: 'Give on behalf of every free person and slave'), and 'ushr (hadith: 'What is watered by the sky, one-tenth is due'). Each of these is absolute with respect to time, so one does not become negligent by delaying.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed explanation of the classification of commanded acts into absolute (mutlaq) and time-bound (muqayyad). It begins by stating the two types. Then it provides a lengthy commentary clarifying the nature of absolute commands: they are acts like zakat, fitra, expiations, and absolute vows that are not tied to a specific deadline; performing them at any time fulfills the command, though promptness is recommended. A dispute is noted: some Hanafis and Shafi'is argue for immediate performance (fawr), but the author sides with the view of delay (tarakhi) for absolute commands, citing Imam Muhammad's example from al-Jami' al-Kabir about vows. The passage then defends the tarakhi position with several rational arguments: (1) the command seeks occurrence of the event in the future, not necessarily immediately; (2) specifying a time for an absolute command would make it time-bound, contradicting its nature; (3) the absoluteness is for ease, so requiring immediacy would cause hardship; (4) if immediate were required, then performing after the first moment would be qada' instead of ada', which is contrary to consensus. It also addresses a condition: the postponement is allowed only on condition that one does not miss the act in one's lifetime. The author responds that this is determined by predominant probability; sudden death is rare and not considered in rulings. Finally, the passage supports the absolute nature of zakat, fitra, and 'ushr by citing the general commands from Quran and hadith, concluding that delaying these does not make one negligent.

مقيد بالوقت (muqayyad bi al-waqt): A commanded act for which a specific time is fixed; performance outside that time is qada'.

Absolute Command and Human Restriction: The Hanafi Rule on Mutlaq
Explaining the effect of human restriction on absolute commands

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that an absolute (mutlaq) command from the Lawgiver cannot be restricted by human action, and distinguish between rights of the Law (huquq al-shar') and rights of the servant.

Core Idea
Human restriction cannot override a Lawgiver's absolute command; only the Lawgiver can restrict.

In this passage from Usul al-Shashi, we explore a foundational Hanafi principle: when the Lawgiver issues an absolute command (mutlaq), no human can restrict it. However, supererogatory acts belong to the servant, so he can direct them. This distinction has practical implications, such as the wife's housing during idda being an unwaivable right of the Law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the absolute command
Find the commands that are left unrestricted by the Lawgiver (qada fast, kaffara fast). Note that the Lawgiver did not specify a particular day.
These commands are mutlaq because the textual evidence does not restrict them.
2
Understand the objection
The objector points out that supererogatory fasting is also absolute, yet the fast on a vowed day counts as the vow, not nafl. Why does the servant's intention change the outcome here?
This objection tests the consistency of the rule.
3
Apply the distinction between rights
Recognize that obligations (vow, qada, kaffara) are fixed by the Law; the servant cannot alter them. Supererogatory acts are the servant's right; he can direct them.
This distinction resolves the objection.
4
Extend to the khul' example
See how the same principle applies: housing during idda is a right of the Law (cannot be waived), while maintenance is a contractual exchange (can be waived).
This shows the principle's applicability beyond ibadat.
5
Formulate the rule
State the rule concisely: 'An absolute command from the Lawgiver remains absolute unless the Lawgiver restricts it; human actions (intention, stipulation) cannot change the absoluteness. However, for supererogatory acts (right of servant), the servant's intention is effective.'
This rule is a key tool in usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling that examines the interaction between absolute (mutlaq) commands from the Lawgiver and human attempts to restrict them. The author argues that if the Lawgiver has made a command absolute (e.g., the time for making up missed fasts is unrestricted), a human cannot change that by restricting it to a specific day. An objection is raised: what if someone intends a supererogatory fast on a day reserved for a vowed fast? The fast counts as the vowed fast, not the supererogatory, although both are absolute. The author explains that supererogatory acts are the servant's right; he can direct them as he wishes, but obligations (like vow, qada, kaffara) are fixed by the Law and cannot be altered. This principle is then applied to a case from khul': spouses cannot by agreement eliminate the wife's right to housing during the waiting period because it is a fixed right of the Law, unlike maintenance which is a contractual exchange. The discussion relies on Zahir al-Riwayah (authority weight) as it draws from al-Ma'dan and al-Fusul, standard Hanafi texts.

Key Terms
المطلق (al-mutlaq): The command that is unrestricted by time or condition in the Lawgiver's text.
النفل (al-nafl): Supererogatory acts; voluntary worship that the servant can direct as he wishes.
حق الشرع (haqq al-shar'): A right established by the Law that individuals cannot alter or waive.

ولو قال لامرأته: "إن خَرَجْتِ من الدارِ إلا بإذني فأنت كذا" تحتاج إلى الإذن كلّ مرَّة؛ في الخروج إذ المستثنى خروج ملصق بالإذن، فلو خرجت في المرة الثانية بدون الإذن طلقت.

أنت طالق بمشيَّة إلخ أي لم تطلق امرأة أصلاً؛ لأنه بمعنى الشرط؛ لأنه لما جعل الطلاق ملصقاً بالمشية لا يقع قبل المشية، وهذا هو معنى الشرط؛ إذ لا وجود للمشروط بدون وجود الشرط غير أن هذا الشرط مما لا يتوقف عليه فلا يقع الطلاق كذا في "المعدن".
لم تطلق: لأن الإلصاق بمشية الله تعالى قيد بوقوع الطلاق، والمقيد لا يوجد ولا يتم وجوده بدون القيد خارجاً كان أو داخلاً، ووجود القيد متردد فيه غير معلوم، فلا يقع بدون العلم كما في جهالة الشرط في المعلق عليه، فهذه الباء أفادت معنى التعليق إفادة بالعرض كذا في "الفصول".
Section on the letter Ba: When the letter Ba enters upon the substitute in the chapter of sale, it indicates that it is a dependent attached to the principal, so it is not a sold item, but becomes a price. Based on this we said: If one says 'I sold you this slave for a kurr of wheat' and describes it, the slave is the sold item and the kurr is the price, so substitution before possession is permissible. If one says 'I sold you a kurr of wheat' and describes it for this slave, the slave is the price and the kurr is the sold item, and the contract is a salam contract valid only with a deferred term. Our scholars said: If one says to his slave 'If you inform me of Zayd's arrival, you are free' then it is conditional on truthful news so that the news is attached to the arrival; if he lies, he is not freed. If he says 'If you inform me that Zayd arrived, you are free' then it is on absolute news; if he lies, he is freed. If one says to his wife 'If you leave the house except with my permission, you are such-and-such' she needs permission every time, because the exception is a leaving attached to permission; if she leaves a second time without permission, she is divorced. In al-Ziyadat: If he says 'You are divorced by the will of Allah' or 'by the desire of Allah' or 'by His decree', she is not divorced. 'You are divorced by the will' etc. means she is not divorced at all, because it is in the sense of a condition; since he made the divorce attached to the will, it does not occur before the will, and this is the meaning of a condition, for there is no existence of the conditioned without the existence of the condition, except that this condition is not suspended upon it, so the divorce does not occur as in al-Ma'din. 'She is not divorced': because the attachment to the will of Allah is a restriction on the occurrence of the divorce, and the restricted does not exist and its existence is not complete without the restriction, whether external or internal, and the existence of the restriction is uncertain and unknown, so it does not occur without knowledge, like ignorance of the condition in the suspended matter; thus this Ba conveys the meaning of suspension indirectly as in al-Fusul.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis applying the usul principle of the letter Ba (باء) to concrete fiqh cases. The core idea is that Ba indicates attachment (الإلصاق), making the following element dependent on the preceding one. In sale contracts, when Ba enters upon the substitute (بدل), it marks that item as the price (ثمن), not the sold object (مبيع). The examples clarify: if Ba is on the commodity (e.g., 'sold you this slave for a kurr'), the slave is sold, the kurr is price; if Ba is on the price ('sold you a kurr for this slave'), the kurr becomes the sold item (making it a salam contract, requiring deferment). For manumission, the Ba in 'inform me of arrival' restricts freedom to truthful news (attachment to actual arrival), so a false report does not free. Without Ba ('inform me that he arrived'), the slave is freed on any news, even false. For divorce, 'except with my permission' (استثناء بإذني) attaches each leaving to permission, requiring permission each time; a second unauthorized leave triggers divorce. The Ziyadat ruling shows that 'divorced by the will of Allah' uses Ba to attach divorce to the divine will, making it conditional on that will—since the will is unknown, the divorce does not occur. The commentary emphasizes that this Ba conveys suspension (تعليق), similar to a condition that is not verified, so the ruling is suspended indefinitely.

Key Terms
الباء (al-ba'): The letter Ba indicates attachment (الإلصاق), making the noun following it dependent on or attached to the preceding noun or action.

The Vinegar Case: Distinguishing Khabath from Hadath
Distinguishing between purification from physical impurity and ritual impurity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between khabath (physical impurity) and hadath (ritual impurity) by identifying their respective causes, and apply the Hanafi rule that a liquid other than water can remove khabath but cannot remove hadath.

Core Idea
Vinegar removes the cause of khabath (physical impurity) but cannot remove hadath; hadath requires a specific purifier—water.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi applies a foundational principle of Hanafi fiqh: rulings cease when their causes cease. The specific case—washing a najis garment with vinegar—illustrates the critical distinction between khabath (physical impurity) and hadath (ritual impurity). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying purification rules in daily life and fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of impurity
Recognize that the passage deals with two distinct categories: khabath (physical impurity, e.g., blood, urine) and hadath (ritual impurity, e.g., state requiring wudu or ghusl).
Khabath is also called najasah 'ayniyyah; hadath is najasah hukmiyyah.
2
Determine the cause of each
For khabath, the cause ('illa) of impurity is the physical presence of the najasah on the place. For hadath, the cause is a legal state that is not physically perceptible. The removal of hadath is legally defined by the use of a specific purifier (mutahhir).
The cause of khabath is concrete; the cause of hadath is abstract.
3
Apply the principle: the ruling follows the cause
When the cause (physical presence) of khabath is eliminated (e.g., by washing with vinegar), the impurity ceases. For hadath, the cause is not removed by merely removing a physical substance; it requires the mutahhir (water) as designated by the Lawgiver.
This is the principle: al-hukm yaduru ma'a al-illa wujudan wa 'adaman.
4
Analyze the effect of vinegar
Vinegar can remove the physical substance (najasah) from a garment, thus purifying it from khabath. However, vinegar cannot serve as the mutahhir for hadath because the Shari'ah has specified water (and earth for tayammum) for that purpose.
The passage explicitly states: 'vinegar does not effect the purification of the place; rather, the purifier effects it, and that is water.'

Commentary
This passage applies the principle that rulings cease when their causes cease. The specific case: washing a najis (impure) garment with vinegar removes the physical impurity (najasah). Since the cause of the garment's impurity is the presence of the najasah, once the najasah is gone, the garment becomes tahir (pure) from khabath. However, this does not mean vinegar can substitute for water in removing hadath (ritual impurity). The difference lies in the cause: for khabath, the cause of impurity is the physical presence of najasah; removal ends it. For hadath, the cause is not physical presence but a legal state that requires a specific purifier (mutahhir), which is water in the case of wudu and ghusl. Vinegar can remove the physical substance but does not fulfill the legal condition of using water. Thus, vinegar cleans the garment from najasah but cannot be used for ritual purification. The commentator emphasizes that this distinction is based on the 'illa (cause): the cause for purification from khabath is removal of the impure substance, while for hadath it is the use of a specific purifier (water).

Key Terms
حدث (hadath): Ritual impurity that prevents acts of worship; requires wudu or ghusl with water.
خبث (khabath): Physical impurity (najasa) that can be removed by any purifying liquid that removes the substance.
مطهر (mutahhir): A purifier that legally removes hadath; primarily water according to the text.

Invalid Qiyas That Changes a Textual Ruling (Taghyir al-Hukm)
Second type of invalid qiyas that alters a ruling of the text

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a qiyas that alters a textual ruling from absolute to restricted and explain why it is invalid in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
Qiyas cannot impose conditions on an absolute text—that would be abrogation.

This lesson continues our study of invalid qiyas. The second type is particularly important: qiyas that attempts to add a condition to an absolute command from the Quran or Sunnah, thereby changing the ruling. We examine two classic examples: requiring intention for wudu by analogy with tayammum, and requiring purity for tawaf by analogy with prayer based on a hadith. The Hanafi position is that such qiyas amounts to naskh (abrogation) and is unanimously prohibited.

Guided Path
1
Identify the absolute text
Determine the Quranic or hadith text that states a ruling in absolute terms without conditions.
For wudu: Quran 5:6 gives commands without mentioning intention. For tawaf: Quran 22:29 commands circumambulation absolutely.
2
Identify the proposed qiyas
Find the analogical argument that seeks to add a condition not present in the absolute text.
Examples: intention for wudu by qiyas on tayammum; purity and covering for tawaf by qiyas on prayer using the hadith.
3
Recognize the change in ruling
The qiyas would restrict the absolute, thus changing the original ruling. This is taghyir al-hukm.
The absolute implied the act (wudu/tawaf) is valid without the condition; adding the condition negates that.
4
Apply the Hanafi principle
Such qiyas is invalid because it amounts to naskh of the textual ruling, and naskh by qiyas is prohibited by consensus.
Only the Lawgiver can abrogate; qiyas is a human tool that cannot override clear texts.
