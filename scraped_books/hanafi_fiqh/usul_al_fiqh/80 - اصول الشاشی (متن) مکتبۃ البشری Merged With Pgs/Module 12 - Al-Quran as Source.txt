Explicit vs. Implicit Evidence: The Tayammum Dispute
Usul dispute: explicit vs. implicit evidence in tayammum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify explicit (صريح) and implicit (كناية) evidence in the tayammum dispute and explain how the Hanafi school prioritizes explicit text over implicit reasoning.

Core Idea
Explicit Quranic text trumps implicit analogical reasoning: tayammum is absolute purification, not a mere coverer.

This passage uses the disagreement over the nature of tayammum to illustrate a fundamental usul principle: when an explicit text (nass) exists, implicit reasoning (kinaayah) cannot override it. The opponent argues that the return of hadath upon finding water proves tayammum is only a coverer; Hanafis counter with the explicit command 'and to purify you' (Quran 5:6), and explain the return of hadath as a result of a lapsed condition, not a flaw in tayammum's purifying nature.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Issue and Two Positions
Read the first few lines to find the dispute: is tayammum an absolute purification (طهارة مطلقة) or merely a coverer (ساتر) of hadath? Note the Hanafi position and the opponent's.
The opponent's view is introduced with 'والآخر' – 'and the other [view]'.
2
Examine the Opponent’s Implicit Evidence
Find the opponent's proof: the return of hadath upon seeing water. Understand why they claim this implies tayammum does not remove hadath – because if it were removed, it could not return.
This is an implicit (kinaayah) inference from a later event.
3
Analyse the Hanafi Explicit Evidence
Locate the Hanafi proof: the Quranic phrase 'وَليُطَهِّرَكُم' (and to purify you) and the hadith 'الصعيد الطيب وضوء المسلم'. These are explicit (sarih) texts directly bestowing purification on tayammum.
The Hanafi argument is that explicit text cannot be abandoned for implicit reasoning.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi Response to the Opponent
See how the Hanafi school rebuts the return-of-hadath argument: the inability to use water is a condition (shart) for tayammum both initially and continuously. When water is found, the condition lapses, so tayammum ends and the original hadath reappears.
This preserves the explicit text's force while explaining the observed phenomenon.
5
Derive the Practical Consequences (Thamarah al-Khilaf)
The text lists several subsidiary rulings that differ based on the two views. For example, performing multiple obligatory prayers with one tayammum, leading prayer as a mutayammim, etc. Note how each flows from the view of tayammum's nature.
Understanding the thamarah shows why this usul dispute matters for fiqh.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute about the nature of tayammum, serving as an example of how explicit (صريح) and implicit (كناية) evidence are used in deriving rulings. The author first presents the opponent's view: tayammum is not a true purification but merely a coverer (ساتر) of the state of ritual impurity (حدث). Then lists several subsidiary issues where the two views differ: permissibility before prayer time, performing two obligatory prayers with one tayammum, the imamah of a mutayammim for those with wudu, permissibility without fear of loss, permissibility for Eid and funeral prayers, and the need for intention. The opponent cites the hadith 'Pure earth is the wudu of a Muslim' to support the covering view. The Hanafi response is that the explicit Quranic text (وَليُطَهِّرَكُم) establishes tayammum as an absolute purification. The return of hadath upon seeing water is explained not as a sign that tayammum is not purification, but because the condition of inability to use water is essential both initially and continuously; once water is found, the condition is lost and tayammum ends, thus the original hadath reappears. The Hanafi position also has different rulings for the subsidiary issues, as summarized in the text.

Key Terms
طهارة مطلقة (tahara mutlaqa): Absolute purification that removes hadath, as claimed for tayammum by Hanafis.
ساتر للحدث (satir li al-hadath): That which covers the state of ritual impurity without removing it.
صريح (sarih): Explicit or clear text that leaves no room for ambiguity regarding the intended meaning.
كناية (kinaayah): Implicit speech or indirect evidence that requires inference to reach the intended meaning.

Defining al-Nass and al-Zahir in Hanafi Usul
Defines nass via matn and hashia with Quranic example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-nass and al-zahir, distinguish them using the Quranic example, and identify the role of contextual indication (qarina) in elevating zahir to nass.

Core Idea
Nass is clearer than zahir because the speaker's intended meaning is known through contextual indication.

In Hanafi usul, not all clear texts are equally clear. This lesson introduces two key categories: al-zahir (apparent) and al-nass (explicit). The difference is subtle but crucial: while both are clear, nass gains additional clarity from the speaker's context. You will see this through the Quranic verse on trade and usury, where the same wording is nass for one purpose and zahir for another.

Guided Path
1
Learn the basic definition of nass
Read the matn definition: 'nass is what the speech is intended for'. Understand that nass means the text directly addresses its intended meaning, not just any possible meaning.
This is the core idea: the text is framed to convey a specific message.
2
Learn the technical definition from the hashia
The hashia adds that nass is clearer than zahir due to a meaning in the speaker's mind, known only through contextual indications (qarina). This contextual clue increases clarity.
Zahir also has clarity, but nass has extra clarity from context.
3
Analyze the Quranic example (Q.2:275)
The verse distinguishes trade and usury. The context: disbelievers claimed equality of trade and usury. The verse was revealed to refute that, making the distinction the intended meaning. Hence the verse is nass for the distinction but zahir for the permissibility of trade and prohibition of usury, as those rulings are clear from the wording alone.
The same wording can be both nass and zahir for different purposes.
4
Distinguish nass from zahir and sarih
The text explains that sarih has complete clarity due to frequent usage; nass has complete clarity due to context; zahir has mere clarity without completeness. This clarifies the hierarchy.
Sarih is like 'divorce' which is clear from common use; nass is clear from context; zahir is clear but open to interpretation.
5
Apply the concept to a hypothetical
Consider the verse 'And take witnesses when you contract a debt' (Q.2:282). Is it nass for commanding witnesses or zahir? The context of protecting rights suggests it is nass for the command.
Practice applying the definitions to new cases.

Nass and Zahir in the Marriage Verse
Analyzes the marriage verse as nass for number and zahir for permissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the nass and zahir aspects in a Quranic verse and apply the Hanafi hierarchy to distinguish between the two.

Core Idea
A verse can be nass for one meaning (count) and zahir for another (permissibility), depending on the purpose of the speech.

This lesson takes you through the famous Hanafi analysis of Quran 4:3, showing how the same verse can contain both nass (definitive intended meaning) and zahir (apparent meaning) simultaneously. You will learn to recognize the textual cues that signal each category and understand why this distinction matters for deriving legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the verse and its structure
Start by recognizing Quran 4:3: 'فَانكِحُوا مَا طَابَ لَكُم مِّنَ النِّسَاءِ مَثْنَىٰ وَثُلَاثَ وَرُبَاعَ'. Note that it contains a general permission ('ma taba lakum') and a specific numerical phrase ('mathna wa thulatha wa ruba' ).
The verse is part of a longer passage on marriage.
2
Define nass and zahir in Hanafi usul
Nass is a text that is clearly intended to convey a specific meaning; zahir is a text whose meaning is apparent from the wording but may be open to qualification. Apply these definitions to parts of the verse.
Reference the key terms: nass and zahir.
3
Locate the nass element
The phrase 'mathna wa thulatha wa ruba' (two, three, four) specifies the maximum number of wives. The speech is primarily intended to clarify this number, so it is nass regarding the count.
The count is the primary purpose (masuq al-kalam li-bayanihi).
4
Identify the zahir element
The general permission to marry ('ma taba lakum') is known 'by mere hearing' (bi-nafs al-sama'), meaning the permissibility is apparent but not the main purpose; thus it is zahir.
Zahir may be subject to further qualification from other texts.
5
Apply the distinction to derive the ruling
From nass, the number limit (up to four) is definitively established (qat'i). From zahir, the permissibility is established but could be restricted by other prohibitions (e.g., unrelated women).
This distinction informs how a mujtahid weighs evidence.

Definition of al-Amr and Its Five Necessary Types
Defines al-amr and introduces its five necessary types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-amr linguistically and list its five necessary types, explaining why each element is integral to the legal analysis of commands.

Core Idea
Al-amr is the foundational speech act of Shari'ah — understanding its five dimensions unlocks the entire structure of obligation.

In this lesson, we explore the opening of the chapter on al-amr (command). The author provides a crisp linguistic definition and then, with commentary from Kashf al-Manar, explains that issues related to command necessarily fall into five categories. This classification is not arbitrary; it is logically demanded by the nature of command itself. By mastering this framework, you gain a systematic tool for analyzing any imperative statement in the Quran and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic definition of al-amr
The passage states that al-amr linguistically is 'a speaker's statement to another: do'. This is the foundational definition that all usul scholars accept.
The definition presupposes a subordinate relationship (taht al-amr).
2
Understand the placement and precedence of the chapter
The author placed this chapter after general/specific because command and prohibition are specific categories. He gave precedence to command over prohibition because command is existential (requires action) and is the first divine address ('Be' – kun).
This reflects the nobility of existence over non-existence.
3

According to Kashf al-Manar, the issues of command necessarily fall into five categories: (1) the command itself and its requirement, (2) the commanded action, (3) the time of the action, (4) the commanded person (mukallaf), and (5) the commander.
This division is exhaustive for any command.
4
Explain the logical necessity of this fivefold division
Every command must have a commander, a commanded action, a time, a person commanded, and the command itself. If any of these is unknown or ambiguous, the legal analysis is incomplete.
This is a 'necessary division' (taqsim daruri) as per the text.
5

Take the command 'Aqimu al-salah' (establish prayer). Identify the five elements: the command is the imperative verb; the action is the act of prayer; the time is the prescribed prayer times; the person is the mukallaf; the commander is Allah.
This application shows the practical utility of the five types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three meanings of husn/qabih, state the Ash'ari and Maturidi positions on the third meaning, and explain the Hanafi counter-argument using the medical analogy.

Core Idea
Goodness of commanded acts is rational in essence but legally established for obligation.

When a wise commander issues a command, it implies the commanded act is good. But is that goodness inherent in the act or purely a product of the command? Classical usul scholars debated this. In this passage, the author presents the dispute between the Ash'aris who hold goodness as purely legal, and the Maturidis (and Mu'tazila) who see it as rational. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping how reason and revelation interact in Hanafi legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three meanings of husn/qabih
The text lists three senses: (1) perfection/deficiency, (2) compatibility with worldly desire, (3) desert of reward/punishment. The first two are rational by agreement; only the third is disputed.
2
Understand the Ash'ari position
According to Ash'aris, before revelation acts are morally neutral. The Lawgiver's command creates goodness; if He had commanded the opposite, that would be good. There is no inherent moral value.
3

Maturidis affirm that acts have real, intrinsic goodness or badness. The Lawgiver reveals these realities, just as a physician discovers the benefits/harm of drugs. Reason can sometimes perceive them, but revelation is the primary guide.
The text also distinguishes Maturidis from Mu'tazila: for Maturidis, inherent goodness does not obligate Allah to reward/punish, while Mu'tazila hold it does.
4

This shows a practical compromise: goodness is real but its binding force comes from revelation.

The Command's Indication of Goodness: Rational or Legal?
Analyzing the rule and the nature of goodness in the commanded act.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi/Maturidi position on the nature of goodness and contrast it with the Ash'ari view, identifying the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A command from a wise authority proves the commanded act is good; the debate is whether this goodness is inherent or purely legal.

This lesson examines the fundamental usul rule that a command (amr) from a wise Lawgiver indicates the goodness (husn) of the commanded act. We then delve into the theological-epistemological dispute over whether this goodness is a real, rational quality of the act or a purely legal designation bestowed by the command. Understanding this dispute is crucial for grasping the Hanafi approach to istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its condition
The rule: 'Al-amru bi al-shay'i yadullu 'ala husni al-ma'muri bihi idha kana al-amiru hakiman.' The condition is that the commander be wise (hakim).
2
Understand the three meanings of husn
The passage distinguishes three senses of goodness: (1) perfection vs deficiency, (2) compatibility with disposition, (3) deserving reward/punishment. The third is the relevant sense for usul.
The first two are rational and agreed upon.
3
Map the dispute: Ash'ari vs Hanafi/Maturidi
The Ash'ari school holds that goodness is purely legal (shar'i) — an act is good only because the Lawgiver commands it. The Hanafi/Maturidi school holds that goodness is real and rational (aqli) — the Lawgiver commands it because it is good.
Both agree the command indicates goodness; they disagree on its ontological basis.
4
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof is aqli: the commander is wise (hakeem), and wisdom implies commanding only what has real benefit/harm. Thus, the commanded act must have real goodness.
This is analogous to medicine revealing real benefits of drugs.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if goodness is rational, acts are commanded because they are good (the Lawgiver's wisdom is tied to the act's nature). If legal, they are good because commanded (the Lawgiver's will creates goodness).
This affects how one views abrogation, istihsan, and the role of reason.

Commentary
This passage from Usool al-Shashi discusses a fundamental usul principle: that a command (amr) from a wise authority (hakeem) indicates the goodness (husn) of the commanded act (mamur bihi). The author begins by stating this rule, then provides an extensive commentary. The passage is a mix of matn and sharh, typical of classical usul works. The key idea is that the command itself implies that the commanded act is good, because a wise commander does not command pointless or harmful acts. The author then delves into the theological dispute regarding the nature of goodness: is it a rational (aqli) or legal (shar'i) quality? The Ash'aris hold that goodness and badness are purely legal categories, established by revelation; acts in themselves have no inherent moral value. The Maturidis (followed by the Hanafis) and the Mu'tazila hold that acts have real, intrinsic goodness or badness that revelation discloses, similar to how medicine reveals benefits or harms. However, the Maturidis differ from the Mu'tazila in that they do not hold that rational goodness/badness necessitates a ruling from Allah; rather, it becomes a cause for deserving reward or punishment. The author then gives the correct view as that of the Ash'aris (goodness is legal), but this represents a commentary note; the main text seems to favor the rational view, as it is attributed to the Maturidis. The passage then moves to types of goodness in the commanded act: good in itself (husn bi nafsihi) like faith, prayer, truthfulness, and good for another (husn li ghayrihi). The ruling for the first type is that if obligatory, it is not dropped except by performance, unless it admits of being dropped (like zakat in case of loss of nisab). Prayer is an example: once obligatory, it can be dropped by performance or by legal impediments (insanity, menstruation), but not by mere difficulty or lack of resources. The commentary notes the precise nature of obligation being established at the beginning of the time (expanded obligation) according to the author. The passage also includes a note on how reason cannot independently determine obligation; only revelation can. The discussion covers the Hanafi position implicitly through the Maturidi view.

Key Terms
حسن بنفسه (husn bi nafsihi): Good in itself; an act that is intrinsically good regardless of external factors.
حسن لغيره (husn li ghayrihi): Good for another; an act that is good because it leads to a good outcome or is a means to an end.
يسقط (yasqutu): It is dropped (of obligation); the obligation ceases or is removed.

Avoiding Takhsis: Interpreting 'Lamastum'
Analysis of a Hanafi usul principle with inter-madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that among two possible interpretations, the one that avoids specification (takhsis) is preferred, using the verse 'Aw lamastumu al-nisa' as an example.

Core Idea
Prefer the interpretation that lets the text apply universally—avoid unnecessary specification.

In this passage from Usul al-Shashi, a key Hanafi hermeneutical principle is introduced: when a text can bear two meanings, the meaning that avoids specification (takhsis) is to be preferred. The classical debate over the word 'lamastum' in the wudu verse illustrates this vividly. Understanding this principle is crucial for deriving rulings that give full effect to the Quranic text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two meanings of 'lamastum'
Recognize that 'lamastum' can mean either intercourse (al-wiqaa') or hand-touch (al-mass). Both are lexically possible.
The verse is Quran 5:6: 'Aw lamastumu al-nisa'.
2
Determine which meaning requires takhsis
If 'lamastum' means intercourse, the ruling applies uniformly: every instance of intercourse nullifies wudu. If it means hand-touch, many exceptions arise (touching mahrams, young children, etc.) – thus the text would need specification.
Al-Shafi'i's position requires takhsis for many cases.
3
Apply the Hanafi preference for avoiding takhsis
The Hanafi rule states: when one interpretation necessitates takhsis and the other does not, the latter is preferred. Therefore, 'lamastum' is interpreted as intercourse.
This preserves the general applicability of the text.
4
Compare with al-Shafi'i's position
Al-Shafi'i argues 'lamastum' literally means hand-touch. Hanafis respond that legal haqiqah (shar'iyyah) is like majaz relative to linguistic haqiqah, and the legal context here favors intercourse.
The dispute is methodological, not just lexical.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical consequence: In Hanafi fiqh, any direct physical contact with desire between spouses nullifies wudu. In Shafi'i fiqh, only hand-touch without barrier nullifies wudu.
This affects daily worship (prayer, touching mushaf).

Reconciling Dual Qira'at: The Feet Verse
Explanation of a principle for reconciling variant Quranic readings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of reconciling dual Quranic readings (qira'at) by assigning each to a specific context, using the example of 'arjulakum' to derive the ruling on wiping over khuffs.

Core Idea
Give effect to both authentic readings by assigning each to a different legal scenario.

The existence of multiple authentic qira'at for a single Quranic verse could be seen as a problem, but Hanafi usul treats it as an opportunity: both readings must be given effect. This passage from Usul al-Shashi demonstrates how the two readings of 'arjulakum' (nasb and khafd) are harmonized to derive the rule of wiping over khuffs while maintaining the obligation to wash feet in the normal state. Understanding this reconciliation is key to handling variant readings in a principled way.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two qira'at of 'arjulakum'
In Quran 5:6, the word 'arjulakum' is read with nasb (accusative) as 'arjulakum' and with khafd (genitive) as 'arjulikum'. The nasb reading suggests washing (following 'wujuhakum'), while khafd suggests wiping (following 'ru'usikum').
Both readings are mutawatir and equally authentic.
2
Understand the apparent conflict
Nasb implies obligation to wash, khafd implies obligation to wipe. If taken literally, they contradict. But both are divine revelation, so reconciliation is necessary.
Cannot reject either reading.
3
Apply the principle of reconciliation
Hanafis assign each reading to a different scenario: khafd (wiping) applies to the state of wearing khuffs (lightened duty), nasb (washing) applies to the normal state (full duty). This gives effect to both readings.
This is based on the sunnah which specifies wiping over khuffs.
4
Consider the role of sunnah
The sunnah of wiping over khuffs provides the context for the khafd reading. Without sunnah, reconciliation would be speculative. The principle is 'act upon both readings as much as possible'.
The majority of Hanafis hold that wiping over khuffs is established by sunnah, not directly by the Quran.
5
Compare with other positions
Some (e.g., Shi'a) take only the khafd reading and conclude wiping is always obligatory. Hanafis reject this because it neglects the authentic nasb reading. The Hanafi method is to harmonize, not select.
This illustrates the importance of not ignoring authentic variants.

The Conjunctive Waw and Absolute Conjunction in Hanafi Usul
Defining the waw as indicating absolute conjunction and refuting the Shafi'i claim of order

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the waw's meaning and refute the Shafi'i claim of order by analyzing linguistic and textual evidence.

Core Idea
The waw indicates absolute conjunction — shared ruling without order or simultaneity.

The conjunctive waw (واو) is central to understanding how legal texts connect obligations. The Particle 'Ila' in Time: Delay vs. Termination
Analysis of 'ila' for deferring legal effect until a specified limit.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the particle 'ila' in a time-bound legal statement and determine whether it indicates tawqit (termination) or ta'jil (deferment) according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
The particle 'ila' can either set a limit to an existing ruling or defer the ruling itself; the correct interpretation depends on the nature of the act.

In legal language, particles carry significant weight. The simple word 'ila' (up to) can radically change a ruling. In this lesson, we examine a Hanafi usul analysis of 'ila' when used with time, focusing on a divorce statement. Understanding this principle will sharpen your ability to interpret sharia texts and recognize how a small word can alter legal outcomes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the particle and its two possible meanings
Locate the particle 'ila' in the source text and recognize that it can signify either tawqit (termination of an existing state at a limit) or ta'jil (deferment of a ruling that would otherwise be immediate).
The author states: 'wa qad tufidu kalimat 'ila' ta'khir al-hukm ila al-ghaya'.
2
Understand the example and the Hanafi position
Consider the divorce statement 'anti taliq ila shahr'. According to the majority, the divorce does not occur immediately but is deferred to the end of the month because 'ila' here indicates delay.

The Particle 'ala in Hanafi Usul: Obligation, Substitution, and Condition
Defining the particle 'ala in Hanafi usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the primary and secondary legal meanings of the particle 'ala in Hanafi usul and apply contextual clues to determine which meaning is intended.

Core Idea
The particle 'ala primarily signifies obligation; its secondary meanings depend on context — substitution in sales and condition in pledges.

A single Arabic particle can carry multiple legal weights. In Hanafi usul, 'ala (على) is not a neutral preposition — its original sense of superiority gives it a default force of obligation. However, the same word can metaphorically mean 'with' in contracts or 'on condition' in pledges. This lesson unpacks those layers using the text of Usul al-Shashi, equipping you to read 'ala in legal texts with precision.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the primary meaning and its linguistic basis
The text states that 'ala is for ilzam (obligation), and its origin is the meaning of superiority (ta'alli) and highness. This etymological root connects physical elevation to legal binding — an obligation rests 'above' the obligor.
Remember: the default legal effect of 'ala is imposing a duty, unlike other prepositions.
2
2. Distinguish 'ala from other prepositions
The passage contrasts 'ala with 'indi (with me), ma'a (with), and qabli (before me). Saying 'for so-and-so upon me a thousand' (علي ألف) creates a debt; saying 'with me a thousand' ('indi) merely indicates possession, not obligation.
This distinction is crucial in contracts and acknowledgments (iqrar).
3
3. Apply to the aman example from al-Siyar al-Kabir
When a fortress commander says 'grant me safety on ('ala) ten people', the particle 'ala gives him superiority, so he retains the right to choose the ten. If he says 'and ten' (without 'ala), the grantor chooses. The presence or absence of 'ala changes the ruling.
Context and the presence of 'ala determine who has the power of selection.
4
4. Recognize secondary meanings: substitution and condition
In exchanges, 'ala can replace the preposition 'ba' (with) metaphorically, as in 'I sold you this on a thousand'. In pledges, it introduces a condition, as in the Quranic verse: 'they pledge allegiance to you on that they do not associate...' (على أن لا يشركن).
The shift to substitution is triggered by the context of a bilateral contract; the shift to condition is triggered by a conditional clause following 'ala.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed analysis of the letter 'ba' (باء) in the Arabic language as a legal tool in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The text comes from the matn of Usul al-Shashi, a foundational work in Hanafi jurisprudence. The author begins by stating the core rule: in its linguistic origin, the letter 'ba' indicates attachment (الإلصاق). This is not merely a grammatical observation but a legal principle with concrete fiqh consequences. The passage then demonstrates this by examining two areas: the laws of sales and conditional oaths/divorce. The text first refutes the Shafi'i and Maliki positions regarding the meaning of 'ba' in the Quranic verse of wiping the head (4:43). Imam al-Shafi'i argued that the 'ba' in 'wamshu bi ru'usikum' indicates partiality (التبعيض), meaning only part of the head must be wiped. Imam Malik held it is redundant (زائدة), implying the entire head. The Hanafi position, as stated here, is that 'ba' primarily signifies attachment, and any alternative meaning is metaphorical and thus not to be relied upon without strong evidence. The rule's application in sales is then presented: when the 'ba' enters upon one of the two items in a sale (e.g., 'I sold you this slave for a kurr of wheat'), the item preceded by 'ba' becomes the price (thaman) and the other becomes the sold item (mabee'). This determines legal consequences such as the permissibility of substitution before possession. The passage then applies the same principle to conditional statements: a master's statement to a slave, 'If you inform me of the arrival of so-and-so, you are free,' is interpreted as requiring truthful news because the 'ba' attaches the informing to the arrival, which must exist. In contrast, 'If you inform me that so-and-so arrived' without 'ba' makes the condition absolute, so even false news triggers freedom. Similarly, a husband's statement to his wife, 'If you leave the house except with my permission,' requires permission each time because the 'ba' in 'except with my permission' attaches the leaving to permission, so any unpermitted leaving triggers the divorce. Throughout, the text emphasizes that the primary meaning of 'ba' must be maintained unless strong evidence supports a metaphorical interpretation, a key principle of Hanafi usul.

Key Terms
الباء (al-ba'): The letter ba is a particle used for attachment (الإلصاق) in the Arabic language, meaning that the following word is attached or related to something else.
الإلصاق (al-ilsaq): The grammatical concept of attachment, meaning the connection and joining of one thing to another; the party preceded by ba is the attached (ملصق) and the other is the attached-to (ملصق به).
التبعيض (al-tab'id): The concept of indicating a part of something; Hanafi holds this is the function of the letter 'min', not 'ba', and using 'ba' for it would be metaphorical.

The Hierarchical Order of Sources: Quran First, Then Sunnah, Then Qiyas

Core Idea
The Quran is the primary source; no resort to qiyas if a clear text exists.

A mujtahid faced with a new incident must follow a strict hierarchy of sources: first the Quran, then the Sunnah, and only then qiyas. This foundational rule ensures that rulings are grounded in divine revelation before resorting to human reasoning.

Why Rulings Are Attached to Their Causes: The Manifest Cause
Explaining why rulings are attached to causes and the role of time as a manifest cause

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Rulings are hidden; manifest causes like time are signs revealing obligation, not efficient causes.

In this lesson, we explore a foundational Hanafi usul principle: legal rulings are attached to causes. But what kind of cause? The passage clarifies that the true cause is Allah's eternal command, while manifest causes (like time) are signs that indicate when obligations apply. This distinction is crucial for understanding how Hanafi jurists derive rulings from textual indicators.

Analyze the Quranic evidence
The verse 'Establish prayer at the decline of the sun' (17:78) uses the preposition 'lam' (liduluk). In Arabic, 'lam' primarily indicates causation. Thus, the time of decline is the cause for the command.
This is the key istinbat: the lam is for ta'lil (causation).
4
Distinguish between real and manifest cause
The real cause is Allah's eternal command (al-ijab al-qadim). The manifest cause (al-sabab al-zahiri) is the sign that reveals the ruling, e.g., time for prayer.
This distinction prevents theological confusion: time is not an efficient cause but an indicator.
5
Recognize the Hanafi definition of sabab
In Hanafi usul, sabab is a sign (amara) that reveals the ruling, not a necessitating cause. This is why the passage says 'the mashayikh chose the word sabab because it is more general.'
This aligns with the idea that causation is primarily from Allah.

Cause of Obligation of Fasting: Witnessing the Month
Deriving the cause of fasting from the Quranic verse and linguistic analysis of idafa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the sabab (cause) of the obligation of fasting from the Quranic verse (2:185) and explain how the Hanafi usul analysis of the idafa construction supports this derivation.

Core Idea
The month of Ramadan is the cause of fasting, as the idafa links effect to its cause.

This lesson examines the Quranic basis for the obligation of fasting. The verse 'So whoever among you witnesses the month, let him fast it' (2:185) establishes that the cause (sabab) of the obligation is the act of witnessing the month. The Hanafi school supports this by analyzing the linguistic construction of related phrases like 'sawm shahr Ramadan,' arguing that in an idafa, the second term (the month) is the cause of the first term (the fasting). This is a classic example of deriving a ruling from Quranic text with linguistic insight.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic verse and its primary command
Locate the verse (2:185) and note the imperative 'falyasumhu' attached to a condition. Understand that the command is directed to those who 'witness the month'.
2
Recognize 'witnessing' as the operative condition
The phrase 'man shahida minkum al-shahr' makes witnessing the month a necessary condition for the obligation. This condition is the key to identifying the cause.
Shuhud includes sensory sighting and reliable knowledge.
3
Understand the idafa principle
In a construct (idafa) like 'sawm shahr Ramadan', the addendum (shahr) is the cause of the added (sawm) because the idafa indicates specification and the strongest specification is between cause and effect.
This principle is from 'al-Ma'din' and 'al-Fusul' as cited in the text.
4
Apply the idafa principle to the verse
The verse itself uses 'al-shahr' with the command 'falyasumhu'. The implicit idafa in the wording reinforces that the month (when witnessed) is the cause of the obligation.
The combination of textual imperative and linguistic structure yields the sabab.
5
Conclude the sabab
Thus, the sabab is 'shuhud al-shahr' (witnessing the month) and not merely the arrival of Ramadan. This is a pure usul derivation relying on dalil naqli and language analysis.
This approach is typical of the Hanafi school in establishing causes for rulings.
