Priority of Haqiqah Over Majaz in Interpreting Texts
Defining the first method to determine intended meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi usul principle that the literal meaning (haqiqah) takes priority over the figurative (majaz) and apply it to textual interpretation.

Core Idea
The literal meaning is the default; only a clear contextual indicator can shift to the figurative.

This lesson introduces the first method (tariq) for determining what the Lawgiver intends through a text. When a word has both a literal and figurative meaning, the literal is the starting point. This principle governs many juristic rulings, including the notable case of the daughter born from zina.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Principle
When a word has both haqiqah (literal) and majaz (figurative), haqiqah is awla (more deserving/prioritized). This is the first rule in determining the murad.
The principle is axiomatic in Hanafi usul: al-haqiqah awla.
2
Understand the Condition
The priority holds only in the absence of a qarinah (contextual indicator) that would necessitate the figurative meaning. If a qarinah exists, the ruling shifts.
A qarinah can be verbal, situational, or rational.
3
Examine the Example
The Hanafi ruling on the daughter from zina: she is mahram to her biological father, so marriage is haram. This relies on taking 'bint' (daughter) literally.
The literal meaning applies even though conception was outside marriage.
4
Apply the Principle
In any text, first assume the literal meaning of the words. Only when a clear qarinah prevents the literal should you move to the figurative.
This principle is applied throughout Hanafi istinbat.
5
Reflect on Shart and Mani
The shart (condition) for haqiqah to apply is the absence of a qarinah. The mani (preventer) is the presence of a qarinah that necessitates majaz.
Understanding these two ensures correct application.

Commentary
This is a short but crucial usul definition lesson from the matn of Usul al-Shashi. The author opens a new section on the methods (turuq) by which the intended meaning (al-murad) of a shari text can be known. The first method stated is the well-known principle of language: when a word has both a literal meaning (haqiqah) and a figurative meaning (majaz), the literal meaning is given priority (al-haqiqah awla). This principle applies unless there is a contextual indicator (qarinah) that prevents taking the literal meaning and necessitates the figurative. The example provided is the Hanafi position on a specific case: the daughter born from zina (a child conceived out of wedlock) is considered the biological daughter of the fornicator (zani) and therefore she is mahram to him; it is haram for him to marry her. The term 'daughter' (bint) here is taken in its literal sense (haqiqah) even though she is born from zina, because the word 'daughter' applies literally to a female offspring regardless of the circumstances of conception. If one were to take it figuratively, one might argue she is not his daughter because no valid marriage exists. The commentary following the example explains the chapter heading itself.

Key Terms
الحقيقة (al-haqiqah): The literal, original meaning of a word as coined in the language.
المجاز (al-majaz): A figurative, derived meaning of a word used in a non-original sense with a contextual indicator.
المراد بالنصوص (al-murad bi-al-nusus): The intended meaning that the Lawgiver (Shari') aims to convey through the texts.

The Rule of 'Bal' in Hanafi Usul: Correcting Mistakes in Insha and Ikhbar
Analyzes the linguistic and legal operation of the particle 'bal'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the effects of 'bal' in insha (creative acts) and ikhbar (reports), and apply the Hanafi rule to determine the number of divorces or the amount owed in acknowledgment cases.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, 'bal' corrects mistakes only in reports; in creative acts, the first effect stands and the second is ignored.

The particle 'bal' is a linguistic tool used to correct a mistake by replacing the first statement with the second. However, its legal effect depends on whether the speech is a creative act (insha) or a report (ikhbar). In this lesson, we will parse the rule of 'bal' from al-Shashi's text, applying it to divorce and acknowledgment, and examine an intra-Hanafi dispute with Zufar. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly deriving rulings in cases where a speaker attempts to correct an earlier utterance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic function of 'bal'
Read the opening definition: 'بل لتدارك الغلط بإقامة الثاني مقام الأول'. Understand that 'bal' is used to correct a mistake by putting the second statement in place of the first.
This is a general linguistic rule, but its legal application depends on the type of speech.
2
Distinguish between insha and ikhbar
In the passage, the author differentiates between creative acts (insha) like divorce, and reports (ikhbar) like acknowledgment. In insha, once uttered, the effect is irreversible; in ikhbar, correction is possible.
This distinction is the key to the different rulings in the examples.
3
Apply the rule to divorce cases
Consider the two scenarios: non-consummated wife (no mahall for further divorces) and consummated wife (mahall exists). For the non-consummated wife, the first divorce falls irreversibly, so the second is ignored. For the consummated wife, all three divorces occur because the mahall persists and the first cannot be undone.
The result is 1 divorce for non-consummated, 3 for consummated, regardless of 'bal'.
4
Apply the rule to acknowledgment (iqrar)
In iqrar, the first acknowledgment is a binding report. 'Bal' attempts to correct, but since it's a report, correction is possible without invalidating the first. Thus the second amount is added to the first, not replacing it.
Result: if one says 'I owe 1000, no rather 2000', total due is 2000 (addition), not 3000 (addition of both) as Zufar claims.
5
Resolve the intra-Hanafi dispute with Zufar
Zufar holds that 'bal' negates the first and affirms the second, but since the first cannot be negated, both stand, resulting in 3000. The Hanafi majority position is that the first remains and the second is an addition, so only 2000. The text supports the majority with the hadith 'A person is bound by his admission.'
This disagreement shows how subtle differences in linguistic interpretation affect fiqh outcomes.

Commentary
This passage from al-Shashi discusses the linguistic particle 'bal' (بل) and its legal implications. The author states that 'bal' is used to correct a mistake by replacing the first statement with the second. He then applies this to divorce (talaq) and acknowledgment (iqrar). 

In divorce, if a man says to his non-consummated wife: 'You are divorced one, no rather two', only one divorce occurs. This is because the word 'bal' aims to replace the first statement, but in the case of in shaa (creation), such as divorce, the first utterance is already effective and cannot be undone; the second statement is therefore ignored. For a consummated wife, three divorces occur because there is a valid subject (mahall) for all three; the mistake cannot be corrected as it was in the first instance. 

In contrast, in acknowledgment (iqrar), if someone says: 'I owe you one thousand, no rather two thousand', only two thousand are owed (not three). This is because acknowledgment is an ikhbar (report) which can be corrected. The first thousand is not invalidated, and the second thousand is added to it, resulting in a total of two thousand. The Hanafi position is supported by the statement of Ali (may Allah be pleased with him): 'A person is bound by his admission.' However, Zufar (another Hanafi scholar) disagrees and says three thousand are owed, treating 'bal' as negation of the first and affirmation of the second, thus requiring the first to be negated and the second affirmed separately, leading to three thousand. The Hanafi response is that in iqrar, the first utterance remains valid because correction is possible, so it is an addition rather than a replacement. This distinction between in shaa (like divorce) and ikhbar (like iqrar) is crucial: in creative acts, once something is created, it cannot be unmade; in reports, correction is possible.

Key Terms
بل (bal): A particle used to correct a mistake by replacing the first statement with the second (لتدارك الغلط بإقامة الثاني مقام الأول).
تدارك الغلط (tadaruk al-ghalat): The act of correcting a mistake by substituting the correct information.
إقرار (iqrar): Acknowledgment or admission of a right or liability; considered an ikhbar (report) in which correction of error is permitted.
إنشاء (insha): Creative speech that brings about a legal effect (e.g., divorce); once uttered, it cannot be reversed by the speaker.

Silence in Context of Need as Explanation: The Hanafi Principle
Stating the principle with applications to nukūl and ijma'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that silence in a context requiring explanation is equivalent to explanation, apply it to the case of nukul (defendant's refusal to take an oath), and evaluate the Shafi'i objection regarding ambiguity.

Core Idea
Silence where explanation is needed functions as an explicit statement—otherwise harm results.

This passage from Uṣūl al-Shāshī articulates a foundational Hanafi rule: when a ruling or statement is expected and silence occurs, that silence is legally treated as an explanation. The author uses the court case of a defendant's refusal to swear an oath (nukūl) to illustrate the rule, then extends it to the formation of silent consensus (ijma' sukūtī). This principle is not without challenge—Imam al-Shafi'i questioned whether ambiguous silence can be evidence, and the Hanafi response rests on preventing harm and deception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle and its condition
Locate the key sentence in the text: 'السكوت في موضع الحاجة إلى البيان بمنزلة البيان'. Note that the principle is qualified by 'في موضع الحاجة' (in a place of need); silence only counts as explanation when there is a clear need for it.
Contrast with silence without need, which has no legal effect.
2
Understand the nukül application
The text explains that if a defendant refuses to take an oath (nukūl) when required, his silence is treated as consent (riḍā) to the claim. This is an example of the principle: there is a need for him to deny, so his silence is explanatory.
Note the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf: is it by way of iqrār (admission) or badhl (surrender)?
3
Examine the Shafi'i objection
The text quotes al-Shafi'i arguing that silence cannot be definitive because it could be due to anger or aversion rather than consent. Identify the Hanafi response: if silence were not treated as permission, it would lead to harm (ghurūr) and deception, which are prohibited by hadith.
This dispute shows the importance of teleological reasoning (maqāṣid) in Hanafi usul.
4
Extend the principle to ijma' sukūtī
The author states that communal silence after some scholars have spoken, with knowledge and opportunity to object, constitutes ijma'. This is a direct application of the same principle: when explanation is needed (a new incident), silence from qualified scholars indicates agreement.
The passage mentions a reflection period of three days.
5
Internalize the qualification
The text emphasizes that the principle only applies when there is a need. The example of the Lawgiver prescribing amputation for theft but not mentioning other penalties shows that silence without need is not explanatory.
This prevents overextension of the rule.

Commentary
This passage from Uṣūl al-Shāshī presents a key Hanafi legal principle: silence in a context where explanation is needed is treated as an explanation itself. The author begins by discussing the case of a defendant who refuses (nukūl) to take an oath in court. The refusal is considered equivalent to accepting the claim (riḍā), but there is a disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples (Sahibayn) regarding the legal mechanism: Abu Hanifa views it as badhl (surrender of the claimed property), while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad view it as iqrār (admission). This difference has practical consequences: for example, in cases involving marriage or divorce (the 'six things'), Abu Hanifa does not allow requesting an oath from the defendant because badhl does not operate in those matters, while the Sahibayn do allow it because iqrār is valid. The author then generalizes: the principle of silence as explanation is used to argue that ijma' can be established by the explicit statement of some scholars and the silence of others (ijma' sukūtī). The text supports this with hadiths about harm and deception, and it refutes the Shafi'i objection that silence could be ambiguous. The author also clarifies that only silence in a place of need (mawḍiʿ al-ḥāja) counts; otherwise, silence is not explanatory, as seen in the example where the Lawgiver prescribes amputation for theft but does not mention other penalties—there is no need at that moment.

Taraduf al-Ilal: Overlapping Causes and the Second Method of Time as Cause
Explaining the second method of time as cause and refuting the objection of multiplication.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi concept of taraduf al-illal and apply it to distinguish between the first and second methods of considering time as a cause.

Core Idea
Multiple causes converge on a single obligation without duplication – overlapping, not transference.

In this passage, the Hanafi usul author presents the second method for time to be a cause: each part of the time is an independent cause, not a transferring one. The objection arises that this would multiply obligations, but the answer lies in the concept of taraduf al-illal (overlapping causes). We will explore how multiple causes can produce a single effect, using examples from fiqh such as bodily impurities, witnesses, and Quran recitation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two methods of time as cause
The first method transfers causality from one part to the next; the second method makes each part an independent cause. Understand the difference.
The first method is called 'transference' (intiqaal), but the author argues it is metaphorical.
2
State the objection to the second method
If each time part is an independent cause, shouldn't each part require its own obligation? That would multiply obligations.
3
Explain the answer using taraduf al-illal
The causes are multiple but the obligation is one; each cause establishes the same obligation, not an additional one. This is overlapping (taraduf) of causes.
The second part only establishes when the first part does not establish – i.e., they are alternative.
4
Analyze the examples
The text gives examples: urine and nosebleed both require wudu but only one wudu is needed; multiple witnesses establish the same testimony; long recitation makes fasting obligatory via each verse/reader.
Each example shows multiple causes leading to a single ruling.
5
Evaluate the dispute about multiple causes
Some reject the possibility of multiple independent causes for one effect. The Hanafi school defends it using these fiqh examples.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf, not a methodological flaw.
