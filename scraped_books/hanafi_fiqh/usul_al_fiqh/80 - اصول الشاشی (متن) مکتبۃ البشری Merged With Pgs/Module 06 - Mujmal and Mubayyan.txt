Definition and Ruling of Mushtarak (Common Word)
Defining mushtarak and its ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mushtarak, identify its examples, and apply the Hanafi ruling that when one meaning is intended, consideration of others drops.

Core Idea
A word with multiple distinct meanings can only intend one at a time; the rest fall away.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces the concept of mushtarak—a word with multiple distinct meanings. Understanding this is crucial for interpreting ambiguous texts in the Quran and Hadith. We will learn its definition, examples, and the key ruling that governs its use.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
Identify that mushtarak is a word with multiple different real meanings (لفظ له عدة معانٍ مختلفة الحقائق). Each meaning is established by a separate linguistic convention (وضع).
2
Review the examples
Recognize the examples given: 'jariya' (slave girl and ship), 'mushtari' (buyer and planet Jupiter), 'ba'in' (separation and clarity).
3
Grasp the ruling
When one meaning is determined as intended, consideration of the other meaning drops (سقط اعتبار إرادة غيره). Note that the mind cannot attend to multiple distinct meanings in a single intention.
4
Understand the implications
Until the intended meaning is clarified by context or explicit statement, the word is suspended (mawquf). Interpretation (ta'wil) via context and ijtihad is required.
The outcome of such interpretation is called al-mu'awwal.

Commentary
This passage defines mushtarak (a word with multiple unrelated meanings) and gives key examples: 'jariya' (can mean slave girl or ship), 'mushtari' (buyer or Jupiter), 'ba'in' (separation or clear). The fundamental ruling is that when one specific meaning is intended, consideration of the other drops. The commentary explains that because each meaning is set by a different convention, the mind cannot attend to multiple distinct meanings simultaneously. It then notes that there is no disagreement on certain permissible uses: intending a single meaning that applies to both (commonality of meaning), intending the combination as a single new meaning, or using one meaning as a metaphor for another. However, using a word for two contradictory meanings (like purity and menstruation) is not allowed.

Mushtarak and Mu'awwal: Definitions and Proofs
Definition of mushtarak and mu'awwal with ijma proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mushtarak and mu'awwal, explain the Hanafi argument that mushtarak cannot be used for all its meanings based on ijma, and apply the rule to fiqh examples like the bequest to mawali and the zihar case.

Core Idea
A common word cannot cover all its meanings at once; scholars agree on one meaning, proving mushtarak lacks generality.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi usul principle regarding mushtarak (a word with multiple literal meanings) and mu'awwal (interpretation). The passage provides a clear definition, a proof from ijma using the Qur'anic word 'al-quroo', and two practical fiqh examples that illustrate the rule. By the end, you will understand why the Hanafi school holds that mushtarak has no generality (umum) and requires ta'wil to determine the intended meaning.

Guided Path
1
Define mushtarak and mu'awwal
Identify the definitions given in the text: mushtarak is a word placed for two or more meanings, and mu'awwal is the interpretation that selects one meaning based on a preponderating factor.
The text defines mushtarak implicitly; the key term section provides the full definition.
2
Analyze the ijma proof from 'al-quroo'
The scholars agreed that 'al-quroo' in the Quran means either menstruation (Hanafi) or purity (Shafi'i), but not both. This consensus shows that a mushtarak cannot be intended for all its meanings, otherwise both could be used together.
The proof is that if generality were possible, the conflicting interpretations would both be valid, but the scholars rejected that.
3
Understand the reasoning for voiding the bequest
In the bequest case, 'mawali' is mushtarak between patrons and clients. Without preponderance, both meanings cannot be intended, so the bequest is void.
The lack of preponderance (tarjih) is key; if one meaning were preferred, it would be valid.
4
Apply the rule to the zihar case
The word 'like' (mithl) is like a mushtarak in effect; it can mean honor or prohibition. Without intention, it defaults to honor, not zihar.
This shows that even words not originally mushtarak can be treated as such in legal analysis.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and proof of the usul principle concerning mushtarak (a word with multiple meanings) and mu'awwal (interpretation). The author begins with a chapter heading and immediately presents the key example: the word 'al-quroo' in the Quran, which the Hanafis interpret as menstruation and the Shafi'is as purity. The fact that scholars differ and cannot use both meanings simultaneously proves that mushtarak cannot be used for all its meanings at once. The passage then supports this with two fiqh examples from Hanafi sources. First, the case of a bequest to 'mawali' which can mean both patrons and clients; because the word mushtarak cannot cover both, and there is no preference, the bequest is void. Second, the case of a man saying to his wife 'You are like my mother'; this could be an expression of honor or zihar (prohibition), and without intention to make zihar, it is not zihar. The commentary explains that the consensus on interpreting 'al-quroo' one way indicates that the other meaning is abandoned, establishing that mushtarak does not have generality. It also addresses an objection about 'mithl' (like) being a common word, arguing that even if not linguistically common, it is legally common in effect. The passage firmly establishes the Hanafi position that a mushtarak word cannot be used to intend all its meanings simultaneously, and that ta'wil (interpretation) is necessary to determine the intended meaning.

Mufassar: Definition and Ruling
Defines mufassar and gives example of its application

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mufassar in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, state its ruling, and apply it to a legal text by identifying when a speaker's clarification creates certainty.

Core Idea
When the speaker clarifies a homonym, that meaning becomes certain and overrides defaults.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, words can have multiple meanings. The category of mufassar (interpreted text) arises when the speaker themselves resolves this ambiguity. This lesson will show how a single phrase can turn a general term into a binding, specific obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the problem of homonyms (mushtarak)
A homonym has multiple possible meanings. Without clarification, interpretation (ta'wil) is needed, typically defaulting to the most common meaning. Recognize this as the starting point.
Mushtarak is a key concept; it creates ambiguity.
2
Recognize the speaker's clarification (bayan)
When the speaker adds a statement that selects one meaning, they have provided a bayan. This act transforms the word from ambiguous to mufassar.
The bayan must come from the speaker, not external evidence.
3
Define mufassar and its ruling
Mufassar is a homonym whose meaning has been given preponderance by the speaker's own bayan. Its ruling: acting upon it is obligatory with certainty (yaqīn).
Certainty here means it cannot be reinterpreted.
4
Apply the example from the text
In 'ten dirhams from the currency of Bukhara', the phrase 'from the currency of Bukhara' specifies which dirhams are meant. Without it, one would default to local currency via ta'wil. With it, the mufassar meaning (Bukharan currency) is binding.
The mufassar overrides the default interpretation.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of mufassar (interpreted text) in usul al-fiqh. When a homonymous word (mushtarak) has several possible meanings, and the speaker provides a clarification (bayan) that gives preponderance to one meaning, that meaning becomes mufassar. Its ruling is that it must be acted upon with certainty (yaqīn). The example clarifies: if someone says 'ten dirhams from the currency of Bukhara', the phrase 'from the currency of Bukhara' specifies the dirhams as Bukharan currency. Without this specification, the word 'dirham' would default to the prevalent currency in that country via ta'wil (interpretation). Since the speaker has interpreted, the mufassar meaning takes precedence, and the obligation is to pay the specified currency, not the local one.

The Metaphor of 'حررتك' in Hanafi Divorce Law
Analyzes the metaphorical use of 'حررتك' to imply divorce

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the metaphorical use of 'حررتك' in divorce, distinguish between sabab and illa, and determine the resulting type of divorce (ba'in).

Core Idea
'حررتك' is a metaphor for removal of ownership of pleasure, not for talaq, hence it yields ba'in divorce.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi usul problem: when a husband says 'حررتك' (I have freed you) and intends divorce, what type of divorce occurs? The passage from Usul al-Shashi unpacks the metaphorical relationship, distinguishing between a pure cause (sabab) and an effective cause (illa), and shows why the divorce must be irrevocable (ba'in). Understanding this reasoning is key to grasping how Hanafi usul handles linguistic ambiguity in legal contexts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the statement and its literal meaning
The phrase 'حررتك' literally means 'I have freed you' from slavery. Recognize that this is not a standard term for divorce.
2
Recognize the metaphorical relationship
The text argues that 'حررتك' is not a direct metaphor for the word 'طلاق' (talaq), but rather for the act of removing ownership of marital pleasure (mut'ah). Understand the causal link: freeing removes ownership of the person, which leads to removal of ownership of pleasure.
This is a sabab-musabbab relationship, not an illa.
3
Distinguish sabab from illa
Sabab is a cause that may be separated from its effect by an intermediary; illa is inseparable and has no intermediary. Freeing can exist without removing marital pleasure (e.g., freeing a slave), so it is a sabab, not an illa.
The key difference: illa is always accompanied by the ruling; sabab is not.
4
Apply to the divorce ruling
Since 'حررتك' is a metaphor for the remover of ownership of pleasure, and only ba'in (irrevocable) divorce completely removes that ownership, the divorce is ba'in, not raj'i.
Raj'i divorce does not fully remove ownership of pleasure, as the husband can return without a new contract.
5
Respond to the objection
The objection claims that if 'حررتك' is a metaphor for divorce, it should behave like explicit talaq and be raj'i. The response: it is not a metaphor for talaq, but for the remover of ownership of pleasure, so the analogy fails.
This illustrates the precision required in defining metaphorical meaning.

This passage is a textual analysis from a Hanafi usul work, specifically a commentary expanding on the matn of Usul al-Shashi. It discusses the metaphorical use of the word 'حررتك' (I have freed you) to effect divorce. The author explains that the word 'حررتك' is not a direct metaphor for talaq (divorce) but rather a metaphor for the removal of ownership of marital pleasure (mut'ah). This distinction is crucial because the Hanafi school holds that revocable divorce does not completely remove the husband's ownership of pleasure, whereas irrevocable divorce does. Therefore, when a husband says 'حررتك' with the intention of divorce, it results in a ba'in (irrevocable) divorce, not a raj'i (revocable) one. The passage also addresses potential objections: if 'حررتك' were a metaphor for talaq, the divorce would be raj'i like explicit talaq; but the Hanafi response is that it is a metaphor for the remover of ownership of pleasure (which is ba'in). The commentary further clarifies that the intention is necessary because the context is ambiguous, and it provides examples of other metaphorical usages that are unambiguous. The author also discusses the credibility of the husband in claiming metaphor: in judicial matters, the claim of metaphor is not accepted from a defendant due to suspicion, but that principle is not relevant here because the discussion is about the effective application of the word. Overall, the passage illustrates the Hanafi approach to linguistic interpretation in legal contexts, emphasizing the importance of the causal link (sabab-musabbab) in determining metaphorical relationships.

Key Terms
الاستعارة (al-isti'ara): Metaphor; using a word in a non-literal sense based on a perceived similarity or causal connection between the original and the transferred meaning.
الحقيقة (al-haqiqah): Literal meaning of a word, as originally placed by convention.
المجاز (al-majaz): Figurative or metaphorical meaning, when a word is used for something other than its literal meaning due to a relationship (like causation).
السبب (as-sabab): Cause; something that leads to a ruling or effect but is not the direct effective cause (illa). In this text, freeing (tahreer) is a sabab for removal of ownership of pleasure, not an illa.
المتعة (al-mut'ah): Marital enjoyment; the right of the husband to sexual pleasure from his wife. In this context, it is the object of ownership that is removed by divorce.
البائن (al-ba'in): Irrevocable divorce that completely severs the marriage tie, removing the husband's ownership of marital pleasure.

The Mufassar Text: Certainty Through the Speaker's Clarification
Defines mufassar with Quranic example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a mufassar text and explain why it cannot be subject to ta'wil or takhsis.

Core Idea
A mufassar text is so clear that neither interpretation nor specification can touch it.

In this lesson, we explore the highest level of textual clarity in Hanafi usul: mufassar. Using the Quranic verse on angelic prostration, you will see how the speaker's own words can seal off all ambiguity. This concept is crucial for establishing rulings with certainty.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition
A mufassar text is one whose intended meaning is made clear by an explanation from the speaker (Allah or the Prophet) such that no possibility of ta'wil (allegorical interpretation) or takhsis (specification) remains.
Note: the clarification must come from the original speaker, not from external sources.
2
Identify the initial zahir/nass
The base text without the clarifier (e.g., 'So the angels prostrated') is a zahir (apparent) and a nass (clear) regarding the honor of Adam, but it still admits possibilities of takhsis and ta'wil.
3
Recognize residual possibilities
In a mere zahir, takhsis could restrict the general term 'angels' to only some angels, and ta'wil could interpret 'prostrated' as performed in separate groups rather than a single act.
4
See how the clarifier removes each possibility
The phrase 'all of them' (kulluhum) blocks takhsis by explicitly including every angel. The phrase 'together' (ajma'un) blocks ta'wil by indicating a unified, simultaneous act.
Each clarifier targets a specific type of ambiguity.
5
Conclude that the text becomes mufassar
With both possibilities eliminated, the text is now mufassar – its meaning is definitive and cannot be altered by interpretation or specification.
The ruling derived from a mufassar is qat'i and must be acted upon without question.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'mufassar' (explicit/exegetically clear text) within the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity. A mufassar is a statement that, after the speaker provides a clarifying explanation, leaves no room for ta'wil (allegorical interpretation) or takhsis (specification). The author uses the Quranic verse 'So the angels prostrated, all of them, together' (Surah al-Hijr 15:30) to illustrate: the phrase 'the angels prostrated' is initially a zahir (apparent) and a nass (clear) regarding the honor of Adam, but it could be interpreted as only some angels prostrating (takhsis) or as performing the act in separate groups (ta'wil). تأويل (ta'wil): Interpretation that takes a text away from its apparent meaning based on a secondary meaning.
تخصيص (takhsis): Specification of a general term ('amm) to exclude some of its referents.

Mujmal and Mutashabih: Definitions, Rulings, and the Ta'wil Dispute
Defining mujmal and mutashabih and their legal status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mujmal and mutashabih, state their rulings in Hanafi usul, and evaluate the disagreement regarding knowledge of ta'wil.

Core Idea
مُجْمَل requires clarification; مُتَشَابِه submits to Allah — both demand belief without exhaustive human knowledge.

In this lesson, we explore two important categories of Quranic texts: mujmal (ambiguous) and mutashabih (unclear). The Hanafi school distinguishes these based on the possibility of clarification and the nature of the intended meaning. We'll examine the definitions, the ruling, and a key dispute about who knows the true interpretation of mutashabih verses.

Guided Path
1
Define mujmal and give its legal example
Identify what makes a text mujmal: it indicates a meaning but requires external clarification. Use the Quranic example of 'riba' to see how the literal meaning differs from the legal intent.
The example shows that mujmal cannot be understood from the word alone; it needs bayan.
2
Distinguish mutashabih as more obscure than mujmal
Understand that mutashabih cannot be clarified in this world; its true meaning is known only to Allah. Note the two types: those with no known meaning (huruf muqatta'at) and those with a known linguistic meaning but a divine intent that transcends the apparent meaning (attributes verses).
The example of 'yad Allah' illustrates how the apparent meaning is impossible for Allah, so it is mutashabih.
3
State the ruling for both mujmal and mutashabih
The ruling is belief in the truth of the intended meaning. For mujmal, clarification may eventually come; for mutashabih, we await disclosure on the Day of Judgment.
This ruling is based on the Quranic imperative to believe in what Allah has revealed.
4
Analyze the Hanafi position on ta'wil of mutashabih
The Hanafis pause at 'except Allah' in verse 3:7, meaning only Allah knows the definitive interpretation. The firmly grounded scholars are praised for their submission, not for claiming knowledge.
The Arabic grammar of exception (istithna') supports the pause.
5
Evaluate the dispute with al-Shafi'i
Understand the alternative reading where the phrase continues; recognize it as largely verbal—the dispute is about definitive vs. speculative knowledge.
Ibn al-Sam'ani notes the majority of Salaf paused at 'Allah', supporting the Hanafi view.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and analysis of two important types of Quranic texts: mujmal (ambiguous) and mutashabih (unclear). The author first illustrates mujmal with the example of the word 'riba' (usury/interest) in the Quran: its literal meaning is absolute increase, but the legally intended meaning is a specific type of increase in certain homogeneous goods. The word alone does not convey this detail, and it cannot be derived by mere reflection. Then mutashabih is introduced as more obscure than mujmal. An example is the disconnected letters (huruf muqatta'at) at the start of some surahs. The ruling for both is the same: one must believe that the intended meaning is true and real, even if unclear, until clarification comes. For mujmal, clarification may come from the Prophet or ijtihad; for mutashabih, the true meaning is known only to Allah and will be revealed on the Day of Judgment. The author distinguishes two types of mutashabih: (1) Those whose meaning is entirely unknown, like the disconnected letters; (2) Those whose linguistic meaning is known but the divine intent is not, because the apparent meaning is incompatible with Allah's transcendence (e.g., 'hand of Allah', 'face of Allah'). For the latter, we affirm the reality of the attribute but reject any anthropomorphic interpretation. The passage then enters a dispute between the Hanafis and Shafi'is/Mu'tazila regarding who knows the interpretation (ta'wil) of mutashabih. The Hanafi position, following the majority of the Salaf, is to pause at 'except Allah' in verse 3:7, meaning only Allah knows the true interpretation. The Shafi'i view connects 'those firmly grounded in knowledge' to the previous phrase, implying that they also know it. The author explains that this is largely a verbal dispute, since those who claim knowledge mean speculative interpretation, while those who deny it mean definitive knowledge. The passage concludes by citing Ibn al-Sam'ani that the majority of early scholars (including the correct view from Ibn Abbas) support the pause at 'Allah'.

المتشابه (al-mutashabih): A text whose intended meaning is unknown to the ummah in this world; it cannot be known definitively except by Allah.
تأويل (ta'wil): The interpretation or actual meaning of a verse that is hidden from human understanding.

The author of al-Shashi appears to adopt the view of al-Qadi Abu Zayd, not distinguishing between them in his definition, which allows for broader application.

The first example is the statement 'Free your slave on my behalf for a thousand dirhams' (aʿtiq ʿabdaka ʿannī bi-alf dirham). Thus, when the agent says 'I freed', it is as if he said 'I sold' and then 'I became your agent and freed'. The emancipation then occurs on behalf of the commander, and the thousand dirhams become obligatory.

Furthermore, if the commander intended the emancipation as expiation (kaffāra), it counts accordingly. It demonstrates the school's tendency to prioritize the validity of legal acts through implication, based on the principle that employing speech is better than nullifying it.

The Waw Does Not Imply Order: Hanafi Ruling on Conjunction
Analysis of waw as non-sequential conjunction in conditional divorce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that the waw of conjunction does not imply order, and distinguish between cases where waw indicates immediate execution (tanjiz) versus suspension (ta'liq).

Core Idea
The waw of conjunction does not require order; only contextual evidence can override its default meaning.

This lesson explores a foundational principle in Hanafi usul: the Arabic particle waw, when used as a simple conjunction, does not impose any sequence or simultaneity between the actions joined. We will examine two rulings from the text: the first concerning a conditional divorce phrased with 'or' or 'and' where the order of occurrences is irrelevant, and the second where the waw is interpreted as immediate (tanjiz) rather than conditional (ta'liq). Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly analyzing legal speech and avoiding misinterpretations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the grammatical role of waw in each example
Determine whether waw is purely conjunctive (atf) or part of a conditional structure. In the first example, waw joins two nouns; in the second, it joins two clauses.
The waw in 'If you enter this house and this house' is conjunctive, whereas in 'If you enter the house and you are divorced' the waw could be ambiguous.
2
Analyze the legal implications of no order
For the divorce with 'or' or 'and' example, note that the ruling does not require the acts to occur in a specific order. The wife is divorced regardless of sequence.
3
Distinguish between tanjiz and ta'liq
In the 'If you enter the house and you are divorced' case, the waw causes immediate divorce (tanjiz) because if it implied order, the divorce would be contingent (ta'liq) on entry. The text states that if order were assumed, it would become a suspension, not an immediate execution.
The waw here is interpreted as purely conjunctive, not conditional.
4
Apply the principle to a new scenario
Given a phrase like 'If you come and you are hired,' determine whether the waw implies order or immediacy based on context.
Default: no order, but if context suggests condition, it may shift.

Commentary
This passage from 'Usul al-Shashi' discusses the Arabic particle waw (واو) and its various meanings in legal contexts, focusing on the Hanafi position regarding whether it implies order (tartib), simultaneity (hal), or mere conjunction (atf). The text begins by presenting cases where waw does not require order: in a conditional divorce with 'or' (او), the divorce occurs upon speaking to either; with 'and' (و) in entering two houses, the order does not matter. Then it contrasts cases where waw indicates a state (hal), making the action conditional on the state. For example, if a master says to his slave 'Pay me a thousand and you are free,' the waw is interpreted as 'while' (hal), meaning 'pay me a thousand while being free' or 'if you pay me a thousand, you are free' – the freedom is conditional on payment. Similarly, if an imam tells disbelievers 'Open the door and you are safe,' safety is conditional on opening. The reasoning is that the literal conjunctive meaning (atf) would be impossible here: a master cannot demand money from his slave while slavery remains, so the waw must indicate the state, making the freedom contingent on payment. Conversely, if a husband says 'You are divorced while you are ill,' the divorce takes effect immediately (not suspended) because the apparent context (illness usually evokes compassion) suggests he means it as a conjunction, i.e., 'you are divorced and you are ill' as a description, not a condition. The passage then transitions to a comprehensive list of eleven types of waw, as cited from al-Mughni, including conjunctive, isti'naf, accompaniment, oath, redundant, and others. Throughout, the Hanafi school emphasizes that the meaning of waw is determined by context and evidence, using the principle that metaphorical interpretation (here, waw as hal) is only adopted when the literal meaning (conjunction) is impossible or contraindicated. This careful analysis reflects the Hanafi usuli methodology of balancing linguistic defaults with contextual clues (qara'in) to derive correct legal rulings.

Key Terms
الواو (al-wāw): The Arabic conjunction particle; in Hanafi usul, it can indicate mere conjunction (عطف) or the adverbial state (حال) depending on context and evidence.
العطف (al-ʿaṭf): Grammatical conjunction; the literal meaning of waw, joining words or phrases without implying order or simultaneity.
التعليق (al-taʿlīq): Suspension of a legal effect upon a future condition; in contrast to immediate execution (التنجيز).
التنجيز (al-tanjīz): Immediate execution of a legal ruling without condition.
الترتيب (al-tartīb): Order or sequence; the waw does not imply الترتيب.

Waw for the Adverbial State (Hal): Conditions for Metaphorical Interpretation
Analysis of waw as adverbial state (hal) in Hanafi usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when the waw must be interpreted as indicating the adverbial state (hal) and apply the Hanafi criteria: impossibility of literal conjunction, suitability of the locus, and evidence for the metaphorical meaning.

Core Idea
Waw is interpreted as 'while' only when literal conjunction is impossible and contextual evidence supports the shift.

In this lesson, we examine cases where the waw abandons its default conjunctive meaning and instead indicates the adverbial state (hal), effectively making the second clause a condition. The Hanafi school carefully delineates when this metaphorical interpretation is required: it depends on the impossibility of the literal meaning, the suitability of the phrase for the state reading, and the presence of contextual evidence. We will analyze the classic examples from the text to internalize this crucial usul principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify cases where literal conjunction is impossible
In the master-slave example 'Pay me a thousand and you are free,' literal conjunction would mean the master is demanding payment from the slave, which is legally impossible because the slave's property belongs to the master.
2
Recognize the shift to hal
When conjunction is impossible, the waw is interpreted metaphorically as hal, meaning 'while' or 'upon condition that.' Thus, 'Pay me a thousand and you are free' means 'Pay me a thousand while you are free' or more accurately 'If you pay me a thousand, you are free.'
3
Note the requirement of evidence (qarina)
The shift to hal is not automatic; there must be clear evidence that the literal meaning is impossible and that the hal reading is intended. In the master-slave case, the impossibility is evident; in the imam-disbelievers case, the context of security conditional on opening provides the evidence.
4
Distinguish from cases where hal is not needed
In the 'You are divorced while ill' example, literal conjunction is possible (husband can divorce an ill wife), and the apparent context (compassion) suggests the literal meaning. Thus, the waw remains conjunctive and the divorce is immediate.
5
Apply the criteria to a new example
Given a phrase like 'You are hired and you have experience,' decide whether the waw is conjunctive or hal based on the criteria.
If the experience is a condition, and it is impossible to hire without experience, then hal may be required.

The Effect of 'Aw' in Dower: Ambiguity vs Choice
Usul al-Fiqh dispute on particle 'or'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that the particle 'aw' in a marriage contract creates ambiguity (jahalah) rather than granting choice, and derive the ruling of mahr al-mithl.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, 'or' in a contract creates ignorance, not choice; the default obligation revives.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi examines a classic dispute: when a husband says 'I marry you on a thousand dirhams or a hundred dinars,' does he get to choose, or is the specification void? Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, differ. Understanding their reasoning deepens your grasp of how linguistic ambiguity (ijmal) interacts with contractual obligations.

1
Identify the issue
The marriage contract states the dower with 'or' between two amounts. Is the specification valid?
The term 'أو' is the crux.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa rules that the specification is void; the husband must pay mahr al-mithl (fair dower).
Why? Because the original obligation is mahr al-mithl; specification is an exception that requires certainty.
3
State the Sahibayn's position
The Sahibayn hold that 'أو' grants the husband a choice to pay either amount.
They liken it to a command (amr) where choice is given.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's proof
The word 'أو' covers one of two unspecified matters, introducing ignorance (jahalah). This corrupts the specification, so resort to the original default.
This is a qiyas to the sale of an unknown price.
5
Analyze the Hanafi rebuttal to the Sahibayn
Choice (takhyeer) is only established by a command to choose; here there is no command, so no choice exists.
The particle 'أو' merely indicates disjunction, not empowerment.

Bayān al-Ḍarūra: Clarification by Silence in Hanafi Usul
Explaining how silence can serve as clarification in legal texts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the concept of bayān al-ḍarūra, explain how silence can serve as clarification, and apply the principle to derive a ruling from a Quranic text.

Core Idea
Silence clarifies legal meaning when context necessitates it.

In this lesson, we explore a unique type of clarification in Hanafi legal theory: bayān al-ḍarūra (clarification by necessity). Unlike explicit statements, this clarification occurs through silence (sukūt) when the context forces a specific understanding. The classic example is the inheritance verse where Allah specifies the mother's share and, by remaining silent about the father's share while establishing partnership, clarifies that the father receives the remainder. This principle is then applied to contracts like mudāraba to determine profit shares.

Guided Path
1
Identify the textual context
Recognize the beginning of the speech that establishes a general ruling (e.g., partnership) and the subsequent specification that creates an implicit allocation.
In the verse, the initial clause establishes partnership between parents. This sets the stage for the implied clarification.
2
Note the point of silence
What is left unstated? The father's share is not mentioned explicitly after the mother's share is specified.
Silence alone is not sufficient; it must be within a context that necessitates completion.
3
Determine the necessity
Why does the silence clarify? Because the context of a complete inheritance allocation requires that both shares be determined. The explicit mention of the mother's share, combined with the established partnership, forces the understanding that the father gets the remainder.
The necessity (ḍarūra) arises from the need for a complete and coherent ruling.
4
Derive the implicit ruling
Conclude that the omitted portion is the remainder. In the verse, the father inherits two-thirds.
This implicit ruling is considered as if it were explicitly stated (manṣūṣ).
5

- فتصح بشرط الوصل: أي إن كان من جملة بيان التغيير فتصح موصولاً لا مفصولاً، وعلى هذا أجمع الفقهاء؛ لقوله علي: 'من حلف على يمين ورأي غيرها خيراً منها فليكفر عن يمينه، ثم ليأت بالذي هو خير' الحديث، جعل مخلص اليمين هو الكفارة، ولو صح الاستثناء متراخياً فجعله مخلصاً أيضاً بأن يقول الآن: إن شاء الله تعالى ويبطل اليمين ولا يجب الكفارة، وعن ابن عباس وهما: أنه يصح مفصولاً أيضاً؛ لما روي أنه عليه قال: 'لأغزون قريشاً' ثم قال بعد سنة : إن شاء الله تعالى، وهذا أي: النقل عن ابن عباس غير صحيح عندنا، ولو صح فلعل مراده أنه إذا نوى رجل الاستثناء عند التلفظ ثم أظهر نيته بعد التلفظ، فيقبل قوله فيما نواه ديانة فيما بينه وبين ومذهبه أن ما يقبل فيه قول العبد ديانة يقبل فيه قوله ظاهراً ، وروي أنه قال أبو جعفر بن منصور الذي كان من الخلفاء العباسية لأبي حنيفة الله : لم خالفت جدي أي: ابن عباس في عدم صحة الاستثناء متراخياً، فقال أبو حنيفة له: لو صح ذلك بارك الله في بيعتك أي يقول الناس الآن إن شاء الله تعالى، فتنتقض بيعتك، فتحيّر أبو جعفر وسكت والحق ما قال به الجمهور، وعليه الاعتماد والله اعلم كذا في 'الحصول'. وأما بيان الضرورة هو بيان يقع بغير الكلام كما في قوله تعالى: ﴿وَوَرِثَهُ أَبَوَاهُ فَلِأُمِّهِ الثَّلث، فإن صدر الكلام أوجب الشركة بين الأبوين في كل الميراث من غير تعيين نصيب كل منهما، ثم تخصيص الأم بالثلث دل على أن الأب يستحق الباقي، فصار بيان الثلثين لهذا التخصيص، فكأنه قال: فلأمه الثلث ولأبيه الباقي، وهذا إنما حصل بمجرد السكوت مع إثبات صدر الكلام الشركة، وتخصيص نصيب الأم بالثلث كذا في 'الفصول'. بياناً لنصيب الأب: وهذا البيان لم يحصل بمجرد السكوت من نصيب الأب، بل بالسكوت مع إثبات صدر الكلام الشركة، وتخصيص الأم بالثلث كذا في 'المعدن'. وعلى هذا: أي بناء على أن السكوت بيان ضرورة، وكأنه بمنزلة المنصوص تتفرع الفروع المذكورة. Chapter on Bayān al-Ḍarūra (Necessity)
Example: His saying, Exalted: 'And if he has only parents, then for his mother is one-third.' The verse established partnership between the parents, then specified the mother's share, so that became a clarification of the father's share.
Based on this we say: If we specify the share of the mudārib (mudārib) and remain silent about the share of the owner of capital (rabb al-māl), the partnership is valid.

[Commentary:]
'Tasih bisharti al-wasl' [it is valid with condition of connection]: meaning if it is part of Bayān al-Taghyīr, it is valid when connected not disconnected; the fuqahā’ have agreed on this. Because of his saying: 'Whoever swears an oath and then sees something better, let him expiate his oath and then do what is better.' This makes expiation the remedy for the oath. If delayed exception were valid, then he could also make it a remedy by saying now 'inshā’allāh' and nullify the oath without expiation. Ibn ‘Abbās and others: it is also valid disconnected, based on the report that he said 'I will attack Quraysh' and then a year later said 'inshā’allāh.' This report from Ibn ‘Abbās is not authentic according to us. Even if authentic, perhaps he intended the exception when speaking and then later showed his intention; his word is accepted internally (diyānatan) but not externally. It is reported that Abū Ja‘far al-Manṣūr said to Abū Ḥanīfa: 'Why do you oppose my grandfather (Ibn ‘Abbās) on the invalidity of delayed exception?' Abū Ḥanīfa replied: 'If that were valid, may Allah bless your caliphate, people would now say "inshā’allāh" and your caliphate would be invalidated.' Abū Ja‘far was astonished and remained silent. The truth is with the majority, and it is relied upon, as in al-Ḥusūl.

And as for Bayān al-Ḍarūra: it is a clarification that occurs without speech, as in His saying: 'And if he has only parents, then for his mother is one-third.' The beginning of the speech obligated partnership between the parents in all inheritance without assigning a share to each, then specifying the mother with one-third indicated that the father gets the rest. Thus it became a clarification of the two-thirds for this specification, as if He said: 'For his mother is one-third and for his father is the remainder.' This was obtained merely by silence (sukūt) along with the establishment of partnership from the beginning of the speech and specifying the mother's share as one-third, as in al-Fuṣūl.

'Clarification of the father's share': This clarification was not obtained merely by silence from the father's share, but by silence with the establishment of partnership from the beginning of the speech and specifying the mother's share, as in al-Ma‘din.

'And based on this': meaning based on the fact that silence is a necessary explanation (bayān ḍarūra) as if it is like the text (manṣūṣ) from which the mentioned branches derive, as in al-Shāfī (p.203). 'The partnership is valid' in profit between them, and partnership in it is a condition for the validity of the contract. If the specification of the mudārib's share were not made a clarification of the owner's share, partnership would not be established and the contract would be invalid. So it becomes as if he said: 'You have half the profit and I have half,' as it is said.

Commentary
This passage introduces Bayān al-Ḍarūra, a type of clarification that occurs not by explicit speech but by silence (sukūt) or implication. The main example is the inheritance verse where Allah specifies the mother's share as one-third, and by remaining silent about the father's share while establishing partnership, it becomes clear that the father gets the remainder. This is called 'clarification by necessity' because the context forces the understanding. The passage also applies this principle to the contract of mudāraba (profit-sharing): if the contract specifies the mudārib's share but remains silent about the owner's share, the silence is taken as clarification that the owner gets the remainder, making the partnership valid. The commentary includes a lengthy digression on the earlier topic of istithnā' (exception), reaffirming the Hanafi position that disconnected exception is invalid.

Key Terms
بيان الضرورة (bayān al-ḍarūra): A clarification that occurs not by explicit expression but by silence or implication, where the context makes the meaning necessarily understood.
السكوت بيان (al-sukūt bayān): The principle that silence, under certain conditions, can serve as a clarification of legal meaning.
