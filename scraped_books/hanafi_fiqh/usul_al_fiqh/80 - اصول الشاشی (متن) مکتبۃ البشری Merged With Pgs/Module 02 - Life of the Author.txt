Decoding the Conventions of Usul al-Shashi
Biographical and methodological introduction listing author's conventions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the speaker or school referenced by the seven key conventions (istilahat) used in Usul al-Shashi, enabling you to correctly attribute arguments during your reading.

Core Idea
Know the shorthand: 'qala ashabuna' = three Hanafi Imams, 'indahu' = Shafi'i, 'indana' = Hanafi.

Usul al-Shashi is a foundational Hanafi usul text, but to read it you need to decode its shorthand. The author, Imam Shams al-Din al-Shashi, uses specific phrases to indicate who is speaking: the Hanafi school, the Shafi'i school, or both. Without this key, you will misattribute arguments and miss the structure of debate. This lesson teaches you the seven essential conventions so you can navigate the text with confidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi trio marker
When you see 'قَالَ أَصْحَابُنَا' (qala ashabuna), it means 'Our companions said' and refers to the three founding Hanafi imams: Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani. This signals a statement of the highest authority within the school.
This convention is specific to Hanafi usul works; other schools use different terms for their early authorities.
2
Recognize the Shafi'i objection marker
When you see 'فَإِنْ قِيلَ' (fa'in qila), meaning 'If it is said', it introduces an objection or question from the Shafi'i school. This is the opening of a dialectical move.
The objection is often followed by a Hanafi response introduced by 'قُلْنَا' (qulna).
3
Recognize the Hanafi response
When you see 'قُلْنَا' (qulna), meaning 'We said', it is the Hanafi response to the preceding objection. This represents the position of the Hanafi school defending its view.
After 'قُلْنَا', the Hanafi argument is presented, often with evidence.
4
Distinguish 'indahu' vs 'indana'
'عِنْدَهُ' (indahu) means 'according to him' and refers to Imam al-Shafi'i. By contrast, 'عِنْدَنَا' (indana) means 'according to us' and refers to the Hanafi school. Getting these confused would lead to attributing a Shafi'i view to the Hanafis.
These possessive phrases appear frequently in comparative discussions.
5
Identify the reference to al-Jami' al-Kabir
When you see 'قَالَ فِي الْجَامِعِ' (qala fi al-jami'), it means 'He said in al-Jami'' and refers to al-Jami' al-Kabir by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani, one of the six authoritative works of Zahir al-Riwaya.
Al-Jami' al-Kabir is a primary source for Hanafi fiqh rulings.
6
Spot the consensus marker
When you see 'قُلْنَا جَمِيعًا' (qulna jami'an), meaning 'We all said', it indicates that both the Hanafis and Shafi'is agree on the statement that follows. This shows a point of agreement (ittifaq) between the two schools.
This is a rare marker; most of the text focuses on points of disagreement.
7
Apply all conventions to sample sentences
Now take a short paragraph from the text and identify each convention. For each phrase state: the speaker, the school, and the authority level. This step drills the skill of real-time decoding.
Example: 'قال أصحابنا: الوتر واجب. فإن قيل: بل سنة. قلنا: الدليل كذا.' → first sentence = Hanafi trio, second = Shafi'i objection, third = Hanafi response.

Commentary
This passage is the introductory front matter of Usul al-Shashi, providing the reader with essential information about the author, the book, its sources, methodology, and conventions. It begins with a biography of the author, Imam Shams al-Din al-Shashi (d. 781 AH), listing several commentaries on his work from prominent Hanafi scholars across India, Pakistan, Syria, and Lebanon. These commentaries are important for understanding the text's deeper meanings. Then, the passage enumerates the primary sources al-Shashi used: the Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma' of the Companions and Successors, and key Hanafi usul works like Taqwim al-Adilla by al-Dabusi and Usul al-Karkhi, as well as the major compilations of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani. Next, it outlines al-Shashi's methodology, which is the typical Hanafi approach of usul: deriving specific fiqh rulings (furu') from general usul principles by identifying the 'illa (effective cause) of a ruling, applying qawa'id (legal maxims), and performing tarjih (preference) between conflicting evidences. Finally, and most importantly for a student of this book, it lists the conventions (istilahat) al-Shashi uses throughout his text. For instance, 'قال أصحابنا' refers to the three founding Imams of the school (Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, Muhammad), giving that statement the highest weight. 'فإن قيل' indicates an objection from the Shafi'i school, and 'قلنا' is the Hanafi response. 'عنده' refers to Imam al-Shafi'i, while 'عندنا' refers to the Hanafi position. Knowing these conventions is crucial to correctly identifying who is speaking in the text and to what school a particular argument belongs. This ensures the reader avoids confusion when engaging with the inter-madhhab debates that permeate the book.

Key Terms
قال أصحابنا (qāla aṣḥābunā): A phrase indicating that the statement is attributed to the three founding Hanafi imams: Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybani.
فإن قيل (fa'in qīla): A phrase introducing an objection or question, typically from the perspective of the Shafi'i school.
قلنا (qulnā): The Hanafi response to the preceding objection, representing the position of the Hanafi school.
عنده (indahu): Refers to the opinion of Imam al-Shafi'i.
عندنا (indanā): Refers to the opinion of the Hanafi school.
قال في الجامع (qāla fī al-jāmi'): A reference to the book al-Jami' al-Kabir by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani.
قلنا جميعاً (qulnā jamī'an): Indicates that both the Hanafis and Shafi'is agree on the statement.

The Hanafi Mujtahid Hierarchy and Imam al-Shashi's Rank
Biographical classification of Imam al-Shashi's ijtihad rank

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the third layer of Hanafi mujtahidun (as-hab al-takhrij) and state the difference between 'mashayikhuna' and 'ulama'una' as used by Imam al-Shashi.

Core Idea
Imam al-Shashi was a mujtahid of the third layer, capable of deriving rulings from the Imam's texts.

This lesson introduces the Hanafi scholarly hierarchy. You will learn that not all Hanafi scholars are equal in ijtihad; they are classified into layers. Imam al-Shashi's own work, Usul al-Shashi, is written from the perspective of a mujtahid in the third layer, known as as-hab al-takhrij. Understanding this rank helps you appreciate the authority of his text.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms used
Look at the two phrases: 'mashayikhuna' and 'ulama'una'. Note how the passage distinguishes them geographically.
'Mashayikhuna' specifically refers to Hanafi masters of Transoxiana (Mawara'un-Nahr).
2
List the scholars mentioned
Al-Maturidi and al-Dabusi are given as examples of 'mashayikhuna'. Al-Karkhi and al-Razi are included in the same layer as al-Shashi.
These names are important for later reference.
3
Understand the layer system
The passage says al-Shashi is from the third layer. This layer is defined as 'as-hab al-takhrij' – those who can derive rulings from their Imam's texts while following his usul and furu'.
The third layer is one of several; the highest are the founding mujtahidun.
4
Define the capacity of this layer
Mujtahidun of this layer know the proofs (adillah) of their Imam's rulings and can apply them to new cases by takhrij.
They do not create independent usul; they work within the madhhab.

Definition of Zahir al-Riwayah
Defining the term 'Zahir al-Riwayah' and listing the six books

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Zahir al-Riwayah, list its six books, and explain why they hold supreme authority in Hanafi fiqh.

Core Idea
Zahir al-Riwayah is the highest level of transmitted rulings in the Hanafi school, based on six books from Imam Muhammad via reliable chains.

In the Hanafi school, not all narrations carry the same weight. The term 'Zahir al-Riwayah' refers to a specific corpus of transmissions that form the backbone of the madhhab. This lesson unpacks that definition, identifies the six books, and explains why they are considered the most authoritative source of fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its referent
Note that 'Zahir al-Riwayah' is a technical term—it does not mean 'apparent meaning' but rather the 'well-known narrations' transmitted from Abu Hanifa and his two main students (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani).
2
Understand the transmission basis
These narrations were compiled by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani and transmitted by reliable narrators, making them either mutawatir (consecutive) or mashhur (well-known). This gives them a high level of certainty.
Mutawatir yields certain knowledge; mashhur yields strong probability.
3
Memorize the six books
The six books are: al-Mabsut, al-Ziyadat, al-Jami' al-Saghir, al-Jami' al-Kabir, al-Siyar al-Saghir, and al-Siyar al-Kabir.
These are the core of al-Zahir al-Riwayah; later scholars added al-Mukhtasar al-Quduri and other texts but these six remain the foundation.
4
Explain the reason for naming
They are called 'Zahir al-Riwayah' because they were transmitted with such strength and clarity (zahir = apparent/manifest) that they are the first source for fatwa in the school.
Opposite of 'nawadir' (rare narrations) which carry less weight.
5
Apply the hierarchy
Recognize that rulings from Zahir al-Riwayah are mufta bihi (acted upon) and are not to be abandoned for weaker narrations without strong evidence.
This hierarchy is critical for usul and for muftis when giving fatwa.

Commentary

These were compiled by Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani and transmitted by reliable narrators, giving them the status of being either mutawatir (consecutive) or mashhur (well-known). This designation carries authority weight: rulings from Zahir al-Riwayah are the most authoritative in the Hanafi school and cannot be overridden by weaker narrations (nawadir). Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for students to know which rulings are binding and which are merely transmitted.

Key Terms
ظاهر الرواية (zahir al-riwayah): The apparent/well-known narrations transmitted from Abu Hanifa and his companions through Imam Muhammad's six books via reliable chains.
متواترة (mutawatirah): Narrations reported by so many chains that collusion to fabricate is impossible.
مشهورة (mashhurah): Well-known narrations that are widespread but not to the level of tawatur.
