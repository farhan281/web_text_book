The Definition of Haqiqah (Literal Meaning) in Hanafi Usul
Defines haqiqah as original linguistic assignment and its relationship to majaz.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the definition of haqiqah in Hanafi usul, IDENTIFY its etymological roots, and DISTINGUISH it from majaz.

Core Idea
Haqiqah is the default meaning of a word as assigned by the language-setter, indicated without a contextual clue.

In this lesson, we examine the foundational definition of haqiqah (literal meaning) as the cornerstone of linguistic interpretation in Hanafi usul. Understanding haqiqah is essential before studying majaz (figurative meaning) and other textual categories.

Guided Path
1
Parse the Definition
Read the Arabic sentence: 'كل لفظ وَضَعَه واضعُ اللغة بإزاء شيء فهو حقيقة له.' Identify each component: the word, the act of placing (wad'), the language-setter, and the meaning.
2
Understand Wad' (Linguistic Assignment)
Wad' is the initial assignment of a word to a meaning without needing any external clue. Haqiqah relies on this original assignment.
This is a key Hanafi usul concept.
3
Trace the Etymology of 'Haqiqah'
The term is derived from either 'haqqa' (to be established) or 'haqaqtu' (to confirm/establish). It means 'the established' or 'confirmed' in its original place.
The ta' marbuta at the end transforms it from an adjective to a noun.
4
Recognize the Contrast with Majaz
Haqiqah is the default; majaz is using a word for a meaning other than its original due to a relationship, with a clue preventing the literal meaning.
The two are combined in one chapter because majaz is built upon haqiqah.
5
Apply the Principle
When encountering a word in a sacred text, assume its haqiqah meaning unless a qarinah (contextual clue) indicates otherwise.
This is the default rule in Hanafi usul.

Kinayah: Definition and Ruling in Hanafi Usul
Defines kinayah and states its legal ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of kinayah and apply the rule that it requires intention or contextual indication to take legal effect.

Core Idea
Kinayah is speech that hides its intended meaning; it takes effect only when intention or context resolves the ambiguity.

In Hanafi usul, explicit speech (sarih) conveys its meaning without need for intention, but implicit speech (kinayah) does not. This lesson defines kinayah both linguistically and technically, and explains that its legal effect depends on intention (niyyah) or contextual indication (dalalah al-hal). Understanding this distinction is foundational to classifying statements in ibadat and muamalat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Read the Arabic definition: kinayah in language is speaking of something while intending something else. In terminology, it conceals the intended meaning, known only by a contextual clue.
Note that kinayah can be a haqiqah (real) or majaz (metaphor) in origin, but concealment is by usage.
2
Note the ruling
The passage states: 'حكم الكناية: ثبوت الحكم بها عند وجود النية، أو بدلالة الحال' - the ruling is established with intention or contextual indication.
This is because kinayah involves hesitation (taraddud) that must be resolved.
3
Understand the reasoning
Why is intention/context necessary? Because the word has multiple possible meanings; evidence is needed to remove hesitation and give weight to one meaning.
Without such evidence, the legal effect is not established.
4
Recognize the contrast with sarih
Sarih (explicit) speech does not require intention; its meaning is clear from the word itself. For example, the word 'talaq' is sarih for divorce and takes effect even if the speaker did not intend it.
In kinayah, intention or context is essential.
5
Locate the authorities cited
The passage references al-Shafi' (a commentary) and 'Umdat al-Hawashi, indicating the definition is drawn from later Hanafi usul works.
These sources help anchor the definition within the school.

Commentary
This passage defines kinayah (implicit speech) in both linguistic and technical terms. Linguistically, kinayah is when you say something but intend something else, requiring a contextual clue (qarinah) for understanding. Technically, it is a word whose intended meaning is concealed, known only through context; the concealment comes from usage even if the word's literal meaning is clear. The ruling: kinayah takes effect only when there is explicit intention (niyyah) or a contextual indication (dalalah al-hal) that resolves the ambiguity. For example, in divorce, words like 'ba'in' or 'haram' are kinayah because they have multiple possible meanings; they do not produce revocable (raj'i) divorce but rather irreparable (ba'in) divorce, unlike the explicit word 'talaq'. The author addresses an objection: if these were kinayah for divorce, they should act like divorce and produce raj'i divorce as al-Shafi'i holds. The Hanafi response is that these words are literal for meanings like 'separation' or 'prohibition', not figurative for divorce; they are called kinayah because they have many possibilities, concealing the intended meaning. Additionally, in criminal law, kinayah does not establish hadd punishments due to the presence of doubt (shubhah), as the Prophet instructed to avert hudud with doubts. So a confession via kinayah for zina or theft does not lead to hadd.

Key Terms
كناية (kinaya): Speech that conceals its intended meaning and requires a contextual indication or intention to be understood.
صريح (sarih): Explicit speech that conveys its meaning without need for intention or context.

Applications of Kinayah: Divorce and Hadd Punishments
Applies the concept of kinayah to divorce and hudud rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling on kinayah to divorce (ba'in vs raj'i) and explain why kinayah confessions do not incur hadd punishments.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, kinayah words like 'bā'in' and 'ḥarām' produce ba'in divorce, and kinayah confessions do not trigger hadd due to doubt.

The passage now applies the general ruling of kinayah to two critical areas: divorce and hudud. In divorce, words like bā'in and ḥarām are classified as kinayah because they have multiple possible meanings, leading to ba'in (irrevocable) divorce rather than raj'i (revocable). This contrasts with the Shafi'i view. In criminal law, confessions made via kinayah are insufficient for hadd punishment because doubt (shubhah) precludes hadd, as per the prophetic instruction to avert hudud by doubts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the application in divorce
Read the passage: 'ولهذا المعنى سُمي لَفظُ البينونة والتحريم كناية في باب الطلاق'. It explains that words of dissolution (bā'in) and prohibition (ḥarām) are called kinayah in divorce due to hesitation and concealment of intent.
The passage notes these words are not treated as explicit divorce (sarih).
2
Determine the type of divorce produced
The Hanafi view is that these words produce ba'in (irrevocable) divorce, not raj'i (revocable). The passage says they do not work the work of divorce (i.e., they do not give raj'i).
Ba'in divorce means the husband cannot take his wife back without a new marriage contract and mahr.
3
Understand the madhhab dispute
The passage addresses an objection: if these words are kinayah for divorce, they should act as divorce and give raj'i (as al-Shafi'i holds). The Hanafi response is that these words are literal for separation and prohibition, not figurative for divorce; they are called kinayah due to multiple possibilities, and they produce ba'in because they are not explicit for the revocable type.
The dispute is between Hanafi (ba'in) and Shafi'i/view of Umar and Ibn Mas'ud (raj'i).
4
Apply the ruling to hudud
The passage states: 'العقوبات كحد الزنا وحد القذف؛ لأنها تندرى بالشبهات' and that kinayah does not establish hadd because of doubt (shubhah). A confession via kinayah (e.g., indirectly admitting zina) does not lead to hadd punishment.
The principle: 'ادرؤوا الحدود بالشبهات' (avert hudud by doubts) is applied here.
5
Note the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
The practical consequence of the dispute in divorce is the type: ba'in vs raj'i. In hudud, the ruling is consistent across schools (no hadd by kinayah), but the reasoning differs.
This illustrates how usul principles directly impact fiqh.

Commentary
This passage defines kinayah (implicit speech) in both linguistic and technical terms. Linguistically, kinayah is when you say something but intend something else, requiring a contextual clue (qarinah) for understanding. Technically, it is a word whose intended meaning is concealed, known only through context; the concealment comes from usage even if the word's literal meaning is clear. While both are clear in meaning, nass is the primary purpose of the speech, whereas zahir is a secondary meaning. Al-Shashi uses examples from the Quran and the hadith of owning a mahram relative to illustrate how one text can carry both a nass and a zahir meaning simultaneously.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary purpose of the speech
Determine what the speaker (Allah or the Prophet) intended to convey as the main ruling. This meaning is the nass.
Nass is the intended purpose.
2
Identify the secondary meaning
Recognize any additional clear implication that is not the main objective. This is the zahir.
Zahir is clear but secondary.
3
Verify with examples
Test this framework with the Quranic verse on divorce (2:236) and the hadith on mahram relative. For the hadith, the emancipation is nass, ownership is zahir.
4
Apply the terminology
The primary intended meaning is nass; the secondary clear meaning is zahir. Both require action, but nass is stronger when they conflict.
Nass prevails over zahir at comparison.

Commentary
This passage from Al-Shashi's Usul is a chapter on contrasting terms (mutqabilat), specifically distinguishing between nass and zahir. The author provides three examples to illustrate: First, the verse about marriage (interpreted from 'انكحوا' as a command for permissibility) which is zahir for permission and nass for the numerical limit of wives. Second, the Quranic verse on divorce without consummation (2:236) which is nass for the ruling of a woman whose mahr was not specified, and zahir for the husband's exclusive right to divorce. It also indicates that marriage is valid without specifying mahr. Third, the hadith 'Whoever owns a mahram relative, he becomes free' which is nass for the relative's entitlement to freedom and zahir for the validity of the ownership (i.e., the purchase is valid). Then the author states the general ruling: both zahir and nass require action (wajib al-'amal) even though they may admit interpretation or specification, similar to how a literal meaning (haqiqa) can bear metaphor (majaz). The difference between nass and zahir appears only when they contradict: nass takes precedence because it is the intended purpose of speech. The author applies this to a fiqh case: if a husband tells his wife 'Divorce yourself' and she says 'I have made myself irrevocably divorced' (بنت), the divorce is revocable (raj'i) because the word 'أبنت' is nass for divorce (since it responds to the request) but only zahir for irrevocability. Thus the nass aspect prevails. The passage also reports an ikhtilaf among Hanafi scholars regarding the epistemological weight of zahir and nass from Quran and mutawatir hadith: some (like al-Maturidi and the scholars of the region) hold that they yield only conjecture (zann) while others (Iraqi scholars like al-Karkhi, al-Jassas, Abu Zayd) hold they yield certainty ('ilm). This dispute is based on whether the possibility of metaphor or specification is considered valid. The Iraqi scholars consider it too remote without evidence. This difference matters for usul principles. The passage also includes a digression on the cause of wala (clientage) from manumission, and how the example of a relative bought is applied.

Ruling on Acting on Nass and Zahir, and the Hanafi Ikhtilaf on Certainty
Explaining the ruling of nass and zahir and the Hanafi internal dispute on their epistemological status

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ikhtilaf on whether zahir and nass from Quran and mutawatir Sunnah yield certainty or conjecture, and apply this to determine how freely ta'wil can be used.

Core Idea
Hanafi scholars differ: some say zahir gives only conjecture; others say it gives certainty. This affects how much interpretation is allowed.

After establishing the definition of nass and zahir, Al-Shashi discusses their legal ruling: both must be acted upon. However, scholars differ on the epistemological weight of the zahir of the Book and mutawatir Sunnah. This lesson explores the two positions within the Hanafi school and their basis in the possibility of metaphor.

Guided Path
1
Recap: Both nass and zahir require action
From the previous lesson, both clear meanings are to be acted upon, but they differ in strength when contradictory. Now we examine whether the zahir of primary sources yields certainty or conjecture.
2
Identify the two positions
First position (Maturidi and others): the zahir of Book and mutawatir Sunnah yields only conjecture (zann). Second position (Iraqi scholars: Karkhi, Jassas, Abu Zayd): yields certainty (qat').
3
Understand the basis
The difference is based on whether the possibility of metaphor (majaz) and specification (takhsis) is considered a real possibility or too remote to affect certainty.
كل حقيقة تحتمل المجاز، وكل عام يحتمل الخصوص
4
Apply the consequence
Those who hold conjecture allow ta'wil more freely; those who hold certainty require strong evidence before departing from the apparent meaning.
This affects how scholars derive rulings from apparent texts.

Nass over Zahir: The Hierarchical Rule and Its Application
Usul rule with examples and internal ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that a Nass meaning overrides a Zahir meaning when they conflict, and identify the practical consequences using the provided examples.

Core Idea
When a text carries both Zahir and Nass meanings, the stronger Nass always prevails.

This lesson from Usul al-Shashi establishes a core principle of Hanafi legal interpretation: the hierarchy between two types of textual clarity—Zahir (apparent) and Nass (explicit). While both demand action, they are not equal when they conflict. The Nass, being the direct purpose of speech, takes precedence. Through concrete examples like a wife's divorce response and a prophetic command about urine, you will see how this rule operates in real rulings and how Hanafi imams differ on the epistemological certainty of these categories.

Guided Path
1
Define Zahir and Nass
Understand the definitions: Zahir is a word that indicates its meaning but allows for another interpretation (e.g., metaphor or specification); Nass is the intended meaning that is the primary object of the speech and does not admit another interpretation without a strong contextual clue.
The passage states that both necessitate action, but with the possibility of intending something else (احتمال إرادة الغير).
2
Grasp the General Rule
The rule is that when both appear in a single expression, the meaning that is Nass takes precedence over the meaning that is merely Zahir. This is because the Nass has greater clarity and is the purpose of the speech.
The author says: 'وإنما يظهر التفاوت بينهما عند المقابلة' (The difference only appears when they are compared).
3
Analyze the Divorce Example
Apply the rule: A husband says 'Divorce yourself' (طلقي نفسك). The wife responds 'I have separated myself' (أبنت نفسي). Her response is Nass for divorce (since it directly answers the command) and Zahir for final separation (baynuna). The Nass meaning prevails, so the divorce is revocable (raj'i), not final.
The passage explains: 'لأن هذا نص في الطلاق وظاهر في البينونة'.
4
Examine the Urine Hadith Example
The Prophet's command 'Drink from their urine and their milk' is Nass for explaining the cause of healing (بيان سبب الشفاء) and Zahir for permitting urine consumption. Another hadith explicitly commands avoiding urine (استنزهوا من البول). The latter is Nass for the obligation to avoid urine, so it overrides the apparent permission, making drinking urine absolutely impermissible.
The passage concludes: 'فلا يحلّ شرب البول أصلاً' (so drinking urine is not permissible at all).
5
Recognize the Intra-Hanafi Ikhtilaf
Note the disagreement among Hanafis: the Maturidi school and Iraqi school differ on whether Zahir and Nass from Quran and Mutawatir Sunnah provide certainty (qat') or only probability (zann). This affects the theoretical foundation but not the practical rule of precedence.
The passage attributes the non-certainty view to Sheikh Abu Mansur al-Maturidi and 'the scholars of our region', and the certainty view to Iraqi scholars like al-Karkhi and al-Jassas.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi addresses the rule when a text has both an apparent (zahir) meaning and an explicit (nass) meaning, and they conflict. The key principle is that the explicit meaning takes precedence over the apparent meaning. The author defines the ruling of both: they both necessitate action, but with the possibility of alternative interpretation. When they are compared, the explicit is stronger because it is the direct purpose of the speech. Two examples are given: (1) If a husband tells his wife 'Divorce yourself' and she says 'I have separated myself', this is explicit in divorce (since it directly responds to the command) and apparent in final separation (baynuna). The explicit meaning prevails, so the divorce is revocable (raj'i) not final. (2) The Prophet's command to the people of 'Urayna to drink camel urine and milk is explicit in explaining the cause of healing and apparent in permitting urine consumption. But another hadith explicitly commands avoiding urine due to punishment in the grave. The explicit command to avoid urine overrides the apparent permission, so drinking urine is absolutely impermissible. The passage also discusses a scholarly difference among Hanafis about the epistemological weight of zahir and nass: some (like al-Maturidi) say they only provide probability (zann), while others (like the Iraqi scholars) say they provide certainty (qat') when from the Quran or mutawatir sunnah. This difference is based on whether one considers the possibility of metaphor or specification. The passage notes that this dispute is foundational for many rulings, and that the stronger view for the author's region is the non-certainty view. The commentary also clarifies details about the hadith and the rulings on urine drinking among the Hanafi imams: Abu Hanifa prohibits it absolutely, Abu Yusuf permits only for treatment, and Muhammad permits it and considers it pure (though the hadith is used to argue against them). This lesson provides the operational rule for preferring nass over zahir in Hanafi usul.

Mu'awwal and Mufassar: Interpreted vs Definitively Clarified Texts
Defines mu'awwal and mufassar with Quranic and fiqhi examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mu'awwal and mufassar, identify examples of each, and apply the Hanafi rule that nass outweighs mu'awwal.

Core Idea
Mu'awwal is interpreted and zanni; mufassar is clarified by the speaker and qati'.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, texts vary in clarity. Some are open to interpretation, others are made definitively clear by the speaker's own words. This lesson from Usul al-Shashi defines two key categories: al-mu'awwal (interpreted text) and al-mufassar (definitively clarified text). By studying the examples, you will learn how the Hanafi school ranks textual evidence and resolves conflicts between general and specific texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the defining characteristics
Read the definitions of mu'awwal and mufassar. Note that mu'awwal requires ta'wil due to multiple possible meanings, while mufassar is clarified by the speaker to remove all ambiguity.
2
Analyze the hadith on vegetables
Examine the prophetic hadith 'There is no sadaqah on vegetables' as an example of mu'awwal. Identify which word ('sadaqah') creates ambiguity and which meanings are possible.
The Hanafi position uses this as an example of a mu'awwal text being outweighed by a nass.
3
Trace the mufassar example from the Quran
Study the verse 'So the angels prostrated, all of them together' (Quran 15:30). See how the words 'all of them' (kulluhum) remove possibility of takhsis, and 'together' (ajma'un) remove possibility of ta'wil.
This shows the progression from zahir to nass to mufassar.
4
Apply the concepts to the fiqh dispute on 'ushr
Read the differing positions on whether 'ushr is obligatory on vegetables. Understand how Abu Hanifa treats the hadith as mu'awwal and gives precedence to the nass 'What the sky waters has 'ushr'.
The madhhab dispute illustrates the practical consequence of the usul hierarchy.
5
Recognize the legal examples of mufassar
Study the examples of 'I married you for a month' and acknowledgment of debt. See how the speaker's additional words clarify the intended meaning, making the text mufassar.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi defines two key terms in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: al-mu'awwal and al-mufassar. The author first introduces al-mu'awwal through the prophetic hadith 'There is no sadaqah on vegetables.' The word 'sadaqah' could refer to obligatory zakat, 'ushr (tithe), or voluntary charity. Since it is interpreted (mu'awwal) to negate 'ushr, it is not definitive. In contrast, al-mufassar is a text whose meaning is clarified by the speaker to the extent that no possibility of ta'wil or takhsis remains. The Quranic verse about the angels prostrating is used as an example: the word 'angels' is general, 'all of them' removes the possibility of specification, and 'together' removes the possibility of interpretive allegory (such as prostrating separately). The passage then extends the discussion to legal examples: a statement like 'I married so-and-so for a month' is zahir (apparent) in marriage, but the phrase 'for a month' specifies it as mut'ah (temporary marriage), which is not valid nikah. Similarly, an acknowledgment of debt with a condition ('from the price of this slave') is explained to defer liability. The commentary also includes a brief fiqh dispute: Abu Hanifa holds that all produce of land, including vegetables, is subject to 'ushr, while Abu Yusuf, Muhammad, and al-Shafi'i restrict it to staple crops reaching five awsuq. However, the primary focus of this passage is the usul distinction between mu'awwal and mufassar and the hierarchy of textual clarity.

Key Terms
المؤول (al-mu'awwal): A text whose intended meaning is reached through interpretation (ta'wil) due to the possibility of multiple meanings; it is not definitive.
المفسر (al-mufassar): A text whose meaning is made perfectly clear by the speaker's own explanation, leaving no room for ta'wil or takhsis. It is qati' (definitive).

The Four Levels of Textual Clarity in Hanafi Usul
Defining the four levels of textual clarity and their opposites with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify a given text as zahir, nass, mufassar, or muhkam, and distinguish each from its opposite.

Core Idea
Text clarity is a hierarchy: zahir → nass → mufassar → muhkam, each less open to alteration.

In Hanafi legal theory, not all texts are equal in clarity. The Usul al-Shashi outlines a hierarchy of four levels of textual expression—zahir, nass, mufassar, and muhkam—each with specific legal consequences. This lesson will help you place any scriptural or contractual text on this scale.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish zahir from nass
Zahir is apparent in meaning but allows specification (takhsis) or interpretation (ta'wil). Nass is clear in meaning, forbidding interpretation, but may still allow specification of the cause. Example: 'angels' is zahir for generality; 'a thousand' is nass for obligation.
The text uses 'kulluhum' to remove specification and 'ajma'in' to remove interpretation.
2
Recognize the move from nass to mufassar
When the cause or condition is added, a nass becomes mufassar, removing interpretation of the obligation's basis. Example: 'a thousand from the price of this slave' makes the obligation conditional on delivery.
Mufassar overrides nass in legal effect.
3
Understand muhkam as the highest level
Muhkam is immune to both interpretation and abrogation. Example: verses about Allah's knowledge and justice. In contracts, once a cause is specified, the text becomes muhkam and cannot be altered.
The difference between mufassar and muhkam is that muhkam cannot be abrogated.
4
Identify the opposites: khafi and mushkil
Khafi is hidden due to an extrinsic cause (e.g., technical terms like 'tarar'), opposing zahir. Mushkil is ambiguous in itself, with two degrees of hiddenness, opposing nass.
The passage explains that mushkil is like a stranger whose forms become mixed.

Commentary
This passage from the matn of Usul al-Shashi defines the four levels of textual clarity (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) and their opposites (khafi, mushkil, and two others not named here). The author begins with the example of the angels to illustrate zahir (apparent) generality, showing that while a plural may be general, it can be specified. He explains how the words 'kulluhum' and 'ajma'un' remove possibilities of specification and interpretation. Then he moves to legal examples: in marriage, the word 'tazawwajtu' is zahir in permanent marriage, but adding 'for a month' reveals the intended is mut'a (temporary marriage), which is invalid. النص (al-nass): A text that is clear in meaning and not open to interpretation, but may still be subject to abrogation or specification of its cause.

المحكم (al-muhkam): A text that is absolutely clear, immune to interpretation and abrogation.
الخفي (al-khafi): A text that is not understood due to an extrinsic cause related to the subject matter, even though the wording is clear.
المشكل (al-mushkil): A text that is problematic and unclear due to similarity with other texts or ambiguity in itself.

Core Idea
Mufassar rules out interpretation; muhkam additionally rules out abrogation — the highest certainty.

In Hanafi usul, not all clear texts are equally binding. Some are so clear that they cannot be twisted in meaning, but they might still be replaced by a later revelation. Commentary
This passage continues the hierarchy of textual clarity in the Hanafi usul tradition. After discussing khafi and mushkil, it introduces mujmal, which is more problematic: it is a word that has multiple possible meanings, and the intended cannot be determined without the speaker's explanation. The example given is the word 'riba' in the Quran: it literally means increase, but the legal riba is a specific type of increase. Since the word alone does not specify this, it is mujmal. The Hanafi view is that these are known only to Allah in this world, and we affirm them without understanding how. Key Terms
المجمل (al-mujmal): A word that has multiple possible meanings and cannot be understood without the speaker's clarification.
المتشابه (al-mutashabih): A word whose intended meaning is so hidden that it cannot be known in this world by anyone except Allah (and the Prophet in his time).
التأويل (al-ta'wil): The interpretation or final meaning of a text, especially of mutashabih passages.

Commentary
This passage defines dalaalah al-nass (indication of the text) as a key concept in Hanafi usul. It begins with an example from fasting: from the verse 'Then complete the fast' (Quran 2:187) we understand by dalaalah al-nass that the fast begins at dawn and that certain actions break it. The commentary then distinguishes dalaalah al-nass from qiyas: dalaalah al-nass is a purely linguistic understanding of the cause (illah) of a ruling, accessible to any Arabic speaker, not requiring ijtihad. It is definitive (qat'i), whereas qiyas is speculative (zanni) and requires a mujtahid. The passage then provides the classic example of 'Do not say to them 'uff'' (Quran 17:23): from the prohibition of expressing frustration (which causes harm), we understand by dalaalah al-nass that all forms of causing harm to parents are prohibited (e.g., hitting, reviling, imprisoning). This ruling is not derived by qiyas but by the linguistic indication of the text. Finally, the passage emphasizes that dalaalah al-nass is like the text itself in establishing punishments, even capital punishment, because it is definitive. The example of Ma'iz being stoned for adultery is cited: the stoning of Ma'iz is established by the text (nas), and the stoning of any other muhsan adulterer is established by dalaalah al-nass because the cause (ihsan and zina) is universally understood. The passage concludes that if dalaalah al-nass were not definitive, it could not establish punishments, since hudud and qisas are repelled by doubts.

Key Terms
دلالة النص (dalaalah al-nass): Linguistic indication of the cause of a ruling from the text, based on the Arabic language, not ijtihad or deduction.
علة (illah): The cause or reason for a legal ruling, understood directly from the text in dalaalah al-nass.

Dalalat al-Nass: Textual Indication and its Binding Force
Defining dalalat al-nass and establishing its equivalence to nass

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a case of dalalat al-nass, explain why it is considered qat'i in Hanafi usul, and distinguish it from qiyas.

Core Idea
Dalalat al-nass extends the ruling via the text's inherent cause, making it as binding as the explicit text.

This lesson introduces a fundamental Hanafi usul concept: dalalat al-nass. Unlike qiyas, which relies on human reasoning, dalalat al-nass derives its authority directly from the text's necessary implication. We'll examine the Quranic example of respecting parents and see how the prohibition of 'uff' implies the prohibition of any harm.

Guided Path
1
Understand ibarat al-nass vs dalalat al-nass
Identify the explicit ruling from the literal wording and the implied ruling from the underlying cause.
2
Extract the 'illa (cause)
From the context of the verse, determine why 'uff' is prohibited – it causes harm (adha).
The cause is inherent in the text, not added externally.
3
Apply the 'illa to new cases
Recognize that hitting and cursing also cause harm, so the prohibition extends to them by dalalat al-nass.
4
Evaluate definitiveness
Understand that this indication is qat'i because it is based on the text's necessary meaning, not personal opinion.
This allows punishments (hudud) to be established.
5
Distinguish from qiyas
Note that qiyas involves independent reasoning and is zanni, while dalalat al-nass is tied directly to the text.
This is a key difference between Hanafi and Shafi'i usul.

Commentary
This passage from al-Shashi's Usul defines and illustrates the concept of dalalat al-nass (textual indication). The author begins by presenting the Quranic prohibition of saying 'uff' to parents as the prime example. Through 'ibarat al-nass (direct expression), only the prohibition of uttering 'uff' is established. However, dalalat al-nass extends this prohibition to hitting and cursing because the underlying cause ('illa) is causing harm (adha), which is the necessary meaning (ma'na lazim) of the text. The key principle is that dalalat al-nass is treated like the text itself (bi-manzilat al-nass) in terms of legal force. This means rulings derived through dalalat al-nass are considered definitive (qat'i) in their indication, since they are based on the text's own meaning, not on personal opinion or ijtihad. This definitiveness is crucial for establishing punishments (hudud and qisas), which are otherwise averted by doubts—hence punishments can only be based on certain evidence. The author then provides further examples: the obligation of expiation for eating and drinking during Ramadan is derived by dalalat al-nass from the hadith of the Bedouin who had intercourse, because the cause (breaking the fast) is common; and the prohibition of selling during Friday prayer is applied only when the sale actually prevents attending the prayer, as the cause is obstruction. Finally, a subtle point: if a community considers 'uff' as a form of respect rather than harm, the prohibition would not apply, because the underlying cause (harm) is absent, even though the apparent text (zahir) is general. This illustrates that dalalat al-nass prioritizes the 'illa over the general wording.

Key Terms
دلالة النص (dalalat al-nass): The indication of the text to a ruling not explicitly stated, but necessarily implied by the underlying cause of the explicit ruling.
عبارة النص (ibarat al-nass): The direct, literal expression of the text that establishes a ruling by its very wording.
علة (illa): The effective cause or rationale behind a legal ruling, by which the ruling extends to similar cases.

The Rule of the Implied (Muqtada) and Its Limitation to Necessity
Stating the rule that the implied is limited to the extent of necessity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the implied meaning (muqtada) is limited to the extent of necessity and apply it to determine the scope of an implied term in a legal utterance.

Core Idea
The implied meaning is only established to the extent necessary for the statement to be valid.

In Hanafi usul, the implied meaning (al-muqtaḍā) is not a free interpretive device; it is strictly constrained by necessity. This lesson unpacks the rule that the muqtada is estimated 'to the extent of necessity' (bi-qadr al-ḍarūra). We will see how this rule operates through the classical example of divorce: when a man says 'You are divorced' but intends three, only one divorce takes effect because the necessity for the utterance to be valid is satisfied by one. Understanding this rule prevents arbitrary expansion of implied meanings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its basis
The rule states that the muqtada is established only by necessity and is estimated to that extent. The basis is the need for the speech to be valid (sihhat al-kalām).
Necessity means the minimal condition for correctness.
2
Apply the rule to the talaq example
The utterance 'You are divorced' requires an implied term (the number of divorces). The necessity for validity is met by one divorce; therefore only one is estimated as mentioned, not three.
Intention cannot extend the implication beyond necessity.
3
Recognize the limit of the rule
The muqtada is not an open-ended tool. If the text can be understood without an implication, no implication is made. The rule ensures that implied meanings remain within the bounds of what is strictly needed.
This distinguishes muqtada from other interpretive devices like bayān.

Commentary
This passage lays down a fundamental usul rule regarding the implied meaning (al-muqtada). The implied meaning is not arbitrary; it is only established to the extent that is necessary for the speech to be valid (lit. 'for the correction of speech'). The necessity is minimal. An example is given from the chapter of divorce: if a man says 'You are divorced' and intends three divorces, the word 'three' is not mentioned but is implied. However, the necessity for the statement to be valid only requires one divorce, so only one divorce is effected. The intention for three does not valid, because the implication is limited to the necessity. This illustrates that the implied meaning is not an open door for arbitrary intentions; it is tightly constrained.
