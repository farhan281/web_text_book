Three Fiqh Cases Proving Mushtarak Cannot Cover Multiple Meanings
Three legal cases demonstrating the rule against using mushtarak for multiple meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key argument from ijma and apply the Hanafi rule that mushtarak cannot be used for multiple meanings simultaneously using the cases of qur'u, mawla, and mithl.

Core Idea
A mushtarak word cannot be applied to both its meanings at once – ijma on qur'u and Hanafi rulings on mawla and mithl prove this.

This passage from Al-Usul al-Shashi presents a powerful proof that a word with multiple meanings (mushtarak) cannot be used to convey all those meanings simultaneously. The proof hinges on the consensus (ijma) of scholars regarding the Quranic word 'qur'u', which is interpreted either as menstruation or purity, but never both. Imam Muhammad and Imam Abu Hanifa then provide concrete fiqh cases – bequests to 'mawali' and the statement 'you are like my mother' – that reinforce this principle. By studying these cases, you will understand how Hanafi usul uses both consensus and substantive law to establish a rule of interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the ijma argument on qur'u
Understand that the scholars agreed that qur'u must mean either hayd or tuhr, not both. This ijma proves that a mushtarak cannot be used for multiple meanings simultaneously.
The ijma is used as a dalil (proof) that mushtarak does not cover both meanings.
2
Apply to the mawla case
If someone bequeaths to 'mawali' of a tribe, and the tribe has both upper (masters) and lower (slaves) clients, the word 'mawla' cannot cover both groups. Since there is no preponderance (tarjih), the bequest is void for both.
This shows the practical consequence: the ruling is void.
3
Apply to the mithl case
Saying 'you are like my mother' (anti 'alayya mithlu ummi) does not automatically make a zihar (prohibited comparison). Because 'mithl' is like mushtarak between honor and prohibition, only the husband's intention can give it the prohibition meaning.
This case shows that even a word that is not a true mushtarak but treated as such requires specification.
4
Extract the general rule
From these three cases, the Hanafi principle is derived: a mushtarak word cannot be applied to all its meanings at once; it must be restricted to one meaning by evidence or preponderance.
This is a key rule in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.
5
Evaluate the strength of the proof
The combination of ijma (on qur'u) and two practical furu' (mawla, mithl) creates a consistent and strong argument against using mushtarak for multiple meanings. The ijma provides a textual basis, while the furu' demonstrate that the rule is applied consistently in fiqh.
This method of proof is typical in Hanafi usul: using both transmitted sources (naql) and derived cases (istidlal).

Commentary
This passage presents three concrete legal cases to prove that a mushtarak word cannot be used for multiple meanings simultaneously. First, the word 'qur'u' in the Quran (2:228) is interpreted by Hanafis as menstruation and by Shafi'is as purity; the ijma (consensus) that it must be one or the other shows it cannot mean both. Second, Imam Muhammad ruled that a bequest to 'mawali' (clients) of a tribe is void if the tribe has both upper clients (masters) and lower clients (slaves), because the word 'mawla' covers both but cannot be applied to both at once. Third, Abu Hanifa ruled that saying 'you are like my mother' is not automatically zihar (a prohibited analogy) because 'mathal' is like mushtarak between honor and prohibition; only if the husband intends the aspect of prohibition through his intention does it become zihar. The commentary explains that each case reinforces the rule that mushtarak cannot cover multiple realities.

Resorting to Figurative Meaning When Literal is Impossible or Abandoned
Explains when to use figurative meaning in oaths based on literal feasibility and common usage.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for interpreting oaths when the literal meaning is impossible, abandoned, or commonly used, and distinguish between the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn when both meanings are common.

Core Idea
When literal is impossible or abandoned, figurative is required; when both are common, preference depends on the definition of 'common'.

In Hanafi usul, the interpretation of oaths (yamin) hinges on whether the literal meaning of the wording is practically feasible. This lesson unpacks the three categories established in Usul al-Shashi: al-muta'azzira (impossible), al-mahjura (abandoned), and al-musta'mala (commonly used). For the first two, there is consensus that one must resort to figurative meaning. The third category introduces a key dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, which turns on how 'common figurative' is defined. By mastering this taxonomy, you will be able to navigate similar interpretive challenges in oaths and legal speech.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
Locate in the passage the three categories: muta'azzira (literal impossible customarily), mahjura (literal abandoned in custom or religion), and musta'mala (literal commonly used).
The first two categories have consensus (ittifaq) that figurative must be used. The third category has ikhtilaf.
2
Apply the rule for impossible and abandoned
For each category, note the ruling: when the literal is impossible or abandoned, one turns to the figurative meaning by default. Work through the examples: the tree, the pot, the empty well, and placing the foot in a house.
The examples illustrate that the impossible literal is not merely rationally impossible but customarily impossible.
3
Analyze the example of the well and the wheat
Study the well example: if the well is not filled, literal drinking (kar') is impossible, so the oath turns to scooping (ightiraf). Similarly, the wheat example introduces the dispute when both literal and figurative are common.
Note that when the well is full, there is a dispute: Abu Hanifa says the literal (kar') is intended, but due to custom it is abandoned, so figurative still applies.
4
Examine the dispute when both are common
Read the section where both literal and figurative are in common use. Abu Hanifa prefers the literal because the original in speech is the literal. The Sahibayn prefer the figurative because it encompasses more meanings and is more appropriate for oaths.
The dispute is illustrated by the oath not to eat wheat: Abu Hanifa's literal interpretation means any eating of the wheat breaks the oath; the Sahibayn's figurative interpretation restricts it to common consumption.
5
Understand the definitional dispute over 'majaz muta'arif'
The passage explains that Iraqi scholars define 'common figurative' by communication (tafahum), while Balkhi scholars define it by transaction (ta'amul). This affects cases like eating human flesh or pork: according to Abu Hanifa it is called meat (communication), so oath is broken; according to the Sahibayn it is not customarily eaten (transaction), so oath is not broken.
This definitional difference is the root of the practical ikhtilaf. The scholars of Transoxiana attribute the Iraqi view to Abu Hanifa and the Balkhi view to the Sahibayn.

The author categorizes situations into three types: (1) al-muta'azzira (impossible), (2) al-mahjura (abandoned), and (3) al-musta'mala (commonly used). For the first two, there is consensus (ittifaq) that one must turn to the figurative meaning (majaz). 

Example of impossible: If someone swears not to eat from a tree or a pot, eating the tree or pot itself is impossible in custom (even if rationally possible), so the oath is taken to refer to the fruit of the tree or the food in the pot. If he eats the tree itself with effort, he does not break his oath. Similarly, if he swears not to drink from a well, and the well is not filled, the literal drinking directly (kar') is impossible, so it turns to scooping (ightiraf) – i.e., drinking with hands or a vessel. If he drinks directly with difficulty, he still does not break his oath. 

Example of abandoned: If he swears not to place his foot in someone's house, placing the foot is physically possible but customarily abandoned; people don't normally intend to place a foot without entering. So the oath is interpreted as not entering. Similarly, appointing someone as an agent for litigation itself (i.e., to engage in disputes) is abandoned both religiously and customarily, so the agency is interpreted broadly to include both admitting and denying claims.

The third case – when the literal is commonly used and there is a common figurative – leads to a dispute. If the literal has no common figurative, the literal is preferred by consensus. If it has a common figurative, Abu Hanifa prefers the literal because the original is the literal and there is no need to abandon it. The two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) prefer the figurative because it encompasses more meanings and is more appropriate for oaths where intent is broad.

A further dispute concerns the definition of 'common figurative' (majaz muta'arif). The scholars of Iraq (followed by Abu Hanifa) define it by common communication (tafahum) – i.e., what people understand in speech. The scholars of Balkh (followed by the two companions) define it by common transaction (ta'amul) – i.e., what people customarily deal with. This difference affects cases like an oath not to eat meat: if one eats human flesh or pork, according to Abu Hanifa he breaks his oath because linguistically they are called meat, while according to the two disciples he does not break because people do not customarily eat such meats.

The passage concludes by affirming that the literally abandoned meaning (whether religiously or customarily) is treated like the customarily abandoned, as Muslims naturally avoid what is religiously objectionable. The overall rule: when the literal is impossible or abandoned, figurative becomes the default; when both are common, the Hanafi position depends on the school's definition of common usage.

Key Terms
متعذرة (muta'azzira): A literal meaning that is impossible to actualize customarily, even if rationally possible.
مهجورة (mahjura): A literal meaning that is physically possible but abandoned by people in custom or religious practice.
مستعملة (musta'mala): A literal meaning that is in common usage among people, as opposed to abandoned.
مجاز متعارف (majaz muta'arif): A figurative meaning that is commonly understood or used in communication (tafahum) or in transaction (ta'amul), depending on the school.

Abandoning Literal Meanings Due to Context

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When context cannot bear literal meaning, the word becomes metaphor — for Abu Hanifa in wording, for Sahibayn only in ruling.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi discusses the fifth way literal meanings are abandoned: through the indication of the context (dalalat mahall al-kalam). It explains that when the context does not accept the literal sense, the word is taken as a metaphor (majaz). The author provides examples from marriage, manumission, and the famous hadith on intentions, and highlights an intra-madhhab dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two companions regarding whether the substitution occurs in the wording or only in the ruling.

Not all contexts force abandonment; only when literal meaning is impossible rationally or legally.

The thamarah al-khilaf appears in cases like divorce oaths where the scope of the word's effect may differ.

Apply the rule: for a free woman saying 'I sold myself to you', the context (her freedom) rejects literal sale, so the words become a metaphor for marriage. Similarly, a master calling his older slave 'my son' is a metaphor for manumission.

Benefits of Usurped Property: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Dispute with Shafi'i on guaranteeing benefits of usurped property

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Benefits vanish; what vanishes cannot be guaranteed.

In this passage from Usul al-Shashi, we examine a classic debate between the Hanafi school and Imam al-Shafi'i: if someone uses a usurped property and then returns the physical object, must they compensate for the benefit of use? The Hanafi answer is no, based on the nature of benefits as fleeting accidents. The Shafi'i school argues that benefits are compensable, analogizing to hire (ijarah). This lesson unpacks the Hanafi proofs and their application to related cases like false testimony and illegal intercourse, revealing deeper usul principles about liability and equivalence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested ruling and the two positions
State the exact issue: whether benefits (manafi') of a usurped property are guaranteed when the substance is returned. Recognize that the Hanafi school says no, and the Shafi'i school says yes.
2

Understand the Shafi'i argument: benefits are compensable because hire contracts (ijarah) prove they have legal value.
3

The Hanafi school rebuts with three rational proofs: 1) Benefits are accidents that do not persist, so they arise in the usurper's possession and are owned by him. 2) Usurpation requires persistence; benefits lack it, so they cannot be usurped or destroyed. 3) Guarantee requires equivalence; benefits do not equal substances due to their transient nature.

The text extends the ruling to benefits of sexual intercourse (bida') in cases of false testimony of divorce, killing another's wife, or actual intercourse. In each, no guarantee for the benefits is owed, because the same principles apply.
Note the exception of dower (uqur) — it is not compensation for benefits but a deterrent.
5
Distinguish the exceptional case of ijarah
The Shafi'i relies on ijarah, but the Hanafi response is that compensability in hire is for necessity (darurah) and does not establish a general principle. This distinction is crucial to maintain the consistency of the rule.
Memorise: 'al-ijarah lil-darurah la tu'addil'

The Particle Waw: Conjunction or Condition?
Establishing the legal interpretation of the particle 'waw' in contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for interpreting the particle 'waw' in contractual statements and distinguish between conjunction (عطف) and state (حال) to determine legal obligations.

Core Idea
The particle 'waw' is presumed for conjunction, not condition, unless context clearly indicates otherwise.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi addresses a critical linguistic question: does the word 'and' (waw) create a condition? The answer determines whether a wife's statement 'Divorce me and you have a thousand' obligates the husband to pay. The Hanafi school, led by Abu Hanifa, holds that the waw is merely conjunctive, so no payment is due. We will explore the reasoning behind this and the dissenting view of the two disciples.

2
Distinguish between conjunction and state
Conjunction (عطف) simply joins statements. State (حال) describes a circumstance simultaneous with the action. The Hanafi default is conjunction.
State requires that the clause be fit to describe a circumstance at the time of the main action.
3

The literal meaning of 'waw' is conjunction. Deviation to state requires contextual evidence, which is absent here.

4
Determine the legal consequence
If conjunction, no condition is created; the divorce is without financial obligation. If state, the thousand becomes a condition, obligating the husband to pay.
The thamarah al-khilaf: practical difference in court rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the interpretation of the particle 'waw' in contractual language. The author first establishes the rule: if someone makes a statement that could be understood literally or metaphorically, their intention is accepted only in religious matters (between them and Allah), not in court, because the literal meaning is presumed. The example given is of a mudarabah contract where the investor says 'work with it in cloth' using 'waw' – the rule is that the 'waw' does not restrict the contract to cloth because the state is not suitable at the moment of taking the money. Then Abu Hanifa's specific ruling is presented: if a wife says 'Divorce me and you have a thousand,' and the husband divorces, she owes nothing according to Abu Hanifa, because the 'waw' is conjunctional and does not create a condition. The commentary explains that the woman's statement is a mere conjunction without legal effect, unlike the dissenting opinion of the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) who interpret the 'waw' as indicating a state, making the thousand a condition for the divorce and thus obligatory. The author supports Abu Hanifa's view by noting that divorce is originally without compensation, and the statement does not resemble a vow or conditional formula.

The Nature of Suspended Causes: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Explains dispute on whether suspended statements are immediate causes or only upon condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on suspended (mu'allaq) statements and apply it to cases where the subject is absent at the time of utterance.

Core Idea
A suspended statement is not a cause until its condition is met; the cause requires a subject to attach to.

This passage introduces 'bayan al-taghyir' (explanatory change) and focuses on ta'liq (suspension) as an example. The core dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools concerns whether a suspended statement (e.g., 'You are divorced if you enter the house') becomes a cause (sabab) immediately or only upon fulfillment of the condition. Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining the validity and timing of conditional divorces and manumissions.

Guided Path
1
Define bayan al-taghyir and its two examples
Recognize that ta'liq (suspension) and istithna (exception) are types of explanatory change where the speaker diverts the word from its apparent meaning while clarifying intent.
2
State the Hanafi position
Hanafis hold that a suspended statement becomes a cause (sabab) only when the condition occurs, not before. This is because the cause requires a subject (mahall) to attach to.
Key principle: al-sabab yaftaqir ila al-mahall (the cause needs a locus).
3
State the Shafi'i position
Al-Shafi'i argues that the utterance itself is an immediate cause; the condition acts as a preventer (mani') that delays the effect.
For Shafi'is, the non-occurrence before condition is due to absence of condition, not failure of causation.
4
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference appears when the subject is absent at utterance, e.g., a man says to a non-wife: 'If I marry you, you are divorced.' Shafi'i rules suspension invalid; Hanafi rules it valid, with divorce occurring upon marriage.
Also applies to manumission of another's slave.
5
Apply the Hanafi condition for validity
For suspension to be valid when the subject is not owned at utterance, it must be attached to ownership or a cause of ownership (e.g., marriage, purchase). Otherwise, the condition does not create a proper attachment.
Example: 'If you enter the house, you are divorced' to a non-wife fails because not attached to marriage.

Commentary
This passage is a classic Hanafi usul al-fiqh exposition on the concept of 'bayān al-taghyīr' (explanatory change) and the legal dispute concerning suspended (mu'allaq) statements. The author first defines 'bayān al-taghyīr' as a linguistic operation where the speaker uses a word in a non-apparent meaning (e.g., metaphor or specification) while still clarifying intent. Two key examples are ta'līq (suspension) and istithnā' (exception). The core of the lesson is the disagreement between the Hanafis and al-Shafi'i on the nature of suspended causes. The Hanafis (imām Abū Hanīfa, Abū Yūsuf, and Muhammad al-Shaybānī) hold that a suspended statement (e.g., 'You are divorced if you enter the house') does not become a cause (sabab) for the legal effect until the condition is fulfilled. This is because the cause requires a subject (mahall) to attach to; before the condition, the subject may not exist (e.g., if said to a non-wife). Therefore, the non-occurrence of the effect before the condition is not due to the condition's absence but due to the original non-existence of the cause itself. Al-Shafi'i argues that the statement is a cause immediately because the utterance itself is valid and would necessitate the effect if not for the condition. The condition acts as a preventer (māni'), not as a constituent of the cause. Thus, the non-occurrence is attributed to the absence of the condition. The practical difference (thamaratu al-khilāf) appears in cases where the subject is absent at the time of utterance. For example, if a man says to an unrelated woman: 'If I marry you, you are divorced,' or to someone else's slave: 'If I own you, you are free,' al-Shafi'i declares the suspension invalid because the cause cannot attach to a non-existent subject. The Hanafis consider it valid because the cause forms only when the condition (marriage or ownership) creates the subject. Hence, if he later marries her, divorce occurs. However, if the suspension is added to a non-relevant condition (e.g., entering a house) without reference to ownership, it fails because at the moment of condition fulfillment, the subject appears but the cause was not properly structured to attach. The passage reinforces the Hanafi principle that a cause must be linked to its subject at the time of its formation; lacking that, it does not exist.

Key Terms
التعليق (al-ta‘līq): Suspension: attaching a legal effect to a future condition, such that the effect occurs only upon fulfillment of the condition.
بيان التغيير (bayān al-taghyīr): Explanatory change: a linguistic operation where the speaker uses a word in a non-apparent meaning (e.g., metaphor or specification) while clarifying the intended meaning.
سبب (sabab): Cause: something whose existence necessitates a legal effect and whose non-existence prevents it, in the context of suspended statements.
محل (mahall): Locus: the subject in which the legal effect occurs (e.g., the wife for divorce, the slave for manumission).

The Effect of Ta'liq on Rulings: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
First part of the dispute on ta'liq with two examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on the effect of conditional suspension (ta'liq), and apply the Hanafi distinction between manee' and sabab to derive rulings in new cases.

Core Idea
Absence of condition does not absolutely prevent a ruling; it only prevents that specific textual basis.

This lesson explores a fundamental usul dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is: when a ruling is suspended on a condition, does the absence of that condition completely bar the ruling, or may it be established by other evidence? The passage presents two key fiqh examples—marriage to a slave woman and maintenance for a divorced woman—to illustrate the practical consequences of this difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two fiqh examples
Locate the marriage (4:25) and maintenance (65:6) examples in the passage. Note the conditional structure.
Both examples involve a ruling suspended on a condition.
2
State the Shafi'i position and its dalil
Al-Shafi'i holds that absence of condition prevents the ruling absolutely. His dalil: the conditional text's apparent meaning implies negation when the condition is absent.
This is based on mafhum al-mukhalafah.
3
State the Hanafi position and its dalil
Hanafis hold that absence of condition only prevents the ruling from that specific text; it may be established by other general proofs (عمومات). Dalil: general verses and the talaq example.
The talaq example shows that a suspended divorce does not prevent an immediate absolute divorce.
4
Analyse the theoretical principle
For Shafi'i, ta'liq acts as a manee' (preventer) of the ruling. For Hanafi, it prevents the sabab (cause), not the absolute ruling.
The dispute reduces to whether absence of condition is a manee' or merely absence of a specific sabab.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi is a detailed usul discussion on the effect of ta'liq (suspension of a ruling on a condition) and its extension to description (wasf). It opens by presenting two fiqh examples where al-Shafi'i (calling him 'he') derives that the absence of a condition prevents the ruling absolutely, while the Hanafi school argues that it does not. The first example: the Quranic verse (4:25) permits marrying a believing slave woman only if one cannot afford a free woman. Al-Shafi'i holds that if a man can afford a free woman (has tawl), the condition for permissibility is absent, so marrying a slave woman is impermissible. The Hanafi response: the verse only suspends the ruling for that specific text; it does not negate the possibility of permissibility from other general proofs (e.g., the general permission to marry). The second example: the verse on maintenance for divorced women (65:6) only obligates spending if she is pregnant. Al-Shafi'i says that if not pregnant, no maintenance. Hanafis again say the absence of pregnancy does not prevent maintenance from being established by other general evidence (e.g., the general obligation of maintenance). The passage then delves into the theoretical principle: for al-Shafi'i, a conditional suspension (ta'liq) acts as a manee' (preventer) of the ruling before the condition occurs; for Hanafis, it prevents the sabab (cause), but the ruling can still arise from another cause. This is illustrated with the example of suspended talaq: if one says 'If you enter the house, you are divorced' and then immediately says 'You are divorced', Hanafis say the immediate divorce is valid, proving that the suspension does not negate the absolute possibility of divorce. For al-Shafi'i, such an immediate divorce would be invalid because the suspension prevents the ruling entirely until the condition is met. The passage then treats the attachment of a ruling to a name described by a quality as analogous to a condition. For example, the verse specifying 'believing slave girls' implies that non-believing slave girls are excluded. Al-Shafi'i takes this as mafhum al-mukhalafah (opposite implication) and forbids marrying a Kitabi slave woman. Hanafis reject mafhum al-mukhalafah as a proof and allow marriage to Kitabi slave women by general permission. The text concludes by clarifying that the description is conceptually like a condition, and the dispute essentially revolves around the validity of mafhum al-mukhalafah. The Hanafi position is that absence of description does not negate the ruling; the ruling can be established by other proofs. Throughout, the passage emphasizes that the Hanafi school relies on general texts (عمومات) to uphold rulings when specific conditional texts are not applicable.

Key Terms
التعليق (al-ta'liq): The suspension of a ruling on the occurrence of a condition, e.g., 'if X, then Y'.
الشرط (al-shart): A condition upon which the ruling is suspended; its absence negates the ruling in the specific text but not absolutely.
الوصف (al-wasf): A description (e.g., 'believing') attached to a noun that specifies the ruling; treated like a condition in terms of effect on the ruling.
المفهوم المخالف (al-mafhum al-mukhalif): The opposite implication: that the ruling applies only to the described case and not to others.

Bayan al-Darurah – Silence as Clarification
Explaining how silence acts as clarification with examples and a dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of bayan al-darurah to identify when silence in a contract or ambiguous situation constitutes a legal clarification.

Core Idea
Silence in a context demanding speech is itself a declaration – bayan al-darurah.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi introduces the principle that silence, when a person is expected to speak, can serve as a legal clarification. In contracts like mudaraba and bequests, specifying one party's share automatically clarifies the other's. The same logic applies to ambiguous divorce: intercourse with one wife clarifies which was divorced. However, Abu Hanifa rejects this for ambiguous manumission because intercourse with a slave woman can be lawful due to either ownership or marriage, so it does not determine the basis. The two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) disagree, creating a classic Hanafi internal dispute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Read the first line: 'likewise, if we clarify the share of the owner of capital and remain silent about the share of the mudarib, it is a clarification.' This establishes that silence operates as clarification when there is a context that requires utterance.
The principle is called bayan al-darurah – clarification through necessity.
2
Apply to contracts and bequests
Note the examples: mudaraba (silence on the agent's share), mazara'a (sharecropping), and bequest (clarifying one recipient's share clarifies the other). Work through how each case demonstrates the principle.
In each, the missing share is filled by implication of the remainder.
3
Apply to ambiguous divorce
If a man divorces one of two wives ambiguously and then has intercourse with one, that intercourse clarifies the divorce in the other. The act indicates preservation of marriage with the non-divorced wife.
This is accepted by all Hanafi scholars.
4
Identify the exception: ambiguous manumission
Abu Hanifa distinguishes between divorce and manumission. In divorce, the primary purpose is offspring, so intercourse indicates desire to preserve marriage. In manumission, the purpose of intercourse is gratification, so it does not indicate preservation of ownership. Thus intercourse does not clarify which slave was freed.
The two disciples (Sahibayn) disagree and analogize to divorce.
5
Analyze the talil (ratio legis)
Abu Hanifa's reasoning: permissible intercourse with a slave woman could be due to ownership (she is still owned) or marriage (she was freed and then married). Since both are possible, the act does not single out one basis. In divorce, the likelihood of the woman remarrying is low, so intercourse indicates original marriage persistence.
This is a pure usul al-fiqh reasoning exercise – comparing the 'illa in two cases.

Core Idea
The sunna in all pillars is prolongation, not repetition; repetition is only a substitute when prolongation is impossible.

First Type of al-Ihtijaj bi-la Dalil: Act Becoming Mubah under Duress
Begins new topic on arguing without evidence, discussing the first type: making an act mubah under ikrah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on acts under duress and evaluate the basis of the ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf/al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Necessity can lift a prohibition, but does the act become truly mubah or merely a concession?

This passage opens a new usul topic: rulings derived from necessity rather than explicit textual evidence. The first type concerns acts performed under compulsion (ikrah). The Hanafi school, based on a Quranic exception, holds that such acts become permissible, while other authorities maintain the prohibition remains but a concession is granted. Understanding this dispute clarifies how the Sharia balances text and necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
What is the specific question being disputed? Is the act under duress rendered mubah (permissible) or does its prohibition persist with a concession to perform it?
The dispute revolves around the legal status of the act itself, not the sinfulness of the person.
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
Abu Hanifa holds that the act becomes mubah. His dalil is Quran 6:119: 'He has detailed for you what He has forbidden except what you are compelled to.' He interprets the exception as removing the prohibition entirely.
The verse makes an exception from 'what He has forbidden', implying the forbidden thing is no longer forbidden when compelled.
3
State the opposing position and its proof
Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i hold that the prohibition remains, but a concession (rukhsa) is given to preserve life. Their proof is the principle that a necessity does not change the intrinsic ruling; it only permits the act to avoid greater harm.
They see the verse as an exception from the obligation to abide by the prohibition, not from the prohibition itself.
4
Analyze the interpretation of the dalil
Both sides use the same verse. Abu Hanifa reads it as a direct exception from the act of forbidding, making the act halal. The opponents read it as an exception from the consequence (sin) when necessity demands action, but the act remains haram in essence.
This is a classic dispute over the scope of the word 'except' (illa).
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: If a person under extreme duress refuses the compelled act and dies, Abu Hanifa considers him sinful for not taking the mubah option, while Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i consider him not sinful because the prohibition remained and he endured hardship.
This difference affects fatwa in real cases of duress.

Coercion and the Ruling of Prohibited Acts: A Usul Dispute
Usul dispute on whether coercion changes the ruling from haram to mubah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of istithna (exception) to Quran 6:119 to derive that coercion makes a prohibited act mubah, and evaluate the opposing position of Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i based on their interpretation of Quran 6:145.

Core Idea
Coercion makes the prohibited act mubah, not merely excusable – the Quranic exception lifts the prohibition entirely.

The effect of coercion (ikrah) on prohibited acts is a classic usul dispute. The central question: does compulsion change the very ruling of the act (from haram to mubah), or does it only remove sin while the prohibition remains intact? This lesson examines the arguments of Abu Hanifa against Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i, focusing on how each school reads the relevant Quranic verses. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to handle texts that contain exceptions and to weigh conflicting evidences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested issue
Clearly state the question: does coercion render a prohibited act (like eating carrion) mubah (permissible) in the person's right, or does it merely remove the sin while the prohibition persists?
The practical difference is seen when the coerced person refuses and dies.
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
Abu Hanifa holds that the act becomes mubah. His proof is Quran 6:119: 'He has detailed for you what is forbidden to you except what you are compelled to.' The word 'except' is an istithna from the object of prohibition, meaning the excepted is not forbidden – hence permissible.
Focus on the linguistic operation: istithna min maf'ul al-tahrim.
3
State the opposing position and its proof
Abu Yusuf (in one narration) and al-Shafi'i hold that the prohibition remains but a concession (rukhsah) is given. Their proof is Quran 6:145: 'But if one is forced by hunger, not inclining to sin, then Allah is Forgiving and Merciful.' They argue this only removes sin, not the prohibition.
Note that the opposing view treats coercion as a temporary dispensation, not a change in ruling.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response and tarjih
The Hanafi response argues that verse 6:119 is more specific: it directly excepts the compelled-to from the category of forbidden. Verse 6:145 addresses a different scenario (inclination to sin vs. true coercion). The text declares that 6:119 'yufidu al-ibahah' (indicates permissibility).
The practical weight: the text favors Abu Hanifa's view as the mufta bihi.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if a coerced person refrains from eating carrion and dies, according to Abu Hanifa he is sinful (he refrained from a permissible act); according to Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i he is not sinful (the prohibition remained, so refraining is correct).
This illustrates how a usul difference can lead to a fatwa difference in life-or-death situations.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute regarding the effect of coercion (ikrah) on prohibited acts. It begins by describing two types of concession: the first where one remains patient and is rewarded, the second where the act becomes permissible. The author then presents the core disagreement: Abu Hanifa holds that coercion makes the act mubah (permissible) in the person's right, citing Quran 6:119 as an exception from prohibition. Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i hold that the prohibition remains but a concession is given to preserve life; the act does not become truly mubah. The commentary then analyzes the Quranic verses, arguing that the verse 'He has detailed for you...' is stronger evidence because it explicitly excepts the compelled-to from the category of prohibited. It further explains that if a person knowingly refrains from the permissible act and dies, he is sinful, like a suicide. The basis of prohibition is to safeguard life and religion, and when life is at risk, the prohibition falls.

Key Terms
الإكراه (al-ikrah): Compulsion that overcomes a person's will, making him do what he otherwise would not.
الرخصة (al-rukhsah): A concession that allows normally prohibited acts due to necessity.
الاستثناء (al-istithna): An exception that excludes something from a general ruling.
