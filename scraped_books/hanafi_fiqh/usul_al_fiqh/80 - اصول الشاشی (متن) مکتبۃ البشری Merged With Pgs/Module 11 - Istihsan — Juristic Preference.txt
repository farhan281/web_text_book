Determined Metaphor in Contracts: Marriage with Hiba without Intention
Analysing the validity of metaphor without intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a determined metaphor (majaz muta'ayyin) does not require intention of metaphor, and derive the ruling for marriage concluded with hiba/tamlik words.

Core Idea
When literal meaning is impossible, the metaphor is determined and intention is irrelevant.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi tackles a subtle linguistic-legal problem: can marriage be contracted using words like 'hiba' (gift) or 'tamlik' (transfer of ownership) without explicitly intending the metaphor? The author defends the Hanafi position that yes, and intention is not needed because the subject (a free woman) makes the literal meaning of ownership impossible, so the metaphor is determined. The passage also answers an objection by showing that literal possibility can exist in extraordinary circumstances (apostasy), drawing an analogy with oaths on normally impossible acts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic issue
Determine whether a word used in a contract is literal or metaphorical. In this case, 'hiba' and 'tamlik' are literally about property, but used for marriage.
The literal meaning (ownership of a free person) is impossible.
2
Apply the principle of determined metaphor
When the subject makes the literal meaning impossible, the metaphor is considered determined (majaz muta'ayyin). No intention is needed to specify the metaphorical meaning because there is only one possible interpretation.
Hanafi principle: intention is for resolving ambiguity; if none remains, intention is unnecessary.
3
Analyse the objection and response
Objection: metaphor requires at least theoretical possibility of literal meaning. How can 'I gift myself' be a metaphor for marriage when a free woman cannot be owned? Response: literal possibility exists in extreme scenarios like apostasy and capture. Analogous to oaths on touching the sky, where expiation is due despite normal impossibility.
Qiyas with oaths: if swearing to do the impossible, expiation is obligatory due to potential divine intervention.
4
Distinguish from other cases
Contrast with using words of emancipation for a free married woman: here the literal meaning ('freeing from service') is possible, so intention is required to specify the metaphorical meaning (divorce).
When literal meaning is possible, the metaphor is not determined and intention is needed.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion on the use of metaphorical expressions in contracts, specifically concerning marriage. The author, after discussing the definition of metaphor, addresses a critical question: can words like 'sale' or 'gift' (hiba) be used to contract marriage? The ruling established is that marriage is validly contracted with such words, and without the need for intention of metaphor, because the nature of the subject (a free woman) determines that the literal meaning (ownership of the person) is impossible. Therefore, the word is necessarily metaphorical, and intention is superfluous. The passage presents an objection based on the principle that for a metaphor to be valid, the literal meaning must be at least theoretically possible. Since a free woman cannot be owned, how can the words 'I gift myself' or 'I sell myself' be considered metaphorical for marriage? The author's response is creative: the literal possibility exists in an extreme scenario (apostasy and capture), drawing an analogy with oaths involving miraculous events (touching the sky, turning a stone to gold), where expiation is due despite normal impossibility, because of the potential for divine intervention. Thus, the metaphor is valid. The passage also distinguishes between using tamlik for a free woman (determined metaphor) and using words of emancipation (ʿitq) for a free married woman (undetermined, requires intention). This shows the subtlety of Hanafi usul in applying linguistic principles to contract law.

المجاز المتعين (al-majaz al-muta'ayyin): A metaphor that is determined by the context, leaving no possibility for the literal meaning

Abandoning Literal Meaning by Indication of the Speaker's State
Explains abandoning literal meaning by speaker's wisdom and circumstantial evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when the literal meaning (haqiqah) of a word is abandoned due to the speaker's state (hal) and apply this principle to derive rulings for agency contracts.

Core Idea
The literal meaning is abandoned when the speaker's state indicates a different intended meaning.

In Hanafi usul, the literal meaning (haqiqah) of a word is not always binding. The context, including the state of the speaker, can override the literal denotation. This lesson explores the principle through the example of an agent appointed to buy meat: a traveler’s need for immediate food shifts the meaning from raw to cooked meat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
Recognize that the literal meaning of a word (e.g., 'meat') may be abandoned due to the state of the speaker or the circumstances.
The principle is based on the wisdom (hikmah) of the commander.
2
Analyze the agency example
Consider two scenarios: a traveler on the road vs. a householder. Determine how the state of the principal affects the interpretation of the agent's task.
The traveler's state indicates need for immediate consumption; the householder's state indicates preparation for cooking.
3
Apply the principle
Derive the ruling: the agent must buy cooked/grilled meat for the traveler and raw meat for the householder, even though the literal instruction was 'buy meat' without qualification.
This shows that the literal meaning (haqiqah) is abandoned due to contextual indication (hal).
4
Understand the dalil
The Quranic verse 'He does not approve for His servants disbelief' shows that literal command for disbelief is reinterpreted as rebuke due to Allah's wisdom.
This supports the principle that the attribute of the commander (hikmat al-ameer) can override the literal meaning.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on when the literal meaning of words is abandoned in Islamic legal interpretation. It opens by stating a principle: disbelief is inherently evil, and Allah, being Wise, does not command it in its literal sense—so the word 'command' when applied to disbelief is interpreted not as an obligation but as a rebuke or permission, based on the attribute of the Commander (hikmat al-ameer). The author then gives two concrete examples from Hanafi fiqh. First, the case of an agent appointed to buy meat: if the principal is a traveler, the agent should buy cooked or grilled meat because that is immediately edible; if the principal is at home, the agent buys raw meat suitable for cooking. The literal meaning of 'meat' is abandoned due to the state (hal) of the principal. Second, the oath of promptness (yameen al-fawr): when someone swears not to eat lunch after being invited, the oath is understood to apply only to that specific invitation. If he later eats lunch elsewhere, he does not break his oath. Similarly, a husband's conditional divorce when his wife intends to leave is limited to that specific instance. The text then reinforces the principle by citing Quranic evidence and scholarly commentary. It traces the origin of the oath of promptness to Imam Abu Hanifa, who derived it from a hadith about Jabir and his son, and explains the linguistic derivation of 'fawr' from boiling, indicating speed or immediate anger. The passage concludes by emphasizing that the ruling in such oaths is tied to the immediate state (al-hal), not extended to future actions.

Oath of Promptness (Yameen al-Fawr)
Explains abandoning literal meaning by speaker's wisdom and circumstantial evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the oath of promptness (yameen al-fawr), identify its conditions, and apply the ruling that it is limited to the immediate situation.

Core Idea
An oath with permanent wording may be limited to the immediate context, not extending to the future.

Imam Abu Hanifa introduced a third type of oath: one that is permanent in wording but temporary in meaning. This 'oath of promptness' (yameen al-fawr) applies only to the immediate situation, not to future actions. This lesson explores the concept through examples like the lunch invitation and the conditional divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the new category
Recognize that before Abu Hanifa, oaths were either permanent or temporary. Abu Hanifa introduced a third type: permanent in wording but temporary in meaning.
Derived from the hadith of Jabir and his son.
2
Analyze the lunch example
When someone swears 'I will not have lunch' after being invited, the oath applies only to that specific invitation. He may eat lunch elsewhere without breaking his oath.
3
Apply to the divorce example
When a husband says 'If you go out, you are divorced' as the wife stands to leave, it applies only to that immediate act of leaving. If she leaves later, she is not divorced.
4
Understand the linguistic basis
The word 'fawr' means boiling, borrowed for speed/immediacy. The oath is called 'yameen al-fawr' because it issues from the boiling of anger and applies to the moment.

Deficient Performance in Worship: Compensation by Similar (Mithl)
Applies the rule of deficient performance to specific worship cases

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a deficient act of worship can be compensated with a similar (mithl) and apply the Hanafi rule to examples from prayer, tawaf, and missed prayers during tashriq.

Core Idea
A deficient performance is only compensable if the Lawgiver or reason provides a similar (mithl) to fill the deficiency.

This passage from Usul al-Shashi applies the earlier theoretical rule of deficient performance (ada' qasir) to concrete acts of worship. It distinguishes between deficiencies that have a legal or rational 'mithl' (similar) and those that do not. By working through examples such as leaving the adjustment of prayer postures (tadil al-arkan), circumambulating while impure, and omitting wajib acts, you will see how the Hanafi school determines whether compensation is possible. The passage also addresses a cross-madhhab dispute on whether missed tashriq prayers should include the takbir.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule
Extract the principle that a deficient performance cannot be compensated unless a mithl exists either by reason (aql) or revelation (shar').
This is the foundational rule guiding all subsequent examples.
2
Distinguish mithl types
Note the two categories: mithl bi al-aql (e.g., paying good coins instead of counterfeit) and mithl bi al-shar' (e.g., dam for tawaf, sajda sahw for wajib omissions).
The text explains this in the latter part: 'إيجاب المثل إما بالعقل... وإما بالسمع'.
3
Apply to tadil al-arkan
Tadil al-arkan (proper posture) is a qualitative description attached to a pillar; it has no independent mithl in isolation, so it cannot be compensated. Only sin remains.
Contrast this with wajib acts like qunut, which do have a mithl (sajda sahw).
4
Apply to tawaf with impurity
Here the Lawgiver (shar') has appointed a mithl: a blood sacrifice (dam) compensates the deficiency. Thus the tawaf is valid but requires dam.
This shows that even a deficient performance can be acceptable if a jabar exists.
5
Analyze the takbir dispute
When making up prayers missed during tashriq, the Hanafi school says no loud takbir because the takbir is a time-specific ritual; Shafi'i requires it to match the original. The Hanafi response: the mithl (takbir) is not transferable to other days.
This is a clear thamarah al-khilaf: the actual performance differs between schools.

The Principle of Ada' and Qada' in Liability
States the general principle and its applications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that performance (ada') is primary and substitution (qada') is only permitted when performance is impossible, and apply it to deposit, agency, and usurpation.

Core Idea
Performance of the exact thing owed is primary; substitution is only when return is impossible.

This lesson introduces a foundational rule in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the obligation to perform (ada') by delivering the very object owed, whether complete or defective, and the exceptional resort to substitution (qada') only when such delivery is impossible. This principle governs liabilities in contracts like deposit, agency, and usurpation, and distinguishes the Hanafi school from others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary obligation
Recognize that the text establishes ada' (performance) as the default duty: the exact thing taken or owed must be returned.
This applies to both complete and defective items.
2
Understand the role of qada'
Qada' (substitution) is only invoked when ada' is impossible, e.g., when the object is destroyed or fundamentally transformed.
Impossibility is key.
3
Apply to specific contracts
In deposit (wadi'ah), agency (wakalah), and usurpation (ghasb), the specific item is determined; the depositary, agent, or usurper cannot substitute an equivalent.
This affirms the primacy of ada'.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Note that al-Shafi'i holds that even a grossly changed object must be returned (ada') with compensation for deficiency, while Hanafis consider such transformation a reason for qada'.
This highlights the practical difference.

Qiyas vs Istihsan: The Dispute on Quality Compensation in Usurpation
Presents an intra-Hanafi dispute with proofs and rebuttals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the return of a defective or crime-tainted object in usurpation, and evaluate the proofs based on qiyas and istihsan.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa uses qiyas to attribute destruction to its cause; the Sahibayn use istihsan to protect the right to quality.

This lesson delves into a specific intra-Hanafi dispute that illustrates the tension between qiyas (analogical reasoning) and istihsan (juristic preference). The case involves a usurped slave who has committed a crime, making his blood permissible. After the slave is delivered to the buyer or owner, he perishes. Abu Hanifa and his two pupils, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, disagree on whether the usurper is freed from liability. Their debate reveals deeper usul principles about the priority of the cause of destruction and the compensability of quality.

Guided Path
1
State the case and the key facts
A slave whose blood is permissible (due to a crime) is delivered to the usurper and then perishes. The question is whether the usurper is liable.
The slave is 'mubah al-dam' because of jinaya.
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position and his proof
Abu Hanifa holds that the delivery counts as ada' and the usurper is freed, because the destruction is attributed to the original cause (the crime), not the delivery. He uses qiyas: the inner aspect (batin) of the analogy is strong.
The 'cause' is the crime; the destruction is linked to it, not to the usurper's act.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position and their proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that the delivery is incomplete due to the defect (the crime), so the usurper remains liable for the deficiency. They use istihsan: the right to quality is analogous to quantity, so the defect prevents full delivery.
They argue that the buyer's right to a defect-free item is violated.
4
Understand Abu Hanifa's rebuttal
Abu Hanifa responds that quality (jouda) has no independent form or meaning. It is an accident ('arad) that cannot be separated, and in ribawi goods, quality has no value because of the hadith: 'Its good and its bad are equal.' Therefore, the deficiency cannot be compensated separately.
This refutes the analogy to quantity.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
According to Abu Hanifa, the buyer recovers the full price from the seller and the value from the usurper? Actually the text says: the buyer recovers the value from the usurper and the full price from the seller. According to the Sahibayn, the buyer recovers only the deficiency (the difference between the market value of a sound slave and the defective one).
The dispute affects the allocation of loss between the usurper and the seller.

The Legal Substitute (al-Mithl al-Shar'i)
Explanation of substitution when no equivalent exists

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that the Lawgiver can establish a legal substitute (mithl shar'i) even when no natural resemblance exists, and apply it to the examples of fidyah and diyah.

Core Idea
The Lawgiver can mandate a substitute even where reason sees no equivalence.

In this passage, the author explains a key usul principle: when an obligation has no natural equivalent, the Lawgiver may designate a legal substitute that must be fulfilled. The examples of ransom (fidyah) for the decrepit old man unable to fast and blood money (diyah) for homicide illustrate that legal similarity can be established without rational analogy. This principle is foundational for understanding how divine commands operate beyond human reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
The text begins with an exception: if the Lawgiver brings a substitute despite no resemblance in form or meaning, it becomes a legal substitute (mithl shar'i) and its fulfillment is obligatory.
2
Recognize the examples
Two examples are given: fidyah (ransom) for the decrepit old man who cannot fast, and diyah (blood money) for homicide. In both cases, there is no rational similarity between the substitute and the original obligation.
Fidyah is a payment of food; diyah is monetary compensation.
3
Understand the reasoning
The commentator elaborates that reason alone cannot grasp the equivalence; e.g., fasting is starving the self while fidyah is feeding, and human life vs property are fundamentally different. Yet the Lawgiver establishes legal similarity (mumathalah shar'iyyah).
This is a type of istihsan or departure from qiyas.
4
Cite the evidence
The Quranic verse (2:184) is used as proof for the fidyah ruling, showing that the Lawgiver directly prescribed the substitute.
The verse is interpreted to apply to those who cannot fast.

Prohibition (al-Nahy): Definition and Implication
Definition and types of prohibition, and its primary implication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define prohibition (nahy) both linguistically and terminologically, distinguish between sensory and non-sensory prohibited acts, and state the Hanafi chosen view that the primary implication of nahy is prohibition (tahrim).

Core Idea
The prohibition form (la taf'al) primarily indicates prohibition, not mere dislike or guidance.

This lesson covers the definition of nahy (prohibition) as a demand to leave an act, issued by a superior to an inferior. The passage also explains the types of prohibited acts: sensory acts, whose wrongness is known by reason independently of revelation, and other acts. The text notes scholarly disagreement on the implication of the prohibition form, but affirms that the chosen view is that it indicates tahrim (prohibition), not just karahah (dislike) or other meanings.

Guided Path
1
Define nahy linguistically and terminologically
Linguistically, nahy means prevention. Terminologically, it is a demand to leave an act by speech from a superior, as in 'Do not do'.
2
Distinguish the types of prohibitions
The author mentions two types: prohibition of sensory actions (af'al hissiyyah) like adultery and drinking, and others (implied). Sensory acts are those known by the senses and do not depend on revelation for their existence or evilness.
Sensory acts are inherently evil (qabih) by rational judgment.
3
Note the ikhtilaf on the implication of the prohibition form
The prohibition form is used in various meanings (tahrim, karahah, guidance, etc.), but by agreement it is metaphorical in other than tahrim and karahah. The chosen view is that its primary implication is tahrim.
This mirrors the discussion on command (amr) which also has multiple meanings.
4
Apply the definition to the examples
Adultery and drinking alcohol are sensory acts; their prohibition is known both by reason and revelation. The form 'la taf'al' indicates that it is haram.
