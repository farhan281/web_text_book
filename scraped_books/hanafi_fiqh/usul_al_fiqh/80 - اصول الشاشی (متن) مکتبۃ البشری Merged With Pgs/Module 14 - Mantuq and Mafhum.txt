'Ibarah and Ishara: Two Pillars of Textual Implication in Hanafi Usul
Defines two types of textual indications with a Quranic example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between 'ibarah and ishara, apply the Hanafi rule that explicit wording establishes primary rulings while implied indication establishes secondary rulings, and cite the Quranic example of the poor emigrants to illustrate the derivation.

Core Idea
'Ibarah gives the direct ruling; ishara gives a secondary ruling from the same wording.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, a single text can convey multiple layers of legal meaning. Imam al-Shashi introduces two fundamental categories: 'ibarah (direct expression) and ishara (indication). Understanding the distinction is crucial for correctly deriving rulings from the Quran and Sunnah. This lesson unpacks the definitions and the famous example from Surah al-Hashr about the poor emigrants, showing how a verse about spoils also indicates a ruling on ownership transfer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms
Locate the definitions of 'ibarah and ishara in the passage. Note that 'ibarah is the ruling intended by the speech, while ishara is a secondary meaning established by the wording without being the primary intent.
The Arabic terms are 'عبارة النص' and 'إشارة النص'.
2
Understand the example
Read the Quranic verse (59:8) cited in the passage. See how it is used for both 'ibarah (entitlement to spoils) and ishara (poverty implies loss of property).
The verse is about the poor emigrants who deserve a share of the spoils.
3
Trace the derived ruling
Follow how the ishara leads to the ruling that seizure of Muslim property by a disbeliever in dar al-harb transfers ownership. The word 'poor' literally means lacking wealth, so their poverty must be real, implying their wealth was taken.
This ruling has practical implications for trade and booty.
4
Note the ikhtilaf
Recognize that Imam al-Shafi'i disagreed with this derivation, considering the term 'poor' as metaphorical. The Hanafi response relies on the principle that the default in language is literal usage (haqiqah).
This is a legitimate methodological difference between madhahib.

Commentary
This passage is a foundational section from Imam al-Shashi's Usul al-Fiqh, defining the different ways in which a legal text conveys its meaning. The author begins by stating that the 'attachments of texts' (mutafallaqat al-nusus) include four categories: expression ('ibarah), indication (isharah), implication (dalalah), and requirement (iqtida'). The focus here is on the first two. 'Ibarah al-nass is the direct, intended meaning of the text—the ruling that the words were put forth to convey. For example, the verse establishing the share of emigrants in spoils directly expresses their entitlement. Ishara al-nass, on the other hand, is a secondary meaning that is derived from the wording but was not the primary intent. It is established by the very wording (bi-nazm al-nass) but is not immediately apparent from one angle. The classic example is the same verse: though it primarily discusses entitlement to spoils, it also indicates that the disbelievers' seizure of the Muslims' property caused the Muslims' poverty, implying that such seizure transfers ownership to the disbelievers. The commentary notes that Imam al-Shafi'i did not accept this indication and considered the term 'poor' as metaphorical, but the Hanafi position rejects shifting to metaphor when the literal meaning is possible. This passage demonstrates how Hanafi usul expands the sources of law beyond the plain text to include implicit meanings derived from the text's wording.

Key Terms
عبارة النص ('Ibarah al-nass): The direct, intended meaning of the text for which the speech was formulated.
إشارة النص (Ishara al-nass): A secondary meaning established by the wording of the text but not the primary intent; it is evident from one perspective but not from another.
نظم النص (Nazm al-nass): The wording or structure of the text itself, as opposed to its implied meaning.

Dilalah al-Nass: The Indication of the Text
Explains how expiation for eating/drinking is derived from the hadith on intercourse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a ruling is established by dilalah al-nass and apply the Hanafi principle that a definitive cause extends the ruling to unmentioned cases.

Core Idea
Dilalah al-Nass: when the cause is certain, the ruling applies even without explicit mention – stronger than qiyas.

In Hanafi usul, some rulings are derived not from explicit words but from the text's intrinsic indication based on a clear underlying cause. This lesson explores the concept of dilalah al-nass using the classic example of expiation for breaking the fast in Ramadan, where intercourse is mentioned explicitly but eating and drinking are included by indication.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit text (nass)
Locate the direct ruling in the hadith: expiation for intercourse in Ramadan. This is the proven case (asl).
The hadith of Salamah ibn Sakhr provides the explicit ruling.
2
Extract the underlying cause (illa)
Determine the reason for the ruling: the act breaks the fast. Both intercourse and eating/drinking share this cause.
The cause is 'breaking the fast' – a definitive, textually indicated illa.
3
Apply dilalah al-nass
Since the cause is the same, the ruling of expiation extends to eating and drinking even though they are not mentioned. This is not qiyas but a stronger form of textual implication.
Hanafis consider dilalah al-nass as between nass and qiyas in strength.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Understanding that Shafi'is treat this as qiyas jali (obvious analogy) rather than dilalah al-nass, leading to minor differences in how the ruling is categorized.
This difference illustrates the distinct Hanafi usul hierarchy.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi is a matn with extensive commentary, discussing the distinction between explicit text (nass) and indication of the text (dilalah al-nass), and the role of the underlying cause (illa) in deriving rulings. The author begins by stating that expiation for intercourse in Ramadan is established by explicit hadith, while for eating and drinking it is by indication of the text, because the common cause is breaking the fast. The principle 'the ruling revolves upon the cause' is illustrated with several examples: if a community does not consider 'uff' as harmful, the prohibition is lifted because the cause (harm) is absent; the Friday verse prohibits sale only if it prevents attendance; oaths about hitting or speaking are interpreted based on the intended meaning (pain, understanding) not the literal act. The passage concludes with a definition of 'muqtada' (requisite) as an unstated element necessary for the text's meaning to be realized, and distinguishes it from 'mahdhuf' (deleted) and 'muqaddar' (implied). The commentary inserts clarifications from later scholars like Akram al-Nadwi and al-Shafi, reinforcing the Hanafi usul principle that meaning (ma'na) and cause (illa) take precedence over literal wording when the cause is definitive (qat'i).

Key Terms
النص (al-nass): Explicit wording that directly states a ruling without interpretation.
دلالة النص (dilalah al-nass): Indication of the text that extends a ruling based on a definitive cause.
العلة (al-illah): The underlying cause or effective reason for a ruling.

The Cause (Illah) Determines the Ruling: Oaths and the Requisite
Applies the 'illah principle to oaths and introduces muqtada.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle that legal rulings revolve around the illah (cause) and distinguish the muqtada (requisite) from other textual implications.

Core Idea
Rulings revolve around the cause – if the cause is absent, the ruling is absent, even if the form is present.

The previous lesson introduced dilalah al-nass. Here we explore its application to oaths and the concept of muqtada (requisite). The passage uses vivid examples: swearing not to hit, not to speak, and not to eat meat. In each case, the ruling is based on the underlying cause (pain, understanding, blood content) not the literal words. The lesson also defines muqtada as an unstated necessity for the text's meaning to be realized.

Guided Path
1
Identify the oath and its literal wording
For each oath (e.g., 'I will not hit', 'I will not eat meat'), note the explicit expression.
Oaths are interpreted based on common understanding ('urf) and the intended cause.
2
Determine the underlying cause (illah)
For hitting, the cause is causing pain; for speaking, the cause is conveying understanding; for eating meat, the cause is consuming blood-originating food.
The cause is deduced from convention and linguistic context.
3
Apply the principle that the ruling revolves around the cause
If the cause is present (e.g., causing pain by pulling hair), the oath is violated. If the cause is absent (e.g., striking a dead person), no violation.
Even if the literal act occurs, without the cause, the oath is not broken.
4
Understand the requisite (muqtada)
Muqtada is something additional to the text that must exist for the text's meaning to be realized. It is not stated but is logically necessary.
Example: The command 'free a slave' implies the existence of a slave to free.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Shashi is a matn with extensive commentary, discussing the distinction between explicit text (nass) and indication of the text (dilalah al-nass), and the role of the underlying cause (illa) in deriving rulings. The author begins by stating that expiation for intercourse in Ramadan is established by explicit hadith, while for eating and drinking it is by indication of the text, because the common cause is breaking the fast. The principle 'the ruling revolves upon the cause' is illustrated with several examples: if a community does not consider 'uff' as harmful, the prohibition is lifted because the cause (harm) is absent; the Friday verse prohibits sale only if it prevents attendance; oaths about hitting or speaking are interpreted based on the intended meaning (pain, understanding) not the literal act. The passage concludes with a definition of 'muqtada' (requisite) as an unstated element necessary for the text's meaning to be realized, and distinguishes it from 'mahdhuf' (deleted) and 'muqaddar' (implied). The commentary inserts clarifications from later scholars like Akram al-Nadwi and al-Shafi, reinforcing the Hanafi usul principle that meaning (ma'na) and cause (illa) take precedence over literal wording when the cause is definitive (qat'i).

Iqtida' (Implication) and the Rukn-Shart Distinction in Hanafi Usul
Explaining the Hanafi usul principle of iqtida through a transaction and manumission example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of iqtida to derive implied legal consequences and distinguish between implied pillars (arkan) and non-implied conditions (shurut).

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, implication (iqtida) establishes only pillars, not conditions; the implied must be of the same kind as the implicator.

This passage delves into one of the most subtle tools in the Hanafi usul arsenal: iqtida', or implication by necessity. You will see how a simple command to free a slave for a price triggers a chain of implied legal acts — a sale, an agency, and their acceptance. More importantly, the passage draws a sharp line between what is automatically included by implication (pillars) and what is not (conditions). This distinction is not merely theoretical; it determines whether a gift is valid without possession. Let us trace the reasoning step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit utterance and the implied necessity
The commander says 'Free your slave for me for a thousand dirhams.' Since freeing requires ownership, and the commander does not currently own the slave, a sale is necessarily implied. Also, because the commander wants the freeing on his behalf, an agency is implied. Recognize what must be true for the command to be valid.
The muqtada (implied meaning) is treated as definitive (qat'i) like the uttered words.
2
Determine what the command to free implies by way of iqtida
The text equates the command to 'Sell him to me for a thousand, then be my agent to free him.' Thus, the sale and agency are muqtada. The owner's response 'I freed' implies acceptance of sale and execution of agency.
The command is an order, not an offer; the sale is completed by the owner's response.
3
Apply the rukn-shart distinction to the Abu Yusuf scenario
When the command is 'Free your slave for me without anything,' Abu Yusuf argues it implies a gift and agency, and possession (qabd) is not needed because the gift is established by implication. The response of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad is that acceptance (a rukn) is implied in sale, but possession (a shart) is not implied in a gift because conditions are external to the essence of the act.
The principle: only what is essential (rukn) to the existence of the implied act is established; external conditions are not.
4
Understand the 'same kind' requirement
The text argues that the imposed (muqtada) must be of the same kind as the implicator (muqtadi). Since the command is a verbal statement, only verbal or conceptual acts (like sale and acceptance) can be implied, not physical acts like possession.
This prevents the implication of physical actions from verbal commands.
5
Derive the practical ruling (thamarah al-khilaf)
For the case with compensation, the freeing counts on behalf of the commander, and he owes the price. For the case without compensation, the Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) holds that the gift is not validly established without possession, which is not implied, so the freeing does not count on behalf of the commander. Abu Yusuf would rule differently.
The difference hinges on whether a condition is automatically included by iqtida.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed investigation into the usul al-fiqh principle of iqtida (implication) as applied to a specific transaction. The author begins by presenting the scenario: a person commands a slave-owner, 'Free your slave for me for a thousand dirhams,' and the owner responds, 'I freed.' The Hanafi ruling is that the slave is freed on behalf of the commander, and the commander owes the thousand. If the commander intended the freeing as expiation (kaffarah), it counts as such. The reasoning is that the commander's statement implies a sale ('Sell him to me') and an agency ('Then be my agent to free him'). Since the manumitter must own the slave, and ownership here cannot be established without a sale (because the commander does not currently own the slave), the command necessarily implies a sale by necessity (iqtida). The owner's response 'I freed' implies both the acceptance of the sale and the execution of the agency. The text stresses that the implied meaning (muqtada) is treated as definitive (qat'i) like the explicit utterance (mal-fuz). The sale established by implication is stripped of certain normal conditions: it does not require explicit offer and acceptance, and options like option of inspection or defect do not apply. However, the commander must be legally capable (mukallaf) for the freeing to be valid. The passage then introduces a dispute with Abu Yusuf. Abu Yusuf extends the same principle to a case where the command is without compensation ('Free your slave for me without anything'). He holds that this implies a gift (hiba) and agency, and possession (qabd) is not needed because the implied gift is like acceptance in sale. The response of Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn) is that this analogy fails because acceptance is a pillar (rukn) of sale, whereas possession is only a condition (shart) of a gift. In iqtida, only what is essential to the existence of the implied act (the pillar) is established; conditions are not automatically implied. The text elaborates with a subtle discussion: the implicator (muqtadi) is the original and the implied (muqtada) is subsidiary; the subsidiary must be of the same kind as the original. Since possession is a physical act, it cannot be implied by a verbal statement. Therefore, in the case of a gift by implication, possession is not established. The discussion reveals the Hanafi commitment to a precise analytical method in usul, distinguishing between essential pillars and external conditions. The passage also references the hadith 'La itq fi ma la yamlikuhu ibn Adam' to support the necessity of ownership for freeing.

Key Terms
اقتضاء (iqtida'): A type of implication in usul al-fiqh where an utterance requires the existence of a necessary (muqtada) meaning for its validity, even if not explicitly stated.
مقتضى (muqtada): The implied meaning that is necessary for the validity of the explicit statement; the implied element such as a sale or agency in the command to free.
ركن (rukn): A pillar or essential element of a legal act without which the act cannot exist (e.g., acceptance in sale).
شرط (shart): A condition external to the act that must be present for its complete validity (e.g., possession in gift).

The Rule of Al-Muqtada (Implied Meaning): Established by Necessity Only
States the ruling of muqtada and applies it to examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of muqtada to determine the minimal scope of an implied meaning in a legal speech act, and evaluate the Shafi'i objection regarding specification by intention.

Core Idea
The implied meaning is only as much as needed; necessity sets the limit, not intention.

This passage introduces a critical principle in Hanafi usul: the muqtada (the meaning necessarily implied for speech to be correct) is established to the minimum extent required. The text demonstrates this with two examples: divorce and oaths, and also addresses the difference between muqtada and other types of omitted elements. By understanding this rule, you will be able to properly limit the scope of implied meanings, avoiding overextension based on mere intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
State the rule: 'al-muqtada yuthbitu bi-tariq al-darura, fa yuqaddaru bi-qadr al-darura.' Understand that the implied meaning is established by necessity and measured by it.
This is the core principle of the lesson.
2
Understand the rationale
Why is it limited to necessity? Because the only justification for implying something beyond the explicit words is to make the speech correct. Anything beyond that is not needed.
Necessity here refers to linguistic necessity, not practical need.
3
Apply to the divorce example
A man says 'You are divorced' intending three. The muqtada is the existence of a divorce. Necessity is fulfilled by one divorce, so the intention of three is ineffective.
This shows that even explicit intention cannot extend the muqtada.
4
Apply to the oath example
A man says 'If I eat' intending a specific food. The muqtada is 'food' in general, expressed as the absolute individual (fard mutlaq). Since there is no generality in the muqtada, specification by intention is invalid.
The absolute individual here is not a general term; it is simply the concept that fulfills necessity.
5
Contrast with the iddah example
After consummation, the command 'Observe iddah' implies divorce by necessity. Only one revocable divorce is implied because irrevocability is beyond necessity.
This reinforces that the muqtada is limited to the minimum attributes required.

Core Idea
الفاء indicates immediate succession (ta'qib) with connection (wasl); delay requires proof.

This lesson introduces the particle الفاء (fa) as a key linguistic tool in Hanafi legal reasoning. Understanding its default meaning of immediate succession with connection is essential for interpreting conditional statements, contracts, and other legal texts. Evidence from 'أو' that Tashahhud is Not a Pillar
Using the disjunctive 'أو' in a hadith to classify tashahhud as wajib.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule regarding the particle 'أو' to derive the ruling that tashahhud is wajib and not rukn, and compare this to the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
The particle 'أو' shows that one of two alternatives suffices, so tashahhud cannot be a pillar when sitting alone completes the prayer.

In this passage, the author uses a hadith where the Prophet ﷺ says 'if you say this or do this, your prayer is complete' to argue that tashahhud (the testimony) is not a pillar. The key is the disjunctive 'أو' (or), which in Hanafi usul indicates that either alternative is sufficient. Since sitting (qa'dah) is already agreed upon as required, tashahhud cannot be individually obligatory. This lesson walks you through the istinbat chain and the school's division into fard, wajib, and rukn.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of the hadith
The word 'this' in 'say this or do this' is identified by commentators as referring to tashahhud (saying) and sitting (doing). This step establishes the two alternatives.
2
Infer from the particle 'أو'
'Or' indicates that either one suffices for completion. If both were required, the Prophet would have said 'and'. This is a standard Hanafi principle for disjunctive particles.
In Hanafi usul, 'أو' for alternatives normally implies sufficiency of one.
3
Apply consensus on sitting
Sitting (qa'dah) is agreed upon as a required element by ijma. Therefore, tashahhud cannot be individually obligatory; otherwise, completion would require both, contradicting the 'or'.
Consensus (ijma) specifies which alternative is actually required.
4
Derive the Hanafi ruling
Since tashahhud is not a pillar (rukn), it is classified as wajib. Omitting it requires sajdah al-sahw, but the prayer remains valid because all pillars are present.
The distinction between rukn (invalidates if omitted) and wajib (requires compensation) is crucial.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i considers tashahhud fard (obligatory pillar), interpreting 'or' differently or relying on other hadiths that explicitly command tashahhud. This cross-madhhab comparison highlights the usul difference.
The practical consequence: in Shafi'i fiqh, prayer is invalid without tashahhud; in Hanafi, it is valid but sajdah al-sahw is needed.

The Effect of Description in Legal Rulings: Wasf and Mafhum al-Mukhalafah
Explains how a qualifying description restricts a ruling like a condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a qualifying description does not imply negation, by distinguishing between the Hanafi and Shafi'i interpretations of Quran 4:25 and deriving the ruling for marrying a Kitabi slave woman.

Core Idea
A description (wasf) affirms the ruling for the described case but does NOT negate it for others — Hanafis reject mafhum al-mukhalafah.

In this passage from Usul al-Shashi, the author dives into a key usul principle: how a qualifying description (wasf) in a legal text affects the ruling. The Hanafi school holds that a description does not automatically negate the ruling when the description is absent, a stance that sets them apart from the Shafi'i school. The classic example is the Quranic verse about marrying believing slave women — does it implicitly prohibit marrying non-believing slave women? We'll walk through the debate and the usul principle behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source text and its description
Locate the Quranic verse quoted in the passage: 'from your believing slave women' (Quran 4:25). Note the qualifying description: 'believing' (mu'minat).
The description is attached to the noun 'slave women'.
2
Contrast the two readings
Shafi'i treats the description as a condition (shart) that restricts the ruling only to believing women, thus forbidding non-believing ones. Hanafi treats it merely as a commendable attribute, not a legal restriction.
This difference stems from acceptance or rejection of mafhum al-mukhalafah.
3
Trace the usul principle: mafhum al-mukhalafah
Understand that mafhum al-mukhalafah infers the opposite ruling when a condition or description is absent. Hanafis reject this as a valid source of law in many contexts.
Hanafis consider the description as affirming the case mentioned, but silence on the opposite case does not imply prohibition.
4
Apply general texts as evidence
The Hanafi position relies on general permissibility verses (Quran 4:24, 4:3) that allow marriage to women in general. The specific verse is interpreted as recommending believing slave women, not excluding others.
Consensus that faith is not a condition for free women supports this interpretation.
5
Conclude with the practical difference
The thamarah al-khilaf: Hanafis permit marrying a Kitabi slave woman; Shafi'is prohibit it. This shows how a single usul principle can lead to different fiqh rulings.
The practical impact is real for those following each school.
