Introducing Nur al-Anwar: A Foundational Hanafi Usul Commentary
Title page introducing the book, author, and contents

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the key authors and texts in the Nur al-Anwar tradition and explain its place in Hanafi usul al-fiqh studies.

Core Idea
Nur al-Anwar is a Hanafi usul commentary on al-Manar, with two super-commentaries, forming a core text for advanced students.

This lesson introduces the title page of Nur al-Anwar, a classic Hanafi usul al-fiqh work. We will identify the main text, its commentary, and the super-commentaries, as well as the scholars involved and the scope of the volume. Understanding these bibliographic details is essential for navigating the Hanafi usul tradition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the base text and its author
The base text is 'Risalat al-Manar' (Epistle of the Beacon) by al-Nasafi. This is a foundational Hanafi usul text. The title page explicitly mentions it as the object of commentary.
Al-Nasafi (d. 710 AH) is a major Hanafi jurist and usuli.
2
Identify the commentary and its author
The commentary is 'Nur al-Anwar' (Light of Lights) by Sheikh Ahmad, known as Mulla Jeevan al-Siddiqi (d. 1130 AH). This is the main commentary on al-Manar.
Mulla Jeevan was a leading Hanafi scholar from India.
3
Identify the two super-commentaries
The edition includes two super-commentaries (hawashi): 'Qamar al-Aqmar' and 'Hashiyat al-Sunbuli'. These are marginal glosses that further explain and sometimes critique the main commentary.
Super-commentaries are common in the Hanafi scholarly tradition to build upon earlier works.
4
Understand the scope of the volume
This first volume covers the foundational sources of Islamic law: Kitab Allah (the Book of Allah), Sunnat al-Rasul (the Sunnah of the Messenger), and Ijma al-Ummah (the Consensus of the Ummah).
These three sources are the first subjects treated in standard usul works.
5
Note the editorial work
The edition was prepared by a group of specialized scholars who reviewed marginalia, authenticated hadiths, and corrected errors. This enhances the reliability of the text.
Such critical editions are important for academic study.

Reading a Critical Edition: The Editor's Methodology
Editorial preface explaining book production choices

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the editorial conventions (color coding, footnote placement, brackets) used in this edition and apply them to correctly distinguish the original matn from the commentary.

Core Idea
Red marks the original matn; black marks the commentary—a critical edition's colors are a map.

Before studying the usul content of Noor al-Anwar and al-Manar, understanding the editor's methodological choices is crucial. This preface explains the color scheme (red for original texts, black for commentaries), the relocation of marginal notes to footnotes, corrections of spelling errors from previous editions, and the use of modern punctuation. Mastering these conventions will help you navigate the edition efficiently and avoid confusion between the source text and scholarly apparatus.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original text and commentary colors
The editor uses red for the original texts of al-Manar and for Quranic verses in footnotes. Black is used for the book's main content (the commentary). This distinction is the first key to understanding the page layout.
Red is only for the Manar matn and verses, not for other elements.
2
Understand footnote transmission
Small comments that originally appeared between the lines have been moved to the bottom footnotes, either independently or within brackets []. This clarifies the page and makes the commentary flow easier to follow.
Brackets indicate editorial additions or clarifications.
3
Recognize editorial corrections
Spelling mistakes present in Indian and Pakistani editions have been corrected in both text and footnotes. This improves reliability of the edition.
Corrections are silently applied; no separate note unless in brackets.
4
Note formatting choices
Topic headings are added at the top of pages, and the text follows modern spelling rules and punctuation. Difficult words are vowelled to resolve ambiguity.
These features aid readability and quick reference.
5
Interpret the editor's purpose
The preface concludes with a prayer for sincerity and acceptance. Recognize that these editorial decisions aim to serve the student and scholar by providing a clean, accurate, and user-friendly edition of a classical Hanafi usul work.
This is a scholarly tool, not a reproduction of a single manuscript.

The Straight Path as the Balanced Middle Way in Hanafi Usul
Definition and theological positioning of the straight path

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the concept of al-sirat al-mustaqim as the balanced middle path and identify how the Sharia of Muhammad mediates between extremes.

Core Idea
The straight path is the moderate middle way, neither excess nor negligence, embodied in the Sharia of Muhammad.

This opening passage of Noor al-Anwar sets the tone for the entire work by defining the straight path (al-sirat al-mustaqim) as a path of balance and moderation. Understanding this concept is essential for appreciating the Hanafi method, which consistently seeks the middle ground between extremes in both creed and practice.

Guided Path
1
Define al-sirat al-mustaqim
Read the definition: it is the public road everyone travels without turning aside, and it is moderate between excess (ifrat) and negligence (tafrit).
The path is not a private or sectarian way but the common highway of Islam.
2
Contrast with previous religions
Note that the Sharia of Muhammad is positioned between the severity (shidda) of Moses' law and the ease (suhula) of Jesus' law.
Examples: Moses' law required cutting the place of impurity, paying wealth in zakat, and killing the soul in repentance; Jesus' law permitted wine.
3
Apply to Ahl al-Sunnah creed
The balanced path also applies to creed: Ahl al-Sunnah is moderate between jabr (compulsion) and qadar (free will), between rafd (rejection) and khuruj (rebellion), and between tashbih (anthropomorphism) and ta'til (negation of attributes).
This reflects the Hanafi commitment to wasatiyya (moderation) in theology.
4
Analyze the permissibility discussion
The commentary from Nata'ij al-Afkar discusses that wine and pork were permissible for previous nations and for early Muslims, but prohibitions specific to Muslims do not extend to non-Muslims, as seen in verses from al-Ma'idah.
This illustrates how the Sharia's rulings are context-specific and not universal across all times and peoples.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of din and identify its four components (wad' ilahi, dhu 'uqul, ikhtiyar, khayr bi al-dhat) using the legal definition dissector.

Core Idea
Din is a divine institution that drives rational beings by choice to ultimate good.

In this opening passage of Noor al-Anwar, the author defines 'din' with precise legal terminology. The definition is not merely theological but serves as a foundation for understanding Islamic law as a divinely guided system that addresses human rationality and free will. Let us dissect this definition step by step.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term defined
Locate the term 'din' in the text and note that it is being defined as a legal concept, not just a religious one.
The definition appears after the discussion on 'khuluq 'azim'.
2
Extract the four components
Break down the definition: (1) wad' ilahi (divine institution), (2) saiq li-dhawi al-uqul (driving those with intellect), (3) bi ikhtiyarihim al-mahmud (by their praiseworthy choice), (4) ila al-khayr bi al-dhat (to the good in essence).
These components are explicitly stated in the Arabic.
3
Understand each component's legal significance
Explain why each component is necessary: divine origin distinguishes din from human systems; rationality ensures accountability; choice ensures moral responsibility; intrinsic good ensures ultimate goal is divine pleasure.
Refer to the legal definition dissector for the sabab, shart, and athar.
4
Note the Quranic allusion
Identify the allusion to Quran 68:4 in the commentary and how it supports the definition's emphasis on character.
The commentator uses the verse to argue that the Prophet's character is part of din.
5
Examine the exclusionary effect
Consider what the definition excludes: acts without intellect (e.g., animals) or without choice (e.g., compulsion). This becomes important for the critique in the next lesson.
This sets up the later discussion on sadaqat al-fitr.

Commentary and Critique: The Definition of Din Expanded
Critiquing and defending the definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Quranic allusion for the definition and evaluate the critique regarding the exclusion of involuntary acts.

Core Idea
The definition of din must account for all acts of worship, including those performed on behalf of another.

The commentator not only defines din but also defends it against objections and refines it. One criticism points out that the definition excludes the sadaqat al-fitr of a newborn, since the infant lacks choice. This forces us to reconsider the condition of choice in the definition. Let us examine the critique and the proposed alternative.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection about the verse's exclusivity
The commentator notes that some might object that the verse only indicates the Prophet's character but not its exclusivity. He responds that the context of praise implies exclusivity.
This is found in the phrase 'wa in lam yadull 'ala al-ikhtisas'.
2
Understand the commentator's response
The commentator argues that since the verse is in a place of praise (mahall al-madh), it implies specificity. This is a standard Hanafi usul argument: context can specify.
This shows the importance of qara'in (contextual indicators).
3
Identify the critique of the definition
The marginal note (attributed to 'al-Qamar') points out that the definition with 'bi ikhtiyarihim' excludes sadaqat al-fitr for a one-day-old child because the child cannot choose. Thus the definition is flawed.
The Arabic: 'فإنه يخرج عنه صدقة الفطر عن ابن يوم'
4
Analyze the proposed alternative
The alternative definition replaces 'li-dhawi al-uqul bi ikhtiyarihim' with 'liman tahaqqaqa fihi' (to one who is realized in it), which includes cases where the act is performed on behalf of the person (e.g., by a guardian).
The phrase 'saiq liman tahaqqaqa fihi' appears in the marginal note.
5
Evaluate the practical effect
The original definition would exclude many valid acts of worship performed by guardians for dependents. The revised definition includes them, making it more comprehensive.
This illustrates the Hanafi method of internal critique and refinement.

Definition, Types, and Subject Matter of Usul al-Fiqh
Explaining definitions and scope of usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the idafi and laqabi definitions of usul al-fiqh, state its goal, and identify the preferred Hanafi view on its subject matter.

Core Idea
Usul al-fiqh defines the rules to derive fiqh; its subject includes both proofs and rulings.

This passage introduces the science of usul al-fiqh by clarifying its definitions (relational and technical), its goal, and its scope. Understanding these foundational elements is crucial before diving into detailed principles. The commentator explains why the original author omitted these and then presents the preferred Hanafi position on the subject matter.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of definitions
The passage distinguishes between an idafi (relational) definition and a laqabi (technical) definition. Understand each as a way to define the compound term 'usul al-fiqh'.
Idafi: based on the parts 'asl' and 'fiqh'. Laqabi: as a label for a specific science.
2
Unpack the idafi definition
Define 'asl' as that on which something is built (sensory or rational) and 'fiqh' as knowledge of practical legal rulings from detailed proofs. Combine to get the relational meaning.
This is the classical Hanafi approach to defining compound terms.
3
State the laqabi definition
The laqabi definition treats the term as a label: the science of the rules by which fiqh is derived.
This is the more common definition across schools.
4
Recognize the goal of usul al-fiqh
The goal (ghaya) is to attain knowledge of subsidiary legal rulings from their detailed proofs.
This goal is consistent across schools.
5
Analyze the dispute on subject matter
The passage presents two views: the exclusive view (only proofs) and the inclusive view (both proofs and rulings). Note the Hanafi chosen view (al-mukhtar) and the reasoning behind it.
The inclusive view is preferred because the science investigates essential attributes of both proofs and rulings.

The Definition and Sources of Usul al-Fiqh
Defining usul al-fiqh, its purpose, and the four sources

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Usul al-Fiqh, identify its four primary sources, and explain why the author uses 'Usul al-Shar'' instead of 'Usul al-Fiqh'.

Core Idea
Usul al-Fiqh studies the evidences from which rulings are derived: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas.

This opening passage from Nur al-Anwar lays the foundation for the entire science of Usul al-Fiqh. It defines key terms, distinguishes between the broader Shari'ah and the narrower fiqh, and lists the primary sources that mujtahids rely upon. Understanding this introduction is essential because it sets the scope and methodology of the discipline.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object of study
Determine what Usul al-Fiqh is about according to the text: the evidences (adillah) of the Shari'ah. Note that 'asl' means that upon which something else is built.
The text says 'Aṣl is what something else is built upon' and 'the intended meaning here is the evidences'.
2
Analyze the term 'al-Sharʿ'
Examine the two possible meanings: Lawgiver (shāriʿ) or prescribed law (mashrūʿ). The author prefers that 'al-Sharʿ' is a name for the religion itself, avoiding the need for interpretation.
The lam in 'al-Sharʿ' is explained: if Lawgiver, it's for covenant; if prescribed law, it's for genus.
3
Understand why 'Usul al-Sharʿ' not 'Usul al-Fiqh'
The author explains that 'al-Sharʿ' is broader, encompassing both theoretical (like theology) and practical rulings, while fiqh is only practical. Thus 'Usul al-Sharʿ' includes both, avoiding the impression of exclusivity to fiqh.
The commentary notes that these three sources are also used in kalam, so they are not exclusive to fiqh.
4
List the three primary sources
The text states: 'The foundations of the Shari'ah are three: the Book (Quran), the Sunnah, and the Consensus of the Umma (Ijmaʿ).' Note that the Quran refers to about 500 legal verses, and the Sunnah to about 3000 traditions.
The fourth source, Qiyas, is introduced as 'the fourth source after the three for the rulings of the Shari'ah'.
5
Recognize the role of Qiyas
Qiyas is not a primary source but is derived from the three. It must be based on the primary sources and is defined as 'establishing a ruling for something based on another thing due to a common effective cause (ʿillah).'
The text distinguishes Qiyas from merely analogical or rational reasoning, requiring it to be 'derived from these three'.

The Four Fundamentals and Their Subsidiaries in Hanafi Usul
Commentary establishing four sources and subsuming others

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four fundamental sources of Islamic law according to the Hanafi school, explain how subsidiary sources are annexed to them, and apply the annexation rules to a given scenario.

Core Idea
All subsidiary sources of Islamic law are annexed to one of the four fundamentals: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas.

In this passage from Noor al-Anwar, the commentator establishes the four fundamental sources of Islamic law (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) and systematically addresses objections that other sources (previous revealed laws, customary practice, Companion statements, and Istihsan) exist independently. He shows that these are all subsumed under the four, demonstrating the comprehensive and hierarchical nature of Hanafi usul al-fiqh. Understanding this framework is essential for correctly deriving rulings from the revealed texts and supplementary proofs.

Identify the four fundamentals and their hierarchical order
Read the opening lines: the author mentions four sources, with Qiyas explicitly listed fourth, indicating its rank after the other three. Note that as long as a ruling can be found in the first three, Qiyas is not needed.

The text explains that all proofs are either revelation (recited Quran or unrecited Sunnah) or non-revelation. Non-revelation if agreed upon by all mujtahids is Ijma, otherwise it is Qiyas. This binary division leaves no room for an independent fifth source.
This is a logical partition: wahi vs. ghayr wahi.

The commentator anticipates objections: previous laws, customary practice, Companion statements, and Istihsan. For each, he shows how it is annexed to one of the four fundamentals, citing examples from fiqh.

Commentary
This passage from the introduction of Noor al-Anwar is a commentary establishing the four fundamental sources of Islamic law: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas. The commentator explains why Qiyas is explicitly mentioned as a fourth source despite being subsidiary to the other three: to emphasize its role and to refute critics who reject analogy. The four sources are themselves branches of higher truths—the Quran and Sunnah are branches of affirming Allah and His Messenger, Ijma is branch of a motivating proof (typically a zanni dalil), and Qiyas branches from the three. The confinement to these four is justified: all proofs are either revelation or non-revelation; revelation is either recited (Quran) or unrecited (Sunnah); non-revelation, if agreed upon by all mujtahids, is Ijma; otherwise it is Qiyas. The commentator then addresses objections that other sources (previous laws, customary practice, Companion's statements, Istihsan) are excluded. He shows they are subsumed under the four: previous laws when narrated in Quran or Sunnah without disapproval are annexed to those texts; customary practice is annexed to Ijma (as a form of silent consensus); Companion's statements in rational matters are annexed to Qiyas, in non-rational to Sunnah; Istihsan is annexed to Qiyas. Examples from Hanafi fiqh illustrate each: the permissibility of manufacturing contracts (istisna') based on customary practice; the requirement to specify capital in salam following Ibn Umar's view; the minimum menstruation period based on a Companion's statement; and the purity of bird of prey's leftover water based on Istihsan overriding Qiyas.

Key Terms
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical deduction: extending a ruling from a primary case to a secondary case due to shared effective cause (illa).
الاستحسان (al-istihsan): The evidence that contradicts the apparent Qiyas; a preferred ruling due to a stronger evidence or maslaha.
شرائع من قبلنا (shara'i' man qablana): Laws of previous prophets and communities (e.g., Judaism, Christianity) that may be binding if affirmed in Islamic revelation without disapproval.

The Hanafi Definition of the Quran
Defining the Quran with its attributes and rebutting objections

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of the Quran, identify its three essential attributes, and explain why the definite article in 'al-masahif' does not lead to circularity.

Core Idea
The Quran is defined by being written in mus'hafs and transmitted mutawatir — its definition avoids circularity through precise linguistic analysis.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar opens the section on the Quran, establishing its definition with three key attributes. The author and commentators carefully dissect each term, particularly the phrase 'al-maktub fi al-masahif', to ensure the definition is precise and free from logical flaws like circularity. This lesson unpacks that analysis.

Guided Path
1
Identify the entity and its definitional components
Identify the entity being defined: the Quran (Kitab Allah). The definition includes three attributes: (1) it is revealed speech, (2) it is written in the mus'hafs, (3) it is transmitted mutawatir. Locate each in the text.
The attribute of being 'written in the mus'hafs' is further analyzed in the next step.
2
Analyze the definite article 'al-' in 'al-masahif'
The commentator explores possible categories for the lam: istighraq (generality), 'ahd khariji (external specific reference), jins (genus), and 'ahd dhihni (mental reference). Discuss why each is problematic and which are acceptable.
The lam cannot be for 'ahd dhihni because it would cause circularity (dawr).
3
Examine the resolution of circularity
The commentator shows that taking the lam as jins with the last qualifier (mutawatir) is acceptable, or as 'ahd referring to the well-known mus'hafs of the seven reciters. This avoids defining 'masahif' in terms of 'Quran'.
Circularity is avoided because the Quran is already known to the listener.
4
Understand the significance of tawatur
The third attribute (mutawatir) excludes ahad transmissions, ensuring the definition's certainty. Note how this attribute also differentiates the Quran from other revelations.
Mutawatir transmission gives qat'i knowledge.

Commentary
This passage is the beginning of the section on 'The Book' in Noor al-Anwar, defining the Quran (Kitab Allah). The author presents the definition with its essential attributes. First, the Quran was revealed in stages; the commentator clarifies that Jibril would review the revealed portions annually in Ramadan, and in the year of the Prophet's death, he reviewed it twice. The attribute 'written in the mus'hafs' is explained to mean that although the actual script (nuqush) is what is physically written, the words and meanings are considered 'established' in the mus'hafs: the words directly, the meanings indirectly. The commentator then addresses a key usul issue: the meaning of 'al' in 'al-masahif'. He considers possibilities: istighraq (generality), 'ahd khariji (external specific reference), jins (genus), and 'ahd dhihni (mental reference). Each is analysed. The 'lam' cannot be for istighraq because not all mus'hafs contain the Quran; nor for 'ahd khariji because there is no specific external set; nor for jins alone because it would include non-Quranic writings, though the last qualifier (transmitted mutawatir) excludes them; nor for 'ahd dhihni because that would require 'mus'hafs' to be defined in terms of 'Quran' and vice versa, leading to circularity. The commentator resolves this by either taking 'lam' as jins with the last qualifier restricting it, or as 'ahd referring to the well-known mus'hafs of the seven reciters, which are conventionally understood and do not need definition. The third attribute is that the Quran is transmitted mutawatir without doubt, excluding ahad transmissions like Ubayy's addition of 'mutatabi'at' in the verse on fasting. The commentator also explains that the exclusion of abrogated recitations (like the stoning verse) is for those who take 'lam' as 'ahd. The passage is a model of Hanafi usul: careful definition, addressing objections, and using linguistic precision to avoid circularity.

Why Usul Begins with Divisions of Words and Meanings
Introduction to the four divisions of words and meanings in usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Hanafi usul al-fiqh begins with the divisions of expression and meaning, identify the four main divisions, and recognize that they are not mutually exclusive.

Core Idea
Knowledge of Shari'ah rulings depends on knowing how words convey meaning — these divisions are the tools of interpretation.

This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary justifies why the author begins the book with divisions of words and meanings. A potential objection is raised: if this is a work of usul (legal theory), why not directly derive rulings from evidence? The answer is foundational: you cannot derive rulings without first understanding the ways in which linguistic expression (nazm) relates to meaning (ma'na). The text then outlines four main divisions that structure the entire discipline and clarifies that these divisions are not mutually exclusive categories but separate systems of classification that can overlap.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Author's Justification
Read the opening sentence: 'And rulings of Shari'ah are only known by knowing its divisions.' This directly answers the objection: the book starts with divisions because knowledge of rulings depends on them. The commentary emphasizes that this dependence is relative to later generations, not the Companions.
The author is responding to a hypothetical critic who expects direct derivation from evidence.
2
Understand the Non-Exclusivity of Divisions
The text stresses that 'al-aqsam' here means 'divisions' (taqsimat), not 'categories' that are mutually exclusive. It gives the example of the noun being divided into inflected/built and definite/indefinite: the same noun can be both inflected and definite. Similarly, in usul, a term can be both 'specific' under one division and 'literal' under another.
This refutes the misconception that divisions are like different boxes; they are separate categorisation systems.
3
List the Four Divisions
The passage enumerates four divisions: 1) by linguistic coinage (wad' — literal vs. metaphorical), 2) by clarity/obscurity of indication (zahir vs. khafi), 3) by usage (original vs. figurative), and 4) by meaning (subject matter). Note that the first three concern expression, the fourth concerns meaning, but the preferred view is that all consider both expression and meaning.
These divisions are arrived at by induction (istiqra'), not pure reason.
4
Clarify the Meaning of 'Wujuh'
The first division is described as being in 'aspects (wujuh) of expression: form and language.' The commentary clarifies that 'wujuh' here means 'modes' (turuq) or 'types' (anwa'), not 'proofs' (adilla). This careful terminological distinction is characteristic of Hanafi usul.
This avoids confusion with the common use of 'wajh' as 'proof'.

Commentary
This is a front-matter passage from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary. The author begins by justifying why he starts the book with the divisions of words and meanings: because knowledge of Shari'ah rulings depends on understanding how linguistic expression (nazm) conveys meaning. He addresses a potential objection: if this is a book on usul, why not directly derive rulings from evidence? The answer is that deriving rulings requires first understanding the tools of interpretation, which are the divisions of words and meanings. The commentary explains that these divisions are not meant to be exhaustive in a linguistic sense; rather, only those relevant to Shari'ah rulings are discussed. For example, grammatical categories like definiteness are left to Arabic grammar. A key point is that the divisions discussed here are not mutually exclusive categories; rather, there are multiple systems of division, and a single term can belong to different divisions under different criteria (e.g., a word can be both 'specific' under one division and 'literal' under another). This is clarified by the analogy of nouns being divided into inflected/built and definite/indefinite: a word can be both inflected and definite. The passage then outlines that there are four main divisions: (1) by linguistic coinage (wad')—literal vs. metaphorical; (2) by clarity/obscurity of indication—clear vs. ambiguous; (3) by usage—used in its original meaning or not; (4) by meaning—the subject matter of the text (the first, second, and third concern expression; the fourth concerns meaning, though the most correct view is that all divisions consider both expression and meaning). The first division is then further specified as being about 'aspects of expression' (wujuh al-nazm) i.e., the types of expression from a linguistic perspective. The commentary notes that the author uses 'wujuh' here to mean 'modes' (turuq) or 'types' (anwa'), not 'proofs' (adilla). This careful terminological distinction is typical of Hanafi usul, which is known for its precise analysis of language.

Commentary
This page is the table of contents (fihrist) of the book Noor al-Anwar, a foundational Hanafi usul al-fiqh text. It lists the main topics covered in the book, along with their page numbers. The topics range from the concepts of determination (عزيمة) and concession (رخصة), definitions of fard, wajib, sunnah, and nafal, to the causes of legislated rulings. It then moves to discussions on contradiction between different sources (Quran, Sunnah, qiyas, reports), types of bayan (clarification), and causes of punishments. A significant portion outlines the divisions of Sunnah (mutawatir, mashhur, ahad) and conditions of narrators. Later topics include abrogation (naskh), actions of the Prophet, revelation, previous laws, taqlid of Sahabi and Tabi'i, and finally consensus (ijma). This table provides a roadmap for students studying the usul al-fiqh methodology according to the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
عزيمة (‘azīmah): Determination; a ruling that is originally required without any concession due to necessity.
رخصة (rukhṣah): Concession; a ruling that permits an exception to the general rule due to necessity or hardship.
فريضة (farīḍah): An obligatory act that is established by definitive evidence and whose obligation is certain.
واجب (wājib): An obligatory act that is established by probable evidence.
سنة (sunnah): A recommended act that the Prophet consistently performed without being obligatory.
نفل (nafl): A supererogatory act, whose performance is rewarded but omission is not sinful.
تعارض (ta‘āruḍ): Contradiction or apparent conflict between two legal proofs.
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation of a legal ruling by a later revelation or Sunnah.
إجماع (ijmā‘): Consensus of the mujtahid scholars on a legal ruling after the era of the Prophet.
