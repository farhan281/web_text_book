Qiyas from the Quran: Prohibition of Liwat
Qiyas derivation from Quran with objection-response

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of takhrij al-'illa to derive the prohibition of liwat with women via qiyas from the menstruation verse.

Core Idea
The 'illa of 'harm' (adha) from the menstruation verse extends prohibition to liwat.

This lesson dissects a classical Hanafi qiyas derivation: the prohibition of liwat (anal intercourse) with women is analogized to the prohibition of intercourse during menstruation. The effective cause (al-'illah) is harm (adha), extracted from Quran 2:222. We will walk through the identification of the original case, the branch, the extraction of the 'illa, and the handling of objections that the ruling is already textual.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original case (asl) and its ruling
The original case is intercourse during menstruation, which is prohibited by Quran 2:222. The ruling is haram.
The asl must have a clear ruling from primary sources.
2
Extract the 'illa from the asl
The verse states: 'They ask you about menstruation. Say: It is harm (adha).' The attribute of 'harm' is the effective cause because it is suitable (munasib) and explicit in the text.
Hanafis require the 'illa to be derived from the text, not purely rational.
3
Identify the branch (far') and confirm the 'illa is present
The branch is liwat with women. It involves harm, so the 'illa of adha exists in the far'.
The far' must not already have an explicit textual ruling for the same case.
4
Apply qiyas and derive the ruling
Since the 'illa (harm) is common, the prohibition of the asl transfers to the far'. Thus liwat with women is haram by qiyas.
This is qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) because the 'illa is manifest.
5
Address the objection that the far' is textually ruled
Objection: Liwat is already prohibited by Quran verses about Lot. Response: Those verses specify men, not women. A further objection cites a hadith and isharat al-nass; the response distinguishes that the text prohibits men and implies prohibition for women via isharah, but qiyas still provides explicit derivation.
The Hanafi position maintains that qiyas is valid even if there is indirect textual indication (isharah).

Qiyas from the Sunnah: Prohibition of Ribawi Excess in Gypsum and Lime
Qiyas derivation from Sunnah with madhhab dispute on 'illa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi 'illa for riba (quantity and genus) and apply it to new commodities via qiyas, contrasting with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
The Hanafi 'illa of quantity and genus extends riba prohibition to non-edible items like gypsum.

This lesson examines the Hanafi qiyas that extends the prohibition of riba (excess) from the six named commodities (gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, salt) to gypsum and lime. The Hanafi 'illa is quantity (qadr) and genus (jins), as opposed to the Shafi'i 'illa of edibility and currency. We will trace the derivation from the hadith, understand the dispute, and see the practical difference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original case (asl) and its ruling
The asl is the six commodities (gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, salt). The ruling: when exchanged for their own kind, any excess (tafadul) is riba and haram.
The ruling is from the mutawatir hadith 'Wheat for wheat...'
2
Determine the 'illa according to Hanafis
Hanafis derive the 'illa as quantity (qadr: weight or measure) and genus (jins). This is because the hadith lists items that are either weighed or measured, and the prohibition is for same genus.
The Shafi'i 'illa is edibility (ta'm) and currency (thamaniyya).
3
Identify the branch (far') and check the 'illa
The branch is gypsum and lime. They have quantity (they are measured) and genus (each is a distinct genus). So the 'illa of quantity+genus exists.
Gypsum and lime are not edible and not currency, so Shafi'i 'illa would not apply.
4
Apply qiyas and derive the ruling
Since the 'illa exists, disparity in exchange of gypsum for gypsum (or lime for lime) is prohibited as riba.
This is qiyas jaliy (clear analogy).
5
Compare with conflicting 'illa (Shafi'i) and note the thamarah al-khilaf
According to Shafi'is, gypsum and lime are not ribawi, so unequal exchange is permissible. The practical difference is that Hanafis prohibit it, Shafi'is allow it.
The passage implicitly supports the Hanafi position by using it to derive prohibitions.

Examples of Qiyas from Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma
Three examples of qiyas from Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma with objections and clarifications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the 'illa in a qiyas, explain the Hanafi method of analogical deduction, and apply it to derive rulings using examples from Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma.

Core Idea
Qiyas extends rulings via a shared 'illa; Hanafis use it even when a text exists, to cover unstated branches.

This passage from Nur al-Anwar presents three paradigmatic examples of qiyas, demonstrating how rulings from the Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma are extended to new cases through a shared effective cause ('illa). You will learn to identify the 'illa, the original case (asl), and the branch case (far'), and understand how the Hanafi school responds to objections about unnecessary duplication. Additionally, the passage clarifies the epistemological status of qiyas—sometimes definitive, sometimes speculative—an important nuance for usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three qiyas examples
For each example (from Quran, Sunnah, Ijma), note the asl (original case), far' (new case), and 'illa (cause). The first example: asl = prohibition of intercourse during menstruation; far' = sodomy (liwata); 'illa = harm (adha).
The 'illa is extracted from the Quranic description of menstruation as 'adha'.
2
Analyze the objection to the liwata example
Some object that liwata is already textually prohibited, so qiyas is unnecessary. The Hanafi response distinguishes: nass prohibits liwata with men, qiyas extends it to women. Also, a hadith directly prohibits anal intercourse with women, so both nass and qiyas support the ruling.
This shows that qiyas can complement nass, not duplicate it.
3
Compare 'illa definitions in the riba example
The hadith prohibits excess in six commodities. Hanafis say the 'illa is quantity and genus, so plaster and lime (measured items) are included. Shafi'is say the 'illa is foodstuff and monetary value, which may not include non-food items.
The difference in 'illa leads to different rulings on riba al-fadl for non-food items.
4
Understand the epistemological point
The author mentions that the three primary sources are usually definitive (qat'i), while qiyas is usually speculative (zanni). However, qiyas with a stated 'illa can be definitive, and primary sources transmitted via solitary reports may be speculative.
This nuance prevents oversimplification and shows the complexity of usul evaluation.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Nur al-Anwar presents three examples of qiyas (analogical deduction) derived from the three primary sources of Islamic law: the Quran, the Sunnah, and Ijma (consensus). The author first gives an example of qiyas from the Quran: the prohibition of sodomy (liwata) is analogized to the prohibition of sexual intercourse during menstruation. Both are prohibited due to the 'illa (effective cause) of harm (adha). The Quran calls menstruation 'adha' (harm/discomfort), and this same harm exists in sodomy, so the ruling applies by qiyas. The passage then addresses an objection: the ruling on liwata is already established by explicit texts (nass) about the people of Lot, so qiyas is unnecessary. The response is that those texts explicitly address men, not women. The qiyas here is intended to prove the prohibition of liwata with women, which is not explicitly stated but derived through analogy. However, a stronger counter is presented: a hadith from Ibn Abbas states that Allah will not look at a man who has anal intercourse with either a man or a woman, so the ruling for women is also textually established by 'isharat al-nass (indication of the text). The gloss clarifies that the anus is not a place for cultivation (i.e., procreation) but for waste, hence the prohibition is clear. The second example is qiyas from the Sunnah: the prohibition of riba al-fadl (excess in exchange) for six specified commodities (gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, salt) is extended to plaster (jiss) and lime (nura) due to the common 'illa of quantity (qadr) and genus (jins). This is the Hanafi position; the Shafi'i school instead uses 'illa of foodstuff (tum) and monetary value (thamaniyya). Thus, in Hanafi fiqh, selling plaster for plaster with excess is forbidden. The third example is qiyas from Ijma: the prohibition of marrying the mother of a woman with whom one has committed zina is analogized to the prohibition of marrying the mother of one's slave woman whom one has had intercourse with. The 'illa is partiality and connection (juz'iyya wa ba'diyya): the child born from intercourse creates a familial bond, making the woman's mother like a mother-in-law. This ruling is from consensus. Finally, the author explains why the matn (original text) mentioned only three sources and added 'and qiyas' rather than saying 'four sources': to indicate that the first three are definitive (qat'i) in their origin, while qiyas is primarily speculative (zanni). However, qiyas with a clearly stated 'illa can be definitive, and conversely, a general text that has been specified or a solitary hadith can be speculative. This nuance prevents misunderstanding.

Key Terms
نظائر القياس (nazair al-qiyas): Analogous cases or examples of qiyas (analogical deduction) used to extend rulings from original cases to new cases.
علة (illa): The effective cause or ratio legis that is common to both the original case (asl) and the new case (far') and upon which the ruling is based.
الجزئية والبعضية (al-juz'iyya wa al-ba'diyya): Partiality and connection; the legal cause that makes the mother of a sexual partner prohibited by creating a familial link through the child.

The Four Usul and the Epistemological Status of Qiyas
Explaining the four usul and epistemological nuances of qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four usul of Sharia according to the Hanafis, explain why qiyas was singled out, and distinguish between qat'i and zanni forms of qiyas.

Core Idea
Qiyas is the fourth usul, generally zanni, but qiyas with a specified cause is qat'i.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar commentary addresses the four usul of Sharia: the Book, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas. The author explains why the original matn mentioned qiyas separately rather than listing it as the fourth: to highlight that the first three are generally certain (qat'i) while qiyas is generally speculative (zanni), though exceptions exist. A concrete example of qiyas derived from ijma is given, and the passage refutes deniers of qiyas by showing its necessity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Four Usul
List the four sources of Sharia as mentioned in the passage: the Book (Quran), the Sunnah, Ijma (consensus), and Qiyas (analogy). Note that the passage calls them 'usul' (foundations).
The Hanafi order: Kitab, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas.
2
Understand Why Qiyas Is Singled Out
The passage states that the matn did not simply say 'the usul are four' but instead presented qiyas separately. The reason given: to alert that the first three are qat'i (certain) while qiyas is zanni (speculative) in most cases. This is a pedagogical choice to emphasize epistemological weight.
The phrase 'ليكون تنبيها على أن الأصول الأول قطعية والقياس ظني' captures this.
3
Recognize Exceptions to the General Rule
The passage notes that a general text that has been specified in part (al-amm al-makhsus) and a solitary report (khabar al-wahid) are zanni, whereas qiyas with a specified cause (illa mansusa) is qat'i. This nuance shows that the certainty classification is not absolute.
This anticipates the student's potential misconception that qiyas is always zanni.
4
Analyze the Example of Qiyas from Ijma
The passage gives an analogy: the prohibition of the mother of a woman with whom one committed zina (muzanna) is analogized to the prohibition of the mother of one's slave woman after intercourse. The illa is the partiality (juz'iyya) created by the child, which establishes a connection between the man and the woman, making their relatives prohibited to each other. This qiyas is derived from ijma because the original case (slave woman) is ruled by a consensus.
This demonstrates how qiyas can be based on ijma, not just on text.

The Command Form Does Not Imply Repetition: The Hanafi Linguistic Proof
Providing rational proof that amr does not imply repetition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rational proof that the command form does not imply repetition, and apply the concepts of fardiyyah and jinsiyyah to analyze command forms.

Core Idea
The command is an abbreviation of a request with the masdar, which is a single entity—so it cannot imply repetition.

In this lesson, we examine the rational proof (dalil aqli) that the command form (amr) does not necessitate or imply repetition. The Hanafi school uses a linguistic argument: the imperative is a shortened form of a request that contains the masdar (infinitive). Since the masdar is a single individual (fard), the command cannot carry any number. We will also explore the concepts of fardiyyah and jinsiyyah, and why the dual is excluded. This proof is fundamental to understanding how commands operate in Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim and its proof
The author claims that the command form does not necessitate or imply repetition. The proof is linguistic: the imperative is an abbreviation of a request involving the masdar. For example, 'Strike!' (اضرب) is an abbreviation of 'I request striking from you' (أطلب منك الضرب).
The proof is based on the nature of the masdar, not on external factors.
2
Analyze the structure of the proof
The proof is a qiyas: the command form (far') is abbreviated from a request with the masdar (asl). The masdar is a single entity, so the command cannot imply repetition. The illa (effective cause) is that the masdar is fard (individual) and does not contain number.
This is a dalil aqli (rational proof) based on linguistic analysis.
3
Understand the masdar as fard
The masdar is a noun denoting an event without time. It is a single individual (fard haqiqi or fard i'tibari). As a single entity, it does not indicate any specific number. Therefore, the command derived from it cannot imply repetition or multiplicity.
The dual is excluded from fardiyyah and jinsiyyah, as it indicates exactly two.
4
Apply fardiyyah and jinsiyyah
The author explains that oneness (tawhid) is observed in the words of oneness (wihdan). The verb abbreviated from the masdar is even more deserving of not implying number. The concepts of fardiyyah (real individuality) and jinsiyyah (generic/legal individuality) cover words that denote single individuals. The dual is isolated from both.
This distinction helps clarify that commands are for one action, not repeated.
5
Confirm the scope of the proof
The commentary clarifies that this proof is for the original claim (al-asl al-kulli) that the command does not imply repetition, not for subsidiary rulings such as the intention for two divorces. The proof is self-contained and does not rely on other discussions.
Some students mistakenly apply this proof to other contexts; stay focused on the principle.

The Obligation of Qada Derived from the Same Text as Ada
Hanafi argument that qada is wajib by the same textual cause as ada

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the derivation of qada from the ada text, explain the evidence using dalala iltizamiyya, and evaluate the opposing view.

Core Idea
The same command that obligates performance also obligates make-up by implied necessity—no new text is needed.

This lesson examines a key usul al-fiqh dispute: does the same scriptural text that requires you to pray on time also require you to make it up if missed? The Hanafi verifiers argue yes, through implied connotation (dalala iltizamiyya), while the Shafi'is and some Hanafis require a separate text. We will analyze the reasoning, evidence, and the practical implications of this disagreement.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
Identify the Hanafi claim: the same text (e.g., 'Establish prayer') that obligates ada also obligates qada. Note that this is the view of 'al-muhaqqiqin' (verifiers) among the Hanafis.
Do not confuse 'ada' (on-time performance) with 'qada' (make-up).
2
Identify the opposing school and its proof
The Iraqi Hanafis and Shafi'is argue that a new text is required, using analogy: the command to fast Thursday does not obligate fasting Friday.
The opposing argument is based on literal non-inclusion (adam al-tanawul).
3
Follow the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that literal inclusion is not claimed; rather, the obligation is derived by implied necessity (dalala iltizamiyya): the original text shows the dhimma is occupied, and qada is the necessary means to free it.
This is a key methodological difference in usul al-fiqh.
4
Determine the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
For acts without an explicit qada text (e.g., missed supererogatory prayers, certain vows), Hanafis still require qada; opponents do not without a text.
This affects legal practice in many areas.
5
Evaluate the tarjih and authority
The text states the Hanafi position is 'ala al-muhaqqiqin' (relied upon by the verifiers), with Shams al-A'imma and Fakhr al-Islam as authorities.
Understanding which view is mufta bihi is important for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute in usul al-fiqh regarding the derivation of the obligation of qada (making up a missed act) from the original command for ada (performance). The Hanafi position, as presented by the verifiers (al-muhaqqiqin) from the majority of Hanafis, is that the same scriptural text (e.g., 'Establish prayer' or 'Fasting is prescribed for you') that obligates ada also obligates qada through implied connotation (dalala iltizamiyya). This is because the text establishes that the dhimma (legal responsibility) of the obligated person is occupied with the duty of performing the act; when the act is missed, the dhimma remains occupied, and qada is the necessary means to discharge it, so the original text implies by necessity the obligation of qada. The opposing view, attributed to the Iraqi scholars among the Hanafis and to the generality of Shafi'is, is that a new, separate textual command is required to impose qada, as the original command does not literally extend to a different time. They argue by analogy: the command to fast on Thursday does not encompass fasting on Friday. The Hanafi response clarifies that while the literal wording does not include qada, the implied necessity—that the duty remains and must be fulfilled—makes qada wajib without a new text. The text also defines the terms 'qada' and 'ada' to address a scenario where someone mistakenly intends Shawwal as Ramadan: it is not qada with intention of ada but rather ada with intention of qada (since the intention to make up Ramadan is misdirected). The error in assumption is excused. The passage further explains that the hadith and Quranic verse often cited for qada are not new texts imposing obligation but serve as reminders that the original obligation persists despite losing the time's excellence. This principle is then extended to other acts like vowed fasts, where qada is required only for time-bound vows.

Key Terms
قضاء (qada): Making up a missed obligatory act after its time has passed.
أداء (ada): Performing an obligatory act within its prescribed time.
سبب (sabab): The cause or basis of obligation, here referring to the textual command (nass) that necessitates the act.
دلالة التزامية (dalala iltizamiyya): Implied connotation: a meaning necessarily attached to the explicit wording, such that the ruling logically follows.

The Three Types of Ada' in Civil Transactions
Applying the three types of ada' to huquq al-ibad with examples from ghasp and sales

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three types of performance (ada') in Hanafi usul and apply them to examples in huquq al-ibad.

Core Idea
The three types of ada' — complete, deficient, and shabih bil-qada — govern both divine obligations and interpersonal rights.

In this passage, the author extends the classification of performance (ada') from the realm of worship to civil transactions. We see how returning a usurped item without encumbrance mirrors complete ada', while an encumbrance makes it deficient. This conceptual framework helps us systematically analyze obligations in muamalat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of ada'
From the text, list the three: ada' kamil (complete), ada' qasir (deficient), and shabih bil-qada (resembling substitute). Recognize that these categories are universal.
The text explicitly states they occur in both rights of Allah and rights of people.
2
Understand complete ada' in civil matters
The example: returning a usurped item in the exact condition taken, without encumbrance (debt/crime) or physical deficiency. This is analogous to delivering sold goods perfectly.
The commentary stresses that absolute return may include encumbrances; hence the qualification.
3
Understand deficient ada' in civil matters
The example: returning a usurped slave who has incurred debt or committed a crime in the usurper's hand. The encumbrance makes the performance deficient, even though the item itself is returned.
Similarly, delivering a sold item while it is occupied by a debt, crime, or illness is deficient.
4
Explore the expansion through commentary
The commentators define sarf and salam, and al-Sunbuli distinguishes four types of sales based on the nature of the countervalue (dAyn or dayn). This shows how the complete/deficient framework applies to specific contracts.
For example, in sarf, hand-to-hand delivery is required; otherwise the performance is deficient.
5
Apply to a new example
Given a scenario such as delivering a car with a mechanic's lien, classify the ada' as deficient and explain why.
The presence of an encumbrance (lien) prevents the performance from being complete.

Commentary
This passage applies the three categories of performance (ada') to the rights of people (huquq al-ibad). Change of Ownership Changes Legal Identity: The Principle from the Hadith of Barira
Example of 'ada' shabih bil-qada' with the hadith of Barira as proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of tabaddul al-‘ayn to derive rulings in cases of dower, zakat, and charity, and distinguish when a change of ownership compels delivery versus when it does not.

Core Idea
Change of ownership transforms the legal identity of an object, allowing rulings to shift even on the same physical item.

This passage introduces a foundational Hanafi usul principle: tabaddul al-‘ayn (change of the object's legal identity) due to transfer of ownership. The author uses the case of dowering a slave of another to illustrate that delivering a different legal entity (even if physically the same) counts as valid performance. The hadith of Barira serves as the proof, where meat given as charity becomes a gift when ownership changes. You will learn how the Hanafi school derives broad applications from this hadith and how it resolves an apparent contradiction with the case of a sold slave that is reclaimed.

1

3
Extract the principle from the hadith of Barira

Qada of Eid Takbirs in Ruku as Adaa-like Qada
Explains a type of qada resembling adaa using Eid takbirs in ruku

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which qada resembles ada according to Hanafi usul, and apply the qiyas khafi reasoning to justify performing missed Eid takbirs in ruku.

Core Idea
Qada of Eid takbirs in ruku is shabih bi al-ada because ruku retains the form and ruling of qiyam.

This lesson explores a specific case in Hanafi usul where qada (making up a missed act) is treated as resembling ada (performance in time). The case is the missed obligatory takbirs in Eid prayer when one joins the imam in ruku. We will see how the Hanafi school uses qiyas khafi and ihtiyat to justify performing these takbirs in ruku, and examine the disagreement with Imam Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scenario and the ruling
Consider the case: a person joins the imam in the ruku of Eid prayer and misses the extra takbirs. According to the Hanafi school, he should recite them in ruku without raising his hands.
Ruling: takbirs are performed in ruku; hands are not raised.
2
Understand the concept of qada shabih bi al-ada
This qada is not purely compensatory; it resembles ada because the state of ruku is attached to qiyam in form (the lower half stands) and in ruling (catching ruku equals catching the rak'ah).
The survival of the enabling condition (place) gives it an ada-like character.
3
Trace the Hanafi qiyas
The key principle: 'Who catches the imam in ruku catches the rak'ah.' This implies that ruku stands in for qiyam for catching the rak'ah. Since takbirs are prescribed in qiyam, and ruku resembles qiyam, the takbirs can be made up there.
This is qiyas khafi (concealed analogy) based on the shared property of being a place where takbirs are prescribed.
4
Consider Abu Yusuf's counter-argument and the Hanafi response
Abu Yusuf argues that missed takbirs should not be made up in ruku, like recitation and qunut, because their place has passed. The Hanafi response distinguishes: recitation and qunut are not prescribed in any act resembling qiyam, but takbirs are prescribed in ruku itself (takbirat al-rukuh), so the analogy holds.
The difference in kind justifies the qiyas.
5
Conclude with the operative ruling and its basis
The Hanafi position (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad) is preferred due to ihtiyat (precaution) and the survival of the aspect of ada. The practical difference: Hanafi perform takbirs in ruku; Abu Yusuf does not.
Thamarah al-khilaf: practical impact in prayer.

The reason given is that both ruku (obligatory) and the takbirs (obligatory) must be accommodated as much as possible. The author then clarifies that raising the hands during these takbirs and placing them on the knees during ruku are both sunna. The commentary (al-Qamar) explains that this is a type of qada that 'resembles ada' because the state of ruku is attached to qiyam in form (the lower half remains standing) and in ruling (catching the ruku is like catching the qiyam and the rak'ah). The text then addresses a secondary issue: if someone fears that if he stands to recite the takbirs, the imam will raise his head from ruku, then he should first pronounce the opening takbir, then the takbir for ruku, and then the remaining Eid takbirs in ruku. If no such fear exists, he should recite them while standing. The passage then presents the ikhtilaf with Imam Abu Yusuf, who held that the takbirs should not be made up in ruku because their proper place (qiyam before ruku) has passed, analogous to missing recitation or qunut. The Hanafi response distinguishes the cases: recitation and qunut are not prescribed in any act that resembles qiyam, whereas the takbirs are prescribed in what resembles qiyam (the takbirs of ruku itself). Since the kind of takbir is prescribed in an act resembling qiyam, the other takbirs can be attached by analogy. The ruling is based on ihtiyat (precaution) because takbirs are worship.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Noorul Anwar discusses two fiqh issues to illustrate a usul principle: that certain obligations are based on ihtiyat (precaution) rather than qiyas (analogy), and therefore they are qualified by 'in sha Allah' (if Allah wills). The first issue is the fidyah (ransom) for missed prayers. Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani states in al-Ziyadat that if one gives fidyah for missed prayers, it suffices 'if Allah wills.' The commentator explains that this qualification indicates the ruling is not based on qiyas, because qiyasi matters are definitive and not contingent on the divine will. The second issue is the case of a heir voluntarily giving fidyah for the deceased's missed fasts. Again, Imam Muhammad expresses hope for acceptance 'if Allah wills,' showing it is based on ihtiyat. The third issue is the qada' (making up) of udhiyah (sacrifice) after its days have passed. Since the sacrifice involves slaughtering, which is not rationally understood as a act of worship, one might think it has no substitute. However, the Shari'ah prescribes giving charity with the value or the animal itself as a precautionary measure, not as a qada'. The commentator discusses the subtlety that the sacrifice itself may be a substitute for an original act of charity, but due to the strong textual evidence for sacrifice during its days, the precautionary charity is only required after the time has passed. The passage also touches on the dispute about whether hajj on behalf of another is considered for the commander or the agent, using similar reasoning. Overall, the passage emphasizes that when the 'illah (effective cause) of a ruling is not known rationally, the ruling may be based on ihtiyat and tied to Allah's will, distinguishing it from qiyasi rulings.

Judgment by an Unintelligible Equivalent: The Example of Diyah
Explaining the concept of judgment by an unintelligible equivalent using diyah as example

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the category of judgment by an unintelligible equivalent (qada' bi-mithlin ghayr ma'qul) in Hanafi usul and apply it to the example of diyah.

Core Idea
Judgment by an equivalent need not be rationally analogous; the Lawgiver can prescribe a substitute.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, not every judgment (qada') must mirror the original performance (ada') in every detail. This lesson explores the concept of judgment by an unintelligible equivalent—a substitute that reason does not find similar to the original obligation. The classic example is the blood-money (diyah) for accidental killing, where property compensates for a life without any rational similarity. Understanding this principle clarifies many rulings in worship and transactions where qada' differs from ada'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim
The text begins with 'They do not analogize judgment to performance.' This is the core principle: the state of qada' is not weighed by the same criteria as ada'.
This refutes the misconception that if ada' is superior in a certain way (e.g., congregation), then qada' must also be superior.
2
Understand the example
The text introduces 'compensation for life and limbs with wealth' as an example of judgment by an unintelligible equivalent. Examine why diyah (blood-money) fits this category.
Diyah is prescribed for accidental killing, where retaliation (qisas) is not possible.
3
Analyze the reasoning
The text states that there is no similarity (mumathala) between a free human and property. Reason cannot find equivalence; the obligation is purely prescribed by the Lawgiver.
The lack of rational analogy is the key feature of an 'unintelligible equivalent'.
4
Connect to usul
This illustrates a broader Hanafi usul principle: the Lawgiver may prescribe a substitute (badal) that is not analogous to the original obligation, and this substitute is valid as a judgment.
This principle is invoked in many fiqh areas, e.g., making up missed prayers without congregation.

Judgment in the Sense of Performance: The Non-Specified Slave Case
A usul analysis of 'qada' fi ma'na al-ada' through the marriage dower case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi usul category of 'qada' fi ma'na al-ada'' and apply it to cases where an obligation is known in genus but unknown in description.

Core Idea
When an obligation is known in genus but unknown in quality, delivering the value is treated like the original performance.

In Hanafi usul, there is a subtle but crucial distinction between pure performance (ada') and judgment (qada'). However, there is a third category where a judgment is treated as if it were performance, known as 'qada' fi ma'na al-ada'. This lesson explores how this concept operates in the case of paying the value of a non-specified slave as mahr. By understanding this, you will see how the Hanafi school resolves disputes when an obligation's quality is uncertain.

Guided Path
1
Identify the obligation
The mahr is a slave, but not specified (bi-ghayr 'aynihi). The slave is known in genus (jins) but unknown in description (sifa).
2
Recognize the two ways to fulfill
The husband may deliver a medium-quality slave (pure ada') or pay the value of a medium slave (qada). The text contrasts these.
3
Understand why paying value is not ordinary judgment
Because the slave's quality is unknown, valuation (taqwim) is the only way to determine the median. Thus, paying the value is like performing the original duty.
The phrase 'الوسط لا يتحقق إلا بالتقويم' is key.
4
Note the consequence
The wife is compelled to accept the value, just as she would accept the slave. This shows the legal equivalence.
5
See the extension
Abu Hanifa applied the same reasoning to cases of cutting wounds, where the injury is unknown in severity but known in type.
This demonstrates the broader usul principle.

Perfect Like Principle: Cut Then Kill in Qisas
Intra-Hanafi dispute on qisas and tadakhul

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of al-mithl al-kamil to determine the qisas options when multiple intentional injuries lead to death, and distinguish when tadakhul (overlap) applies.

Core Idea
In qisas, perfect likeness demands requital act by act; overlapping of penalties is rejected for intentional acts.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar examines a classic intra-Hanafi dispute over a scenario where a killer cuts off a hand and then kills the victim before the wound heals. The disagreement centers on whether the guardian must retaliate with both a cut and a killing, or only a killing, based on the principle of al-mithl al-kamil (perfect likeness). Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi usul principles guide the application of qisas and the conditions for tadakhul (overlapping of penalties).

Guided Path

Identify the act and legal context
The act is intentional cutting followed by intentional killing before the wound heals. Determine the ruling for the guardian's retaliation under qisas.
The act is considered multiple because healing did not occur between injuries.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and proof
Abu Hanifa holds that the guardian may cut then kill, or just kill. His proof: the killer's act is multiple, so perfect likeness (al-mithl al-kamil) requires multiple retaliation.
Alternatively, the guardian may choose to kill only, which is treated as forgiveness of part of the requirement.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and proof
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that only killing is required. Their proof: the requirement of cutting is included in the requirement of killing when the cut leads to death without healing.
They treat the cut as part of the killing, so the penalty overlaps (yatadākhalān).
4
Analyze the evidence and underlying usul
Abu Hanifa prioritizes the multiplicity of acts in form; the Sahibayn prioritize the causal outcome. This reflects different applications of qiyas: act-by-act analogy vs. result-oriented analogy.
The dispute is a legitimate ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school.

The text outlines eight scenarios based on intention (intentional/accidental) and whether healing occurred between cut and kill. Identify where tadakhul is agreed or disputed.
If healing occurred, they are separate crimes and tadakhul does not occur by agreement. If no healing and both intentional, it is disputed; otherwise, agreement on tadakhul or no tadakhul based on intention.

Why Benefits of a Usurped Item Are Not Guaranteed
Fiqh ruling on non-guarantee of benefits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that benefits (manafi') of a usurped property are not compensable when destroyed, and contrast it with the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
In Hanafi law, the usurper owns the benefits through possession, so destruction of benefits incurs no compensation.

This lesson explores a distinctive Hanafi ruling: if a person usurps an item and benefits from it, but then the benefits are destroyed (e.g., the animal dies or the house burns), the usurper is not liable for the lost benefits. The source passage builds on the premise that guarantee (daman) requires equivalence (mumathala), which benefits lack. This ruling opposes the Shafi'i school, which treats benefits as property.

Guided Path
1
Understand the premise
The passage derives a general principle: guarantee (daman) is only obligatory when there is equivalence (mumathala) between the compensation and the thing lost. Benefits (manafi') are not equivalent to anything tangible.

The commentary explains that the usurper owns the benefits through possession. When he takes possession, he owns the right to use the item. Therefore, if the benefits are lost, no compensation is due because they were his property.

The passage notes that this opposes al-Shafi'i, who holds that benefits are property and must be compensated if destroyed. Shafi'i views benefits as separate from the item itself.

Core Idea
Possible capability is a pure condition for the initial obligation; easy capability contains causal meaning and is needed for continuance.

The Hanafi school makes a critical distinction between two types of capability that affect obligations. One type, once triggered, does not need to persist; the other must remain for the obligation to continue. This lesson unpacks that distinction using the examples of kharaj, 'ushr, Hajj, and marriage witnesses.

Kharaj attaches to produce, not land; Hajj attaches to the person once minimally able.

The author explains that possible capability is like witnesses in marriage: a condition for existence but not continuance. Easy capability gives the obligation an attribute of ease, so if ease is lost, the obligation falls. Identify the analogy and its limits.
The analogy with witnesses shows that a pure condition does not need to persist.

Are Disbelievers Addressed by Sharia Commands?
Ruling on whether disbelievers are obligated by sharia commands

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of tahsil al-hasil to determine that disbelievers, not believers, are the primary addressees of the command to believe, and state the three areas where disbelievers are addressed.

Core Idea
Disbelievers are addressed by sharia commands because the command to believe is for those who lack faith, not for those who already have it.

This lesson examines a foundational usul question: Are disbelievers obligated by the commands of sharia? The Hanafi school holds that they are, in matters of faith, penal laws, and transactions. The proof lies in the nature of the command: it cannot be for someone who already possesses what is commanded, as that would be tahsil al-hasil (attaining the attained), which is impossible. The Quranic verse 'O you who have believed, believe' is reinterpreted to mean firmness in faith, not initial belief.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key text and its apparent meaning
Look at Quran 4:136, 'O you who have believed, believe.' The addressees are believers, and the command is 'believe.' On the surface, it seems to command believers to believe, but that would be tahsil al-hasil.
2
Classify the textual category
In Hanafi usul, this is a zahir text: the address is clear but requires interpretation to avoid impossibility.
3
Apply the usul principle of tahsil al-hasil
Tahsil al-hasil (attaining the attained) is impossible; a command cannot require what already exists. Since believers already have faith, the command cannot be for initial faith.
4
Derive the intended meaning
The command is interpreted as firmness in faith (thabat) or harmony of heart with tongue. This shows the address is effectively for disbelievers, not believers.
5
Extend to other areas
The passage extends the same principle: disbelievers are also addressed by penal laws and transactional rules because they are capable of fulfilling them and are more suitable recipients.

Impossibility of Both Literal and Figurative Meanings
Explains the principle of ta'addhur al-haqiqah wal-majaz with the example of a man calling his wife 'my daughter'.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a statement's literal and figurative meanings are both impossible and state the Hanafi ruling that the speech is void.

Core Idea
When both haqiqa and majaz are legally impossible, the statement is void ab initio.

How does Hanafi usul handle a statement that can neither be interpreted literally nor figuratively? This lesson explores the principle of ta'addhur al-haqiqah wal-majaz, where both meanings are impossible, leading to the nullity of speech. We will examine the classic example of a man calling his wife 'my daughter' and the legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the statement and its possible meanings
Consider the statement: 'This is my daughter' spoken to his wife. Determine the possible literal meaning (she is his biological daughter) and figurative meaning (intended as divorce).
The statement must be analyzed under the assumption that the speaker is sane and capable.
2
Check the literal meaning (haqiqa)
Assess if the literal meaning is legally possible. For literal impossibility, the wife must be of known lineage (so cannot be his daughter) or older than him (biologically impossible). If either condition holds, literal meaning is impossible.
3
Check the figurative meaning (majaz)
Assume the figurative meaning is intended as divorce. Divorce requires a valid marriage. But calling her 'daughter' implies perpetual prohibition, which is incompatible with a valid marriage. Therefore, the figurative meaning is also legally impossible.
The contradiction: divorce presupposes a marriage, but daughterhood negates marriage.
4
Conclude the ruling
When both meanings are impossible, the statement is void (mulgah) — no legal effect. Exception: If the husband insists persistently, the judge may separate them, analogous to cases of jabb and 'unnah (physical incapacity).
Void means no divorce occurs, no paternity is established.

Haythu and Ayna: Place Words in Conditional Divorce
Ruling on conditional divorce using place words vs time words

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that place words in conditional divorce are interpreted as the conditional particle 'in' and are restricted to the session, distinguishing them from time words that indicate generality.

Core Idea
Place words in divorce are treated as 'if' — confined to the session; time words are general.

In Hanafi usul, when a word cannot be taken literally due to the nature of the subject matter, the closest suitable meaning is adopted. This lesson examines how place words like 'haythu' (where) in conditional divorce are interpreted metaphorically as the conditional particle 'in', and why this confines the wife's wish to the same session. In contrast, time words like 'idha' (when) indicate generality of time and are not so restricted.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic category
Recognize that 'haythu' and 'ayna' are nouns for place (ism makan). The husband uses them in a conditional divorce statement.
Literal meaning: 'where' or 'wherever'.
2
Determine why literal interpretation fails
Divorce is a legal event that does not relate to a specific place; it cannot be 'in' a place literally. Therefore, the literal meaning is impossible (muta'adhdhir).
Divorce occurs everywhere; it is not localized.
3
Apply the principle of metaphorical interpretation
When literal meaning fails, the word is interpreted metaphorically (majaz). The closest meaning to a place word that can function as a condition is the conditional particle 'in' (if), because both involve indefiniteness/ambiguity (ibham).
This is a form of qiyas (analogy) based on shared ambiguity.
4
Derive the ruling from 'in'
'In' in conditional statements (e.g., 'If you wish, you are divorced') restricts the condition to the current session (majlis) unless otherwise specified. Hence, the wife's wish must be expressed in the same session.
This is the Hanafi rule for ta'liq bi al-shart (suspension on a condition).
5
Contrast with time words
Time words 'idha' and 'mata' indicate generality of time (zaman). They are not confined to the session; the wife's wish can be expressed at any time.
Difference arises because time words are compatible with the nature of divorce; place words are not.

Definition and Authority of Dalālat al-Naṣṣ
Defining dalālat al-naṣṣ with proofs and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dalālat al-naṣṣ, distinguish it from ʿibārat al-naṣṣ and qiyas, and identify its proofs.

Core Idea
Dalālat al-naṣṣ is a necessary textual implication understood by native speakers, not a product of qiyas.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi concept of dalālat al-naṣṣ (textual implication). The passage defines it as a necessary meaning derived from the text without ijtihad, using the famous verse 'Do not say 'uff'' to illustrate. We will see how this differs from explicit wording (ʿibāra) and from analogical reasoning (qiyas), establishing its authority as a source of Sharia.

Commentary
This passage defines and explains the concept of dalālat al-naṣṣ (textual implication) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author begins by stating that the implication of a text is recognized by any native speaker of the language, is valid before the legislation of qiyas (analogical reasoning), and is not rejected even by those who reject qiyas. The key example is the Quranic verse 'So do not say to them 'uff'' (Al-Isra:23). The explicit wording (ʿibārat al-naṣṣ) only prohibits uttering 'uff'. However, the necessary consequence of not causing annoyance is that hitting and cursing are also prohibited. This derived prohibition is called dalālat al-naṣṣ, and it is a necessary implication, not a result of ijtihad. The passage then distinguishes dalālat al-naṣṣ from ishārat al-naṣṣ (indication of the text). Both are considered definitive (qaṭʿī) in attribution to the text, but ishāra takes precedence when they conflict because it is derived directly from the wording without an intermediary meaning. The text then presents a legal dispute between the Hanafīs and al-Shāfiʿī regarding the obligation of expiation (kaffārah) for intentional killing. The verse about mistaken killing (Al-Nisa:92) obligates expiation on the mistaken killer. Al-Shāfiʿī uses the principle of fahwā al-khiṭāb (first priority of the unspoken) to argue that if expiation is due for a lesser offense (mistake), it is even more due for a greater offense (intentional). However, the Hanafīs counter with the verse about intentional killing (Al-Nisa:93) which indicates, by ishārat al-naṣṣ, that the only recompense for intentional killing is Hell, thus negating any other expiation. The passage further explains two additional arguments from the structure of the verse: the particle 'fa' implies that what follows is the complete recompense, and if anything else were intended, it would have been mentioned. This is a classic example of conflict between ishāra and dalāla, and the Hanafī position is that ishāra prevails because it is more direct. The commentary by ʿAbd al-ʿAlī (Bahr al-Ulum) notes that ishāra is often unintended, but the correct approach is to weigh the strength of each in cases of conflict. The Hanafī school maintains that in dalālat al-naṣṣ, what matters is the existence of the effective cause (ʿillah), regardless of whether the unspoken case is more or less deserving of the ruling. This passage underscores the sophistication of Hanafi textual interpretation and its departure from qiyas, insisting that dalālat al-naṣṣ is a self-evident implication, not an analogical deduction.

Key Terms
دلالة النص (dalālat al-naṣṣ): The necessary implication of a text that is understood by native speakers without ijtihad, deriving a ruling for the unspoken from the spoken via a necessary meaning.
إشارة النص (ishārat al-naṣṣ): The indication of a text that is derived from the arrangement of the words themselves, without an intermediary meaning, considered more direct than dalāla.
عبارة النص (ʿibārat al-naṣṣ): The explicit wording of the text, which gives the direct ruling for the mentioned case.

Conflict Between Ishāra and Dalāla: The Case of Kaffārah for Intentional Killing
Analyzing the conflict between ishāra and dalāla using the kaffārah dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of preference for ishārat al-naṣṣ over dalālat al-naṣṣ and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument regarding kaffārah for intentional killing.

Core Idea
When ishāra and dalāla conflict, ishāra prevails because it is direct from the wording, not through an intermediary meaning.

This lesson presents a classic usul dispute: does the Quran require expiation (kaffārah) for intentional killing? The Shafi'i school argues yes via dalālat al-naṣṣ from the verse on mistaken killing. The Hanafi school argues no, relying on ishārat al-naṣṣ from the verse on intentional killing. We will dissect the arguments, the Hanafi response, and why ishāra takes precedence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The question is whether kaffārah (freeing a slave or other expiation) is obligatory for intentional killing, in addition to qisas or pardon.
2
State the Shafi'i position and its proof
Al-Shafi'i uses the verse on mistaken killing (4:92) which obligates kaffārah. By fahwā al-khiṭāb (priority of the unspoken), if a lesser offense (mistake) requires expiation, a greater offense (intentional) is even more deserving.
This is dalālat al-naṣṣ, based on the effective cause of killing.
3
State the Hanafi position and its proof
The Hanafi school rejects this, citing the verse on intentional killing (4:93) which says 'his recompense is Hell'. This indicates by ishārat al-naṣṣ that Hell is the complete recompense, so no kaffārah.
Ishāra is derived from the arrangement of the words, e.g., the particle 'fa' and the fact that only Hell is mentioned.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to Shafi'i
The Hanafi response includes: (1) the 'fa' shows that what follows is the full recompense; (2) silence about expiation in a context of need implies its absence; (3) ishāra is more direct than dalāla and thus takes precedence.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Shafi'i ruling requires kaffārah for intentional killing (along with qisas/diya); Hanafi ruling does not require kaffārah.

The verse about mistaken killing (Al-Nisa:92) obligates expiation on the mistaken killer. Al-Shāfiʿī uses the principle of fahwā al-khiṭāb (first priority of the unspoken) to argue that if expiation is due for a lesser offense (mistake), it is even more due for a greater offense (intentional). However, the Hanafīs counter with the verse about intentional killing (Al-Nisa:93) which indicates, by ishārat al-naṣṣ, that the only recompense for intentional killing is Hell, thus negating any other expiation. The passage further explains two additional arguments from the structure of the verse: the particle 'fa' implies that what follows is the complete recompense, and if anything else were intended, it would have been mentioned. This is a classic example of conflict between ishāra and dalāla, and the Hanafī position is that ishāra prevails because it is more direct. The commentary by ʿAbd al-ʿAlī (Bahr al-Ulum) notes that ishāra is often unintended, but the correct approach is to weigh the strength of each in cases of conflict. The Hanafī school maintains that in dalālat al-naṣṣ, what matters is the existence of the effective cause (ʿillah), regardless of whether the unspoken case is more or less deserving of the ruling. This passage underscores the sophistication of Hanafi textual interpretation and its departure from qiyas, insisting that dalālat al-naṣṣ is a self-evident implication, not an analogical deduction.

Key Terms
دلالة النص (dalālat al-naṣṣ): The necessary implication of a text, used here by Shafi'i to derive kaffārah for intentional killing.
إشارة النص (ishārat al-naṣṣ): Direct indication from the arrangement of words, used by Hanafis to derive that no kaffārah is due.
فَهْوَى الْخِطَاب (fahwā al-khiṭāb): The first priority of the unspoken: if a ruling applies to a lesser case, it more strongly applies to a greater case.
كفارة (kaffārah): Expiation for sins, e.g., freeing a slave, fasting, or feeding the poor.

Resolving Qiyas Conflict: The Hanafi Choice by Heart's Testimony
Explains ta'arud resolution for qiyas with Hanafi preference for shahadat al-qalb.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school permits a mujtahid to choose between conflicting qiyas based on shahadat al-qalb and contrast this with the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
In qiyas conflict, the mujtahid follows his heart's testimony, not defaulting to al-hal.

When two analogies conflict, Hanafi usul does not require the mujtahid to default to the current state (al-hal). Instead, he is granted the liberty to choose whichever qiyas his heart inclines to, guided by divine insight (firasa). This principle distinguishes the Hanafi approach from others like the Shafi'i, where inner conviction is not a valid basis, leading to multiple simultaneous opinions. In this lesson, we unpack the rationale, the definition of tarjih, and the intra-Hanafi nuances.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflict scenario
Recognize that two qiyas are in opposition, and acting on both is impossible. The issue is: what should the mujtahid do?
This is a mu'arada haqiqiyya (true conflict) for which some might think both fall away.
2
Reject the fallback to al-hal
Understand that al-hal (current state) is not a valid proof in Hanafi usul except in the specific case of su'r al-himar. Thus, the mujtahid cannot simply resort to it.
Al-hal is only used by necessity when no other proof exists.
3
Apply shahadat al-qalb
The mujtahid may choose whichever qiyas his heart inclines to, based on the inner conviction (shahadat al-qalb) and the light of firasa given by Allah to every believer.
This choice is made out of precaution (ihtiyat) and personal conviction.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Imam al-Shafi'i does not require shahadat al-qalb, thus a mujtahid may have multiple opinions on the same issue at the same time. In the Hanafi school, multiple opinions usually relate to different time periods.
The practical effect: Hanafi fatwa may vary over time, but not simultaneously.
5
Define tarjih and note intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf
Define tarjih as showing an advantage in one evidence by something that is not an independent proof. Note the disagreement: the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf) accept tarjih, while Imam Muhammad argues that all proofs cancel at conflict.
This definition is crucial: tarjih does not rely on quantity of evidence.

The definition of tarjih is given: it is showing an advantage in one proof by something that does not constitute an independent proof. This is the view of the two shaykhs (Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf). Imam Muhammad held that multiple proofs cancel each other out at conflict, hence no tarjih is possible. The margin notes clarify these positions.

Preference of Affirmation over Negation in Conflicting Reports
Analyzing two hadith-based disputes to illustrate preference of ithbāt over nafī

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify conflicting reports as nafī or ithbāt and apply the Hanafi rule of preferring ithbāt to derive a ruling.

Core Idea
A report based on direct evidence outweighs one based on presumption of continuity.

When two authentic hadiths seem to conflict—one negating a status and one affirming it—how does a Hanafi jurist decide? This passage from Noor al-Anwar presents two key examples: the case of Barirah (freed slave woman with a husband) and the Prophet's marriage to Maymunah. In both, the Hanafi school gives precedence to the report that affirms a change based on direct evidence over the report that negates it based on the original state. This principle, rooted in the strength of knowledge (ʿilm) over supposition, is a cornerstone of Hanafi tarjīḥ. You will learn to recognize the reasoning and apply it to similar conflicts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting reports
In each dispute, find the two reports: one that negates a status (e.g., husband is slave, Prophet is muḥrim) and one that affirms a new status (husband is free, Prophet is ḥalāl). Note which report is based on original state (nafī) and which is based on new evidence (ithbāt).
For Barirah: report 'her husband was a slave' (nafī) vs. 'her husband was free' (ithbāt). For Maymunah: 'he married while muḥrim' (nafī) vs. 'he married while ḥalāl' (ithbāt).
2
Classify the basis of each report
Determine the epistemological basis: the nafī report relies on istiṣḥāb (presumption of continuity) because it assumes no change from the known original state. The ithbāt report relies on direct testimony (samāʿ) from a witness who observed the change.
The Hanafi position holds that ithbāt requires knowledge (ʿilm), while nafī rests on lack of contrary evidence.
3
Apply the rule of preference (tarjīḥ)
Since the ithbāt report is grounded in evidence and knowledge, it is stronger. According to Hanafi usul, when nafī and ithbāt conflict, the ithbāt is preferred unless the nafī has its own independent evidence.
This is not a mechanical rule; it applies when the nafī is merely based on original state. If nafī had its own strong proof, the analysis would differ.
4
Derive the ruling for the case
Applying the preference, the ruling that follows the ithbāt report is adopted. In Barirah's case: the husband is deemed free, so she has the option (khiyār) even if he were free. In Maymunah's case: the Prophet married while ḥalāl, indicating that marriage in iḥrām is not inherently invalid.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilāf) between Hanafi and Shafi'i rulings is clear: Hanafis grant the option universally; Shafi'is restrict it to when the husband is a slave.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion on the principle of resolving contradiction between proof types: negation (nafi) and affirmation (ithbat). The commentator expands on two examples from the life of the Prophet (saw) to illustrate that when a narration negates a status (e.g., slavery) based on original state, and another affirms a changed status (e.g., freedom) based on evidence, the affirmed report is preferred. The first example is the case of Barirah, a slave woman who was given the option to dissolve her marriage upon emancipation. The Shafi'is hold that the option only exists if the husband is a slave; the Hanafis hold it exists even if free, based on a narration stating her husband was free. The Hanafi reasoning: the report of his freedom is based on knowledge (samāʿ), while the report of his slavery is based on istiṣḥāb (presumption of continuity). Since the latter lacks independent evidence, the former prevails. The second example is the Prophet's marriage to Maymunah while in iḥrām. One view says he broke his iḥrām before marrying; the Shafi'is take this and forbid marriage in iḥrām. Another view says he remained in iḥrām; this is the Hanafi view, relying on a report from Maymunah that he married her while ḥalāl (non-muḥrim). The commentator notes that the negation (iḥrām) is known by its own evidence (the ḥadīth of being muḥrim), but the affirmation (Ḥalāl) is from direct testimony, so again affirmation is preferred. The passage teaches: when two reports conflict, the one that affirms a new fact based on evidence outweighs the one that negates it based on continuance of the original state, unless the negation has its own clear proof. This is a key usul principle applied by the Hanafis.

Key Terms
التعارض (al-ta'āruḍ): The apparent conflict between two legal proofs that cannot both be applied simultaneously.
النفي (al-nafī): A report that negates a status (e.g., slavery) usually based on the original state or lack of evidence to the contrary.
الإثبات (al-ithbāt): A report that affirms a new status (e.g., freedom) based on specific evidence.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi usul, when a narration that affirms a status (ithbāt) based on evidence conflicts with a narration that negates it (nafī) based on original state, the affirming report is given precedence.
