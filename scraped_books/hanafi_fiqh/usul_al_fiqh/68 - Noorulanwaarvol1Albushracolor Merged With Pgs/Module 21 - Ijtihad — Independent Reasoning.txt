Combining Haqiqah and Majaz: The Case of 'Sawmu Rajab'
Intra-Hanafi dispute on combining literal and metaphorical meanings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi usul principle of combining haqiqah and majaz by analyzing the intra-madhhab dispute regarding the phrasing 'Lillahi alayya sawmu Rajab'.

Core Idea
A single expression can simultaneously convey a literal vow and a metaphorical oath—but not all Hanafi jurists agree.

This passage presents a classic usul dilemma: can one statement bear both a literal and a metaphorical meaning at the same time? The case involves a specific wording, 'Lillahi alayya sawmu Rajab', which is literally a vow, but when the speaker also intends an oath, Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad accept the combination, while Abu Yusuf rejects it. Analyzing this dispute will sharpen your understanding of how Hanafi usul handles haqiqah and majaz.

Guided Path
1
Identify the usul principle at stake
The core principle is al-jam' bayna al-haqiqah wa al-majaz (combining literal and metaphorical meanings in a single utterance). Recognize that this is a contested usul rule within the Hanafi school.
The passage assumes the principle is accepted by Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, but contested by Abu Yusuf.
2
State the positions of the three imams
Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad hold that the expression becomes both a vow (literal) and an oath (metaphorical). Abu Yusuf disagrees, asserting that no such combination occurs.
The text explicitly says 'This applies only against Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, contrary to Abu Yusuf.'
3
Analyze the reasoning behind each position
The literal meaning (vow) comes from the language; the metaphorical meaning (oath) is derived through intention (niyyah). Abu Hanifa and Muhammad consider the addition of intention permissible without contradicting the literal sense. Abu Yusuf likely argues that the two meanings are mutually exclusive.
The text does not elaborate on Abu Yusuf's detailed argument, but the opposition is clear.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
If the person fails to fast, according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, he must both make up the fast (qada for the vow) and pay expiation (kaffara for the oath). According to Abu Yusuf, only one obligation applies.
This difference has direct fiqh implications for the individual's liability.
5
Evaluate which position carries more weight
The passage does not explicitly declare a preferred view. It notes the objection (al-ward) against Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, suggesting that the combination creates a theoretical difficulty, but Abu Yusuf avoids it. Later Hanafi fatwa practice may follow either, but the issue remains a noted ikhtilaf.
This is an intra-madhhab ikhtilaf where both sides are respected.

Commentary
This passage addresses a specific case: a person says, 'Lillahi alayya sawmu Rajab' (I owe to Allah the fast of Rajab) and intends both a vow (nathr) and an oath (yamin), or just an oath. According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad, this statement becomes both a vow and an oath simultaneously. The word 'nathr' (vow) is taken in its literal meaning, while 'yamin' (oath) is a metaphorical use of the same expression. Thus, combining literal and metaphorical meanings is necessary. If the person fails to fulfill the fast, he must make it up (qada) due to the vow and also pay expiation (kaffara) for breaking the oath. To avoid this, it is recommended to read 'Rajab' without tanwin (as a proper noun referring to a specific Rajab), so that the consequence becomes clearer. However, Abu Yusuf disagrees: he holds that there is no combination of literal and metaphorical meanings in this case, so the issue does not arise. This highlights a notable intra-Hanafi disagreement between Abu Yusuf on one side and Abu Hanifa and Imam Muhammad on the other, regarding the possibility of merging haqiqah and majaz. The passage also discusses the grammatical tamwin of 'Rajab' and its implications for identifying the intended Rajab.

Key Terms
النذر (al-nathr): A vow; a commitment to perform an act of worship for Allah. Here, it is the literal meaning of the phrase.
اليمين (al-yamin): An oath; a statement by which one swears to do or not do something, often requiring expiation if broken. Here, it is the metaphorical meaning.
الجمع بين الحقيقة والمجاز (al-jam' bayna al-haqiqah wa al-majaz): Combining the literal and metaphorical meanings of a single expression simultaneously.

What is a Cause (Sabab) in Hanafi Usul?
Defining sabab and its attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'sabab' as a shar'i cause and explain why rulings are attributed to it only apparently.

Core Idea
A cause is a shar'i attribute to which the ruling is ascribed apparently, though Allah alone is the true effecter.

This definitional passage opens the chapter on causes by clarifying what a cause means. It stresses that when we say 'the time causes prayer to be obligatory', we refer to the apparent link only; the real agent is Allah. This distinction preserves tawhid and prevents misunderstanding of independent causality in shari'ah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition.
Locate the sentence: 'ولها أسباب تضاف إليها، أي علل شرعية تنسب الأحكام إليها من حيث الظاهر وإن كان المؤثر الحقيقي في الأشياء كلها هو الله تعالى.' This is the definition of sabab.
The term 'sabab' is equated with 'illah' (effective cause) here.
2
Understand 'min hayth al-zahir'.
The ruling is attached to the cause only in appearance. The true agent is Allah. This preserves tawhid and refutes any idea of natural causation in legal rulings.
This is a key theological safeguard in Hanafi usul.
3
Differentiate between hukm and mahkum bih.
The passage states: 'المراد بالأحكام المحكوم بها من العبادات وغيرها، لا نفس الأحكام'. The request (talab) attaches to the mahkum bih (the act), not the ruling itself.
This subtlety helps in understanding the structure of legal obligation.

Commentary

He critiques the order, suggesting it would be better to place this after qiyas. The passage clarifies that 'ahkam' here refers to the mahkum bih (the things ruled upon), not the rulings themselves. Listing asbab and explaining their function.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the specific causes (asbab) enumerated in the text and explain the purpose of identifying them for ijtihad.

Core Idea
Causes include time, ownership, dependents, land, transactions—they guide rulings after revelation ceased.

After defining sabab, the author provides concrete examples from various areas of fiqh. These range from cosmic (origination of the world) to daily (time, money, family). The list shows the breadth of causes. The text then states the benefit: to derive rulings for new cases after the revelation era, making sabab a key tool for qiyas.

When he finished explaining the legally prescribed rulings, he mentioned after it an explanation of their causes in this approximation, following Fakhr al-Islam, and it would have been better to mention it after qiyas in the discussion of causes and effective causes as did the author of al-Tawdih. He said: i.e., the causes, chapter on the causes of legally prescribed rulings. Command and prohibition with their divisions, such as the command being temporal or absolute, expansive or restrictive, and the prohibition being temporal, concerning legal or sensory matters, or ugly in itself or due to something else, etc. For the request of legally prescribed rulings; by 'rulings' is meant the things ruled upon (the mahkum bih) from acts of worship and other things, not the rulings themselves. And by 'request' is more general, whether it be for an act or for a refrain. And they have causes attributed to them, i.e., legal causes to which rulings are ascribed from the apparent perspective, even though the true effect in all things is Allah Almighty: from the origination of the world, time, ownership of wealth, the days of the month of Ramadan, the head (dependant) whom one supports and has guardianship over, the house, and land that grows crops externally in actuality or potentially, prayer, and the relation of the decreed continuity through engagement. All these are causes. i.e., the continuity of the world is related to growing things, and so forth as has passed in detail, so remember. (al-Qamar) not the ruling itself because the request does not relate to the ruling itself but to the thing ruled upon. (al-Qamar) and by 'request' etc.: conjoined to 'by rulings'. (al-Qamar) from being for an act as in command, or for a refrain as in prohibition. (al-Qamar) causes etc.; and the benefit of establishing them is to know rulings after the cessation of revelation due to the difficulty of knowing the address in every incident. (al-Sunbuli) and they have etc.: i.e., the legally prescribed rulings have causes to which those rulings are attributed, and this attribution is a sign of causality. (al-Qamar) i.e., effective causes etc., indicating that the intended meaning of 'cause' in the text is the effective cause (illah) because it necessitates the ruling. (al-Qamar) from the apparent perspective, i.e., from the perspective that rulings are attached to them apparently. (al-Qamar) 'يمونه' i.e., the legally responsible person undertakes his sufficiency, bearing his maintenance and burden by giving provision, clothing, and housing. It is said: 'manahu yamunuhu' when he undertakes his sufficiency. (al-Qamar) 'ويلي عليه': he said this because guardianship is a condition for maintenance. (al-Qamar) or potentially etc., i.e., by being able to cultivate. (al-Sunbuli) 'المقدور': i.e., what Allah decrees and determines; so 'al-maqdur' comes from 'al-qadr' (decree) not from 'al-qudrah' (power). The commentator pointed to this later by saying: 'For when Allah decreed...' etc. (al-Qamar) 'المقدور' etc.: i.e., what is decreed by Allah, which is the continuity of the world. (al-Sunbuli) 'بالتعاطي': i.e., direct engagement and transaction; this is related to the relation (ta'alluq). (al-Qamar)

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of 'asbab al-ahkam' (causes of rulings) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author explains that after discussing the rulings themselves, he now addresses their causes. He critiques the order, suggesting it would be better to place this after qiyas. The passage clarifies that 'ahkam' here refers to the mahkum bih (the things ruled upon), not the rulings themselves. 'Talab' (request) includes both command (for action) and prohibition (for refraining). The causes are shar'i (legal) causes to which rulings are attributed apparently, though the true agent is Allah. A list of causes is given: origination of the world (which necessitates contingency and divine command), time, ownership, days of Ramadan, dependents (one's head), house, arable land, prayer, and the sustenance of the world through transactions. The glosses further elaborate: the benefit of identifying causes is to derive rulings after revelation; 'sabab' is equated with 'illah' (effective cause); the attribution is only apparent; and specific terms like 'yamunu' (support) and 'waliya' (guardianship) are explained.

Key Terms
السبب (al-sabab): A shar'i cause to which a ruling is attributed from the apparent perspective, though the true agent is Allah.
العلة (al-illah): The effective cause that necessitates the ruling; here identified with 'sabab' in the matn.
المحكوم به (al-mahkum bih): The thing ruled upon, i.e., the act (like prayer, zakat) that is the object of the ruling.
