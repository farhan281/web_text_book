Ruling on the Polysemous Word (Mushtarak)
Usul dispute on the mushtarak word

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that a mushtarak has no umum and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the verse on salat.

Core Idea
A mushtarak word cannot intend all its meanings at once without invalid combination of literal and metaphorical.

The term mushtarak refers to a word that has multiple distinct meanings assigned by the language. A classic example is 'quru' in Arabic, which can mean either menstruation or purity. A key usul dispute arises: can such a word be used in a single utterance to simultaneously intend all its assigned meanings? The Hanafi school holds that this is impermissible, while Imam al-Shafi'i permits it under certain conditions. In this lesson, we examine the arguments and the precise point of contention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position
State the Hanafi rule: a mushtarak has no generality (umum) and cannot be used to intend all its meanings at once. Understand the reasoning: the linguistic assignment fixes each meaning exclusively, so intending both would create a contradiction.
This is the foundation of the dispute.
2
Examine the Shafi'i proof
Read the Shafi'i proof from Quran 33:56, where 'yusalluna' is claimed to intend both mercy (from Allah) and seeking forgiveness (from angels). Note how the Shafi'i school interprets this as evidence that a mushtarak can carry multiple meanings.
The verse is the strongest textual evidence for the opponent.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
Understand the Hanafi answer: the verse does not require separate intentions; rather, a general meaning 'care' encompasses both. Thus the word is used metaphorically in a general sense, not as a mushtarak intending distinct literal meanings.
The Hanafi school reinterprets the verse to avoid the implication.
4
Identify the precise point of dispute
Pinpoint the exact issue: is it permissible to intend by a single word at one time each of the literal meanings simultaneously as the basis for the ruling? The Hanafi say no, because it leads to each meaning being both intended and not intended unless one is taken metaphorically, which combines haqiqah and majaz invalidly.
The dispute is over the possibility of simultaneous literal intentions.
5
Note the consensus and exceptions
Observe that both schools agree that if the meanings are contradictory (like menstruation and purity), it is impermissible by consensus. Also note the discussion on intending the sum metaphorically: some later scholars permit it, others not.
Consensus on opposition shows the limits of the Shafi'i position.

Section on the mushtarak (polysemous word). Explanation of the ruling of the mushtarak: it is that which is collected and transferred even if not comprehensive, unlike purity (tahr), for it is neither collected nor comprehensive nor transferred. And if it is the days of blood, they are the place of collection and transfer, unlike the days of purity; they are not a place of transfer although they are a place of collection in initial opinion. I have clarified this in 'al-Tafsir al-Ahmadi' and here the space does not accommodate it. And it (the mushtarak) has no generality in our view, so it is not permissible to intend both its meanings together. Al-Shafi'i said: it is permissible to intend both meanings together, as in His saying: 'Indeed Allah and His angels send blessings upon the Prophet' (al-Ahzab 33:56). So 'salat' from Allah is mercy, and from the angels is seeking forgiveness, and both were intended by one word, i.e., 'yusalluna'. And we say: the verse was brought to oblige the believers to follow Allah and the angels, and that is not suitable except by taking a general meaning inclusive of all, which is caring for his affair. So the meaning is that Allah and His angels care for his affair; O you who believe, also care for his affair. That care from Allah is mercy, from the angels is seeking forgiveness, and from the believers is supplication. And the precise point of dispute is: is it permissible to intend by one word at one time each of the two meanings such that each is intended and a basis for the ruling? In our view, it is not permissible; because the assigner (wadi') specified the word for the meaning such that nothing else is intended by it. So considering its assignment to this meaning necessitates intending it specifically, and considering its assignment to that meaning necessitates intending it specifically. Thus it would necessitate that each is both intended and not intended. So it cannot be except that one of the two meanings is intended as that which is assigned for it, and the other as that which is suitable for it, resulting in a combination of literal and metaphorical usage, which is invalid. According to him (al-Shafi'i), it is permissible on condition that there is no opposition between them. If there is opposition between them, like menstruation and purity, it is not permissible by consensus. Likewise, it is not permissible to intend the sum of the two meanings literally because the word is not assigned for the sum. As for metaphorically, it is permissible as stated in the commentary of 'al-Muslim' by the teachers of India. Ibn al-Malik said: it is not permissible even metaphorically because there is no relationship between the sum and each of the two meanings; so reflect.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute concerning the polysemous word (mushtarak). The author first defines the mushtarak as a word that encompasses multiple meanings and allows transition between them, unlike the term for purity (tahr). He clarifies that in the Hanafi school, a mushtarak word has no general application that would permit intending all its meanings simultaneously. The Shafi'i position is then presented, citing the Quranic verse about Allah and the angels sending blessings (salat) upon the Prophet, arguing that 'yusalluna' intends both meanings (mercy from Allah, seeking forgiveness from angels). The Hanafi response explains that the verse actually implies a single general meaning of 'care' that accommodates both, not a literal combination of distinct meanings. The core of the dispute is whether a single word can be used in one utterance to intend multiple assigned meanings as the basis of a ruling. The Hanafi argument rests on the nature of linguistic assignment: each meaning requires exclusive intention, leading to contradiction unless one meaning is taken literally and the other metaphorically, which is invalid. The Shafi'i view allows it provided the meanings are not contradictory. The passage also notes that intending the sum of meanings is not permissible literally, but opinions differ on metaphorical usage. The author follows the order: define, state Hanafi position, present Shafi'i proof, refute it, and clarify the point of dispute.

Key Terms
المشترك (al-mushtarak): A word that has multiple meanings (e.g., 'quru' meaning both purity and menstruation, 'salat' meaning mercy and seeking forgiveness).
الحقيقة (al-haqiqah): Literal meaning as assigned by the wadi'.
المجاز (al-majaz): Metaphorical usage where the word is used for a meaning that is suitable (munasib) to the literal meaning.

The Example of Mufassar: The Verse on Angels Prostrating
Analyzing the verse of prostration as an example for mufassar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the verse 'So the angels prostrated, all of them together' is considered mufassar in Hanafi usul, and evaluate the three objections raised against it.

Core Idea
Mufassar is not free from all probability, only harmful probability; exceptions may be disconnected or based on taghlib.

In this lesson, we analyze a key example used in Hanafi usul to illustrate the category of mufassar. The verse describes the angels prostrating to Adam, with the emphatic phrase 'all of them together' (أجمعون). The commentator addresses three potential objections to calling this text mufassar, showing how each is resolved within the Hanafi framework. This deepens our understanding of how textual clarity is established and what legal implications mufassar carries.

Guided Path
1
Identify the text and its category
Locate the verse and the specific phrase that makes it mufassar. The phrase 'all of them together' (أجمعون) is the qualifying emphasis.
The base text is a zahir or nass; the qualifier removes harmful probability.
2
Analyze the first objection
The objection: the possibility that they were in circles or rows (i.e., not all together in one group) remains. Determine why this does not harm the mufassar classification.
Only harmful probabilities—those contradicting the speaker's purpose—are removed. The remaining possibilities are innocent.
3
Analyze the second objection
The objection: Iblis is excepted, which might imply takhsis (specification). Examine the two Hanafi responses: either the exception is disconnected (istithna' munqati') or based on taghlib.
Disconnected exception does not count as takhsis because the excepted item is not part of the category. Taghlib treats Iblis as an angel by predominance.
4
Analyze the third objection
The objection: since the verse is a report (khabar), it cannot be abrogated, so it should be muhkam not mufassar. Understand the refutation: originally it might have been a command, but after its occurrence it became a report; the possibility of abrogation existed in principle.
Mufassar does not require impossibility of abrogation, only clarity at the time of utterance.
5
Compare with a better example
The commentator cites al-Tawdih that a better example for mufassar is the verse 'Fight the polytheists all together' because it is a ruling (hukm), not a report. Reflect on why this distinction matters for usul.
This shows that mufassar can apply to both reports and commands, but the example from rulings is preferred to avoid the abrogation issue.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the example given in the matn for mufassar. The matn likely stated that the verse 'So the angels prostrated, all of them together' is an example of mufassar because the word 'all of them' (أجمعون) removes the harmful probability that they prostrated separately. The commentator addresses three objections: (1) that the possibility of them being in circles or rows still remains; (2) that Iblis is excepted, which could imply takhsis; (3) that because it is a report (khabar) it cannot be abrogated, so it should be muhkam not mufassar. The commentator shows each objection is invalid. For the first, the remaining possibility does not harm the purpose of glorification; the word 'all of them' was inserted precisely to remove the harmful probability of them being scattered, as congregational prayer is superior to individual prayer. For the second, the exception of Iblis is either disconnected (since Iblis is not an angel) or based on taghlib (predominance), so it is not a genuine takhsis. For the third, the verse originally conveyed a command (to prostrate) which could be abrogated, but after the event it became a report of a past event, so abrogation no longer applies; however, the category of mufassar does not require immunity from abrogation in principle, so it remains a valid example. The commentator concludes by citing al-Tawdih that a better example for mufassar is the verse 'Fight the polytheists all together' because it is a ruling (hukm) of Sharia, not a report.

مفسر (mufassar): A text whose clarity is increased by an explicit qualifier that removes a harmful probability
استثناء منقطع (istithnā' munqaṭi‘): An exception where the excepted item is not part of the general category
تغليب (taghlīb): Giving predominance to the majority in a mixed group when referring to them

Definition and Examples of Al-Mushkil
Definition and ruling of Al-Mushkil with two Quranic examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mushkil, describe the three-step method of resolving its ambiguity, and apply it to Quranic verses to derive speculative rulings.

Core Idea
Al-Mushkil is text requiring ijtihad; its intended meaning is determined through seeking and reflection, yielding a zanni (speculative) ruling.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, not every text is immediately clear. Some texts—called mushkil—present ambiguity that must be resolved through active intellectual effort. This lesson unpacks the definition, obligation, and method, using Quranic examples to show how context and reflection guide the mujtahid to the correct meaning.

Guided Path
1
Define Al-Mushkil and its ruling
State the three-step process: belief in truth, seeking possible meanings, and reflection to determine the intended meaning.
The ruling is wajib: it is obligatory to pursue understanding.
2
Analyze the first Quranic example (2:223)
Identify the word 'anna' as a homonym meaning 'where' or 'how'. Show how the word 'harth' (cultivation) resolves the ambiguity to 'how', forbidding anal intercourse.
The prohibition is zanni (speculative), not qat'i.
3
Trace the reasoning for the speculative ruling
Understand why the ruling does not lead to takfir of one who disagrees, and how it interacts with other clear texts (e.g., 2:222 on menstruation).
This shows the hierarchy of texts: nass over mushkil.
4
Examine the second example: metaphorical mushkil (76:16)
Analyze 'vessels of silver' as a metaphor combining clarity of glass and whiteness of silver. Explain how reflection resolves the apparent contradiction.
Isti'ara (metaphor) can also create mushkil.
5
Evaluate the methodological discussion on qiyas vs. isharat al-nass
Consider the argument that the prohibition of anal intercourse is established by qiyas to menstruation, and the Hanafi preference for isharat al-nass from hadith.
This illustrates when qiyas is superseded by textual indication.

Commentary
This passage defines 'Al-Mushkil' (the problematic or ambiguous text) in Hanafi usul. It begins by stating the ruling: when encountering a mushkil text—one where the intended meaning is not immediately clear—the first duty is to believe it is true (since it comes from Allah), then one must actively seek the correct meaning by examining the word's possible senses and reflecting on context to resolve the ambiguity. The author illustrates with the Quranic verse 'Then come to your cultivation however you wish' (2:223). The word 'anna' is a homonym that can mean either 'where' (question of place) or 'how' (question of manner). If taken as 'where', it would permit intercourse from any direction, including the anus. If taken as 'how', it permits any posture but only vaginal intercourse. By reflecting on the word 'cultivation' (harth), which implies a seedbed (the vagina), we conclude it means 'how', thereby forbidding anal intercourse. The prohibition is speculative (zanni), so one who considers it permissible does not become an unbeliever. The passage then engages with potential objections: it notes that even a person already knowing the word's ambiguity still needs reflection; and it clarifies that the ruling does not permit intercourse during menstruation, which is independently prohibited by a clear text (nass). A later part of the passage discusses whether the prohibition of anal intercourse with one's wife is established by analogy (qiyas) to the prohibition during menstruation (based on the common cause of harm) or by direct textual indication. The preferred view is that it is by indication of text (isharat al-nass), citing hadiths. Finally, the passage introduces another type of mushkil: metaphorical usage, exemplified by 'vessels of silver' (76:16). Since vessels are made of glass, not silver, the phrase is ambiguous. Reflection reveals that Paradise vessels combine the clarity of glass and the whiteness of silver, a subtle composite image. Throughout, the commentary emphasizes that mushkil texts yield speculative rulings and require ijtihad to resolve.

Defining al-Mujmal and Its Legal Ruling
Defining al-Mujmal and its legal ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-Mujmal, distinguish it from other ambiguous categories (al-khafi, al-mushkil, al-mushtarak), and explain the Hanafi ruling on handling a mujmal text.

Core Idea
Al-Mujmal is a text so ambiguous it requires stepwise clarification from the speaker, then seeking, then reflection—unless the clarification is sufficient.

This passage delves into one of the key categories of ambiguous texts in Hanafi usul: al-Mujmal. Unlike other ambiguous expressions, al-Mujmal cannot be resolved by the wording alone; it demands external clarification from the speaker. The text also contrasts it with similar categories like al-khafi and al-mushkil, and explains the proper procedure for deducing the intended meaning.

Guided Path
1
Parse the definition
Read the definition: 'what is crowded with meanings, and the intended meaning is confused in a way not grasped by the expression itself, but by resorting to inquiry from the one who made it ambiguous (al-mujmil), then seeking, then reflection.' Identify the two views: one requiring actual crowding of meanings, the other – followed by the commentator – that any speaker-ambiguity suffices.
Note the debate: is 'crowding' a real feature or just an explanation of the usual cause?
2
Identify the subtypes
List the three subtypes mentioned: (1) mushtarak (homonym) when no preference is possible, (2) strange word (e.g., 'haloo‘'), (3) speaker's intentional ambiguity even if the word is lexically clear (e.g., 'salah' before clarification).
Each subtype may require a different path to clarification.
3
Understand the procedural hierarchy
Note the three steps: inquiry from the mujmil, seeking (talab), and reflection (ta'ammul). The passage clarifies that if the clarification given is sufficient, the latter two steps are not needed. This distinguishes mujmal from khafi (needs only seeking) and mushkil (needs seeking and reflection).
The text uses the analogy of a stranger among unknown people to illustrate the extra step.
4
Apply to Quranic examples
Analyze the example of 'haloo‘' (Q. 70:19-21): it was mujmal until the verse clarified it. Also 'salah' (prayer) in Q. 2:43: the Prophet's practice provided a sufficient clarification. Note that after sufficient clarification, no further seeking or reflection is required.
These examples show the contrast between pre- and post-clarification.
5
State the ruling
The ruling (hukm) of a mujmal text is: (1) one must believe that the intended meaning is true, and (2) one must withhold judgment (tawaqquf) until clarification is obtained. After clarification, the text becomes mubayyan (clear) and is acted upon.
This ruling applies regardless of the subtype.

Mujmal and Insufficient Bayan: The Case of Riba
Explains mujmal and how insufficient bayan leads to ishkal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a mujmal text, assess the sufficiency of its bayan, and apply the Hanafi principle that insufficient bayan leads to ishkal requiring ijtihad.

Core Idea
Insufficient clarification of a mujmal can deepen ambiguity into ishkal.

The Quranic prohibition of riba is a classic example of a mujmal text. The Prophet's hadith provided clarification, but because it did not exhaustively define riba, scholars disagree on its underlying cause. This lesson examines the usul concept of mujmal and the threshold for sufficient clarification (bayan shafi).

Guided Path
1
Identify the mujmal text
Locate the Quranic verse 'wa harrama al-riba' (2:275) and note that the term 'riba' is linguistically ambiguous, meaning 'increase', without specifying which increase is prohibited.
2
Identify the clarifying hadith
The Prophet (peace be upon him) specified six commodities in a well-known hadith: wheat, barley, dates, salt, gold, silver, with conditions of equality and hand-to-hand exchange.
Hadith: 'Wheat for wheat... the excess is riba.'
3
Assess sufficiency of the bayan
Check whether the hadith resolves all ambiguity. The hadith does not use words of restriction like 'only', nor does it mention all possible items. Therefore, the bayan is insufficient (ghayr shafi).
Bayan shafi requires exhaustive clarification.
4
Recognize the transition to ishkal
Because the bayan is insufficient, the text exits ijmal (conciseness) and enters ishkal (ambiguity). This means scholars must exert ijtihad to determine the underlying cause ('illah) for ruling on new items.
Ishkal is a higher level of ambiguity than ijmal.
5

Definition of Haqiqah and its Ruling
Defines haqiqah and its types: lughawi, shar'i, 'urfi.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define haqiqah, distinguish its three types of coining (lughawi, shar'i, 'urfi), explain the condition of usage, and apply the concept to interpret Quranic verses of attributes.

Core Idea
Haqiqah is a word used for its coined meaning; without usage, it is neither haqiqah nor majaz.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, understanding haqiqah (literal meaning) is foundational for interpreting sacred texts. This lesson unpacks the definition of haqiqah, the three categories of coining (linguistic, legal, and customary), and the critical role of usage. We will also see how later Hanafi scholars applied haqiqah and majaz to the verses of divine attributes (ayaat al-sifat), clarifying the permissible approaches to avoid anthropomorphism while preserving revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition components
Parse the statement: 'Haqiqah is a name for every word used for what it was coined for.' Note that 'word' is the genus, and 'used for what it was coined' is the differentia excluding majaz (figurative) and muhamal (unused coined words).
The term 'wad'' (coining) is central: it is the assignment of a word to a meaning such that it indicates it without contextual indication.
2
Distinguish the three types of wad'
Classify coining based on the authority: linguistic (language setter), legal (Lawgiver), customary (specific group, further divided into general and particular). Memorize examples: 'insan' (linguistic), 'salat' (legal), 'fi'l' (particular customary).
Customary coining can be 'amm (general, like common usage) or khass (specific to a group like grammarians).
3
Recognize the condition of usage
Understand that a coined word is not haqiqah or majaz until it is actually used. Usage (isti'mal) is a prerequisite; before usage, it is merely a coined term waiting for application.
This condition is emphasized in the commentary: 'the word before usage and after coining is neither haqiqah nor majaz.'
4
Understand the ruling (hukm)
Haqiqah conveys the coined meaning, whether the application is specific (e.g., the act of bowing in the verse 'bow') or general (the command to all believers). The ruling is that the meaning exists as coined.
This is illustrated by the Quranic examples: 'O you who believe, bow' is specific in act but general in addressee.
5
Apply to ayaat al-sifat
Examine how later Hanafi scholars used the haqiqah/majaz distinction to interpret verses like 'the hand of Allah' and 'istawa' – they permitted figurative interpretation (ta'wil) when literal meaning would imply impossible attributes (like physical direction). This is a practical usul application.
This demonstrates the importance of haqiqah in theology: knowing when a word can shift to majaz prevents anthropomorphism while keeping the text authoritative.

Definition of Majaz and the Principle of Prioritizing Haqiqah
Defines haqiqah and majaz, then states the priority rule.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of majaz, contrast it with haqiqah, and apply the Hanafi principle that majaz is dropped whenever haqiqah is possible to operate.

Core Idea
Majaz is dropped whenever haqiqah can be applied — the borrowed never competes with the original.

In Hanafi usul, the real meaning (haqiqah) always takes precedence over the metaphorical (majaz). This lesson defines both terms, shows how to distinguish them through negation, and establishes the foundational rule that majaz is set aside as long as the real meaning is possible. This rule governs countless Islamic legal interpretations, from oaths to contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the haqiqah and majaz definitions
Read the passage's opening. Note that haqiqah is 'what cannot be negated from what it applies to' (e.g., a father is always a father), while majaz is 'what can be both affirmed and negated' (e.g., a grandfather can be called 'father' metaphorically but may also be denied that title).
The key test: can the word be truthfully denied of the subject? If yes, it's majaz.
2
Apply the test to examples
The passage gives examples: a real father vs. a grandfather; a real lion vs. a brave man. For each, determine whether the term can be negated. If it cannot be negated, it is haqiqah; if it can, it is majaz.
This test works only for words with both real and metaphorical uses.
3
State the principle of priority
The principle is: 'Whenever it is possible to operate with the real meaning, the metaphorical is dropped.' This is because majaz is borrowed from the real, and the borrowed does not compete with the original. The 'possibility' is existential — meaning the real meaning is feasible given the context (causes present, obstacles absent).
If the real meaning is legally impossible (e.g., intercourse with a stranger in an oath context), then majaz is used.
4
Recognize what this principle excludes
The passage refutes the claim that when both real and metaphorical meanings are possible, the word becomes mujmal (ambiguous). In Hanafi usul, priority is given to haqiqah, so the word remains clear (zahir), not ambiguous.
This distinguishes Hanafi usul from some other usul schools that might see ambiguity.

يصح ما صدق عليه والمراد أن المعنى الحقيقي لا يسقط ولا ينتفي عما صدق عليه، بخلاف المعنى المجازي، فإنه أن يصدق عليه، ويصح أن ينفي عنه، يقال للأب: أب ولا يصح أن يقال: إنه ليس بأب بخلاف الجد، فإنه يصح أن يقال: إنه أب ويصح أن يقال: إنه ليس بأب، وكذا أي مجازا الهيكل المعلوم يصح أن يقال عليه : إنه أسد ولا ينفي عنه بأن يقال: إنه ليس بأسد، بخلاف الرجل الشجاع، فإنه يصح أن يقال: إنه أسد وأن يقال: إنه ليس بأسد: ومتى أمكن العمل بها سقط المجاز ، هذا أصل كبير لنا يتفرع عليه كثير من الأحكام أي ما دام أمكن العمل بالمعنى الحقيقي سقط المعنى المجازي؛ لأنه مستعار والمستعار لا يزاحم الأصل. المعنى المجازي فيكون العقد لما ينعقد دون العزم أي يكون العقد المذكور في قوله تعالى: ﴿يُؤَاخِذُكُمْ بِمَا عَقَدْتُمُ الْأَيْمَانَ﴾ محمولاً على ما ينعقد، وهو المنعقدة فقط؛ لأنه حقيقة هذا اللفظ دون معنى العزم حتى يشمل الغموس والمنعقدة جميعًا؛ لأنه مجاز، والمجاز لا يزاحم الحقيقة. عما صدق عليه : إيماء إلى أن المراد بالمسمى في المتن ما صدق عليه. (القمر) أمكن العمل إلخ المراد بالإمكان الإمكان الوقوعي أي إذا جاز العمل بالمعنى الحقيقي بحصول أسبابه، وارتفاع موانعه سقط المجاز، فلا يحمل اللفظ على المجاز، ولا يجوز التوقف في الحقيقة بواسطة المجاز لا كما زعم بعض الناس أنه إذا أمكن أن يراد المجاز بلفظ كما أمكن إرادة الحقيقة يكون اللفظ مجملاً . (القمر) لا يزاحم الأصل: ولهذا قلنا إذا حلف لا ينكح فلانة وهي منكوحته أنه يقع على الوطء دون العقد حتى لو طلقها ثم تزوجها لا يحنث قبل الوطء؛ لأن هذا اللفظ في الوطء حقيقة، وفي العقد مجاز ، فكان حمله على الحقيقة أولى، بخلاف ما إذا كانت المرأة أجنبية حيث يقع على العقد؛ لأن وطيها لما حرم عليه كانت الحقيقة مهجورة شرعًا فتعين المجاز . (السنبلي) على ما ينعقد أي يرتبط، وهو ربط اللفظ باللفظ لإيجاب حكم كربط لفظ القسم بالمقسم عليه لإثبات البر، وهذا أقرب إلى الحقيقة؛ لأن أصل العقد عقد الحبل، وهو شد بعضه ببعض ثم استعير للألفاظ التي عقد بعضها ببعض لإيجاب حكم ثم استعير لما يكون سببًا لهذا الربط، وهو عزم القلب، وكان الحمل على ربط اللفظ أولى؛ لأنه أقرب إلى الحقيقة بدرجة وهذا إنما يوجد فيما يتصور فيه البر وهو اليمين المنعقدة في المستقبل، وفي الغموس لم يتصور ذلك هذا ما قاله ابن الملك. (القمر) لأنه مجاز :إلخ وليس للخصم أن يمنع كون العزم معنى مجازيا للعقد بدلالة استعماله فيه عرفا؛ لأن مداره على الأئمة الواضعين. (القمر)
بيان أقسام التقسيم الثالث وتحقيقه أن اليمين ثلاث لغو، وغموس ومنعقدة. فاللغو أن يحلف على فعل ماض كاذبًا ظاناً أنه حق ولا إثم فيه ولا كفارة والغموس أن يحلف على فعل ماض كاذبًا عمدًا، وفيه الإثم دون الكفارة عندنا، وعند الشافعي له فيه الكفارة أيضًا. والمنعقدة: أن يحلف على فعل آتٍ فإن حنث فيه يجب الإثم والكفارة جميعًا بالاتفاق؛ وذلك لأن الله تعالى ذكر هذه المسألة في الموضعين، فقال في سورة البقرة: لَا يُؤَاخِذُكُمُ اللَّهُ بِاللَّغْوِ فِي أَيْمَانِكُمْ وَلَكِنْ يُوَاحِدُكُمْ بِمَا كَسَبَتْ قُلُوبُكُمْ، وقال في سورة المائدة عوضه: ﴿وَلَكِنْ يُوَاحِدُكُمْ بِمَا عَقَدْتُمُ الْأَيْمَانَ فَكَفَّارَتُهُ (الآية)، فالشافعي له: يقول: بأن قوله: ﴿بمَا عَقْدتُمُ الْأَيْمَانَ﴾ معناه ومعنى بِمَا كَسَبَتْ قُلُوبُكُمْ ،واحد، فيشمل كلا الآيتين الغموس والمنعقدة جميعًا، والمؤاخذة في المائدة مقيدة بالكفارة، فتحمل عليها المؤاخذة المطلقة المذكورة في البقرة، فيكون الإثم والكفارة في كليهما فيطبق بين الآيتين بهذا النمط، ونحن نقول: إن معنى العزم والكسب مجاز في قوله تعالى: بِمَا عَقَدْتُمُ الْأَيْمَانَ ، والحقيقة هو المنعقدة فقط، فآية المائدة تدل على أن الكفارة في المنعقدة فقط، بخلاف بِمَا كَسَبَتْ قُلُوبُكُمْ في البقرة، فإنه عام للغموس والمنعقدة جميعًا، والمؤاخذة فيها مطلقة فتصرف إلى الفرد الكامل، وهو المؤاخذة الأخروية، فيكون الإثم في الغموس والمنعقدة جميعًا، هذا هو غاية التحرير في هذا المقام، وسيجيء هذا في بحث المعارضة أيضًا إن شاء الله تعالى. والغموس: مبالغة في الغمس سميت به؛ لأنها تغمس صاحبها في الإثم ثم في النار. Statement of the divisions of the third division. Definition of majaz and its ruling. It is valid [to affirm] what it applies to. The intended meaning is that the real meaning is not dropped nor negated from what it applies to, unlike the metaphorical meaning, for indeed it may be affirmed of it and it is valid to negate it. It is said of the father: 'father' and it is not valid to say: 'he is not a father' contrary to the grandfather, for indeed it is valid to say: 'he is a father' and it is valid to say: 'he is not a father'. Similarly, any known figure metaphorically, it is valid to say of him: 'he is a lion' and it is not negated from him by saying: 'he is not a lion', contrary to the brave man, for indeed it is valid to say: 'he is a lion' and to say: 'he is not a lion'. And whenever it is possible to operate with [the real meaning], the metaphorical is dropped. This is a great principle for us from which many rulings branch off, i.e., as long as it is possible to operate with the real meaning, the metaphorical meaning is dropped because it is borrowed, and the borrowed does not compete with the original. The metaphorical meaning then 'al-‘aqd' [contract/oath] is for what is tied without 'al-‘azm' [intention], i.e., the word 'al-‘aqd' mentioned in His saying: 'Allah will not hold you accountable for what you have confirmed of oaths' is carried upon what is tied, which is the mun‘aqidah [confirmed oath] only, because it is the real meaning of this word, not the meaning of intention such that it would include both ghamus and mun‘aqidah together, because that is metaphorical, and the metaphorical does not compete with the real. 'What it applies to': a hint that what is meant by the named in the matn is what it applies to. (al-Qamar) 'Whenever it is possible to operate etc.': what is meant by possibility is existential possibility, i.e., if it is permissible to operate with the real meaning by the occurrence of its causes and the removal of its obstacles, the metaphorical is dropped. So the word is not carried upon the metaphorical, and it is not permissible to suspend [judgment] regarding the real because of the metaphorical, contrary to the claim of some people that if it is possible that the metaphorical be intended by a word just as it is possible to intend the real, the word becomes mujmal. (al-Qamar) 'Does not compete with the original': and for this we said: if one swears, 'I will not marry so-and-so' while she is his wife, the oath falls upon intercourse not upon the marriage contract, even if he divorces her then marries her, he does not break his oath before intercourse; because this word for intercourse is real, and for marriage contract is metaphorical, so carrying it upon the real is preferable. Contrary to if the woman is a stranger, where it falls upon the marriage contract; because since her intercourse is forbidden to him, the real is legally abandoned, so the metaphorical is determined. (al-Sunbuli) 'Upon what is tied': i.e., binds, which is binding word to word to establish a ruling, like binding the word of oath to the thing sworn upon to establish piety. This is closer to the real; because the origin of 'al-‘aqd' is tying a rope, which is tightening some parts to others, then it was borrowed for words that are tied to each other to establish a ruling, then it was borrowed for what is a cause of this binding, which is the intention of the heart. So carrying it upon the binding of words is preferable because it is closer to the real by one degree, and this only exists where piety is conceivable, which is the mun‘aqidah oath concerning the future; as for ghamus, that is not conceivable. This is what Ibn al-Malik said. (al-Qamar) 'Because it is metaphorical etc.': and the opponent cannot prevent that the meaning of intention is metaphorical for 'al-‘aqd' based on its usage in custom; because the criterion is the lexicographers who set it. (al-Qamar) Statement of the divisions of the third division and its verification: that the oath is three: laghw, ghamus, and mun‘aqidah. Al-laghw: that one swears concerning a past act falsely while thinking it is true; there is no sin and no expiation in it. Al-ghamus: that one swears concerning a past act falsely deliberately; there is sin without expiation according to us, and according to al-Shafi‘i, there is also expiation for it. Al-mun‘aqidah: that one swears concerning a future act, then if he breaks it, sin and expiation are both obligatory by agreement. And that is because Allah mentioned this issue in two places: He said in Surat al-Baqarah: 'Allah will not hold you accountable for useless oaths, but He will hold you accountable for what your hearts have earned (2:225)', and He said in Surat al-Ma’idah in its place: 'but He will hold you accountable for what you have confirmed of oaths, so its expiation (5:89)'. Al-Shafi‘i says: his saying: 'by what you have confirmed of oaths' has one meaning with 'by what your hearts have earned', so it includes in both verses both ghamus and mun‘aqidah together, and the accountability in al-Ma’idah is restricted by expiation, so the unrestricted accountability mentioned in al-Baqarah is carried upon it, so sin and expiation are in both, thus reconciling between the two verses in this manner. And we say: the meaning of intention and earning is metaphorical in His saying: 'by what you have confirmed of oaths', and the real is mun‘aqidah only. So the verse of al-Ma’idah indicates that expiation is in mun‘aqidah only, contrary to 'by what your hearts have earned' in al-Baqarah, for it is general for both ghamus and mun‘aqidah, and the accountability in it is unrestricted, so it is diverted to the perfect individual, which is the otherworldly punishment. Thus sin is in both ghamus and mun‘aqidah. This is the ultimate exposition in this context, and this will also come in the topic of conflict, Allah willing. And al-ghamus is an exaggeration in 'al-ghams' [dipping]; it is named thus because it dips its doer in sin then in fire. (al-Qamar) 'By what you have earned etc.': i.e., by what your hearts have intended and purposed, which is ghamus and mun‘aqidah. (al-Qamar) 'In its place': i.e., in place of His saying: 'but He will hold you accountable for what your hearts have earned' (2:225). (al-Qamar) 'The two verses': i.e., the verse of al-Baqarah and al-Ma’idah. (al-Muhashshi) 'Upon it': i.e., upon the accountability mentioned in al-Ma’idah. (al-Qamar) 'Unrestricted': because the Shafi‘is carry the unrestricted upon the restricted. (al-Muhashshi) 'In both': i.e., ghamus and mun‘aqidah. 'Only': because the metaphorical after the possibility of operating with the real is dropped. (al-Muhashshi) 'In it': i.e., not restricted by expiation.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a matn from Noor al-Anwar, a Hanafi usul work. It defines the concept of majaz (metaphorical meaning) and establishes the fundamental principle that when the haqiqah (real meaning) is possible to apply, the majaz is dropped. This is a key Hanafi principle for interpreting texts. The commentary gives examples: a father is always a father (cannot be negated), whereas a grandfather can be called father metaphorically but also can be denied that status. Similarly, a brave man can be called a lion metaphorically but also denied, while a real lion cannot be denied. The principle is then applied to the interpretation of Quranic verses about oaths. In Surat al-Ma’idah (5:89), Allah says 'by what you have confirmed of oaths' (bima ‘aqadtum al-ayman). The Hanafis hold that the word '‘aqd' (contract/oath) has its real meaning in 'tying' or 'confirming' an oath for future acts (mun‘aqidah), while the meaning of 'intention' (azm) is metaphorical. Thus, the verse only imposes kaffarah (expiation) on mun‘aqidah oaths. In Surat al-Baqarah (2:225), the phrase 'by what your hearts have earned' (bima kasabat qulubukum) is general, including both ghamus (deliberate false oath about past) and mun‘aqidah, but the accountability there is unrestricted (not tied to kaffarah), so it is interpreted as otherworldly punishment (ithm). Thus, Hanafis rule that ghamus has sin but no kaffarah, while mun‘aqidah has both. The Shafi‘i view is that both phrases have the same meaning, so both types require kaffarah. The Hanafi argument relies on the priority of haqiqah over majaz, as long as haqiqah is possible. The commentary also gives a fiqh example: if a man swears not to marry a woman who is currently his wife, the oath is interpreted as referring to intercourse (real meaning) rather than the marriage contract (metaphorical), so he only breaks the oath by intercourse. But if she is a stranger, the real meaning is impossible (intercourse is already forbidden), so the metaphorical meaning (contract) is used.

Key Terms
مجاز (majāz): A word used in a meaning other than its original lexical meaning due to a relationship, and it is valid to both affirm and negate it.
حقيقة (ḥaqīqah): A word used in its original lexically-assigned meaning; it is not negated from what it applies to.

Applying Haqiqah Priority to the Oath Verse — The Hanafi vs Shafi'i Dispute
Applies the haqiqah priority rule to the oath verses, contrasting Hanafi and Shafi'i views.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of haqiqah priority to derive the Hanafi ruling on ghamus oaths and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using textual analysis.

Core Idea
Because 'عقد' in the verse is haqiqah for future confirmed oaths, ghamus carries sin but no kaffarah in the Hanafi school.

The previous lesson established the principle: majaz is dropped when haqiqah is possible. Now we see this principle in action in a famous dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is over whether a deliberate false oath about the past (ghamus) requires expiation (kaffarah). Both schools use the same two Quranic verses but reach opposite rulings due to different usul frameworks. This lesson walks through each school's istidlal and the thamarah al-khilaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two relevant verses
Al-Baqarah 2:225 says 'Allah will not hold you accountable for useless oaths, but He will hold you accountable for what your hearts have earned (bima kasabat qulubukum).' Al-Ma'idah 5:89 says 'but He will hold you accountable for what you have confirmed of oaths (bima ‘aqadtum al-ayman), so its expiation...' Note that only al-Ma'idah mentions kaffarah.
Both are clear (zahir) but the key is the meaning of '‘aqadtum'.
2
Understand the Hanafi analysis of 'عقدتم'
Apply the haqiqah priority rule: 'عقد' (tie/confirm) is haqiqah for an oath that is 'tied' to a future action (mun‘aqidah), and majaz for intention (azm). Since haqiqah is possible (there exist future oaths), the verse is restricted to mun‘aqidah. Thus, kaffarah applies only to mun‘aqidah, not to ghamus.
The Hanafis argue that the word 'عقد' is not used for past events; it literally means to tie, and an oath about the past cannot be 'tied' to a future consequence.
3
Understand the Shafi'i counter-argument
The Shafi'is argue that the phrase 'bima kasabat qulubukum' (what your hearts have earned) in al-Baqarah is general, including both ghamus and mun‘aqidah. They then apply the usul principle of carrying the unrestricted (mutlaq) to the restricted (muqayyad): the unrestricted accountability in al-Baqarah is carried to the kaffarah in al-Ma'idah, so both types require kaffarah.
For the Shafi'is, the word 'عقد' in al-Ma'idah includes both types because they see the two phrases as having the same meaning.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to the Shafi'i argument
The Hanafi response has two parts. First, 'عقد' is haqiqah only for mun‘aqidah, so it cannot be extended. Second, the accountability in al-Baqarah is unrestricted (not tied to kaffarah) and refers to otherworldly punishment (sin in the afterlife), so carrying it to the kaffarah of al-Ma'idah would mix two different types of accountability.
The Hanafis also note that the word 'كسب' (earn) in al-Baqarah is general, and its unrestricted accountability is interpreted as sin (ithm), not kaffarah.
5
State the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
For ghamus: Hanafi says sin but no kaffarah; Shafi'i says sin and kaffarah. For mun‘aqidah: both schools agree on sin and kaffarah. This difference affects how judges and muftis advise on oath-breaking and expiation.
The tag 'ghamus' comes from 'yaghums' (dips) — it dips the swearer into sin.

Statement of the divisions of the third division. Definition of majaz and its ruling. It is valid [to affirm] what it applies to. The intended meaning is that the real meaning is not dropped nor negated from what it applies to, unlike the metaphorical meaning, for indeed it may be affirmed of it and it is valid to negate it. It is said of the father: 'father' and it is not valid to say: 'he is not a father' contrary to the grandfather, for indeed it is valid to say: 'he is a father' and it is valid to say: 'he is not a father'. Similarly, any known figure metaphorically, it is valid to say of him: 'he is a lion' and it is not negated from him by saying: 'he is not a lion', contrary to the brave man, for indeed it is valid to say: 'he is a lion' and to say: 'he is not a lion'. And whenever it is possible to operate with [the real meaning], the metaphorical is dropped. This is a great principle for us from which many rulings branch off, i.e., as long as it is possible to operate with the real meaning, the metaphorical meaning is dropped because it is borrowed, and the borrowed does not compete with the original. The metaphorical meaning then 'al-‘aqd' [contract/oath] is for what is tied without 'al-‘azm' [intention], i.e., the word 'al-‘aqd' mentioned in His saying: 'Allah will not hold you accountable for what you have confirmed of oaths' is carried upon what is tied, which is the mun‘aqidah [confirmed oath] only, because it is the real meaning of this word, not the meaning of intention such that it would include both ghamus and mun‘aqidah together, because that is metaphorical, and the metaphorical does not compete with the real. 'What it applies to': a hint that what is meant by the named in the matn is what it applies to. (al-Qamar) 'Whenever it is possible to operate etc.': what is meant by possibility is existential possibility, i.e., if it is permissible to operate with the real meaning by the occurrence of its causes and the removal of its obstacles, the metaphorical is dropped. So the word is not carried upon the metaphorical, and it is not permissible to suspend [judgment] regarding the real because of the metaphorical, contrary to the claim of some people that if it is possible that the metaphorical be intended by a word just as it is possible to intend the real, the word becomes mujmal. (al-Qamar) 'Does not compete with the original': and for this we said: if one swears, 'I will not marry so-and-so' while she is his wife, the oath falls upon intercourse not upon the marriage contract, even if he divorces her then marries her, he does not break his oath before intercourse; because this word for intercourse is real, and for marriage contract is metaphorical, so carrying it upon the real is preferable. Contrary to if the woman is a stranger, where it falls upon the marriage contract; because since her intercourse is forbidden to him, the real is legally abandoned, so the metaphorical is determined. (al-Sunbuli) 'Upon what is tied': i.e., binds, which is binding word to word to establish a ruling, like binding the word of oath to the thing sworn upon to establish piety. This is closer to the real; because the origin of 'al-‘aqd' is tying a rope, which is tightening some parts to others, then it was borrowed for words that are tied to each other to establish a ruling, then it was borrowed for what is a cause of this binding, which is the intention of the heart. So carrying it upon the binding of words is preferable because it is closer to the real by one degree, and this only exists where piety is conceivable, which is the mun‘aqidah oath concerning the future; as for ghamus, that is not conceivable. This is what Ibn al-Malik said. (al-Qamar) Statement of the divisions of the third division and its verification: that the oath is three: laghw, ghamus, and mun‘aqidah. Al-laghw: that one swears concerning a past act falsely while thinking it is true; there is no sin and no expiation in it. Al-ghamus: that one swears concerning a past act falsely deliberately; there is sin without expiation according to us, and according to al-Shafi‘i, there is also expiation for it. Al-mun‘aqidah: that one swears concerning a future act, then if he breaks it, sin and expiation are both obligatory by agreement. And that is because Allah mentioned this issue in two places: He said in Surat al-Baqarah: 'Allah will not hold you accountable for useless oaths, but He will hold you accountable for what your hearts have earned (2:225)', and He said in Surat al-Ma’idah in its place: 'but He will hold you accountable for what you have confirmed of oaths, so its expiation (5:89)'. And we say: the meaning of intention and earning is metaphorical in His saying: 'by what you have confirmed of oaths', and the real is mun‘aqidah only. Thus sin is in both ghamus and mun‘aqidah. This is the ultimate exposition in this context, and this will also come in the topic of conflict, Allah willing. And al-ghamus is an exaggeration in 'al-ghams' [dipping]; it is named thus because it dips its doer in sin then in fire. Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a matn from Noor al-Anwar, a Hanafi usul work. It defines the concept of majaz (metaphorical meaning) and establishes the fundamental principle that when the haqiqah (real meaning) is possible to apply, the majaz is dropped. This is a key Hanafi principle for interpreting texts. The commentary gives examples: a father is always a father (cannot be negated), whereas a grandfather can be called father metaphorically but also can be denied that status. Similarly, a brave man can be called a lion metaphorically but also denied, while a real lion cannot be denied. The principle is then applied to the interpretation of Quranic verses about oaths. In Surat al-Ma’idah (5:89), Allah says 'by what you have confirmed of oaths' (bima ‘aqadtum al-ayman). The Hanafis hold that the word '‘aqd' (contract/oath) has its real meaning in 'tying' or 'confirming' an oath for future acts (mun‘aqidah), while the meaning of 'intention' (azm) is metaphorical. Thus, the verse only imposes kaffarah (expiation) on mun‘aqidah oaths. In Surat al-Baqarah (2:225), the phrase 'by what your hearts have earned' (bima kasabat qulubukum) is general, including both ghamus (deliberate false oath about past) and mun‘aqidah, but the accountability there is unrestricted (not tied to kaffarah), so it is interpreted as otherworldly punishment (ithm). Thus, Hanafis rule that ghamus has sin but no kaffarah, while mun‘aqidah has both. The Shafi‘i view is that both phrases have the same meaning, so both types require kaffarah. The Hanafi argument relies on the priority of haqiqah over majaz, as long as haqiqah is possible. The commentary also gives a fiqh example: if a man swears not to marry a woman who is currently his wife, the oath is interpreted as referring to intercourse (real meaning) rather than the marriage contract (metaphorical), so he only breaks the oath by intercourse. But if she is a stranger, the real meaning is impossible (intercourse is already forbidden), so the metaphorical meaning (contract) is used.

Key Terms
اليمين الغموس (al-yamīn al-ghamūs): An oath sworn deliberately about a past event while knowing it is false; it dips (yaghums) the doer in sin.
اليمين المنعقدة (al-yamīn al-mun‘aqidah): A confirmed oath about a future action, which if broken requires both sin and kaffarah (by agreement).
حمل المطلق على المقيد (ḥaml al-muṭlaq ʿalā al-muqayyad): An usul principle: when a restricted ruling is found, the unrestricted is applied to that restriction if the legal cause is the same.

The Particle 'على': Obligation vs. Deposit in Hanafi Usul
Analysis of 'على' as obligation and deposit exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the legal meaning of 'على' as obligation and apply the condition that shifts its meaning to safekeeping in the case of deposit.

Core Idea
The particle 'على' indicates debt obligation; only attachment to 'deposit' changes it to safekeeping duty.

This lesson unpacks the primary legal function of the Arabic particle 'على' as a marker of obligation on liability. We examine how this meaning is preserved even when the word 'deposit' is attached, with a subtle shift from payment to preservation.

Guided Path
1
Identify 'على' as a particle of obligation
In Hanafi usul, 'على' is primarily used to indicate obligation (الإلزام) on the liability. For example, 'له علي ألف درهم' means a debt of one thousand dirhams is owed.
This is its legal-technical meaning derived from its literal sense of elevation.
2
Recognize the literal meaning and its legal extension
The literal meaning of 'على' is elevation (الاستعلاء). In legal context, it is used metaphorically to impose something on the liability, as if the debt 'rides' upon the person.
This is a common shift from concrete to abstract in legal language.
3
Understand the exception with deposit
When 'على' is followed by the word 'وديعة' (deposit), the obligation changes from payment to safekeeping. For example, 'له علي ألف وديعة' means the thousand is a trust, not a debt.
The obligation to preserve remains, but the duty to deliver (payment) is removed.
4
Note why preservation is still obligatory
Even as a deposit, the holder must keep it safe. So 'على' still indicates some obligation, but the nature of that obligation changes from liability to trust.
This shows the flexibility of 'على' in legal usage.

The Meaning of 'على' in Pure Commutative Contracts and Divorce
Analysis of 'على' in commutative contracts and divorce dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that 'على' in pure commutative contracts means 'باء' (with) and explain the intra-Hanafi dispute regarding its use in divorce with compensation.

Core Idea
In pure commutative contracts, 'على' means 'with' (price); in divorce, the Sahibayn extend this but Abu Hanifa keeps it as release.

This lesson explores how 'على' shifts meaning when used in contracts of exchange such as sale and hire. We also analyze the famous disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding divorce with compensation, where the interpretation of 'على' determines whether the payment is proportional or lump sum.

Guided Path
1
Understand the rule for pure commutative contracts
In contracts where exchange is essential (sale, hire, marriage), 'على' is interpreted as 'باء' (with) indicating the price. For example, 'بعت هذا على ألف' means 'I sold this for a thousand dirhams'.
'باء' indicates adherence, which suits the binding nature of exchange.
2
Recognize the reason: suitability between obligation and adherence
The particle 'على' indicates obligation, and 'باء' indicates adherence. Both share the concept of binding, so 'على' can metaphorically serve as 'باء' in exchange contexts.
This is a contextual interpretation based on customary usage in contracts.
3
Extend to divorce with compensation according to the Sahibayn
According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, when a wife says 'طلقني ثلاثاً على ألف', 'على' is also interpreted as 'باء' (with). Thus, it becomes like a sale and the payment is divided over the divorces.
If only one divorce is given, one-third of the thousand is owed.
4
Contrast Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that divorce is fundamentally a release (إسقاط), not an exchange. Therefore, even if compensation is involved, 'على' retains its meaning of obligation for the entire sum, not divided proportionally.
This leads to a different practical outcome: the full amount is due for a single divorce.
5
Note the practical consequence
According to the Sahibayn, the payment is divided: one divorce requires one-third of the specified amount. According to Abu Hanifa, the entire amount is due regardless of the number of divorces (unless otherwise agreed).
The Hanafi school relies on the Sahibayn's view in fatwa.

Usul Analysis of 'إذا': Condition vs. Time
Analyzing 'إذا' as mushtarak between time and condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether 'إذا' is used as a conditional or temporal particle in a given Arabic text, and explain the Hanafi usul principle governing its interpretation.

Core Idea
When 'إذا' is used for condition, it loses its time meaning entirely, like 'إن'.

The particle 'إذا' appears frequently in legal texts, yet its precise meaning—temporal or conditional—can significantly affect rulings. In this lesson, we analyze the linguistic nature of 'إذا' according to Kufan and Basran grammarians, focusing on the Hanafi position derived from Abu Hanifa and the disagreement with his disciples Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two possible meanings of 'إذا'
According to Kufan grammarians, 'إذا' is shared (mushtarak) between being a time adverb (zarf) and a conditional particle (shart). Recognize this dual nature.
Mushtarak means a word has more than one distinct meaning.
2
State Abu Hanifa's view and the underlying principle
Abu Hanifa holds that when 'إذا' is used for condition, it loses its temporal meaning entirely because a mushtarak word, when used in one meaning, does not indicate another.
This principle is known as 'la umum lil-mushtarak'.
3
Recognize the grammatical signs of conditional 'إذا'
Conditional 'إذا' jussifies the present tense verb (e.g., 'تصبك' from the poetic example) and often introduces the result with 'fa'. Temporal 'إذا' does not jussify.
The jussive mood is a key indicator.
4
Understand the opposing views (Basrans and disciples)
Basran grammarians consider 'إذا' essentially temporal, even when used conditionally (as a metaphor). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad follow this view, analogizing to 'متى' which retains time in condition.
The disciples' view leads to combining real (time) and metaphorical (condition) in one usage.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response and the practical consequence
The Hanafi response argues that combining haqiqah and majaz is impermissible. In legal texts, Abu Hanifa's interpretation means that a conditional 'إذا' imposes a pure condition without specifying time, whereas the disciples' view adds a temporal dimension.
This difference (thamarah al-khilaf) affects how rulings are derived.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of the Arabic word 'إذا' (idha) and its legal-usul implications. The text begins by stating the Kufan grammarians' view that 'إذا' is shared (mushtarak) between being a time adverb (zarf) and a conditional particle (harf al-shart). It can be used in either sense, and when used as a condition, the present tense verb after it is jussive and the result may take 'fa'. Two poetic examples are given: one where 'إذا' is clearly conditional (with jussive verb) and one where it is temporal (without jussive). The commentary then focuses on Abu Hanifa's position: since 'إذا' is shared between two meanings, when it is used for condition, it loses its time meaning entirely and becomes purely like 'إن' (inna). This is contrasted with the Basran grammarians, who hold that 'إذا' is primarily temporal and can be used conditionally only as a metaphor (majaz), still retaining its temporal sense. The text then addresses the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. According to the commentary, the disciples follow the Basran view that 'إذا' retains time even in condition. However, an objection is raised: if it retains time, then using it conditionally would combine the real (haqiqah) and metaphorical (majaz) meanings, which is impermissible. The author of al-Da'ir responds that there is no contradiction because time can serve as a condition. But the commentator (likely al-Qamar or al-Sanbali) refutes this, insisting that combining real and metaphorical is absolutely impermissible in intention. This discussion is crucial for usul al-fiqh because the interpretation of 'إذا' in legal texts (e.g., Quranic verses or hadith) affects whether a ruling is conditional (requiring both cause and effect) or merely temporal (indicating time of occurrence). Abu Hanifa's view leads to a stricter separation: if 'إذا' is conditional, it does not imply any specific time, whereas the disciples' view allows both time and condition simultaneously. The passage uses linguistic analysis (grammar and usul principles like ishtirak and haqiqah/majaz) to resolve a legal hermeneutical issue.

Key Terms
إذا (idha): A word shared (mushtarak) between being a time adverb and a conditional particle according to Abu Hanifa; primarily temporal according to Basrans.
مشترك (mushtarak): A word with more than one meaning; used in the sense of being shared between condition and time.
شرط (shart): Condition; here refers to the conditional usage of 'إذا' as a particle of condition.
ظرف (zarf): Adverb of time; the primary meaning of 'إذا' according to Basrans, indicating the time of an event.

Bayan Darurah: Definition and Quranic Evidence
Defining bayan darurah with Quranic example and rebutting an objection.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define bayan darurah and explain how the Qur'anic inheritance verse exemplifies this type of bayan.

Core Idea
Silence can be valid legal communication when necessity demands, as shown by the Qur'an's implicit indication.

This lesson introduces a special type of legal communication (bayan) in Hanafi usul: bayan darurah, or 'bayan of necessity.' Unlike ordinary bayan that relies on explicit speech, this type occurs through silence or contextual clues when clarity is needed. The Qur'an itself provides a classic example in the inheritance verse, where specifying the mother's share implicitly indicates the father's share.

Guided Path
1
Understand the term 'bayan darurah'
Examine the definition: it is a bayan that occurs through a means not originally appointed for expression, such as silence. Note that the normal instrument of bayan is speech, but necessity (darurah) allows silence to serve the same function.
The Arabic phrase 'بما لم يوضع له' refers to silence not being originally intended for communication.
2
Identify the Quranic example
The text cites Q. 4:11: 'And his parents inherit from him, so to his mother is the third...' The beginning of the verse creates an undefined partnership, and specifying the mother's share clarifies that the father gets the remainder—this implicit clarification is a form of bayan.
The explicit part (mantuq) is the mother's one-third; the tacit part (maskut) is the father's two-thirds, revealed by context.
3
Analyze the legal mechanism
The verse's explicit statement (mother's share) becomes a strong contextual clue (qarina) that determines the omitted portion (father's share). This is not explicit speech but an implicit indication that fulfills the purpose of bayan.
The Hanafi principle is that 'the explicit speech is a strong indication for specifying the omitted part.'
4
Differentiate from terminological substitution
The text addresses an objection: the author's use of 'baddala' (change) is linguistic, not the technical 'bayan al-tabdil' that would cause contradiction. The shift is from one description (undefined partnership) to another (defined shares).
This clarifies that the concept is about clarification through re-description, not nullification.
5
Recognize the broader usul context
Understand that bayan darurah is one of several types of bayan in Hanafi usul. It is used when the Lawgiver communicates a ruling indirectly, relying on the audience's reasoning to derive the full meaning.
This principle is foundational for understanding how silence (sukut) can carry legal weight.

Commentary
This passage is from a chapter on the divisions of bayan (legal communication). It defines bayan darurah (bayan of necessity) as a type of bayan that occurs through means not originally designated for expression, such as silence. The author explains that normally bayan is through speech, but when necessity arises, silence can function as a bayan. The commentary then provides an example from the Quran: in the inheritance verse (4:11), the explicit statement that the mother gets a third implicitly indicates that the father gets the remainder. This shows how the explicit part (mantuq) can clarify the tacit part (maskut). The commentary also discusses a disagreement regarding the nature of conditions in legal statements, referencing Fakhr al-Islam and Shams al-A'imma, but the focus remains on the concept of bayan darurah. Next, the passage explains that bayan darurah can also be proven by the 'indication of the state of the speaker' (dalalat hal al-mutakallim). This is exemplified by the silence of the Prophet (peace be upon him) when he witnessed practices like mudaraba or sales without objection; his silence stands in place of a command indicating permissibility. Similarly, the silence of the Companions, under specific conditions (ability to object and the doer being Muslim), also serves as bayan. The passage further illustrates this with the case of a runaway slave woman who married and had children. Caliph 'Umar ruled in favor of the master regarding the woman but kept silent about compensating for the usufruct (services and intercourse) of the woman and children. This silence, given the context, indicated that no compensation was due. The commentary addresses objections: how can silence be considered 'speech'? It clarifies that the silent person is a 'speaker' through the tongue of the state (tongue of circumstance), not through verbal articulation. The passage also discusses conditions: silence only indicates permissibility if there is no prior statement declaring prohibition; if there was, the silence would be interpreted as abrogation. Also, the doer must be a Muslim; silence regarding the actions of a disbeliever (e.g., eating pork) does not imply permission. Overall, the lesson establishes that under necessity and contextual indications, silence can be a valid form of legal communication (bayan darurah), carrying the weight of a ruling.

Key Terms
بيان ضرورة (bayan darurah): A type of bayan that occurs through means not originally appointed for expression, such as silence or contextual indication, due to necessity.
سكوت (sukut): Remaining silent; in this context, it is considered a form of bayan (communicating a legal ruling) when it occurs in a situation indicating approval.
دلالة حال المتكلم (dalalat hal al-mutakallim): The indication derived from the circumstance or state of the speaker (or silent person) that communicates a legal meaning, as opposed to explicit verbal communication.

Prophetic Silence as a Source of Law: Conditions and Application
Explaining how prophetic and companion silence serves as bayan darurah with conditions and a historical application.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the conditions under which prophetic silence (sukut) indicates permissibility (ibahah) and apply them to historical and contemporary cases.

Core Idea
The Prophet's silence when witnessing actions, combined with contextual indicators, establishes legal rulings through taqrir.

This lesson continues the study of bayan darurah by focusing on the silence of the Prophet (peace be upon him) and the Companions as a source of legal rulings. The passage explains that silence can be considered a form of 'speech' through the tongue of the state (dalalat hal al-mutakallim). It then lays out the conditions under which such silence carries legal weight, and provides a historical case from the time of Caliph 'Umar to illustrate the principles in action.

Guided Path
1
Understand the concept of 'dalalat hal al-mutakallim'
The phrase refers to the indication derived from the state or circumstance of a silent person. Even if no words are spoken, the context can 'speak' to convey a legal meaning. In Islamic law, this is primarily exemplified by the Prophet's silence (taqrir) when witnessing practices without objection.
The commentary clarifies that the silent person is a 'speaker' through the tongue of the state (لسان الحال), not through verbal articulation.
2
Identify the conditions for silence to indicate permissibility
The text specifies three main conditions: (1) The silent person must have the ability to object (إذا كان قادرًا على الإنكار). (2) The act must be done by a Muslim (كون الفاعل مسلمًا) – silence regarding the actions of non-Muslims does not imply permission. (3) There must be no prior explicit prohibition (لا يوجد قول دال على كونه حرامًا) – if there is, the silence may be interpreted as abrogation instead.
These conditions ensure that silence is only invoked when it indeed signifies approval, not apathy or inability.
3
Analyze the historical case of the runaway slave woman
The passage recounts a case where Caliph 'Umar ruled that the master takes back the slave woman but remained silent about compensating for the usufruct (services and intercourse). This silence, given the circumstances (ability to object, Muslim parties, no prior prohibition), indicated that no compensation was due – a ruling derived from the contextual indication.
This case shows how bayan darurah operates in companion-level practice, not just with the Prophet.
4
Address the objection: why call a silent person a 'speaker'?
The commentator explains that describing the silent person as a speaker is metaphorical: he speaks through his state (حال), not through words. This resolves the apparent contradiction and validates using state-indication as evidence.
This is a delicate point in usul: reconciling the act of silence with the concept of communication.
5
Apply the conditions to contemporary scenarios
When a mufti or scholar is faced with a new practice and does not voice an objection (assuming he is able to and the doer is Muslim), his silence can be taken as a form of bayan darurah supporting permissibility, provided no prior prohibition exists. This is especially relevant for emerging financial transactions and social customs.
The condition that the silent person must be able to object prevents misuse when objections are impossible or risky.

Commentary
This passage is from a chapter on the divisions of bayan (legal communication). It defines bayan darurah (bayan of necessity) as a type of bayan that occurs through means not originally designated for expression, such as silence. The author explains that normally bayan is through speech, but when necessity arises, silence can function as a bayan. The commentary then provides an example from the Quran: in the inheritance verse (4:11), the explicit statement that the mother gets a third implicitly indicates that the father gets the remainder. This shows how the explicit part (mantuq) can clarify the tacit part (maskut). The commentary also discusses a disagreement regarding the nature of conditions in legal statements, referencing Fakhr al-Islam and Shams al-A'imma, but the focus remains on the concept of bayan darurah. Next, the passage explains that bayan darurah can also be proven by the 'indication of the state of the speaker' (dalalat hal al-mutakallim). This is exemplified by the silence of the Prophet (peace be upon him) when he witnessed practices like mudaraba or sales without objection; his silence stands in place of a command indicating permissibility. Similarly, the silence of the Companions, under specific conditions (ability to object and the doer being Muslim), also serves as bayan. The passage further illustrates this with the case of a runaway slave woman who married and had children. Caliph 'Umar ruled in favor of the master regarding the woman but kept silent about compensating for the usufruct (services and intercourse) of the woman and children. This silence, given the context, indicated that no compensation was due. The commentary addresses objections: how can silence be considered 'speech'? It clarifies that the silent person is a 'speaker' through the tongue of the state (tongue of circumstance), not through verbal articulation. The passage also discusses conditions: silence only indicates permissibility if there is no prior statement declaring prohibition; if there was, the silence would be interpreted as abrogation. Also, the doer must be a Muslim; silence regarding the actions of a disbeliever (e.g., eating pork) does not imply permission. Overall, the lesson establishes that under necessity and contextual indications, silence can be a valid form of legal communication (bayan darurah), carrying the weight of a ruling.

Conjunction as Clarification (Bayan) in Hanafi Usul
Linguistic analysis of conjunctive phrase clarifying ambiguous debt amount

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school considers conjunction (ataf) as a contextual clue that clarifies an ambiguous plurality (mujmal), and distinguish when this clarification applies.

Core Idea
Conjunction can clarify an ambiguous term when both items share the same genus and frequent usage supports it.

In this passage, the author explores how a simple conjunction like 'and' can resolve ambiguity in a debt acknowledgment. When a debtor says 'I owe you a hundred and a dirham,' does the conjunction tell us the hundred is also dirhams? The Hanafi school says yes, based on common usage, while the Shafi'i school says no, requiring the speaker's interpretation. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ambiguous term
The word 'hundred' (mi'a) is mujmal (ambiguous) because it does not specify the type of units.
In usul al-fiqh, mujmal requires bayan (clarification) to become mubayyan.
2
Recognize the conjunction as a contextual clue
The word 'and a dirham' (wadiham) is a conjunction that introduces a second item. The question is whether this conjunction can serve as a qarīna (contextual clue) to specify the genus of the first item.
Hanafis accept qarā'in (contextual clues) as a valid form of bayan.
3
Apply the Hanafi distinction
If the conjoined item is of the same genus (e.g., dirhams) and belongs to a category commonly transacted (measured/weighed), frequent usage supports the understanding that the first item is of the same type.
If the second item is of a different genus (e.g., a garment), the conjunction does not clarify because the usage is not frequent for such combinations.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i view
Shafi'is hold that a mujmal can only be clarified by explicit statement (al-bayan bi al-qawl), not by mere conjunction or contextual clues.
Their default is that the speaker's interpretation is needed in all cases.
5
Determine the practical consequence
In Hanafi, the debt is 101 dirhams; in Shafi'i, the phrase remains ambiguous and the debtor's interpretation is accepted (though he cannot claim less than 2 dirhams).
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (fruit of the disagreement).

Commentary
This passage analyzes the linguistic device of conjunction (عطف) as a form of Bayan (clarification) in debt acknowledgments. The Hanafi position is that when a person says 'I owe one hundred and a dirham', the conjunction clarifies that the hundred is also dirhams, because both are of the same genus (dirhams) and common usage deems the first item to be of the same type as the second. This applies to items typically measured or weighed in transactions. However, if the second item is of a different genus, like a garment, the conjunction does not serve as clarification because the hundred could be dirhams or something else; the statement becomes ambiguous and the speaker's intention must be sought. Al-Shafi'i disagreed, holding that the speaker's interpretation is always sought, even when both items are of the same genus. The Hanafi justification rests on frequent usage (كثرة الاستعمال) providing a contextual clue (قرينة) for clarification, while the Shafi'i view considers the conjunction insufficient for such clarification.

Key Terms
بيان (bayan): Clarification of an ambiguous expression through contextual or explicit means.
عطف (ataf): Conjunction linking two nouns, potentially serving as bayan when the second specifies the genus of the first.
تمييز (tamyiz): Specifying the unit or type of a vague quantity.
