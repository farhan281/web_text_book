Valuation of Fungible Goods When Like is Unavailable
States Abu Hanifa's ruling on value assessment for mithli when like is unavailable

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on valuation of fungible goods (mithli) and distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and Abu Yusuf's positions based on the underlying usul principle.

Core Idea
For fungible goods, compensation is assessed at litigation day, not at usurpation, because priority is given to the possibility of obtaining the actual like.

This passage addresses a specific ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school regarding the timing of valuation for fungible goods when the like becomes unavailable. Understanding this dispute reveals how Hanafi usul prioritizes actual restitution (al-mithl al-suri) over monetary compensation (al-mithl al-ma'nawi), and how the possibility of performance affects the determination of damages.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal scenario
Understand the case: a person usurps a fungible good (mithli) and later the like becomes unavailable. The question is what value to use for compensation.
2
State Abu Hanifa's position
Abu Hanifa holds that compensation is based on the value on the day of litigation (yawm al-khusuma), not at the time of usurpation.
This is the mufaa bihi in the text.
3
Analyse the rationale (ta'lil)
The reason is that before litigation, there is a possibility that the usurper could obtain the actual like (al-mithl al-suri). Since actual like takes precedence over monetary compensation, valuation is deferred until that possibility is extinguished at litigation.
4
Contrast with Abu Yusuf's view
Abu Yusuf argues that when the like becomes unavailable, the good is attached to value from that moment, so valuation should be at the day of usurpation.
His reasoning is cut off in the source but implies immediate conversion to value.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The difference affects the amount of compensation: if prices rise or fall between usurpation and litigation, the two methods yield different outcomes.
The text supports Abu Hanifa's view as the relied-upon one.

Core Idea
Complete capacity means the obligation is easy from the start, not that the person is already equipped.

This lesson introduces the second type of legal capacity (al-qudra al-muyassara) and explains why prayer and Hajj are treated differently regarding the assumption of means. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping how Hanafi usul balances obligation with ease.

The Scope of 'Mamluk': Full vs. Partial Ownership
Example of qarina for preferring majaz over haqiqah based on completeness of a concept

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a word is interpreted according to its complete meaning when a contextual indicator (qarina) points to that completeness, and derive which slaves are included or excluded in a manumission oath.

Core Idea
The word 'mamluk' in an oath applies only to slaves with full ownership (yad and raqaba), excluding the mukatab.

This passage from a classical usul text uses a concrete example to illustrate how the completeness of a concept determines the scope of a legal term. In the oath 'every mamluk of mine is free', the term 'mamluk' is interpreted according to its full meaning of complete ownership. By understanding the two components of ownership (yad and raqaba), you will see why a mukatab is excluded while a mudabbir and umm walad are included. This lesson trains you to identify qara'in that narrow the application of general terms.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal statement
The example is a man's declaration: 'Every mamluk of mine is free.' This is an oath or a declaration of manumission. The key term is 'mamluk'.
2
Define the components of 'mamluk'
Complete ownership ('mamluk kamil') requires two elements: yad (possession/control over the slave's labor) and raqaba (ownership of the slave's person). The passage states that the full meaning includes both.
Yad = hand, here meaning the master's control over the slave's handiwork. Raqaba = neck, the slave's personhood.
3
Classify the types of slaves
Identify the three categories: mudabbir (promised freedom upon master's death), umm walad (mother of master's child), and mukatab (slave with kitaba contract).
Mudabbir and umm walad have full ownership in both respects; mukatab has a deficiency.
4
Apply the completeness test
Mudabbir and umm walad are fully owned in both yad and raqaba, so they are included. Mukatab is owned in raqaba (still a slave) but free in yad (can work for himself), so he is deficient. Therefore the word 'mamluk' in its complete sense does not apply to him.
The qarina (context) is the completeness of ownership; the word is used majaz (metaphorically) for the complete meaning.
5
Derive the ruling
The ruling: The declaration frees the mudabbir and umm walad but not the mukatab. The term 'mamluk' is interpreted with a qarina that restricts it to those with full mamlukiyyah.

Commentary
This passage is an example from the discussion of qara'in (indicators) for preferring metaphorical interpretation over literal. The word 'mamluk' is used in the context of a man freeing all his slaves. Literally, 'mamluk' means someone owned fully, i.e., the owner has full control over the slave's labor (yad) and the slave's person (raqaba). A mudabbir (a slave promised freedom upon the master's death) and an umm walad (a slave mother of her master's child) are fully owned in both respects, so they are included in the oath. However, a mukatab (a slave who has contracted to buy his freedom) is owned in raqaba (he is still a slave) but not in yad (he is free to work and earn to pay the kitaba), so he is deficient in mamlukiyyah. Therefore, the word 'mamluk' in its complete sense does not apply to him, and he is excluded from the ruling. This shows how legal reasoning uses the completeness or deficiency of a concept to determine inclusion or exclusion.

Linguistic Deficiency and the Scope of Words: Fruit vs. Grapes
Example of qarina where a word's deficiency prevents inclusion of a fuller item

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a word placed for a deficient meaning excludes items with additional completeness, and distinguish it from the case of dalalah al-nass.

Core Idea
A word meaning deficiency excludes what has completeness; a word meaning completeness can include what lacks it via textual implication.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar presents a key example of how qara'in (linguistic indications) determine the scope of a word in Hanafi usul. The ruling: if someone swears not to eat 'fruit' (fakihah), it does not include grapes or dates because the word is placed for luxury (deficient) while grapes have a sustaining completeness. The contrast with the inclusion of a pickpocket under 'thief' by dalalah al-nass illustrates the subtlety of textual reasoning.

The ruling is that grapes are not included, unlike the earlier case where 'mamluk' (complete ownership) included the deficient mukatab.
2
Understand the linguistic classification of 'fakihah'
The passage states that 'fakihah' is placed for deficiency (nuqsan): it refers to something consumed for luxury beyond bodily sustenance. Identify the locus of this classification in the Arabic text.

The complete is not included in the deficient, even though both are fruits in common usage.

This principle is used in Hanafi usul for interpreting oaths and other linguistic scopes.

The Ba and Its Legal Implications in Contracts
Definition of ba as ilṣāq and its application in sale and salam contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that the letter ba indicates adhesion (ilṣāq) to determine the price and sold item in sale and salam contracts, and distinguish the resulting rulings on substitution before possession.

Core Idea
The ba in contracts has a literal meaning of adhesion; its object is always the price in a sale.

This passage explores the linguistic foundation of the letter ba in Hanafi usul. The author establishes that the primary meaning of ba is ilṣāq (adhesion), and all other uses are metaphorical. He then applies this to two contractual scenarios: a spot sale and a salam contract, showing how the same phrase structure yields different rulings because of the function of ba.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning of ba
The passage states 'al-bā'u lil-ilṣāq' — the ba is for adhesion. Understand that in Arabic, the ba originally indicates attachment. All other uses are metaphorical extensions (majāz).
This is a foundational usul principle: lafdhī haqīqah in Hanafi theory.
2
Classify the ba in contract phrases as haqīqah
In phrases like 'ishtaraytu... bi...', the ba is used in its literal sense (haqīqah). Therefore, the noun it enters upon is the thing adhered to, which in a sale context is the price (thaman).
This classification determines the legal effect.
3
Apply to the sale example
In 'ishtaraytu minka hādhā al-'abda bikurri hinta jayyidin', the ba enters upon 'kurr' (measure of wheat). Thus, the kurr is the price, and the slave is the sold item. Substitution of the kurr (price) before possession is allowed.
The ruling: substitution in the price before qabd is permissible.
4
Apply to the salam example and contrast
If the phrase is 'ishtaraytu minka kurra hinta bihādhā al-'abdi', the ba enters upon 'hādhā al-'abdi' (the slave). Thus, the slave is the price (ra's al-māl), and the kurr becomes the salam commodity (muslam fīh). The kurr is unspecified, so the contract is salam, not sale. Substitution before possession is not allowed.
The same verb form yields different contract types based on ba placement.
5
Note the seven conditions of salam
The passage lists the conditions for validity of salam per Imam Abu Hanifa: known genus, type, quality, quantity, term, capital amount, and place of delivery. These are required because the muslam fīh is a debt (dayn).
The absence of these conditions invalidates the salam contract.

Commentary
This passage is a Sharh (expansion) on the meaning of the letter 'ba' (ب) in Arabic grammar as used in legal contexts. The author states that the primary meaning of 'ba' is adhesion (ilṣāq): what enters after it is the thing adhered to. All other uses are metaphorical extensions. The key legal application discussed is in sales contracts. When the ba enters upon the price (thaman), it indicates that the object of the preposition is the price. For example, in the phrase 'I bought this slave for a kurr of wheat,' the kurr is the price, so the slave is the sold item (mabīʿ). This makes the sale a spot sale (ḥālan), and substitution of the price before possession (qabḍ) is permissible. In contrast, if the ba enters upon the sold item in a salam contract (e.g., 'I bought a kurr of wheat for this slave'), the structure is reversed: the slave becomes the price (raʾs al-māl) and the kurr becomes the salam commodity (muslam fīh). The kurr is unspecified, so the contract is a salam, which requires strict conditions (like known genus, type, quantity, term, etc.) and substitution before possession is not allowed. The commentary elaborates on these two scenarios and lists the seven conditions of salam according to Imam Abu Hanifa.

الثمن (al-thaman): The price in a sale; in the example, the kurr of wheat that is the object of ba.
السلم (al-salam): A forward contract where the price is paid in advance for deferred delivery of a specified commodity.

The Particle 'fī': Inclusion and Omission in Temporal Contexts
Analyzing 'fī' for its meaning as container and its omission in temporal expressions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle regarding the omission of 'fī' in temporal expressions and apply it to derive rulings in oaths.

Core Idea
Omitting 'fī' from a time expression does not change its legal scope; both are equivalent containers.

This lesson examines the Hanafi treatment of the particle 'fī' in time expressions. While all agree 'fī' denotes containment, the key question is whether dropping it alters whether the time is a measure or surplus. This distinction has direct implications for oaths and other legal time bounds. We will explore the disagreement and the preferred view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original meaning of 'fī'
The passage states that 'fī' is for container/containment (ẓarfiyyah) in place or time. This is the agreed-upon original meaning in language.
This is the starting point for the usul analysis.
2
State the point of disagreement
The Hanafis differed on whether omitting or affirming 'fī' affects whether the time is a measure (miʿyār) that exactly encompasses what precedes, or a surplus container (fāḍil).
The disagreement is about the legal implication, not permissibility of omission.
3
Present the position of the two disciples
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that omission and affirmation are equivalent; both encompass all that follows. The meaning of 'ghadan' (tomorrow) is the same as 'fī ghadin' (in tomorrow).
They consider dropping 'fī' as mere abbreviation.
4
Understand the proof and application to oaths
The proof is linguistic: the meaning is identical. In oaths, the 'absolute' (muṭlaq) does not imply perpetuity, so a limit (ghāyah) is not included. The two disciples' view (non-inclusion of the limit) corresponds to the apparent narration.
The practical result: doubt in the limit area leads to caution (shubhah).
5
Derive the ruling for a specific oath
For an oath like 'I will not speak to Zaid until Rajab', according to the two disciples, Rajab is not included in the prohibition. Speaking in Rajab does not break the oath (or at least no expiation due to doubt).
This shows how the general rule on 'fī' omission translates into a concrete fiqh ruling.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The absolute is carried to the qualified only when both share the same cause and ruling; otherwise, it remains independent.

This lesson explores a key difference between Hanafi and Shafi'i usul: when an absolute (mutlaq) term in one verse is carried onto a qualified (muqayyad) term in another verse. The Hanafi position restricts this to cases where the cause (sabab) and ruling (hukm) are the same, while the Shafi'i school applies it more broadly. We will examine five possible scenarios and the rulings in each, drawing on Quranic examples.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion of invalid aspects in reasoning, specifically addressing the rule that the absolute (mutlaq) is carried onto the qualified (muqayyad). The Hanafi view is that this rule applies only under certain conditions. The text outlines five possible scenarios based on whether the cause (sabab) and ruling (hukm) are the same or different, and whether the incident (haditha) is one or more. (1) Same cause and same ruling: Hanafis differ — some say the absolute is carried, most say it is not. (2) Same cause but different rulings: unanimously the absolute is not carried. (3) Different causes but same ruling: unanimously the absolute is carried (e.g., three days fasting in hajj qualified by 'consecutive' in Ibn Mas'ud's reading). (4) Different causes and different rulings: Hanafis say no, Shafi'is say yes (e.g., the absolute 'freeing a neck' in zihar and the qualified 'believing neck' in intentional killing — Shafi'is carry the qualification). (5) Same cause, same ruling, but different incidents: Hanafis agree not to carry, but some Shafi'is carry. The text provides examples from the Quran, such as the verses on zihar and intentional killing, to illustrate the disagreement. The passage also includes extensive commentary clarifying definitions and responding to potential objections.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A term that refers to the essence without any qualifying attribute.
مقيد (muqayyad): A term that refers to the essence with an additional attribute or condition.
حمل (haml): Carrying the meaning of an absolute text to that of a qualified text, making the absolute subject to the qualification.

The Five Divisions of Mutlaq and Muqayyad and the Hanafi Position on Haml
Analyzing scenarios where mutlaq and muqayyad occur and rulings on carrying restriction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the five divisions of mutlaq and muqayyad based on unity/difference of ruling and incident, and apply the Hanafi rule that carrying the restriction is obligatory only when both ruling and incident are identical, and distinguish when qiyas is required.

Core Idea
Carrying a restriction from muqayyad to mutlaq is obligatory by consensus only when the ruling and incident are the same; otherwise, Hanafis require qiyas or reject it.

The relationship between absolute (mutlaq) and qualified (muqayyad) expressions is a critical topic in usul al-fiqh. Scholars divided possible scenarios into five categories based on whether the ruling and incident are the same or different. This lesson presents these divisions and explains the Hanafi position that obligatory carrying (haml) is limited to one category, while Shafi'is extend it to others. Understanding this dispute is essential for deriving rulings from Quranic texts.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the five divisions
Identify the three variables: ruling (hukm) same/different, incident (hadithah) same/different, and whether a condition is mentioned for part of a composite ruling. Map out the five combinatorial possibilities.
The passage divides: 1) same ruling, same incident, condition only on part; 2) same ruling, same incident, condition on all; 3) different rulings, different incidents; 4) different rulings, same incident; 5) same ruling, different incidents.
2
Locate consensus and disagreement
Determine which divisions have consensus (ijma') and which have ikhtilaf. Division 2 (same ruling, same incident) mandates carrying by consensus. Division 3 (different ruling, different incident) does not require carrying by consensus. Divisions 1, 4, and 5 are disputed.
Within Hanafis, division 1 has internal ikhtilaf: some say obligatory, most say impossible.
3
Understand the Shafi'i reasoning
Study how Shafi'is carry restrictions across incidents. They use two methods: (a) analogy (qiyas) because all expiations share the genus of expiation; (b) a linguistic principle that omitting a restriction in one place relies on its mention elsewhere.
The Shafi'i position extends the condition of 'believing slave' from killing expiation (4:92) to oath expiation (5:89).
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis object that the linguistic principle is insufficient without evidence (qarinah). Analogical carrying requires a shared effective cause ('illah). They argue that the Legislator's omission of a restriction indicates its intentional absence.
The objection: if you carry the belief condition, why not also carry the feeding of ten poor persons from oath expiation to killing expiation?
5
Apply the practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf)
Identify real-world fiqh consequences. For zihar expiation, Hanafis do not require the condition 'before touching' for the feeding option; Shafi'is do. For oath expiation, Hanafis do not require a believing slave; Shafi'is do.
The dispute directly affects the validity of expiation acts.

Carrying Mutlaq to Muqayyad: The Distinction Between Wasf and Ism 'Alam

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An attribute negates the ruling when absent; a proper noun only affirms it when present.

This lesson delves into a critical usul dispute: when a text contains a qualification (e.g., 'believing slave'), does that qualification automatically apply to other similar texts? The Hanafis distinguish between qualifications that are attributes (wasf) and those that are proper nouns (ism 'alam). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly deriving rulings across different cases.

1

Carrying the Absolute to the Restricted Across Rulings: Hanafi Rule
Explains why the qayd of one ruling does not transfer to another based on ism 'alam vs wasf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that the absolute is not carried to the restricted across different incidents or rulings, and identify when carrying is necessary based on the type of basis (ism 'alam vs wasf).

Core Idea
A restriction based on a proper noun does not travel; only a description can negate a ruling when absent.

This passage addresses a key usul dispute: when does a restriction in one ruling transfer to another? The Hanafi school maintains that the absolute and restricted are independently intended in different contexts. Only when they share the same ruling and same incident must the absolute yield to the restricted. The passage also introduces the crucial distinction between 'ism 'alam' (proper noun) and 'wasf' (description) to explain why the restriction of food in oath expiation does not apply to killing.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
The objection is: why does the Hanafi school not apply the restriction of 'food' (ta'am) from expiation of oath to expiation of killing, when both involve feeding the poor?
The objector assumes a similarity that warrants transferring the restriction.
2
Understand the Hanafi response based on ism 'alam
The phrase 'ten poor persons' is a proper noun (ism 'alam). Proper nouns only establish the existence of a ruling when the term is present; they do not negate the ruling when absent. Thus, the absence of feeding in killing does not negate the ruling of killing's expiation.
Contrast with wasf: a description would negate the ruling if absent.
3
Distinguish ism 'alam from wasf
Ism 'alam (proper noun) only proves existence (wujud), not negation ('adam). Wasf (description) can prove both existence and negation, according to both Hanafis and Shafi'is. This distinction is the core of the argument.
The concept of mafhum al-laqab (concept of title) is rejected by both schools, but the Shafi'i school sometimes uses it.
4
Apply the general rule of haml al-mutlaq 'ala al-muqayyad
Hanafis rule that the absolute is carried to the restricted only when both occur in the same ruling (hukm) and same incident (haditha). If they are in different incidents or different rulings, both are applied independently because each is intentionally expansive or restrictive for its context.
The exception is when they must be combined to avoid contradiction, as in the case of fasting three days (mutlaq) and three consecutive days (muqayyad) for the same expiation.
5
Analyze the Shafi'i position and Hanafi critique
The Shafi'i school generally carries the absolute to the restricted even across incidents if the ruling is the same (e.g., fasting in expiations). However, they do not apply the condition of continuity (tawatur) from the reading of Ibn Mas'ud to oath expiation, which is inconsistent. The Hanafi critique uses this inconsistency to support their own rule.
The passage also notes that the Shafi'i position on feeding in killing is a weak narration; the stronger view is that feeding is not required.

So when it does not necessitate negation in the original (which is expiation of the oath), how can it be transferred to the branch (which is expiation of killing), unlike the description (wasf) which necessitates negation when it is absent according to his principle as we have established. And the restriction of food in the oath is because the food in zihar (which is feeding sixty poor persons) is established in killing in a narration from al-Shafi'i as it is said. And according to us, the absolute is not carried to the restricted even if they are in one incident due to possibility of operation.' Explanation of the answer: 'The food considered in expiation of the oath is not established in expiation of killing; because the difference (i.e., between expiation of killing and expiation of the oath) is established by a proper noun, which is the word 'feeding' or 'ten poor persons', and it only necessitates existence of the ruling in the text when it exists, and the ruling is not negated by its absence; so the absence of feeding ten poor persons does not necessitate the absence of expiation of the oath; so it did not necessitate negation of the ruling in the original text (expiation of the oath), so how can this negation be transferred to the branch (expiation of killing)? Commentary
This is a contested usul discussion about whether a restriction (qayd) from one legal ruling transfers to another ruling that shares a similar component. The passage addresses an objection: why does the Hanafi school not apply the restriction of 'food' (ta'am) found in the expiation of an oath (kafarat al-yamin) to the expiation of killing (kafarat al-qatl)? The author explains that the difference lies in the type of basis: the name 'ten poor persons' is a proper noun (ism 'alam) which only establishes existence, not negation. Since it does not negate the ruling in the original case (oath), it cannot transfer a negation to the branch (killing). In contrast, a description (wasf) would carry negation. The passage then elaborates on the general principle of carrying the absolute (mutlaq) to the restricted (muqayyad). According to Hanafis, the absolute is not carried to the restricted across different incidents or different rulings because both are independently intended; only when they share a single ruling and single incident is carrying necessary due to impossibility of combining contradictories. The passage also discusses the Shafi'i position, noting that al-Shafi'i does not accept the concept of 'mafhum al-laqab' and that his school generally carries the absolute to the restricted even across rulings. However, al-Shafi'i's own example regarding the fasting in oath expiation shows inconsistency: he does not impose the condition of continuity (tawatur) from the non-mutawatir reading of Ibn Mas'ud. The commentary from al-Tanwir criticizes the objection as weak, because the text explicitly specifies freeing a slave or fasting two months in kafarat al-qatl, so imposing food would nullify the text.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A word that indicates the essence of something without any additional restriction (qayd). Here, it refers to the word 'three days' without the condition of consecutiveness.
مقيد (muqayyad): A word that indicates the essence of something with an additional restriction. Here, it refers to 'three consecutive days'.
اسم العلم (ism al-'alam): A proper noun or a specific name. In this context, 'ten poor persons' is considered ism 'alam because it is a fixed expression that does not negate by its absence.
الوصف (al-wasf): A description or attribute that can necessitate the ruling. In contrast to ism 'alam, a wasf can necessitate negation of the ruling when absent.
حمل المطلق على المقيد (haml al-mutlaq 'ala al-muqayyad): The legal principle of interpreting an absolute term in light of a restricted term to achieve consistency. The passage explains when this applies and when it does not.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi usul, the absolute is carried to the restricted only when they share the same ruling and the same incident; otherwise, each is applied independently, especially when the restriction is based on a proper noun (ism 'alam) that does not carry negation.
