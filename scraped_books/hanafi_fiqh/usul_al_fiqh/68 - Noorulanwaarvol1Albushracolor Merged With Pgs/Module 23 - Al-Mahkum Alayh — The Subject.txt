Qualifications of the Consensus Body (Ahl al-Ijma)
Defines who qualifies as a member of the consensus body in Hanafi usul.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a person to be counted among ahl al-ijma in Hanafi usul and explain the exception for clear texts.

Core Idea
Consensus requires mujtahids who are righteous and free from bid'ah — a muqallid's opinion carries no weight.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar defines who qualifies as a member of the consensus body (ahl al-ijma). Understanding these qualifications is crucial because only the agreement of qualified scholars constitutes binding proof (hujja). The author specifies that a mujtahid must be righteous (salih) and free from heretical desires (hawa) and sin (fisq), but in matters where ijtihad is not needed, even non-mujtahids may be considered. We will dissect these conditions and their implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and legal significance
The term 'ahl al-ijma' refers to those whose agreement constitutes binding consensus. Recognize that consensus is a primary source of law in Hanafi usul.
2
List the explicit conditions
The text states: must be mujtahid, righteous (salih), free from hawa (heretical desire) and fisq (sin). Note each condition and understand what they entail.
The condition of ijtihad is primary; muqallids are excluded.
3
Understand the exception
In matters that require no ijtihad—such as texts with clear rulings—the condition of being a mujtahid is waived. In such cases, any Muslim's agreement may be considered.
This exception prevents the impossibility of requiring ijtihad for self-evident rulings.
4
Analyze who is excluded
Muqallids (followers), innovators (ahl al-hawa), and sinful persons (fasiq) are not part of ahl al-ijma. Their opinions are not considered in forming consensus.
Exclusion is based on lack of qualification (mujtahid) or lack of moral uprightness.
5
Reflect on the epistemological weight
The consensus of qualified scholars is a hujja (binding proof). The strict conditions ensure that consensus is reliable and authoritative.
Hanafi scholars emphasize that ijma is only valid when formed by those who meet these conditions.

Commentary
This passage specifies the conditions for those who constitute consensus (ahl al-ijma). They must be mujtahids (qualified legal scholars) and righteous (salih), free from heretical desires (hawa) and sin (fisq). However, in matters where ijtihad is not needed (such as texts with clear rulings), even a non-mujtahid's silence may count. The text clarifies that the qualification of being a mujtahid excludes muqallids (followers) from participation in consensus; only mujtahids have a voice. Additionally, a person with heretical desires or a sinful person is not considered because their opinion is blameworthy and consensus is based on honor. The passage also revisits the Ibn Abbas incident to argue that even if he later claimed he remained silent out of awe, the narration is not reliable. It cites Sahih al-Bukhari to show that Umar actually honored Ibn Abbas and consulted him, making awe unlikely. Finally, it adds another anecdote from Umar encouraging people to speak truth, reinforcing the principle that silence cannot be excused by awe.

Key Terms
مجتهد (mujtahid): A scholar who has the ability to derive rulings directly from primary sources (Quran and Sunnah) through ijtihad.
هوى (hawa): Heretical desire or innovation in religion (bid'ah) that leads a scholar to deviate from the established methodology.
