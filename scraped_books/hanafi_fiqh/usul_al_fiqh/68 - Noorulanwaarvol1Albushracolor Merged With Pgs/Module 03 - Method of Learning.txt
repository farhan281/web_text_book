The Definition of Fiqh: Language and Technical Terms
Defining the term fiqh linguistically and in juristic terminology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and technical definitions of fiqh and explain each component of the technical definition.

Core Idea
Fiqh is knowledge of subsidiary legal rulings acquired through reasoning, not revelation or opinion.

This lesson anchors a foundational term in Islamic jurisprudence: fiqh. Before diving into rulings, every student of usul must know precisely what fiqh means—linguistically as 'understanding' and technically as a specialized discipline. The Hanafi definition carves out the exact boundaries of the science.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning
Recognize that fiqh in Arabic means understanding (fahm). The Quranic verse (11:91) is cited as evidence.
The verse is from Surah Hud, where the people say 'We do not understand much of what you say.'
2
State the technical definition
The definition used by jurists: 'knowledge obtained of a body of subsidiary legal rulings through reflection and inference.'
Memorize this definition as it is the standard Hanafi definition.
3
Analyze each exclusionary phrase
The definition uses five key phrases: 'knowledge', 'a body', 'legal', 'subsidiary', 'through reflection and inference'. Each excludes a specific category.
'Knowledge' excludes assumption; 'a body' excludes one or two rulings; 'legal' excludes non-legal matters; 'subsidiary' excludes usul al-fiqh; 'through reflection' excludes divine knowledge.
4
Connect to usul al-fiqh
Recognize that this definition distinguishes fiqh from usul al-fiqh (which deals with evidence principles) and from mere opinion or divine knowledge.
Usul al-fiqh defines the tools; fiqh applies them.

Commentary
This is a definition passage. The author begins by giving the linguistic meaning of fiqh as 'understanding,' citing Quran 11:91. He then presents the technical definition used by the jurists (mutasharri‘in): 'knowledge obtained of a body of subsidiary legal rulings through reflection and inference.' He proceeds to break down each component of this definition to explain what it excludes. 'Knowledge' excludes mere assumption or opinion about rulings—though common people may call that fiqh, it does not qualify in the technical sense. 'A body of rulings' excludes knowing just one or two rulings. 'Legal' excludes non-legal matters like rationality or sensory perceptions. 'Subsidiary' excludes knowledge about the types of evidence themselves, which belongs to usul al-fiqh, not fiqh. Finally, 'through reflection and inference' excludes the knowledge of Allah, angels, and prophets, which comes through revelation, not human reasoning. Thus the definition precisely delimits the scope of fiqh as a human discipline of inferential legal knowledge.

Definition and Foundations of Usul al-Fiqh
Defining usul al-fiqh, its subject, goal, issues, derivation sources, and principles

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define usul al-fiqh, identify its subject, goal, issues, and list the three disciplines from which it derives.

Core Idea
Usul al-fiqh is the science of the evidences of fiqh, built upon kalam, Arabic, and fiqh.

Before we dive into specific proofs, we must understand what usul al-fiqh is as a science. This passage from Noor al-Anwar lays out its definition, subject, goal, issues, and the foundational disciplines it relies on. Think of it as the map of the science, showing its scope and prerequisites.

Guided Path
1
Define 'asl' and usul al-fiqh
The passage begins by explaining that 'asl' (root) is the foundation upon which something depends. So usul al-fiqh are the evidences (adillah) from which fiqh rulings are derived.
This is a linguistic and technical definition.
2
Identify the subject, goal, and issues
The text then specifies the subject (mawdu‘): the states of the evidences (their types, ranks, etc.). The goal (ghayah) is to attain knowledge of legal rulings for worldly and otherworldly happiness. The issues (masa’il) are precisely those states of the evidences.
These three elements define any Islamic science.
3
Explain the derivation sources (istimdad)
Usul al-fiqh derives from three disciplines: kalam (theology), Arabic (linguistics), and the legal rulings themselves (ahkam). Each is essential: kalam confirms the authority of revelation, Arabic interprets it, and knowledge of rulings enables proof and disproof.
This interdependence shows usul is not an isolated science.
4
Summarize the principles (mabadi')
The principles (mabadi') of usul are the same three disciplines: kalam, Arabic, and fiqh. The passage states that since usul derives from these, its principles are not outside them.
Thus the foundational tools of usul are these three.

Commentary
This passage outlines the structure of usul al-fiqh as a science. First, it defines 'asl' linguistically as the foundation upon which something relies, so usul al-fiqh are the evidences (adillah) from which fiqh rulings are derived. Then it specifies the subject matter (mawdu‘): the incidental states of the evidences that lead to rulings, their types, and hierarchical ranks. The goal (ghayah) is knowledge of legal rulings that bring happiness in this world and the next. The issues (masa’il) are precisely the states of those evidences. The sources from which usul al-fiqh derives (istimdad) are three: 1) ‘Ilm al-Kalam (theology), because confirming that the evidences yield rulings requires belief in Allah and prophethood; 2) Arabic linguistics, because interpreting the Quran and Sunnah depends on understanding language (literal, figurative, general, etc.); 3) Knowledge of legal rulings themselves (ahkam), because one must understand the nature of rulings before proving or disproving them through evidences. Finally, its principles (mabadi’) are exactly these three disciplines: kalam, Arabic, and fiqh. This standard Hanafi framework emphasizes the interdisciplinary nature of usul.

Defining the subject matter, aim, and sources of usul al-fiqh

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define usul al-fiqh by identifying its subject (mawdu'), aim (ghayah), problems (masa'il), sources (istimdad), and principles (mabadi) as outlined in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
The subject of usul al-fiqh is the evidences that lead to Sharia rulings, not the rulings themselves.

Every science has a defined subject, aim, and principles. Usul al-fiqh is no exception. This passage from Noor al-Anwar lays out the foundational definition that shapes how Hanafi scholars approach legal reasoning. Understanding this structure helps you see what usul al-fiqh actually studies and why it depends on other sciences.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the structure of a science definition
The author describes every science as having a subject (mawdu'), aim (ghayah), problems (masa'il), sources (istimdad), and principles (mabadi). Identify these five elements in the passage.
This taxonomy is standard in Islamic epistemology.

The subject is not the rulings themselves but the tools and proofs.

The aim connects usul to actual fiqh.

List the sources of derivation (istimdad)

These reflect the interdisciplinary nature of usul.

This concludes the definitional framework.

Commentary

The Four Sources and Their Definitions
Commentary on the four sources of fiqh and their definitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four sources of Islamic law in Hanafi usul and explain the scope of the Book and Sunnah as legal sources.

Core Idea
The four sources are the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, and Qiyas, but only the first three are primary.

This passage from Nur al-Anwar introduces the fundamental sources of Islamic jurisprudence. It clarifies that not all of the Quran and Sunnah are legal sources—only the verses and hadith that contain legal rulings. The consensus of the community (Ijma) is the third source, and Qiyas is the fourth, derived from the previous three.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four sources
The passage mentions the Book, Sunnah, Consensus, and Qiyas. Recognize these as the four usul al-fiqh.
2
Understand the scope of the Book
Only about 500 verses containing legal rulings are considered source; the rest are stories and parables.
This is a key Hanafi nuance.
3
Understand the scope of the Sunnah
Similarly, only about 3000 ahadith with legal import are included.
4
Define Ijma
Consensus of the mujtahids of the Muslim community on a legal ruling.
The hadith 'My community will not agree on an error' supports this.
5
Note the fourth source Qiyas
Qiyas is derived from the three sources, not independent.

Ijma: The Consensus of the Community
Definition and scope of Ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of Ijma and evaluate the different opinions on which groups constitute the consensus.

Core Idea
Ijma is the agreement of the mujtahids of the Muslim community, but its scope is contested among schools.

This passage delves into the third source, Ijma. It defines Ijma as the consensus of the mujtahids of the Ummah, supported by the hadith 'My community will not agree on an error'. However, Imam Malik and others restricted Ijma to specific groups. The Hanafi view is inclusive, accepting consensus of any era's mujtahids.

Guided Path
1
Define Ijma as per the passage
The passage defines Ijma as 'إجماع أمة محمد' but clarified as consensus of mujtahids. Understand that it is agreement on a legal ruling.
2
Read the supporting evidence
The hadith 'لا تجتمع أمتي على ضلالة' is cited. Note that it is a ahad hadith.
3
Identify the broad Hanafi position
Hanafis accept Ijma of any group of mujtahids, whether from Medina, Companions, or later generations.
4
Compare with opposing views
Imam Malik restricted to Medina, some to Companions, some to the Prophet's family. Understand the basis of their restriction.
5
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
A ruling agreed by mujtahids outside Medina would be Ijma for Hanafis but not for Malikis, affecting which rulings are considered binding.

Qiyas: The Fourth Source
Definition of Qiyas and its valid scope

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Qiyas in Hanafi usul and distinguish it from invalid forms of analogy.

Core Idea
Qiyas is a valid source only when derived from the three primary sources; mere similarity or rational reasoning does not constitute valid qiyas.

This passage defines the fourth source, Qiyas, as establishing a ruling for a new case due to a common effective cause ('illah) with a case from the primary sources. It explicitly excludes two types: al-qiyas al-shabahi (analogical qiyas based on similarity) and al-qiyas al-aqli (rational qiyas). This lesson unpacks the definition and the exclusions.

Guided Path
1
Define Qiyas
The passage defines Qiyas as 'أن يثبت حكم شيء في آخر بعلة مشتركة'. Understand the components: asl (original case), far' (new case), 'illah (cause), and hukm (ruling).
2
Identify the condition for valid Qiyas
The Qiyas must be derived from the three primary sources (Islam = Quran, Sunnah, Ijma). The phrase 'المستنبط من الإسلام' indicates this.
3
Examine the exclusion of al-qiyas al-shabahi
This is analogy based on mere similarity without a proper 'illah. Example: equating first sitting in prayer to last sitting based on similarity. Hanafis reject this.
The passage notes that attributing this to Imam Malik is an error; he considers first sitting as sunnah.
4
Examine the exclusion of al-qiyas al-aqli
Rational qiyas uses logical deduction unrelated to divine texts. Example: The world is changing, so it must have a creator. This is not a legal source.
5
Understand the purpose of inclusion
By excluding these two, the author ensures that only properly derived legal analogies are used as sources.

Qada by Rational Like: Complete and Deficient
Defines qada by mithl ma'qul into kamil and qasir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qada by mithl ma'qul, distinguish between its complete and deficient types, and explain why the formal like takes precedence over the meaning-like in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, qada by rational like is either complete (formal like) or deficient (value), and the formal like always takes precedence.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar defines the concept of 'qada by a rational like' (al-qada bi mithl ma'qul) and expands it into two types: complete and deficient. The author clarifies that both paying the actual like (e.g., wheat for wheat) and paying its value (qima) are forms of rational compensation, but the formal like is superior. The text also addresses a potential confusion from ibadat (prayer) and explains why the Hanafi school treats qada of prayer as complete even when performed individually. Understanding this distinction is essential for deriving rulings on compensation in muamalat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its components
Locate the phrase 'qada by mithl ma'qul'. Break it down: qada (compensation after loss), mithl (like), ma'qul (rational). Note that the text divides it into two: paying the actual like (mithl suri) and paying value (mithl ma'nawi).
The Arabic term 'mithl ma'qul' refers to any substitute that is rationally equivalent, either in form or meaning.
2

Identify that the formal like (mithl suri) is called 'kamil' (complete) because it matches in both form and meaning. The meaning-like (value) is 'qasir' (deficient) because it matches only in meaning, not form.
Example: paying wheat for wheat is complete; paying its cash value is deficient.
3

The text states: 'the formal like precedes the meaning-like. So as long as the formal like exists, one does not move to the meaning-like.' Understand that if the actual like is available in the market, the owner cannot be forced to accept value.
The rule ensures maximum compensation; the guarantor must provide the exact item if possible.
4

The text anticipates an objection: in prayer, qada in congregation seems complete, and individually deficient. But the Hanafi response clarifies that qada of the obligation is complete either way; the congregation is an attribute of excellence, not a requirement for completeness.
This shows that the division of qada into complete/deficient applies primarily to property (muamalat), not necessarily to ibadat.
5

The commentary explains that the primary divisions of qada are three (matching ada), but the additional example (complete/deficient mithl) is only to highlight that mithl ma'qul itself can be subdivided. This does not create a new primary division.
Thus, the three primary types of qada remain: by like, by value, and by service (the latter not discussed here).

Conditions for Fa Entering upon Causes and the Debate on Continuity
Discussing the conditions for 'fa' to enter upon causes and the dispute about continuity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and evaluate the three Hanafi usul positions on the function of 'fa' when it enters upon a cause, and apply the correct interpretation to legal examples.

Core Idea
Fa can indicate logical causation, not just temporal sequence – a key usul insight for interpreting causal language in legal texts.

In Arabic, the particle 'fa' typically indicates sequence. But when it appears before a cause, a logical tension arises: the cause should precede the effect, yet 'fa' suggests sequence after. How do Hanafi usulis resolve this? This lesson unpacks three approaches: the condition of continuity, the final cause theory, and the breakthrough view that 'fa' itself indicates causality.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic problem
Recognize that 'fa' is meant for sequence, but causes logically precede their effects. This creates a contradiction when 'fa' enters upon a cause. Ask: how can a cause be subsequent to the ruling it produces?
This is the core tension that all solutions attempt to resolve.
2
Analyse Fakhr al-Islam's continuity condition
Study the requirement that the cause be continuous (da'im) in existence both before and after the ruling. This way, the cause exists after the ruling as well, satisfying the sequence demanded by 'fa'.
Example: 'Rejoice, for help has come' – the coming of help is momentary but its essence endures.
3
Examine the final cause alternative
Consider al-Tawdih's proposal: the cause should be a final cause (gha'iyya), whose existence comes after the effect. This reverses the temporal order, allowing 'fa' to indicate sequence.
This approach avoids the need for continuity but introduces a different logical framework.
4
Evaluate Bahral Uloom's causality interpretation
Understand the revolutionary idea that 'fa' can indicate pure causality (illiyya) without any sequential implication. This dissolves the problem entirely: no continuity or finality required.
Bahral Uloom argues that 'fa' is shared between sequence and causality – a key usul principle.
5
Apply to the legal example 'Pay me and you are free'
Analyse the sentence using each position. Under continuity/final cause, the slave is free immediately (freedom exists perpetually). Under a conditional reading, freedom depends on payment. Bahral Uloom's causality view supports the immediate freedom interpretation without need for contrivance.
This demonstrates the practical impact of the usul analysis on actual legal rulings.

Identifying the Cause by Attribution to the Ruling
Defining how to identify the legal cause by attributing the ruling to it.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a cause is identified by the attribution of the ruling to it, and distinguish literal attribution from metaphorical attribution to conditions.

Core Idea
The cause of a ruling is known by direct attribution; attribution to a condition is merely metaphorical.

This lesson introduces a fundamental principle in Hanafi usul: how to identify the legal cause (sabab) of a ruling. The author explains that whenever a ruling is attributed to something, that something is the cause—this is the default linguistic principle. However, a common objection arises because rulings are sometimes attributed to conditions (shart), such as 'Zakat al-Fitr' or 'Hajj al-Islam'. The passage resolves this by showing that such attribution is metaphorical, not literal. You will learn to distinguish between literal cause and condition by examining how the ruling relates to each.

1
Identify the cause by attribution
The text states that the cause of a ruling is known by attributing the ruling to it and by its connection. If you say 'Zakat al-mal', the mal (wealth) is the cause. This is the default: the attributed thing is the cause, unless proven otherwise.
