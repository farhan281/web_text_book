Metaphorical Use of Ada and Qada in Intention
Discusses permissibility of using ada and qada interchangeably in intention

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the concept of legal metaphor (majaz shar'i) to the interchangeability of 'ada' and 'qada' in intention and evaluate the two scholarly positions on its basis.

Core Idea
Ada and qada can be used metaphorically in intention, enabling valid performance even when the intended term is mismatched.

In Hanafi usul, intention is an act of the heart, yet the terms we use in verbalizing intention matter. This passage explores whether it is valid to perform an act with the intention of the other category (e.g., intending qada while actually performing ada). The discussion delves into definitions of ada and qada, the role of legal metaphor, and objections resolved by later scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
Determine the central question: Is it permissible to perform an act with the intention of the other category (ada with niyyah of qada and vice versa)?
2
Understand the definitional objection
Realize that the definition of ada requires an act to be wajib, yet supererogatory acts are not wajib. This objection challenges the comprehensiveness of the definition.
The passage offers multiple solutions, including distinguishing real ada from broad usage.
3
Analyze the two scholarly positions
Compare the view of 'the greatest scholars' (confirmation of validity without deriving from verbal usage) with that of the author of Kashf al-Bazdawi (deduction allowing verbal mention of the opposite term in intention).
The preferred view is that it is confirmation, not deduction.
4
Apply the concept of legal metaphor
Recognize that the Quranic verse {And when you have completed (qadaytum) your rites} uses 'qada' to mean 'ada', demonstrating metaphorical usage. The shared element is delivering what is due.
This is a legal metaphor (majaz shar'i), not a literal one.
5
Evaluate the practical examples
Examine the examples: performing with intention of qada when time remains, and making up with intention of ada when time has passed. Both are valid due to the metaphorical interchangeability.

Commentary
This passage discusses the metaphorical interchangeability of the terms 'ada' and 'qada' in legal usage. The author argues that it is permissible to perform an act with the intention of the other because the words can be used metaphorically. The discussion includes a response to an objection that intention is a matter of the heart, not verbal substitution. Two scholarly positions are presented: one views this as confirmation of validity (without deriving from verbal usage), and the other (from Kashf al-Bazdawi) sees it as a deduction, allowing one to verbally mention the other term in intention. Examples are given, such as someone who mistakenly thinks time has ended and intends qada while performing in time, which is valid as ada. The passage also addresses the objection that the definition of ada does not cover supererogatory acts, offering multiple solutions: either the definition is for real ada, or the discussion is specific to acts that necessitate a command.

Key Terms
مجاز (majaz): metaphorical usage; one term used in place of another due to shared meaning
أداء (ada): performance of an act at its prescribed time, or fulfillment of an obligation; also used broadly for supererogatory acts
قضاء (qada): make-up of an obligatory act after its time; also used metaphorically for performance within time

The Conjunction 'Waw' in Marriage Acceptance: Hanafi Usul Analysis
Analysis of waw's role in acceptance of marriage of two sisters

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that the waw does not indicate simultaneity in acceptance of two sisters' marriages and apply the principle of sadr al-kalam's dependence on akhir.

Core Idea
The waw in 'I accept this and this' does not cause simultaneity; the beginning of speech is suspended pending an ending that changes the ruling.

This lesson analyzes a key Hanafi usul argument concerning the conjunction 'waw' in marriage acceptance. When a husband says 'I accept the marriage of this and this' for two sisters, both marriages are invalidated. An opponent might claim this is because waw indicates simultaneity, but the Hanafi school provides a deeper grammatical and legal reason: the beginning of the statement (sadr al-kalam) depends on its end (akhir) when the end contains a legal changer. This lesson unpacks that reasoning and its implications for usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
State the scenario and ruling
Read the passage: a husband accepts two sisters with 'أجزت نكاح هذه وهذه'. The ruling: both marriages are invalid. Why?
This is the core factual question.
2
Identify the opposing literalist view
A literalist argues that the waw indicates simultaneity (muqaranah), so both acceptances happen together. Since one cannot marry two sisters at the same instant, both are invalid. The Hanafi agrees with the result but rejects the cause.
The opponent's assumption is that waw inherently implies simultaneousness.
3
Present the Hanafi response: sadr al-kalam depends on akhir
The Hanafi answer: the invalidation is not from the waw but because the second acceptance changes the first from valid to invalid. This is analogous to a condition or exception that suspends the beginning of speech. The entire phrase becomes one unit.
Key terms: taghyir (change), mughtayyir (changer).
4
Identify the evidence for the changer
The changer is the legal prohibition of combining two sisters (Quran 4:23). Because accepting the second sister would necessitate combining them, the first acceptance is also voided.
The Quranic verse is the dalil for the legal changer.

Discussion of the waw: Explanation of the sections of the third division. If the master frees them with one word, saying 'I free them both', the marriage of neither is invalidated because there is no concurrence (jam') between a free woman and a slave woman. If he frees them with disconnected speech and the husband accepts both or one, the marriage of the first freed woman is valid, and the marriage of the second is invalidated and acceptance does not apply to her. This is if the two marriages are in one contract. As for if they are in two contracts: if the master of the two slave women is one, the ruling is as we mentioned; if there are two masters and the two slave women are freed successively, then both marriages are suspended. Whichever the husband accepts becomes valid; if he accepts both simultaneously, the marriage of the first freed woman is valid. And if someone marries a man to two sisters in two contracts without the husband's permission, and the news reaches him and he says, 'I accept the marriage of this one and this one', both are invalidated, just as if he accepted both together. If he accepts them separately, the second marriage is invalidated. 'Because there is no concurrence' etc., meaning neither at the time of the contract nor at the time of acceptance, so the contract is binding from the master's side because his right is forfeited by freeing. As for the husband, if he wishes he accepts both, if he wishes he accepts one specific one. This passage is a detailed Hanafi usul analysis of the grammatical conjunction 'waw' (واو) and its legal implications in marriage acceptance (ijazah). The text begins with a scenario where a master frees two slave women using either one word (connected) or separate words (disconnected), affecting the validity of the husband's subsequent acceptance. This is a classic Hanafi usul approach: giving priority to legal objectives and contextual reasoning over rigid grammatical literalism.

Three kinayah expressions yield raj'i divorce because the word talaq is implied through legal necessity or metaphor.

Guided Path

For 'I'dati' and 'Istabri'i', the Hanafi scholars apply iqtida': the command to observe iddah implies divorce because iddah is only required after divorce. Scholars of bayan (rhetoric) argue that 'anti ba'in' is not a proper kinayah because divorce is not a necessary concomitant of 'ba'in' linguistically. The Hanafi response is that the term 'ba'in' is understood in its shar'i sense when intended, and the exception still holds.

Commentary
This passage from Noor al-Anwar is a commentary on the definition and ruling of kinayah (metaphorical innuendo) in divorce. It begins by stating that the definition given is not without defect, and calls for reflection. The author then presents an exception: three specific expressions—'I'dati' (observe iddah), 'Istabri'i rahimaki' (ensure the purity of your womb), and 'Anti wahida' (you are one/alone)—are not ba'in (irrevocably divorced) like other kinayah expressions, but rather raj'i (revocable divorce). The commentary then engages with criticism from the perspective of bayan (rhetoric): according to scholars of rhetoric, kinayah requires transferring from the literal meaning to a necessary concomitant (lazim), not to the actual intended meaning. The author argues that 'ba'in' linguistically means 'separated', which is general, and cannot be transferred to the specific shar'i meaning of 'ba'in' divorce. Hence, 'anti ba'in' is not a proper kinayah by their definition. The text then justifies the exception by citing that the Prophet (ﷺ) told Sawdah bint Zam'ah 'I'dati' and then took her back, so it is a proof that these expressions are raj'i. The passage further discusses the concept of iqtida' (implied divorce) in detail, explaining that when a husband commands her to observe iddah, the command necessitates divorce as a precondition for iddah. For non-consummated marriage, no iddah, so no divorce by iqtida'. The second part of the passage deals with the third division, arguing that 'I'dati' is actually a metaphor (isti'ara) for 'be divorced' because the cause (talaq) is intended by mentioning the effect (iddah). This is permissible because the effect is specific to the cause. The same applies to 'Istabri'i rahimaki'. The commentary also refutes an objection that metaphorical usage requires same verb form. In summary, this passage clarifies the Hanafi position that three specific kinayah expressions yield raj'i divorce due to implied talaq, refining the general rule that kinayah yields ba'in divorce.

Key Terms
الكناية (al-kināyah): An expression that is not explicit but implies divorce through context or intent.
الرجعي (al-raj‘ī): Revocable divorce during the iddah period.
اقتضاء (iqtidā’): Legal implication: the command or statement necessarily implies the existence of something else (here, talaq) for validity.
مستعار (musta‘ār): Metaphorical usage: mentioning the effect (iddah) to denote the cause (talaq).

Defining Dalaalat al-Nass and Distinguishing It from Qiyas
Defining 'indication of the text' and refuting it being qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define Dalaalat al-Nass, identify its necessary (iltizami) meaning, and explain the Hanafi arguments that distinguish it from qiyas.

Core Idea
Dalaalat al-Nass is a definitive linguistic implication, not an analogical reasoning.

This lesson examines a foundational concept in Hanafi usul: Dalaalat al-Nass (indication of the text). The text defines it as the necessary meaning understood from the wording without ijtihad, and refutes the claim that it is a form of qiyas. We will analyze the definition, the four proofs, and the legal implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concept and its definition
Locate the term 'Dalaalat al-Nass' in the passage and note how it is defined: the necessary (iltizami) meaning derived linguistically, not through ijtihad.
2
Distinguish literal meaning from necessary meaning
Understand the example: the literal meaning of 'Do not say uff' is prohibition of that utterance; the necessary meaning is prohibition of all forms of harm (e.g., hitting). This necessary meaning is dalaalah.
The necessary meaning is immediate and linguistic, not derived through reasoning.
3
Examine the four proofs that dalaalah is not qiyas
List and explain the four proofs: (1) qiyas is speculative, dalaalah is definitive; (2) qiyas requires ijtihad, dalaalah is known to all native speakers; (3) dalaalah was legislated before qiyas; (4) those who reject qiyas accept dalaalah.
These proofs establish the independence of dalaalah as a source.
4
Understand the opposing view and the Hanafi response
Know that Imam al-Razi considered dalaalah as clear qiyas (qiyas jali). The Hanafi response refutes this by emphasizing the linguistic immediacy and the lack of need for identifying a cause (illa).
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is that Hanafis use dalaalah directly, while others may require qiyas conditions.
5
Apply the concept to examples
Work through examples from the text: deriving the prohibition of hitting from 'uff', the hadd for sodomy from zina, and the kaffarah for eating from intercourse by dalaalah.
These applications show how dalaalah functions in real fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage defines 'Dalaalat al-Nass' (indication of the text) and distinguishes it from qiyas. The author begins by clarifying that the term 'in the sense of the text' in the matn refers to the explicit wording (ibara) and indication (ishara), which are true categories of textual composition. However, what is meant by 'meaning' here is not the literal linguistic meaning but the necessary (iltizami) meaning, such as the implication of causing pain from the prohibition of saying 'uff'. The phrase 'linguistically' separates this from the meaning of the text proper, and excludes the implied (iqtida) and omitted (mahdhuf) because they are established by revelation or reason, not purely by language. The statement 'not by ijtihad' confirms that this understanding is linguistic, not requiring independent reasoning. This refutes those who claim that Dalaalat al-Nass is a form of qiyas—hidden or clear. The passage offers four proofs: 1) Qiyas is speculative (zanni) and known only to mujtahids, while Dalaalat al-Nass is definitive (qat'i) and known to any native speaker. 2) Dalaalat al-Nass was recognized before qiyas was legislated. 3) Even those who reject qiyas accept Dalaalat al-Nass. 4) The understanding from the verse 'Do not say uff' leads directly to prohibitions of hitting and insulting without any analogical reasoning. The text then delves into examples: from the prohibition of 'uff' we infer the prohibition of harming parents; from the hadd for zina we derive the same for sodomy; from the kaffarah for intercourse we derive it for eating and drinking during fast by dalaalah. The commentary by al-Qamar and al-Sunbuli clarifies nuances, such as the difference between the commentators' views on classification, and the fact that dalaalah may be speculative in some cases. Overall, the passage establishes Dalaalat al-Nass as a linguistic, non-ijtihadi source of rulings, stronger than qiyas and closer to the explicit text.

Key Terms
دلالة النص (Dalalat al-Nass): The indication of the text: the necessary (iltizami) meaning derived linguistically from the explicit wording, without ijtihad or qiyas.
القياس (al-Qiyas): Analogical reasoning that requires ijtihad to identify the cause (illa) and extend the ruling; it is speculative (zanni) and known only to mujtahids.
الالتزامي (al-Iltizami): The necessary implication: a meaning that follows necessarily from the literal meaning, such as pain from saying 'uff'.
القطع (al-Qat'): Definitive knowledge; in this context, dalaalah yields certainty (qat'i) because it is immediately understood by native speakers.
الظن (al-Zann): Speculative knowledge; qiyas is zanni because it depends on ijtihad and identification of cause.

Commentary
This passage from Nur al-Anwar explains the concept of iqtida' al-nass (textual implication) and its distinction from dalalah al-nass (textual indication). The author begins by defining iqtida' through a concrete fiqh example: when a master tells someone 'Free your slave for me for a thousand dirhams,' the word 'for a thousand' implies a sale (iqtida' al-bay'). This implication allows the transaction to be valid without the usual sale conditions like offer and acceptance (ijab wa qabul), and options (khiyar) do not apply. However, the conditions of emancipation (like legal capacity of the commander) are still required. The text then presents a dispute: Abu Yusuf holds that if the phrase omits 'for a thousand,' it implies a gift (hiba), which would dispense with possession (qabd) since gift normally requires possession. Abu Hanifa (Imam) disagrees, arguing that while offer and acceptance can be dropped in sale (as in mutual dealing ta'ati), possession in gift is essential and cannot be dropped. Thus the implication of gift is weaker. Next, the passage establishes that what is proven by iqtida' is equal to what is proven by dalalah in obligating the ruling, except when they conflict—then dalalah is preferred. The proof is the hadith where the Prophet instructed Aisha to wash menstrual blood with water. By iqtida', specifying water implies that other liquids are not sufficient. However, by dalalah (the obvious meaning), the purpose is purification, which can be achieved by any liquid, so dalalah takes precedence. The commentators (Al-Qamar and Al-Muhashi) clarify these rulings, referencing sources like Radd al-Muhtar and al-Qamus. The passage also discusses the strength of ishara (indication) relative to iqtida', concluding that iqtida' is more fundamental to the text's coherence, but in conflict, dalalah (and ishara) are given precedence. This is a key usul principle: when a text's necessary implication opposes its clear indication, the indication prevails.

Key Terms
اقتضاء النص (iqtida' al-nass): A meaning necessarily implied by the text for its coherence, without which the text would be defective or impossible.
دلالة النص (dalalah al-nass): The obvious meaning derived from the text itself, understood by any Arabic speaker as the primary intent.
التعاطي (ta'ati): Mutual dealing without formal offer and acceptance, where parties exchange goods and price by mutual consent, considered a valid sale in Hanafi fiqh.

Intention of Triple Divorce in Implied Text: Command vs. Declarative
Analyzing triple divorce intention in implied text (mqtadā al-naṣṣ)

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of mqtadā al-naṣṣ to distinguish between valid and invalid intentions of triple divorce in implied divorce utterances.

Core Idea
Intention of triple divorce is valid in a declarative statement with an ambiguous genus, but not in a command that denotes a single act.

This lesson examines a key usul al-fiqh distinction in divorce law: why Hanafi scholars treat 'You are irrevocable' differently from 'Divorce yourself' when a husband intends triple divorce. You will learn how the concept of mqtadā al-naṣṣ (implied meaning) interacts with linguistic structure to produce different rulings, and how this exemplifies Hanafi methodology of textual interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two utterances
Recognize the two statements: 'Divorce yourself' (طلقي نفسك, a command) and 'You are irrevocable' (أنت بائن, a declarative). Note their grammatical difference.
The command is an imperative (amr); the declarative is a predicate (khabar).
2
Analyze the linguistic structure of each
Understand that 'Divorce yourself' is a command that denotes a single verbal noun (a single divorce act). 'You are irrevocable' is a declarative that reports a state, where the term 'irrevocability' (baynūna) has two possible types.
The command cannot inherently signify multiple acts without qualification.
3
Distinguish the types of baynūna
Note that baynūna divides into light (revocable, one or two divorces) and severe (irrevocable, three divorces). Thus the term is a genus with subtypes.
This division is unique to Hanafi analysis; Shafi'i may treat baynūna as a single concept.
4
Apply the principle of intention in implied meaning
In Hanafi usul, when a text implies a meaning that has subtypes, intending a specific subtype is valid specification (takhṣīṣ), not generalization. Therefore, specifying the severe type in 'You are irrevocable' is valid.
This contrasts with 'Divorce yourself', which cannot be divided into subtypes.
5
Conclude the ruling
The intention of triple divorce is valid in 'You are irrevocable' because the husband is specifying a type within the genus. It is invalid in 'Divorce yourself' because the command only denotes a single act.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Types of Mafhum: Muwafaqa and Mukhalafa
Defines mafhum and its two categories with subtypes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between mafhum al-muwafaqa and mafhum al-mukhalafa, identify their subtypes, and apply them to derive rulings from textual examples.

Core Idea
Mafhum al-muwafaqa extends the ruling by cause; mafhum al-mukhalafa restricts by specification.

This lesson unpacks two foundational types of implied meaning (mafhum) in Hanafi usul: mafhum al-muwafaqa (concept of conformity) and mafhum al-mukhalafa (concept of opposition). Understanding these is key to extending or restricting legal rulings from the explicit text. We will define each, explore their subtypes, and see how they function in legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two primary categories of mafhum
The text opens by stating that mafhum is of two types: mafhum al-muwafaqa and mafhum al-mukhalafa. Note the defining characteristic: in muwafaqa, the unmentioned case follows the mentioned; in mukhalafa, it takes the opposite ruling.
2
Define mafhum al-muwafaqa
From the wording: 'the understanding that the unmentioned (maskut 'anhu) is in accordance with the mentioned (mantuq)'. This is based on a shared underlying reason (manat). In Hanafi usul, this is also called dalalat al-nass and is considered a strong form of inference.
Example: the prohibition of 'uff' implies prohibition of striking, because striking is a greater offense.
3
Define mafhum al-mukhalafa and list its subtypes
Mafhum al-mukhalafa gives the opposite ruling for the unmentioned. The text then lists its subtypes based on the linguistic element that triggers the opposite understanding: laqab (proper name), shart (condition), wasf (description), 'adad (number), ghaya (limit), and hasr (restriction). Each will be detailed later.
Hanafi school accepts mafhum al-mukhalafa with conditions, but does not accept mafhum al-laquab (concept of epithet).
4
Understand the Hanafi position on these concepts
The commentary notes that mafhum al-muwafaqa is a type of qiyas jali (obvious analogy) and is used widely. Mafhum al-mukhalafa is accepted only when the specification is for a legal purpose, not for praise, blame, etc. This distinction affects how rulings are derived.
Cross-madhhab: Shafi'is accept mafhum al-mukhalafa more broadly.

Conditions for Valid Mafhum al-Mukhalafa
Lists conditions for valid mafhum al-mukhalafa with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the five conditions for mafhum al-mukhalafa and apply them to determine whether a given textual restriction yields a valid mafhum al-mukhalafa.

Core Idea
Mafhum al-mukhalafa is valid only when no other purpose rules out negation.

The Hanafi school accepts mafhum al-mukhalafa (the implied opposite ruling) but only under strict conditions. This passage enumerates those conditions and illustrates them with the famous hadith 'al-ma'u min al-ma''. Understanding these conditions is essential for correctly interpreting restrictive phrases in the Qur'an and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concept
Recognize that mafhum al-mukhalafa derives an opposite ruling for the unmentioned case from a textual restriction (e.g., condition, description, number, ghayah, or exclusivity).
2
List the five conditions
The author states five conditions that must be satisfied: (1) the unmentioned case is not more deserving or equal in ruling, (2) the expression is not habitual, (3) it is not a response to a question or incident, (4) it is not for praise or blame, and (5) it conveys no other benefit.
Each condition ensures that the restriction is intended for limitation, not for another purpose.
3
Apply conditions to the hadith
The hadith 'al-ma'u min al-ma'' (water from water) was understood by the Ansar to mean that ghusl is required only when water (semen) is discharged, implying no ghusl for withdrawal (ikhsal) without discharge. This example meets all five conditions, so it is a valid mafhum al-mukhalafa.
The Ansar were native Arabic speakers; their understanding validates the concept when conditions are met.
4
Understand the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school accepts mafhum al-mukhalafa when these conditions are present. This distinguishes it from mere logical inference; it is a recognized proof (dalil) in istinbat.

The Hanafi Rejection of Mafhum al-Laqab (Concept of Name)
Argues against mafhum al-laquab with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi argument against mafhum al-laquab and evaluate the opposing view.

Core Idea
Accepting mafhum al-laquab leads to absurdities like denying other prophets.

While mafhum al-mukhalafa is accepted under conditions, the Hanafi school firmly rejects mafhum al-laquab (concept of name). The argument is stark: if a proper name implies negation of the ruling from others, then the fundamental statement 'Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah' would negate all other prophets—which is kufr. This lesson examines the reasoning and the inter-madhhab dispute.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that mafhum al-laquab (the implication from a proper name) does not negate the ruling for anyone else. The name is part of the essential structure of the statement, not a tool for restriction.
2
Understand the proof from absurdity
If mafhum al-laquab were valid, saying 'Muhammad is the Messenger of Allah' would imply that no one else is a messenger, which contradicts the belief in previous prophets (kufr). Similarly, 'Zaid is present' would imply no one else exists, which is false.
This reductio ad absurdum shows the concept cannot hold.
3
Compare with opposing view
Some Ash'ari and Hanbali scholars accepted mafhum al-laquab with conditions. They argue that a name specifies the subject, confining the ruling to that entity. The Hanafi response: the name is rukn al-kalam (essential component), not a limitation.
4
Apply the ruling to a fiqh example
In fiqh, the Hanafi do not apply mafhum al-laquab. For example, the verse 'And from the fruits of date palms and grapes you take strong drink and good provision' (Quran 16:67) does not limit the ruling to those two fruits; it merely mentions common examples.

Commentary
This passage details the conditions for mafhum al-mukhalafa to be valid: the unmentioned must not be more deserving or equal (otherwise it is mafhum al-muwafaqa), the wording must not be by way of habit (like 'foster daughters in your laps' which is descriptive, not restrictive), it must not be in response to a question or incident, it must not be for praise or blame, and it must not serve another benefit like aesthetic enjoyment. Similarly, 'Zaid is present' would imply no one else exists, which is false. Therefore, mafhum al-laquab is not accepted. The text also addresses those who distinguish between with and without a number, saying that the correct view is that even when a number is mentioned, mafhum al-laquab is still invalid.

The Invalid Mode of Inference from a Title (Mafhum al-Liqab)
Usul rule: invalid mode of inference from a title

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on mafhum al-liqab and apply it to evaluate the hadith 'al-ma'u min al-ma''.

Core Idea
Specifying a name does not negate the ruling for others—mafhum al-liqab is invalid in Hanafi usul.

This lesson examines the fourth invalid mode of inference in usul al-fiqh: deriving a negative ruling from a specific title. The Hanafi school holds that mentioning a specific noun (e.g., 'Zaid') does not imply negation for others. We explore the debate using the famous hadith 'al-ma'u min al-ma'' and understand why the Hanafis reject mafhum al-liqab, relying instead on the particle of inclusiveness (lam al-istighraq) and abrogation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the contested usul principle
Recognize that the dispute is about whether specifying a title (like 'Zaid') implies negation for other titles (like 'Amr'). The Hanafi position is that it does not.
This is known as mafhum al-liqab, which Hanafis reject as a valid source of law.
2
State the Hanafi position and its proof
The Hanafis argue that the hadith 'al-ma'u min al-ma'' uses the lam of inclusiveness, meaning all ghusl (for janaba) is from water (semen), not that ghusl is negated for other emissions. The hadith is also abrogated by later commands for ghusl on any emission.
The dalil is the hadith itself, but interpreted as inclusive, not exclusive.
3
Examine the opposing view (Shafi'i)
The Shafi'i school accepts mafhum al-liqab. They argue that the hadith, before abrogation, indicated that ghusl is only required from ejaculation (water), thus negating it for emission without ejaculation.
They rely on the apparent specification of the title 'al-ma''.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to the opposing dalil
The Hanafis respond that the hadith's lam indicates inclusiveness, not exclusivity. Moreover, the hadith is abrogated by later commands (e.g., the hadith 'idha al-taqa al-khitanan wajaba al-ghusl'). Thus the original ruling is no longer operative.
The Hanafis also use precaution: they oblige ghusl when the circumcised parts meet, as an indication of water (semen) emission.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
In fiqh, Hanafis do not require ghusl for emission alone without ejaculation, but they require it when the circumcised parts meet (by precaution). Shafi'is require ghusl for emission based on mafhum al-liqab.
This difference affects rulings of ritual purity.

Commentary
This is a discussion of the fourth invalid mode of inference in usul al-fiqh, namely the concept of the title (mafhum al-laqab). The author explains that specifying a particular noun (like 'Zaid') does not imply the negation of the ruling for other nouns (like 'Amr'). The Hanafi position is that mere mention of a specific title does not indicate anything about other titles; the ruling only affirms for the mentioned entity. The text then engages with a counterargument from those who accept mafhum al-laqab, using the hadith 'al-ma'u min al-ma' (water from water) which originally indicated that ghusl is only required from ejaculation. The Ansar inferred from this that ghusl is not required for emission without ejaculation. The Hanafi response is twofold: first, the hadith uses the lam of inclusiveness (all ghusl is from semen) not to negate, but to affirm the category; second, the hadith is abrogated. The comment clarifies that the Hanafis still hold the ruling but by different reasoning: water is established either by actual sight (seeing semen) or by indication (concealment of the circumcised parts), so ghusl is required in both cases by precaution. The marginal notes by Qamar and Sanbali elaborate on these points.

Key Terms
مفهوم اللقب (mafhum al-liqab): The inferred meaning from a specific title/noun that the ruling applies only to that named entity and is negated for others.
لام الاستغراق (lam al-istighraq): The particle 'lam' that indicates inclusiveness, meaning the ruling applies to all individuals of a category.
ناسخ (nasikh): Abrogating evidence; a later revelation or sunnah that supersedes an earlier one.

Hanafi Rejection of Mafhum al-Wasf and al-Shart
Explains the second and third invalid modes: mafhum al-wasf and mafhum al-shart.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school rejects mafhum al-wasf and mafhum al-shart as valid proofs, and compare this with the Shafi'i position using the example of the believing maidens verse.

Core Idea
A description or condition does not negate a ruling when absent — Hanafis reject mafhum al-wasf and al-shart as proofs.

In this passage, the author continues listing invalid modes of inference. The second and third modes are the inferred implications from a description (wasf) and a condition (shart). The Hanafi position is clear: these are not valid sources of law. However, the Shafi'i school accepts them. Let us analyze the reasoning and see why the Hanafis maintain their position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Invalid Modes
Recognize that mafhum al-wasf and mafhum al-shart are being introduced as a single mode because both share the same ruling: they do not negate.
The text calls them collectively a 'second mode'.
2
Understand the Hanafi Position
State that Hanafis do not accept that a description or condition implies the negation of the ruling when absent. The ruling is affirmed only in the presence of the description/condition.
General descriptions (never absent) have no concept at all.
3
Examine the Proof Text
Look at the verse 'from your believing maidens' (4:25). The ruling about marriage is attached to 'believing maidens' but does not prohibit marriage to other categories by itself.
The attribution here is not grammatical idafa but mere relation.
4
Contrast with the Shafi'i View
The Shafi'is argue that a specific description indicates that the ruling is confined to it. Thus, marrying non-believing maidens would be prohibited.
This is a legitimate ikhtilaf rooted in different usul principles.
5
Recognize the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical difference: Hanafis allow marriage to believing maidens but do not derive a general prohibition from this verse; Shafi'is infer a prohibition on marriage to non-believing maidens from the same wording.
Other proofs may independently establish rules, but mafhum alone is insufficient for Hanafis.

Commentary
This passage discusses two more invalid modes of inference: the concept of description (mafhum al-wasf) and the concept of condition (mafhum al-shart). The author explains that when a ruling is attached to a specific description (e.g., 'believing maidens') or a condition (e.g., 'if you meet'), it does not imply that the ruling is negated when the description or condition is absent. The Hanafi position is that such attribution only indicates the ruling in the presence of the description/condition, but does not necessarily negate it elsewhere. The commentary clarifies that these are considered together as one mode in the text, because the ruling of description and condition is the same in this regard. The marginal notes elaborate on the concept of description: the specific description (al-wasf al-khass al-mukhasṣis) is the one under dispute, not general descriptions that are inherent to the subject. The note from al-Sanbali also addresses an objection regarding the grammatical idafa (genitive construction) versus attribution in the verse about marriage, stating that the term 'addition' here means attribution not grammatical possession. Thus, the Hanafis reject mafhum al-wasf and mafhum al-shart as independent proofs, considering them invalid unless supported by other evidence.

Key Terms
مفهوم الوصف (mafhum al-wasf): The inferred implication from a specific description that the ruling applies only when that description is present and negated when absent.
مفهوم الشرط (mafhum al-shart): The inferred implication from a condition that the ruling applies only when the condition is met, negated otherwise.
