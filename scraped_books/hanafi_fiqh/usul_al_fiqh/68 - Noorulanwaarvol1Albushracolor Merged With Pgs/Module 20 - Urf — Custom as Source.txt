Inclusion of Descendants in Aman by Shubha
Textual analysis of the shubha-based inclusion of descendants in aman.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why grandsons and the mawali of mawali are included in an aman granted for 'sons' and 'mawali' by applying the Hanafi concept of shubha and distinguishing literal intention from precautionary inclusion.

Core Idea
Shubha from common usage allows inclusion of metaphorical extensions in aman to protect blood, even without literal intention.

In Hanafi usul, one word cannot simultaneously convey its literal and metaphorical meaning. Yet, when a harbi seeks aman for his 'sons' (abna') and 'clients' (mawali), should grandsons and the clients of clients be covered? The text resolves this by invoking shubha—a semblance of truth that creates reasonable doubt. Because blood is sacred, the law leans toward inclusion to preserve life. This lesson unpacks this layered reasoning, blending usul principles with the fiqh of aman.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Problem
The harbi uses the words 'sons' and 'mawali' which, if taken literally, exclude grandsons and further clients. But using them metaphorically would combine haqiqah and majaz in one utterance, which is impossible.
This is based on the usul rule that the same word cannot be intended literally and metaphorically at the same time.
2
Spot the Solution
The author introduces the concept of shubha: the apparent meaning is treated as a semblance that justifies inclusion for the sake of caution in blood matters, without actually intending the metaphorical meaning.
Shubha is weaker than certainty but sufficient to prevent bloodshed.
3
Trace the Evidence
The Quranic usage 'Bani Adam' (7:26) shows that 'abna' is used for all descendants, not just immediate sons. Similarly, 'mawali' is customarily extended. This creates a shubha that the harbi's words might also be intended broadly.
Common usage (urf) provides the qarinah here.
4
Apply to the Ruling
Due to the shubha and the principle of haqn al-dam (preserving blood), the grandchildren and second-level clients are included in the aman, even though they are not literally intended.
This shows how usul principles are applied pragmatically in fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis applying the usul principle of impossibility of combining literal and metaphorical meaning to a practical fiqh issue: granting aman (safe passage) to harbis (enemy combatants) who seek protection for their 'sons' (abna') and 'clients' (mawali). The question is whether the term 'sons' includes grandsons and 'mawali' includes the mawali of mawali, given that these extended meanings are metaphorical. The Hanafi answer is that they do enter, but not because the word literally includes them in the same intention. Rather, the apparent usage creates a 'shubha' (semblance) that justifies including them out of caution for preserving blood (ihqan al-dam). The primary intention is for immediate sons and mawali, but because the language is commonly extended (e.g., 'Bani Adam' for all descendants) and because blood is sacred, the benefit of the doubt is given. An objection is raised: if we go by shubha, why not include grandparents under 'fathers'? The response distinguishes: seeking aman for sons is to preserve progeny (nasl), which naturally extends to all descendants. In contrast, for fathers, the purpose is different. Thus, the qarinah (contextual indication) supports a general metaphorical interpretation for sons, but not for fathers. The commentary also notes that aman can be established by shubha due to the gravity of bloodshed. This shows how usul principles are applied in fiqh with attention to underlying purposes and contextual clues.

Key Terms
الشبهة (al-shubhah): A semblance or probability that resembles the truth; not certain but sufficient for precaution in serious matters like blood.
حقن الدم (haqn al-dam): Preservation of blood; the legal principle that killing is to be avoided unless absolutely necessary.
القرينة (al-qarinah): A contextual indication that guides interpretation away from the literal meaning toward the intended meaning.

The Objection and the Role of Qarinah in Interpretation
Textual analysis of the objection and response regarding inclusion of ancestors.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the objection against shubha-based inclusion and explain why the same reasoning does not extend to 'fathers' due to the controlling qarinah of preserving progeny.

Core Idea
A qarinah (contextual indication) can limit shubha: for 'sons', the purpose of preserving progeny extends descendants; for 'fathers', no such extension exists.

Having established that shubha can include grandsons and further clients in aman, the text anticipates an objection: why not also include grandparents when seeking aman for 'fathers'? The answer lies in the qarinah (contextual clue). For sons, the evident purpose is preserving progeny (nasl), which naturally extends to all descendants. For fathers, the purpose is different, so the shubha does not carry the same weight. This lesson explores how al-qarinah shapes interpretation and refines the application of usul principles.

Guided Path
1
State the Objection
If shubha from common usage allows inclusion of metaphorical extensions for 'sons', then consistency demands that it should also include 'grandfathers' under 'fathers', because 'fathers' can also be used metaphorically (e.g., 'ancestors').
This objection tests the consistency of the shubha principle.
2
Analyze the Response
The response does not deny the possibility of shubha for 'fathers', but introduces a controlling factor: the qarinah (contextual indication) that the harbi's purpose in seeking aman for his sons is to preserve his progeny. This qarinah supports a broad interpretation of 'sons'.
Qarinah is external evidence that guides interpretation.
3
Understand the Distinction
For 'sons', the qarinah of preserving progeny is strong because progeny naturally descends. For 'fathers', no similar purpose exists; the harbi is not trying to preserve his ancestors, since they are already gone. Thus, the same shubha does not trigger inclusion.
The difference is in the underlying objective (maqsad) of the aman request.
4
Apply the Role of Qarinah
This shows that in Hanafi usul, shubha is not a standalone tool; it operates only when supported by a qarinah that aligns with the purpose of the legal act. Without qarinah, the literal meaning prevails.
Qarinah can be from the text (Quranic usage) or from the context (purpose of the request).

Impossibility of Combining Literal and Metaphorical in Oaths
Analyzing whether an oath covers both literal and metaphorical senses

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of haqiqah mahjoorah (abandoned literal meaning) to interpret oaths and distinguish between literal and general metaphorical meanings in usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
In oaths, the literal meaning is abandoned by custom; the oath operates solely on the general metaphorical meaning of entry.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar tackles a key usul problem: when a person swears 'I will not place my foot in this house,' does the oath cover both barefoot (literal) and shod (metaphorical) entry? The Hanafi answer is no — the literal meaning is abandoned by custom, so the oath is understood through the general metaphorical meaning of 'entering,' which includes all modes. This lesson will analyze the reasoning and the role of custom and intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify linguistic senses
In the oath 'I will not place my foot in so-and-so's house,' identify the literal meaning (haqiqah: placing foot barefoot) and the metaphorical meaning (majaz: entering, which can be barefoot or shod). Also, 'house' has literal (owned) and metaphorical (leased/borrowed) senses.
The passage also mentions grandmothers under 'mothers' as a parallel metaphorical expansion.
2
Understand the objection
The opponent argues that if the oath covers both barefoot and shod entry, it combines literal and metaphorical meanings, which is impossible. Similarly, if it covers both owned and leased houses, the same problem arises.
The objection is a common critique of Hanafi rulings on oaths.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that the oath is interpreted via the general metaphorical meaning (majaz mursal) of 'entering,' which includes both barefoot and shod. The literal meaning is abandoned ('haqiqah mahjoorah') and thus not operative. Custom ('urf) indicates that the intended prohibition is on entry, not mere foot placement.
The passage cites that if he only places his feet without entering, he does not break the oath.
4
Apply the concept of haqiqah mahjoorah
A literal meaning that is no longer used in customary speech is considered abandoned. In this case, 'placing the foot' is customarily understood as 'entering,' so the literal meaning (barefoot placement) is disregarded. The metaphorical meaning is given preference.
This principle appears in Fatawa Qadhi Khan and al-Mabsut.
5
Distinguish between general metaphorical meaning and technical general metaphor
The passage clarifies that 'general metaphorical meaning' here means absolute metaphor (majaz mursal), not the technical general metaphor that requires the literal to be an individual of the metaphorical. Otherwise, the literal would still be operative.
If it were technical general metaphor, the oath would also be broken by literal foot placement without entry.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a matn that discusses the impossibility of combining literal and metaphorical meanings in legal interpretation, specifically in oaths. The commentators (likely from a sharh) explain that when a person swears 'I will not place my foot in so-and-so's house', the oath is understood to refer to the general metaphorical meaning of 'entering' (dukhul) rather than the literal act of placing the foot. This is because custom ('urf) indicates that the intent is to forbid entry, not merely the physical placement. The literal meaning (being barefoot) is considered abandoned (mahjoorah) and thus not operative. If the swearer had a specific intention (niyyah), that intention is followed; for example, if he intended not to enter barefoot, entering shod would not break the oath. However, if he intended only the literal meaning of placing the foot without entering, he would not be believed judicially because that meaning is abandoned in custom. The commentary also addresses an objection regarding grandmothers being included in the verse 'forbidden to you are your mothers', explaining that this is either by textual indication or by a general metaphorical reading that includes ancestors under 'mothers'. The passage emphasizes that the oath operates on the general metaphorical meaning (majaz mursal) that covers all modes of entry, avoiding the need to combine literal and metaphorical simultaneously.

Key Terms
الحقيقة (al-haqiqah): The literal meaning of a word, e.g., 'placing the foot' means actually putting the foot on the ground barefoot.
المجاز (al-majaz): The metaphorical meaning, e.g., 'placing the foot' meaning 'entering', which can be barefoot or shod.
حقيقة مهجورة (haqiqah mahjoorah): A literal meaning that is abandoned in customary usage; it is not considered in interpreting an oath.

The Meaning of 'inda: Custody Not Debt
Clarifying that 'inda implies custody/trust, not debt

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the linguistic basis (urf) for the Hanafi rule that 'inda implies custody (amanah) rather than debt, and apply it to distinguish wadi'ah from dayn.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, 'inda denotes physical presence and trust — debt must be explicitly stated.

This passage from Noor al-Anwaar examines the linguistic implications of the word 'inda in legal declarations. In Hanafi usul, the customary meaning of words is given weight. Here, 'inda is shown to indicate custody and trust (wadi'ah) rather than debt, unless the word 'debt' is added. Understanding this principle helps us correctly interpret statements of obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key term
Locate the word 'inda in the passage and note its asserted primary meaning: presence (hadra).
2
Understand the claim
The author claims that 'inda indicates custody without obligation (hifz duna luzum). This is because 'inda means proximity, and certain proximity is that of trust (amanah), not debt.
Debt is only a possibility (muhtamil), so it requires explicit expression.
3
Recognize the legal effect
If someone says 'You have X with me', the default classification is wadi'ah (trust). To create a debt, one must add the word dayn (debt).
Example: 'You have a thousand dirhams with me as a debt' becomes a debt.
4
Note the basis in usul
This ruling is derived from linguistic custom (urf) — the common understanding of 'inda in Arabic speech. Hanafi usul treats this urf as a valid source for determining legal meanings.
5
Appreciate the scope
The rule applies to both literal and figurative uses of 'inda, as the passage mentions: 'Zaid is with Amr' (literal) and 'I have money with me' even if the money is elsewhere (figurative).
The principle is about the default meaning, not the actual location.

Commentary
This passage is an expansion probe from a Hanafi usul work clarifying the legal implications of the word 'inda. The author states that 'inda primarily denotes 'presence' (hadra), and when used in declarations like 'you have a thousand dirhams with me' (indi), it indicates a trust (wadi'ah) — something held in custody for safekeeping — not a debt. This is because the literal meaning of 'inda is proximity, and the most certain kind of proximity is physical custody (amanah). Debt is only a possibility and requires explicit mention. Therefore, to make a debt binding, one must add the word 'debt' (dayn). The passage then digresses into marginal comments reiterating the rule for talaq in non-consummated marriages, and the distinction between iqrar of debt and talaq. The core ruling is that 'inda implies trust unless specified otherwise, a principle rooted in the literal and customary usage of the term.

Key Terms
عند (inda): A preposition meaning 'at' or 'with'; in legal context it denotes presence and implies custody/trust (amanah) rather than debt.
وديعة (wadi'ah): A trust/deposit where the holder is a custodian (amin) and not liable for loss unless negligent.
