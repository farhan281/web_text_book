The Cause (Sabab) of Obligation of Purification: Refining from Obligation to Legislation
Commentary on refining the sabab for tahara

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the sabab (cause) of the obligation of tahara in Hanafi usul and explain why the cause was refined from 'obligation of prayer' to 'legislation of prayer'.

Core Idea
The cause of purification is the legislation of prayer, not its obligation, because even supererogatory prayer requires purification.

In this passage from Noor al-Anwar, the author examines the sabab (cause) that triggers the requirement of ritual purification (tahara). The original formulation tied tahara to the obligation of prayer, but a refined cause—the legislation of prayer—is proposed to cover both obligatory and non-obligatory prayers. This lesson unpacks that refinement and the usul reasoning behind it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the original proposed cause
The text refers to an initial view that the obligation of prayer (wujub al-salat) is the cause for tahara. Understand why this was plausible: tahara is required before obligatory prayer.
This is the first sabab considered.
2
Recognize the objection
The objection is that supererogatory prayer (salat al-nafl) also requires tahara even though it is not obligatory. Thus 'obligation' is too narrow to cover all cases where tahara is needed.
3
Understand the refinement
The commentator (sharih) changed the cause to 'legislation of prayer' (shar'iyyat al-salat), meaning the very fact that prayer is prescribed by Shariah. This includes both obligatory and supererogatory prayers.
This refinement makes the cause comprehensive (aamm).
4
Consider alternative proposals
The passage also mentions 'intention to pray' (iradat al-salat) as a possible cause, but notes that if one is already pure, intention alone does not obligate tahara. The refinement adds the condition of being in a state of hadath.
This shows that causes must be refined to fit all cases.
5
Reflect on the methodology
This passage illustrates the Hanafi usul process: proposing a cause, testing it against all relevant cases, and refining it until it covers every instance without exception. This is the talil (causal reasoning) approach.

The Cause of Kaffarah: Between Prohibition and Permission
Defining the cause of kaffarah as oscillating between prohibition and permission.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school requires the cause of a kaffarah to oscillate between prohibition and permission, and identify the dual nature of kaffarah as worship and punishment.

Core Idea
A kaffarah must be caused by an act that is both permissible and prohibited, because kaffarah itself is both worship and punishment.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, every ruling has a cause (sabab). For punishments (uqubat) and fixed penalties (hudud), the causes are generally prohibited acts. But kaffarat (expiations) are unique: they serve as both worship (requiring reward) and punishment (deterrence). This lesson unpacks why the cause of a kaffarah must therefore be an act that has both a permissible and a prohibited dimension — an oscillation between the two. We will trace the reasoning through examples and the Quranic proof.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish Uqubat, Hudud, and Kaffarat
First, clarify the three categories: uqubat is general punishment, hudud are fixed Allah's rights, and kaffarat are expiations that combine worship and penalty.
Uqubat includes hudud and qisas; kaffarat are a separate category.
2
Grasp the Dual Nature of Kaffarah
Understand that kaffarah is performed through worship (fasting, feeding) yet is a recompense for a prohibited act, giving it both 'ibada and 'uquba aspects.
This duality is central to the Hanafi position.
3
Identify the Required Oscillation in the Cause
Since kaffarah combines worship (requires permissible basis) and punishment (requires prohibited act), its cause must be an act that is permissible in one aspect and prohibited in another — i.e., oscillating between hazr and ibaha.
Pure permissibility cannot ground punishment; pure prohibition cannot ground worship.
4
Apply to Examples from the Text
Work through the given examples: accidental killing (permissible in aiming at game, but prohibited in lack of care), breaking an oath (necessary yet prohibited), and zihar (permissible as discipline, prohibited as false speech).
Each example illustrates the oscillation.
5
Address the Repentance Objection
Consider the counterexample of tawba (repentance): it is worship triggered by a purely prohibited act (sin). The text acknowledges this and notes the premise lacks evidence but maintains the oscillation requirement based on the compensatory nature of kaffarah.
This is a recognized scholarly discussion within Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary on the causes (asbab) of punishments, hudud, and kaffarat. It begins by reiterating the broader context of causes for acts of worship and transactions, then focuses on the causes for punitive measures. The key principle stated is that the cause of a kaffarah must be an act that has a dual nature: it is both permissible in one aspect and prohibited in another. This duality is necessary because kaffarah combines elements of worship (which requires a permissible basis) and punishment (which requires a prohibited basis). Examples given include accidental killing (permissible in aiming at game but prohibited in lack of caution), breaking an oath (permissible in necessity but prohibited in breaking the vow), and zihar (permissible as discipline but prohibited as false speech). The passage also clarifies that punishments (uqubat) are general, encompassing hudud (fixed punishments for Allah's rights) and retaliation (qisas, a slave's right). The text rebuts a potential objection that murder cannot be a punishment because retaliation is a slave's right; the response is that punishment here is broader, meaning any recompense for a prohibited act. The discussion on cleanliness (tahara) earlier is a digression to explain cause for obligation of purification. The commentary also notes a criticism: if repentance (tawba) is worship and its cause is a sin (a purely prohibited act), then why cannot a kaffarah's cause be a sin? The text does not fully resolve this; it merely presents the dominant Hanafi position that the cause must be oscillating. This is a typical Hanafi usul approach where the illa (effective cause) must be suitable for both the worship and punitive aspects of kaffarah.

Key Terms
سبب (sabab): That which leads to the existence of a ruling in a way that is apt by creation or command.
عقوبة (uquba): A general term for punishment, including hudud, qisas, and other penalties that are a recompense for a prohibited act.
حد (hadd): A fixed punishment legislated for the right of Allah, such as for zina, theft, slander, and drinking wine.
كفارة (kaffara): An expiation that combines elements of worship (like fasting, feeding, freeing a slave) and punishment, required for acts like accidental killing, zihar, broken oaths, and intentional fast-breaking.

Defining the Cause (Sabab) in Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh
Defining the cause and its relation to rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of sabab, explain its distinction from shart, and apply the criteria of repetition to identify the true cause in attributed rulings.

Core Idea
A ruling's cause is whatever it depends on for existence and repetition; attribution to a condition is merely metaphorical.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text defines the technical term 'sabab' (cause) and clarifies its relationship to rulings. The author explains that the cause is known by the fact that the ruling is attributed to it and connected to it in such a way that the ruling does not exist without the cause and is repeated when the cause is repeated. A key challenge is that some rulings are named after conditions, like 'zakat al-fitr.' The passage resolves this by showing that such attribution is figurative (majaz), and the true cause lies elsewhere.

Guided Path
1
Extract the definition
Identify the key phrase: 'by attribution of the ruling to it and its connection with it.' Note that connection means the ruling does not exist without it and is repeated with its repetition.
This is the core technical definition.
2
Distinguish sabab from shart
A condition (shart) is necessary for the ruling's existence but is not the cause. Attribution to a condition is metaphorical, as in 'zakat al-fitr' (fast-breaking is a condition; the cause is the head/person supported).
Attribution to a cause is literal (haqiqah); to a condition, figurative (majaz).
3
Analyze the objection and response
The objection: 'You sometimes attribute to the condition.' Response: it is metaphorical because the condition resembles the cause in co-occurrence, but the ruling cannot precede the cause, whereas it can precede a condition.
This resolves apparent counterexamples.
4
Apply to other examples
For 'hajj al-Islam,' the cause is the House of Allah (Kaaba), and Islam is a condition. For 'zakat al-mal,' the cause is owned wealth, not the passage of a year (which is a condition).
Practice identifying cause vs. condition in common fiqh terms.

Commentary
This passage is a usul definition of 'sabab' (cause) in the context of legislated rulings. It opens by stating that a cause is known by the attribution (nisbah) and connection (ta'luq) of the ruling to it. The key idea is that when a ruling is attributed to something and depends on its existence (the ruling does not exist without it and is repeated with its repetition), that something is the cause. The author addresses an objection: why then do we sometimes attribute rulings to conditions (shart), as in 'zakat al-fitr'? He explains that such attribution is metaphorical (majaz), because the condition resembles the cause in that the ruling occurs with it. The true cause for zakat al-fitr is the head (the person being supported), while the day of breaking the fast is a condition. Similarly, for hajj, the cause is the House of Allah (Ka'bah), while Islam is a condition. The passage also clarifies that causality involves complete specificity (kamaal al-ikhtisas), and that attribution may not indicate causality if a preventer (mani') exists. Examples from Hanafi fiqh are given: the expiation for breaking a fast (kafarah) is like the expiation for zihar. The commentary follows the order of the source, explaining each marginal note.

Key Terms
السبب (al-sabab): That to which the ruling is attributed and connected, such that the ruling does not exist without it and is repeated with its repetition.
الشرط (al-shart): Something upon which the existence of the ruling depends but is not the cause; attribution to it is metaphorical.

The Hanafi Definition of Intellect as a Narrator Condition
Defining the condition of intellect for a narrator

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of intellect ('aql) as a condition for narrator reliability, identify its role as a tool for the heart, and apply the Quranic evidence (Qaf:37) to support this definition.

Core Idea
Intellect is a light from senses to reason, a tool for the heart, not the perceiver itself.

Before a report can be accepted as evidence, the narrator must meet certain conditions. The first of these is intellect ('aql). But what exactly is intellect in Hanafi usul? This lesson unpacks the definition given in the text, showing how intellect is a light that bridges sensory perception to rational understanding, and how it serves as an instrument for the heart, which is the true perceiver. The commentary also cites a Quranic verse to ground this distinction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition being defined
The passage introduces intellect (al-'aql) as the first of four conditions for a narrator: intellect, precision, uprightness, and Islam. Recognize that this is a condition for the validity of the narrator's testimony.
The other conditions are defined elsewhere; focus here on intellect.
2
Extract the core definition of intellect
Look at the Arabic phrase: 'هو نور في بدن الآدمي يضيء به طريق يبتدأ به من حيث ينتهي إليه درك الحواس'. Intellect is described as a light in the human body that illuminates a path starting where sensory perception ends. This means it allows reasoning from physical observations to abstract conclusions.
The light metaphor emphasizes that intellect makes things clear.
3
Analyze the example given
The text gives an example: seeing a tall building leads the intellect to conclude it must have a wise maker. This illustrates how sensory data (the building) is the starting point for intellectual reasoning (knowledge of a maker). The 'starting point of intellects is the end point of senses.'
This example is crucial for understanding the transition from al-mahsus to al-ma'qul.
4
Understand the relationship between intellect and heart
The commentary explains that the heart is the true perceiver, and the intellect is its tool. This is supported by the Quranic verse 'For indeed in that is a reminder for whoever has a heart' (Qaf:37). The heart perceives intelligible matters, while the intellect provides the means for that perception.
This distinguishes the Hanafi view from those who treat intellect as an independent faculty.
5
Apply to narrator reliability
Since intellect is a condition, a narrator must possess it to be considered reliable. A person lacking intellect (e.g., insane or severely mentally deficient) cannot be accepted because they cannot correctly perceive or transmit reports.
This condition ensures that only trustworthy transmissions enter the legal system.

Intellect as a Condition for Narration
States the condition of complete intellect for the narrator

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition of complete intellect for a narrator and apply the Hanafi rule to evaluate the validity of a child's hadith narration.

Core Idea
Complete intellect of an adult is required for narration; a child's narration is accepted only if he heard with tamyiz and narrates after attaining intellect.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar establishes a foundational condition for hadith narration: the narrator must possess complete intellect, i.e., the intellect of a mature adult. The reasoning is drawn from the Shariah's treatment of individuals who cannot manage their own affairs. However, a nuanced exception is made for a child with discernment who hears hadith and later narrates after puberty. We will unpack the condition, its rationale, and the exception, and then apply it to evaluate real scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition and its scope
Locate the statement 'complete intellect is the condition' and understand that it refers to the intellect of a baligh (adult). Recognize that the deficient intellect of a child, imbecile, or insane person does not suffice.
The condition applies at the time of narration, not necessarily at the time of hearing.
2
Understand the reasoning (ta'lil)
The text gives a qiyas (analogy): since the Shariah does not allow such individuals to manage their own worldly affairs, they are even less fit for religious matters. Trace the illa (effective cause): lack of complete intellect.
This is an argument a fortiori (awla).
3
Distinguish between hearing and narrating
The passage separates the act of hearing (tahammul) from narrating (ada'). For hearing, the child needs tamyiz (discernment). For narrating after puberty, he needs complete intellect. Acceptability depends on both times.
If both occur before puberty, narration is rejected. If hearing was during childhood (with tamyiz) and narrating after puberty, it is accepted.
4
Apply the rule to scenarios
Test different combinations: (a) child hears and narrates before puberty → rejected; (b) child hears before puberty with tamyiz, narrates after → accepted; (c) adult hears and narrates → accepted; (d) insane person hears → rejected regardless.
The condition of intellect at narration is absolute.

When a Narrator's Practice Defects a Hadith: The Rule of Ta'n in Hanafi Usul
A usul rule explaining when a narrator's contradictory practice invalidates his hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a narrator's contrary practice only defects a hadith if it occurs after the narration and is not based on a legitimate interpretation, and identify the exceptions.

Core Idea
A narrator's contrary practice defects a hadith only if it is certain and after narration; interpretation and prior practice do not harm.

In Hanafi usul, not every contradiction between a narrator's words and actions undermines his hadith. The critical factors are timing and certainty. This lesson unpacks the rule from Nur al-Anwar, clarifying when a narrator's practice constitutes a ta'n (defect) and when it does not, using three classic examples: Aisha and marriage, Ibn Umar and khiyar al-majlis, and raising hands in prayer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule
The passage states that a narrator's contrary practice only becomes a defect if it is certain and occurs after the narration. If it occurred before or its date is unknown, it is not a defect. Note the emphasis on timing and certainty.
The rule is derived from the principle that the narrator may have changed his practice upon hearing the hadith.
2
Analyze the first example: Aisha's marriage case
Aisha narrated the hadith that marriage requires a guardian, yet she married her brother's daughter without the guardian's explicit permission. But the passage explains that this was not a true contradiction because she acted based on a specific interpretation (the guardian was absent, marriage valid pending consent). Thus, her practice does not defect the hadith.
This illustrates that not every apparent contradiction is a defect; interpretation can resolve it.
3
Examine the second example: Ibn Umar and khiyar al-majlis
Ibn Umar narrated the hadith 'المتبايعان بالخيار ما لم يتفرّقا' and interpreted 'separation' as physical separation (Shafi'i view). The Hanafi school interprets it as verbal separation (completion of offer and acceptance). The passage argues that Ibn Umar's interpretation as a narrator does not bind others, and the hadith remains a proof for the Hanafi interpretation.
This shows that a narrator's opinion on the meaning of a hadith is not a defect against the hadith itself.
4
Consider the third example: Raising hands in prayer
Ibn Umar narrated that the Prophet raised his hands at bowing and rising, yet some reports suggest Ibn Umar himself did not always do so. The passage suggests this may be due to abrogation or different occasions, and that abstention from acting on a hadith after narration could be a defect, but prior abstention is not.
This highlights the complexity of determining when a narrator's practice is truly contradictory.
5
Synthesize the Hanafi rule
The rule: A narrator's contrary practice invalidates his hadith as proof only if (1) it occurs after the narration, (2) it is certain (not due to interpretation), and (3) it is not based on legitimate reasons like abrogation. Otherwise, the hadith remains valid.
This rule is consistent with the Hanafi principle that a narrator is not a legislator; his personal practice does not override the text.

Key Terms

جرح (jarh): A defect that impugns a narrator's reliability, potentially leading to rejection of his hadith.

Handling Additions and Differences in Narrations: Hanafi Usul Rules
Explains rules for conflicting narrations from same and different reporters.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules for handling additions in narrations and distinguish between cases of a single narrator versus multiple narrators.

Core Idea
A single narrator's addition is accepted; different narrators' contradictions are treated as independent reports.

This passage from Noor al-Anwaar addresses a classic usul problem: when a trustworthy narrator sometimes includes an additional detail and sometimes omits it, which version do we accept? The Hanafi school takes a principled stance: the version with the addition is authoritative. But when two different narrators report conflicting versions, the rule changes — both reports are treated as separate and both applied where possible. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind these rules using the famous hadith of al-tahāluf (the oath between buyer and seller).

Guided Path
1
Identify the narrator scenario
Determine whether the conflicting reports come from the same narrator or different narrators. This determines which rule applies.
If the same narrator, apply the rule of al-ziyādah; if different, treat as two independent reports.
2
If same narrator: apply the rule of al-ziyādah (addition)
The narration containing the addition is preferred (al-muthbit) because the omission may be due to lack of precision. The added detail is considered part of the original report.
Hanafi principle: 'al-muthbit li al-ziyādah muqaddam' (the one affirming the addition is given precedence).
3
Examine the example: hadith of al-tahāluf
Ibn Mas'ud reported the hadith with and without the condition 'wa al-sil'u qā'imah' (while the commodity is still in existence). The Hanafis take the version with the condition, limiting the option to cases where the commodity is present.
The Shafi'i school argues both versions are valid, but Hanafis attribute the omission to lesser precision.
4
If different narrators: treat as two independent reports
When two different narrators report conflicting versions, the absolute (mutlaq) is not carried onto the restricted (muqayyad) if the reports concern different rulings or subjects. Each is applied within its own scope.
Example: prohibition of selling food before possession vs. prohibition of selling anything before possession — the more general prohibition applies to all goods.
5
Explore the madhhab dispute
The Shafi'i school argues that the absolute should be carried onto the restricted because the subject matter is the same. The Hanafi response is that the addition pertains to the cause (sabab), not the ruling (hukm), so the principle of carrying does not apply.
This is a key nuance in Hanafi usul: the nature of the addition matters.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on how to handle conflicting narrations. The author first presents the rule: when a single narrator reports a hadith sometimes with an added detail and sometimes without, the version with the addition is accepted, provided the narrator is trustworthy and precise. The example given is the hadith of Ibn Mas'ud about the option of the two parties to a sale (al-tahāluf). One narration includes the condition that the commodity is still in existence, another omits it. The Hanafis take the version with the addition, so the option only applies when the commodity is still there. The omission is attributed to lesser precision. The author then discusses the Shafi'i position, which holds that the option applies whether the commodity is present or destroyed, arguing that both narrations must be used. The Hanafis respond that the absolute (sale without condition) should be carried onto the restricted (with condition) because they are the same incident and ruling, but the author notes a subtlety: here the addition is about the cause, not the ruling, so the principle of carrying the absolute onto the restricted does not apply. Then, the passage turns to the case where the narrators differ (i.e., two different narrators report different versions). In that case, both reports are treated as independent, and both are acted upon. The Hanafi principle is that the absolute is not carried onto the restricted in two different rulings. The example is two hadiths: one forbids selling food before taking possession, another forbids selling anything before taking possession. Since the second is more general, it includes the first, so we apply the general prohibition to all goods, not just food. This shows that when the narrators are different, we do not restrict the absolute by the restricted; rather, we treat each as an independent rule and apply the more comprehensive one.

Key Terms
الزيادة (al-ziyādah): An additional wording in one narration of a hadith that is absent from another narration by the same reporter.
المطلق والمقيد (al-mutlaq wa al-muqayyad): An absolute (mutlaq) ruling without a condition, and a restricted (muqayyad) ruling with a condition. The rule discussed is when they apply to different rulings.
التحالف (al-tahāluf): The mutual oath taken by buyer and seller when they differ over the price or terms of a sale and have no evidence.
المثبت (al-muthbit): The narration that includes the additional wording, as opposed to the narration that omits it.

The Four Categories of the Prophet's Actions
Classification of Prophet's actions into four categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four categories of the Prophet's actions (mubah, mustahabb, wajib, fard) and explain the Hanafi distinction between fard and wajib in this context.

Core Idea
The Prophet's actions are of four types: fard, wajib, mustahabb, and mubah — but not all are obligatory for us.

In this passage from Noor al-Anwar, the commentator explains the classification of the Prophet's actions as a foundation for understanding how his Sunnah informs Islamic law. The passage emphasizes that the Prophet's actions are divided into four categories, with the explicit exclusion of 'zalla' (slips). This taxonomy is essential for determining whether a particular prophetic action is to be emulated as obligatory, recommended, or merely permissible. The Hanafi school uniquely distinguishes between fard (established by definitive proof) and wajib (established by speculative proof), a nuance that shapes much of Islamic jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four categories
List the four categories: mubah (permissible), mustahabb (recommended), wajib (obligatory), fard (emphatically obligatory). Understand the meaning of each in Hanafi usul.
Fard and wajib are both obligatory but differ in the certainty of their evidence.
2
Understand the exclusion of zalla
Analyze the definition of 'zalla' — a slip that is an unintentional forbidden act occurring while intending a permissible act. Recognize why it is not a model for emulation.
The Prophet's slips are rare and do not affect his sinlessness.
3
Grasp the relative nature of the classification
Note that the classification is relative to the Ummah. For the Prophet himself, there is no conventional obligation because all proofs are definitive in his case.
This means the four categories apply to how we view his actions, not to his own legal status.
4
Note the Hanafi distinction between fard and wajib
Distinguish fard (established by qati' proof, denial leads to kufr) from wajib (established by zanni proof, denial is sinful but not kufr). This influences how we treat different actions of the Prophet.
This distinction is unique to the Hanafi school and is not adopted by other schools.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary on Noor al-Anwar, explaining the chapter 'Af'al al-Nabi' (Actions of the Prophet). It begins by discussing the classification of the Prophet's actions into four categories: mubah (permissible), mustahabb (recommended), wajib (obligatory), and fard (more emphatically obligatory). The author excludes 'zalla' (slips), which are rare unintentional lapses, because they are not followed. The commentary elaborates that this classification is relative to the Ummah; for the Prophet himself, all his actions are either acts of obedience or permissible, and there is no conventional obligation since all proofs are conclusive for him. The passage then addresses three types of actions that are not necessarily followed: those done forgetfully, those habitual (like eating), and those specific to him (like marrying more than four wives). It also discusses a subtle point: whether wajib can exist for the Prophet, and reconciles by noting that wajib may be interpreted as a perfection attribute or that it applies initially before revelation.

Key Terms
الزلة (az-zalla): A slip: an unintentional forbidden act committed while intending a permissible act; it is excluded from the four categories of the Prophet's actions.
الفرض (al-fard): An obligatory act established by a definitive proof, requiring belief and action.
الواجب (al-wajib): An obligatory act established by a speculative proof; its denial is not disbelief but is sinful.
المستحب (al-mustahabb): A recommended act; doing it earns reward but leaving it is not sinful.
المباح (al-mubah): A permissible act; doing or leaving it is equal in reward.

Commentary
This passage is from the commentary on Noor al-Anwar, explaining the chapter 'Af'al al-Nabi' (Actions of the Prophet). It begins by discussing the classification of the Prophet's actions into four categories: mubah (permissible), mustahabb (recommended), wajib (obligatory), and fard (more emphatically obligatory). The author excludes 'zalla' (slips), which are rare unintentional lapses, because they are not followed. The commentary elaborates that this classification is relative to the Ummah; for the Prophet himself, all his actions are either acts of obedience or permissible, and there is no conventional obligation since all proofs are conclusive for him. The passage then addresses three types of actions that are not necessarily followed: those done forgetfully, those habitual (like eating), and those specific to him (like marrying more than four wives). It also discusses a subtle point: whether wajib can exist for the Prophet, and reconciles by noting that wajib may be interpreted as a perfection attribute or that it applies initially before revelation.

Key Terms

الطبعي (al-tab'i): Habitual or natural; actions that are instinctive and common to human nature.
المخصوص به (al-makhsoos bihi): Specific to the Prophet; actions that are unique to him and not to be imitated by the Ummah.

Binding Nature of Previous Shariahs
Explains the Hanafi rule on binding nature of previous shariahs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition under which the Hanafi school considers previous divine laws binding upon Muslims and apply the rule to derive rulings from Quranic examples.

Core Idea
Previous shariahs bind us only when narrated in the Quran/Sunnah without disapproval — otherwise they are not our law.

This passage addresses a foundational question in usul al-fiqh: to what extent are the laws of earlier prophets part of Islamic law? The Hanafi school offers a nuanced answer, conditional on how the law is transmitted and whether it is accompanied by divine disapproval. We will work through the conditions, examples, and the underlying principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule and Its Condition
The passage states that previous shariahs are binding (لازمة) if Allah or His Messenger narrates them 'without disapproval' (من غير إنكار). Locate this key condition in the Arabic text.
The condition has two parts: narration (قص) and absence of disapproval (إنكار).
2
Differentiate Sources: Quran/Sunnah vs. Distorted Scriptures
Even if a law exists in the Torah or Gospel, it is not binding because of textual corruption (تحريف). The narration must come through the Quran or authentic Sunnah.
This avoids reliance on altered texts.
3
Recognize Disapproval: Explicit and Implicit
Disapproval can be explicit (e.g., 'do not do like that') or implicit (e.g., indicating it was a punishment for wrongdoing). Find the examples in the passage that illustrate disapproval.
The dietary restrictions on Jews are an example of implicit disapproval.
4
Apply the Rule to Examples
The passage gives three examples of laws that bind us (qisas, division of water, prohibition of sodomy) and one that does not (Jewish dietary restrictions). For each, confirm the presence or absence of disapproval.
Qisas: narrated without disapproval → binding. Jewish prohibitions: narrated with disapproval → not binding.
5
Understand the Underlying Principle
These laws bind us not as independent laws of previous prophets, but as part of the shari'ah of Prophet Muhammad (صلى الله عليه وسلم) because their narration in the Quran without disapproval makes them part of our religion. The proof is Quran 6:90.
This harmonizes the command to follow previous prophets with the finality of Islam.

Chapter on the Actions of the Prophet. Explanation of previous laws. Some said: They are never binding upon us. The chosen opinion is what the author mentioned. And the laws of those before us are binding upon us if Allah or His Messenger narrates them to us without disapproval. If Allah does not narrate them to us but they exist only in the Torah and the Gospel, they are not binding upon us because they distorted the Torah and the Gospel much and inserted rulings from their own desires, so it is not certain that they are from Allah. Likewise, if Allah narrates them to us and then disapproves of them after narrating the story explicitly by saying 'do not do like that' or implicitly by indicating that that was a punishment for their wrongdoing, then it is forbidden for us to act upon it. This is a great principle for Abu Hanifa, may Allah have mercy on him, from which most jurisprudential rulings branch. Commentary
This passage is a usul al-fiqh ruling about the binding nature of previous divine laws (shara'i' sabiqa) for Muslims. The author presents the Hanafi position (al-mukhtar) that such laws are binding upon us only if they are narrated to us by Allah or His Messenger in the Quran or authentic Sunnah without any disapproval. If they are only found in the Torah or Gospel, they are not binding due to textual corruption. Even if narrated to us, if the narration includes explicit or implicit disapproval (like the prohibitions on Jews as punishment), then it is not binding. This is a foundational principle (asl kabir) for Imam Abu Hanifa, from which many fiqh rulings derive. The text provides examples: the law of qisas in Surah al-Ma'idah remains binding because it was mentioned without disapproval; the story of the division of water between Prophet Salih's she-camel and his people shows the permissibility of muhaya'ah (time-sharing); the story of Prophet Lut condemning homosexuality proves its prohibition. Conversely, the dietary restrictions imposed on Jews (e.g., prohibition of certain animals and fats) are mentioned as punishments for their transgression, so they are not binding on Muslims. The passage also explains that these laws bind us as part of the shari'ah of Prophet Muhammad, not as independent laws, because they become part of our religion when narrated without disapproval. The proof is Quran 6:90. The passage then transitions to the topic of taqlid of the Companions, which is annexed to the discussions of Sunnah because the possibility of direct hearing from the Prophet is present in a Companion's statement.

Shared Worker as Amin: Liability Derived from Prophetic Actions
States the ruling on guarantee for a shared worker based on Prophetic action

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi ruling that a shared worker (ajir mushtarak) is an amin and identify the usul principle (qiyas) by which this ruling is derived.

Core Idea
A shared worker is a trustee, not a guarantor, unless negligent.

This passage from Noor al-Anwaar addresses the liability of a shared worker (ajir mushtarak). In Hanafi fiqh, the worker is considered an amin (trustee) and is not liable for loss of the entrusted item unless he is negligent. The ruling is derived through qiyas (analogy) to a private employee (ajir khas) and confirmed by the general principle from the Prophet's actions. However, in cases of unavoidable calamities, there is consensus on non-liability.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal entity
Identify the shared worker (ajir mushtarak) and his legal status as stated in the text: 'innahu amin' (he is a trustee).
An amin is a person entrusted with property who is not liable for loss unless negligent.
2
Locate the basis of the ruling
The passage states that Abu Hanifa's ruling is based on qiyas (analogy) to the private employee (ajir khas). The private employee is also an amin and does not guarantee loss.
The shared worker and private employee share the quality of being trustees of the property.
3
Analyze the qiyas structure
Construct the qiyas: Asl (private employee) – ruling: no liability. Far' (shared worker) – ruling: same. 'Illa (effective cause): both are trustees with possession but no ownership.
Hanafi usul validates qiyas where the 'illa is present in both cases.
4
Confirm the source of the principle
The passage is placed under 'Af'al al-Nabi' (actions of the Prophet), indicating that the general principle of trust in custodianship is derived from the Prophet's practice.
Prophetic actions are a source of legislation (hujja) in Hanafi usul.
5
Note the exception
The passage declares that for unpreventable calamities (like widespread fire or general raid), there is consensus (ittifaq) that the worker does not guarantee.
This is because no negligence is attributable to the worker.

Definition of Ijma' and Its Two Pillars
Defines ijma' and its two types of rukn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ijma' from a Hanafi perspective, distinguish between its two types of rukn ('azimah and rukhsah), and explain why Hanafis accept silent consensus.

Core Idea
Ijma' is the agreement of all qualified jurists; its active pillar is explicit agreement, and its passive pillar is silent consent.

In this lesson, you will encounter the Hanafi definition of ijma', one of the four sources of Islamic law. The passage explains not only the basic concept but also the two modes in which consensus can be established: through active expression or through silent acquiescence. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping how Hanafis derive rulings when explicit agreement is absent.

Guided Path
1
Define ijma' linguistically and terminologically
Identify the lexical meaning (agreement) and the technical definition (agreement of righteous mujtahids from the Ummah). Note the conditions: all mujtahids, righteous, and from the Ummah of Muhammad.
The definition excludes laypeople and sinful individuals.
2
Distinguish the two types of rukn
Identify 'azimah (active) where all express or act, and rukhsah (passive) where some speak/act and others remain silent after a period of reflection. Memorize the Arabic terms and examples.
For 'azimah: all say 'we have agreed'. For rukhsah: scripture-like, e.g., mudarabah, muzara'ah.
3
Grasp the Hanafi argument for silent consensus
Understand that silence of capable scholars after a reasonable period (three days or a session) indicates consent due to the obligation to enjoin good and forbid evil. If they disagreed, they would have spoken.
Hanafis consider this binding (hujjah).
4
Understand the Shafi'i objection and Hanafi response
Shafi'i argues silence can be from awe, citing Ibn Abbas's silence before Umar on the issue of 'awl. Hanafis respond that Ibn Abbas was a minor then; in general, silence is consent.
The practical result: Hanafis accept silent consensus; Shafi'is do not count it as definitive evidence.

Commentary
This passage defines the concept of ijma' (consensus) and its essential pillar (rukn) according to the Hanafi school. It begins with a lexical and technical definition: linguistically, ijma' means agreement; in Islamic law, it is the agreement of all qualified jurists (mujtahids) who are upright and from the Ummah of Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) on a matter, whether it is a statement or an action. The definition excludes the agreement of laypeople and sinful jurists. The pillar (rukn) of ijma' is twofold. The first type is 'azimah (determination/active): this is when all jurists verbally express their consent by saying 'we have agreed' if the matter is verbal, or they all actually perform an act, such as engaging in mudarabah (profit-sharing), muzara'ah (sharecropping), or partnership, thereby establishing consensus on its permissibility. The second type is rukhsah (concession/passive), known as silent ijma' (ijma' sukuti): this occurs when some jurists speak or act, and the rest remain silent and do not object after a period of reflection (three days or the duration of a scholarly gathering). The Hanafi school accepts this silent consensus as binding, unlike Imam al-Shafi'i. The reason for the disagreement is that silence could be due to agreement or due to awe or fear. An example is given from Ibn Abbas, who did not openly object to Caliph Umar's ruling on 'awl (increase in shares) because of Umar's awe-inspiring presence, as narrated in a hadith. The passage clarifies that the Hanafi acceptance is based on the principle that if the silent ones were opposed, they would have spoken out given their ability; their silence is thus a strong indication of agreement, unless there is a clear sign otherwise.

الرخصة (al-Rukhsah): Passive concession: only some express or act, others remain silent
