The Hanafi Analysis of Istithna: Ibarah vs Isharah
Commentary on divisions of bayan focusing on istithna and its linguistic implications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that istithna indicates the remainder by wording (ibarah) and the negation of the excepted item by indication (isharah), and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using linguistic evidence from the Quran.

Core Idea
Istithna directly affirms the remainder; it only implies the negation of the excepted.

This passage from Noor al-Anwar delves into the usul al-fiqh concept of istithna (exception) as a division of bayan. The author reconciles two conflicting linguistic views: one that istithna primarily affirms the remainder, and another that it negates the excepted. The Hanafi school resolves this by distinguishing between ibarah (direct wording) and isharah (implication). Understanding this distinction is crucial for interpreting legal texts, contracts, and oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Conflicting Views
Read the first part of the passage where the author mentions 'affirmation and from affirmation negation.' Recognize that the debate is whether istithna indicates both the remainder (affirmation) and the negation of the excepted by its wording.
The two views are attributed to different linguists; the Hanafis side with the view that the primary function is affirming the remainder.
2
Understand the Hanafi Reconciliation
The author states: 'we say that it speaks of the remainder by its wording and affirmation and negation by its indication.' Grasp that the Hanafi position assigns the direct wording to the remainder and the implication to the negation. This is because the excepted item is not the main intended meaning.
The analogy of 'ghayah' (limit) is used: the limit is not intended from the limited; similarly, the excepted is not intended from the excepted-from.
3
Analyze the Shafi'i Objection and the Hanafi Response
The Shafi'i position holds that the three-word compound (mustathna min + adat al-istithna + mustathna) is not a typical Arabic construction, so it cannot have a conventional meaning. The Hanafi response cites examples like 'Shabba qarnaha' (a three-word proper noun) and argues that the compound is analyzed as a restrictive phrase with a type-based conventional meaning.
This linguistic argument is central to the tarjih (preference) for the Hanafi view.
4
Apply the Quranic Examples
The passage presents two Quranic verses: Surah Luqman 31:25 and Surah al-Shu'ara' 26:77. The first illustrates that polytheists affirmed Allah's existence but negated other gods (showing the negation as intended). The second shows Abraham's statement which can be interpreted as either a connected (muttasil) or disconnected (munfasil) exception. Work through how the Hanafi analysis applies to each.
The example of 'la ilaha illa Allah' is also invoked to show that the negation is the primary intended meaning in the word of tawhid.
5
Distinguish Muttasil and Munfasil Istithna
The passage concludes by distinguishing between connected exception (muttasil) and disconnected exception (munfasil). Understand that only muttasil is true istithna; munfasil is figurative because it forms an independent statement. Note the legal consequence: when istithna follows coordinated phrases, it applies to all (like a conditional according to the Shafi'i).
This distinction affects how exceptions in contracts are interpreted: Hanafis treat munfasil as a separate statement.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the divisions of bayan, specifically focusing on istithna (exception). The author reconciles two opposing linguistic views: one holds that istithna primarily affirms the remainder (the excepted-from after exclusion), the other that it negates the excepted item. The Hanafi position is that istithna indicates the remainder by its wording (ibarah) and indicates negation of the excepted item by indication (isharah). The Shafi'i position is countered using linguistic arguments and Quranic examples. The commentary then distinguishes between muttasil (connected) and munfasil (disconnected) istithna, explaining that the latter is only figuratively called istithna because it forms an independent statement. Examples from Quran (e.g., Abraham's statement) illustrate the two types. The text also discusses the rule that when istithna follows coordinated phrases, it applies to all of them, similar to a conditional statement according to Shafi'i.

Key Terms
بيان (bayan): Explanation or clarification; a term in usul al-fiqh for the modes of clarifying a legal ruling.
استثناء (istithna): Exception; a grammatical and legal device to exclude part of a general statement from its ruling.
عبارة (ibarah): Explicit wording; what is directly stated by the utterance.
إشارة (isharah): Indication; meaning conveyed implicitly or by implication, not by direct wording.
متصل (muttasil): Connected exception; where the excepted item is of the same genus as the excepted-from and could be included in the general statement.
منفصل (munfasil): Disconnected exception; where the excepted item is of a different genus and cannot be extracted from the first part; it is figuratively called istithna.

Dispute on the Scope of Istithna vs Shart: Hanafi and Shafi'i Positions
Dispute on whether istithna returns to all preceding sentences or the nearest

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule distinguishing istithna from shart to determine the scope of an exception in a series of conjuncted legal statements, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using principles of bayan taghyir.

Core Idea
Istithna applies only to the nearest clause because it contradicts the earlier ruling; shart applies to all because it only suspends.

In this passage, the author presents a dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the scope of an exception (istithna) when it appears after multiple sentences joined by 'and'. The Shafi'i school analogizes istithna to a condition (shart), arguing that both are types of 'clarification by change' (bayan taghyir) and should apply to all preceding clauses. The Hanafi school, however, distinguishes them: istithna is a mubaddil that completely overturns the previous ruling, so it can only be attached to the nearest sentence, while shart merely suspends the ruling and thus can apply to all. Understanding this difference is crucial for deriving correct rulings in contracts, divorce, and manumission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the operative texts
Locate the examples of istithna (e.g., 'except 100 from each thousand') and shart (e.g., 'if you enter the house') and note how they appear after multiple conjuncted sentences.
The Shafi'i example uses a series of thousands; the Hanafi example uses a series of divorces.
2
State the Shafi'i position
Under the principle of bayan taghyir, both istithna and shart modify the previous ruling; due to conjunction, they apply to all preceding clauses.
Al-Shafi'i holds that the waw creates a partnership that makes the modifier affect all.
3
Analyze the Hanafi distinction
Istithna is mubaddil because it negates the prior ruling entirely (e.g., from affirmation to negation). Shart only changes the modality from immediate to suspended; it does not remove the original ruling's applicability.
The Hanafi objection is that analogy (qiyas) between istithna and shart fails because the nature of change differs.
4
Apply the principle
In a series of pledges or divorces, Hanafis apply the exception only to the last named clause; Shafi'is apply it to all. The necessity for istithna's validity is satisfied by restricting it to the nearest.
The thamarah al-khilaf: practical consequence in contracts and family law.
5
Evaluate the tarjih
The Hanafi view is preferred because it preserves the operative nature of adult speech and limits the exception's contradictory effect.
The text implies the Hanafi position is correct, noting the necessity of istithna is fulfilled by the nearest clause.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute between the Hanafis and al-Shafi'i regarding the scope of istithna (exception) and shart (condition) when they appear after multiple conjuncted sentences. The author begins by stating the classification of bayan (clarification) into types: bayan taghyir (clarification by change). He reports that according to al-Shafi'i, istithna (like excepting one hundred from a series of thousands) applies to all the preceding sentences, just like a shart (condition) does. For example, if one says, 'Hind is divorced, Zaynab is divorced, Amrah is divorced if you enter the house,' then each wife's divorce is suspended on entry. This is because both istithna and shart are considered 'bayan taghyir' (clarification that changes the previous ruling), so they should have the same effect—returning to all preceding clauses.

Then the author states the Hanafi position: according to us (Hanafis), istithna only applies to the sentence immediately adjacent to it, unlike the shart. The reason is that istithna is 'mubaddil' (a changer) because it removes the later part of speech from being operative on all preceding parts—istithna contradicts the previous ruling entirely (e.g., changing affirmation to negation). If it were applied to all preceding sentences, the earlier sentences would be invalidated, which contradicts the principle that the speech of a sane adult is operative in all parts. So istithna's validity is only due to necessity (since it lacks independent meaning), and that necessity is satisfied by limiting it to the nearest sentence. In contrast, shart does not remove the original ruling entirely; it only changes it from immediate (munajjaz) to suspended (mu'allaq). Therefore, shart can apply to all preceding conjuncted sentences because the conjunction (waw) creates a partnership. Thus the Hanafi view maintains a distinction: istithna is restricted to the nearest clause, while shart affects all preceding clauses.

The text then notes an apparent contradiction in the author's own classification: earlier he counted both shart and istithna as 'bayan taghyir', but here he specifically counts shart as 'mubaddil' (showing change). The glosses resolve this by explaining that 'mubaddil' here is used linguistically as 'changer', not in the technical sense of 'bayan tabdil'. Alternatively, the author's different classifications (by Fakhral-Islam and Shams al-A'imma) reflect different madhhab interpretations; Shams al-A'imma considered shart as bayan tabdil because it replaces the original immediate effect.

The second part introduces 'bayan darurah' (clarification by necessity), which occurs by silence (sukut) rather than speech. An example is Quran 4:11: 'And if his [only] parents inherit him, then to his mother one-third.' The verse initially implies absolute partnership between parents without specifying shares, but specifying the mother's third clarifies that the father gets the remainder. This silence about the father's share is a bayan darurah because the spoken part strongly indicates the unspoken, making the unspoken in the ruling of the spoken. The commentary stresses that this type of bayan is valid and there is no difficulty in using it as proof.

The lesson demonstrates a key usul principle: the difference in how linguistic tools (istithna vs. shart) affect multiple conjuncted sentences, and how necessity (darurah) allows for clarification through what is not explicitly stated.

مبدل (mubaddil): Something that changes or replaces the ruling; here applied to istithna (it removes the governing effect from earlier parts) and also to shart (it changes immediate to suspended).

Bayan Darurah: Clarification by Necessity
Definition and example of bayan darurah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a case of bayan darurah (clarification by necessity) and explain how silence can serve as legal evidence when context strongly implies the ruling.

Core Idea
Silence becomes legal clarification when the spoken words make the unspoken meaning necessary.

The passage introduces another type of bayan: bayan darurah (clarification by necessity). Unlike bayan taghyir which actively changes a ruling, bayan darurah occurs when the speaker remains silent about a part of the ruling, but the context of the spoken words forces that silent part to be understood as if it were spoken. The classic example is from the Quran: 'And if his parents inherit him, then to his mother one-third' (4:11). The verse does not explicitly state the father's share, but the context necessitates that the father receives the remainder. This section explains that such tacit clarification is valid evidence in Hanafi usul.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish when a grammatical conjunction ('atf') functions as a clarifying bayan (bayan al-tabdil) in Hanafi usul and apply this distinction to debt acknowledgment scenarios.

Core Idea
A conjunction can clarify an omitted tamyiz only when 'urf makes the omitted word obvious—otherwise the statement remains ambiguous.

This passage explores a specific type of bayan called bayan al-tabdil, where a conjunction (like 'wa') serves to clarify an omitted specifying noun. The Hanafi school relies on customary usage ('urf) and the nature of the item (whether it is typically owed in the dhimma) to determine when the conjunction has this clarifying power, while al-Shafi'i insists that the speaker always retains the right to explain ambiguous numbers. Understanding this dispute reveals how context and custom shape legal interpretation in the Hanafi madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Core Example and its Structure
Locate the example 'I owe a hundred and a dirham' in the passage. Note that the conjunction 'wa' is claimed to clarify that the hundred refers to dirhams. Understand the logical structure: the word 'dirhams' is omitted from the first part and supplied by the conjunction + 'urf.
The phrase 'as if he said: a hundred dirhams and a dirham' shows the underlying clarity.
2
Distinguish the Two Scenarios
Contrast the dirham example with the garment example ('I owe a hundred and a garment'). In the garment case, the conjunction does not clarify because garments are not commonly owed in the dhimma except in salam. This distinction hinges on whether the item is measured/weighed and frequently transacted in debt.
The passage explicitly says: 'because a garment is not established in the dhimma except in salam.'
3
Analyze the Hanafi Proof: 'Urf and Dhimma
The Hanafi position rests on frequency of usage (kathrat al-isti'mal) and the nature of the transaction. Because people commonly say 'a hundred and ten dirhams' meaning all dirhams, the conjunction becomes a contextual indicator (qarina) for the omitted tamyiz. This applies only to items that are customarily owed in the dhimma, like measured or weighed goods.
The marginal commentary by al-Qamar emphasizes that the mind moves to the tamyiz due to the existence of the qarina in frequently used contexts.
4
Contrast the Shafi'i Objection
Al-Shafi'i argues that the conjunction cannot be a bayan because the conjoined item (dirham) is different from the conjoined-to (hundred); a mufassir must be identical to the mufassar. Therefore, the speaker always retains the right to explain what the hundred means. The Hanafi rebuttal is that the conjunction is not a direct interpretation but a qarina based on 'urf, not a literal tafsir.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) is: Hanafis oblige 101 dirhams, Shafi'is leave the hundred open to the debtor's clarification.
5
Apply the Distinction to a New Case
Test the rule: 'I owe two hundred and five dinars.' Dinars are gold coins, commonly owed in the dhimma? Yes, they are weighed and frequently transacted. According to Hanafi, the conjunction clarifies that the two hundred refers to dinars, so total is 205 dinars. If the debtor said 'two hundred and a garment,' it would not clarify because garments are not typically owed in the dhimma.
Practice this distinction to internalize the condition of 'urf and genus.

Commentary
This passage discusses another type of bayan called 'bayan al-tabdil' (clarification by substitution), which is essentially naskh (abrogation) in linguistic terms. The main example is a person who says 'I owe a hundred and a dirham.' The conjunction 'and' here serves as a clarifier (bayan) because the hundred is understood to be dirhams as well—it is as if he said 'a hundred dirhams and one dirham,' but the word 'dirhams' was omitted for brevity. This works only in transactions involving things customarily owed in the dhimma (like measured or weighed goods). In contrast, if someone says 'I owe a hundred and a garment,' the conjunction does not clarify because garments are not typically owed in the dhimma except in salam contracts; thus 'hundred' remains ambiguous (mujmal) and the debtor must clarify his intent. Al-Shafi'i disagreed, holding that even in the first example, the conjunction is not a bayan; rather, the speaker always retains the right to explain what 'hundred' means. The Hanafi position relies on 'urf and frequency: because it is common to say 'a hundred and ten dirhams' meaning all dirhams, the conjunction serves as a qarina. The commentator also clarifies that this type of bayan (where a conjunction clarifies an omitted tamyyiz) is different from bayan al-darura, and he notes an editorial suggestion about the text's structure.

Key Terms
بيان تبديل (bayan al-tabdil): A type of bayan where a conjunction or other linguistic element clarifies an omitted word, here equated with naskh.
عطف (atf): Grammatical conjunction (like 'wa') that can serve as a contextual indicator (qarina) for an omitted tamyyiz.
تمييز (tamyiz): The specifying noun (e.g., 'dirhams') that clarifies the number; it can be omitted when 'urf supplies the specification.

Bayan al-Tabdil: Abrogation as Clarification and Substitution
Defining naskh as both clarification and substitution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define bayan al-tabdil (naskh) according to the Hanafi school, distinguish its dual nature (bayan and tabdil), and construct the Hanafi response to objections against naskh.

Core Idea
Abrogation is clarification from Allah and substitution from humans—it reveals the term Allah had set.

Naskh (abrogation) is a critical concept in usul al-fiqh, but its nature is often misunderstood. In this lesson, we examine a passage from a Hanafi usul work that defines a specific form of bayan called 'bayan al-tabdil'. The author clarifies that abrogation is not a change of divine mind but a manifestation of prior knowledge, appearing as a substitution to us but as pure clarification from Allah. We will unpack the definition, explore the example of wine prohibition, and see how the Hanafis refute objections from those who deny naskh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of bayan al-tabdil
Read the opening lines where the author quotes Quranic verses and states that abrogation is clarification from one aspect and substitution from another. Note the dual perspective: divine and human.
The key phrase is 'بيان من وجه وتبديل من وجه' (clarification from one aspect and substitution from another).
2
Analyze the example of wine permission
The author uses the example of wine being initially permitted absolutely, then prohibited. Understand how this illustrates the absolute ruling that was known to Allah to have a term, but was made absolute in revelation so that humans perceived it as permanent.
3
Understand the analogy of killing
The author compares naskh to a person killing another: it clarifies the predetermined time of death (divine perspective) but appears to cut short life (human perspective). Grasp how this reinforces the dual nature.
4
Examine the Hanafi proof for the permissibility of naskh
The author cites Quranic verses (16:101, 2:106) as primary evidence. Then consider the rational argument: Allah is wise and acts according to knowledge and interests of servants, like a physician changing a prescription.
5
Evaluate the objection and the Hanafi response
The text mentions the Jewish objection that naskh implies foolishness. Note the Hanafi rebuttal: it is based on divine wisdom, not ignorance. Recognize that this defense also applies to internal Muslim objections.

Commentary
This passage is a section from a Hanafi usul work defining a specific type of bayan called 'bayan al-tabdil' (clarification through substitution), which is essentially the abrogation (naskh) of a ruling. The author begins by quoting two Quranic verses: 'When We substitute one verse in place of another' (Surah an-Nahl, 16:101) and 'Whatever verse We abrogate or cause to be forgotten' (Surah al-Baqarah, 2:106). He explains that these two verses are one in meaning regarding the concept of naskh. The core teaching is that abrogation has a dual nature: from Allah's perspective, it is pure clarification (bayan) of the original term He had set for the ruling, which was known to Him but not revealed to people. From the human perspective, it appears as a substitution (tabdil) because the ruling is changed unexpectedly. The author uses the example of wine: it was initially permitted absolutely; in our understanding, it would remain permitted forever. But Allah knew He would prohibit it later. When the prohibition came, it felt like a substitution, but in divine knowledge it was merely the end of the original term. To illustrate, the author compares naskh to killing a person: the killer's act clarifies the precise moment of death (known to Allah) but seems to cut short life from a human viewpoint. The passage then defends the permissibility of naskh against Jewish objections, citing the Quranic text and using a rational argument: Allah is wise and acts according to His knowledge of what is beneficial for His servants, just as a doctor changes a prescription daily based on the patient's condition. The author affirms that naskh is permissible and has occurred in Islamic law.

Key Terms
بيان التبديل (bayan al-tabdil): A type of bayan where a ruling is abrogated, appearing as substitution to humans but as clarification from Allah.
النسخ (al-naskh): The removal of a legal ruling by a later divine text, or the replacement of one ruling with another.
الحكم المطلق (al-hukm al-mutlaq): The ruling that was not explicitly limited to a duration in the text, giving the appearance of permanence.
السفاهة (al-safahah): Foolishness; the quality attributed to Allah by the opponents of naskh, which the Hanafis refute.
