Ihtiyat over Qiyas: Fidya for Missed Prayers
Explaining why fidya for prayers is based on ihtiyat, not qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi preference for ihtiyat over qiyas in ambiguous worship rulings and apply it to the case of fidya for missed prayers.

Core Idea
When textual evidence is ambiguous, precaution (ihtiyat) can establish a ruling where analogy would hesitate.

This passage addresses a subtle point in Hanafi usul: can we extend a textual ruling to a new case when the text's rationale is unclear? For fidya (ransom) in fasting, the Quran gives a fixed penalty for the decrepit. When it comes to missed prayers, some might argue that analogy (qiyas) is insufficient. The author clarifies that the Hanafi school relies on precaution (ihtiyat) to require fidya for prayers, a principle that prioritizes worship and caution over strict analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal problem
The implied question: fidya for fasting is text-based (unintelligible), so why extend it to prayers? Recognize this as a challenge to the Hanafi position.
2
Contrast qiyas and ihtiyat
Understand that pure qiyas would require a known cause (illa) from the text. Since the text is ambiguous (could be specific or have a general cause), qiyas is weak. Ihtiyat (precaution) steps in to ensure the act of worship is not neglected.
3
Analyze the text's ambiguity
The Quranic text (2:184) mentions fidya for those unable to fast. It could be limited to fasting, or it could indicate a general cause: incapacity. This ambiguity is the key that triggers ihtiyat.
The text is qati al-thubut but zanni al-dalala.
4
See the takbir analogy
The author uses the example of takbirs: from the takbirs of bowing (established), the Hanafi school argued for other takbirs by ihtiyat because they share a genre. This illustrates how ihtiyat works in worship.
The principle: if a genre of worship is prescribed in one context, precaution suggests performing it in similar contexts.
5
Derive the ruling
Conclusion: fidya for prayers is wajib (obligatory) on the heir when the deceased bequeathed it, based on ihtiyat and the text's indication, not on pure qiyas.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage addresses a potential objection: how can fidya be required for missed prayers when the only textual basis is for fasting? The author clarifies that the ruling is not based on analogy (qiyas) but on precaution (ihtiyat) and the text's broader implication. The text on fasting (Quran 2:184) mentions fidya for those unable to fast. This could be specific to fasting, or it could indicate a general cause (incapacity) that applies to prayer as well. Since the evidence is ambiguous, the Hanafi school applies ihtiyat to require fidya for prayers, especially because prayer is more significant than fasting. The passage also references the earlier discussion on takbirs to show that ihtiyat can dictate rulings where analogy would differ. The conclusion is that fidya for prayers is established by precautionary reasoning, and the authority of Imam Muhammad's statement in al-Ziyadat supports this, as it uses the phrase 'If Allah wills' which is not used in pure analogical rulings.

الفدية (al-fidya): A monetary ransom given in place of a missed act of worship when one is unable to perform it.
الاحتياط (al-ihtiyat): Precautionary measure in law, used here to establish fidya without relying solely on analogy.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning; the passage argues that fidya is not based on pure qiyas but on ihtiyat.

Usul of Daman al-Manafi' in Ghasp: Refuting the Shafi'i Qiyas
Hanafi refutation of Shafi'i on liability of usufructs in usurpation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that usufructs in usurpation are not guaranteed by property, and refute the Shafi'i analogy to lease by explaining the necessary condition of consent (rida) in establishing guarantee.

Core Idea
Usufructs are not guaranteed in usurpation because guarantee requires consent, which aggression does not provide.

This lesson examines a central ikhtilaf between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: whether a usurper must compensate for usufructs consumed (e.g., riding a stolen animal). The Hanafi school holds that usufructs are not guaranteed at all, neither by destruction nor consumption, while the Shafi'i school analogizes to lease. You will learn the precise distinction between usufructs and accessions, and the usul principle that consent (rida) can impose property for non-property, but aggression has no such effect.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi claim and Shafi'i objection
The passage begins by stating the Hanafi position: usufructs in usurpation are not guaranteed with property (lā yaḍmān bi al-māl). The Shafi'i objection is that they should be guaranteed by analogy to lease, where usufructs are guaranteed by rent according to custom.
The Shafi'i qiyas is based on the similarity that both involve consumption of usufructs.
2
Analyze the Hanafi proof based on consent (rida)
The Hanafi proof: Consent can impose property for non-property (e.g., sulh on intentional homicide) and even surplus and usufructs by mutual agreement. Aggression (udwan) has no such effect. Therefore, in lease where consent exists, usufructs are guaranteed; in usurpation without consent, they are not.
The key is that guarantee by property requires a legitimate basis (sabab); consent is such a basis, aggression is not.
3
Distinguish between usufructs (manafi') and accessions (zawa'id)
The text defines usufructs as non-substantial benefits (riding, dwelling) and accessions as offspring, milk, fruit. The ruling: the principal asset is guaranteed by both destruction and consumption; accessions are guaranteed by consumption only; usufructs are guaranteed by neither.
This distinction is crucial because accessions have permanence (baqa') and are considered property, unlike usufructs which are fleeting (a'rad).
4
Examine the refutation of the Shafi'i counter-argument
The Shafi'i might argue that in accidental homicide, property (blood money) is due despite aggression (the killing is unintentional but still an aggression). The Hanafi response: that is not due to pure aggression but because of khatar al-mahal (the danger of the life going unavenged) — a special case by text against qiyas, not analogizable.
This rebuttal preserves the principle that aggression alone cannot create property liability.
5
Explain the condition of ihtiraz for taqawwum
The Hanafi require ihtiraz (containment/preparation) for something to be considered valuta (mutaqawwim). Wild grass in the wilderness lacks ihtiraz, hence not property. The Shafi'i rejects this, saying taqawwum depends on ownership and free disposal. This difference affects the scope of what is considered property.
This part of the passage responds to a broader theoretical objection.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul al-fiqh discussion on two related issues: the guarantee of usufructs in usurpation and the guarantee of qisas when a third party kills the killer. The commentary is a refutation of al-Shafi'i's positions. The Hanafi author first establishes that usufructs (manafi') are not guaranteed by destruction (halak) or consumption (istihlak), unlike the principal asset (ayn) and accessions (zawa'id). The reasoning is that guarantee by property requires consent (rida), present in lease but absent in usurpation which is aggression. The objection that in homicide by error property is due despite aggression is rebutted: there it is due by necessity (khatar al-mahal) to prevent the blood of a protected person from going unavenged, not by pure aggression. The passage then distinguishes usufructs from accessions: accessions are guaranteed by consumption but not destruction, usufructs by neither. The later note mentions a fatwa exception for waqf and orphan property based on a riwaya from Imam Abu Hanifa. The second issue: qisas (retaliation) is not guaranteed by property because it is not a substantiated property (ghayr mutaqawwim). If an outsider kills the qisas-killer, he does not owe blood money to the victim's heirs, contrary to al-Shafi'i who analogizes from accidental killing. The Hanafi response: blood money in accidental killing is established against qiyas (by special permission) to avoid waste of blood, so it cannot be analogized to the case of qisas. The passage ends with agreement that the outsider is not liable for qisas itself, but only for the life of the killer he took.

Conditions for Leaving Haqiqah for Majaz
Explanation of when literal meaning is abandoned for metaphorical meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of abandoning haqiqah for majaz in cases of impossibility (muta'dhir) and abandonment (mahjur) and identify the closest metaphorical meaning.

Core Idea
When the literal meaning is impossible or customarily abandoned, Hanafi usul requires adopting the nearest metaphorical meaning to avoid invalidating the statement.

In Hanafi usul al-Fiqh, the default is to interpret words literally (haqiqah). However, when the literal meaning is physically or customarily impossible (muta'dhir) or abandoned by usage (mahjur), the interpreter must shift to a metaphorical meaning (majaz). This lesson from Noor al-Anwar explains these conditions using oath examples and addresses critical refinements by later commentators. By analyzing the rule and its application, you will learn how to determine the intended meaning when the literal sense fails.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Identify the core principle: when the literal meaning is muta'dhir (impossible) or mahjur (abandoned), the metaphorical meaning is adopted.
The rule is not limited to oaths; it applies to any utterance.
2
Define the two conditions
Define muta'dhir as that which cannot be performed except with great difficulty (e.g., eating a palm tree), and mahjur as that which is possible but people have abandoned (e.g., entering barefoot).
These definitions are from the Hanafi tradition; mahjur relies on 'urf (custom).
3
Apply to the oath of the palm tree
Consider the oath 'I will not eat from this palm tree.' Since eating the tree itself is impossible, the metaphorical meaning (the fruit) is intended. If no fruit, the price is intended. Literally eating the trunk does not break the oath.
The impossibility here is customary, not logical; one could physically bite wood, but customarily it is not considered eating.
4
Examine the critique of the example
Al-Qamar criticizes the example of a palm tree without fruit, noting that every palm tree bears fruit and that the oleander is not a palm. This shows that the illustration is flawed but the principle stands.
This critique does not undermine the rule; it only questions the example's correctness.
5
Understand the purpose behind the shift
Some commentators (e.g., al-Sanbali) argue that the real reason for leaving haqiqah in oaths is to make the oath effective (kaf al-nafs), not simply impossibility. This highlights the pragmatic dimension of the rule.
This nuance is important for understanding the rule's application in affirmative vs. negative oaths.

Commentary
This passage explains the usul rule of when to abandon the literal meaning (haqiqah) for the metaphorical meaning (majaz). Two conditions trigger this shift: muta'dhir (impossible) and mahjur (abandoned by custom). For muta'dhir, the literal meaning is physically or customarily impossible to perform; e.g., an oath 'not to eat from this palm tree' cannot literally be fulfilled because one cannot eat the tree itself. Therefore, the oath is interpreted metaphorically as the fruit of the tree, or if no fruit, its market value. If someone literally tries to eat the wood, they do not break the oath because the literal act is impossible and not the intended meaning. For mahjur, the literal meaning is possible but people no longer use it; e.g., an oath 'not to put one's foot in a house' where putting a bare foot is not the customary way. So the metaphorical meaning (e.g., entering) is intended. The passage also contains critical commentary: al-Sanbali notes that the real reason for leaving haqiqah in the oath example is not impossibility but to make the oath effective in restraining oneself; al-Qamar points out inconsistencies in the example of a palm tree without fruit, since palm trees always have fruit. The relied-upon Hanafi principle is that the metaphorical meaning is resorted to when it is the closest to the literal meaning, to avoid nullifying the oath.

Key Terms
متعذر (muta'dhir): Something impossible to reach or perform except with great difficulty; here, literal meaning is impossible to fulfill (e.g., eating a palm tree).
مهجور (mahjur): Something that is possible but abandoned by people in usage; here, literal meaning is abandoned (e.g., putting foot barefoot in house).
مجاز (majaz): Metaphorical meaning; the usage of a word in a sense other than its original/literal one for a contextual reason.

Abandoning Haqiqah for Majaz: When Literal Meaning is Deserted
Explains the rule and gives two applications in agency and oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that a word's literal meaning is abandoned when custom or Shariah abandons it, and identify when metaphorical interpretation is mandatory.

Core Idea
When literal meaning is abandoned by custom or Shariah, the word is interpreted metaphorically—even if literal is possible.

In Hanafi usul, words are normally taken in their literal sense. However, a crucial exception arises when the literal meaning is effectively deserted by people (custom) or by the Lawgiver (Shariah). This lesson unpacks that rule, showing how it overrides strict analogy and governs real-world cases like agency appointments and oaths. You will see how istihsan and urf interact with haqiqah and majaz.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Read the opening: when a literal meaning is abandoned by custom (urf) or Shariah, the word is interpreted metaphorically. Identify the two triggers: custom and Shariah.
The rule applies even if the literal meaning is technically possible.
2
Analyze the agency example
Follow the reasoning: an agent appointed to 'contest' (khusumat) is given broad authority to answer (jawab), including admission. Understand why: the literal meaning (denial only) is abandoned by Shariah (false denial is haram). Note the dispute with Zufar and al-Shafi'i who apply qiyas instead.
This is istihsan overriding qiyas.
3
Analyze the oath example
If someone swears 'I will not speak to this child', the oath applies permanently because avoiding a child (not speaking) is abandoned by Shariah (based on the hadith). Contrast with 'a child' indefinite which is restricted to childhood.
The description 'child' in the definite form is incidental, not the motive.
4
Identify the evidence
Locate the two proofs: Quran 8:46 (prohibiting false dispute) for the agency case, and the hadith 'Not of us...' (Tirmidhi) for the oath case. See how they establish Shariah's abandonment of the literal meaning.
The hadith is zanni in transmission and meaning.
5
Apply the rule to new scenarios
Using the same logic, consider other examples: swearing not to eat meat (pork is excluded) or not to drink (khamr is excluded). Confirm that words are interpreted metaphorically when literal is abandoned.
The rule is a key tool in Hanafi fatwa.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a Hanafi usul principle: when the literal meaning of a word is abandoned—whether by custom (urf) or by Shariah (because the act is prohibited or reprehensible)—the word is interpreted metaphorically. The author first states this rule: if people have abandoned the literal meaning (e.g., saying 'enter' but only placing the foot is not considered entering), the intended meaning is the metaphorical one. Similarly, if Shariah abandons a literal meaning (e.g., false denial in litigation), the word is also treated as a metaphor. Two applications follow. First, in agency: if a person appoints an agent 'to contest' (khusumat) a plaintiff, the agent's authority extends to any answer—including admission—because the literal meaning of contestation (only denial) is abandoned by Shariah (false denial is haram). This is based on istihsan, not strict qiyas. Zufar and al-Shafi'i disagree, using qiyas: admission contradicts the appointment. The Hanafi response: the literal meaning is abandoned, so the word is a metaphor for any answer; thus the agent's admission is valid. Second, an oath: if someone swears 'I will not speak to this child', it applies permanently to the person's essence, even after he grows up. Because avoiding children (not showing mercy) is abandoned by Shariah based on the hadith 'Not of us...' Thus the description 'child' is not the intent; the oath is on the essence. But if he says 'a child' indefinitely, it is restricted to childhood because the description is then the motive (e.g., to avoid a foolish child). The objection that interpreting by essence leads to multiple sins (abandoning mercy, not honoring elders, severing ties) is answered: these are incidental, not intended, so they do not affect the interpretation. The hadith quoted is a proof that abandoning a child (by not speaking) is reprehensible, hence the literal meaning of 'child' is abandoned. This lesson shows how 'istihsan' and 'urf' (and shari'ah abandonment) override literal meanings.

Explains Hanafi ruling on khiyar al-ta'yin via istihsan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of istihsan to validate khiyar al-ta'yin and evaluate the Shafi'i objection based on qiyas.

Core Idea
Non-disputative uncertainty in contracts is tolerated by istihsan when need justifies departure from strict analogy.

This lesson explores a classic usul dispute: the validity of khiyar al-ta'yin (option of specification). The Hanafi school upholds it by istihsan, while Zufar and al-Shafi'i reject it by qiyas due to uncertainty (jahala). Understanding this dispute sharpens your grasp of how istihsan functions as a source of law, and why not all uncertainty invalidates a contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling and the issue
Khiyar al-ta'yin allows a party to specify which of two or three items the contract applies to after the contract is made. The core issue is whether this uncertainty (jahala) invalidates the contract.
The option can cover up to three items only; a fourth is unnecessary.
2
Understand the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that khiyar al-ta'yin is valid by istihsan. This is based on a hidden analogy to khiyar al-shart (option of condition), which is established by text. The uncertainty is non-disputative because the party holding the option is known.

3
Understand the opposing position
Zufar (a Hanafi dissenter) and al-Shafi‘i reject khiyar al-ta'yin by qiyas. They analogize it to other contracts where uncertainty invalidates, arguing that jahala necessarily leads to dispute and vitiates consent.
This shows a difference in how broadly the prohibition of gharar is applied.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response distinguishes between disputative and non-disputative uncertainty. Here, the uncertainty is limited and the option holder is specified, so no dispute arises. Moreover, need (haja) allows a departure from strict qiyas – the very basis of istihsan.
Istihsan is an exception to qiyas, not arbitrary preference; it relies on a stronger hidden analogy.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf and tarjih
The practical consequence: Hanafis permit khiyar al-ta‘yin in sales and leases; Shafi‘is do not. The mufta bihi in the Hanafi school is the validity, confirmed by the text's phrasing 'fasahha istihsanan'.

Khiyar al-Ta'yin in Mahr: Benefit as the Condition for Valid Choice
Usul rule with internal intra-madhhab ikhtilaf and talil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that khiyar al-ta'yin requires benefit (naf') to determine the validity of a mahr condition, and derive the resulting ruling when the condition is invalid, including the intra-madhhab difference.

Core Idea
Khiyar al-ta'yin in mahr is valid only when each option involves benefit; otherwise, the lesser is obligatory, based on bara'at al-dhimma.

In this lesson, we examine how the Hanafi school applies the option of specification (khiyar al-ta'yin) to marriage dower (mahr). The key usul question is: what conditions make the choice legally effective? The passage from Noor al-Anwaar explains that the choice must bring benefit to the person choosing—otherwise the principle of immunity of liability (bara'at al-dhimma) requires the lesser amount. We'll trace the reasoning (talil) of the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and contrast it with Abu Hanifa's view when the items differ in value.

Guided Path
1
Identify the condition for valid khiyar al-ta'yin

Apply the condition to the two scenarios
First scenario: valid choice (e.g., 1000 dirhams or 100 dinars) → husband may give either. If one slave is defective (or inferior), the two companions rule the lesser value is obligatory (since no real benefit). 4
Extract the usul principle and its application
The underlying principle is that khiyar must be beneficial, otherwise it is void (the lesser is due). The ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the two companions turns on whether the wife's implicit consent to the marriage (rida bi al-nikah) can override the lack of benefit in the choice. Oath Particles: Ba vs. Anna and the Requirement of Truth
Differentiating the effect of ba vs. anna in conditional oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply Hanafi istinbat to distinguish between ba and anna in conditional oaths and determine when the oath is binding based on the truth of the news.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, ba requires adhesion (ilṣāq) so the news must be true; anna does not, so even false news triggers the oath.

This passage explores a subtle but critical distinction in the language of conditional oaths. The choice between the preposition ba and the conjunction anna changes the legal outcome when the informant lies. Understanding this distinction requires applying Hanafi usul principles to identify how each particle affects the condition. By the end, you'll be able to analyze similar linguistic constructions and derive rulings accordingly.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two particles
Locate the phrases 'bi-qudūmi fulānin' (with ba) and 'anna fulānan qadima' (with anna) in the passage. Note that both introduce the condition of informing about someone's arrival.
The same verb 'akhbartani' is used with both particles.
2
Analyse the meaning of ba
The passage explains that ba conveys 'ilṣāq' (adhesion): the news must be 'attached' to the actual arrival. Without actual arrival, there is no attachment.
This is the original meaning (haqiqah) of ba according to Hanafi linguistics.
3
Apply ba to the ruling
Since the condition requires attachment, the oath is only violated if the news is true. If the informant lies, the condition is not met.
Thus the slave is not freed if the news is false.
4
Analyse anna
Contrastingly, anna does not imply adhesion. It simply introduces the content of the report with no connection to reality.
Therefore, the condition is fulfilled regardless of truth or falsehood.
5
Compare the outcomes
With ba, the oath-taker is bound only if the news is true; with anna, the oath-taker is bound even if the news is false.
This is a clear example of how a single particle changes the legal effect.

Ruling on Rukhsah for Duress and Coercion
Explains suspension of prohibition for mudtar/mukrah with evidence and disagreement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a genuine istithnāʾ from a general prohibition lifts the prohibition itself, and evaluate the opposing view that it only lifts accountability.

Core Idea
Duress lifts the prohibition itself, not just accountability — crucial for oaths and other secondary rulings.

This passage delves into the Hanafi treatment of legal concessions (rukhṣah) under necessity. Using Quran 6:119, the author demonstrates that the exception ‘except what you are compelled to’ removes the prohibition entirely for the person in duress (muḍṭarr) and the coerced (mukrah). The discussion contrasts the case with coercion to disbelief (Quran 16:106) where the exception is from punishment only, and highlights a disagreement between Abu Hanifa and a narration from Abu Yusuf and al-Shafiʿi. The practical consequence appears in cases like vows and oaths.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source text and its structure
Locate the Quranic verse cited: 'He has detailed for you what He has forbidden to you except that which you are compelled to' (6:119). Recognize that 'except that which you are compelled to' is an istithnāʐ connected to 'what He has forbidden'.
In Arabic, the exception governs the preceding general term directly.
2
Classify the textual category
The verse is a general prohibition (ʿāmm) with an explicit exception (istithnāʾ). In Hanafi usul, an istithnāʾ from a prohibition removes the ruling (ḥurma) from the excepted case entirely.
3
Apply the Hanafi rule of istithnāʾ
The rule: al-istithnāʾ min al-ḥurma yufīdu rafʿa al-ḥurma. Thus, during duress, the prohibition does not apply at all. Contrast with coercion to disbelief (16:106) where the exception is from punishment, not the prohibition.
This distinction is key: the same grammatical structure may serve different functions based on context.
4
Examine the disagreement and its basis
Note that Abu Yusuf and al-Shafiʿi (in one narration) hold that the prohibition remains but accountability is removed, analogizing to coercion to kufr. The Hanafi response is that the exception in 6:119 is explicitly from the prohibition itself, not from punishment.
The analogy fails because the context of the two verses differs.
5
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The dispute matters for cases like an oath not to consume haram: if one drinks wine under duress, the Hanafi school says the oath is not broken (since the wine is not haram for that person), while the opposing view says the oath is broken.
This shows how a usul disagreement translates into different fiqh rulings.

Only Tamyīz Required for Non-Binding Reports
Usul rule on conditions for non-binding reports

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a report is non-binding and apply the Hanafi rule that only discernment (tamyīz) is required for its acceptance, without conditions of ʿadālah, number, or wording.

Core Idea
Non-binding reports need only tamyīz; requiring full conditions would disrupt society.

In the previous lessons, we saw that binding testimony requires numerous conditions – uprightness, number, specific wording, etc. But what about reports that simply inform you of an optional matter, like a gift or agency? The Hanafi school here introduces a crucial distinction based on the nature of the report. This passage explains that when a report carries no legal imposition, the stringent conditions are dropped, and only basic discernment (tamyīz) is needed. The reasoning stems from the principle of istiḥsān, which prevents hardship in daily transactions.

Guided Path
1
Classify the report by its legal effect
Determine whether the report imposes an obligation (ilzām) on the recipient or merely informs about an optional matter. Binding reports require full witness conditions; non-binding ones do not.

2
Identify the minimum condition for the reporter
For non-binding reports, the reporter need only be discerning (mumayyiz) – able to understand and convey the message correctly. Age, religion, freedom, and uprightness are irrelevant.
Tamyīz is the basic capacity to distinguish and communicate.
3
Understand the rationale: istiḥsān and ʿurf
The text gives two reasons: (a) practical necessity – finding a perfectly reliable messenger is rare; (b) since the report is non-binding, its conditions are light. This is a clear application of istiḥsān, overriding strict qiyas that would require full witness qualifications.
Istiḥsān here preserves public interest (maṣlaḥa) by facilitating everyday dealings.

Although tamyīz suffices, some Hanafi authorities (e.g., al-Sarakhsī) add that one should scrutinize (taḥarrī) the reporter's reliability when acting on the report. Others (in al-Jāmiʿ al-Ṣaghīr) do not require this. The passage presents both narrations without a final tarjīḥ.
