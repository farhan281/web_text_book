Obligation of Following a Companion's Opinion
States the ruling and intra-school dispute on taqlid al-sahabi

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on taqlid al-sahabi and evaluate the intra-Hanafi disagreement with al-Karkhi regarding its scope.

Core Idea
Following a Companion's opinion is obligatory and overrides qiyas due to possible prophetic transmission and their unique insight.

The authority of a Companion's opinion (qawl al-sahabi) is a debated source in Hanafi usul. This passage presents the dominant view that it is wajib and takes precedence over qiyas, and contrasts it with al-Karkhi's limitation. Understanding this dispute sharpens your ability to rank sources in istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dominant Hanafi position
Note that the author states following a Companion is obligatory (wajib) and abandons qiyas because of it. The reasoning includes possibility of hearing from the Prophet and the Companions' superior knowledge.
Distinguish between the Companion's own qiyas (not abandoned by another Companion) and later qiyas.
2
Identify al-Karkhi's opposing view
Al-Karkhi argues that taqlid of a Companion is obligatory only in matters beyond qiyas. In matters amenable to qiyas, the Companion's opinion may be his own ijtihad and could be erroneous.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf, not across madhhabs.
3
Analyze the proofs and response
The author's proof: even if the opinion is not explicitly heard from the Prophet, the possibility suffices, and even if it's the Companion's own view, it is stronger due to their special status. Al-Karkhi focuses on uncertainty when qiyas can be applied.
Note the epistemological weight: 'possibility of hearing' is treated as strong presumption.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: in a case where a Companion's statement contradicts a valid qiyas, the first view requires following the Companion, while al-Karkhi allows following qiyas.
Consider a modern scenario: a Companion's report on a transaction method vs. analogical reasoning from general principles.
5
Apply the taqlid definition
The passage defines taqlid: following another without examining evidence, as if placing their statement as a necklace. This clarifies that the muqallid does not independently verify the dalil.
This definition applies to taqlid of mujtahids generally; here specifically to Companions.

Statement on following the Companion. Following the Companion is obligatory, and qiyas is abandoned because of it—i.e., the qiyas of the followers and those after them; because the qiyas of a Companion is not abandoned by the statement of another Companion due to the possibility of hearing from the Messenger, rather it is apparent in his case even if he did not attribute it [to the Prophet]. Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute regarding the authority of a Companion's opinion (qawl al-sahabi) in the Hanafi school. The author (presumably the muharrir) asserts that following a Companion's opinion is obligatory (wajib) and that it takes precedence over qiyas (analogical reasoning) derived by later scholars. The primary reason is the possibility that the Companion heard the ruling directly from the Prophet, even if not explicitly attributed. Moreover, Companions have a unique advantage as eyewitnesses to revelation. Al-Karkhi, a major Hanafi usuli, limits this obligation to matters beyond the reach of qiyas, arguing that where qiyas can be applied, the Companion's statement may be his own ijtihad and thus fallible. The passage also defines taqlid as unquestioning acceptance of another's opinion without examining the evidence.

Key Terms
تقليد (taqlid): Following another person's statement or action without examining the evidence, assuming one is correct.
صحابي (sahabi): A Companion of the Prophet; specifically a mujtahid Companion as mentioned in the passage.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning in Islamic law; here it refers to qiyas by later scholars (tabi'un and after).

Taqlid of a Tabi'i: When a Follower Becomes an Authority
Explanation of the ruling on following a tabi'i with a fatwa in the Sahaba's era

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on taqlid of a tabi'i whose fatwa appeared in the Sahaba's time, identify the evidence from the Sahaba's practice, and distinguish the operative ruling from the weak narration.

Core Idea
A tabi'i whose fatwa appeared among the Sahaba is followed like a Sahaba himself.

In this passage from Noor al-Anwar, we examine the status of a tabi'i (the generation after the Companions) when his legal opinion was known and accepted during the time of the Sahaba. Does his ruling carry binding authority? The Hanafi school answers yes, based on the practice of the Sahaba themselves. We will explore the evidence from the actions of Ali (RA) and Masruq, and understand how this position fits within the broader usul framework of taqlid and ijma.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal question
What is the specific issue being addressed? Is taqlid (following) of a tabi'i obligatory, permissible, or prohibited? The passage focuses on the scenario where the tabi'i's fatwa appeared during the lifetime of the Sahaba.
The tabi'i in question is like Shuraih and Masruq, whose rulings were known and not rejected by the Sahaba.
2
State the Hanafi position (Zahir al-Riwaya)
The correct and operative view is that such a tabi'i is like a Sahaba, so his taqlid is obligatory. This is the mufta bihi position.
Contrast with the weak narration from Abu Hanifa (Nawadir) which rejects taqlid of a tabi'i.
3
Analyze the primary dalil: the story of Ali (RA) and Shuraih
Ali (RA) submitted his case to Shuraih, and Shuraih rejected the testimony of Ali's son. Ali did not rebuke Shuraih, indicating acceptance of his ijtihad. This tacit approval is evidence that the tabi'i's ruling is authoritative.
The hadith is narrated by Ibn 'Asakir and cited in Suyuti's Tarikh al-Khulafa'.
4
Examine the secondary dalil: Masruq and Ibn Abbas
Masruq (a tabi'i) opposed Ibn Abbas on the ruling of a vow to slaughter a son. Masruq held that only a sheep is due, while Ibn Abbas said one hundred camels. When Masruq's opinion became known, no Sahaba objected, and even Ibn Abbas later agreed. This silence constitutes tacit ijma.
This demonstrates that a tabi'i's opinion can become part of the consensus of the Sahaba.
5
Address the intra-Hanafi dispute and tarjih
A weak narration from Abu Hanifa states he does not follow tabi'is. However, the relied-upon position (Zahir al-Riwaya) is that taqlid of such a tabi'i is obligatory. The tarjih is based on the stronger evidence from the Sahaba's practice.
Imam Sarakhsi (Shams al-A'imma) is said to have chosen the view of not doing taqlid, but the later Hanafi consensus follows the Zahir al-Riwaya.

Commentary
This passage from Noor al-Anwar explains the ruling on following (taqlid) a tabi'i (the generation after the Sahaba). It first states that the third opinion resulting from the ikhtilaf of the Sahaba is invalid by consensus. Then it addresses the tabi'i: if his fatwa emerged during the time of the Sahaba, is he like them? The correct view is yes, so his opinion must be followed. The proof is the story of Ali (RA) and Shuraih: Ali submitted his case to Shuraih, and Shuraih rejected the testimony of Ali's son but accepted that of his freed slave. Ali did not object, showing that he accepted Shuraih's ijtihad. Similarly, Masruq opposed Ibn Abbas, and no one rebuked him, indicating ijma. However, there is a dispute: Abu Hanifa (RA) reportedly said he does not do taqlid of a tabi'i (in a weak narration), but the relied-upon position (Zahir al-Riwaya) is that taqlid of such a tabi'i is obligatory. The text also clarifies that if the tabi'i's fatwa did not appear in the Sahaba's time, his opinion is not binding. Additionally, the issue of whether a tabi'i's opinion counts in forming ijma among the Sahaba is discussed: the Hanafis count it, while al-Shafi'i does not.

Key Terms
تابعي (tābi‘ī): A member of the generation after the Sahaba who met the Sahaba and learned from them.
تقليد (taqlīd): Following the legal ruling of a mujtahid without knowing the evidence.
إجماع (ijmā‘): Consensus of the mujtahids of the Muslim community on a legal ruling.
