Definition of Qa'ida and Syllogistic Method
Defines qa'ida and demonstrates syllogistic deduction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define a qa'ida (legal maxim) and apply the syllogistic method (qiyas jaliy) to derive rulings for particular cases from a universal rule.

Core Idea
A qa'ida is a universal proposition from which rulings for its particulars are derived through syllogism.

This passage defines the foundational concept of qa'ida (legal maxim) in Hanafi usul and then demonstrates the syllogistic method for deriving rulings from it. Understanding this will enable you to see how general principles generate specific fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1

2
Understand the inference method
The author provides a syllogistic method: take a universal rule as the major premise, a particular instance as the minor premise, and deduce the ruling (conclusion) for that instance.
This is a clear syllogism (qiyas jaliy).
3
Apply to grammatical example
The universal rule 'Every doer (fa'il) is nominative (marfu')' is applied to the particular 'Zayd is a doer in 'Zayd came'' to conclude 'Zayd is nominative'.

The universal usul rule 'Everything commanded is obligatory (wajib)' is applied to the particular 'Prayer is commanded' to conclude 'Prayer is obligatory'.

The same method applies to zakah, fasting, and hajj: each is commanded, so each is wajib.

Definition of Qiyas as Self-Necessitating Evidence
Defining qiyas as a compound of two premises that self-necessarily entail a conclusion, distinguishing it from istiqra and tamthil

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qiyas as a self-necessitating composite of two premises and distinguish it from istiqra' and tamthil by explaining the role of the additional premise.

Core Idea
Qiyas: two premises self-necessarily entail conclusion; otherwise not qiyas.

In defining qiyas, Hanafi usul scholars emphasize its logical structure. This passage presents the definition as a 'statement composed of two statements that self-necessarily entails another statement.' The key phrase 'self-necessarily' (lidhatihi) excludes induction and analogy. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly applying qiyas as a valid proof in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Parse the definition components
Break down 'qawl mu'allaf min qawlayn yastilzimu lidhatihi qawlan akhar' into: two premises, conclusion, and the condition of self-necessitation.
2
Understand 'self-necessarily' (lidhatihi)
This means the entailment is intrinsic; no external premise is required for the conclusion to follow from the premises. Contrast with reasoning that needs an extra step.
In Hanafi usul, this ensures qiyas is a valid self-contained proof.
3
Exclude istiqra' and tamthil
Induction (istiqra') requires an additional premise about completeness of enumeration. Analogy (tamthil) requires a premise establishing similarity between cases. Neither is self-necessitating.
This ihtiraz distinguishes qiyas from other argument forms.
4
Apply to a fiqh example
Consider the qiyas of date wine (nabidh) to grape wine (khamr): Major premise: 'Every intoxicant is prohibited.' Minor premise: 'Date wine is an intoxicant.' Conclusion: 'Date wine is prohibited.' This follows necessarily. If one argued from many intoxicants, that would be istiqra'.
You can test other examples to see if the entailment is self-necessitating.

Commentary
This passage presents the third definition of evidence, which is specific to qiyās (analogical syllogism). The definition states: 'Two statements that self-necessarily entail another statement.' The author explains each part. 'Statement' (qawl) means a complete informative composite. 'Composed of two statements' clarifies that it consists of two propositions. The crucial phrase 'self-necessarily entails' (yastilzimu lidhātihī) serves as a reservation (ihtirāz) to exclude induction (istiqrā') and analogy (tamthīl). Unlike qiyās, induction and analogy do not by themselves necessitate the conclusion; they require an additional premise—for example, in induction, one must first enumerate all instances to derive a general rule. Thus, qiyās is distinct because its internal logical structure (two premises and a middle term) directly produces the conclusion without external support. This understanding is fundamental in Hanafi usul, where qiyās is considered a valid but probable source of law, provided its conditions are met.

Istiqra', Tamthil, and the First Figure of Qiyas
Defines two speculative proofs and the first figure of productive qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define istiqra' and tamthil, explain why they are speculative, and state the two necessary conditions for the first figure of conjunctive qiyas.

Core Idea
Induction and analogical reasoning are speculative; only conjunctive qiyas yields certainty if its conditions are met.

This passage from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary clarifies the logical foundations of qiyas by distinguishing it from induction (istiqra') and analogical reasoning (tamthil). It then details the first figure of conjunctive qiyas, which is the standard form for deriving rulings with certainty. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying qiyas in Hanafi jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two speculative proofs
Recognize that istiqra' (induction) and tamthil (analogical reasoning) are introduced as speculative (zanni) proofs. They are valid but do not yield certainty because exceptions are possible.
The text uses the example of the crocodile to show that induction can have exceptions.
2
Understand the definitions and examples
Read the definitions: istiqra' is tracing many particulars to derive a universal; tamthil is demonstrating equality of branch and original in the cause. Note the examples: animal jaw movement for istiqra', date-wine intoxicating for tamthil.
These examples illustrate how each proof operates.
3
Contrast with qiyas
Notice that the text specifies qiyas (analogy) is different because it entails the conclusion by itself, without needing an additional premise. This makes qiyas productive by nature, unlike istiqra' and tamthil which require extra premises.
This distinction is central to Hanafi usul.
4
Examine the first figure conditions
Focus on the conjunctive qiyas first figure. The two conditions are: (1) the minor premise must be affirmative, and (2) the major premise must be universal (whether affirmative or negative). The text provides examples: universal affirmative and universal negative.
The middle term is predicate in the minor and subject in the major.
5
Apply the conditions to examples
Work through the examples: 'Every human is an animal, every animal is moving by will, so every human is moving by will' – minor is affirmative, major is universal affirmative. 'Every rational being is human, no human is stone, so no rational being is stone' – minor affirmative, major universal negative.
In both, the conditions are satisfied, so the conclusion is valid.

The author emphasizes that these conditions are essential and inherent to the first figure.

You will learn why the minor must be affirmative and the major must be universal, and how these conditions ensure that the conclusion follows necessarily from the premises.

Ibn al-Humam's Objection and the Distinction of Formal vs Material Entailment
Refutes an objection against the affirmative minor condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain Ibn al-Humam's objection to the affirmative minor condition and evaluate the author's response based on the distinction between formal (bi-dhat al-qiyas) and material entailment.

Core Idea
An apparent counterexample does not break a logical rule if the conclusion owes its truth to material equivalence, not syllogistic form.

Ibn al-Humam raised an objection to the condition that the minor must be affirmative, citing an example where a negative minor still yields a correct conclusion because the two terms of the major are co-extensive. The author of this commentary defends the rule by showing that the conclusion in that case depends on the specific material relationship, not on the logical form. This lesson will help you understand how to distinguish between formally valid inference and accidental material truth.

Guided Path
1
Identify Ibn al-Humam's objection
Read the text where Ibn al-Humam argues that when the subject and predicate of the major are equal (mutasawiyan), the minor need not be affirmative, as in 'Some animal is not human; every human is rational; therefore some animal is not rational.' Note that the minor is negative but the conclusion seems correct.
2
Understand the author's response
The author says the objection 'is not something to be relied upon' because the entailment is not due to the essence of the syllogism. Instead, it is due to an extraneous premise: that negating one of two equals implies negating the other. This is material necessity, not formal.
3
Test the distinction with a counterfactual
Imagine the major premise were 'every human is white' (not co-extensive with human). The same syllogism form would give 'some animal is not human; every human is white; therefore some animal is not white' — but this conclusion does not follow materially because 'human' and 'white' are not co-extensive. This shows the conclusion depends on material content.
4
Apply the principle to evaluate qiyas
In Hanafi usul, when evaluating an analogical argument, one must check whether the entailment is due to the formal structure of the syllogism or due to accidental material relations. Only formal entailment guarantees the transfer of the ruling.

Ibn al-Humam's Objection on the Affirmative Minor Premise
Objection and response on affirmative minor condition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the condition of the affirmative minor premise in the first figure of a syllogism, analyze Ibn al-Humam's counterexample, and evaluate the Hanafi response that distinguishes essence from extraneous matter.

Core Idea
A valid syllogism requires formal necessity, not accidental truth from extraneous principles.

In this lesson, we examine a logical dispute within Hanafi usul al-fiqh regarding the conditions of the first figure of a syllogism. The standard rule states that the minor premise must be affirmative. Ibn al-Humam raises a counterexample where a negative minor still yields a correct conclusion when the terms of the major premise are equal. The author of our text defends the rule by arguing that such validity is not due to the essence of the syllogism but to an extraneous logical principle. Understanding this distinction is crucial for evaluating arguments in legal analogy.

Guided Path
1
State the standard rule and its conditions
Recall that the first figure requires two conditions: the minor premise must be affirmative and the major premise must be universal. The rule is that without an affirmative minor, the syllogism cannot produce a valid conclusion by itself.
This is the baseline position being challenged.
2
Identify Ibn al-Humam's objection
Ibn al-Humam presents a counterexample: 'Some animal is not a man, and every man is rational. Therefore, some animal is not rational.' Here the minor is negative, yet the conclusion is correct. He argues this disproves the necessity of an affirmative minor, except when the extremes of the major are equal.
The key point is that the subject and predicate of the major (man and rational) are coextensive.
3
Analyze the author's response
The author responds that the validity in this counterexample is not due to 'dhat al-qiyas' (the essence of the syllogism) but to an extraneous principle: the negation of one equal entails the negation of the other. This principle is not part of the syllogistic form itself.
If the major's predicate were not coextensive, the conclusion would not follow.
4
Distinguish formal vs. material validity
The author distinguishes between validity arising from the syllogistic form (essential) and validity arising from specific content (accidental). The rule about affirmative minor is about essential validity; the counterexample only shows material validity.
This preserves the rule as a necessary condition for a formally valid syllogism.
5
Apply the distinction to usul al-fiqh
In Hanafi legal reasoning, qiyas (analogy) is built on syllogistic logic. Understanding that conditions like affirmative minor are about formal necessity helps jurists properly evaluate proofs and avoid false analogies.
This lesson strengthens the foundation for constructing sound legal arguments.

Objection to Affirmative Minor Condition and Initial Response
Second objection and initial answer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the second objection to the rule of affirmative minor premise in the first figure and explain the author's initial response using the concept of 'removal negation'.

Core Idea
A negative minor premise can still be considered affirmative if the subject is posited.

This lesson examines a logical objection to a key Hanafi usul rule: that the minor premise must be affirmative for a first-figure syllogism to be valid. The objector provides counterexamples where a negative minor still yields a correct conclusion. The author's initial answer pivots on the nature of negation and the contract of position in the major premise.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Objection
State the objector's example: 'A is not B, and everything not B is C, so A is C.' Note that the minor premise is negative (A is not B) yet the conclusion is valid. Recognize that this challenges the necessity of an affirmative minor.
The objector also gives a physical example: 'Void is not existent, and everything not existent is not perceptible, so void is not perceptible.'
2
Analyze the Author's Initial Answer
Extract the author's brief response: negation is pure removal (رفع محض), and the contract of position (عقد الوضع) in the major premise involves affirmation. If you consider that affirmation in the minor, then the minor is not truly negative but an 'affirmation of negation' (إيجاب سلب).
This hints that the minor premise, despite its negative form, implicitly affirms the subject's existence.
3
Understand the Role of عقد الوضع
The contract of position ensures the subject individuals are attributed to the subject concept. In a negative proposition, the subject is still posited (e.g., 'A' exists as a concept). Thus, the minor premise retains an affirmative aspect in terms of position.
This concept will be detailed in the next lesson.
4
Apply to the Objection's Examples
For 'A is not B': the subject A is still posited; the negation applies only to the predicate. Therefore, the minor premise is not a pure removal of the subject, but a removal of the predicate from the subject. This preserves the requirement for an affirmative in the sense of position.

Detailed Answer: Classification of Negatives and the Two Contracts
Detailed answer with classification and definitions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify negative propositions into three types and explain how the contracts of position (ʿaqd al-waḍʿ) and predication (ʿaqd al-ḥaml) justify the Hanafi rule on affirmative minors.

Core Idea
Affirmative propositions require both a posited subject and a predication link.

To fully resolve the objection, the author provides a detailed logical framework. He classifies propositions containing negation into three types: simple negative, modified negative, and affirmative modified. He then defines two essential contracts: the contract of position (ʿaqd al-waḍʿ) and the contract of predication (ʿaqd al-ḥaml). In an affirmative proposition, both must be present. The objection's examples, though negative in form, still involve the contract of position, thus they are effectively affirmative in that sense.

Guided Path
1
Classify Propositions with Negation
Learn the three types: (1) Simple negative (e.g., Zayd is not a writer); (2) Modified negative (e.g., The stone is not non-inanimate); (3) Affirmative modified (e.g., Every non-animal is not a human). Note that the negation can be part of the subject or predicate.
This classification is crucial for understanding when a proposition is truly negative or only appears so.
2
Define عقد الحمل (Contract of Predication)
This is the attribution of the subject's individuals to the concept of the predicate. In 'Every human is an animal', it is the connection between the individual humans and the concept 'animal'.
It is the 'predication aspect' of a proposition.
3
Define عقد الوضع (Contract of Position)
This is the attribution of the subject's individuals to the concept of the subject itself. It specifies and allocates the individuals under the subject concept. In 'Every human is an animal', it is the recognition that 'human' refers to specific beings.
This contract is necessary for the subject to be posited, especially in affirmative propositions.
4
Apply to the Objection's Examples
In the example 'A is not B', the subject A is posited via ʿaqd al-waḍʿ; the negation only affects the predication. Thus, the minor premise has an affirmative position contract, satisfying the requirement that the subject be affirmed.
The same applies to the void example: 'void' is posited as a concept.
5
Synthesize the Full Answer
The author's detailed answer shows that the condition of affirmative minor does not require the form to be verbally affirmative; it requires that the subject be posited (ʿaqd al-waḍʿ). The objection's examples meet this condition.
Thus the Hanafi rule stands once the logical underpinnings are understood.

The solution is that in such premises, the copula already includes an affirmation of 'not-being-B' for A, so the minor is not a pure negation but an affirmation of a negative attribute. Thus, the subsumption holds, and the qiyas is valid. The author uses the example of 'A is not B' analyzed as 'A is characterized by non-B-ness'. This aligns with Hanafi usul's emphasis on the validity of qiyas even with negative premises, as long as the copula is properly considered.

سالب معدولة (saliba ma'dula): A negative proposition where the predicate is negated twice, resulting in an affirmation of the negated predicate.
عقد الوضع ('aqd al-wad'): The copula or the positing of the subject-predicate relation, which contains an implicit affirmation of the subject's characterization.

The Two Forms of Conditional Syllogism in Qiyas Istithna'i
Logical rules of conditional syllogism

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the valid inference rules of a conditional syllogism and explain why affirming the consequent is invalid, using the Hanafi usul concept of luzum.

Core Idea
In conditional syllogism, only affirming antecedent affirms consequent, and negating consequent negates antecedent.

This passage from the commentary 'Izalat ar-Rahbut' elucidates the logical rules governing conditional syllogisms (qiyas istithna'i), which are essential for understanding how premises yield conclusions in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author explains the two valid forms and addresses a potential objection concerning necessary existence, reinforcing the definition of true logical entailment (luzum).

Guided Path
1
Identify the two valid inference forms
From the passage, extract the two valid rules: (1) affirming the antecedent (wad' al-muqaddam) produces affirming the consequent (wad' al-tali); (2) negating the consequent (raf' al-tali) produces negating the antecedent (raf' al-muqaddam).
These are the only two valid forms; the other two are invalid.
2
Explain why affirming the consequent is invalid
The author states that the consequent (lazim) may be more general than the antecedent (malzum). Therefore, the existence of the general does not necessitate the existence of the particular. Example: 'If the sun is shining, the earth is illuminated'—affirming illumination does not prove the sun is shining because there could be other causes.
This is a critical logical point for istinbat: you cannot infer the cause from the effect alone.
3
Analyze the objection about impossible negation
An objector argues that if the consequent is impossible to negate (e.g., the existence of Allah in 'If the world exists, Allah exists'), then negating the consequent cannot occur, so the rule 'negating consequent negates antecedent' may not apply. The objector claims that if the consequent's negation is impossible, the entailment may cease.
The objection tests the universal applicability of the rule.
4
Apply the definition of luzum to resolve the objection
The response defines true luzum as the impossibility of separation of the consequent from the antecedent at all times and under all conditions. Therefore, even if the consequent's negation is impossible in reality, the logical rule still holds in hypothetical reasoning: assuming the negation of the consequent leads to the negation of the antecedent, whether or not that negation is actualizable. Otherwise, a contradiction would arise (the antecedent could exist without the consequent).
This reinforces that the rule is absolute: negating consequent always negates antecedent, regardless of possibility.

The example is explained: assuming the non-existence of Allah (impossible) would entail the non-existence of the world, which is also impossible, but the logical structure holds. The summary stresses that negating the consequent always negates the antecedent, regardless of whether the negation is possible or impossible, because the definition of entailment prevents separation.

Core Idea
The logical force of a disjunction depends on whether its components can be true together, false together, or both.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the logical structure of qiyas includes conditional syllogisms (qiyas istithna'i). These involve disjunctive statements (munfasila) where two possibilities are compared. Understanding the type of disjunction—whether it excludes simultaneous truth, simultaneous falsehood, or both—is essential for drawing valid legal conclusions. The Hanafi Proof and Shafi'i Rebuttal on the Cause
Examines the Hanafi proof from ijma and the Shafi'i response, culminating in the real/relative first distinction.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi argument from consensus and the Shafi'i response regarding the determination of the cause in an expanded time, and explain the reconciliation through real vs. relative first.

Core Idea
The Hanafi proof from consensus is refined by the Shafi'i distinction between real and relative first, which the Hanafis accept, preserving the expansive nature of the cause.

In this passage, the author presents the central Hanafi proof for their view that the first part of time is the cause but expansive: they claim consensus (ijma) that a person who becomes obligated in the middle of the time must perform. The Shafi'is respond that the middle time is a 'first' relative to that person. The author then adds a reconciling note: 'first' can be real (for existing obligatees) or relative (for new ones). This lesson unpacks that proof and rebuttal, and shows how the dispute ultimately turns on the semantics of 'first'.

Guided Path
1
Recall the Hanafi position
The Hanafis hold that the first part of the time is the cause for an expanded obligation, but it is expansive and transfers to later parts as needed.
This is the mainstream position from the previous lesson.
2
Identify the Hanafi proof from consensus
The Hanafis argue by ijma: both Hanafis and Shafi'is agree that a person who accepts Islam or reaches puberty in the middle of the time must pray in the remaining time. If the first part alone were the cause (and not expansive), a new obligatee would not be obligated at all, because their cause (the first part) already passed. Since they are obligated, the first part must be expansive.
The proof hinges on the fact that both schools accept the obligation of the new obligatee.
3
Explain the wajh al-dalalah
The common agreement on obligation shows that the cause cannot be exclusively the first part; it must be able to extend to later parts. Thus 'first' in the Hanafi view is not exclusive but expansive.
The phrase 'فثبت أن الوقت هو الأول ولكن موسعاً' is the conclusion.
4
State the Shafi'i rebuttal
The Shafi'is respond that the middle of the time becomes a 'first' time specifically for the new obligatee. In other words, for that person, the time they become obligated is their 'first' part, so the cause is still the first part exclusively, but it is a relative first.
The rebuttal preserves the Shafi'i view that the cause is the first part without expansion, by relativizing 'first'.
5
Understand the reconciliation
The author adds that the intended meaning of 'first' differs: for those who were already obligated, it is the real first (al-awwal al-haqiqi); for new obligatees, it is a relative first (al-awwal al-idafi). Thus the Hanafi expansive view can be seen as a special application of a more general principle: everyone's first moment of accountability is their 'first' part. This aligns with the Shafi'i rebuttal but still allows the Hanafi expansive conception of the cause.
The phrase 'فكل وقت صار فيه المرء مكلفاً هو أول الوقت بالنسبة إليه' captures this.

Causation Transfer and Purity of Time for Qada
Derives a ruling on qada of Asr based on causation transfer to last part.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that causation transfers from the first part of time to the last part to determine the validity of performing current-day and make-up prayers in deficient time.

Core Idea
Causation transfers to the last part; make-up of a partially deficient cause is invalid in a time deficient in every aspect.

This lesson examines a nuanced ruling from Hanafi usul: why today's 'Asr can be prayed in the final moments of its time, but yesterday's missed 'Asr cannot. The key lies in the Hanafi doctrine of causation transfer (intiqāl al-sababiyya) and the distinction between time deficiency in one aspect versus every aspect. We will trace the derivation from established principles, analyse an objection, and understand the sound position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core ruling
Read the passage to isolate the two rulings: today's 'Asr is valid in deficient time; yesterday's 'Asr qada is not. Note the basis: 'because its cause... is deficient in one aspect, so it is not performed by that which is deficient in every aspect'.
The 'deficient time' is the period near sunset when the sun changes colour (isfār).
2
Understand the principle of causation transfer
The Hanafi principle states that the cause (sabab) of an obligation transfers from the first part of the time to the last part. For a current prayer, the time is still present, so the entire time is considered for causation. For a make-up prayer, the time has fully exited, so the entire time is determined for causation after the exit.
This principle is used to justify performing 'Asr even in the last moments.
3
Apply the principle to distinguish between the two cases
Today's 'Asr: its time is partially complete (before sunset) and partially deficient (near sunset). Because causation transfers to the last part, the deficiency is incidental; the prayer is valid. Yesterday's 'Asr: its time was partially complete and partially deficient. For qada, the cause is the entire original time, which is deficient in one aspect. This cannot be fulfilled in a time deficient in every aspect (the near-sunset period).
The key is that the cause of qada inherits the deficiency of the original time.
4
Analyse the objection and the response
An objector argues: if part of the qada occurs in complete time and part in deficient time, the whole should be considered complete by majority. The response denies the validity of this scenario, citing lack of transmitted rulings from early Hanafis, and asserts that the deficiency of the time is incidental (bi al-'arad) and does not affect the essence.
The author concludes that the ruling is sound and the objection is based on an unverified scenario.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The existence of disliked prayers proves that obligation and dislikedness—and by analogy, obligation and prohibition—can coexist in one act.

This lesson presents a second proof from the majority of usul scholars that obligation and prohibition can combine in a single act. Instead of relying on textual evidence, this argument uses rational inference (dalil aqli) from the observable reality of disliked prayers. By showing that an obligatory prayer can be disliked, the proof establishes that contradictory rulings can coexist from different aspects.

Two Bases for Qada in the Last Moment
Explaining two possible bases for qada obligation at the last moment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi analysis of qada when a person becomes mukallaf in the last part of time, and differentiate between the two possible bases: the original obligation itself versus the obligation to preserve a part of the act.

Core Idea
Qada in the last moment is either from the original obligation or from preserving a part; the law does not obligate parts alone.

This passage examines a subtle usul question: when a person becomes legally responsible at the very end of the time for a prayer, does the obligation of qada arise from the original obligation (like a sleeper) or from the obligation to preserve a part of the act (like an invalidated supererogatory prayer)? The author presents both possibilities and hints at the preferred view by noting that the law does not command completing deficient acts. We will dissect the two analogies and their implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
The issue is: if someone becomes mukallaf in the last part of the time for an obligatory act, is qada due? If so, what is its basis?
Mukallaf here means legally responsible (adult, sane). The last part is insufficient to perform the full act.
2
Examine the first analogy: the sleeper
The sleeper is obligated to pray while asleep, but cannot perform. The obligation itself necessitates qada. Similarly, the one who becomes mukallaf late cannot perform, so qada is due from the same obligation.
This analogy treats qada as a consequence of the original wujub.
3
Examine the second analogy: the invalidated supererogatory act
If one begins a nafl prayer and then invalidates it, he must make it up because he was obligated to preserve the part he performed. Similarly, by realizing a part of the time, he is obligated to preserve that part, which necessitates making up the whole.
This analogy introduces the idea of preserving a part as the basis.
4
Note the author's verdict
The phrase 'so reflect' indicates that there is no proof for obligating a part alone. The law holds us to complete acts, not parts. Thus the first basis (original obligation) is more coherent, though the second shows another perspective.
This is a classic Hanafi move: preferring a straightforward reading over a fragmented one.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Consider a person who becomes mukallaf just before Maghrib time ends. According to the first basis, qada is wajib directly. According to the second, qada is wajib because he 'caught' a part. Evaluate which is stronger.
Think about whether capacity (being mukallaf) is a condition for obligation or for performance.
