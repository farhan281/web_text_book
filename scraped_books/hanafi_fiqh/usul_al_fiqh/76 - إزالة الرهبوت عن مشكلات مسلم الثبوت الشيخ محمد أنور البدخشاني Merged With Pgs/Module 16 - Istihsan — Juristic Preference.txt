Fourth Ash'ari Proof on Rational Good and Evil
States and refutes Ash'ari fourth proof on rational good and evil.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the fourth Ash'ari proof that rational good/evil necessitates divine compulsion and evaluate the Hanafi response distinguishing wisdom from causality.

Core Idea
Allah’s actions are based on wisdom, not rational necessity — thus He is free, not compelled.

The debate over rational good and evil (al-ḥusn wa al-qubḥ al-'aqliyyān) has profound implications for divine freedom. The Ash'ari argue that if good and evil were rationally known, Allah would be compelled to command the good and forbid the evil, otherwise He would act evilly. The Hanafi response reframes the discussion: Allah’s commands always align with wisdom (ḥikma), but this alignment does not constitute compulsion because wisdom is not a cause that forces His hand. In this lesson, we examine the fourth Ash'ari proof and its refutation.

Guided Path
1
State the Ash'ari position
Identify what the Ash'ari are claiming: if good and evil are rationally known, then Allah must rule in accordance with reason, otherwise He would be acting evilly (impossible). This implies He is compelled.
2
Identify the logical consequence
Analyze the implication: if compulsion is attributed to Allah, that is theologically unacceptable (bāṭil). Therefore the premise (rational good/evil) must be false.
This is a modus tollens argument.
3
Trace the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response distinguishes between rational necessity and divine wisdom. Allah’s actions are always wise (ḥakīm), but wisdom does not compel; it is a quality of His free choice.
The word لام in Quranic verses (e.g., 'created for you') indicates purpose (ḥikma), not cause ('illa).
4
Compare with Mu'tazila
Note that the Mu'tazila agree with the Ash'ari that Allah's actions are caused, but they affirm rational good/evil. The Hanafi position rejects both compulsion and causation (ta'līl) in divine actions.
5
Synthesize the thamara
The practical consequence: in usul al-fiqh, this debate affects how we derive 'illa (effective cause). The Hanafi school does not view 'illa as a necessary cause that compels the Lawgiver, but as a sign of wisdom.
This underlies concepts like istiḥsān where maslaḥa is considered without necessitating a textual 'illa.

Commentary
This passage presents and refutes the fourth argument of the Ash'ari school in the debate over rational good and evil. The passage type is a madhhab dispute. The author first states the Ash'ari proof: if good and evil are known rationally, then Allah would be compelled to act according to reason, since acting otherwise would be evil—a logical impossibility. This would negate His freedom. The response clarifies that Allah's actions are always based on wisdom (hikma), not rational necessity. The lam in the Quranic verses indicates purpose (li-l-hikma) not causation (li-l-'illa). In Hanafi/Maturidi theology, Allah's actions are not caused by external motives, unlike the Mu'tazila who affirm causation. Thus, conformity to wisdom does not imply compulsion.

Resolving the Objection of Categorical Difference between Ijab and Wujub
Refuting an objection based on Aristotelian categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi concept of i'tibar to refute the objection that ijab and wujub cannot be identical because they belong to different Aristotelian categories.

Core Idea
Ijab and wujub are one in essence but distinguished by mental consideration (i'tibar), allowing the same imperative to be both act and passion.

In this passage, the author addresses a philosophical objection against the Hanafi position that the divine command (ijab) and the resulting obligation (wujub) are essentially the same. The objection, based on Ibn Sina's categories, argues that since ijab is an act and wujub is a passion, they cannot be one. The answer, drawn from Sayyid al-Sharif al-Jurjani, uses the distinction between real categories and mental considerations (i'tibarat). This lesson walks through the objection and its resolution, highlighting the key role of i'tibar in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection
State the opponent's claim: that ijab and wujub cannot be identical because they belong to different Aristotelian categories (action vs. passion).
This objection is based on the principle that categories are mutually exclusive.
2
Understand the categories
Explain that the opponent sees ijab as an act (fi'l) and wujub as a passion (infi'al), two distinct categories that cannot apply to the same thing in reality.
Reference is made to Ibn Sina's al-Shifa where categories are said to be distinct (mutabayina).
3
Trace the answer using i'tibar
Analyze how Sayyid al-Sharif responds: the same imperative 'If'al' can be considered ijab from the commander's perspective and wujub from the obligated person's perspective.
The emphasis is on 'consideration' (i'tibar), not on the essence.
4
Analyze the refutation of Ibn Sina's authority
Show that Ibn Sina's statement applies only to real categories, not to mental considerations (i'tibariyya). The categories applied here are metaphorical, not real.
Thus the objection does not stand.
5
Apply the concept to a parallel case
Extend the logic to other attribute pairs like prohibition (tahrim) and unlawfulness (hurma) that also differ by aspect.
Consistency in Hanafi usul.

Ruling on Qada 'Asr in Deficient Time (al-Naqis)
Usul analysis: ruling, objection, refined proof, deeper truth

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of sabab (cause) and consider its integrity (completeness/deficiency) when deriving the ruling on qada prayer in a deficient time, and evaluate objections using the method of istihsan.

Core Idea
The sabab of a prayer must be complete; a deficient cause cannot fulfill a complete obligation.

This passage delves into a subtle Hanafi legal discussion: can the qada (makeup) of yesterday's 'Asr be performed in the last part of today's 'Asr time? The answer hinges on the nature of the sabab (cause) of the obligation. The author, al-Badakhshani, walks through an initial ruling, an objection that forces a refinement of the proof, and a further objection that leads to a deeper truth about the deficiency of time being accidental. This lesson will guide you through the layers of istidlal (legal reasoning) in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial ruling and its basis
Extract the original ruling: today's 'Asr is valid in the deficient time, but yesterday's qada is not. Then identify the stated reason: the sabab of yesterday's 'Asr is the whole time, which is deficient from one aspect, so it cannot be performed in a time deficient from every aspect.
The sabab here is the entire 'Asr time of yesterday.
2
Analyze the first objection and the modified proof
Understand the objection: if part of the qada falls in the complete time and part in the deficient, then the ruling should allow it. Then see how the author modifies the proof: the whole time is considered complete because most of it is complete (al-ghalabah), so the obligation is complete and cannot be fulfilled by a deficient performance.
This uses the principle that the majority takes the ruling of the whole.
3
Examine the second objection and its two answers
Consider the scenario of a new Muslim who becomes obligated in the deficient time. The objection: can he make it up in another deficient time? The first answer says validity is accepted because there is no transmitted prohibition. The second, deeper answer clarifies that time itself is not deficient; the deficiency is accidental due to resemblance to devil worshippers, so it is tolerated for ada but not for qada.
The second answer reveals the true 'illah (reason) behind the ruling.
4
Derive the usul principles at play
Identify the key usul principles: sabab as cause of obligation, consideration of completeness/deficiency of cause, the rule that the majority governs (al-ghalabah), and the concept of deficiency being accidental (bi-al-'arad) rather than essential.
These principles are applied to the specific case.

Understanding the Hanafi Rule on Azima and Rukhsa
Responding to a critique about performing the stricter ruling when a concession is available.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain that performing the stricter ruling (azima) when a concession (rukhsa) exists is sinful but valid, and apply this rule to the specific case of wiping over khuffs as presented in the passage.

Core Idea
Performing the stricter ruling when a rukhsa exists is sinful but still fulfills the obligation; the concession does not invalidate the act.

This passage addresses a critical question in Hanafi usul: if a legal concession is granted, can one still perform the original stricter ruling? The author responds to an objection using the example of wading in water while having wiped over khuffs. He clarifies that the negation of the legislated nature of the azima (nafy al-mashru'iyya) means it is blameworthy, not invalid. We will walk through his argument to understand the precise relationship between azima and rukhsa.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the objection
Identify the initial claim that when a concession exists, performing the stricter ruling is impermissible because the concession has negated its legislated nature.
This objection may arise from a literal reading of 'nafy al-mashru'iyya'.
2
Define nafy al-mashru'iyya
Understand that in the Hanafi school, 'nafy al-mashru'iyya' means the act is sinful (ithm), not that it is invalid or insufficient to discharge the obligation.
The passage uses the traveler's prayer as an analogy: the full prayer is valid but sinful.
3
Analyze the consensus proof
The author cites consensus (ijma) that a remover of impurity (like water for ablution) does not affect a later impurity. This supports that wading water does not nullify the previous wiping; thus the azima of washing feet later still has effect.
This consensus is qat'i and establishes the validity of the azima despite the concession.
4
Apply the rule to the khuff case
From the consensus, derive that the water waded did not break the previous wiping; the obligation to wash feet is not triggered during the period. If one washes feet after removing the khuff, it is valid but sinful if done unnecessarily.
The passage distinguishes between the period of wiping and after expiry.
5
Resolve the preference contradiction
When jurists say 'azima is preferable,' it means when one intends to remove the cause of the concession (e.g., removing the khuff), not while the concession remains operative.
This resolves the apparent contradiction between 'azima is sinful' and 'azima is preferable.'

The Usul Principle of Raf' al-Haraj and Its Fiqh Applications
Commentary on raf' al-haraj with multiple fiqh examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of raf' al-haraj to explain why certain concessions (rukhas) are granted in specific fiqh rulings and identify the limits of these concessions.

Core Idea
Raf' al-haraj removes hardship, but the law specifies exactly when and how—concessions are not unlimited.

This passage is an expansion (sharh) of the usul rule that Islamic law is built on removing hardship. The commentator illustrates this principle with a series of fiqh examples: obligations lifted for minors and the insane, concessions for menstruating women, the sick, travelers, and those who err or forget. Understanding this principle is key to grasping the Hanafi approach to rukhsah (concession) and 'azimah (original ruling).

First application: minors and insane
Analyze why nothing is obligatory on a sane minor or an adult insane person: due to weakness of intellect and body (minor) or intellect only (insane). This removes hardship because they cannot fully comprehend or perform obligations.

3

Identify that a menstruating woman is not required to make up missed prayers, but must make up missed fasts. The reason is that requiring qada' for five daily prayers would cause excessive hardship, while fasting occurs only once a year.
The distinction is based on the frequency of the act and the hardship of making up many prayers.
4

Explain that prayers can be performed in a reduced manner (sitting, lying, or gesturing) to remove hardship for the sick.

If a traveler intends residence before completing three days of travel, he becomes a resident and loses concessions, even if in a desert. After three days, he can only become a resident in a proper settlement. This shows that the law carefully defines when hardship (safar) is considered to exist and when it ends.
The key concept is the difference between removing travel before it is actualized (by intending residence early) vs. after it is actualized (which requires a proper abode to re-establish residency).

فلهذا أي لأجل عدم الحرج في الدين شرعاً لم يجب شئ على الصبي العاقل، ولا على المعتوه البالغ لضعف العقل والبدن في الأول ولضعف العقل فقط في الثاني. خلافاً لأبي زيد الدبوسي، فإنه قال في "التقويم": فإنا لا نسقط وجوب العبادات لأجل العته احتياطاً. ولأجل رعاية الحرج لم يجب قضاء الصلاة في حال الحيض والنفاس دون الصوم، أي يجب قضاء الصوم على الحائض والنفساء بعد طهرهما. ومن أجل ذلك شرعت العبادات (الصلوات) في المرض قاعداً أو مضطجعاً أو إيماء. وانتفى الإثم في الخطأ اجتهاداً (حال كونه مجتهداً) كما في القبلة. وانتفى الإثم في النسيان. وسقط أكل الصائم نسياناً. وخفف في السفر، فشرعت الرباعية ركعتين. وزيدت مدة المسح في السفر إلى ثلاثة أيام وليالها. وثبتت الرخصة بالشروع في السفر قبل تحققه ووصول المسافر إلى نهاية السفر، وتمام مدة السفر إلى ثلاثة أيام، ولو أقام المسافر (نوى الإقامة) قبل المدة (قبل مدة السفر) صح إقامته، ولزمته أحكام الإقامة من التمام، والمسح إلى يوم وليلة وغيرهما، ولو كانت إقامته في المفازة التي لا بنيان فيها، مع أن المفازة ليس بمحل الإقامة، لأنه (صيرورته مقيماً) قبل المدة رفع للسفر قبل تحققه، ورفع السفر بنية الإقامة رفع للرخصة التي كان سببها السفر، وبعدها (بعد مدة السفر) لا يصح كونه مقيماً إلا فيها (في موضع) يصح فيه كونه مقيماً من مصر أو قرية، لأنه (كونه مقيماً بعد مدة السفر) رفع للسفر بعد تحققه، وتجديد الإقامة.
Thus, for the sake of removing hardship in the religion legislatively, nothing is obligatory upon a sane minor, nor upon an adult insane person, due to weakness of intellect and body in the first and weakness of intellect only in the second. Contrary to Abu Zayd al-Dabusi, who said in 'al-Taqwim': 'We do not drop the obligation of acts of worship due to insanity, as a precaution.' And for the sake of observing hardship, making up prayer is not obligatory during menstruation and postpartum bleeding, but fasting is (obligatory to make up), i.e., the menstruating and postpartum woman must make up fasting after purification. And because of that, acts of worship (prayers) are prescribed in illness sitting, lying down, or by gesture. And sin is removed in error by ijtihad (when one is a mujtahid) as in the case of the qibla. And sin is removed in forgetfulness. And the eating of a fasting person forgetfully is nullified (i.e., does not break the fast). And concession was made in travel: four-unit prayers were prescribed as two units. And the period of wiping (over socks) in travel was increased to three days and nights. And the concession is established by commencing travel before its actual occurrence and the traveler's reaching the end of travel, and the completion of the travel period is three days. And if the traveler (intends residence) before the period (before the travel period), his residence is valid, and the rulings of residence become binding upon him such as completing (prayers) and wiping for a day and night and other things, even if his residence is in a desert where there is no building, even though the desert is not a place of residence, because his becoming a resident before the period removes travel before its actual occurrence, and removing travel by intention of residence removes the concession whose cause was travel. And after it (after the travel period), it is not valid for him to be a resident except in a place where being a resident is valid, such as a city or village, because his being a resident after the travel period removes travel after its actual occurrence and is a renewal of residence.

Commentary
This passage is an usul rule commentary explaining the principle of raf' al-haraj (removal of hardship) in Islamic law. The commentator uses this principle to explain multiple fiqh rulings. He begins by stating that due to the legislated removal of hardship, nothing is obligatory on a sane minor or an adult insane person—the minor lacks both intellect and physical capacity, the insane lacks intellect alone. He notes the contrary view of Abu Zayd al-Dabusi who, out of precaution, does not drop obligations due to insanity. Then he applies the principle to women in menstruation: they are not required to make up missed prayers (to avoid hardship of repetition), but they must make up fasting. Next, concessions for the sick: prayers may be performed sitting, lying down, or by gesture. Errors in ijtihad (like determining qibla) and forgetfulness (like eating while fasting) remove sin or nullify the effect. Travel concessions include shortening four-unit prayers to two and extending the wiping period to three days. Finally, a detailed hukm on when travel concessions begin: they start upon commencing travel, and if a traveler intends residence before three days, he becomes a resident and loses concessions, even in a desert. After three days, residence is only valid in a proper settlement. The passage demonstrates how the legal principle of hardship removal shapes specific rulings.

Key Terms
الحرج (al-ḥaraj): Hardship or difficulty that the law seeks to remove in granting concessions or lifting obligations.
الرخصة (al-rukhṣah): A concession that deviates from the original ruling due to necessity or hardship.
