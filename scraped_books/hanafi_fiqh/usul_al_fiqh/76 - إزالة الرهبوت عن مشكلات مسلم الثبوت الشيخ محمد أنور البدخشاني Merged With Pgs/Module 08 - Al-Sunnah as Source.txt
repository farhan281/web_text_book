Reasons for Salawat after Hamd in Hanafi Usul
Explains three reasons for invoking blessings on the Prophet after the praise of Allah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state three reasons for placing salawat after hamd and explain how the Quranic command and the concept of wasila support this practice.

Core Idea
Sending prayers upon the Prophet after praise is both a Quranic command and a rational necessity for divine acceptance.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text explains why scholars begin their works with praise (hamd) followed by salawat and salam upon the Prophet. The author provides two scriptural-rational reasons and a third practical one: the need for divine assistance in composing such works. Understanding these reasons illuminates the scholarly etiquette (adab) rooted in the Quran and sunna, and the concept of tawassul (seeking means) through the Prophet.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first reason (scriptural)
The author cites Quran 33:56 as a command, and that early scholars (salaf) followed it by placing salawat after hamd at the start of books. Recognize the verse as a dalil naqli.
The verse uses the imperative 'sallu' and 'sallimu', indicating a recommended act according to Hanafi usul.
2
Analyze the second reason (rational)
Human souls are defiled by sins; Allah is absolutely holy. A mediator (wasila) is needed: salawat upon the Prophet. The hadith that Allah sends ten mercies upon one who sends one salawat shows that Allah's mercy becomes a means of nearness.
This is a rational argument (aqli) complementing the textual command.
3
Examine the third reason (practical)
The author needed divine help to complete the difficult work of compiling usul al-fiqh. Supplication (du'a) is more readily accepted when preceded by praise and salawat, following the sunna of beginning du'a with praise.
This reason is added by the commentator, showing the living application of the principle.
4
Synthesize the overall function
Bring together the three reasons: they establish a sunna practice that combines obedience to Quran, rational need for mediation, and practical seeking of divine aid. This forms a key usul principle of tawassul.
The passage demonstrates that usul practices are not arbitrary but grounded in multiple evidences.

A Sahabi is defined by meeting the Prophet with faith, at age of discernment, and dying Muslim; they are the guides of human intellects through Quran and Sunnah.

This passage defines two key terms in usul al-fiqh: the Sahabi and the concept of 'daleel'. Understanding who qualifies as a Companion is essential because their words and actions are a primary source of Sunnah. Moreover, describing the family and Companions as 'adillat al-‘uqool' elevates their role as intellectual guides of the Ummah after the Prophet. Let's break down the definition and its significance.

Connect to usul al-fiqh
Recognize that the Companions' statements and actions are authoritative proofs (adillah) because of their proximity to revelation and their role in transmitting and interpreting it.
This underpins the Hanafi principle that qawl al-sahabi (the saying of a Companion) is a source of law.

The phrase 'سيما الأربعة الأصول' appears in the opening of the matn. Debate on whether children are legally obligated, with proofs and refutations

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The child is not legally obligated until puberty; the hadith 'rafa'a al-qalam' conclusively lifts obligation.

This lesson examines a foundational usul dispute: are children (sabi) legally obligated to perform religious duties? The Hanafi school relies on the explicit hadith 'the pen has been lifted from three' to assert that no obligation exists before puberty. However, some scholars argue that reason ('aql) alone creates obligation, or that obligation exists but performance is excused. We will analyze the proofs and counter-proofs to understand the decisive Hanafi position.

Issue: The inclusion of sinful travel under travel concessions. Sinful travel does not prevent concession according to us, contrary to the three Imams. Sinful travel (like travel for theft and plunder, and for killing a believer to a distant place of the distance of legal travel) does not prevent concession according to us: concession of breaking the fast, concession of shortening prayer, and concession of wiping for three days and nights, contrary to the three Imams. Evidence of the Hanafis: For us, the absolute (unrestricted) nature: Allah said: 'And whoever among you is sick or on a journey, then a number of other days.' And in Sahih Muslim from Ibn Abbas: 'Allah prescribed the prayer on the tongue of your Prophet: four in residence, and two on a journey.' And our evidence is the absoluteness of the text of concession, like His statement: 'And whoever among you is sick or on a journey, then a number of other days', and Allah did not restrict travel to travel of virtue and not disobedience. And in Sahih Muslim from Ibn Abbas: 'Allah prescribed the prayer on the tongue of your Prophet: four in residence, and two on a journey.' In the hadith also the word 'journey' is absolute. Evidence of the three Imams: They said: Concession is a blessing, so it cannot be attained through disobedience, like intoxication. We said: It is not the same, rather it is adjacent to it, so it becomes like prayer in usurped land, unlike a cause that is disobedience itself, like intoxication from drinking prohibited intoxicants. They said: Concession is a blessing, so it cannot be attained through disobedience, because of the loss of intellect due to intoxication; for that is neither a pardon nor a concession, rather legal consequences follow such as divorce, manumission, and liability for destroying others' property, unlike loss of intellect due to insanity or fainting, for that is a pardon and a concession, and no legal consequence is attached to it. The author said in response to their evidence: We said: The disobedience is not the travel itself, rather disobedience is adjacent to travel and external to it. For the traveler who is a highway robber has two attributes: one is moving from one place to another, the second is waging war against Allah and His messenger by highway robbery and plunder; the disobedience is the second, not the first. The cause for attaining the blessing is the first, i.e., the travel itself. (This is a strange interpretation, for actions are by intentions, and if there were no travel there would be no highway robbery, plunder, and corruption.) Then he analogized this travel to prayer in usurped land, and said: Like prayer in usurped land, for it in itself is obedience and not disobedience, being a fundamental act of worship in the religion, while occupying the property and land of another without his permission is adjacent to his prayer and is disobedience. So prayer in itself is a cause for the blessing of reward, unlike occupying the property of another without permission. This differs from a cause that is inherently disobedience, like intoxication resulting from drinking a prohibited intoxicant, and occupying land.

Commentary
This passage addresses a dispute in usul al-fiqh: whether a journey that is sinful (safar al-maʿṣiyah) entitles the traveler to the legal concessions (rukhaṣ) of travel, such as shortening prayer, breaking the fast in Ramadan, and wiping over socks for three days. The Hanafis hold that any journey of sufficient distance qualifies, even if the purpose is sinful (e.g., theft, highway robbery). Their evidence is the absolute wording of the Quranic verse (Qurʾān 2:184) and the hadith (from Ṣaḥīḥ Muslim) which do not qualify 'travel' by its uprightness. The three other Imams (mālik, al-Shāfiʿī, Aḥmad) argue that concession is a divine blessing, which cannot be earned through disobedience, analogous to the prohibition of giving a blessing (like water) to a disobedient person. They also compare it to the case of intoxication, which is a cause of loss of intellect but does not excuse legal liability. The Hanafi response, as presented by the author, distinguishes the travel itself from the sinful act that may accompany it. Travel is a neutral act; sin is a separate attribute. They analogize it to prayer performed on usurped land: the prayer itself is valid and rewarded, while the sin of usurpation is a separate matter. The author critiques this analogy as strange, noting that actions are judged by intentions, and without the travel the sins would not occur. The passage ends mid-sentence, continuing the analogy.

Key Terms
سفر المعصية (safar al-maʿṣiyah): A journey undertaken for a sinful purpose, such as theft or murder.
رخصة (rukhṣah): A legal concession that allows deviation from the general rule under certain conditions, e.g., shortening prayer during travel.
