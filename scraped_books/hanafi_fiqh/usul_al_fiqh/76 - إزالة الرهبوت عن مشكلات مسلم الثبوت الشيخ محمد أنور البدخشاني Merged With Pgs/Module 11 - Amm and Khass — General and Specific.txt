The Relationship Between Usul al-Fiqh and Fiqh
Explains how usul principles serve as general evidence for fiqh rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the usul rule 'al-amr lil-wujoob' to a detailed evidence to derive a fiqh ruling, and explain why usul al-fiqh is not separate from fiqh like logic is from philosophy.

Core Idea
Usul rules are directly applied to the same detailed evidences that yield fiqh rulings, making them integral, not external.

This passage from Musallam al-Thubut clarifies the essential link between usul al-fiqh and fiqh. It shows that usul principles, such as 'the command indicates obligation', are not abstract theories but are actively applied to the detailed evidences (like Quranic verses) to derive specific rulings. The author also refutes a common misconception that usul is like logic to philosophy, emphasizing that the detailed evidences themselves are individuals of the subject matter of usul. By working through the example of Zakah, you will see how a fiqh ruling is built from a detailed evidence plus a usul principle.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two types of evidences
Identify detailed evidences (verses, hadiths, ijma, qiyas) and general evidences (usul rules like 'al-amr lil-wujoob').
Detailed evidences are the raw material; general evidences are the principles that interpret them.
2
Understand the role of usul principles
Usul principles serve as the major premise or implication in qiyas, linking the detailed evidence to the ruling.
In the example, 'al-amr lil-wujoob' is the major premise.
3
Trace the example of Zakah through qiyas
Follow the conjunctive qiyas: Zakah is commanded (minor premise from Quran), everything commanded is obligatory (major premise = usul rule), so Zakah is obligatory.
Also note the exceptional qiyas form: If Zakah is commanded, it is obligatory; and it is commanded.
4
Recognize the linguistic rule as part of usul
The statement 'Give is an imperative form' is a linguistic rule that itself belongs to usul al-fiqh, since usul studies commands and prohibitions.
Both the linguistic and the usul rule are considered usuli.
5
Refute the misconception
The author rejects the idea that usul is like logic to philosophy. Unlike logic (which studies secondary intelligibles), usul's detailed evidences are individuals of its own subject matter (e.g., 'Give zakah' is an instance of the usul topic 'command').
This shows the intrinsic connection: usul does not just provide abstract tools; it directly engages with the same texts.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a matn from Musallam al-Thubut, explaining the relationship between usul al-fiqh and fiqh. The author begins by stating that fiqh evidences are of two types: detailed (tafseeli) and general (ijmali). Detailed evidences are particular verses, hadiths, ijma, and qiyas. General evidences are the rules of usul al-fiqh that guide the application of detailed evidences to specific rulings. The author then provides a practical example: deriving the obligation of Zakah from the Quranic command 'And give Zakah'. This derivation follows either conjunctive qiyas (qiyas iqtirani) or exceptional qiyas (qiyas istithnai). In both forms, the usul rule 'al-amr lil-wujoob' (command indicates obligation) serves as the major premise or the implication. The author also notes a linguistic rule: 'Give' is an imperative form, which itself is a usul topic. Thus both the linguistic and the usul rule are considered part of usul al-fiqh. The passage then refutes a misconception held by some scholars who think the relationship between usul and fiqh is like that of logic to philosophy. The refutation explains that detailed evidences (like 'Give Zakah') are themselves individuals of the subject matter of usul al-fiqh (e.g., the principle that command indicates obligation). In contrast, logic deals with secondary intelligibles that are not identical to the primary subject matter of philosophy. Therefore, the connection is much closer: usul al-fiqh directly studies the very evidences used in fiqh. This underscores the Hanafi view that usul al-fiqh is an integral tool for deriving rulings, not a separate discipline.

Key Terms
الدليل التفصيلي (al-dalil al-tafseeli): A specific textual or rational evidence for a particular fiqh ruling, e.g. a verse or hadith.
الدليل الإجمالي (al-dalil al-ijmali): A general usul principle that governs the interpretation of detailed evidences, e.g. al-amr lil-wujoob.
المعقولات الثانية (al-ma'qulat al-thaniyah): Secondary intelligibles: mental concepts that arise after primary concepts (e.g. universality, particularity, genus, species).
الأمر للوجوب (al-amr lil-wujoob): A ruling principle: a command (from the Lawgiver) indicates obligation unless there is a contextual indicator otherwise.

Detailed Evidences and the All-or-Some Objection: Refining the Hanafi Definition of Fiqh
Explaining the effect of the qualification 'from detailed evidences' and responding to the all-some objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the exclusionary effect of the qualification 'from detailed evidences' in the Hanafi definition of fiqh and evaluate the response to the all-or-some objection.

Core Idea
The Hanafi definition of fiqh excludes the muqallid, the Prophet, and even Allah — because fiqh requires theoretical derivation from the four detailed evidences.

In this passage, the commentator expands the definition of fiqh by analyzing the crucial qualification 'from its detailed evidences' (min adillatiha al-tafsiliyya). This qualification serves to exclude all forms of knowledge that are not derived through human theoretical reasoning from the four primary sources. The passage then presents and responds to a powerful logical objection: if 'all rulings' is required, no one qualifies; if 'some rulings' is enough, even a muqallid qualifies. The response refines our understanding of what 'knowledge' means in the definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the qualification being analyzed
Locate the phrase 'min adillatiha al-tafsiliyya' in the passage. Understand that 'adilla' refers specifically to the Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas by covenant (bi al-ahd).
The commentator al-Tawdih explains that the pronoun 'ha' refers to 'rulings' (ahkam), and 'evidences' are specified as the four sources.
2
List the exclusions that follow from this qualification
Identify which entities are excluded from the definition of fiqh: the muqallid, the Prophet (except his ijtihad), Jibril, and Allah. For each, state the reason: their knowledge is either from taqlid, intuitive, or immediate, not theoretically derived from the four evidences.
The passage explicitly says the muqallid's knowledge comes from 'the statement of his mujtahid' and prophetic knowledge is 'hadsiy'.
3
Distinguish the types of knowledge
Note the three categories: hadsi (intuitive, as with Prophethood and angels), nazari (theoretical, required for fiqh), and kasbi (acquired through effort). Fiqh requires nazari and kasbi, not hadsi.
This distinction is key to understanding why divine and angelic knowledge are excluded.
4
Present the 'all-or-some' objection (al-irdad)
State the two horns of the dilemma: if 'all rulings' is meant, even the greatest imams like Abu Hanifa and Malik are not faqihs (since they admitted ignorance of some rulings). If 'some rulings' is meant, a muqallid who knows one ruling with its evidence would be a faqih, which is false.
The objection is based on a literal reading of 'rulings' without further qualification.
5
Understand the author's answer
The answer (partially given) is that 'knowledge' in the definition refers to the malaka (faculty) of ijtihad, not actual knowledge of every ruling. Thus, a mujtahid who possesses the faculty is a faqih even if he lacks knowledge of some specific rulings.
This answer resolves the dilemma: the definition applies to all those who have the capacity to derive rulings from detailed evidences, not to those who have actual knowledge of all rulings.

Commentary
This passage expands the definition of fiqh by analyzing the qualification 'from its detailed evidences' (adillatiha al-tafsiliyya). The commentator al-Tawdih clarifies that 'evidences' refers specifically to the four established sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas). This excludes the knowledge of a muqallid who relies on a mujtahid's ruling, not on direct evidence. Also excluded are prophetic knowledge (which comes from wahy, not from human ijtihad), angelic knowledge (intuitive), and divine knowledge (immediate). This answer links back to the first lesson's discussion.

Key Terms
الأدلة التفصيلية (al-adilla al-tafsiliyya): The detailed evidences, i.e., the four primary sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas) considered individually.
المقلد (al-muqallid): One who follows a mujtahid without knowing the evidences; his knowledge is not considered fiqh in the technical sense.
الحدس (al-hads): Intuitive knowledge, as opposed to theoretical (nazari) and acquired (kasbi).

Universal Concepts as Descriptions: The Hanafi Logical Refinement
Refining the claim that universal concepts in science definitions are mere descriptions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why universal concepts in definitions are considered descriptions (rusum) not true definitions (hudud) and apply the distinction between mental and external parts to resolve the objection of dual realities.

Core Idea
Universal concepts define sciences only as descriptions; the objection of dual realities is resolved by distinguishing mental and external essences.

In Hanafi usul, defining a science is not a simple matter. The universal concepts used in introductory definitions are often called descriptions (rusum) rather than true definitions (hudud). A common objection is that if these concepts were definitions, each science would have two realities. This lesson unpacks that objection and shows how the Hanafi-inclined verifying logicians resolve it by distinguishing mental from external composition, a key logical tool in Hanafi epistemology.

Guided Path
1
Understand the initial claim
The passage states that universal concepts in definitions of sciences are descriptions (rusum), not definitions (hudud), because a definition gives the complete reality of a thing, while these concepts only provide insight (basira).
The author restricts this claim to recorded sciences (al-ulum al-mudawwana).
2
Grasp the objection
If these universal concepts were complete definitions, each science would have two realities: its own reality and the reality given by the definition. This would mean multiple essences (dhatiyyat) for one science, which is absurd.
The objection is based on the idea that a thing cannot have two independent essences.
3
Analyze the two schools on mental and external parts
Al-Sayyid al-Sharif holds that a thing cannot have both mental parts (genus and differentia) and external parts (matter and form). The verifying logicians argue that mental parts entail external parts because genus and differentia are predicated on the whole in different respects.
This metaphysical distinction is crucial to the Hanafi response.
4
Apply the Hanafi resolution
The verifying logicians (al-muhaqqiqun) show that having two realities is only contradictory if both are of the same type (both external or both mental). A mental reality and an external reality are compatible and entail each other. Thus, the universal concepts can be true definitions without causing a problem.
This removes the original objection, so the concepts could be definitions after all.
5
Note the remaining problem
Even if the dual-reality objection is resolved, a new problem arises: if the universal concepts are definitions, they would make conception (tasawwur) and assent (tasdiq) identical, which is incorrect. The author leaves this as a reflection (tafakkar).
This shows the depth of Hanafi critical analysis; the discussion is not complete.

Commentary

Classification of Names and the Dispute on Names of Sciences
Classifications and dispute over names of sciences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ism al-jins, 'ilm al-jins, and 'ilm al-shakhs, identify the three positions on the classification of science names, and evaluate the author's rebuttal using the counterexample of 'house'.

Core Idea
The name of a science like fiqh is an ism jins, not a proper noun, because it applies universally and the objection about part-whole is refuted by 'house'.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, precise classification of terms is essential for proper derivation of rulings. This passage tackles a classic dispute: are the names of sciences (like fiqh, nahw) generic nouns, proper nouns for a genus, or proper nouns for a specific collection? The author defines three types of names and defends the view that they are generic nouns, using logical analysis and a powerful counterexample.

Guided Path
1
Define the three name types
Memorize the definitions: ism al-jins (generic noun) for universals without mental specification; 'ilm al-jins (proper genus noun) for universals with specification; 'ilm al-shakhs (proper individual noun) for specific individuals. Example: 'water' vs 'Usama' vs 'Zayd'.
These are standard categories in Arabic grammar and usul.
2
Identify the three positions on science names
List the three views: (1) ism jins – the author's preferred, (2) 'ilm jins – al-Harawi's view, (3) 'ilm shakhs – Ibn al-Humam's view. Understand the reasoning behind each.
The author explicitly calls the first view 'apparent'.
3
Analyze the argument for 'ilm shakhs
Ibn al-Humam argues: if 'fiqh' were a universal, it would apply to each individual issue, but it does not (we don't say 'this single ruling is fiqh'). Therefore, it must be a proper noun for the collection.
This is a key objection that the author addresses.
4
Examine the author's rebuttal via counterexample
The author invokes 'bayt' (house) – an acknowledged ism jins that does not apply to each part (e.g., a single brick). This shows that a generic noun need not apply to every part; it applies to the whole as a universal concept. Thus the objection fails.
This rebuttal preserves the classification as ism jins.
5
Conclude the preferred position
The passage ends with the author's rebuttal, establishing that names of sciences are ism jins. Understanding this affects how we treat such terms in usul – as generic nouns subject to rules of generality and specification.
Later scholars largely accept this view.

Existence of the Natural Universal: Two Proofs
Defines and proves the natural universal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i) and explain how its existence is proven by logical and geometric arguments in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
The natural universal exists within individuals; otherwise, similarity of substances would be impossible.

This passage from Izalat al-Rahbut on Musallam al-Thubut tackles a foundational question in usul al-fiqh: does the natural universal (absolute essence) exist externally in its individuals? The author presents two proofs: a logical argument based on the necessity of a common essence among similar substances, and a geometric argument based on the impossibility of indivisible parts. The first proof is critiqued and partially refuted, leading to the second, more robust proof.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of the natural universal
Read the opening sentence carefully: the absolute essence (al-mahiyya al-mutlaqa) in the mode of 'not conditioned by anything' exists externally within its individuals. Identify what is meant by 'natural universal' and contrast it with mental or logical universals.
The term 'natural' (tabi'i) indicates existence as a common reality in concrete individuals.
2
Analyze the first proof and its reasoning
Trace the logical argument: if the universal did not exist in its individuals, each individual would be an independent reality requiring a unique name. But we know that substances (e.g., water) share a common reality. Hence, the universal must exist externally.
This is a reductio ad absurdum (khulf).
3
Identify the objection raised against the first proof
The author notes an objection: deniers of the universal do not accept that individuals are similar in 'haqiqa' (reality). They only accept similarity in 'wujud' (existence). Also, the argument could be circular if similarity itself depends on the universal.
This shows the first proof is insufficient, leading to a second proof.
4
Examine the second geometric proof
Study the geometric argument using the right triangle. Assume indivisible parts (atoms). Then contradictions arise: the hypotenuse cannot be consistently assigned a number of parts (neither 2 nor 3). Thus, the indivisible part is false; continuity (ittisal) follows. Continuity implies unity of essence among individuals, proving the natural universal.
The 'donkey shape' and 'bridal shape' are specific geometric figures used to prove the contradictions.
5
Synthesize the conclusion
Combine the insights: the first proof is critiqued, but the second proof from geometry establishes the existence of the natural universal on firmer ground. This concept is essential for qiyas (analogical reasoning) in usul al-fiqh.
Without a common essence, analogical extension between cases would be invalid.

Logical Definitions of Proof: Syllogism, Induction, Analogy
Defines proof logically and its three types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish syllogism from induction and analogy based on self-necessity, and evaluate their epistemic weight in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A proof that self-necessarily entails its conclusion is a syllogism; induction and analogy require an external premise.

This passage from the logical-theological tradition defines proof (الدليل) in three senses: the broad logical definition (two statements from which a third results), a narrower definition requiring self-necessity (syllogism), and the specific definition of syllogism itself. Understanding these categories is crucial for evaluating the strength of legal arguments in Hanafi usul, where qiyas (analogical reasoning) is a primary source but distinguished from probabilistic analogy (تمثيل). The passage uses Aristotelian logic to clarify epistemic certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three definitions of proof
The passage gives three definitions: (1) 'two statements from which another statement results' (broad, includes induction and analogy); (2) 'two statements that self-necessarily entail another statement' (specific to syllogism); (3) a statement composed of two statements that self-necessarily entails a third (the formal definition of syllogism).
The first definition is attributed to logicians (المنطقى).
2
Understand the tripartite structure of syllogism
A syllogism requires three parts: minor term (subject of conclusion), major term (predicate of conclusion), and middle term (connects them in two premises). The example: world (minor), contingent (major), changing (middle). The minor premise 'world is changing' and major premise 'everything changing is contingent' yield the conclusion 'world is contingent'.
The middle term appears in both premises but not in the conclusion.
3
Contrast syllogism with induction and analogy on self-necessity
Syllogism's conclusion follows necessarily from the premises alone. Induction and analogy require an external premise: for induction, the premise that surveyed particulars represent all; for analogy, the premise that the cause is identical in strength. Without that external premise, the conclusion does not self-necessarily follow.
The passage states induction and analogy do not 'self-necessarily entail' the conclusion.
4
Analyze the epistemic weight of each type
Syllogism yields certainty if premises are true. Induction is probabilistic because exceptions may exist (crocodile example). Analogy is probabilistic because the cause may differ in strength. In Hanafi usul, analogy (قياس) is considered definitive when conditions of illa are met, but the logical analogy (تمثيل) here is weaker.
Hanafi usul distinguishes قياس (binding analogical reasoning) from تمثيل (weak analogy).

Learning Objective

Core Idea
An absolute command's indication—inherent or contingent goodness—shapes the basis of obligation.

This passage presents a nuanced dispute within the Hanafi usul tradition. When a command is given without any qualifying context, does it directly tell us that the act is inherently good and therefore obligatory, or does it indicate that the act is good due to some external reason? The answer affects how we derive the level of obligation from such commands.

Consider the thamarah al-khilaf: if the command indicates inherent goodness, the obligation is immediate and does not accept falling away; if due to another, the obligation may be weaker or conditional.

The text does not provide proofs or a preferred opinion. It simply records the two views, leaving the matter undecided in this passage.
As a student, you should recognize that some usul questions remain unresolved among early Hanafi authorities.

Time of Hajj: A Dual Nature Criterion and Container

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the time of Hajj according to Hanafi usul, identifying it as dhu shibhayn, and derive the ruling on intention from that classification.

Core Idea
Time of Hajj is both a criterion (only one Hajj per year) and a container (actions don't fill the season), giving flexibility in intention.

In Hanafi usul, times for obligations can be purely a criterion (mi'yar) or purely a container (zarf). The time of Hajj is unique because it combines both aspects. This dual nature has a direct impact on how intention works: a general intention suffices for the obligatory Hajj, but a specific intention can divert it to supererogatory. Understanding this classification deepens your grasp of how the Lawgiver's rulings are derived from the nature of time itself.

These are conceptual categories used in Hanafi usul to classify times for obligations.

The time cannot accommodate more than one Hajj in a given year. This limitation makes it act as a criterion — it measures and restricts the obligation to a single performance, like a measuring tool.

The actions of Hajj (the rites) do not fill up the entire Hajj season (Shawwal, Dhul Qa'dah, first 10 days of Dhul Hijjah). Extra time remains, so the time is like a container that holds the action but is not consumed by it.

Why Resolve Cannot Substitute for the Act in a Broad Obligation
Dispute over whether resolve substitutes for act in broad obligation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi refutation of the claim that 'azm substitutes for the act in a broad obligation, and identify the usul principle that distinguishes a condition from a substitute.

Core Idea
'Azm is a condition of faith, not a substitute; the act itself remains obligatory.

This passage delves into a key usul dispute between the Hanafi school and al-Qadi al-Baqillani concerning the role of 'azm (resolve) in broad obligations (wajib muwassa'). The central question: can resolve, especially defined as 'absence of intention to abandon,' serve as a substitute for the actual performance of the act? The Hanafi response is a categorical 'no,' grounded in the nature of obligation, the text of the Lawgiver, and the logical consequences of substitution. Understanding this refutation sharpens your ability to distinguish between conditions, substitutes, and the inherent requirements of a command.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Positions
State the claim attributed to Baqillani: that in a broad obligation, the person has two equally valid options—perform the act or have the resolve ('azm). If he does either, it suffices; if he neglects both, he is sinful. Then state the Hanafi position: resolve is not a substitute; only the act fulfills the obligation.
The text refers to 'the author' as the Hanafi commentator refuting this.
2
Examine the Refutation of the First Aspect (Suffiency of Resolve)
The Hanafi response argues that resolve does not render the act unnecessary. The act is still required at the end of the time, and the revealed text commands the act, not resolve. Therefore, resolve cannot be a substitute.
This is based on the fact that the act is mentioned in the nass, not resolve.
3
Examine the Refutation of the Second Aspect (Sinfulness of Neglecting Both)
The Hanafi response denies that neglecting both necessarily incurs sin. A person may resolve at the start but be prevented (e.g., by sleep) and perform the act later without sin. Thus, the claim of inevitable sin is false.
This shows that resolve alone does not trigger obligation of immediate performance.
4
Analyze the Redefinition of 'Azm and the Hanafi Rebuttal
Baqillani's followers may redefine 'azm as 'absence of intention to abandon.' The Hanafi response shows that this absence is a ruling of faith, not obligation. It is a sign of iman that exists even before the time enters. Making it a substitute would mean the branch precedes the root (the act), which is logically invalid.
If one intends to abandon before the time, he sins due to faith, not because of the obligation.
5
Understand the Logical Argument from Substitution
If 'azm were a substitute like tayammum for wudu, it would render the act unnecessary. But in fact, the act remains necessary. Hence, 'azm cannot be a badal.
The text references al-Badi' to reinforce this point.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute (usul dispute) regarding the nature of 'azm (resolve) in the context of al-wajib al-muwassa' (the broad obligation). The author refutes the claim that resolve can serve as a substitute for the actual performance of the obligated act. The argument presented initially posits that in a broad obligation (like the obligatory prayer whose time is extensive), the obligated person has two options: either perform the act or have the resolve to perform it, and if he neglects both, he becomes sinful. The author rejects both aspects of this argument. First, he argues that resolve does not suffice for the act itself; the act must be performed by the end of the time, and the textual evidence (nass) mentions the act, not resolve. Second, he denies that neglecting both necessarily results in sinfulness, as a person may have the resolve but be prevented from acting, and later perform the act without sin. The discussion then turns to a redefinition of 'azm as 'absence of intention to abandon' (adam iradat al-tark). The author rebuts this by showing that such absence is a necessary consequence of faith (iman) and its signs, not a component of the obligation itself. He supports this by noting that if one intends to abandon the act before the time even enters, he becomes sinful due to the sanctity of the act as a requirement of faith. The passage concludes with a reference to al-Badi' by al-Nizam al-Sa'ati and the view of al-Qadi al-Baqillani, who considered resolve a substitute that would abrogate the act. The author denies the entailment and clarifies that according to Baqillani, the necessary consequence is the fall of obligation (sujud al-wujub), not the act itself. Throughout, the author maintains the Hanafi position that resolve is not a substitute but a condition or sign of faith, and the obligation remains to be fulfilled by the actual act within the broad time frame.

Key Terms
العزم (al-‘azm): In this passage, 'azm is defined initially as 'resolve to perform the act', but later corrected to 'absence of intention to abandon' (adam iradat al-tark). The author rejects both as substitutes for the act.
الواجب الموسع (al-wajib al-muwassa‘): An obligation with a broad time frame (e.g., the five daily prayers within their time). The obligated person must perform the act within the entire time, but the resolve at the beginning is not a substitute.
بدل (badal): A substitute that renders the original (mubdal) unnecessary. Here, the debate is whether 'azm can be a badal for the actual act.

Coexistence of Command and Prohibition: The Hanafi Principle
Analyzing the compatibility of prayer command and prohibition of usurpation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that a command and prohibition can coexist in the same act, and apply the sewing analogy to distinguish the two objects.

Core Idea
A command and prohibition can coexist when they target different aspects of an act; obedience and disobedience can be simultaneous.

In Hanafi usul, the coexistence of amr and nahy is a key principle. This lesson introduces the foundational argument using the example of prayer in a usurped property, showing how the command for prayer and the prohibition of usurpation are not contradictory because they apply to different dimensions of the action. The author uses a vivid analogy of a servant ordered to sew but forbidden to use a sharp instrument, illustrating simultaneous obedience and disobedience.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act and the two rulings
The act in question is performing prayer in a usurped property. The ruling on prayer is a command (wujub), and the ruling on usurpation is a prohibition (haram). You need to recognize that both rulings apply to the same physical act but from different perspectives.
The command is for the existence of prayer, the prohibition is for existing in a usurped property.
2
Distinguish the objects of command and prohibition
Notice that the command 'pray' does not specify location, so it is absolute. The prohibition 'do not usurp' forbids occupying property wrongfully but does not mention prayer. Therefore, the objects (متعلق) are different: prayer and usurpation are not the same act.
The existence required by the command (الوجود المطلوب) is different from existence in the usurped property.
3
Check for logical contradiction (tadhaad)
For two rulings to contradict, they must apply to the same object from the same aspect. Here, the command requires praying; the prohibition requires not occupying the property. They can both be true simultaneously.
No contradiction because the command is general regarding location, and the prohibition is general regarding property ownership.
4
Apply the sewing analogy
The author compares this to a master commanding his servant to sew but prohibiting him from using a sharp instrument. The servant sews (obeys the command) but by using the instrument (disobeys the prohibition). This shows that obedience and disobedience can occur in the same person at the same time.
The analogy proves that the two rulings do not nullify each other.
5
Conclude the Hanafi position
Thus, the person praying in a usurped property is obedient with respect to the prayer command and disobedient with respect to the prohibition. The prayer is valid (obligation fulfilled) but sinful.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

The existence required by the command for prayer is different from existence in the usurped property; because the command for prayer and its existence are general as to whether it is in a usurped property or other than it. Indeed, the command for prayer and its fulfillment are absolute, not restricted by the condition of being in any property (whether owned or usurped). The prohibition only indicates the otherness of the two existences if there is contradiction between them, but there is no contradiction between the existence of prayer and the prohibition of existence in a usurped property, nor any indication of difference and incompatibility. This is the meaning of his statement: 'So where is the indication?' Rather, between them (between existence in the usurped property and existence of prayer) there is a general-and-specific-from-one-aspect relationship. So the command (the command for prayer) remains upon its absoluteness as is its reality. Thus, it is possible that the property belongs to something else and prayer does not occur in it, and it is possible that prayer occurs in a non-usurped property as is the original state. This is the meaning of his statement: 'So where is the indication?' So performing prayer in a usurped property is like when one commands his servant to sew and prohibits him from the needle/scissors, and he sews (i.e., he performs the commanded act) and travels, then the servant is definitely both obedient and disobedient. Similarly, in performing prayer in a usurped property, the one praying is obedient by performing prayer and disobedient by performing it in a usurped property.

Objection: [against] the validity of prayer in a usurped property by the invalidity of fasting on the Day of Sacrifice.

Detail of the objection: If prayer were valid in a usurped property due to the non-unity of the two objects (the object of obligation and the object of prohibition), then fasting on the Day of Sacrifice would also [be valid] by considering the two aspects (the aspect of obligation related to worship and the aspect of prohibition related to turning away from hospitality, so its being a fast is worship, and its being on the Day of Sacrifice is prohibited, so they are two different matters, like prayer and prayer in the usurped property. So fasting on the Day of Sacrifice (vowed therein) must be valid and prayer in the usurped land must not be valid? The author answered with two answers: the first is rejection (by premise) and the second is conceding. (This objection) is refuted because the difference between the ruling of prayer in the usurped property and the ruling of fasting on the Day of Sacrifice (as a vow) is denied; for we commit to the validity of fasting on the Day of Sacrifice like the validity of prayer in the usurped property. According to us (the Hanafis), the one who vows to fast on the Day of Sacrifice is released from the obligation of performing the fast by fasting on that day, and if he does not fast on it, he must make it up. Yes, he sins by fasting on the Day of Sacrifice due to turning away from God's hospitality. And the conceding answer: If it were conceded that fasting on the Day of Sacrifice is invalid, and the ruling of the validity of prayer in the usurped property is conceded, then it is due to an impediment, namely the prohibition that indicates the corruption of fasting on it (on the Day of Sacrifice), unlike the prohibition of usurpation, for it does not indicate the corruption of prayer, because the prohibition of usurpation is silent about prayer in that property as to validity and corruption, and likewise the command for prayer is silent about its performance in the usurped property and other than it.

Mention of Ibn al-Hajib's answer to that objection and then the rebuttal: The gist of Ibn al-Hajib's answer to the objection is that the permissibility of combining obligation and prohibition in one type, when it has multiple aspects, is specific to a type where there is between its two aspects a general-and-specific-from-one-aspect relationship, like prayer in a usurped property. For between prayer and the usurped property there is a general-and-specific-from-one-aspect relationship allowing the separation of one from the other. So when the separation of one aspect (obligation) from the other (prohibition) is established, then as for fasting on the Day of Sacrifice, there is not between the vowed fast and the Day of Sacrifice a general-and-specific-from-one-aspect relationship; rather, between them there is a general-and-specific-in-an-absolute-way relationship. And it is said that between absolute fasting and fasting on the Day of Sacrifice there is a general-and-specific-in-an-absolute-way relationship, because absolute fasting is general, and fasting on the Day of Sacrifice is specific, because the Day of Sacrifice occurs without the vowed fast, and because the vowed fast does not occur on the Day of Sacrifice on other days. So its being a vowed fast necessitates obligation, and its being on the Day of Sacrifice necessitates the prohibition of fasting, so it is impossible to combine obligation and prohibition in it, unlike prayer in the usurped property. And he mentioned in the detail of the rebuttal of his statement, and the answer by restricting the claim of combining obligation and prohibition to that (type) in which there is between them (between the two aspects), the aspect of obligation and the aspect of prohibition, a general-and-specific-from-one-aspect relationship, like prayer and the usurped property, does not remove the objection from the generality of the evidence which is the target of the objection, not the claim; for the evidence is not specific to what has between the two aspects a general-and-specific-from-one-aspect relationship, since the premises of the evidence apply to fasting on the Day of Sacrifice, even though between the two aspects there is a general-and-specific-in-an-absolute-way relationship. Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul al-fiqh discussion on the coexistence of a command and a prohibition in the same act, using the example of performing prayer in a usurped (ghasb) property. The author begins by establishing that the command to pray is absolute and not conditioned by the location, while the prohibition of usurpation is separate. Hence, there is no contradiction (tadhaad) between the two because they apply to different aspects: the act of prayer itself and the act of occupying the property. This is likened to a master commanding his servant to sew but prohibiting him from using a sharp instrument (shifr); if the servant sews and then travels, he is both obedient (for sewing) and disobedient (for using the instrument or for traveling? Actually, the example says 'travels' (safara), but the point is he fulfills the command and violates the prohibition simultaneously). Similarly, the praying person in a usurped property is obedient regarding the prayer and disobedient regarding the usurpation. This establishes the principle that command and prohibition can coexist when their objects are different.

Then an objection (naqd) is raised: if this coexistence is allowed, then why is fasting on the Day of Nahr (10th Dhu al-Hijjah) invalid, even though it also seems to combine a command (vow to fast) and a prohibition (fasting on that day is prohibited)? The author provides two responses. The first is a 'rejection' (man'i): the Hanafi school actually holds that a vowed fast on the Day of Nahr is valid (the obligation is fulfilled) but sinful due to turning away from God's hospitality. Thus, the analogy holds. The second is 'conceding' (tasleemi): even if one concedes that fasting on that day is invalid, it is because the prohibition specifically indicates the corruption (fasad) of fasting on that day, unlike the prohibition of usurpation which does not indicate corruption of prayer. The prohibition of usurpation is 'silent' (saakit) about the validity of prayer, and the command to pray is silent about the location.

Next, the passage presents Ibn al-Hajib's attempted rebuttal, which argues that the coexistence of obligation and prohibition is only permissible when the two aspects have a 'general-and-specific-from-one-aspect' ('umum wa khusus min wajh) relationship, like prayer and usurped property, but not when they have an absolute general-specific relationship, as with the vowed fast and the Day of Nahr. However, the author of this work rebuts Ibn al-Hajib by pointing out that this restriction (takhsis al-da'wa) does not invalidate the original objection because the objection is based on the general evidence (dalil) that allows coexistence, not on the specific claim. The premises of the evidence apply to the fasting case even though its aspects have absolute general-specific relationship, so the objection stands. Thus, Ibn al-Hajib's response is insufficient.

This discussion exemplifies the Hanafi method of analyzing commands and prohibitions, emphasizing that they are not inherently contradictory when their subjects differ. The key tools used are the distinctions between types of generality and specificity, and the concept of 'silence' (sukuut) of one ruling about the other.

Key Terms
الوجود المطلوب (al-wujud al-matloob): The existence (of an act) that is required by a command, considered separately from the circumstances of its occurrence.
عموم وخصوص من وجه (umum wa khusus min wajh): A relationship where two concepts are each more general than the other in one aspect and more specific in another; they overlap partially but can exist separately.
النقض (al-naqd): An objection or counterexample brought against a claimed ruling or principle to show inconsistency.

Defending the Principle: The Objection of Fasting on the Day of Nahr and Ibn al-Hajib's Criticism
Analyzing the compatibility of prayer command and prohibition of usurpation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the objection from fasting on the Day of Nahr against the Hanafi principle, and explain the Hanafi responses as well as Ibn al-Hajib's rebuttal and the Hanafi counter-rebuttal.

Core Idea
The objection that fasting on the Day of Nahr is invalid despite involving two aspects fails because Hanafis actually consider it valid but sinful, or the prohibition of usurpation is different from the prohibition of fasting on that day.

The Hanafi principle of coexistence faces a powerful objection: if prayer in usurped property combines command and prohibition, why is fasting on the Day of Nahr (a vowed fast) not also valid? This lesson unpacks the objection, the Hanafi responses, and Ibn al-Hajib's attempted refutation based on the type of generality-specificity relationship.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection (naqd)
The opponent argues that if the Hanafi position holds (that coexistence is possible because the objects differ), then a vowed fast on the Day of Nahr should also be valid, because it also has two aspects: the vow (command) and the prohibition of fasting on that day. However, scholars generally rule it invalid. This is used as a counterexample.
The objector uses a qiyas: the structure is the same, so the ruling should be the same.
2
Understand the Hanafi first response (man'i)
The Hanafi school actually does not accept that the vowed fast is invalid. According to Hanafis, if someone vows to fast on the Day of Nahr, the fast is valid and releases the vow, but the person sins due to the prohibition. Thus, the analogy holds: both cases are valid but sinful.
This is a denial of the claim (man' al-naqd).
3
Understand the second response (tasleemi)
Even if one concedes (tasleem) that the vowed fast is invalid, the case is different. The prohibition of fasting on the Day of Nahr indicates corruption (fasad) of the fast itself, whereas the prohibition of usurpation is silent about the validity of prayer. Hence, the two cases are not analogous.
The difference lies in the nature of the prohibition: one affects the act itself, the other only the circumstance.
4
Examine Ibn al-Hajib's rebuttal
Ibn al-Hajib argues that the coexistence of command and prohibition is only possible when the two aspects have a 'general-and-specific-from-one-aspect' (umum wa khusus min wajh) relationship, as in prayer and usurpation. In the fasting case, the relationship is 'general-and-specific-absolute' (umum wa khusus mutlaq), so coexistence is impossible.
He tries to restrict the rule to a specific type of relationship.
5
Analyze the Hanafi counter-rebuttal
The Hanafi author points out that Ibn al-Hajib's restriction does not defeat the original objection. The objection was not against a specific claim but against the general evidence (dalil) used to support the rule. The premises of that evidence apply even to the fasting case (which has absolute-specific relationship), so the restriction is irrelevant.
This is a methodological critique: the objector targeted the proof, not the restricted conclusion.

The Hanafi Principle: No Coexistence of Wujub and Hurmah from One Aspect
Exposition of the principle preventing coexistence from one aspect

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that obligation and prohibition cannot coexist in a single act type from the same aspect, and explain how it applies to the distinction between prohibitory and discouraging prohibitions.

Core Idea
Obligation and prohibition cannot coexist in one act type from one aspect, but can from two aspects.

This lesson introduces a foundational usul principle: that wujub (obligation) and hurmah (prohibition) cannot occupy the same act type from a single aspect. The commentator uses the example of nahy al-tahrim (prohibitory prohibition) and nahy al-tanzih (discouraging prohibition) to illustrate that both are parallel in their opposition to their opposites. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly deriving rulings where an act involves multiple aspects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of prohibition
Recognize that nahy al-tahrim (e.g., usurpation) and nahy al-tanzih (e.g., abandoning sunan) are both prohibitions, but the former is for the essence (dhat) and the latter for an attribute (wasf).
Both share the same nature in opposing their opposite (obligation).
2
Understand the concept of al-wahid al-naw'i
Al-wahid al-naw'i refers to the type of an act considered as a category, not a specific instance. The principle applies to the generic type, not individual occurrences.
This distinguishes from al-wahid al-'ayni (specific instance).
3
Apply the principle to the salah example
The commentator states that the act of prayer (salah) with karahah due to leaving sunan is a single type (al-wahid al-naw'i). The obligation (wujub) of prayer and the prohibition (karahah) cannot both apply to the same type from the same aspect, but they can from two aspects (e.g., prayer as act and attribute of leaving sunan).
This shows the distinction between essence and attribute.
4
Extract the general rule
The principle: With multiplicity of aspect, obligation and prohibition (or even karahah) can coexist; from one aspect, they cannot. This applies to both prohibitory and discouraging prohibitions equally.
The rational basis is that the simultaneous command and prohibition would be a taklif bi al-muhal.

Refuting Arguments for Coexistence: The Third Proof and Abu Hashim's Example
Refutation of specific arguments for coexistence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the weak proof of the majority and Abu Hashim's argument for coexistence, and evaluate the Hanafi response based on taklif bi al-muhal and the role of repentance.

Core Idea
The arguments for coexistence are refuted by denying consensus and by demonstrating that they lead to command of the impossible.

This lesson examines two key challenges to the Hanafi principle: the claim by Jumhur that praying in a usurped house removes the obligation of qada, and Abu Hashim al-Mu'tazili's example of exiting a usurped property. Both arguments appear to show obligation and prohibition coexisting, but the commentator refutes them by showing that the first lacks consensus and the second entails an impossible command (taklif bi al-muhal). Understanding these refutations solidifies the Hanafi position and reveals how rational arguments are used in usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the third weak proof of the majority
The Jumhur argue that if one prays in a usurped house, the obligation of the prayer falls, and no qada is required. This implies that obligation (to pray) and prohibition (to use usurped property) coexisted in that prayer.
They claim consensus on this point.
2
Analyze the Hanafi refutation of the consensus claim
The commentator denies that there is consensus (ijma), because Imam Ahmad ibn Hanbal did not know of such a ruling. If it were true consensus, early scholars would have known it. Therefore, the proof is weak.
This is a methodological critique: the claimed consensus is not established.
3
Examine Abu Hashim's example
Abu Hashim says a usurper who repents is obligated to vacate the land (wajib) but cannot do so without using the owner's land (haram). Thus, the single act of 'exiting' contains both obligation and prohibition.
He argues this proves coexistence from two aspects, but the Hanafis respond that the act is one type (exiting) from one aspect.
4
Apply the taklif bi al-muhal argument
The Hanafis argue that if exiting is simultaneously wajib and haram from the same aspect, it would require the person to both perform and avoid the same act, which is impossible. This would be a command of the impossible, which is not from Allah.
Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity (Quran 2:286).
5
Distinguish additive vs. descriptive impossibility
The commentator distinguishes between 'commanding something impossible' (taklif al-muhal) and 'an impossible command' (taklif muhal). The first has an impossible object; the second is itself impossible. Abu Hashim's argument falls under the first, which is impermissible.
This refinement shows the depth of Hanafi usul reasoning.

Permissibility of Prohibiting One Unspecified Thing Among Known Things
Explaining the permissibility of prohibiting one unspecified thing among known things.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi usul principle that it is permissible to prohibit one unspecified thing among known things, identify its purpose (manʿ al-jamʿ), and distinguish it from the analogous obligatory choice (wajib mukhayyar).

Core Idea
In a prohibitory choice, the Lawgiver prevents combination (manʿ al-jamʿ), not voidance.

The source text draws a parallel between obligatory and prohibitory choices in usul al-fiqh. Just as a person may be obligated to perform one unspecified act from a set, they may also be prohibited from performing all but one unspecified act. The passage explains the legal purposes behind each and begins analyzing how prohibition attaches to the concept of 'one of things', using a Quranic example.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two contexts
Recognize that the Lawgiver may obligate one unspecified thing from a set (e.g., expiation options) or prohibit one unspecified thing from a set. Both involve a choice but with opposite purposes.
The obligatory choice is known as 'al-wājib al-mukhayyar'.
2
State the Hanafi position
It is permissible to prohibit one unspecified thing among known things, just as it is permissible to obligate one. This is a recognized principle in Hanafi usul.
3
Differentiate the purposes
In obligation, the purpose is 'manʿ al-khalw' (prevention of voidance) — the agent must perform at least one to avoid leaving all. In prohibition, the purpose is 'manʿ al-jamʿ' (prevention of combination) — the agent must leave at least one, not perform all collectively.
This distinction is crucial for understanding the intent behind each ruling.
4
Recognize the applicability of prior debates
The same scholarly dispute that exists for the wajib mukhayyar — whether the obligation is on the whole set, a specific part, or an unspecified part — applies also to prohibitory choice. This dispute affects how the ruling is interpreted.
Hanafis typically hold it is upon an unspecified part.
5
Analyze the first type of prohibition
When the prohibition attaches to the concept of 'one of them' itself (not to the individuals), it results in generalization: all individuals are prohibited because the universal nature (ṭabīʿa) is negated only by negating all individuals. Example: Quran 76:24 'Do not obey from them a sinner or an ingrate' — the prohibition covers all sinners and ingrates.
This analysis shows the linguistic mechanism by which 'one of them' can imply generality.

Four Types of Prohibition with Compound Objects
Explaining three types of prohibition and their effect with 'wa' and 'aw'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the four types of prohibition (nahy) when the prohibited acts are conjoined with 'wa' or 'aw', and apply each type to derive the correct ruling.

Core Idea
A prohibition on a compound subject may prohibit each part individually, the combination only, or indicate an ambiguous command — the conjunction determines the scope.

This passage from the Sharh of Muslim al-Thubut unpacks the typology of prohibitions (nahy) when the object of prohibition is a compound conjoined by 'wa' (and) or 'aw' (or). Understanding these four scenarios is essential for correctly interpreting legal texts that use such constructions. As a Hanafi usuli, you must determine whether the prohibition strikes each individual, the collective, or is structurally ambiguous.

Guided Path
1
Locate the four types
Identify the four distinct categories of prohibition described in the passage. The author presents them sequentially, though the third and fourth are numbered after a repetition. Note the key differences: first type applies to each individual; second applies to the concept accidentally; third prohibits the collective; fourth uses an ambiguous abandonment.
The passage has some repetition; focus on the distinct logical structures.
2
Distinguish 'wa' from 'aw'
The third type uses the conjunction 'wa' (and) and prohibits the combination of the two acts (e.g., 'Do not eat fish and drink milk' means do not combine them). The fourth type uses 'aw' (or) and creates an ambiguous prohibition that is best understood as two separate sentences conjoined.

This distinction is crucial for deriving rulings that are not absolute.

The Hanafi school asserts that its literal meaning is limited to obligation (wujub), while the majority (including Shafi'is) see it as encompassing both obligation and recommendation. Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul dispute regarding the nature of the imperative form (sigha al-amr) and its real (haqiqi) meaning. The Hanafi position, stated first, is that the imperative is haqiqah only for obligation (wujub), not for recommendation (nadb), permission (ibaha), or any other meaning. Three proofs are given: (1) linguistic: the imperative word is real only for obligation; (2) if mandub were really commanded, leaving it would be sin, but consensus holds leaving mandub is not sin; (3) the hadith about siwak shows that the Prophet did not command it despite it being recommended, proving command is for obligation only. The opposition (muhaqqiqun, likely Shafi'i or Mutakallimin) argue that mandub is obedience, and obedience is defined as doing what is commanded, so mandub must be commanded. They also argue that lexicographers divide 'amr' into obligatory and recommended categories, so 'amr' applies to both really. The Hanafi response distinguishes between obedience to command and obedience to recommendation, and points out that lexicographers also use 'amr' for threat and permission, so the division does not prove haqiqah; rather, the recommended is only metaphorically called 'commanded'. This clarifies the Hanafi usul principle that the imperative is qat'i in dalalah for obligation unless there is a qarinah.

Key Terms
صيغة الأمر (sigha al-amr): The imperative form (e.g., if'al 'do') that is originally placed for obligation in the Hanafi view.
الحقيقة (al-haqiqah): The real, literal meaning of a word as originally set by the language.
المندوب (al-mandub): A recommended act that is not obligatory, whose doer is praised and whose leaver is not blamed.

Understanding the Genus: Is Mubah a Genus for Wajib?
Refutes the notion that mubah is a genus for wajib via definitional analysis.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the claim that mubah is a genus for wajib by applying the Hanafi definition of mubah and distinguishing substantive from verbal disputes in usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
Mubah is not a genus for wajib because its definition requires equal permission to omit, which wajib lacks.

A common misunderstanding in usul al-fiqh is that since both mubah and wajib are 'permitted to do,' the former is a broader category that includes the latter. In this lesson we dissect that claim, clarify the Hanafi definitions of mubah and wajib, and show that the dispute reduces to a verbal one once proper definitions are applied.

Guided Path
1
Identify the mistaken claim
The claim is that mubah is a genus for wajib because both involve permission to act. Recognize that this depends on an incomplete definition of mubah.
The claim arises from focusing only on the 'permission to act' aspect.
2
State the complete Hanafi definition of mubah
In Hanafi usul, mubah is defined as 'equal in act and omission' (al-musawi fi'lan wa tarkan). Permission to act is not enough; permission to omit is equally essential.
This definition is crucial because it distinguishes mubah from wajib.
3
Apply the definition to wajib
Wajib is defined as 'permitted to do but not permitted to omit' (ma udhina li fi'lihi wa lam yu'dhan tarkuhu). Thus wajib lacks the permission to omit, which is a necessary part of mubah's essence.
Therefore, wajib does not share the complete reality of mubah.
4
Present the second response: verbal dispute
The commentator notes that if one defines mubah merely as 'permitted to do,' then it would include wajib. But that definition is rejected by the Hanafi school. The dispute becomes verbal when definitions differ.
This shows the importance of precise definition in usul.
5
Conclude the analysis
Substantively, mubah is not a genus for wajib. The disagreement is only verbal if one uses an incomplete definition. The Hanafi position is clear: mubah requires equal permission of act and omission.
This analysis sharpens your ability to detect definitional confusions.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute addressing the claim that mubah (permissible) is a genus for wajib (obligatory). The claim arises from an incomplete definition: if mubah is merely 'permitted to do,' then wajib shares that property, making mubah a broader category. The commentator refutes this by first denying that 'permitted to do' is the full reality of mubah; rather, mubah is defined by equal permission of both act and omission. Wajib, on the other hand, requires the act and prohibits omission, so it cannot be a species of mubah. The second response simplifies the matter: the dispute is ultimately verbal. Validity of Particulars and Universals in Usul al-Fiqh
Analyzing the relationship between particular acts and universal categories in Shari'a rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the distinction between the validity of particular rulings (juz'iyyat) and universal rulings (kulliyyat) and apply the Hanafi principle that particulars are validated by conformity to universals, while universals are validated by divine command.

Core Idea
Particular rulings are valid rationally by matching universals; universal rulings are valid by divine convention.

In this passage from 'Izalat al-Rahbūt', al-Badakhshani refines a comment by urging reflection on the structure of legal validity. He distinguishes between universals (kulliyyat) like the contract of sale, and particulars (juz'iyyat) like a specific sale by Zayd. The validity of each operates on a different plane: universals are valid because the Lawgiver set them; particulars are valid because they conform to those universals. This framework is crucial for understanding how Hanafi usul harmonizes rational coherence with revealed authority.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two levels of ruling
Recognize that there are universals (kulliyyat) and particulars (juz'iyyat) in both transactions and worship. The universal is the general type (e.g., sale); the particular is an instance (e.g., Zayd's sale).
2
Determine the basis of validity for each
The text states: validity of particulars is by conformity (mutabaqa) to universals; validity of universals is by divine command (amr al-shari').
3
Classify the epistemological source
The validity of particulars is rational ('aqli) because it is apprehended by reason; the validity of universals is conventional (wad'i) because it depends on revelation.
This classification is a hallmark of Hanafi usul, balancing reason and revelation.
