The Maturidi View: Good and Evil as Absolute Attributions (al-Itlaq al-A'amm)
Definition of Maturidi position on good/evil and refutation of naskh objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Maturidi position of al-itlaq al-a'amm and apply it to explain the possibility of naskh (abrogation).

Core Idea
Good and evil are absolute, not essential; thus abrogation can change them without contradiction.

In this passage, al-Badakhshani presents the Maturidi stance on the nature of moral values. The key is the concept of 'absolute attribution' (al-itlaq al-a'amm), which allows good and evil to be real yet changeable by divine command. This resolves the objection that naskh would be impossible if values were intrinsic.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Maturidi position: al-itlaq al-a'amm
Locate the key term in the passage: الإطلاق الأعم. Understand that it means absolute attribution covering both essence and attributes, both real and accidental.
2
Distinguish between essence/real attribute vs. accidental attribute
Recognize that essence (الذات) and real attribute (الصفة الحقيقية) are inseparable from the act, while accidental attributes (الصفة الاعتبارية) can change. The Maturidi view includes all three.
This distinction is crucial for the naskh argument.
3
Apply to the examples: visiting graves and 'iddah
Consider the two examples given: visiting graves (was prohibited, then permitted) and the 'iddah period (reduced from one year to four months). Note that the act itself remained the same, but the ruling changed.
If good/evil were essential, such changes would be impossible.
4
Contrast with the Mu'tazila view
The Mu'tazila claim good/evil are caused by essence or real attribute, making them unchangeable. The Maturidi respond that this leads to the impossibility of naskh, which contradicts observed sharia changes.
The passage explicitly says the naskh objection only applies to Mu'tazila.
5
Conclude: why naskh is possible
Since good/evil are absolute and not tied to essence, they can be changed by divine command. This preserves both objective morality and the reality of naskh.
This position is deemed 'al-haqq' (the truth) in the passage.

Fifth Ash'ari Proof on Rational Good and Evil and the Maturidi Response
States and refutes Ash'ari fifth proof using Quranic verse on punishment before messengers

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the fifth Ash'ari proof against rational good and evil and explain the Hanafi/Maturidi rebuttal using the Quranic verse and denial of pre-revelation rulings.

Core Idea
The verse 'We were not to punish until We sent a messenger' refutes Mu'tazila but not Maturidi, who deny any ruling before revelation.

In this passage, the author presents the fifth Ash'ari argument against rational good and evil: if actions were inherently good or evil, then punishment would be permissible even before prophets came, yet the Quran negates punishment before messengers. The Hanafi/Maturidi response distinguishes between permissibility in essence and permissibility in wisdom, and denies the necessary link between rational moral qualities and divine punishment. Understanding this response requires grasping the Maturidi principle that there is no ruling (ḥukm) before revelation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ash'ari claim and its proof
The Ash'ari argue that if good and evil were rational, then punishment for evil would be permissible before messengers are sent. They cite Quran 17:15, 'We were not to punish until We sent a messenger', to show that punishment does not occur before revelation.
The Ash'ari use this verse to negate the possibility of rational moral obligation.
2
Understand the apparent contradiction
The verse seems to directly contradict the claim that rational good/evil exist, because if they did, punishment could occur before revelation. The Ash'ari take this as a refutation of rational morality.
This argument assumes a necessary connection between rational good/evil and divine punishment.
3
Analyze the first Maturidi response: essence vs wisdom
The author replies that permissibility of punishment with respect to the essence of the act does not contradict impermissibility with respect to wisdom. Before revelation, humans have excuses due to deficient intellect or obscurity of moral causes, so punishment would not be wise.
This response accepts that acts might have rational moral qualities, but denies that punishment automatically follows.
4
Analyze the second Maturidi response: denial of necessary connection
The second answer denies the necessary connection between rational good/evil and punishment before messengers. Reward and punishment are based on Allah's ruling (ḥukm), which only comes after revelation. Maturidis do not affirm any ruling before revelation.
This shifts the ground: the Ash'ari argument only refutes the Mu'tazila, who believe in pre-revelation rulings.
5
Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Ash'ari hold punishment before messengers impossible; Mu'tazila say it is possible; Maturidi say it is not permissible due to wisdom but do not affirm pre-revelation obligation. The verse thus does not undermine the Maturidi position.
The dispute highlights different stances on the existence of pre-revelation rulings.

Eternal Ruling and Temporal Attachment: The Mu'tazila Objection
Mu'tazila objection and Hanafi response on eternal address vs temporal rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi response to the Mu'tazila objection regarding the definition of hukm, using the concepts of kalam nafsi and ta'alluq.

Core Idea
The ruling itself is eternal; only its attachment to actions is temporal.

In this lesson, we examine a critical dispute between the Mu'tazila and the Hanafi (Ash'ari) school concerning the definition of hukm. The Mu'tazila argue that abrogation (naskh) proves rulings are temporal, contradicting the idea that Allah's address is eternal. The Hanafi response introduces a key distinction: the ruling per se is eternal; what changes is its ta'alluq (attachment) to specific actions. This distinction preserves the eternity of divine speech while accounting for the observed changes in rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the Mu'tazila objection
Summarize their argument: hukm is defined as Allah's address, but Allah's address (kalam nafsi) is eternal, while rulings change through abrogation (naskh), proving they are temporal. Therefore the definition is inconsistent.
The Mu'tazila reject kalam nafsi, but here they argue from the Ash'ari premise.
2
Identify the Hanafi/Ash'ari premise
Recognize that the Hanafis (and Ash'aris) affirm that Allah's essential speech (kalam nafsi) is eternal and not composed of letters or sounds. This is the basis for defining hukm as address.
This premise is shared by Hanafis and Ash'aris; the objection targets this shared view.
3
Understand the response: ta'alluq
The Hanafi response is that the ruling itself is eternal; only its attachment (ta'alluq) to the action of the responsible person is temporal. Abrogation changes the attachment, not the ruling per se. The ruling's existence does not depend on the action.
This distinction is crucial for maintaining the eternity of divine attributes while allowing for apparent change in legislation.
4
Apply to examples
Work through the two examples given: drinking wine (permissibility then prohibition) and facing Bayt al-Maqdis in prayer (permission then prohibition). In each case, the eternal ruling (permissibility or prohibition) attaches to the action at the appropriate time.
These examples illustrate that the same action can have different attachments at different times.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
Understand the practical consequence: for the Hanafis, the definition of hukm as Allah's address remains valid and consistent with the eternal nature of divine speech. For the Mu'tazila, such a definition fails because they believe rulings are created and temporal.
This difference affects how one understands the relationship between God's attributes and His legislation.

Commentary
This passage addresses the Mu'tazila objection to the Ash'ari (and Hanafi) definition of hukm as the eternal address of Allah. The Mu'tazila argue that if Allah's address is eternal (the internal speech, kalam nafsī), then the rulings derived from it would also be eternal, but we see that rulings change through abrogation (naskh), proving they are temporal. For example, the ruling of permissibility for drinking wine was eternally present in Allah's knowledge, but it attached to the action 'drinking wine' at a certain time; later, the ruling of prohibition attached to the same action. The ruling itself does not change; only its attachment does. This distinction preserves the eternity of the ruling. The commentator emphasizes that the ruling's existence does not depend on the temporal action.

The Reality of Divine Threats: True News Conditioned on No Pardon
Refutes that divine threats are merely performative intimidations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on divine threats (al-wa'id) and evaluate the opposing view that they are merely performative intimidation.

Core Idea
Divine threats are true news (khabar) of punishment, but conditioned on Allah not pardoning.

In this passage, the author refutes a common misconception that Allah's threats of punishment in the Quran are merely rhetorical scares (insha'). He provides three arguments to show that such an interpretation contradicts the Quran's emphatic language, undermines the necessity of the Hereafter, and makes the concept of pardon meaningless. The Hanafi position is that threats are real news, but they are conditional upon no pardon. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and its implications for usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
What is the debate? Is Allah's threat (wa'id) real news (khabar) or a performative scare (insha')? Determine the two positions.
The opposing view treats threats like a master's threat to discipline a slave without intending actual punishment.
2
Analyze the first Hanafi argument
The author cites Quran 4:93 with emphatic language (e.g., 'khālidan fīhā', 'ghaḍaba', 'la‘ana', ‘adhāban ‘aẓīman'). If the threat were not true news, such emphasis would be unnecessary. Interpret it as mere inshā' is a departure from literal meaning (haqīqah) without proof.
Wajh al-dalālah: the emphatic constructions indicate certainty and reality.
3
Examine the second argument
If threats are mere scares, then promises (wa‘d) would likewise be mere inducements. This eliminates reward and punishment, removing the need for the Hereafter. The proof of resurrection and judgment collapses.
This is a rational implication (luzūm) drawn from the opposing view.
4
Examine the third argument
If there is no real punishment, then pardon becomes irrelevant. The author's opponent cannot consistently speak of pardon exiting after defining the wajib, because without punishment there is no pardon. The opposing interpretation nullifies the entire discussion of amnesty.
This shows internal inconsistency in the opponent's framework.
5
Synthesize the Hanafi resolution
The correct position: divine threats are true news (khabar ṣādiq) indicating punishment, but that punishment is conditioned on Allah not pardoning. Pardon is possible, and the threat is real unless pardon occurs. This preserves both divine truthfulness and mercy.
The author supports this by citing verses about forgiveness (e.g., 4:48, 8:38).

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute addressing the nature of divine threats (al-wa'id). The author refutes the claim that Allah's threats in the Quran are merely performative speech (insha') intended to scare, not real news (khabar). The argument proceeds in three steps. First, the verse about killing a believer is quoted; if the threat were not true news, the emphatic language would be superfluous. To interpret it as performative is a departure from the literal reality (haqiqah) to metaphor (majaz) without necessity. Second, if threats are mere scares, then promises would also be mere inducements, eliminating reward and punishment, thus destroying the need for the Hereafter. Third, if there is no real punishment, the concept of pardon becomes meaningless. The author then states the correct Hanafi position: divine threats are true news (khabar sadiq) indicating punishment, but that punishment is conditioned upon Allah not pardoning. Pardon remains possible, and the threat is real unless pardon occurs.

Abrogation of Obligation Leaves Permissibility
Stating the rule and defending it against Ghazali

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that abrogation of obligation leaves permissibility, and evaluate the evidence of al-Ghazali's objection using the genus-differentia analogy.

Core Idea
Obligation includes permissibility; removing obligation does not remove permissibility.

When Allah abrogates an obligation (wajib), does the act become merely permissible, or does it become something else? The majority of scholars, including the Hanafis, hold that permissibility remains—indeed, it often becomes recommended (mustahabb). This lesson unpacks the logical argument behind that ruling and examines a classical objection from al-Ghazali.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and the dispute
Locate the core claim: 'When obligation is abrogated, permissibility remains.' Note that the author explicitly disagrees with al-Ghazali. Frame the question: what is the practical difference?
The passage uses the example of fasting on Ashura: originally obligatory, later abrogated, but still recommended (mustahabb).
2
Define naskh and its types
Read the terminological definition of naskh and note the three types listed: (1) absolute abrogation (removing both obligation and permissibility), (2) abrogation of obligation only (the debated category), and (3) presumably abrogation of permissibility only? (the text lists three, but focuses on type 2).
Type 2 is the subject of the dispute; type 1 applies to previous laws. Type 3 is implied but not elaborated.
3
Analyze the argument: obligation includes permissibility
The majority argue that wujub includes (yatadammanu) jawaz. If x is obligatory, then by definition it is at least permissible. The abrogator removes the added element of obligation, but the underlying permissibility logically remains unless explicitly negated.
This is a logical (not textual) argument. It depends on the relationship between wujub and jawaz in Hanafi usul.
4
Examine the objection and the Hanafi response
Al-Ghazali and some Hanafis object using a genus-differentia analogy: if the differentia (obligation) is removed, the genus (jawaz) ceases. The Hanafi response reinterprets the differentia: jawaz is constituted by a different differentia (lack of difficulty in leaving), not by obligation. The analogy of a growing body is used: when growth ceases, the body remains as inanimate matter, not nothing.
The response preserves jawaz as a distinct category from wujub. The counterexample of the change of qibla (obligation abrogated, no permissibility to pray to Jerusalem) is not addressed here but could be resolved by noting that qibla is a pure obligation without independent permissibility.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: for Ashura fasting, the Hanafi position (and majority) say it is mustahabb (recommended, a type of jawaz), while al-Ghazali would say it is simply mubah (permissible without recommendation) if any jawaz remains, but he argues no jawaz remains at all.
The text sides with the majority, implying the mufta bihi position is that jawaz remains and often becomes mustahabb.

Does Permissibility Remain After Abrogation of Obligation?
Dispute on whether jawaz remains after naskh of wujub

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi position on whether jawaz remains after naskh of wujub and apply the genus-differentia analogy to defend the position.

Core Idea
Abrogation of obligation removes only the unlawfulness of leaving; permissibility remains as a distinct ruling.

The question of whether permissibility (jawaz) survives the abrogation of an obligation (wujub) is a classic usul dispute that reveals the structure of legal rulings. The Hanafi majority argue that wujub is composed of jawaz plus a prohibition on leaving; naskh removes only the latter, leaving jawaz intact. Opponents cite the change of qibla to argue that jawaz also vanishes. This lesson unpacks the logical anatomy of wujub and the philosophical analogy of genus and differentia used to resolve the impasse.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two positions
State the majority (jumhur) view: jawaz remains after naskh of wujub. Then state the opposing view of al-Ghazali and some Hanafis: jawaz does not remain; any current permissibility comes from a new independent proof.
The example of fasting Ashura is key for the majority, while the qibla change is the opponent's main evidence.
2
Understand the composition of wujub
Recognize that wujub is defined as jawaz al-fi'l plus hurmat al-tark. Naskh removes only hurmat al-tark, leaving the jawaz component. This is the core argument of the majority.
The passage explicitly states: 'al-wujub yatadammandu al-jawaz' – obligation includes permissibility.
3
Analyze the objection using genus and differentia
The opponent objects: if wujub is a composite of genus (jawaz) and differentia (hurmat al-tark), then removing the differentia should destroy the genus too. Understand the logical principle: a genus is constituted by its differentia.
The text uses 'jins' and 'fasl' from Aristotelian logic, common in usul.
4
Apply the Hanafi response: substitution of differentia
The Hanafi response: the genus (jawaz) can be constituted by a new differentia after the old one is removed. Here, after hurmat al-tark is gone, jawaz becomes defined by 'absence of hardship in leaving' (adam al-haraj fi al-tark). The example of a growing body becoming inanimate illustrates this.
The text explicitly says: 'qad yataqawwamu al-jins bi-faslin akhara' – the genus may be constituted by another differentia.
5
Examine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
Identify the practical effect: for acts like fasting Ashura, the majority rule that it remains permissible and recommended (mustahabb) based on the original abrogated proof, while opponents say the recommendation comes from a separate hadith. This affects how scholars derive rulings in similar cases.
The passage notes that the Prophet (peace be upon him) encouraged Ashura after its obligation was abrogated by Ramadan.

Survival of Jawaz After Naskh of Wujub
Logical defense of Hanafi view using genus-differentia replacement

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi argument that jawaz remains after naskh of wujub, explain the objection based on genus-differentia logic, and reconstruct the Hanafi response using replacement of the differentia.

Core Idea
Wujub is composite: naskh removes the prohibition of omission, leaving permissibility with a new differentia.

When an obligation is abrogated, does its permissibility survive? The jamhur say no: any remaining permissibility must come from a new evidence. The Hanafis argue that wujub is composite of jawaz and hurmat al-tark; abrogation removes only the second part, so jawaz persists, now defined by absence of restriction. This lesson unpacks the logical debate and the Hanafi defense rooted in Aristotelian genus-species analysis.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi composite definition of wujub
Recognize that for Hanafis, wujub = jawaz al-fi'l + hurmat al-tark. The abrogator (nasikh) removes only the second component.
This composite view is distinct from the Shafi'i single-attribute view.
2
Understand the jamhur objection using jins and fasl
If wujub is like a genus (jins = jawaz) and hurmat al-tark is its differentia (fasl), then removing the differentia should dissolve the genus entirely, so no jawaz remains.
The objector claims genus cannot survive without its specific differentia.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response: replacement of the differentia
The Hanafi answer: a genus can be reconstituted with a new differentia. Here, after naskh, jawaz becomes associated with 'adam al-haraj (no restriction on abandonment) as its new differentia.
Use the analogy of body (jism) with growth (nami) being replaced by inanimacy (jamid).
4
Apply the philosophical grounding from matter and form
The text invokes the philosophers' principle that matter (hayula) persists while forms change. The genus is like matter, differentia like form; matter remains when form changes.
This metaphysical analogy strengthens the logical claim.
5
Illustrate with a fiqh example
Consider the abrogation of the obligation to pray toward Jerusalem: according to Hanafis, the act of praying toward Jerusalem remains permissible (not sinful) after the change of qibla, unless evidence of prohibition exists.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf – the practical consequence of the dispute.

Core Idea

In this passage, the Hanafi school addresses a crucial usul question: does making up a missed obligatory act (qada) require a new capacity (qudra mumkina) distinct from the capacity that made the original performance (ada) obligatory? Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of la haraj to identify when a legal concession (rukhsah) applies and distinguish the Hanafi position from the Mu'tazila.

Core Idea
Hardship in Islamic law is graded, and concessions are proportionate to its severity.

This passage introduces the foundational usul principle that there is no hardship in religion (la haraj fi al-din). It explains that hardship is a graded concept (mushakkak), meaning that the degree of concession varies with the degree of difficulty. The Hanafi school holds that neither rational nor legal hardship negates obligation; rather, rulings are lightened proportionally. This principle underpins many well-known rulings such as the shortening of prayer during travel and the permissibility of wiping over khuffs for three days.

The issue of no hardship in religion, neither legally nor rationally: There is no hardship in religion, rationally or legally, and it is graded. Because of this, nothing is obligatory upon the sane minor nor upon the insane adult, contrary to Abu Zayd. And the making up of prayer is not obligatory during menstruation and postnatal bleeding, unlike fasting. And acts of worship are prescribed in sickness sitting or lying down. And sin is negated in error for the mujtahid, and in forgetfulness. And the fasting person's eating is omitted (i.e., he is not blamed for unintentional eating). And concessions were made in travel, so the four-rak'ah prayers were prescribed as two rak'ahs, and wiping over the khuff for three days. And the license is established by the Law before its occurrence (i.e., the ruling of license is valid before the actual need arises). And if one establishes residence before the period (of travel), his residence is valid and the rulings of residence apply, even if in a desert, because it is a prevention (of the license). And after the period, no (the license does not apply) except in what is valid, because it is a removal after its occurrence. 'Allah has not placed upon you in religion any hardship' (22:78), 'Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity' (2:286), 'Allah does not burden a soul except what He has given it' (65:7). There is no hardship in religion rationally, as the Mu'tazila claim, so whatever involves rational hardship, the servant is not obligated with it according to them, nor legally, as it is with us. And the proof is the verses mentioned before the author's words. And (hardship) is a universal graded term, meaning its existence varies among its instances: it applies to some instances by way of increase, and to some by way of decrease and weakness. For the person incapable in every aspect, his hardship is more severe and stronger than the hardship of the person incapable in some aspects. So the lightening of rulings is proportionate to the hardship in every case of hardship.

Commentary
This passage states the important usul principle that there is no hardship (haraj) in religion, which is not only a legal principle but also a rational one according to the Hanafis, contrary to the Mu'tazila who claim that rational hardship alone can negate obligation. The author explains that 'haraj' is a graded term (مشكك), meaning it applies with varying intensity. This principle justifies many legal concessions (rukhsas): a minor or insane person is not obligated, rulings on prayer are lightened in sickness, travel, etc. The Quranic verses are cited as evidence. The author also discusses the timing of licenses: a license is established before the actual hardship occurs (as a preventive measure), but once the hardship is removed, the license also lifts. The key takeaway is that Islamic law is built on ease, but the degree of easing depends on the degree of hardship.

Key Terms
الحرج (al-ḥaraj): A concept of hardship or difficulty that is recognized by the Law as a cause for easing rulings. It is a graded term (مشكك) meaning it applies in different degrees.
المشكك (al-mushakkak): A term that applies to its instances with varying degrees of intensity (e.g., whiteness applies to snow and milk differently). Here, 'haraj' is applied with severity or lightness.
الرخصة (al-rukhṣah): A legal dispensation or concession that allows an exception to the original ruling due to a valid excuse (e.g., travel, illness).

Key Takeaway
The principle of no hardship (la haraj) is a graded concept in Hanafi usul: concessions are proportionate to hardship, and obligations are never completely negated by hardship.
