Parsing 'Tala'a al-Din' and 'Taba'a al-Yaqin'
Parsing two phrases from the matn with variant readings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to parse two phrases from the matn by identifying the grammatical effect of tashdid on the verb and the case of the following noun.

Core Idea
A single tashdid changes agency – from Allah to the attribute itself – transforming the meaning.

In Hanafi usul, precise understanding of matn texts is essential. The author here analyzes two key phrases from the opening of Musallam al-Thubut, showing that variant readings (tashdid vs. no tashdid) change who is the agent. This grammatical precision affects how we understand the relationship between divine action and the manifestation of religion and certainty.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two verbs under analysis
Locate the verbs 'tala'a' and 'taba'a' in the passage. Note that each appears in the matn and is discussed by the commentator.
These are not the only verbs, but they are the focus.
2
Recognize the two possible readings for each verb
For each verb, there are two readings: with tashdid (شدّة) and without (تخفيف). The choice affects transitivity.
Tashdid on the second root letter often indicates a causative meaning in Arabic.
3
Determine the grammatical consequence
With tashdid, the verb is transitive (fa''ala pattern) and takes an object (مفعول به). Without tashdid, it is intransitive or middle, and the noun following is the subject (فاعل).
This is a standard grammatical rule for Form II vs. Form I verbs.
4
Apply to the first phrase: 'tala'a al-din'
If 'tala'a' is read with tashdid, 'al-din' is accusative (object) and the hidden pronoun (Allah) is the subject. If without tashdid, 'al-din' is nominative (subject).
The meaning shifts from 'Allah made the religion appear' to 'the religion appeared'.
5
Apply to the second phrase: 'taba'a al-yaqin'
Similarly, with tashdid, 'al-yaqin' is object (Allah stamps certainty); without tashdid, it is subject (certainty becomes imprinted).
This parallels the first analysis exactly.

The Three Types of Metaphor in the Opening Phrases
Explains the three types of metaphor used in the opening phrases.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and distinguish the three types of isti'ara (makniyya, takhayyuliyya, tarshihiyya) in a given Arabic text and explain their combined use.

Core Idea
Three aspects of one metaphor: implicit likeness, imagined attribute, reinforcing detail.

The commentator unpacks the author's rhetorical craft, showing how each phrase contains a layered metaphor. Understanding these layers is essential for grasping the stylistic precision of the matn, which is a work on Usul al-Fiqh. This lesson will help you identify and analyze these metaphorical components.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two key phrases
The phrases are 'أعنة المبادي' (reins of beginnings) and 'نواصي المقاصد' (forelocks of objectives).
2
Recognize the base metaphor (makniyya)
In 'أعنة المبادي', beginnings are implicitly likened to a horse. In 'نواصي المقاصد', objectives are implicitly likened to a human head.
The likened thing is not explicitly stated, only a property of it.
3
Note the imaginary attribute (takhayyuliyya)
The author mentions 'reins' which belong to a horse, and 'forelocks' which belong to a head. These are imaginary attributes that reinforce the metaphor.
4
Identify the reinforcing term (tarshihiyya)
The phrase 'أعنة المبادي بيدك' includes 'hand' for taking reins, and 'نواصي المقاصد مفوض إليك' includes 'entrusting', which is appropriate to the original meaning of the metaphor.
5
Combine the three aspects
All three aspects are part of a single metaphorical expression, not separate figures.

Commentary
This passage is the opening of the commentary where the author explains the rhetorical devices used in the original text (the matn). The author first defines 'objectives' (maqāṣid) in three ways: as definitions and results, as general worldly and otherworldly aims, and as legal objectives including correct beliefs, good character, and deeds. He then analyzes the author's use of metaphor in two key phrases. In the second, he likened 'objectives' (maqāṣid) to a human head, also an implicit metaphor, with 'forelocks' as imaginary and the concept of entrusting (tafwīḍ) as reinforcing. The meaning of 'fa-anta al-musta'ān' is that all existence and authority belong to Allah alone; others have only borrowed existence and delegated authority. The two sentences either derive from the preceding metaphors or directly state that the reins and forelocks are in Allah's hands, so we seek His help and rely on Him. The grammatical definiteness of subject and predicate, along with the fronting of the prepositional phrase, indicates restriction (ḥaṣr). The commentator then relates differing views of Taftazani and Zamakhshari on the direction of restriction. Taftazani holds that the predicate (al-musta'ān) is restricted to the subject (anta), meaning help is sought only from You. Zamakhshari says both directions are possible in Arabic, but rational evidence (al-qarīna al-ʿaqliyya) determines that in this context the predicate is restricted and the subject is the restrictor, because the opposite would negate Allah's other attributes. This analysis remains at the level of linguistic and rhetorical explanation; no legal ruling (ḥukm) is derived here.

Key Terms
استعارة مكنية (isti'āra makniyya): A metaphor where the likeness is implied and a property of the likened thing is mentioned.
استعارة تخييلية (isti'āra takhayyuliyya): A metaphor that affirms an imaginary attribute to the likened thing to reinforce the comparison.
استعارة ترشيحية (isti'āra tarshīḥiyya): A metaphor accompanied by a term that suits the original meaning of the word, strengthening the comparison.
حصر (ḥaṣr): Restriction, i.e., restricting a predicate to a subject or vice versa.

Grammatical Restriction (Hasr) in the Opening Supplication
Examines the grammatical restriction in the opening supplication and the differing interpretations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two possible directions of restriction in definite subject-predicate constructions and apply rational evidence to resolve ambiguity in a Hanafi context.

Core Idea
When subject and predicate are both definite, restriction can go either way; rational evidence determines the intended meaning.

The commentator analyzes the phrase 'fa-anta al-musta'ān' (For You are the One sought for help) and notes that the definiteness indicates restriction. However, grammarians Taftazani and Zamakhshari disagree on the direction of restriction. This lesson explores their views and shows how rational evidence (al-qarīna al-ʿaqliyya) resolves the ambiguity, a method relevant to Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase
The phrase is 'فأنت المستعان'. Both subject (أنت) and predicate (المستعان) are definite.
2
Understand the grammatical rule
When both are definite and the predicate has the definite article, it can indicate restriction (hasr). The question is which element is restricted to the other.
3
Taftazani's view
Al-Taftazani holds that the predicate (المستعان) is restricted to the subject (أنت), meaning 'Only You are sought for help.'
4
Zamakhshari's view
Al-Zamakhshari says both directions are grammatically possible; context decides. He allows that the subject could be restricted to the predicate.
5
Apply rational evidence
If the subject were restricted (i.e., 'You are the one sought for help' excluding other attributes of Allah), that would negate His other attributes, which is theologically unsound. Therefore, rational evidence favors Taftazani's view.
6
Conclude
The intended meaning is exclusive help from Allah, consistent with the context of reliance.

وهو في الاصطلاح يأتي على أربعة معان: الراجح، والمستصحب والقاعدة والدليل. مثال الراجح، كقولهم: الحقيقة أصل بالنسبة إلى المجاز، أو الأصل في الكلام
ومثال المستصحب (استصحاب الحال جعل الحالة السابقة باقية ودليلاً على الحكم في زمان الحال، وإجراء الحكم عليه كالسابق، كما يقال الأصل في الماء الطهارة، والنجاسة طارئة.
ومثال القاعدة: كقولهم: الفاعل مرفوع، والمفعول منصوب، وهما أصلان من أصول النحو
ومثال الدليل قول الفقهاء: الأصل في الطهارة، قوله تعالى: (إذا قمتم إلى الصلاة فاغسلوا وجوهكم) الآية.
Definition of 'asl' linguistically and terminologically. He said in defining 'asl' (whose plural is 'usul'): 'Asl' linguistically is that upon which something else is built, like the foundation upon which the wall is built, physically, and like the principle upon which particulars are based, intellectually, just as 'far' (branch) linguistically is that which is built upon something else, and that is the 'asl'. The reality. And terminologically, it comes in four meanings: the preponderant, the presumed continuity, the rule, and the evidence. Example of the preponderant: their saying: the literal meaning is 'asl' relative to the metaphorical, or the 'asl' in speech. Example of presumed continuity: the presumption of continuity is considering the previous state as remaining and as evidence for the ruling at the present time, and applying the ruling upon it as before, as it is said: the 'asl' in water is purity, and impurity is incidental. Example of the rule: their saying: the subject is in the nominative, and the object is in the accusative, and they are two 'asls' from the principles of grammar. Example of the evidence: the jurists' saying: the 'asl' in purity is the saying of Allah the Exalted: 'When you rise for prayer, wash your faces' etc. verse.

Commentary
This passage is a definition of the term 'asl' (pl. usul) both linguistically and terminologically. The author begins by stating the linguistic meaning: 'asl' is that upon which something else is built. He gives two examples: the physical foundation of a wall, and the intellectual principle upon which particulars are based. The opposite is 'far' (branch), which is built upon something else. Then he explains that terminologically, 'asl' has four distinct usages in Islamic sciences. The first is 'al-rajih' (the preponderant), as in 'the literal meaning is the asl relative to metaphor', meaning the literal meaning takes precedence. The second is 'al-mustashab' (presumed continuity), which is the principle that a previous state remains in effect unless changed, as in 'the asl in water is purity'. The third is 'al-qaida' (the rule), like grammatical rules that serve as foundational principles. The fourth is 'al-dalil' (the evidence), as when jurists say 'the asl in purity is the verse so-and-so'. Importantly, in the compound 'usul al-fiqh', the meaning intended is 'dalil' (evidence), because the phrase is an idafa (construct) where 'usul' is added to 'fiqh', indicating that the usul are the proofs of fiqh. This is a key point: the term 'usul' in 'usul al-fiqh' does not refer to the preponderant or presumed continuity or rule, but specifically to the evidences (adilla) from which fiqh rulings are derived. The passage thus sets the stage for understanding the subject matter of usul al-fiqh.

Key Terms
أصل (asl): Linguistically: that upon which something else is built. Terminologically: one of four meanings (al-rajih, al-mustashab, al-qaida, al-dalil). In the phrase 'usul al-fiqh', it specifically means 'dalil' (evidence).
