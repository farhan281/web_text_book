This passage is a detailed linguistic analysis of the word 'al-hamd' (praise) as it appears in the opening phrase of the Qur'an and the matn. The commentator first explains why the author of the matn preferred 'al-hamd' over 'al-madh' (commendation): because 'hamd' requires choice and will, whereas 'madh' can be given to anyone. The commentator argues that it is a real two-part sentence (jumatun thanā‘iyyah) that exists in external reality, contrary to Bahr al-‘Ulum's claim. Finally, the seventh possibility is merely a relational commonality without independent existence. This analysis shows the depth of linguistic consideration in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, where precise understanding of terms is foundational for legal reasoning.

The Hanafi Definition of Qawa'id in Usul al-Fiqh
Defines qawa'id linguistically and terminologically in usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define qawa'id linguistically and terminologically, and identify the universal nature (positive/negative, categorical/conditional) of qawa'id as per the Hanafi usul framework.

Core Idea
A qa'ida is a universal proposition from which particular fiqh rulings are derived.

In usul al-fiqh, the term qawa'id (plural of qa'ida) is foundational for deriving rulings. This passage from the matn clarifies both the linguistic root (foundation) and the precise technical meaning: a universal proposition that yields the conditions of its particulars. The author also corrects a common oversimplification—that qawa'id are only positive categorical statements—emphasizing that they encompass negative and conditional forms as well.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning
The passage states that qa'ida linguistically means 'the foundation and what branches off from it.' Recognize that the term implies both stability and derivation.
This root meaning underlies the technical usage—rules are stable principles that generate specific rulings.
2

The Second Proof: Geometric Refutation of the Indivisible Part
Geometric proof to refute atomism and establish universals.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the geometric proof using the donkey-shaped and bride-shaped figures demonstrates the impossibility of indivisible parts and thus supports the existence of universals in external reality.

Core Idea
The geometric proof shows that indivisible parts lead to mathematical contradiction, establishing continuity and the reality of universals.

In this passage, the author presents a second proof for the existence of universals (al-mahiyya al-mutlaqa) in the external world, this time using a geometric argument against the concept of the indivisible atom (juz' la yatajazza'). This argument draws on two classic geometric theorems: the triangle inequality (donkey-shaped) and the Pythagorean property (bride-shaped). The impossibility of a fractional number of indivisible parts refutes atomism and supports the continuous nature of bodies, which in turn grounds the realist position on universals essential for legal analogy in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the target of the proof
The proof aims to refute the existence of the indivisible part (juz' la yatajazza'), a concept held by some theologians. If this concept is false, then bodies are continuous, which supports the existence of universals.
The indivisible part is also called the atom (al-jawhar al-fard).
2
Set up the geometric construction
Assume a right triangle where each leg is composed of exactly two indivisible parts (degrees). The total length of the two legs together is three parts because they share a common point (the vertex).
3
Apply the donkey-shaped theorem
The donkey-shaped theorem (shakl himari) states that any one side of a triangle is less than the sum of the other two sides. Therefore, the hypotenuse must be less than three parts.
4
Apply the bride-shaped theorem
The bride-shaped theorem (shakl 'arusi) states that in a right triangle, the hypotenuse is longer than each leg. Therefore, the hypotenuse must be greater than two parts.
5
Derive the contradiction
From steps 3 and 4, the hypotenuse is longer than 2 parts and shorter than 3 parts, so it must be a fractional number of parts (e.g., 2.5). But since parts are assumed indivisible, fractional parts are impossible. Hence the assumption of indivisible parts is false.
6
Conclude continuity and universals
With indivisible parts refuted, bodies are continuous. This continuity allows a universal essence (like 'triangle' or 'human') to exist in multiple individuals without fragmentation, supporting the realist position on universals needed for qiyas and definitions in usul al-fiqh.

Commentary
This passage presents the second proof (dalil thani) for the existence of the absolute reality (al-mahiyya al-mutlaqa) or natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i) in the external world. The author, Shaykh Muhammad Anwar al-Badakhshani, is commenting on the matn 'Musallam al-Thubut'. After refuting the first proof (logical), he now presents a geometric proof. The argument is a reductio ad absurdum against the concept of the indivisible part (juz' la yatajazza') upheld by some theologians (mutakallimun). The proof uses two geometric figures: the 'donkey-shaped figure' (al-shakl al-himari) and the 'bride-shaped figure' (al-shakl al-'arusi). The core reasoning: Assume a right triangle with legs each equal to two indivisible parts (degrees). According to the donkey-shaped figure, any one side of a triangle must be less than the sum of the other two sides; thus the hypotenuse cannot be equal to the sum (3 degrees). According to the bride-shaped figure, the hypotenuse of a right triangle is longer than either leg; thus the hypotenuse cannot be equal to either leg (2 degrees). Therefore the hypotenuse must be between 2 and 3 degrees. But if parts were indivisible, the hypotenuse would have to be composed of a whole number of parts; a fractional number is impossible. Hence the assumption of indivisible parts leads to a contradiction, proving that parts must be divisible (continuous). From continuity, the author argues that the parts of the sides are united in reality, supporting the existence of a universal that inheres in multiple individuals. The author notes this is the method of the philosophers (ahl al-hikma) like Ibn Sina, not the theologians. The argument thus defends the realist position on universals necessary for certain usul al-fiqh discussions.

Proof of Continuous Substance and the Natural Universal
Geometric proof against atomism and for the external existence of the natural universal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the geometric argument that refutes atomism and derive the existence of the natural universal in the external world.

Core Idea
Geometric division of the hypotenuse proves indivisible parts cannot exist, establishing continuous substance and the reality of universals.

This passage presents a rational proof rooted in geometry to establish two foundational concepts for Hanafi usul: the continuous nature of substance and the extramental existence of the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i). This is not merely philosophical speculation but a necessary judgment supported by intuitive reasoning, crucial for understanding how legal rulings apply to individuals through common essence.

Guided Path
1
Understand the geometric premise
Consider a right triangle with each leg composed of two indivisible parts (degrees). The hypotenuse must be longer than either leg but shorter than their sum, forcing its length to be between two and three degrees. This implies it contains a fraction of an indivisible part, contradicting indivisibility.
The 'arusi form' refers to a specific geometric configuration; the proof holds generally.
2
Identify the conclusion against atomism
Since the hypotenuse cannot be an integer number of indivisible parts, the concept of the indivisible part (al-juz' alladhi la yatajazza') is invalidated.
3
Deduce continuous substance
From the invalidity of indivisible parts, the continuous substance (al-jawhar al-muttasil) is established as the basis of corporeal reality. All bodies are composed of a single continuous extension.
This aligns with the Hanafi view rejecting atomism.
4
Extend to the natural universal
Since all bodies share continuous substance, they share a common corporeal reality. This common reality (al-kulli al-tabi'i) exists externally as a single essence instantiated in each individual.
The proof moves from physics to metaphysics: the natural universal is a necessary consequence of continuous substance.
5
Consider the supportive argument from Usul
The author cites a logical argument: if the natural universal did not exist externally, the division of universal into essential and accidental would be impossible, and definition by essentials would fail. This reinforces the necessity of external existence.
This argument is attributed to Shaykh al-Martunki and is independent of the geometric proof.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A valid definition must prevent both outsiders from entering and insiders from leaving the defined category.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, precise definitions are essential for delineating legal concepts. This lesson introduces the two foundational conditions that any definition (المعرف) must satisfy: comprehensiveness (الطرد) and exclusivity (العكس). Mastering these conditions will enable you to critically assess whether a definition is valid or flawed, a skill vital for deriving and applying legal rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definiendum and the proposed definition
Determine the term (مُعَرَّف) that is being defined and the exact wording of the definition (مُعَرِّف). This step sets the stage for testing the definition.
In Hanafi usul, the definiendum could be a legal term like 'salat' or 'ghasb'.

Test for al-tard (comprehensiveness)
Ask: Does the definition include every instance of the definiendum? If it fails to include some members, it lacks comprehensiveness.
Al-tard prevents outsiders from entering – i.e., ensures all individuals of the category are covered.
3
Test for al-'aks (exclusivity)
Ask: Does the definition exclude everything that is not an instance of the definiendum? If it includes non-members, it lacks exclusivity.
Al-'aks prevents insiders from leaving – i.e., ensures no non-members are included.

The Rational Proof for the External Existence of the Natural Universal
Rational proof for external existence of natural universal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the rational argument that if the natural universal did not exist externally, then the division of universals into essential and accidental would be invalid, and explain its implications for the validity of definitions.

Core Idea
If the natural universal is not in external reality, definitions and divisions collapse – so it must exist.

This lesson presents a rational (aqli) proof for the real existence of the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i), a concept foundational to many usul discussions. The argument demonstrates that without this universal, essential logical tools such as the division of universals into essential and accidental, and the framework of definitions (hadd and rusm), would become impossible. We will trace the logical steps of the proof as laid out by the commentator, following the aqli reasoning method accepted in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Claim
The passage asserts that the natural universal (al-kulli al-tabi'i) exists externally. First, pinpoint this claim.

To prove the claim, the argument works by reductio: suppose the natural universal does not exist externally. What follows?
3

If the natural universal is not in the external world, then the essential (al-dhati) – which is a type of universal – also cannot exist externally. Consequently, the division of the universal into essential and accidental would be invalid because the essential would have no external realization.

This rational proof is considered strong by the author and is attributed to philosophers like Ibn Sina and al-Martunki.

Core Idea
Exclusive disjunction has three types of conflict: truth-only, falsehood-only, and both – each yields a different conclusion.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, reasoning often involves weighing contradictory evidence. Core Idea

This passage presents a sophisticated usul al-fiqh dispute: does the text that obligates performance of a vowed act also obligate its qada if missed? Understanding this principle is crucial for grasping how the Hanafi school derives obligations of compensation.

1
Identify the Contested Issue
The core dispute is whether the same text that obligates performing a vowed act also suffices to obligate its qada when missed. The refuter says no; the Hanafi school says yes.

The Hanafi school holds that vowing i'tikaf implicitly includes its condition (fasting), so the obligation of qada is already contained in the vow. The proof is that the vow of a conditional act (i'tikaf which requires fasting) is a vow for its condition.

The Hanafi response introduces the concept of a 'preventer' (mani'): during Ramadan, the effect of the vow (obligation of a new fast) is blocked by the existing fard fast. This passage introduces a fundamental Hanafi usul rule: when someone makes a conditional vow (nazar) that obligates an act of worship, the obligation extends to the necessary condition for that act. Commentary
This passage discusses the legal capacity (ahliyyah) of a slave in Hanafi fiqh. The author begins by stating the established Hanafi position: a slave possesses full capacity to be addressed by divine commands (ahliyyat al-wujub) and to perform acts of worship, and his confession regarding hudud and qisas is valid. However, the slave is restricted (mahjur) from financial transactions due to the master's right of ownership. This restriction is not due to lack of capacity but due to a specific preventer (mani'), namely the master's ownership. If the master grants permission, the restriction is lifted, but capacity is not newly granted—it was always present.

The author then presents the Shafi'i argument: they reason that if a slave had capacity to transact, he would necessarily have capacity to own (since transaction is a cause of ownership), but ownership by a slave is impossible by consensus, so he cannot have capacity to transact. The Hanafi response rebuts this: the absence of ownership is not due to absence of capacity but due to a preventer (the slave being owned). Capacity to transact is independent and can exist without capacity to own; the two are not necessarily linked.

Finally, a branch issue (mas'alah) illustrates the practical effect: if a master permits his slave to engage in a specific type of trade, the slave acquires full transactional capacity in all types, and his earnings come under his possession (yad) just like a mukatib (a slave contracted for manumission). The difference between a permitted slave and a mukatib is that the permitted slave's restriction is lifted gratuitously (like a gift), whereas the mukatib's freedom is purchased through a contract (kitabah) with compensation (like a sale). Thus, the permitted slave does not become entitled to his person (he remains owned), but he owns his earnings.

Key Terms
أهلية (ahliyyah): Legal capacity to acquire rights and obligations. For a slave, capacity for religious duties is present but capacity for financial transactions is blocked.
حجر (hajr): Restriction or interdiction preventing a person from exercising financial transactions due to a legal cause, such as minority or slavery.
رقيق (raqiq): Slave; a person owned by another, which prevents full legal capacity in financial matters.
مأذون (ma'dhun): A slave who has received permission from his master to engage in trade; he gains the right to transact and owns his earnings.
كتابة (kitabah): A contract of manumission by which a slave purchases his freedom in installments; the slave (mukatib) owns his person upon completion.
تصرف (tasarruf): Legal disposition or transaction, such as sale, purchase, or gift.

The Hanafi Rule on the Permitted Slave (Ma'dhun) – General Authority from Specific Permission
States the Hanafi ruling on the scope of a ma'dhun's trading authority

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that a master's permission to a slave in one type of trade grants general authority, and distinguish it from the mukatab's compensatory removal of restriction.

Core Idea
Permission in one trade implies all trades; the nature of the gift versus contract determines the scope of ownership.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi ruling: when a master permits his slave to trade in a specific commodity (e.g., food), the slave gains the right to trade in all commodities. This principle is derived from the nature of permission (idhn) which, like a gift, removes the restriction broadly. We will contrast this with the mukatab, whose trading authority is tied to a compensatory contract (kitaba). Understanding this distinction illuminates how the Hanafi school uses the compensation element to define different legal capacities.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and its Legal Category
The passage discusses a slave who is permitted to trade (ma'dhun). Identify the act: the master gives permission for a specific trade (e.g., food), and the effect is that the slave has absolute authority in all types of trade. This is a ruling on the scope of permission.
The ruling is a fiqh-rule with usul implications about the nature of idhn.
2
Isolate the Usul Principle
The principle is: 'al-idhn fi naw'in min al-tijarah yafid al-idhn fi kull naw'in' (permission in a type grants permission in all types). This is based on the idea that the master's intent is to enable the slave to earn, and the restriction of hajr is removed without compensation, like a gift.
This principle is specific to the Hanafi school; other schools may limit permission to the specified type.
3
Compare with Mukatab
The passage contrasts the ma'dhun with the mukatab. For the mukatab, removal of restriction (fakk al-hajr) is through kitaba, which is a compensatory contract (sale-like). Consequently, the mukatab owns his person towards freedom, while the ma'dhun only owns his acquired property.
Compensation changes the legal nature: non-compensatory removal resembles a gift (hiba), while compensatory removal resembles a sale (bay').
4
Apply the Rule to a New Scenario
Suppose a master tells his slave, 'I permit you to sell textiles.' The slave, under Hanafi law, can then sell not only textiles but also food, livestock, etc. The slave's hand (yad) controls all earnings, and he can be sued for debts arising from trade.
This contrasts with a limited agency where the agent can only act within the explicit scope.

Commentary
This passage is a fiqh ruling from the Hanafi school regarding a slave who has been given permission (idhn) by his master to engage in trade. The ruling states that if the master permits the slave in a specific type of trade (e.g., food trade), the slave gains general authority to trade in all types of trade. This is because permission in one type implies permission in all types, as the slave's hand (yad) becomes established over all his earnings, similar to a mukatab. However, there is a key difference: the ma'dhun only owns his acquired property, while the mukatab's ownership is more extensive. The reason is that the removal of the restriction (hajr) from the ma'dhun is without compensation, akin to a gift (hiba), whereas removal through kitaba is with compensation, like a sale.

Key Terms
المأذون (al-ma'dhun): A slave whom the master has given permission to engage in trade; such a slave has authority to trade and his earnings are his own, but he remains a slave.
المكاتب (al-mukatab): A slave who has a contract of kitaba with his master, agreeing to pay a certain sum for his freedom; he owns his earnings and his person is partially free.
فك الحجر (fakk al-hajr): The removal of the legal restriction (hajr) that prevents a slave from transacting; in the case of ma'dhun, it is without compensation; in the case of mukatab, it is with compensation.
