Defending Nazar: The Hanafi Response to the Sumaniyya
Refuting the Sumaniyya's denial of knowledge from nazar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Sumaniyya's objection to nazar and explain the two-part Hanafi response using distinguishing attributes and badaha.

Core Idea
Knowledge from reasoning is real – distinguished by attributes and confirmed by intuition.

The Sumaniyya argued that theoretical reasoning cannot yield certain knowledge because the result might be ignorance. This strikes at the heart of usul al-fiqh, where qiyas and ijtihad depend on the reliability of nazar. The Hanafi school answers with a twofold defense: first, knowledge has distinctive marks not shared with ignorance; second, our innate intuition (badaha) testifies that sound reasoning produces genuine knowledge. In this lesson, we unpack that argument and its significance for Hanafi methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the challenge
Understand the Sumaniyya's claim: nazar cannot produce knowledge because the same mental act can yield either certainty or false conviction, and there is no way to tell them apart.
They rely solely on sense perception.
2
Distinguish the two responses
The Hanafi answer has two parts: (1) knowledge has distinguishing attributes ('awarid) that ignorance lacks; (2) badaha (intuition) directly judges that correct nazar gives knowledge.
Part 1 is rational differentiation; part 2 appeals to immediate human awareness.
3
Apply the reasoning to a new case
Consider a scholar performing qiyas: the Sumaniyya would call it guesswork. Apply the Hanafi response to show why the scholar can be confident his conclusion is knowledge.
The attributes of a well-constructed qiyas (e.g., sound illa, agreement with usul) align with knowledge, not ignorance.

Commentary
This passage introduces a theological principle that is part of the foundations of usul al-fiqh: the validity of theoretical reasoning (nazar) in producing knowledge. The author summarizes that up to this point, he has covered the definition of nazar, its types, evidence, and the forms of qiyas. Now he presents the objection of the Sumaniyya, a group that denied that reasoning can yield certain knowledge. They argued that since the conviction obtained through reasoning could be false (ignorance mistaken for knowledge), there is no way to distinguish true knowledge from apparent certainty. The Hanafi response, as given by the author, is twofold: first, knowledge has distinguishing attributes (such as coherence, evidence, etc.) that are different from those of ignorance; second, basic human intuition (badaha) tells us that sound reasoning produces confidence that is distinct from ignorance. This defense is crucial because if nazar were unreliable, the entire discipline of qiyas and ijtihad would collapse. The author later will presumably present a rebuttal to this answer, but the passage ends with the positive response.

Key Terms
النظر (al-nazar): Theoretical reasoning or intellectual reflection aimed at acquiring knowledge.
السمنية (al-Sumaniyya): A group (possibly a sect) that denied knowledge can result from reasoning, relying only on sense perception.
البداهة (al-badaha): Intuition or immediate judgment that does not require proof.

Key Takeaway

Commanding the Impossible: Ash'ari Argument and Hanafi Response
Ash'ari argument that commanding the impossible must be valid because it occurs in revelation, with Hanafi response.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Ash'ari argument from actual occurrence and explain the Hanafi analysis that distinguishes the command from its occurrence.

Core Idea
A command remains valid even when Allah knows it will not be fulfilled—the command follows knowledge, not occurrence.

This passage presents a classic usul dispute: the Ash'aris argue that because Allah commands sinners despite knowing they will never obey, commanding the impossible must be permissible. The Hanafis respond by distinguishing the command itself from its actualization, explaining that the command is not impossible; only the occurrence is. Understanding this distinction clarifies how divine commands function as genuine legal instructions regardless of compliance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ash'ari claim and its evidence
Find the premise: 'if it were not valid, it would not occur' (لولم يصح لم يقع). The Ash'aris cite actual commands to sinners as proof that commanding the impossible is valid.
The evidence is naqli (observational) – the occurrence itself is taken as proof.
2
Understand the Hanafi critique of the inference
The Hanafi response denies that occurrence proves validity of the impossible. It distinguishes between the command and its occurrence: the command itself is not impossible; the impossibility is only in the occurrence (non-compliance).
Key: 'العام تابع للمعلوم وليس سببا له' – the general command follows what is known, not causing it.
3
Distinguish between the command and its outcome
The command is a request; its validity depends on the form, not on future compliance. The fact that it does not occur does not retroactively invalidate the command.
This preserves the obligation of the command and the reward/punishment based on intention.
4
Apply the principle to analogous cases
Consider the cases mentioned: a person who knows he will die before acting, or a command abrogated before action. In each, the command is valid but the occurrence is prevented by external factors, not by the command's invalidity.
This reinforces the Hanafi position that commands are valid regardless of eventual occurrence.

The proofs of those who say commanding the impossible in its occurrence is permissible said: First, if it were not valid, it would not occur, but it has occurred; because the sinner is commanded, while Allah knows that it will not occur, and that which contradicts His knowledge is impossible. Likewise, one who knows his death, and one for whom the command is abrogated before he is able to act. The answer: It is not impossible to conceptualize occurrence from him; rather it indicates that the actuality is non-occurrence. For the general [command] follows the known, and is not the cause of it.

Commentary
This passage presents an Ash'ari argument for the permissibility of commanding the impossible, followed by a Hanafi response. The argument: If commanding the impossible were invalid, it would never occur in divine commands. But it does occur, as seen in commands given to sinners whom Allah knows will never obey, or to someone who will die before acting, or to someone whose command is abrogated before they can comply. Therefore, commanding the impossible must be valid. The Hanafi response (likely from the author) explains that it is not impossible to conceive the occurrence from the commanded person; rather, the fact that it does not occur indicates that the actual outcome is non-occurrence. The general command (al-‘āmm) is dependent on what Allah knows will happen; it is not a cause for that outcome. Thus, the command itself remains valid, but the commanded act may not materialize due to Allah's foreknowledge. This preserves the validity of the command without requiring actual occurrence.

Key Terms
عام (‘āmm): A general command that applies to all individuals in a category, but its application is subject to Allah's knowledge of actual outcomes.
معلوم (ma‘lūm): That which is known by Allah in His eternal knowledge, including whether a commanded act will occur.
