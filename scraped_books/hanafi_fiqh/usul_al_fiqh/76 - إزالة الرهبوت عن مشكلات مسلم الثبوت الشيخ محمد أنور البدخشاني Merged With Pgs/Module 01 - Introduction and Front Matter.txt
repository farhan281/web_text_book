Core Idea
A commentary that unlocks the difficult passages of a core Hanafi uṣūl text.

This lesson introduces the book 'Izaalat al-Rahbūt', a commentary on the Hanafi uṣūl work 'Muslim al-Thubūt' by al-Qadi Muhhibb Allah al-Bihari. As a front matter piece, the title page tells us who wrote it, who prepared this edition, and hints at the scholarly lineage. Understanding this work's place in the Hanafi tradition is the first step to grasping the uṣūl principles it explains.

Guided Path
1
Identify the full title
The Arabic title 'إزالة الرهبوت عن مشكلات مسلم الثبوت' means 'Removing the Fear from the Difficulties of Muslim al-Thubut'. Note that 'Muslim al-Thubut' is the base text being commented upon.
The title itself indicates the purpose: to clarify challenging points in the original.
2
Name the author
The author is al-Qadi Muhhibb Allah al-Bihari (d. 1119 AH), a prominent Hanafi jurist and usuli scholar.
He is known for his works in both fiqh and usul al-fiqh.
3
Identify the editor/publisher
This edition was prepared by Muhammad Anwar al-Badakhshani, a sheikh of hadith at Jamia Ulum al-Islamiyya in Karachi. The publisher is associated with 'Banuri'.
The editor's role is to produce a reliable printed version of the manuscript.
4
Understand the base text
'Muslim al-Thubut' is a key Hanafi usul text. Its full title is 'Musallam al-Thubut' but often referred to as 'Muslim al-Thubut'. This commentary (sharh) explains its difficult passages.
The base text is itself a commentary on another work, but this lesson focuses on the commentary directly.
5
Appreciate the scholarly chain
Knowing the author and context helps situate this work within the Hanafi usul tradition. Al-Bihari's commentary is studied in advanced usul circles alongside the original text.
This aids in tracing ayyat al-istidlal (chains of reasoning) in later Hanafi scholarship.

Commentary
This passage is the introduction (mukaddimah) to the commentary 'Izalat al-Rahbut' by Sheikh Muhammad Anwar al-Badakhshani on 'Muslim al-Thubut' of Muhibbullah al-Bahari. The author begins with praise of Allah and attestation of faith. He then explains the importance of Islamic Sharia as a constitution suitable for all times, with built-in capacity for development under specific conditions. The secret of its perpetual applicability lies in its ability to transcend time and place. He argues that Islamic law has historically succeeded in diverse circumstances, proving its flexibility. The key science enabling this is Usul al-Fiqh, which provides the tools to derive rulings from primary sources (Quran and Sunnah) and apply them to new situations. The author highlights that the texts are finite while human needs are infinite, necessitating continuous ijtihad. Without this, Sharia would appear stagnant. Therefore, scholars must master usul principles to address emerging issues. This work combines the method of the Mutakallimin (theologians) who base principles on logical and theological premises. The author notes al-Bahari's concise style and his starting point with haqiqah and majaz (literal and metaphorical meaning). The passage sets the stage for the detailed exposition of usul al-fiqh that follows.

The Sixfold Analysis of the Masdar 'al-Hamd'
Grammatical analysis of the masdar 'hamd' and refutation of a counterargument

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the six interpretations of the masdar 'hamd' and explain why the resultant state is considered a real entity in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
The masdar 'hamd' yields six distinct meanings; its resultant state is a real binary sentence, not a mental construct.

After justifying the use of 'al-hamd', the author delves into a detailed grammatical analysis of the word itself. He identifies six possible interpretations for the masdar 'hamd', ranging from active and passive forms to states arising from the action. He then refutes the claim by Bahr al-'Ulum that the resultant state is merely mental, arguing that it exists as an actual binary sentence in the external world. This precision is typical of Hanafi usul, which relies on exact linguistic analysis for deriving correct meanings.

Guided Path
1
Understand the distinction between active/passive masdar and masdar built for doer/object
The author first distinguishes between the active masdar (masdar ma'lum) and passive masdar (masdar majhul), and then between masdar built for the doer and masdar built for the object. Active masdar refers to the act of praising itself (e.g., 'hamd' as praise), while passive masdar refers to the one praised. Masdar built for doer refers to the state of being a praiser (hamidiyya), and for object refers to the state of being praised (mahmudiyya).
These are not just synonyms; each has a distinct conceptual focus.
2
List the six possibilities
The author enumerates six possibilities: (1) active masdar, (2) passive masdar, (3) masdar built for doer, (4) masdar built for object, (5) resultant state from the active masdar, (6) resultant state from the passive masdar. Learn each definition.
A seventh possibility is dismissed as purely mental with no external existence.
3
Note the entailment relationships
The active masdar entails the masdar built for doer (if there is praise, there must be a praiser), and the passive masdar entails the masdar built for object (if there is a praised, there is a praised entity). This logical entailment is important for understanding how these concepts are connected.
4
Define the resultant state (al-hasil bi'l-masdar)
The resultant state from the active masdar is the condition or attribute that results from the action of praising, such as the state of being a praiser (al-hamidiyya) or the state of the act itself. The resultant state from the passive masdar is the condition of the one praised. The author gives examples: the state of the eater after eating, the state of the beaten after being beaten.
These resultant states are not just mental ideas; they have real existence as binary sentences.
5
Critically evaluate Bahr al-'Ulum's objection
Bahr al-'Ulum claimed the resultant state is purely mental. The author refutes this by arguing that the resultant state, when considered with its operative verb, forms a binary sentence (e.g., 'Zaid ate' results in the state of eatingness). Such sentences exist in reality. Therefore, the resultant state has external existence.
This step demonstrates how Hanafi usul defends the ontological reality of linguistic constructs.

Commentary
This passage is the introductory front matter of the commentary, establishing the foundational principle that complete human happiness requires the perfection of both the soul (through knowledge) and the body (through ethical character). The author first defines two key terms: 'al-qawl' (statement) refers to knowledge related to legal texts or rulings from the Lawgiver's speech, while 'al-hal' (state) refers to the acquisition and embodiment of lawful moral traits until they become a disposition in the heart, producing good character with ease. A Persian couplet is cited to emphasize moving beyond mere words to inner states and seeking the company of a perfect teacher who combines complete knowledge and complete character. 

The core thesis is that happiness in this world and the next (al-sa'adah fi al-darayn) comes through the perfection of the rational soul and the material body. The soul, though abstract in essence, requires the body for administration and action. If one acquires knowledge but does not translate it into ethical behavior and action, that knowledge becomes a burden and a cause of punishment, not a means to salvation. The text uses a striking metaphor: knowledge that enters the heart and leads to action is a helper, but knowledge that stays only in the head (memory) without heartfelt acceptance is like a snake that bites. 

Perfection of the soul is called 'tahqeeq' (verification) — arriving at true certainty in acquiring all Islamic sciences, resulting in a disposition of correct beliefs. Perfection of the body is called 'takhalluq' (character formation) — acquiring a practical disposition to perform commands and avoid prohibitions. Note that 'khuluq' (character), 'malakah' (disposition), and 'muqam' (station) are different terms but share a single reality. 

These two perfections are achieved by two means: 'tafaqquh fi al-din' (deep understanding of the religion) and 'tabahhur' (being well-versed) in the 'mawaqif' (stations) of truth and certainty. 'Tafaqquh' is derived from Quran 9:122 and the hadith on understanding religion, and it encompasses all dimensions of Islam: beliefs, worship, morals, transactions, politics, and economics. 'Tabahhur' literally means going into the depth of the sea, and here it means penetrating deeply into religious issues and their proofs. The 'mawaqif' (stations) refer to the sources: the Book (Quran) and the Sunnah. 

Thus, this introductory passage sets the tone for the entire work: sound knowledge (usul al-fiqh) must be accompanied by ethical character and practice; both together lead to true happiness.

Core Idea
Tahqiq proves a claim; tadqiq proves the evidence itself—a recursive demonstration of usul truths.

This passage from the commentary on Musallam al-Thubut introduces the author's methodological claims. The Four Types of Definition in Usul al-Fiqh
Classifies real and formal definitions into complete and incomplete types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between real, formal, and nominal definitions in Hanafi usul al-fiqh and classify them into complete/incomplete types.

Core Idea
Definitions in usul are either real (through essence) or formal (through accident), and both have complete and incomplete forms—nominal definitions are conventional.

In this lesson, you will explore the foundational classification of definitions used in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. Understanding whether a definition is real or nominal is crucial for correctly identifying the essence of a legal concept and deriving rulings. The passage distinguishes four types and introduces a fifth viewpoint.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
Recognize that definitions are divided into real (ḥaqīqī) and formal (rasmī) categories. Real definitions capture the essence (dhātiyyāt), while formal definitions use accidental properties.
This division is foundational in both logic and usul.
2
Subdivide each category
Both real and formal definitions can be complete (tāmm) or incomplete (nāqiṣ). A complete real definition (ḥadd tāmm) includes all essential properties; an incomplete one (ḥadd nāqiṣ) misses some. Similarly for formal definitions (rasm tāmm and rasm nāqiṣ).
This yields four types initially.
3
Note the fifth type: nominal definition
According to al-Tawdih, there is a fifth type: the nominal definition (ta‘rīf ismī), which defines conventional essences (māhiyyāt i‘tibāriyya) like shar‘ī terms.
Shar‘ī terms such as ṣalāh or zakāh are conventional, not real essences.
4
Understand the scholarly difference
Some scholars include nominal definition within real definition; al-Tawdih treats it separately. This affects how we categorize definitions of fiqh terms.
Hanafi usul often favors treating shar‘ī terms as nominal definitions.

The topics include discussions on the permissibility of prohibiting unspecified things, the status of recommended acts, the nature of concessions and determinations, the impossibility of obliging the impossible, debates on power (qudra) in relation to action (Ash'ari vs Maturidi), obligation of the disbeliever in subsidiary matters, and various other usul issues. As a front matter, it provides an overview of the book's structure and subjects.

Key Terms
المندوب (al-mandub): A recommended act; one that is not obligatory but rewarded.
العزيمة (al-azimah): An original ruling that is constant and not replaced by a concession.
الرخصة (al-rukhsah): A concessionary ruling that replaces the original ruling due to an excuse.
القدرة (al-qudrah): Capacity or power to perform an act; its relation to the act is debated.
