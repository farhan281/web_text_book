Three Types of Time for Time-Bound Obligations and the Debate on Performance
Defines three types of time for time-bound obligations and debates the condition of performance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of time for a time-bound obligation, explain the Hanafi ruling on intention for Ramadan vs vowed fasts, and evaluate the debate on whether time is a condition for the performance (الأداء) or the performed act (المؤدى).

Core Idea
Time for an obligation is a vessel, a standard, or mixed; intention rules depend on whether time itself specifies the duty.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text classifies time for time-bound obligations into three categories: vessel (extensive), standard (narrow), and a hybrid. The author also delves into a subtle debate about the nature of 'performance' (الأداء) and whether time conditions the act of performing or the performed act itself. Understanding this distinction is key to grasping Hanafi usul on time-conditioned duties.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three types of time
Read the opening: time is either excessive (zarf), equal (mi'yar), or resembling both. List them.
The text uses 'فضل' for excessive and 'ساوى' for equal.
2
Analyze the 'zarf' type and its three considerations
Zarf time has three roles: cause, vessel, condition. Note the clarification that the 'contained' (المؤدى) is not identical to the 'conditioned' (الأداء). This sets up the debate.
The author insists on the distinction between the verbal noun (الأداء) and the passive participle (المؤدى).
3
Analyze the 'mi'yar' type and intention rules
Mi'yar time can be a cause (Ramadan) or not (a vow). For Ramadan, absolute intention suffices because the time specifies; Hanafis differ from the majority. For a vow, specific intention is needed.
Contrast with the traveler's case where intention for concession is needed.
4
Examine the hybrid type: Hajj
Hajj time is like both: only one per year, but act doesn't fill time. Thus absolute intention suffices for the obligatory Hajj.
This shows that the classification is not binary but a spectrum.
5
Evaluate the rebuttal against Ibn al-Humam
The author refutes Ibn al-Humam's claim that 'الأداء' in the condition means 'المؤدى'. The author argues that الأداء is conceptual and cannot be the condition; the condition must be a real thing, which is the performed act (المؤدى).
This is a deep usul point about the nature of conditions and the existence of conceptual entities.

Commentary
This passage is a usul definition lesson on the division of time for time-bound obligations (الواجب الموقت). The author defines three types of time: 1) ظرف (vessel/extensive): time that exceeds the duration needed for the act, like prayer times. This time is a cause for obligation, a vessel for the performed act, and a condition for performance. The author clarifies that the 'contained' (المظروف = المؤدى) is not identical to the 'conditioned' (المشروط = الأداء) because الأداء (performance) is a verbal noun (conceptual) while المؤدى (performed act) is a passive participle (what is performed). However, a rebuttal against Ibn al-Humam (from al-Tahrir) follows: Ibn al-Humam claimed that when the time is a condition, 'الأداء' really means 'المؤدى' (the done act), so they are identical. The author argues against this, insisting that الأداء is the conceptual act of the doer and cannot be the same as the performed act, which is the object. 2) معيار (standard/narrow): time exactly sufficient for the act, e.g., Ramadan for fasting. It may be a cause for obligation (like Ramadan, where no other time is valid) or not (like a specific vow). In Ramadan, no intention of 'specification' is needed because the time itself specifies; Hanafis allow absolute intention here, unlike the majority. For a specific vow, intention must be specific. 3) ما يشبه الظرف والمعيار (resembling both): like Hajj time, which is narrow enough to allow only one Hajj per year but not so narrow that the act fills the whole time; thus it has aspects of both. The passage ends mid-rebuttal against Ibn al-Humam, but the key definitions are established.

Key Terms
الظرف (al-zarf): Time that is more than the duration of the obligation; acts as cause, vessel, and condition.
المعيار (al-mi'yar): Time equal to the duration of the obligation; may be a cause (like Ramadan) or not (like a vow).
الأداء (al-ada'): The performance of the obligation; a verbal noun that is conceptual.
المؤدى (al-mu'adda): The performed act; the passive participle of الأداء.
النذر المعين (al-nadhr al-mu'ayyan): A vow specified for a particular time (e.g., fasting on a specific day).

The Obligation of Prerequisites (Muqaddimat al-Wajib)
Usul dispute on obligation of prerequisites

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the obligation of prerequisites of a wajib, identify the rational proof (dalil aqli) used, and apply the principle to distinguish absolute vs. restricted obligations.

Core Idea
If Allah commands an act, He necessarily commands its prerequisites — otherwise obligation becomes impossible.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, a central question is whether the prerequisites of an obligatory act are themselves obligatory. For example, if prayer is obligatory, is wudu also obligatory? This lesson unpacks the Hanafi position, which holds that all prerequisites—whether causes or conditions—are wajib by implicit command. We will examine the rational proof, the opposing views, and the practical consequences of this principle for deriving rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
The issue is whether the prerequisite (muqaddima) of a wajib is itself wajib. Recognize that this is a dispute between schools; the text states multiple positions.
The positions range from 'not wajib at all' to 'wajib only for causes' to 'wajib for all prerequisites'.
2
State the Hanafi position clearly
The Hanafi school holds that all prerequisites are wajib, whether they are a cause (sabab) or a condition (shart), and whether they are shar‘i, intellectual, or customary. This is the view adopted in the text.
Example: wudu is a shar‘i condition for prayer, leaving the opposite act is an intellectual condition, washing part of the head is a customary condition for washing the face.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof (dalil aqli)
The proof is rational: if an act is wajib but its prerequisite is not, the act could become impossible to perform. The text states: 'Obligating the act without obligating its preliminary leads to obligation of the impossible (التكليف بالمحال).' This is supported by consensus that causes of wajib are wajib and causes of haram are haram.
The proof does not rely on a specific verse or hadith but on the rational necessity of enabling the commanded act.
4
Examine the Hanafi response to objections
Opponents argue that if prerequisites were wajib, one would need to understand the obligator (i.e., know the command explicitly). The Hanafi response: the obligation is implicit, not explicit; the command entails its prerequisites without being stated. Hence only one sin is incurred for missing the original wajib.
The principle 'إيجاب المشروط إيجاب الشرط' (obligation of the conditioned entails obligation of the condition) is key.
5
Apply the distinction: absolute vs. restricted wajib
An absolute wajib (wajib mutlaq) is not restricted by its prerequisites (e.g., prayer is wajib regardless of wudu). A restricted wajib (wajib muqayyad) is conditional upon the prerequisite (e.g., Hajj is wajib only if one has ability). This distinction determines when the prerequisite itself is wajib.
For a restricted wajib, the prerequisite (e.g., ability for Hajj) is not wajib if the condition is absent, but if the condition is present, the prerequisite becomes wajib to enable the act.

Commentary
This passage presents a classic usul dispute: Are the prerequisites (muqaddima) of an obligatory act (wajib) themselves obligatory? The Hanafi position, following the principle of 'obligation of the conditioned entails obligation of its condition', holds that all prerequisites—whether cause (sabab) or condition (shart), and whether shar'i, intellectual, or customary—are wajib. The proof is rational: if the act is wajib but its prerequisite is not, it could lead to an impossibility (e.g., the wajib cannot be performed without the prerequisite). The text also defines two types of wajib: absolute (mutlaq), which is not restricted by its prerequisites (e.g., prayer is wajib regardless of wudu being a prerequisite), and restricted (muqayyad), which is conditional upon a prerequisite (e.g., Hajj is wajib only if one has ability). The passage then explains the distinction between cause (sabab) and condition (shart): a cause directly leads to the wajib, while a condition is simply required for the wajib's existence. Finally, it notes a variant opinion that obligation of prerequisites applies only to causes. This lesson is crucial for understanding how Hanafi usul derives the obligation of preparatory acts (like wudu for prayer).

شرط (shart): A condition upon which the wajib depends but does not lead to it (e.g., wudu for prayer)

Defining Muqaddimat al-Wajib: Types and Distinctions
Definition and classification of muqaddima

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define muqaddimat al-wajib, distinguish between al-wajib al-mutlaq and al-wajib al-muqayyad, and classify a preliminary as sabab or shart (shar'i, aqli, adi).

Core Idea
A muqaddima is what a wajib depends on; its obligation stems from the original wajib.

Before we can discuss why a preliminary becomes obligatory, we must first understand what counts as a preliminary and how it relates to the original obligation. This passage from a Hanafi usul text lays out the key definitions and classifications that form the foundation of the ruling on muqaddimat al-wajib.

Guided Path
1
Define muqaddima
Identify the definition given in the text: 'that on which the wajib depends' (ما يتوقف عليه الواجب). Note that this definition covers all types of preliminaries.
The text explicitly states this definition at the beginning.
2
Distinguish al-wajib al-mutlaq from al-wajib al-muqayyad
Compare the examples: prayer (salat) is absolute because its obligation is not explicitly conditioned by wudu, whereas hajj is restricted by istita'ah and zakat by nisab. Understand that absolute does not mean unconditional; it means the original command does not mention the precondition.
The author uses 'absolute' to describe the ruling itself, not the act.
3
Differentiate sabab from shart
Read the definitions: sabab 'leads to' the wajib externally (like sale leads to ownership for zakat), while shart is something on which the wajib depends but does not cause it (like wudu for prayer).
This distinction is crucial for the later dispute on obligation scope.
4
Identify the three types of shart
List the three types with examples: shart shar'i (wudu), shart aqli (abandoning the opposite of an action), shart adi (washing part of the head when washing the face). Understand that the Hanafi position includes all three as muqaddimat.
The text explicitly mentions these types after the author's ruling.

Commentary
This passage from the Hanafi usul commentary defines and discusses the obligation of muqaddimat al-wajib (the preliminaries of an obligatory act). It opens by defining the concept of muqaddima as that on which a wajib act depends. It then distinguishes between two types of wajib: al-wajib al-mutlaq (absolute wajib), which is not explicitly tied to its precondition in the original text of obligation (e.g., salat, which has wudu as a condition but the obligation of salat is not explicitly conditioned by wudu in the Quran), and al-wajib al-muqayyad (restricted wajib), which is explicitly conditioned by its prerequisite (e.g., hajj conditioned by istita'ah in Quran 3:97). The author states that the muqaddima of an absolute wajib is itself wajib in an absolute sense, meaning whether that muqaddima is a sabab (cause) or a shart (condition). The sabab is something external that leads to the wajib (like sale leading to ownership for zakat), while the shart is something on which the wajib depends but does not cause it (like wudu for salat). The shart is further divided into three: legal (shart shar'i, e.g., wudu), rational (shart aqli, e.g., abandoning the opposite of an action), and customary (shart adi, e.g., washing part of the head when washing the face). The passage then presents a disagreement (ikhtilaf) on the exact scope of obligation of muqaddimat al-wajib. Three opinions are reported: (1) Only the sabab is wajib; (2) Only the legal shart (shart shar'i) is wajib, not rational or customary; (3) No muqaddima is wajib at all. The author implicitly advocates the first and strongest position (the one attributed to 'us' i.e., the Hanafi school) that muqaddimat al-wajib are wajib absolutely. The proof given is rational: if Allah commands an act (wajib), but does not command its necessary precondition, then performing the act would be impossible, leading to an impossible obligation (taklif bil muhal), which is negated by the Quranic verse 'Allah does not burden a soul beyond its capacity' (2:286). Additionally, the consensus that acquiring the causes of the wajib is obligatory supports this. The passage also addresses an objection: that the obligation of the muqaddima might be for itself (like iman) rather than for the sake of the original wajib. The Hanafi response is that the discussion here concerns the obligation derived from the original wajib's dependence; if the muqaddima has no other independent proof of obligation, it becomes wajib by necessity to avoid impossibility. Finally, a question is raised about the absence of an explicit command for the muqaddima; the answer clarifies that obligation of the muqaddima is not by explicit command but by entailment: obligating the conditioned (wajib) obligates the condition (muqaddima).

The Obligation of Muqaddimat al-Wajib: Proof and Dispute
Proof and dispute on obligation of muqaddima

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi proof for the obligation of muqaddimat al-wajib, explain the Quranic verse used, and evaluate the three opposing views in the ikhtilaf.

Core Idea
Without obligating the muqaddima, the original wajib becomes impossible, contradicting Allah's justice.

Now that we know what muqaddimat al-wajib are, we need to understand why they become obligatory themselves. The Hanafi school provides a powerful rational proof grounded in Quranic principle. This lesson unpacks that proof and the scholarly disagreement surrounding it.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi claim
The author asserts that muqaddimat al-wajib are wajib unconditionally (mutlaqan), meaning for all types of sabab and shart.
This is the position defended by the Hanafi school.
2
Understand the rational proof
The argument: if Allah commands a wajib but does not obligate its necessary muqaddima, then performing the wajib becomes impossible, leading to taklif bil muhal (impossible burden). This is negated by Quran 2:286.
The proof is a reductio ad absurdum.
3
Apply the Quranic verse
Quran 2:286 proves that Allah does not burden anyone beyond capacity. If the muqaddima is not wajib, the original wajib cannot be performed, which would be an impossible burden. So the muqaddima must be wajib.
This is a qat'i proof from the Quran.
4
Examine the ikhtilaf
Three opposing views: (1) only sabab is wajib, (2) only shart shar'i is wajib, (3) no muqaddima is wajib at all. Understand the justification for each.
The author rejects these as weakening the necessity of the proof.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response to objections
One objection: the muqaddima might be wajib for itself (like iman) not for the wajib. The Hanafi response: we are discussing cases where the only reason for obligation is the dependence of the original wajib. In such cases, the muqaddima is wajib to avoid impossibility.
This shows the precision of the usul argument.

The passage also addresses an objection: that the obligation of the muqaddima might be for itself (like iman) rather than for the sake of the original wajib. The Hanafi response is that the discussion here concerns the obligation derived from the original wajib's dependence; if the muqaddima has no other independent proof of obligation, it becomes wajib by necessity to avoid impossibility. Finally, a question is raised about the absence of an explicit command for the muqaddima; the answer clarifies that obligation of the muqaddima is not by explicit command but by entailment: obligating the conditioned (wajib) obligates the condition (muqaddima).

Key Terms
مقدمة الواجب (muqaddimat al-wajib): ما يتوقف عليه الواجب (that on which the obligatory act depends)
تكليف بالمحال (taklif bil muhal): Impossible obligation: a burden that cannot be fulfilled
السبب (sabab): ما يكون مفضياً إلى الواجب، ويكون خارجاً عنه (that which leads to the wajib and is external to it)
الشرط (shart): ما يتوقف عليه الواجب، ولا يكون مفضياً إليه (that on which the wajib depends but does not lead to it)
