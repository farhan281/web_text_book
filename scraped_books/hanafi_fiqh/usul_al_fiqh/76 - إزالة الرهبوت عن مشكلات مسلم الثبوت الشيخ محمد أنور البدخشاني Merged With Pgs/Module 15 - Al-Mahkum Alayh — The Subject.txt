Ruling for a Person Unreached by Da'wah
Deriving ruling for the person in isolation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the ruling for a person who reaches puberty in isolation without da'wah by applying the Hanafi usul principle that punishment depends on revealed law.

Core Idea
No messenger, no punishment: the Quran explicitly ties punishment to the sending of a messenger.

This lesson examines a key furu' (branch) of the usul disagreement on rational obligation. The case: a person who reaches puberty in a remote mountain, never reached by any prophetic call. The Hanafi majority, following the Ash'ari position, rules that such a person is not punished for disbelief. The proof is the Quranic verse 17:15. We will follow the istinbat chain and understand why the majority prefers this view.

Guided Path
1
Define the case
Identify the subject: a person who reaches puberty in isolation (e.g., a high mountain) where no da’wah (prophetic call) reaches. He holds no beliefs and performs no religious acts.
2
State the ikhtilaf
The Mu'tazila and a group of Hanafis hold that reason alone obligates faith, so this person is punished. The Ash'aris and majority Hanafis hold that obligation comes only through revelation, so no punishment.
Know which position is mufta bihi.
3
Locate the dalil
The majority relies on Quran 17:15: 'And We were not to punish until We send a messenger.' This verse is qat'i al-thubut and qat'i al-dalalah.
Dalil is naqli (revealed text).
4
Explain wajh al-dalalah
The verse explicitly makes punishment conditional on the sending of a messenger. Since no messenger reached this person, the condition is absent, so punishment is negated.
This is a clear cause-effect link.
5
Conclude with tarjih
The majority Hanafi position (with Ash'aris) is preferred because it is based on a clear Quranic text. The opposing view relies on rational obligation, which is overridden by the explicit verse.
This is the operative ruling for fatwa.

Commentary
This passage applies the previous discussion to a concrete case: a person who reaches puberty in remote mountains without receiving any prophetic call, and who does not believe or practice any law. The ruling differs based on the stance on rational obligation. The Mu'tazila and some Hanafis hold that reason alone obligates faith, so such a person is punished. The Ash'aris and majority of Hanafis disagree, arguing that obligation and punishment are matters of divine law, which did not reach this person. They cite the Quranic verse (17:15) that punishment occurs only after sending a messenger. This is a key furu' (branch) of the disagreement about rational good and evil.

The Three Meanings of Husn and Qubh in Hanafi Usul
Defining three meanings of husn and qubh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the three meanings of husn and qubh and identify which are rationally knowable according to Hanafi/Maturidi usul.

Core Idea
Husn (good) and qubh (evil) have three meanings; only the first (perfection/deficiency) and third (desert of reward/punishment) are rational in Hanafi usul.

This passage clarifies the concept of rational good and evil (al-husn wa al-qubh al-aqliyyan). The author distinguishes three distinct meanings: (1) conformity to perfection or deficiency, (2) suitability to nature, and (3) deserving praise/blame and reward/punishment. The first meaning is universally accepted as rational. The third meaning is the point of contention between the Hanafi/Maturidi and Ash'ari schools. This lesson unpacks these definitions and their methodological stakes.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three meanings
From the passage, list the three meanings of husn and qubh. The first relates to perfection/deficiency, the second to suitability/unsuitability to nature, and the third to desert of reward/punishment.
The author explicitly mentions the first and third; the second is implied from standard usul discussions.
2
Note agreement and disagreement
Determine which meaning is agreed upon by all schools (Ash'ari, Hanafi, Mu'tazila) and which is disputed. The first meaning is agreed; the third is disputed. The second is not discussed here.
Ash'aris affirm the first meaning as rational but deny the third; Mu'tazila affirm both.
3
Apply to a pre-revelation obligation
Consider the obligation of gratitude to God before revelation. According to Hanafi/Maturidi, it is rationally wajib because gratitude is ‘good’ in the third meaning. Ash'ari would say it is not known without revelation.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence of the dispute).

Commentary
This passage is a dispute in usul al-fiqh regarding the nature of good (husn) and evil (qubh). The author, following the Maturidi/Hanafi school, argues that certain actions are rationally good or evil independently of divine revelation. He distinguishes three meanings of husn and qubh: (1) conformity to perfection or deficiency, (2) being a cause for praise/blame in this world and reward/punishment in the hereafter (this is the disputed third meaning). The Ash'aris accept the first meaning as rational but deny the third. The Mu'tazila affirm both. The author argues that if miracles could appear through liars, prophethood would be undermined, and since lying is a deficiency, God cannot do it. He critiques the Ash'ari theologian al-Iji's argument in al-Mawaqif that deficiency in actions only relates to the first meaning of qubh, showing that this would still entail the third meaning because attributing deficiency to God is impossible. He then addresses an objection: if attributing deficiency to God is impossible, then Ash'aris must also deny that God can punish the obedient (since that would be injustice/deficiency). The Ash'ari response: punishment has two aspects; as a servant, God has full authority over His property, so no deficiency. This shows the nuanced debate on the limits of rational moral judgment in theology and law.

The Rational Impossibility of Lying from God and the Argument from Prophethood
Arguing against the possibility of miracles for liars

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi argument that lying is rationally impossible for God and how this supports the requirement of miracles to confirm prophethood.

Core Idea
If lying were not rationally evil, miracles could be performed by liars, making prophethood indistinguishable from fraud.

This passage builds on the distinction of three meanings of husn and qubh. The author argues that if miracles could be performed by a liar, the proof of prophethood would collapse. Since lying is a deficiency (first meaning) and thus rationally evil (third meaning), God cannot attribute lying to Himself. Therefore, miracles cannot occur through liars. The author then critiques al-Iji's argument from al-Mawaqif and addresses an Ash'ari objection regarding God's punishment of the obedient.

Guided Path
1
Set up the hypothetical problem
The author states: If the display of miracles at the hands of a liar were possible (i.e., not prevented), then the proof of prophethood would be undermined. Understand why: miracles are the primary evidence that a person is a true prophet. If a liar could also perform miracles, we could not distinguish a true prophet from an impostor.
This is a reductio ad absurdum argument.
2
Identify the Ash'ari response
The text mentions an Ash'ari response: 'it is a deficiency, and it has already passed that there is no dispute about it.' They acknowledge that lying is a deficiency (first meaning) and thus rationally evil, even before revelation. So they agree that God cannot lie. But the argument from prophethood requires explaining why miracles cannot occur through a liar.
Ash'aris accept the first meaning but not the third. Their response seems to concede the point, but the author pushes further.
3
Follow the author's refutation of al-Mawaqif
The author critiques the argument in al-Mawaqif that deficiency in actions (like lying) relates only to the first meaning of rational evil. He argues that this concession leads to the conclusion that lying is rationally evil for God, but then the third meaning also becomes relevant: if attributing deficiency to God is impossible, then attributing evil in the third meaning (deserving blame) is also impossible. This strengthens the Maturidi position.
The author uses the concept of essential necessity (wujub dhati) to bridge the meanings.
4
Analyze the Ash'ari counter on punishing the obedient
The author raises a problem: if attributing deficiency to God is impossible, then Ash'aris, who allow God to punish the obedient, must deny that possibility because that would be an injustice (deficiency). The Ash'ari response is that punishment has two aspects: as a servant, God has full authority; as an obedient person, it would be injustice. They prioritize the master's authority over the servant, citing the Quranic verse 'If You punish them, they are Your servants' (5:118). Thus no deficiency.
This shows the nuanced debate on how to define deficiency in divine actions.

Distinguishing Habitual and Intrinsic Capacity in Taklif
Refuting that allowing obligation of the impossible leads to obligation of animals

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between habitual capacity (isti'dad 'adi) and intrinsic capacity (isti'dad dhati) and apply this distinction to refute the argument that obligating animals is not impossible.

Core Idea
Animals lack habitual capacity for action, not intrinsic capacity; thus their obligation is irrelevant, not impossible.

This passage addresses a rationalist challenge: if Allah can obligate the impossible (like combining opposites), why can He not obligate animals? After all, obligating an animal seems less far-fetched. The Hanafi response reframes the issue: the impossibility lies not in the act of obligating, but in the lack of a specific type of capacity—the habitual readiness to act. This lesson unpacks that critical distinction and shows how it protects the coherence of taklif (legal obligation).
