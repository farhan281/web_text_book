Nazzala vs Anzala and the Definition of Ayah
Explains nazzala/anzala difference and defines ayah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between nazzala and anzala and define ayah both linguistically and terminologically.

Core Idea
The verb form nazzala (geminated) indicates gradual revelation, while anzala indicates one-time descent; ayah is a 'sign' — literally and legally.

This passage clarifies a subtle but crucial distinction in Qur'anic vocabulary. Understanding the difference between nazzala and anzala affects how we interpret the timing of revelation, and knowing the dual definition of ayah — as a linguistic 'sign' and a technical 'verse' — is fundamental for using the Qur'an as legal evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the verb forms
Locate the two verbs nazzala (with shaddah) and anzala (with alif) in the text. Note the morphological difference.
The shaddah on the second radical indicates intensification or repetition in Arabic.
2
Understand the distinction
The author explains that nazzala means gradual revelation (star by star), while anzala means one-time descent from the Preserved Tablet to the lowest heaven.
This is based on Qur'anic usage: anzala often refers to the initial descent, nazzala to the gradual revelation to the Prophet.
3
Define ayah linguistically
The text states that in language, ayah means an absolute sign (علامة). Make sure to distinguish this from the technical definition.
Examples of linguistic usage: 'the sign of rain clouds' etc.
4
Define ayah terminologically
In Islamic terminology, ayah is a piece of Allah's speech with a beginning and an end, linked to the validity of prayer and i'jaz (inimitability).
The 'validity of prayer' refers to the requirement of reciting a verse in salah for it to be valid.
5
Connect linguistic and technical meanings
The author explains that a verse serves as a sign of prophethood. This shows how the linguistic concept of 'sign' is carried into the technical definition.
This connection is important for usul al-fiqh: the Qur'an as evidence (dalil) relies on each verse being a sign.

Commentary
This passage is an explanatory commentary (sharh) on the opening lines of the matn 'Musallam al-Thubut'. The author, Shaykh Muhammad Anwar al-Badakhshani, begins with a grammatical analysis of the matn's phrases, clarifying that the verb 'tala'a' (rose) can be read with God as the subject or with 'ad-din' as the subject, leading to two meanings: either God made religion manifest or religion itself became manifest. He then parses the word 'taba'a' (stamped), noting two possible readings affecting the case of 'al-yaqin' (certainty). The core theological point emerges when explaining 'Rabbanā' (Our Lord): the fronting of 'laka' (to You) indicates restriction—all true, fixed existence belongs solely to Allah, as He is self-existent, unlike contingent beings whose existence is derived and perishable. The commentary cites Quranic verses (28:88, 55:27) to support this. The term 'majāz' (metaphor) is then defined: the existence of all contingent beings is metaphorical because it is not self-subsistent but derived from Allah, as proven by verses like 39:62 and 2:28, and the hadith 'God was and there was nothing else'. The author notes a rhetorical benefit: the use of 'haqiqah' and 'majāz' in the introduction exemplifies 'barāʿat al-istihlāl'—a prologue that hints at the main topics. Returning to the matn, 'laka al-amru ḥaqīqatan' (to You is the command truly) is again fronted for restriction, and 'al-amr' here refers to the divine command of creation and legislation. The final part explains the matn's supplication that all means and ends ('aʿinnah', 'mabādī', 'nawāṣī', 'maqāṣid') are dependent on Allah. 'Mabdaʾ' is defined terminologically as that upon which something else depends, with examples: the definer for the defined, the proof for the conclusion, and time for prayer. The overall message is that all beginnings, instruments, and objectives are subject to Allah's will and favor.

Key Terms
الحقيقة (al-ḥaqīqah): The fixed, real, actual existence that belongs in truth to Allah alone, as opposed to the metaphorical existence of contingent beings.
المجاز (al-majāz): The existence of contingent beings, which is not self-subsistent but derived from the Necessary Existence; it is opposite to haqiqah.
براعة الاستهلال (barāʿat al-istihlāl): A rhetorical device where the author uses words in the introduction that hint at the topics and rulings to be discussed in the main body of the work.
المبدأ (al-mabdaʾ): That upon which something depends, whether in existence, knowledge, or condition; e.g., the definer for the defined, the proof for the conclusion, time for prayer.

The Prophet's Comprehensive Speech (Jawami' al-Kalim)
Analysis of the attribute 'jawami' al-kalim' as a prophetic miracle.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the concept of jawami' al-kalim, identify its linguistic and legal significance in Hanafi usul, and evaluate its role as a proof of prophethood.

Core Idea
Jawami' al-kalim: concise words with vast meanings, a prophetic miracle and an usul tool for understanding divine intent.

The passage describes the Prophet Muhammad as 'al-mub'athu bi jawami' al-kalim' (sent with comprehensive speech). This lesson unpacks this attribute: it refers to the Quran and the Prophet's own words that are concise yet packed with meaning, covering both worldly and otherworldly matters. Such conciseness is a miracle, as even the most eloquent cannot produce words like 'iyyaka nasta'in'. We explore its significance in Hanafi usul, where such compressed texts require careful interpretation to derive multiple rulings.

Guided Path
1
Parse the phrase 'jawami' al-kalim'
Identify that it is a construct phrase (idaafah) where 'jawami' (pl. of jaami') is an adjective describing 'al-kalim' (words). The commentary explains it as 'words that encompass various meanings despite conciseness'.
This is a key descriptor of the Prophet's speech, including the Quran.
2
Examine the Quranic examples given
The passage provides verses: 'iyyaka nasta'in' (Quran 1:5) and 'rabbana atina fid-dunya hasanah wa fil-akhirati hasanah' (Quran 2:201). Analyze how these verses are concise yet comprehensive.
These are primary examples of jawami' al-kalim in the Quran.
3
Understand the grammatical analysis
The commentary notes that the idaafah 'jawami' al-kalim' is of the type where an adjective attaches to a noun; also the plural-to-plural construction indicates generality and inclusiveness. Similarly, 'afham al-umam' indicates universality of the Prophet's message.
This grammatical point underscores the universal scope of the Prophet's mission.
4
Relate to the miracle of the Quran
The passage states that the Prophet brought what the eloquent could not match. This refers to the i'jaz (inimitability) of the Quran, which is a proof of his prophethood.
Jawami' al-kalim is one aspect of the Quran's i'jaz.
5
Apply to usul: interpreting concise texts
In Hanafi usul, concise texts (like 'iyyaka nasta'in') require careful extraction of rulings. The generality of the wording may indicate a universal principle. For example, 'iyyaka nasta'in' implies that all forms of isti'aanah (seeking help) are directed to Allah alone.
This principle affects how Hanafi jurists derive rules from such verses.

Al-Razi's Objection to Defining Essence and the Hanafi Response
A logical argument against real definitions and its rebuttal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain al-Razi's objection to real definition and evaluate the Hanafi response using the concept of taḥṣīl al-ḥāṣil.

Core Idea
A real definition (ḥadd) that properly orders genus and differentia yields new knowledge of the essence and avoids circularity.

In usul al-fiqh, precise definitions (ḥudūd) of legal terms like ṣalāh or zakāh are foundational. Imam Fakhr al-Din al-Razi raised a powerful logical objection: defining an essence either by itself, by its parts, or by external accidents—each seems futile or circular. This lesson unpacks his argument and the classical response, which defends the validity of real definition. Understanding this debate sharpens your ability to construct and critique definitions in Hanafi legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify al-Razi's trilemma
Al-Razi argues that defining an essence (māhiyya) must fall into one of three categories: by itself (circular), by its parts (already known → obtaining the obtained), or by external accidents (don't capture essence). Each is problematic.
The key term taḥṣīl al-ḥāṣil (obtaining the obtained) is the fallacy of redundancy.
2
Analyze the third alternative
The third alternative (defining by accidents) fails because accidents are external and do not reveal the quiddity. So real definition seems impossible.
This challenges the very possibility of ḥadd ḥaqīqī.
3
Understand the response's structure
The response shows that when parts (genus and differentia) are arranged in a specific order—general first (e.g., animal) then specific qualifying it (e.g., rational)—the composite forms a unitary concept not previously known as a whole. This provides new knowledge.
The order and qualification (taqyīd) are crucial: the differentia becomes a description of the genus.
4
Apply to Hanafi legal definitions
In Hanafi usul, definitions of legal terms follow this structure. For example, defining 'ṣalāh' as 'specific sayings and actions, beginning with takbīr and ending with taslīm, with intention'—the genus is 'sayings and actions', the differentia are the specifics, and the composite gives the essence of the act.
This avoids circularity: the whole definition conveys the meaning.
5
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: if real definition were impossible, legal definitions would be reduced to mere labels. The response preserves the utility of precise definitions in fiqh.
Al-Razi's objection is a legitimate ikhtilāf, but the Hanafi position maintains the validity of ḥadd ḥaqīqī.

The Three-Part Logical Structure of Evidence: Subject, Predicate, and Middle Term
Logical analysis of evidence (dalīl) into three components

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three components of a logical evidence (dalīl) and explain why the logician defined it as 'two statements from which another statement arises'.

Core Idea
Every evidence (dalīl) requires three parts: subject, predicate, and a middle term that links them.

The logical structure of evidence is essential for deriving a conclusion. The passage explains that any valid argument must consist of a subject (minor term), a predicate (major term), and a middle term that connects them. This yields two premises (minor and major) and a conclusion. Understanding this three-part structure is crucial for constructing sound proofs in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three parts of evidence
Understand the subject of the desired conclusion (الموضوع), its predicate (المحمول), and the middle term (الحد الأوسط) that links them.
The subject is the minor term; the predicate is the major term.
2
See how they form two premises
The minor premise connects the subject and middle; the major premise connects the middle and predicate. For example: 'The world is changing' (minor) and 'Everything changing is originated' (major).
The middle term appears in both premises.
3
Understand the derivation of the conclusion
The middle term is repeated in both premises, linking the subject and predicate. This allows the conclusion: 'The world is originated.'
The conclusion attributes the predicate to the subject via the middle.
4
Recognize the logician’s definition of evidence
The passage states: 'Evidence is two statements from which another statement arises.' This definition captures the three-part structure: two premises produce a conclusion.
This definition includes qiyās, istiqrā', and tamthīl.
5
Apply the structure to a given example
Take the Zayd example: minor premise 'Zayd roams at night', major premise 'Everyone who roams at night is a thief', middle term 'roaming at night'. Conclusion: 'Zayd is a thief'.
The middle term must be truly common; here it is only probable.

Refuting Sense-Empiricism: How Nazar Yields Certain Knowledge
Refutation of Sumaniyya using essential attributes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi argument using essential attributes (fusuul muqawwima) to prove that correct nazar yields knowledge, and evaluate the Sumaniyya objection.

Core Idea
Knowledge and ignorance are essentially different: correct nazar leads to knowledge because its constitutive attribute is correspondence with reality.

The Sumaniyya, ancient Indian sense-empiricists, denied that rational inquiry (nazar) can produce knowledge. In this passage, the Hanafi author refutes them by showing that knowledge and ignorance have distinct essential attributes—correspondence and non-correspondence with reality. Once we correctly distinguish nazar from faulty reasoning, we see that correct nazar necessarily yields knowledge. This argument is foundational for accepting rational proofs (qiyas, istidlal) in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Opponent's Claim
The Sumaniyya argue that only sense perception yields knowledge; nazar is uncertain because it can produce error. This is their major premise: nothing from rational matters is productive of knowledge.
The minor premise is that nazar is a rational matter.
2
Examine the First Two Failed Responses
The text presents two attempted answers: (1) distinguishing correct nazar by its accidents (a'rad), and (2) using sense perception to judge nazar's correctness. Both are refuted—the first because it begs the question, the second because sense perception only gives partial knowledge, not universal.
Sense perception yields juz'i (partial) knowledge, while nazar deals with kulli (universal) matters.
3
Analyze the Final Hanafi Answer
The author argues that knowledge and ignorance are essentially different. Their constitutive attributes (fusuul muqawwima) are opposites: knowledge is correspondence with reality, ignorance is non-correspondence. Since correct nazar produces correspondence, it yields knowledge, not ignorance.
This mirrors the distinction between 'rational' for humans and 'neighing' for horses—different essences.
4
Connect to the Practical Consequence
This defense validates the use of rational inquiry (nazar) in deriving fiqh rulings, such as qiyas and istidlal. Without this epistemological grounding, rational proofs would be suspect.
The thamarah al-khilaf: acceptance of qiyas as a source of law.

Commentary
This passage from 'Izalat al-Rahbut' is a commentary on Musallam al-Thubut, addressing a key epistemological objection. The author, Sheikh Muhammad Anwar al-Badakhshani, explains how rational inquiry (nazar) can yield certain knowledge despite the possibility of error. The objection is raised by the Sumaniyya, a group that denies knowledge except through sense perception. The passage progresses through several responses: First, an attempt to distinguish correct nazar by its accidents (a'rad) is refuted because we need a criterion to know the correctness of the nazar itself. Second, the suggestion that sense perception can judge nazar is rejected because sense perception only yields partial (juz'i) knowledge, while nazar deals with universal (kulli) matters. The final correct answer is that knowledge and ignorance are distinguished by their essential attributes (fusuul muqawwima): knowledge is correspondence with reality, ignorance is non-correspondence. These are essentially different, like the attribute of rational for man and neighing for horse. Thus, correct nazar, by its very nature, leads to knowledge, not ignorance. The author then mentions that this is the position of Ahl al-Sunnah. Finally, the passage introduces a new topic: the second theological principle concerning how nazar produces knowledge, and lists four schools (Ash'ari, Mu'tazila, Philosophers, Razi) that will be discussed. This sets the stage for further elaboration.

Refutation of Skeptics on Knowledge from Reasoning
Response to skeptics proving knowledge results from reasoning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi epistemological argument that reasoning yields knowledge by distinguishing the constitutive differentiae of knowledge and ignorance.

Core Idea
Knowledge and ignorance are distinct in essence; occasional indistinguishability does not invalidate reasoning as a source of knowledge.

This passage addresses a fundamental challenge from skeptics who deny that rational reasoning (nazar) can produce knowledge. The author refutes the claim that only sensation yields knowledge, arguing that sensation gives only partial, particular knowledge, whereas reasoning deals with universals. The core of the defense lies in the essential difference between knowledge and ignorance—they have different constitutive differentiae—so reasoning can lead to one or the other.

Guided Path
1
Identify the skeptical claim
Determine what the Sumaniyya argue: that sensation is the only source of knowledge, and that reasoning cannot produce knowledge because errors occur.
The skeptics' position is implicitly presented as the objection being answered.
2
Understand the limitation of sensation
The author states that sensation yields only partial (juz'i) knowledge and cannot provide knowledge about reasoning, which is universal (kulli). Sensation cannot judge correctness or error of reasoning.
Partial knowledge is not productive (kasib) of universal truths.
3
Grasp the main rational proof
The key argument: knowledge and ignorance have different constitutive differentiae (conformity vs. non-conformity with reality). Different differentiae indicate different essences, just as 'speaking' and 'non-speaking' differentiate man from horse.
The proof is aqli (rational) and relies on the principle that difference in essential properties entails difference in essence.
4
Apply the proof to rebut the objection
Since knowledge and ignorance are essentially different, occasional ambiguity (e.g., when a person is unsure) does not negate that reasoning can produce knowledge. Accidental similarities do not erase essential distinction.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf: the Hanafi position preserves reasoning as a valid source for usul.
5
Recognize the doctrinal affiliation
The author concludes that this view is the doctrine of Ahl al-Sunnah wa al-Jama'ah, aligning the Hanafi school with the mainstream Sunni epistemological stance.

Opening the chapter on al-Hakim and defining the dispute on حسن/قبح

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three meanings of hasan and qabih, explain the dispute regarding the third meaning, and state the Hanafi (Maturidi) position.

Core Idea
The dispute over good and bad is only about whether acts inherently deserve divine reward or punishment.

This passage opens the chapter on al-Hakim, establishing that only Allah issues rulings. It then transitions into a crucial usuli and theological dispute: what does it mean to say an act is 'good' or 'bad'? This passage is a detailed exposition on the Maturidi doctrine of kasb (acquisition) in the context of human actions and divine creation. The author begins by presenting the Ash'ari definition of kasb as the appearance of eternal power in the locus of temporal power, followed by a critique that such an imagined power is tantamount to jabr (compulsion). This lesson delves into a foundational debate in Hanafi uṣūl al-fiqh: does the command to perform an obligatory act within its time also obligate making it up if it is missed? The Hanafi school holds that it does, and that separate Qur’anic verses and hadiths are merely identifiers (mu‘arrafāt) of the manner of qadā', not new obligators. We will examine the Hanafi rational proof, the majority’s textual objection, and the Hanafi response, including a critique of a popular but weak counterargument.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
What is the exact legal question under debate? Does the command for adā' (e.g., 'fast Ramadan') automatically obligate qadā' when the act is missed? Recognize that the dispute is about the source of the obligation: is it the original command or a separate command?
The Hanafi position is that the original command suffices; the majority demands a new command.
2
State the Hanafi position and its rational proof
The Hanafi view: the demand for a thing includes demand for its like when missed. The obligation established by the first text remains in the liability until performed. Therefore, the original adā' command directly obligates qadā'.
This is a rational (dalil aqli) argument, not based on a specific text.
3
Present the majority’s objection
The majority of uṣūlīs point to new texts (e.g., Quran 2:184, hadith 'pray when you remember') as evidence that qadā' requires a separate command. They argue: if the original command implied qadā', these texts would be superfluous.
These texts seem to explicitly command qadā', which appears to support the majority's position.
4
Explain the Hanafi response using mu'arraf
The Hanafis respond that these texts are not new obligators but identifiers (mu'arrafāt). They clarify the way, time, and mode of qadā', but the obligation itself is already established by the original command. The original obligation persists in the liability; a new text for obligation would be redundant and unnecessary.
The key is distinguishing between the source of obligation and the modality of its fulfillment.
5
Analyze the well-known but weak answer
Some offer an alternative defense: the command 'Fast on Thursday' implies both fasting and the day; when the day is lost, fasting remains absolutely. The author rejects this as extremely weak because there is no obligation of fasting without the restriction (Thursday). From an obligation of a restricted act, an unrestricted obligation does not follow.
This critique reinforces the Hanafi position that the original command's obligation is tied to its specific context, but the general principle of obligation remains.

Nafil Becomes Wajib by Commencement
Stating the Hanafi ruling that nafil becomes wajib upon starting it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi usul principle that nafil becomes wajib by commencement and identify the Quranic proof used to derive this ruling.

Core Idea
A voluntary act, once started, becomes binding — the prohibition of invalidating deeds transforms mubah into wajib.

This passage addresses a fundamental Hanafi usul principle: that a voluntary (nafil) act of worship, once commenced, becomes obligatory to complete. The ruling is derived from rational permissibility of change and a clear Quranic prohibition. Understanding this principle is crucial for distinguishing between initial and continuing obligation in worship.

Ash'ari Proof on Capacity Timing and Hanafi Refutation
Usul dispute: refuting Ash'ari proof on capacity timing.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi definition of capacity as suitability (صلاحية) to refute the Ash'ari claim that capacity must accompany the act.

Core Idea
Capacity is suitability, not actual relation; it exists before the act, as does Allah's power.

The timing of capacity (qudra) is a key usul dispute between Ash'aris and Hanafis. The Ash'aris argue that capacity must exist only at the moment of the act, using an analogy to hitting. This lesson unpacks their first proof and the Hanafi (Maturidi) refutation, which redefines capacity as a prior suitability and cites divine power and the Quran to show that capacity is given before obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ash'ari proof
The Ash'aris analogize capacity to hitting: just as hitting cannot exist before the object hit, capacity cannot precede the act. They argue that relation (ta'alluq) requires both relata to exist simultaneously.
This is an argument from analogy (qiyas).
2
Find the first refutation via divine power
The Hanafi author points to Allah's power, which existed eternally without any act. If the Ash'ari view held, then the world would have to be eternal as well, which is false.
This is a rebuttal by counterexample (naqd).
3
Redefine capacity as suitability
The second refutation redefines capacity: it is not an actual relation to an existing act but a suitability (salahiyya) to relate, which can exist whether the act exists or not.
Imam al-Haramayn (a Shafi'i) supports this: 'ability is only conceivable before the act'.
4
Apply the Quranic proof
The verse 'Allah does not charge a soul except with its capacity' indicates that capacity is given before the obligation, not at the moment of the act.
The Hanafi understanding: 'wus'' (capacity) precedes taklif.
5
State the practical consequence
The difference affects moral responsibility: Hanafi says the infidel has capacity to believe before the moment of belief, so he is blameworthy; Ash'ari says he lacks capacity until that moment, so he is not blameworthy.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (fruit of the dispute).

Do Travel Concessions Apply to Sinful Journeys? The Hanafi Position
Madhhab dispute with Hanafi proof from Quran and hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that the general (mutlaq/amm) text includes all instances unless restricted, to derive the ruling on travel concessions for sinful journeys.

Core Idea
General text rules: travel concessions apply to all journeys, even sinful ones.

This lesson examines a classic usul dispute: does the moral purpose of a journey affect the applicability of travel concessions? The Hanafi school holds that concessions such as shortening prayer and breaking fast are tied to the factual condition of travel, not its permissibility. We will analyze the Hanafi proof from Quran 2:184 and understand the underlying usul principle that drives this ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Contested Case
Recognize that the issue is whether travel concessions (rukhsas) apply to a journey undertaken for sin (safar al-ma'siyah). The Hanafi school says yes; the three other imams (Shafi'i, Maliki, Hanbali) say no.
The sinful travel includes theft, looting, murder, etc.
2
State the Hanafi Proof
The primary Hanafi proof is the Quranic verse 2:184: 'So whoever among you is ill or on a journey, then a number of other days.' The word 'journey' is general and unqualified (mutlaq), thus including all journeys regardless of purpose.
They also cite a general hadith from Sahih Muslim (about the Prophet praying two rak'ahs during travel).
3
Apply the Usul Principle
Apply the Hanafi principle: al-mutlaq yujra 'ala itlaqihi (the absolute term is applied as it is, without restriction). Since the text does not differentiate, the ruling applies to all journeys meeting the distance condition.
This contrasts with the opposing school's use of qiyas or purposive interpretation.
4
Identify the Thamarah al-Khilaf
The practical consequence (thamarah) is that a thief traveling to steal may shorten prayer according to Hanafis, but not according to the other schools.
This difference arises purely from usul methodology.
5
Reflect on Cross-Madhhab Reading
Consider how the Shafi'i school would read the same verse: they may restrict the general term by the purpose of travel or by other texts, leading to a different ruling.
This illustrates how usul principles shape fiqh outcomes.

Permissibility of Accountability for Error (Hanafi vs Mu'tazila)
Dispute between Hanafis and Mu'tazila on accountability for error, with a secondary ikhtilaf on divorce.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on accountability for error, state the Hanafi proof from the Quran and the Mu'tazila objection, and apply the distinction between criminal and financial liability.

Core Idea
Error involves negligence but carries a semblance of innocence, so criminal penalties are dropped but financial liability remains.

This usul dispute examines whether it is rationally permissible to hold someone accountable for an act done in error. The Mu'tazila argue that accountability requires intent, so error cannot incur liability. The Hanafis respond that error involves a degree of negligence, but the severe penalties are dropped due to doubt, while financial compensation remains obligatory. This distinction has practical implications across fiqh, from torts to family law.

Guided Path
1
State the two positions
Clearly articulate the Hanafi position (rational permissibility of accountability) and the Mu'tazila position (impossibility).
2

This is a form of istidlal (inference) based on the principle that the Quran does not contain incoherence (laghw).
3
Examine the Mu'tazila objection and Hanafi response
The Mu'tazila say accountability requires intent. The Hanafis respond that error involves negligence (tark al-tathabbut), but due to shubhat al-'adam, hadd and qisas are dropped. Financial liability remains as compensation.
4
Explore the secondary dispute on divorce in error
The text adds that a divorce uttered in error is effective according to the Hanafis, contrary to al-Shafi'i. Understand the reasoning: heedlessness of meaning is subtle, so puberty is taken as a substitute for intent.
This illustrates how the Hanafis deal with situations where ascertaining intent is difficult.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
State the practical difference: Mu'tazila would impose no liability for error; Hanafis impose financial compensation but not criminal penalties. Also, Hanafis give effect to divorce in error, Shafi'is do not.

Commentary
This passage is a dispute in usul al-fiqh regarding the rational permissibility of holding someone accountable for an act done in error. This illustrates the usul principle that when the presence of a condition (intent) is difficult to ascertain, the law substitutes a clear indicator (puberty) to establish rulings.
