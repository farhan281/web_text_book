Subject Matter of Usul and the Hujjiyya Dispute
Definition of usul's subject and dispute on hujjiyya's science

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the subject matter of usul al-fiqh and explain why the study of hujjiyya of ijma and qiyas belongs to kalam, not fiqh, according to the preferred Hanafi view.

Core Idea
The four sources are one subject by their common function; hujjiyya is a kalam issue, not fiqh.

What exactly is the subject of usul al-fiqh? And if it includes multiple sources, why isn't it multiple sciences? This passage from a commentary on Musallam al-Thubut answers these questions and then takes up a classic dispute: should the discussion of the authority (hujjiyya) of consensus (ijma) and analogy (qiyas) be considered part of fiqh, kalam, or is it axiomatic? The preferred Hanafi position places it in kalam, and the refutation reveals an important principle about the relationship between a source's authority and the obligation to act.

Guided Path
1
Identify the subject of usul al-fiqh
Locate the passage that states the subject of usul al-fiqh is the four proofs (Book, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) considered collectively (إجمالاً). Understand the distinction: detailed study of each proof belongs to tafsir or hadith.
The common factor among the four is 'leading to legal rulings'.
2
Understand the objection of multiplicity and its answer
Why might someone think usul would be four sciences? Because sciences are distinguished by their subjects. The answer: the four proofs share a common factor (الإيصال إلى الحكم الشرعي), making them one subject.
If no common factor, multiplicity would indeed mean multiple sciences.
3
Extract the three opinions on hujjiyya of ijma and qiyas
The passage lists three views: (1) It is a fiqh issue because it entails obligation to act; (2) It is axiomatic (بديهية); (3) It is a kalam issue (the correct view according to the author).
The author sides with the third opinion.
4
Analyze the first opinion and its refutation
First, the first opinion argues that 'ijma is a proof' means 'acting upon it is wajib', so it's fiqh. Then, the refutation has two points: (a) the obligation to act is a branch (far') of authority, not authority itself; (b) acting is a fruit of authority, not identical to it. Thus, hujjiyya is the root (asl) and belongs to kalam.
The refutation hinges on the distinction between root (asl) and branch (far').
5
Synthesize the preferred position
The third opinion (kalam) is preferred because it establishes the foundational validity of the sources before fiqh can derive branches. The discussion of hujjiyya is a preliminary to fiqh, not part of fiqh itself.
This classification affects how a mujtahid approaches the sources.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary addresses the subject matter of usul al-fiqh and a related dispute. First, the author clarifies that the subject of usul al-fiqh is the four sources (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas) considered collectively from the angle that they lead to legal rulings. When examined in detail (specific verses or hadith), that belongs to other sciences like tafsir and hadith. An objection is raised: if the subject is four things, shouldn't usul be four sciences? The answer is that they share a common unifying aspect—leading to a legal ruling and establishing it—so they are considered one subject.

Then the author discusses a dispute: Where does the discussion of the authority (hujjiyya) of ijma and qiyas belong? Three opinions are mentioned: (1) It is a fiqh issue because it concerns the obligation of acting upon them. (2) It is an axiomatic necessity of religion and does not need a separate science. (3) It is a kalam issue, like the study of the authority of the Quran and Sunnah. The author (presumably the matn author, Musallam al-Thubut) takes the third as correct.

The commentary expands on the first opinion and its refutation. The first opinion argues: the statement 'ijma is a proof' means it is obligatory to act upon it, and the subject of the proposition is 'action' and predicate 'obligatory', which is a fiqh issue. The refutation has two points: (a) The obligation of acting upon them is a branch (far') that depends on establishing their authority (asl), so they are not the same. (b) The permissibility of acting is also a result of authority, not authority itself. Thus, the discussion of authority is prior to the fiqh ruling of obligation and belongs to kalam (theology) which establishes the foundations of the sources.

Key Terms
الموضوع (al-mawḍū‘): The subject matter of a science; in usul al-fiqh, it is the four sources collectively from the aspect of leading to legal rulings.
الحجية (al-ḥujjiyya): The quality of being a valid proof (hujja) that establishes a legal ruling.
الإجماع (al-ijmā‘): Consensus of qualified jurists on a legal ruling as a source of law.
القياس (al-qiyās): Analogical deduction from the sources to derive rulings for new cases.

The Authority of Ijma' and Qiyas: Which Science Does It Belong To?
Discussion of three opinions on the subject-matter of authority of Ijma' and Qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three opinions on the classification of the authority of Ijma' and Qiyas, explain the Hanafi refutation of the second opinion, and state the Hanafi position regarding the proper science for this issue.

Core Idea
The authority of Ijma' and Qiyas is a theological matter, not intuitive, discussed in Usul only due to disagreement.

This passage from Muslim al-Thubut discusses a foundational question: to which science does the authority (hujjiyya) of Ijma' (consensus) and Qiyas (analogy) belong? Three opinions are presented and evaluated. The author ultimately endorses the view that it is an issue of Kalam (theology), not Usul al-Fiqh, though it is discussed in Usul because of extensive disagreement. Understanding this classification clarifies the scope and method of both Kalam and Usul.

Guided Path
1
State the three opinions
List the three opinions on the classification of hujjiyya of Ijma' and Qiyas as presented in the text: (1) that it entails only obligation, (2) that it is an intuitive matter, and (3) that it belongs to Kalam.
The first opinion is refuted early; the second is refuted in detail; the third is endorsed.
2
Analyze the refutation of the first opinion
Note that the first opinion incorrectly limits the legal qualities of actions to obligation. The author broadens it to include permissibility and disapproval.
This demonstrates that the authority of Ijma' and Qiyas covers all shari' qualities.
3
Examine the second opinion and its refutation
The second opinion claims the authority is intuitive. The author refutes by pointing to disagreement and uses the cause-effect analogy (e.g., oneness of God is intuitive to people but theoretical in itself).
Focus on the logical structure: even if the results are intuitive, the principle may be theoretical.
4
Identify the Hanafi position (third opinion)
The author states the truth: the authority of Ijma' and Qiyas is a matter of Kalam, like the authority of Quran and Sunnah. Usul discusses it only secondarily due to disagreement.
This classification has practical implications for curriculum and methodology.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
The thamarah is where this topic is studied: primarily in Kalam, secondarily in Usul. This affects how students approach the sources.
Compare with the second opinion's implication that no study is needed.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion of three opinions regarding the epistemological classification of the authority (hujjiyya) of Ijma' (consensus) and Qiyas (analogy). The author begins by refuting the first opinion, which holds that the authority of Ijma' and Qiyas necessarily entails obligation (wujub) to act. He argues that the legal qualities of an action include not only obligation but also permissibility (jawaz), permission (ibaha), and disapproval (karaha). Since Ijma' and Qiyas can establish any of these, their authority is not confined to obligation. The second opinion claims that the authority of Ijma' and Qiyas is an intuitive (badihi) matter, not a theoretical one, and thus does not require investigation. The author refutes this: if it were truly intuitive, there would be no disagreement, but in fact there is significant disagreement. Even if we accept that the results of Ijma' and Qiyas are intuitive, the authority itself can still be theoretical, analogous to the oneness of God being intuitive for ordinary people but demonstrable in theology. The third opinion, which the author endorses, states that the authority of Ijma' and Qiyas belongs to the science of Kalam (theology), just like the authority of the Qur'an and Sunnah. However, because of the extensive disagreement and debate among scholars, Usul al-Fiqh discusses it as a secondary and digressive topic, whereas in Kalam it is discussed as a primary subject. The passage ends by noting that the authority of the Qur'an and Sunnah is universally agreed upon, so it is not discussed in Usul. This passage is a typical Hanafi engagement with the classification of legal sources, emphasizing that Usul al-Fiqh does not aim to prove the authority of these sources but rather to apply them, while Kalam establishes their foundational authority.

Key Terms
حجية (hujjiyya): The quality of being a proof or authoritative source of law.
إجماع (ijma'): Consensus of the mujtahid scholars of the ummah on a legal ruling after the death of the Prophet.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical deduction extending a ruling from an original case to a new case based on shared effective cause ('illa).
بديهية (badihiyya): Intuitive, self-evident truth requiring no proof.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed debate (usul dispute) about the nature of collective obligation (fard kifaya) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The opponents argue that a collective obligation is only obligatory upon some individuals, not all, because if it were obligatory upon all, performance by some would not drop the obligation from others. The author (commentator) responds by clarifying the purpose of such obligations: the legislator intends the existence of the act itself, not that every individual performs it. Once the act is performed by some, the obligation is fulfilled for all, analogous to two guarantors for a debt—payment by one discharges both. The opponents' second argument compares ambiguity in the obligated person to ambiguity in the commanded act; just as an ambiguous act cannot be performed, an ambiguous obligation on an unspecified person is impossible. The author rejects this analogy, noting that ambiguity in the act can be resolved by the individual's choice (e.g., kaffara), but ambiguity in the person (i.e., not knowing who is obligated) leads to impossible imputation of sin to an unspecified person (tathim al-mubham). The author then addresses a counterargument that the sin falls on all for the omission of some, proving that the obligation is on all. The author insists that if the sin is first on some, those some remain unspecified, so tathim al-mubham still occurs. Finally, the author offers a refined response: the 'whole' (all obligated individuals) is itself an individual of the 'unspecified some' (the group that fulfills the obligation). Since all are obligated, the sin of all for omission is the sin of the 'some' who omitted—but that 'some' is now specified as 'all', thus tathim al-mubham is avoided. This nuanced argument preserves the Hanafi position that fard kifaya is initially obligatory on every individual (like fard ayn), but performance by some drops it from others.

تأثيم المبهم (tathim al-mubham): Imputing sin to an unspecified person, considered irrational because it entails punishing someone not known to be sinful.
الواجب الكفائي (al-wajib al-kifa'i): Collective obligation; a duty that if performed by some members of the community, the obligation is fulfilled for all.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed debate (usul dispute) about the nature of collective obligation (fard kifaya) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The opponents argue that a collective obligation is only obligatory upon some individuals, not all, because if it were obligatory upon all, performance by some would not drop the obligation from others. The author (commentator) responds by clarifying the purpose of such obligations: the legislator intends the existence of the act itself, not that every individual performs it. Once the act is performed by some, the obligation is fulfilled for all, analogous to two guarantors for a debt—payment by one discharges both. The opponents' second argument compares ambiguity in the obligated person to ambiguity in the commanded act; just as an ambiguous act cannot be performed, an ambiguous obligation on an unspecified person is impossible. The author rejects this analogy, noting that ambiguity in the act can be resolved by the individual's choice (e.g., kaffara), but ambiguity in the person (i.e., not knowing who is obligated) leads to impossible imputation of sin to an unspecified person (tathim al-mubham). The author then addresses a counterargument that the sin falls on all for the omission of some, proving that the obligation is on all. The author insists that if the sin is first on some, those some remain unspecified, so tathim al-mubham still occurs. Finally, the author offers a refined response: the 'whole' (all obligated individuals) is itself an individual of the 'unspecified some' (the group that fulfills the obligation). Since all are obligated, the sin of all for omission is the sin of the 'some' who omitted—but that 'some' is now specified as 'all', thus tathim al-mubham is avoided. This nuanced argument preserves the Hanafi position that fard kifaya is initially obligatory on every individual (like fard ayn), but performance by some drops it from others.

Key Terms
المبهم (al-mubham): Unspecified, ambiguous; a person or entity not clearly identified.
تأثيم المبهم (tathim al-mubham): Imputing sin to an unspecified person, considered irrational because it entails punishing someone not known to be sinful.
الواجب الكفائي (al-wajib al-kifa'i): Collective obligation; a duty that if performed by some members of the community, the obligation is fulfilled for all.

Majority's Proof for Broad Time and Refutation of al-Baqillani's View

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the majority's argument for broad time and refute al-Baqillani's claim of takhyir between act and intention.

Core Idea
Compliance in any part of broad time is by the act itself; the claim of a choice is an unwarranted addition.

This passage examines the usul dispute between the majority (including Hanafis) and al-Qadi Abu Bakr al-Baqillani regarding the nature of compliance when a wajib act is performed in any part of its broad time. The majority argues that the Lawgiver expanded the time, making any part valid, while al-Baqillani insists that the wajib is a choice between performing the act and merely intending it. The author critically analyzes the arguments, appealing to consensus and exposing weaknesses in al-Baqillani's reasoning.

Guided Path

2
