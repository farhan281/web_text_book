On the Causes of Legal Rulings (Asbab al-Shara'i)
Defining sabab and presenting three schools on causation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define sabab in Hanafi usul, state the three schools on legal causation, and apply the Hanafi position to examples like Hajj and fasting.

Core Idea
Causes are signs set by Allah, not independent agents; attribution does not deny divine power.

This passage tackles a foundational usul question: are legal rulings 'caused' by descriptions like the Ka'ba for Hajj or the month for fasting? The Hanafi school affirms causal attribution as a sign (علامة) from the Lawgiver, countering deniers and Ash'aris who restrict causation. Understanding this debate clarifies how Hanafi jurists derive obligations from textual and rational indicators.

Guided Path
1
Define sabab linguistically and technically
Extract the definition: in language, a means to reach an objective; in law, a manifest, regulated description that the transmitted proof shows as a sign for a ruling.
Note the distinction from 'illa (effective cause) in later usul.
2
Identify the three schools on causation
List: (1) Hanafi majority and others: rulings attributed to causes; (2) Some usuliyyun: deny causes; (3) Ash'aris: distinguish ibadat from rights/penalties.
Each school's core argument and evidence.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to each opponent
To deniers: attribution does not imply independent efficacy (like killing with a sword). To Ash'aris: once attribution is allowed, it applies uniformly.
Key rebuttal: causes are signs, not obligators.
4
Apply the concept to the examples given
Work through Hajj (Ka'ba as cause), fasting (month as cause), prayer (time), penalties (offenses), expiation (ambiguous act), transactions (preservation of world).
Note intra-Hanafi difference on whether the day or the whole month causes fasting.
5
Distinguish cause (sabab) from condition (shart)
The passage explicitly says time for prayer is a condition, not a cause. Understand why: cause is attributed to the ruling, condition is not.
Attribution test: if the ruling is attributed to X (e.g., 'Hajj of the House'), X is a cause.

والمذهب الثاني لبعض الأصوليين وهو أنهم ينكرون الأسباب أصلا ويقولون: الحكم في المنصوص عليه يثبت بظاهر النص، وفي غير المنصوص عليه يتعلّق بالوصف الذي جعل علةً ويكون ذلك علامة لثبوت الحكم في الفرع بايجاب الله تعالى مستدلّين بأن الإيجاب صفة خاصة له تعالى كصفة التخليق وفي إضافة الإيجاب إلى الأسباب الله تعالى وبأن الأسباب لو كانت عللا للأحكام لما تصوّر الانفكاك بينها وبين الأحكام والحال أنه يوجد؛ لأن الأسباب كانت موجودةً قبل الشرع ولم تكن الأحكام، وكذا من أسلم في دار الحرب ولم يهاجر إلينا يتحقق الأسباب في حقه ولا يجب العبادات عليه، وأشار الشيخ إلى جوابهما بقوله: "جعلها الشرع أسبابا لها" فإنا لا نقول: إن الأسباب موجبة بنفسها حتى يلزم قلعه عن الله تعالى؛ إذ الإيجاب لا يتصور إلا من مفترض، لكن السبب مايكون موصلا إلى الحكم، فإضافة الحكم إلى السبب لا يمنع من إضافته إلى غيره كما ترى في القتل، فإنه يحصل حقيقة بآلة كالسيف ومع ذلك يضاف إلى القاتل حتى يجب عليه القصاص، ولا تجعل الأسباب موجبة إلا بجعله تعالى إياها كذلك فنقول قبل ورود الشرع لم يجعل الله لها أسبابا. وأما سقوط العبادات عن من آمن و لم يهاجر إلينا فمبني على دفع الحرج.
والمذهب الثالث لجمهور الأشاعرة وهو أنهم فرّقوا بين حقوق العباد والعقوبات وبين العبادات، فقالوا: إن الأول له أسباب يضاف وجوب إليها؛ لأنه حاصل بكسب العبد فيضاف إليه وأما الثاني أي العبادات فلا يضاف وجوبها إلا إلى إيجاب الله تعالى وخطابه ونحن نقول: إن هذا الفرق ضعيف؛ لأنه لما جاز إضافة بعض الأحكام إلى الأسباب بالدليل جاز إضافة سائرها إلى الأسباب بالدليل، وهذه أمثلة للأسباب.

Book of Allah Almighty. Chapter on explaining the causes of the laws. [Causes of the laws] i.e., established in the law. Chapter on explaining the causes of the laws. Know that the fundamentals of religion and its branches are legislated by causes which the Law made causes for them. Chapter on explaining etc., i.e., explaining the ways by which the legislated matters are known and established. And the cause (sabab) in language is that by which one can reach the objective; from that the rope is called a sabab because it makes possible reaching the objective. In the Law, it is applied to every apparent and regulated description which the transmission (i.e., the transmitted proof) indicates that it is a marker for establishing a legal ruling, in that the transmitted proof indicates that the Lawgiver made it a sign for the establishment of a certain ruling. There are three schools regarding this: The first school is the school of the generality of our companions (Hanafis) and some Shafi'is and the majority of the theologians (mutakallimun), and it is what the author explained by his saying: 'Know, etc.' The fundamentals of religion are faith in Allah Almighty and His attributes. Its branches are the rest of the legal rulings, acts of worship, transactions, expiations, and penalties. 'Causes for them' i.e., for those fundamentals and branches. The summary is that legal rulings have causes to which they are attributed, and the obligator and legislator is Allah Almighty, not the cause; because the obligator of rulings is Allah Almighty alone. The second school is of some usuliyyun (scholars of principles), who deny causes altogether and say: The ruling in what is explicitly stated is established by the apparent meaning of the text, and in what is not explicitly stated it relates to the description which is made an effective cause ('illa), and that is a sign for the establishment of the ruling in the branch by the obligation of Allah Almighty. They argue that obligation is a specific attribute of Him like the attribute of creation, and in attributing obligation to causes (they see) a denial of Allah Almighty. And also if causes were truly effective for rulings, then separation between them and rulings would be inconceivable, but the situation is that separation exists; because causes existed before the law but rulings did not exist. Likewise, one who becomes Muslim in the abode of war and does not emigrate to us, the causes are realized in his case yet acts of worship are not obligatory on him. The author (shaykh) pointed to the answer to them by his saying: 'The Law made them causes for them.' For we do not say that causes are obligators by themselves so that there would be a diversion from Allah Almighty; indeed obligation can only be conceived from an obligator. Rather the cause is what leads to the ruling. So attributing the ruling to the cause does not prevent its attribution to another, as you see in killing: it is actually accomplished by an instrument like a sword, yet it is attributed to the killer such that retaliation becomes obligatory. And causes are not made obligators except by His making them so. So we say: Before the advent of the law, Allah did not make causes for them. As for the omission of acts of worship from one who believed and did not emigrate to us, it is based on removal of hardship. The third school is of the majority of Ash'aris, who distinguished between the rights of servants and penalties on one hand, and acts of worship on the other. They said: The former have causes to which their obligation is attributed, because they arise from the acquisition of the servant, so they are attributed to him. As for the latter, i.e., acts of worship, their obligation is attributed only to the command of Allah Almighty and His address. And we (Hanafis) say: This distinction is weak; because when it is permissible to attribute some rulings to causes by evidence, it is permissible to attribute all rulings to causes by evidence. And these are examples of causes. Like Hajj by the Ka'ba, fasting by the month, prayer by its times, penalties by their causes, expiation by the thing to which its obligation relates, and transactions by the preservation of what is controlled through undertaking them, i.e., the rulings from Allah that are legislated. Like Hajj by the Ka'ba: the object of its obligation is the Ka'ba, because the cause of the obligation of Hajj is the Ka'ba; it is attributed to the Ka'ba in the law. Allah Most High said: 'And for Allah upon the people is the pilgrimage to the House' (Al-Imran 3:97). Attribution is evidence of causality as you will know. As for time, it is a condition for the validity of performance, not a cause, because it is not attributed to it. Fasting: the object of its obligation is the month; it is its cause because it is attributed to it and is repeated by its repetition, and performance is valid after the month begins, not before. But they differed after [establishing] that it is a cause: Imam Abu Zayd and Sadr al-Islam held that the cause of the obligation of fasting is the days, not the nights; the indivisible part from the beginning of each day is the cause for fasting that day, so fasting the entire day becomes obligatory concurrently. Imam al-Sarakhsi held that the cause is simply witnessing the month, the days and nights with their times being equal; thus prayer times are causes for the obligation of prayer because prayer is attributed to them and is repeated by their repetition, and it is not valid before them, and it becomes valid upon their entry. Penalties, i.e., the fixed punishments: their obligation is attached to their causes, which are the offenses to which the punishments are attributed, like adultery and theft; it is said 'the penalty for adultery' and 'the penalty for theft', and penalties are repeated by their repetition, and they are not obligatory before but after. Between worship and penalty: because it (expiation) is worship in one respect (it is not obligatory on disbelievers) and penalty in another (it results from an offense). To it, i.e., the obligation of expiation is made to relate to the cause that is suitable for attribution of obligation. 'From a cause that is ambiguous etc.': meaning that since expiation is between worship and penalty, its cause is made something ambiguous between prohibition and permissibility, so that cause and effect are suitable. Like accidental killing, which is ambiguous between prohibition (due to lack of caution) and permissibility (because shooting at game is permissible). So this killing is a cause for the obligation of expiation, which is between worship and penalty: worship is attributed to permissibility, and penalty to prohibition. For this reason, pure prohibition like intentional killing and the false oath (yamin ghamus) are not made a cause for it, just as pure permissibility like killing with right and the oath contracted before breaking is not a cause for it. Transactions: like sale, purchase, marriage, etc. are subject to the preservation of survival, i.e., the preservation of the world, self, and species. 'Through their undertaking': i.e., by directly engaging in transactions. Meaning the cause for the legislation of transactions is the preservation of the world that is attached to their undertaking, because the survival of the world depends on the human species, which in its survival needs artificial things like food, clothing, and shelter, which cannot exist without mutual assistance and participation among its individuals, and also needs pairing between male and female. This requires universal principles from the Lawgiver to preserve justice and order among them, and these are the legal transactions. If it is said: Since the survival of the world needs transactions, transactions are the cause of survival; how then is survival the cause for them? I say: Survival is not the cause for their existence such that what you said would be correct; rather it is the cause for their legality. So reflect.

Commentary
This passage is a classical Hanafi usul al-fiqh discussion on the nature of 'causes' (asbab) in law. The author begins by defining sabab linguistically as a means to an end, and technically as a manifest, regulated description that the Lawgiver has made a sign (علامة) for the existence of a ruling. The central dispute is whether rulings are truly caused by such descriptions. Three positions are presented: (1) The majority of Hanafis, some Shafi'is and the Mutakallimun hold that rulings are legitimately attributed to causes, though the true obligator is Allah alone. (2) A minority of usuliyyun deny effective causation entirely, arguing that rulings are only by divine command and that causes cannot be independent agents. The author refutes this by noting that attribution of a ruling to a cause is permissible by God's design, just as killing is attributed to the murderer even though the sword is the instrument. (3) The Ash'aris distinguish: rights of servants and penalties have causes, but acts of worship do not. The Hanafi commentator rejects this as weak, since once attribution to causes is allowed by evidence, it applies uniformly. The passage then provides examples: Hajj is caused by the Ka'ba (Quranic proof), fasting by the month, prayer by its times (with internal dispute between Abu Zayd and Sarakhsi on whether the cause is the day or the entire month), penalties by their offenses, expiation by ambiguous acts like accidental killing (neither pure prohibition nor pure permission), and transactions by the need for societal preservation. This exemplifies how Hanafi usul grounds legal causation in both textual (naqli) and rational (aqli) arguments, and shows the school's preference for consistent application of causal attribution.

Key Terms
سبب (sabab): In law: a manifest, regulated description that the transmitted proof indicates the Lawgiver has made a sign for establishing a ruling.
شرعة (shar'ah): The law or Shari'ah that designates causes.
أصول الدين (usul al-din): Fundamentals of religion: faith in Allah and His attributes.
فروع (furu'): Branches of religion: all legal rulings, worship, transactions, expiations, penalties.

The Hanafi Definition of Ijma' and Its Possibility
Defines Ijma' technically and defends its possibility.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi technical definition of Ijma' with all its qualifiers and explain why the Hanafis affirm its possibility in practice.

Core Idea
Ijma' is the agreement of all pious mujtahids of the Ummah in one era — it is possible and a binding proof.

After establishing the Qur'an and Sunnah, Usul al-Fiqh turns to Ijma' (consensus) as the third primary source. This passage provides the formal Hanafi definition, carefully delimiting who is included and excluded, and then defends the very possibility of such a consensus against sceptics. Mastering this definition is essential before studying how Ijma' is established and applied.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the Two Linguistic Meanings
Identify that 'ijma'' in Arabic means either determination (like in the verse 'fa-ajmi'u amrakum') or agreement. The technical term is built on the second meaning.
The verse is used as a lexical proof, not for legal authority.
2
Parse the Technical Definition
Break down the definition: 'agreement of all pious mujtahids from the Ummah of Muhammad (s) in one era upon an event.' Understand each qualifier: 'agreement' covers words, actions, and tacit approvals; 'all mujtahids' excludes partial consensus and commoners; 'pious' excludes sinners and heretics; 'from this Ummah' excludes previous nations; 'in one era' refutes the misconception that it requires all eras; 'upon an event' includes both positive/negative rulings, rational and legal.
Note the debate about including commoners in matters not requiring ijtihad.
3
Understand the Exclusions and Conditions
Appreciate why the Hanafis exclude commoners, sinners, and heretics: their agreement lacks authority. Also, Ijma' is a distinction of this Ummah, not applicable to previous prophets' communities.
This narrow definition differs from some other schools.
4
Engage with the Possibility Dispute
An-Nazzam and some Shi'a argue that Ijma' is impossible because of the dispersion of scholars. The Hanafi response: transmission is possible for those who strive; the objection only applies to the lazy. Furthermore, for early consensus (Companions, Ahl al-Bayt, Madinah), transmission was easy.
The practical import: if Ijma' were impossible, it could not be a source.

Chapter of Ijma'. When he finished from the topics of the Sunnah, he began with the topics of Ijma'. It has two linguistic meanings: one is determination upon something—it is said: 'ajma'a so-and-so on such-and-such' if he determined upon it. Our saying 'all the mujtahids' excludes the agreement of some of them and also excludes the agreement of the common people; for neither of these is ijma'. Our saying 'the pious' excludes the ijma' of sinful and heretical mujtahids, for it is not a proof. Our saying 'from the Ummah of Muhammad (peace be upon him)' excludes the ijma' of the mujtahids of previous nations, because it is a distinction of this Ummah. The intended meaning of our saying 'in one era' is in a time, whether little or much, so that one should not imagine that ijma' is only realized by the agreement of all mujtahids in all eras until the Day of Judgment. The intended meaning of our saying 'upon an event' is ijma' upon a ruling that includes negative and positive, and rational and legal rulings. The author of al-Tawdih restricted it to legal rulings, so according to him, ijma' is upon a legal ruling. This definition is only valid according to the opinion of one who does not consider the agreement or disagreement of the common people in ijma' at all. As for the opinion of one who considers it in matters that do not require ra'y, then the correct definition according to him is to say: it is the agreement in every era upon a matter from matters, from all those who are of this Ummah. So it includes specifically the mujtahids in matters requiring ra'y, and all [including commoners] in matters not requiring ra'y. We say: The impossibility of transmission for one who strives in seeking and investigating evidence is denied, unlike one who sits in the depth of his house. And this [objection] does not apply at all to the ijma' of the Companions, the Ahl al-Bayt, and the people of Madinah, because transmission of rulings to them was not difficult, let alone impossible.

Commentary
This passage is a definition of Ijma' (consensus) and an initial discussion of its possibility as a source of Islamic law. The author first gives the linguistic meanings of ijma': determination and agreement. Then he provides the technical definition: agreement of all pious mujtahids of the Ummah of Muhammad (peace be upon him) in one era on a ruling. He explains each part of the definition: 'agreement' includes verbal, action, silence, and tacit approval; 'all mujtahids' excludes partial agreement and the agreement of common people; 'pious' excludes sinful and heretical mujtahids; 'from the Ummah of Muhammad' excludes previous nations; 'in one era' clarifies that it does not require agreement across all eras; 'upon an event' includes both negative and positive rulings, both rational and legal—but the author of al-Tawdih restricts it to legal rulings. The author notes that this definition assumes that commoners are not counted in ijma'; if one counts them in matters not requiring ijtihad, then the definition should include all members of the Ummah. Then the passage turns to the first requirement for one who asserts the authority of ijma': considering its possibility. It mentions the doubts of An-Nazzam and some Shi'a who argue that agreement on a ruling is impossible because rulings cannot be transmitted to all mujtahids scattered across the globe. The Hanafi response is that transmission is not impossible for those who strive in seeking knowledge; the objection only applies to those who sit idly at home. Moreover, for the ijma' of the Companions, the Ahl al-Bayt, and the people of Madinah, transmission was entirely feasible.

Key Terms
الإجماع (al-Ijmā‘): In usul al-fiqh, the agreement of all pious mujtahids of the Ummah of Muhammad (peace be upon him) in one era upon a ruling, whether legal or rational.
المجتهد (al-Mujtahid): A scholar who has the capacity to derive rulings from the primary sources (Quran and Sunnah) through ijtihad.
الصالحون (al-Ṣāliḥūn): The pious, righteous mujtahids who are free from major sin and heresy; their consensus is valid and binding.

Definition and Epistemological Foundation of Ijma‘
Defines ijma‘ and defends its possibility, verification, and transmission against objections.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi technical definition of ijma‘, identify its constituent conditions, and explain the Hanafi responses to three epistemological objections concerning its establishment, verification, and transmission.

Core Idea
Ijma‘: unanimous agreement of all upright mujtahids of the Ummah in one era – a binding proof defended against rational objections.

This passage introduces the definition of ijma‘ (consensus) as a source of Islamic law in the Hanafi school. It carefully sets out the conditions that make consensus binding and then addresses three major objections raised by skeptics. Understanding this foundation is essential for evaluating claims of consensus in legal reasoning.

It has two linguistic meanings: one is resolve upon something; it is said: 'So-and-so resolved upon such-and-such' if he resolved. Allah said: 'Then resolve your plan' [Yunus:71], i.e., resolve. The second is agreement. In technical terminology: a specific agreement, which is the agreement of all the upright mujtahids from the ummah of Muhammad, peace be upon him, in an era upon a case. So our saying 'agreement' includes statements, actions, silence, and tacit approval. Our saying 'all the mujtahids' excludes the agreement of some of them and also excludes the agreement of the common people, because these are not ijma‘. Our saying 'the upright' excludes the agreement of the sinful and heretics among the mujtahids, because it is not proof. Our saying 'from the ummah of Muhammad' excludes the agreement of mujtahids of previous nations, because it is a characteristic of this ummah. What is meant by our saying 'in an era' is a period of time, short or long, so that one may not think that ijma‘ is only realized by the agreement of all mujtahids in all eras until the Day of Judgment. What is meant by our saying 'upon a case' is agreement on a ruling that includes the negative and affirmative, and rational and legal rulings. The author of al-Tawdih restricted it to legal rulings, so in his view ijma‘ is on a legal ruling. This definition is only valid according to the opinion of those who do not consider the agreement or disagreement of the common people in ijma‘ at all. As for the opinion of those who consider it in what does not require opinion, the correct definition according to him is: it is the agreement in every era on a matter from all who are of this ummah; it includes the mujtahids specifically in what requires opinion, and everyone in what does not require opinion. And when you know this, know that it is incumbent upon the one who asserts the authority of ijma‘ to examine its establishment, verification, transmission, and authority. The first station: examination of its establishment. Al-Nazzam and some of the Shi‘a said: It is impossible for it to be established in itself, because agreement on a ruling cannot occur without that ruling being transmitted to them, and transmission to them is impossible due to their dispersion in the East and West. We say: The impossibility of transmission in the case of one who strives in seeking and researching evidence is denied, contrary to one who sits in the bottom of his house. And this with regard to the ijma‘ of the Companions, the Ahl al-Bayt, and the people of Medina does not apply at all, because transmission of the ruling to them was not difficult, let alone impossible. Then these people said: Agreement is either based on definitive evidence or speculative evidence, and both are invalid. As for the first: if there were definitive evidence, it would inevitably be transmitted, and if it is not transmitted, it is known that it does not exist; because if it existed, there would be no need for ijma‘, rather that definitive evidence would suffice. As for the second: it is impossible to agree upon it normally due to the difference of natures and views. The response to the first is that one may dispense with transmitting the definitive evidence by the occurrence of ijma‘, which is stronger than it, and in it there is removal of disagreement that necessitates inference. The response to the second is that speculative evidence may be clear where disagreement is not normally possible, so difference of natures does not prevent agreement in it, contrary to subtle and hidden speculative evidence. The second station: examination of its verification. The deniers said: Even if we grant its establishment in itself, its verification from them is impossible, because it is normally impossible to establish from every single scholar of the East and West that in such-and-such issue he ruled with such-and-such ruling, because they are not known individually, let alone the details of their rulings. Moreover, some of them may hide out of fear of agreement or disagreement, and may outwardly express contrary to his opinion, and consideration is for his opinion, not his words. Also, their agreement at one moment is impossible, so a long time is necessary, and then some may retract before agreement. The third station: examination of its transmission to those who will rely on it. The deniers said: It either is transmitted by isolated report (khabar al-ahad), and that is not beneficial, because it is not obligatory to act upon it in ijma‘ as will come; or by mutawatir transmission, and that is impossible, because it is very unlikely that the transmitters of tawatur would observe all the mujtahids East and West, hear from them, and transmit from them to the transmitters of tawatur, layer after layer, until it reaches us. The response to both proofs is that this is denial of the obvious, because it is known with certainty that the Companions and the Tabi‘in agreed on giving priority to definitive evidence over speculative evidence, and this is only by its verification from them and its transmission to us.

Commentary
This passage presents a comprehensive treatment of ijma‘ (consensus) as a source of Islamic law from the Hanafi usul perspective. It begins by defining ijma‘ linguistically and technically, carefully specifying conditions: agreement of all mujtahids (not merely some or the common people), who are upright (not sinful or heretical), from the ummah of Muhammad (excluding previous nations), in a particular era (not requiring all eras), on a legal ruling (according to al-Tawdih). The definition is nuanced, noting that some scholars include common people in matters that do not require opinion. Then the text addresses epistemological challenges to ijma‘ in four stations (maqamat). This lesson covers the first three: (1) whether ijma‘ can exist in itself—al-Nazzam and some Shi‘a deny it because transmission of the ruling to all mujtahids is impossible; the response is that it is possible for those who diligently seek evidence, and for early generations transmission was easy. Also they argue agreement cannot be on definitive evidence (since that would make ijma‘ redundant) nor on speculative (due to differences); the response: ijma‘ can be stronger than definitive evidence, and speculative evidence can be clear enough to command agreement. (2) Whether ijma‘ can be verified—deniers say it's impossible to know the opinion of every scholar; response is that historical examples like the Companions and Tabi‘in show known agreements. (3) Whether ijma‘ can be transmitted—deniers say either by ahad or tawatur, both problematic; response: we know by certainty that the early community agreed on giving priority to definitive evidence, proving transmission occurred. The fourth station on the authority of ijma‘ begins but is incomplete. The Hanafi school holds ijma‘ as a binding proof, and this text defends it rationally.

Ijma' as a Proof: Defending Consensus in Hanafi Usul
Systematic defense of Ijma' as a source of law

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Quranic and hadith proofs for Ijma', distinguish the Hanafi position from opposing views on who constitutes Ijma', and evaluate the epistemological status of Ijma' as definitive or speculative.

Core Idea
Ijma' is a binding proof despite objections; its authority is established by Quran, Sunnah, and reason.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text defends Ijma' (consensus) as a valid source of Islamic law. It systematically addresses four stations: the possibility of Ijma', its verification, its transmission, and its authority. Along the way, it rebuts objections from deniers and engages with the minority view that Ijma' is restricted to the Companions. The discussion culminates with the Quranic and hadith evidence for Ijma' and the debate on whether it yields certainty or probability.

Commentary
This passage is a comprehensive treatment of the Hanafi Usul discussion on Ijma' (consensus). It forms part of the chapter on Ijma' and addresses four main 'maqamat' (stations) concerning Ijma': its possibility, its actual occurrence, its transmission, and its authority as a proof. The author begins by presenting objections from those who deny Ijma' as a valid source of law, followed by responses. The first objection: that Ijma' cannot be based on a definitive proof (because it would then be redundant) nor on speculative proof (because consensus on speculative matters is conventionally impossible). The Hanafi response distinguishes between cases where Ijma' itself becomes the stronger proof, and notes that speculative matters can be clear enough to prevent disagreement. The second objection: that it is impossible to verify the actual agreement of all scholars across time and place. Hanafi response: we know with certainty that the Companions and Followers did agree on giving precedence to definitive over speculative, and this knowledge is transmitted. The third objection: that Ijma' cannot be reliably transmitted because solitary reports are not sufficient and tawatur is impractical. Hanafi response: this is a denial of the obvious; we have certain knowledge of transmitted Ijma'. The fourth station establishes Ijma' as a hujja (proof) using Quranic verses (4:115, 3:103, 4:59), hadith (the hadith of no consensus on error, the hadith on following the majority, etc.), and rational proof (the impossibility of a large number of scholars agreeing on a shar'i ruling without a basis). The discussion then turns to two important sub-issues: the epistemological status of Ijma' (whether its proof is qat'i or zanni) and the question of who constitutes the consensus participants. On the first, the majority (including most Hanafi usulis) hold Ijma' to be qat'i, while some, like the author of al-Mahsul, consider it zanni due to the strength of objections. On the second, a minority (Dawud al-Zahiri, Ahmad in one narration) restrict Ijma' to the Companions only, using arguments from Quranic verses (2:143, 3:110) that they think address only the Companions, and from hadith praising them. The Hanafi response refutes this by arguing that the Quranic address is general and applies to all generations, and that the hadith about the ummah's infallibility is general. The passage demonstrates the Hanafi method of combining naqli and aqli proofs to defend Ijma' and shows the internal debates within the madhhab.

Ijma': Its Proof and the Dispute over Its Constituents
Discusses proof of Ijma' and three exclusive views on who forms it

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the proofs for Ijma' and explain the Hanafi position on who can form a binding Ijma', refuting the exclusivity claims.

Core Idea
Ijma' is proved by text and reason; it is not limited to Sahaba, Madina, or Ahl al-Bayt but can be formed by any era's mujtahids.

This lesson covers two central issues in the theory of Ijma': its proof and the dispute over its constituents. The author first presents textual and rational evidence establishing Ijma' as a valid source, then notes a secondary dispute over its epistemic weight (qat'i or zanni). The bulk of the passage is a detailed examination of three exclusivist claims—that only the Companions, only the people of Medina, or only the Ahl al-Bayt can form Ijma'. For each, the author provides the arguments and then a Hanafi rebuttal. Understanding these debates is essential for appreciating the Hanafi position that Ijma' is a consensus of qualified mujtahids of any age.

Guided Path
1
Identify the proofs for Ijma'
Locate the hadiths and rational argument cited. Note that despite individual chains being solitary (ahad), their common meaning reaches tawatur.
The rational proof: scholars agreeing on declaring an opponent mistaken implies a decisive text.
2
Understand the qat'i vs zanni dispute
After proofs, the text notes disagreement on whether Ijma' is definitive or speculative. Most (including Hanafis) hold it qat'i.
Memorize the key figures: author of al-Badi' (qat'i), author of al-Mahsul (zanni).
3
Examine the claim: Ijma' only by Companions
Read the argument: Quranic address, hadiths on Companions, possibility of total agreement. Then follow the Hanafi rebuttal: address is general, otherwise absurd consequences (e.g., exclude later Companions).
4
Examine the claim: Ijma' only by Madina
Read the argument: hadith of Madina as bellows, its virtues. Then Hanafi rebuttal: virtue does not entail exclusivity; if so, Mecca would have stronger claim.
Note the alternative interpretation: it refers to precedence of riwaya, not Ijma'.
5
Examine the claim: Ijma' only by Ahl al-Bayt
Read the argument: verse of tathir, hadith of thaqalayn, rational reasons. Then Hanafi rebuttal: verse refers to wives, hadith is weak and contradicted by other narrations.
This shows careful handling of nusus by Hanafis.
6
Synthesize the Hanafi position
Conclude that no group is exclusive; Ijma' is by qualified mujtahids of any era, as supported by general proofs.
Practical thamarah: if only Sahaba, Ijma' after their time would be impossible.

Even if accepted, it is interpreted to mean that their narration is a proof.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on Ijma' addresses two key issues: the proof of Ijma' as a source of law, and the dispute over who can form a valid Ijma'. The text first presents a series of hadiths (with a rational proof) establishing that the Ummah cannot agree on error, thus Ijma' is a valid proof. It then notes a secondary dispute: among those who accept Ijma' as a proof, most consider it definitive (qat'i), while a minority view it as speculative (zanni). The core of the passage is the detailed exposition of three exclusive claims regarding the constituents of Ijma': (1) only the Companions (Sahaba) — held by Dawud al-Zahiri and in one narration from Ahmad ibn Hanbal; (2) only the people of Medina — a view attributed to Malik; (3) only the Ahl al-Bayt (the Prophet's family) — held by the Zaydis and Imamis. For each claim, the text provides the arguments and then a Hanafi rebuttal. The rebuttal to the Sahaba-only view argues that the Quranic address is general, and if limited to those present at revelation, absurd consequences follow (e.g., Ijma' of later Companions would be invalid). The rebuttal to the Medina-only view is that virtue does not entail exclusivity; Mecca has equal or greater virtues but that does not limit Ijma' to its people. The rebuttal to the Ahl al-Bayt-only view is twofold: first, the verse of purification (tathir) refers to the Prophet's wives, not to a narrow definition of Ahl al-Bayt; second, the hadith of thaqalayn (two weighty things) is weak and contradicted by the more authentic narration that mentions only the Book and Sunnah. The Hanafi position, implicit in these refutations, is that Ijma' can be formed by the qualified mujtahids of any era, as long as they meet the conditions of ijtihad.

Core Idea
Consensus requires at least two mujtahids; a single scholar's opinion is not ijma' even if he is the only one in his era.

Core Idea
An innovator's opinion is discounted in matters linked to his innovation; a commoner's opinion is irrelevant in matters requiring ijtihad.

The answers are not absolute—they depend on the nature of the innovation and the type of ruling. By the end, you will understand why the Hanafi majority dismisses commoners' opinions in ijtihadi matters, while the author adopts a nuanced middle path.

Guided Path

Distinguish the two categories

Levels of Ijma: Explicit and Silent Consensus in Hanafi Usul
Expansion probe on the levels of ijma and the dispute over silent consensus

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between explicit and silent consensus, state the conditions for silent consensus, and evaluate the four scholarly opinions on its legal status within the Hanafi school and beyond.

Core Idea
Silent consensus is disputed: most Hanafis accept it as binding proof; some reject it; its strength lies between explicit ijma and individual ijtihad.

In this passage, the Hanafi usuli author presents the hierarchy of consensus. The strongest is explicit agreement among the Companions, which is infallible. Then comes silent consensus (ijma sukuti), where some scholars voice an opinion and others remain silent. The passage unpacks the debate around whether that silence truly constitutes consent. We will explore the conditions, the four views, and the Hanafi position.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two levels of ijma
The strongest is explicit consensus of the Companions (ijma al-sahaba nasan) because it includes the people of Madinah and the Prophet's clan; its denier is declared a disbeliever. The second is silent consensus (sukuti), inferred from the silence of mujtahids after a ruling spreads and time passes without opposition.
This hierarchy establishes epistemic weight: explicit is qat'i, silent is zanni.
2
Analyze the conditions for silent consensus
Key conditions: the ruling must spread among scholars of the era; sufficient time must pass for reflection; no explicit opposition must appear; the silent scholars must have had the opportunity to speak. Some (al-Jubba'i) require the entire era to pass without opposition.
These conditions are crucial to prevent false claims of consensus.
3
Map the four opinions on silent consensus
First opinion: it is consensus and proof (most Hanafis, Ahmad, some Shafi'is). Second: neither consensus nor proof ('Isa ibn Aban, Dawud, Baqillani, Ghazali). Third: proof but not consensus (Abu Hashim, Shafi'i in one view). Fourth: conditional – if it is a fatwa then consensus, if a judge's ruling then not (Abu 'Ali ibn Abi Hurayra).
The Hanafi majority falls under the first opinion, but internal ikhtilaf exists.
4
Evaluate the Hanafi majority's reasoning
They argue from custom: it is customary for senior scholars to take the lead and for others to submit; silence under opportunity indicates agreement. Additionally, it is obligatory for a mujtahid to speak against falsehood; silence thus implies consent. Opponents counter that silence may be due to lack of ijtihad, fear, or other reasons.
The majority's argument relies on 'custom' as a usul principle.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the levels (maratib) of consensus (ijma). The Validity of Later Consensus After Prior Disagreement
Dispute on later ijma' after prior ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the validity of later consensus when a prior dissenting opinion exists, evaluate the opposing argument, and apply the ruling to determine when such consensus is binding.

Core Idea
Later consensus after prior disagreement is valid but weaker — like khabar al-wahid, it binds action without certainty.

What happens if scholars from a later generation agree on a ruling, but there was a known disagreement among earlier scholars (e.g., Sahaba) on that same point? Does that later agreement constitute ijma'? The Hanafi school answers yes, but with lower epistemic weight — similar to a solitary report (khabar al-wahid). Some other schools, including certain Ash'aris and Zahiris, deny its validity entirely. This passage from the Usul text lays out the dispute and the Hanafi reasoning, particularly the importance of whether the earlier disagreement was 'firmly established' (mustaqirr).

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Issue
State the precise scenario: later scholars agree on a ruling where a known earlier scholar (e.g., a Sahabi) had a different view. The question is whether this later agreement is a valid ijma'.
This scenario is distinct from when no prior view is known — that is a stronger ijma'.
2
State the Hanafi Position
The Hanafi school holds that such consensus is valid (a hujja) but weaker than absolute ijma'. It is analogous to khabar mashhur or khabar al-wahid: it must be followed in practice but does not produce certainty (yaqin).
The author (al-Husami) mentions that this is the chosen view (al-mukhtar) attributed to Muhammad ibn al-Hasan and some transmissions from Abu Hanifa.
3
Identify the Opposing Position
The opposing view (attributed to Al-Ash'ari, Ahmad ibn Hanbal, Al-Ghazali, etc.) states that this is not a valid ijma' because the dissenter's opinion is not nullified by death. The issue remains open for ijtihad.
It is also reported from Abu Hanifa by some mashayikh, but the author favors the other transmission.
4
Analyze the Hanafi Response
The Hanafi response refutes the opposition by pointing to cases where the prior disagreement is not 'firmly established' (mustaqirr). If the earlier dissenting view was not widely adopted or was time-bound, later consensus can override it. The analogy to 'khabar mashhur' shows it gives tranquility (tuma'nina) but not certainty.
The disagreement must be examined: was it a settled difference or a momentary ijtihad that died with the scholar?
5
Determine the Thamarah al-Khilaf (Practical Consequence)
If the later consensus is valid, it becomes binding and cannot be challenged by qiyas. If not, the issue remains ijtihadi and one may follow the earlier dissenting view.
Example: the sale of umm walad — after initial disagreement between Umar and Ali, later scholars agreed on impermissibility. According to Hanafis, this later consensus binds.

The Levels of Ijma': Transmission and Prior Agreement
Compares ijma' transmission to hadith transmission and discusses its authority.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify ijma' according to the presence of prior disagreement and the method of transmission, and state the authority level of each type.

Core Idea
Ijma' is always a proof, but its binding force ranges from certainty to mere action depending on transmission.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text explains that ijma' from the Companions can reach us in two ways: mass transmission (mutawatir) or solitary reports (ahad). Each has a different legal effect. Additionally, the presence of prior disagreement among the mujtahids before the ijma' affects its rank. Learn to distinguish these layers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two scenarios of prior disagreement
The passage states that ijma' where no prior disagreement occurred is like a well-known (mashhur) hadith, giving tranquility; where prior disagreement occurred, it is like an authentic solitary (sahih ahad) hadith, giving action without certainty.
This distinction affects how confidently the ijma' is applied.
2
Distinguish the two modes of transmission
If ijma' is transmitted by tawatur (mass transmission through every generation), it gives certainty and action, like the ijma' on Abu Bakr's caliphate. If transmitted by ahad (solitary reports), it requires action but not certainty.
The transmission method determines the epistemological weight.
3
Apply the analogy to sunnah
Just as the sunnah is certain in origin (from the Prophet) but becomes probable in transmission when via ahad, so too ijma' is certain in origin (from the infallible ummah) but probable when transmitted by ahad.
This qiyas (analogy) is central to the Hanafi justification.
4
Note the precedence over qiyas
Even ijma' transmitted by ahad takes precedence over individual qiyas because qiyas is probable in its origin, while ijma' is certain in origin.
This establishes the hierarchy of proofs.

Core Idea
Most usulis accept ahad ijma' as binding; a minority rejects it, arguing fundamentals require certainty.

While the majority of scholars hold that ijma' transmitted by solitary reports is a binding proof for action, some notable figures like al-Ghazali and certain Hanafi jurists argue that such ijma' cannot be established because fundamentals must be based on certain proofs. This lesson examines both sides and the practical implications.

Commentary
This passage explains that ijma' is a proof (hujja) both in matters where prior disagreement occurred and where it did not. However, its rank differs: when there is no prior disagreement, it is like a well-known hadith (mashhur) giving tranquility; when there is prior disagreement, it is like an authentic solitary report (sahih ahad) giving action without certainty. The authority of ijma' also depends on how it is transmitted to later generations. If transmitted by tawatur (mass transmission) through successive generations, it gives certainty, like the ijma' on Abu Bakr's caliphate. If transmitted by ahad (solitary reports), it requires action but not certainty, similar to ahad hadith. The author then argues that just as the sunnah is certain in its origin (from the infallible Prophet) but may become probable in transmission, so too ijma' is certain in its origin (from the infallible ummah) but may be probable in transmission. Therefore, ijma' transmitted by ahad is binding for action but does not produce certainty, and its denier is not a kafir. It takes precedence over qiyas. The passage also notes a minority view (some Hanafi jurists and al-Ghazali) that ijma' via ahad is not established and should not be a proof. Finally, it mentions a related dispute about whether the ijma' of the majority (with minority opposition) is considered, noting six opinions and that the first (not considering it) is the view of the majority.

Key Terms
المتواتر (al-mutawatir): A transmission that occurs through so many chains that collusion on falsehood is impossible, giving certainty.
الآحاد (al-ahad): A transmission that does not reach the level of tawatur; gives probability (zann) and requires action.
الحجة (al-hujja): Proof that is legally binding; requires compliance.

The truth is the second, by three points: First: the words 'the believers' and 'the community' occurring in the proofs indicating the infallibility of the community and that consensus is a proof are true of the majority even if one or two oppose, as is customary, like saying 'Banu Tamim honor guests' meaning most of them. Second: his statement 'Follow the great majority, which is the majority' (Ibn Majah no. 3590) and his statement 'Whoever deviates deviates into the Fire' (Tirmidhi no. 2167). The one or two relative to the majority are deviant and reprehensible, so their opinion is not heeded. Third: consensus: indeed the Companions agreed on the caliphate of Abu Bakr despite the opposition of Ali and Sa'd ibn 'Ubadah to them, and this is considered consensus. Commentary
This passage is a detailed commentary on the transmission and authority of Ijma' (consensus). It begins by stating that Ijma' is a proof (hujjah) in all cases, whether it resolves a pre-existing disagreement or arises in a novel matter. However, its epistemological weight differs: an Ijma' on a matter with no prior disagreement is like a 'mashhur' (well-known) hadith, while an Ijma' on a disputed matter is like a 'sahih ahad' (authentic solitary) hadith. The key distinction here is how the Ijma' is transmitted to later generations. If it is transmitted by the consensus of each generation (i.e., via tawatur), it becomes certain (qat'i) like mutawatir hadith. If transmitted through isolated reports (khabar al-wahid), it remains certain in its origin (because it stems from the infallible community) but becomes zanni (speculative) in its transmission, thus necessitating action ('amal) but not certain knowledge ('ilm). This transmitted Ijma' is given precedence over qiyas (analogy) because qiyas is zanni in its origin. The passage then records a disagreement: some Hanafi scholars and al-Ghazali argue that Ijma' transmitted via ahad reports does not constitute a valid proof because it would be establishing a fundamental usul principle on a zanni basis. The commentator refutes this by affirming the majority view. Finally, the passage addresses the validity of Ijma' when the majority agrees but a minority dissents. Six opinions are listed, and the author supports the second opinion: that the consensus of the majority is valid, citing three proofs: (1) the general terms 'believers' and 'community' in the proofs for infallibility apply to the majority by common usage; (2) Prophetic hadiths commanding following the 'swad al-a'zam' (great majority) and warning against deviance; (3) the historical precedent of the Caliphate of Abu Bakr where the majority agreed despite opposition from Ali and Sa'd ibn 'Ubadah, and this was considered a valid consensus.

Key Terms
الإجماع (al-ijmāʿ): Consensus of the mujtahids of the Muslim community after the Prophet's death on a legal ruling.
المتواتر (al-mutawātir): A report transmitted by a large number of narrators in every generation, making it certain.
خبر الآحاد (khabar al-āḥād): A report that does not reach the level of tawatur, transmitted by one or a few narrators.
السواد الأعظم (al-sawād al-aʿẓam): The great majority; used in the hadith to indicate the main body of Muslims.

Majority Consensus as Binding Ijma: The Hanafi Position
Presents six positions on majority consensus as ijma and defends the second.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on majority consensus as ijma and evaluate the three proofs supporting it.

Core Idea
Majority consensus (akthar) is binding ijma; requiring unanimity destroys ijma's foundation.

Does a ruling become authoritative when the majority of mujtahids agree, even if a minority dissents? This passage presents six scholarly positions, then defends the Hanafi view that majority consensus suffices for binding ijma. You will analyze the three proofs the author cites: linguistic custom, prophetic hadith, and the Companions' practice.
