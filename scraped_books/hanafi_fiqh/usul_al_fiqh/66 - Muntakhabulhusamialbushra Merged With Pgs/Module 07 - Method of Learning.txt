Definition of Haqiqah (Literal Usage) with Qayd al-Haythiyya
Defines haqiqah and explains the need for qayd al-haythiyya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define haqiqah with the necessary qualification of qayd al-haythiyya and identify the four types of wad' (placement).

Core Idea
A word can be haqiqah from one perspective and majaz from another; definition requires qayd al-haythiyya.

The third division of expression usage yields four categories: haqiqah, majaz, sarih, and kinayah. This lesson focuses on haqiqah—a word used in its original meaning. However, 'original meaning' is not absolute; it depends on the type of placement (wad'). The Hanafi precision of qayd al-haythiyya resolves apparent contradictions, ensuring the definition remains logically sound.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four divisions of usage
The passage divides usage into haqiqah, majaz, sarih, and kinayah. Understand that haqiqah and majaz pertain to usage (istimal), while sarih and kinayah pertain to clarity (jary).
This distinction avoids overlap between the categories.
2
Define haqiqah as per the author
Haqiqah is 'a word used in its original meaning.' Break down the definition: 'word' is genus, 'intended for what it was placed for' is differentia. Recognize that 'placed' implies a setter (wadi').
The original meaning is determined by the type of wad'.
3
List the four types of wad'
The placer may be a language setter (wad' lughawi), the Lawgiver (wad' shar'i), a specialized group (wad' urfi khas/istilahi), or the general custom (wad' urfi 'amm).
These types affect whether a word is considered haqiqah or majaz.
4
Understand the necessity of qayd al-haythiyya
Without qualifying the perspective, a single word like 'salat' would be both haqiqah (linguistically for du'a) and majaz (legally for ritual prayer) simultaneously, contradicting the definition. Qayd al-haythiyya specifies which wad' is being considered.
This prevents logical invalidation of the definition.
5
Apply the definition to examples
Test the definition on 'salat' (linguistic haqiqah, legal majaz) and 'dabbah' (horse: linguistic majaz as quadruped, linguistic haqiqah as crawler). Confirm that qayd al-haythiyya resolves the dual classification.
This step demonstrates practical use.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary defining 'haqiqah' (literal usage) within the Hanafi usul framework. The author begins by explaining the third division of the usage of expressions, which results in four categories: haqiqah, majaz, sarih (explicit), and kinayah (implicit). Haqiqah is defined as a word used in its original meaning. The commentary clarifies that the original meaning is determined by the type of placement (wad'): linguistic, legal (shar'i), customary general (urfi 'amm), or customary specific (urfi khas/istilahi). The author emphasizes that a single word can be haqiqah in one type of wad' and majaz (metaphorical) in another, as illustrated with the word 'salat' (prayer/supplication). To avoid contradictions in definition, the author insists on the necessity of 'qayd al-haythiyya'—qualifying the perspective from which the word is considered haqiqah. This is a key Hanafi precision that safeguards against logical pitfalls. The passage also underscores that haqiqah and majaz are attributes of words, not meanings. The commentary is rooted in the Hanafi tradition, drawing on earlier works like Sharh al-Badi'.

Key Terms
الحقيقة (al-haqiqah): A word used in the meaning it was originally placed for, considering the type of placement (wad').
الوضع (al-wad'): The assignment of a word to a meaning, which can be linguistic, legal (shar'i), or customary (urfi).
قيد الحيثية (qayd al-haythiyya): The qualification of perspective, necessary in the definition of haqiqah to avoid contradictions.

Definition of Majaz and Its Core Condition
Defines majaz with its essential condition of 'alaqah.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of majaz, identify the necessary condition of 'alaqah, and distinguish majaz from ghulat, hazl, and murtajal.

Core Idea
Majaz requires a genuine link between meanings; without it, you have error or jest, not figurative speech.

Majaz (figurative usage) is a cornerstone of legal interpretation in Hanafi usul. Understanding its precise definition and the essential condition of 'alaqah allows you to correctly analyze Quranic and hadith texts. This lesson unpacks the definition, clarifies what is excluded, and explores a nuanced debate about the scope of majaz.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core definition
Read the opening: 'Majaz is a word used in a meaning other than its original due to a connection.' Focus on the two key components: the shift in meaning and the requirement of a connection ('alaqah).
The word 'majaz' itself indicates 'crossing over' – the word crosses from its original meaning to a new one.
2
Analyze the condition of 'alaqah
The 'alaqah is the relationship that justifies the figurative use. The passage lists examples (causality, similarity, etc.) and notes that scholars have enumerated them differently. Understand that without a valid 'alaqah, the usage is not majaz.
The example 'asad' for a brave man works due to the relationship of bravery.
3
Distinguish exclusions
The definition excludes ghulat (error – no intended meaning connection), hazl (jest – no real intent), and murtajal (new coinage – considered haqiqah from a new placement). Recognize why each fails the condition of 'alaqah.
Murtajal, like 'car', is haqiqah because it was placed for that meaning from the start.
4
Engage with the ziyadah debate
The passage raises an objection: does 'majaz bi al-ziyadah' (e.g., the extra kaaf in 'laysa kamithlihi shay') fall under the definition? The author notes it is used in a non-original meaning but questions the presence of an 'alaqah. Reflect on why this is a methodological challenge.
This debate tests the boundaries of the definition and the role of 'alaqah.
5
Review the enumerations of 'alaqat
The passage cites different scholars who have counted the possible relationships from 4 to 25. Appreciate the inductive (istiqra') approach of Hanafi usul in establishing these categories.
This shows that the number is not fixed but based on thorough analysis of linguistic usage.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary defining 'majaz' (figurative usage) in Hanafi usul. It defines majaz as a word used in a meaning other than its original, conditioned on the existence of a connection ('alaqah) between the original and figurative meanings. The author uses the example of 'asad' (lion) for a brave man, due to the relationship of bravery. The definition excludes several cases: ghulat (outright error, e.g., using 'earth' for 'sky'), hazl (jest without real intent), and murtajal (a term coined for a new meaning without prior relationship). Murtajal is actually haqiqah from the new placement, not majaz. The commentary also discusses the challenge of 'ziyadah' (excess letters like the kaaf in the Quranic verse 'Laysa kamithlihi shay'), which some argue is majaz, but the author notes that without a clear relationship, it may not qualify. The passage concludes with a note about the various enumerations of 'alaqat (relationships) by different scholars, reflecting the extensive inductive analysis in Hanafi usul. The footnote clarifies the morphological form of 'maf'al' used in the definition.

Key Terms
المجاز (al-majaz): A word used in a meaning other than its original due to a relationship ( 'alaqah).
العلاقة (al-'alaqah): The relationship between the original and figurative meanings that justifies the figurative usage.
المرتجل (al-murtajal): A term coined for a new meaning without a prior relationship; considered haqiqah from the new placement.

Using Cause as Metaphor for Effect in Legal Language
Explaining the usul rule of using cause for effect and the limits of metaphor in legal language

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on cause-effect metaphor, apply it to legal examples, and evaluate the Shafi'i counterargument.

Core Idea
Cause may be used for effect in metaphor; effect cannot be used for cause except by special specificity.

This lesson explains a key rule in Hanafi usul al-fiqh regarding the use of metaphor (isti'arah) in legal language. When a word is used metaphorically, the relationship between the original meaning and the intended meaning is governed by the nature of the connection. The passage focuses on the case where the word for a cause is used to express its effect, and vice versa, and clarifies why the Hanafi school permits only one direction. Through an examination of Quranic evidence and a comparison with the Shafi'i position, you will understand the underlying principle and its practical legal consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule and its formulation
The passage states that it is permissible to mention the cause (origin) and intend the effect, but not the reverse. Understand the key terms: cause (sabab/origin) and effect (musabbab/ ruling).
Note the asymmetry: the cause is independent of the effect.
2
Examine the Hanafi proof
The cause is independent of the effect; the effect depends on the cause. Therefore the connection is one-sided. The Quranic example (wine for grapes) is an exception because of specificity.
Quran 12:36 shows effect-for-cause only when the effect is specific to the cause.
3
Contrast with the Shafi'i position
The Shafi'i school permits both directions based on a conceptual connection (both involve removal/isqat). The Hanafi response distinguishes between formal and conceptual connection.
Hanafis argue that in divorce and emancipation, the legal meanings differ (removal vs. establishment).
4
Analyze the grammatical analogy
The deficient sentence conjoined to a complete sentence illustrates asymmetrical dependence, mirroring the cause-effect relationship.
The first sentence is independent; the second depends on the first, just as the cause is independent of the effect.
5
Derive the ruling of metaphor
The ruling (hukm) of metaphor is that the intended meaning, whether specific or general, is realized. This is the legal effect of using a word metaphorically.
This ruling applies to both literal and metaphorical usage, but the means of establishing the intended meaning differs.

Commentary
This passage is a section from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh work discussing the principle of using one word to refer to another through metaphor (istiaarah) in legal language. The author first explains the rule that it is permissible to mention the cause (origin) and intend the effect (branch), but not the reverse. For example, if a man says to his wife “You are free” but intends divorce, the divorce occurs because the word “free” is a cause (origin) for emancipation, but divorce is an effect that arises from the same concept of removing ownership. However, saying “You are divorced” to a slave woman intending emancipation does not free her, because the effect (divorce) is not inherently tied to the cause (emancipation) in the same way. The author contrasts the Hanafi view with the Shafi'i view, which permits using the effect for the cause, arguing that there is a conceptual connection (ittisaal ma'nawi) between emancipation and divorce—both involve removal (isqaat) and pervasion (sarayaan). The Hanafi response is that the connection is only formal (soori) not conceptual, because emancipation establishes power while divorce removes marital ownership; they share no famous characteristic like the lion and brave man. The author then draws an analogy with grammar: a deficient sentence (e.g., “Zaynab”) when conjoined to a complete sentence (“Hind is divorced”) causes suspension of the complete sentence until the end, but only for the sake of the deficient sentence's validity. Similarly, the connection between cause and effect is one-sided. Finally, the author discusses the ruling of metaphor (hukm al-majaaz): the intended meaning (whether specific or general) is realized. He notes that while the ruling of literal meaning is agreed upon, there is disagreement among some Shafi'is about metaphor. The author clarifies that the generality of metaphor pertains to the type of connection, not all connections simultaneously.

Key Terms
استعارة (isti'ārah): Using a word for something other than its original meaning due to a connection (علاقة). Here, using the cause (أصل) for the effect (فرع) or vice versa.
أصل (aṣl): The original word or meaning that is the basis for the metaphor; here it refers to the cause (سبب) in a causation relationship.
فرع (far'): The derived meaning or effect; here it refers to the ruling (حكم) or effect (مسبب) that is intended through the metaphor.
سبب (sabab): The cause; the initiating factor that leads to a ruling in law.
مسبب (musabbab): The effect or result that follows from a cause.
مجاز (majāz): A word used for other than its original meaning with a connection. Its ruling is that the intended meaning (specific or general) is established.
اتصال (ittiṣāl): Connection; here it refers to the relationship between cause and effect that allows one to stand for the other in metaphor.

Causes of Obligation: Sabab vs. Shart and the Nafs al-Wujub Distinction
Explaining causes of Sharia rulings with Quran and ijma evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the cause (sabab) of obligation and the condition (shart) of performance in Hanafi usul, and apply the distinction to identify the basis for obligation of acts like Hajj, fasting, and prayer, using the Quranic and ijma evidence.

Core Idea
Obligation (wujub) is established by the cause; the command only mandates performance — this explains makeup duties for those incapable of address.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text introduces the foundational concept of causes (asbab) in Sharia. Understanding what triggers an obligation versus what merely conditions its valid performance is critical for deriving rulings correctly. The author presents examples from Hajj, fasting, prayer, penalties, and expiation, and then defends the principle that the very existence of obligation (nafs al-wujub) is attached to the cause, while the duty to perform (wujub al-ada) is imposed by the divine command. The consensus on the sleeping and insane person's duty to make up prayers serves as key evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause for each example
For each act mentioned (Hajj, fasting, prayer, hudud, kaffara, transactions), locate the entity to which the ruling is attributed in the text. That entity is the cause (sabab). For instance, Hajj is attributed to the House (Ka'ba), so the House is the cause.
Attribution (idafa) in Arabic grammar signals causation; the Quranic verse 'Hajj of the House' is quoted to prove this.
2
Distinguish cause from condition (shart)
The text explicitly notes that for Hajj, time is a condition (shart) for valid performance, not the cause. Understand the difference: the cause triggers obligation, the condition merely makes performance sound.
e.g., the time of Hajj (the months) is necessary for the pilgrimage to be valid, but the obligation itself arises from the House itself.
3
Recognize the internal Hanafi difference on fasting's cause
Two opinions are given: (1) Imam Abu Zayd and Sadr al-Islam hold that the cause is the dawn of each day; (2) Imam al-Sarakhsi holds that the cause is witnessing the entire month. This affects when exactly the obligation for each day's fast is established.
The text does not resolve which is mufta bihi; it presents both as legitimate Hanafi views.
4
Understand the distinction between nafs al-wujub and wujub al-ada
The passage addresses an objection: if obligation is by cause, the command seems redundant. The author replies by dividing obligation into two aspects: the very existence of obligation (nafs al-wujub) is caused by the sabab; the duty to perform (wujub al-ada) comes from the command.
Example: a deferred sale — the price is owed (wajib in the dhimmah) from the contract, but performance is not due until the term or demand.
5
Apply to the case of the insane/unconscious person
The text cites ijma: the sleeping, insane, and unconscious must make up prayers if the condition lasts less than a day and a night. Since they are not addressed by the command during that state, this proves their obligation is caused by the arrival of prayer time (the sabab), not by a command they cannot hear.
This directly demonstrates the distinction. The Shafi'i position (only the sleeping must make up) is noted as a contrast.

Commentary
This passage is a chapter from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh commentary explaining the concept of causes (asbab) of Sharia rulings. The author begins by listing examples: Hajj's cause is the House (Ka'ba), fasting's cause is the month of Ramadan, prayer's cause is its times, hudud penalties have their causes (crimes), expiation (kaffara) has a cause that is between prohibition and permission (like accidental killing), and transactions are legislated for the preservation of the world. The key principle is that a cause is that to which a ruling is attributed, and it recurs leading to recurrence of the ruling. For Hajj, the Quranic verse (3:97) shows attribution to the House, proving it as cause, while time is merely a condition for performance. For fasting, there is a difference of opinion among Hanafi scholars: Imam Abu Zayd and Sadr al-Islam hold that the cause is the days (specifically the dawn of each day), while Imam al-Sarakhsi holds that the cause is witnessing the entire month. This affects when the obligation is triggered. For prayer, the times are the cause. For penalties, the cause is the crime itself. Expiation (kaffara) is unique because it shares features of both worship and punishment, so its cause must be something that is between prohibition and permission, like accidental killing (which involves both a prohibited element (lack of caution) and a permissible element (hunting)). Transactions (like sales, marriage) are legislated to preserve human existence, which depends on cooperation and social interaction. The author then discusses the cause for obligation of faith (iman), which is the temporality of the world (huduth al-alam)—the fact that the world is created. This cause is outward to facilitate enforcement of obligation. The passage then addresses an objection: if obligation is by causes, then what is the role of the divine command? The author distinguishes between the very existence of obligation (nafs al-wujub), which is by the cause, and the obligation to perform (wujub al-ada'), which is by the command. As evidence, he cites the consensus of jurists that a sleeping, insane, or unconscious person must make up missed prayers if the condition does not exceed a day and a night. These individuals are not subject to the command during their state, yet they still owe prayers, proving that obligation (wujub) is established by the cause (e.g., entering the prayer time), while the command to perform applies only when they become capable. The Shafi'i position is noted: they do not require makeup for the insane/unconscious if the whole prayer time passed in that state, except for the sleeping. This difference arises from the usul principle of whether obligation is attached to causes or to the command directly.

Key Terms
سبب (sabab): That to which a legal ruling is attributed (yudaf ilayhi al-hukm) and which recurs leading to recurrence of the ruling. It is the cause that triggers obligation (wujub).
وجوب (wujub): The legal obligation itself (nafs al-wujub) which is established by the cause, as distinct from the obligation to perform (wujub al-ada') which is by command.
أداء (ada): Performance of an act at its proper time or when due. The obligation of performance becomes binding only after the command (amr) is addressed to the capable person.

The Three Aspects of Bayan (Clarification)
Defining bayan and presenting three scholarly perspectives

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three aspects of bayan and identify which usul scholars emphasized each aspect.

Core Idea
Bayan has three facets: the act of clarification, the evidence, and the resulting knowledge.

In usul al-fiqh, the term 'bayan' (clarification) is foundational, yet its precise meaning can vary depending on context. This lesson unpacks a classical definition that distinguishes three distinct aspects of bayan: the process of clarifying, the evidence used to clarify, and the understanding that results. Recognizing these facets will sharpen your ability to analyze how Shari'ah rulings are derived and communicated.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three aspects
From the passage, extract the three matters: (1) al-i'lam (the act of informing), (2) ma yahṣulu bihi al-tabayyun (that by which clarification is obtained), and (3) ma yahṣulu min al-dalil (that which is obtained from evidence).
These correspond to 'act', 'means', and 'result'.
2
Link aspects to scholars
Each aspect has proponents: Abu Bakr al-Sayrafi and Sahib al-Tawdih define bayan as the act (first aspect); most fuqaha' and mutakallimun define it as the evidence (second aspect); Abu Bakr al-Daqqaq and Abu Abdullah al-Basri define it as the resulting knowledge (third aspect).
Memorize which scholar/school holds which view.
3
Understand the equivalence
The passage notes that 'al-tabayyun' equals 'al-ʿilm' (knowledge). This clarifies that the third aspect is not merely information but certain knowledge derived from dalil.
Knowledge here is the outcome of proper istidlal.
4
Apply the definition
When reading a fiqh text, identify which sense of bayan is intended: is the author discussing the act of explaining, the evidence that explains, or the resultant understanding? This prevents confusion.
Example: 'The bayan of this verse is in the hadith' uses the second aspect (evidence).

Qiyas as Revealer: The Two Categories of Rulings
Methodological explanation of what the three proofs establish and the placement of Qiyas discussion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school regards Qiyas as a revealer (muzhir) not an establisher (muthbit), and identify the two categories of rulings established by primary proofs.

Core Idea
Qiyas reveals what is implicit in primary sources; it does not establish new rulings.

This passage addresses a fundamental methodological question in Hanafi usul: why is the discussion of rulings (ahkam) placed after the chapter on Qiyas? The answer lies in the nature of Qiyas as a mere revealer of already established rulings. We also explore a practical fiqh example where this principle affects the preference for validity over corruption in worship, contrasting with the Shafi'i approach.

Guided Path
1
Understand the role of Qiyas
Recognize that the text explicitly states Qiyas is 'muzhir la muthbit' (revealer, not establisher). This means Qiyas does not create new rulings from nothing; it only manifests what is already indicated by the Quran, Sunnah, or Ijma.
This is a distinctively Hanafi emphasis; other schools may view Qiyas as an independent source.
2
Identify the two categories established by primary proofs
The three primary proofs establish: (1) 'al-ahkam al-mashru‘ah' (taklifi rulings like obligation, prohibition, permission) and (2) 'ma yata‘allaq bihi al-ahkam al-mashru‘ah' (wad‘i rulings like causality, conditionality, and signs). Note that these are the foundation for all fiqh.
Taklifi rulings directly address the mukallaf's actions; wad‘i rulings are auxiliary.
3
Analyze the arrangement rationale
The author places the discussion of rulings after Qiyas because this discussion is a 'tamimah' (supplement) to the chapter on Qiyas. Qiyas requires knowledge of the cause ('illah) and the original ruling to transfer it. Without understanding rulings and their causes, Qiyas cannot function. Thus, the supplement follows the main topic.
This defies the objection that means should precede ends; here, the end (Qiyas) is prioritized because it is a proof of Shari‘ah.
4
Examine the fiqh dispute on preferring corruption
The text uses a fiqh example: when an act of worship combines aspects of validity and corruption, the Shafi‘i school prefers corruption out of caution (as in a traveler's fast). The Hanafi school does not prefer corruption here because the attribute (travel) is transitory ('aridi) and does not affect the essential validity. This illustrates how the nature of the ruling affects methodological preferences.
This dispute shows the practical consequence of understanding the distinction between essential and accidental attributes.

Chapter of Qiyas. 235. What is established by the previous proofs in Qiyas. And we do not prefer corruption out of caution in acts of worship; because it is a preference based on a transitory meaning. The ba is extra. Preference. Corruption in worship. What is established by the previous proofs in Qiyas. Section: Then the totality of what is established by the proofs that were mentioned before the chapter on Qiyas are two things: the legislated rulings and what pertains to the legislated rulings. And reasoning for Qiyas is only valid after knowing this totality, so we attached it to this chapter to be a means to it after perfecting the method of reasoning. He prefers. i.e. this totality. Knowing this totality means [a means] to Qiyas. Chapter of worship: The meaning is we do not prefer the corrupt side in worship here just as we prefer the corrupt side in the chapter of other acts of worship for caution. When in them a side of corruption and a side of validity are combined, the side of corruption is preferred out of caution unanimously. So ash-Shafi'i also has [the rule of] corruption in fasting due to caution. The reason the side of corruption is not preferred here is because it is a preference based on a transitory meaning, i.e. an accidental attribute, since worship in fasting is an accidental matter as passed earlier. So ponder this place, for it is a slippery place. Know that it has preceded that the subject of Usul al-Fiqh according to the chosen madhhab is the four proofs and the rulings altogether. So when he finished from discussing the proofs that establish rulings, he began discussing the rulings. So he said: Then the totality of what etc. He mentioned it through his statement: 'which were mentioned before the chapter on Qiyas' which indicates that Qiyas does not establish anything; because it is a revealer, not an establisher. So the meaning: the three proofs — the Book, the Sunnah, and the consensus of the Ummah — establish two things: one is the legislated rulings, and the second is what pertains to the legislated rulings. The author: The legislated rulings: i.e. the taklifi rulings like permissibility, prohibition, vow, dislikedness, and clarity will come to you inshaAllah. And 'what pertains to it' etc. are the wad'i rulings like the ruling of causality and conditionality. For the author discusses in the second section about the cause, the effective cause, the condition, and the sign. The discussion of these two sections is a discussion about the ruled object, i.e. the action of the legally responsible person. After that is the discussion of the ruled subject (the legally responsible person) as the author will explain in the chapters on intellect, capacity, and the incidental matters. Those matters are among the attributes of the legally responsible person that are discussed in this science. The reason for this arrangement is that the ruling, which is a quality of the action of the legally responsible person — like obligation, prohibition, and dislike — needs the Ruler, which is Allah Almighty, not the intellect, and needs the ruled subject, which is the legally responsible person, and the ruled object, which is the action of the legally responsible person. Since it might be said that the establisher of the rulings and what pertains to them is the three proofs, not Qiyas, so why did the author attach the discussion of this totality to Qiyas, bringing this discussion after Qiyas? He repelled this by his statement: 'And indeed reasoning is only valid' etc. Knowing this totality, i.e. the rulings and what pertains to them. 'To this chapter' i.e. to the chapter of Qiyas, because it is brought after Qiyas after perfecting the conditions of Qiyas and its ruling, and explaining the difference between the effective cause and the non-effective cause, and explaining the objections and preferment. The conclusion is that the discussion of this totality is a supplement to the discussion of Qiyas; because Qiyas is for transferring a ruling known by its cause. Then he said: And its condition is [that it is] for a known attribute, and that is only realized after knowing this totality. Therefore we presented the discussion of this totality after Qiyas, just as we presented earlier the conditions of Qiyas, its ruling, the effective cause, non-effective cause, objections, and preferment. So it should not be said: Since knowing this totality is a means to Qiyas, it should have been presented before Qiyas just as means are presented before ends. For we say: Qiyas is a proof from the proofs of the Shari'ah, so its rank is that it comes before this discussion and is attached to the three proofs. And because we say this discussion is a supplement to the discussion of Qiyas, this objection is repelled; for the supplement is only mentioned after what it supplements, even if knowledge of the supplement is a means to it. So understand.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary explaining why the discussion of rulings (ahkam) and their objects (like causes and conditions) is placed after the chapter on Qiyas. It opens by stating that the three primary proofs (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) establish two categories: the actual rulings (taklifi) like obligation and prohibition, and the wad'i rulings like causality and conditionality. Qiyas does not establish anything new; it only reveals the ruling that is already indicated by the proofs. Therefore, the discussion of these two categories is a supplement to Qiyas, because Qiyas requires knowledge of the cause and the ruling to transfer it. The author justifies the order: since Qiyas is a proof of the Shari'ah, its chapter comes first, and then the supplementary discussion follows. He also addresses a methodological point about preferring corruption over validity in acts of worship. In other acts of worship, when both validity and corruption are possible, the corrupt side is preferred out of caution (e.g., a disputed fast). However, in the case under discussion (the traveler's fast), the Hanafi position does not prefer corruption because the attribute of travel is transitory and does not affect the essential validity. Imam Shafi'i prefers corruption even in fasting out of caution. The commentary clarifies that this is a matter of preference based on a transitory description, not a permanent rule. The passage thus demonstrates the logical arrangement of the topics in Usul al-Fiqh according to the Hanafi school.

Key Terms
القياس (al-qiyās): Analogical reasoning that transfers a known ruling from an original to a branch due to a common cause; it is a revealer (muzhir) not an establisher (muthbit).
الأحكام المشروعة (al-aḥkām al-mashrū‘ah): The legislated rulings, here meaning the taklifī rulings such as obligation, prohibition, permission, etc., which are established by the primary sources.
الأحكام الوضعية (al-aḥkām al-waḍ‘iyyah): Rulings that are not directly commands or prohibitions but rather cause, condition, or sign for other rulings, e.g., the ruling of causality.
