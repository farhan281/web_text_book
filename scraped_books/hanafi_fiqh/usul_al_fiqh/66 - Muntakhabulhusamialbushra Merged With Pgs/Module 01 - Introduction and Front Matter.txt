Understanding the Editorial Methodology of Muntakhab al-Husami
Editors explain their methodology for producing a new edition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the editorial changes made in this edition of Muntakhab al-Husami and explain their importance for studying Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
A properly edited classical text preserves the author's intent while making it accessible to modern students.

This lesson examines the editorial methodology behind the new edition of Muntakhab al-Husami, a key Hanafi usul al-fiqh text. The publishers recognized that contemporary students face challenges with older prints due to outdated typography and scattered notes. By understanding their approach, you will learn how to critically evaluate printed editions and appreciate the care needed to preserve classical scholarship.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the introduction from the original text
Identify which parts of the passage belong to the publishers (the introduction and methodology) versus the original work. The basmalah and hamdalah are standard Islamic openers, but the rest is editorial.
This is a modern editorial addition, not part of the original matn.
2
Identify the specific editorial changes
List the six methodological points: moving comments to footnotes, correcting spelling errors, adding headings, using black/red colors, modernizing orthography, and adding vowel marks.
Each change addresses a specific problem in older editions.
3
Understand the rationale behind each change
For each change, consider the problem it solves. For example, moving comments from between lines to footnotes reduces clutter and improves readability.
The rationale is to make the text more student-friendly.
4
Evaluate the impact on learning
Reflect on how these changes affect your ability to study the text. Better layout and clear distinctions between main text and commentary enhance comprehension.
This is a practical skill for using any classical edition.

Commentary
This passage is the introduction (مقدمة) and editorial methodology (منهج عملنا) provided by the publishers of the new edition of Muntakhab al-Husami. It begins with the basmalah and hamdalah, typical of Islamic texts. The editors highlight the importance of Muntakhab al-Husami in Hanafi usul al-fiqh and note that contemporary students face difficulties with older editions due to changes in printing and modern educational contexts. They explain that they formed a committee of scholars to produce a corrected, modernized edition. The methodology includes moving marginal notes to footnotes, correcting spelling errors (especially from Indian and Pakistani prints), adding section headings, using black for the main text and red for words explained in footnotes, modernizing orthography and punctuation, and placing vowel marks on difficult words. The editors end with a supplication for acceptance and benefit. This is purely editorial front matter and does not contain substantive fiqh rulings or usul principles.

Key Terms
منتخب الحسامي (Muntakhab al-Husami): A classical work on Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the subject of this new edition.
أصول الفقه (Usul al-Fiqh): The principles and methodology of Islamic jurisprudence.
الحواشي (al-Hawashi): Footnotes; in this context, the commentary that was originally placed between lines is now moved to footnotes.

The Nobility of Usul al-Fiqh and the Purpose of the Commentary
Author's praise of the original text and explanation of his need for a commentary

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the four foundations of shari'ah as identified in this introduction and explain why the commentator considered usul al-fiqh the noblest science.

Core Idea
Usul al-Fiqh is the noblest science because it combines transmitted and rational knowledge to derive rulings.

This lesson introduces you to the commentary we will study. The commentator, Abd al-Haqq ibn Muhammad Amin, begins by praising Allah for establishing the palace of rulings upon the four foundations of Shari'ah. He then explains why the science of usul al-fiqh is the most magnificent, and why he was compelled to write a commentary on the famous Mukhtasar of al-Akhsikathi, despite his own perceived limitations and the decline of learning in his time.

Guided Path
1
Identify the matn author and commentator
The original text (matn) is the Mukhtasar by Imam Muhammad ibn al-Akhsikathi (Husam al-Din). The commentary is by Abd al-Haqq ibn Muhammad Amin. Clarify their roles.
The commentator is not the original author – a common confusion.
2
Extract the four foundations of Shari'ah
In the hamdala, the commentator says Allah built the palace of rulings upon 'usul al-shari'ah' and 'the Book and Sunnah' and adorned it with 'Ijmā' and Qiyās'. List these four: Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijma', Qiyas.
These are the classical sources of Islamic law in Hanafi usul.
3
Understand why usul al-fiqi is the noblest science
The commentator states it 'combines transmitted and rational knowledge' and through it rulings are known and halal/haram distinguished. This gives it supreme rank.
This is a standard Hanafi emphasis on the integration of naql and aql.
4
Analyze the commentator's hesitation and motivation
He hesitated due to: (1) decline of sciences in his time, (2) his own 'scant merchandise' (lack of knowledge), (3) many distractions. But divine success (tawfiq) drove him forward after being asked by brothers.
This reflects the adab of a scholar – humility and reliance on Allah.
5
Appreciate the historical context
The commentator laments that sciences are destroyed, scholars hidden, and knowledge is being lifted. This mirrors the hadith he cites. Understanding this context shows the value of preserving usul works.
The introduction ends mid-sentence, suggesting the next page continues with the commentary proper.

Commentary
This passage is the commentator's introduction to his sharh (commentary). He begins with the basmala and hamdala, praising Allah who established the structure of Shari'ah rulings on the foundations of the Book, Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas. He sends blessings upon the Prophet and his family and companions. He then identifies himself as al-faqir Abu Muhammad Abd al-Haqq ibn Muhammad Amin and states that Usul al-Fiqh is the noblest science because it allows one to know rulings and distinguish halal from haram. He praises the original matn, the Mukhtasar of Muhammad ibn al-Akhsikathi (known as Husam al-Din), describing it as the best in its field, widespread like rain and proverbs. However, because of its conciseness, it needed explanation. He was asked by some brothers to write a sharh that would resolve its difficulties. He hesitated due to the decline of knowledge in his time (sciences destroyed, scholars hidden), his own limited ability, and distractions, but divine guidance pushed him to proceed. He completed the work and presents it as a humble offering to a patron (likely a ruler) whose city he praises. The introduction ends mid-sentence, indicating the page break. This sets the stage for the commentary that follows.

Key Terms
أصول الشريعة (usul al-shari'ah): The foundations of Islamic law: the Book (Qur'an), the Sunnah, Ijma', and Qiyas.
مختصر (mukhtasar): A concise summary; here referring to the Mukhtasar of al-Akhsikathi, a well-known Hanafi usul work.
شرح (sharh): A commentary that explains and expands upon a concise text.
المنقول والمعقول (al-manqul wa al-ma'qul): Transmitted knowledge (from revelation) and rational knowledge (derived through reason).
قائد التوفيق (qa'id al-tawfiq): The guide of divine success – referring to Allah's help.

The Science of Usul al-Fiqh: Its Importance and Scope
Opening praise and definition of usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of usul al-fiqh as combining transmitted and rational knowledge and explain why it is considered the most noble science in the Hanafi tradition.

Core Idea
Usul al-fiqh combines revelation and reason to distinguish halal from haram.

The commentator opens his preface with a powerful description of usul al-fiqh as the science that combines transmitted (naql) and rational ('aql) knowledge. This lesson unpacks why this science is held in such high regard in the Hanafi school and what it aims to achieve. You will learn the foundational significance of usul al-fiqh for deriving rulings and understanding the structure of Islamic law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the praise of Allah
Read the opening hamdulillah and note how the commentator describes the building of rulings upon the foundations of Shariah, using the Book and Sunnah, and then adorning with consensus and analogy. This sets the stage for the sources of usul.
The 'palace of rulings' metaphor highlights the systematic nature of fiqh.
2
Locate the definition of usul al-fiqh
Find where the commentator says 'the most sublime of sciences... is the science of usul (principles) which combines transmitted and rational knowledge'. Underline this sentence.
This dual nature distinguishes usul from purely transmitted or purely rational disciplines.
3
Understand the purpose of usul al-fiqh
The commentator says 'by it rulings are known and the lawful is distinguished from the unlawful'. Reflect on how usul provides the methodology for jurists to derive rulings from the sources.
Without usul, the process of ijtihad would lack a systematic framework.
4
Note the mention of consensus and analogy
Observe that the commentator includes consensus (ijma) and analogy (qiyas) as adornments of the Shariah. In Hanafi usul, these are secondary sources after Quran and Sunnah.
This reflects the four-source structure of Hanafi usul: Quran, Sunnah, Ijma, Qiyas.

The Need for Commentary and the Decline of Knowledge
Commentator's explanation of his motivation and hesitation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the commentator undertook the commentary despite his hesitation, and identify the challenges to religious learning in his time.

Core Idea
Even when scholars feel inadequate, the duty to preserve knowledge compels them to act.

In this part of the preface, the commentator shares his personal struggle: he was asked to write a commentary on the Mukhtasar, but he hesitated due to the decline of religious sciences and his own perceived insufficiency. However, he eventually was guided to write it. This lesson explores the reasons behind his reluctance and the factors that compelled him to proceed, offering insights into the ethics of scholarly work and the challenges of preserving Islamic knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the request and the text
Note that some brothers asked the commentator to explain the Mukhtasar with a commentary that would resolve its difficulties.
The Mukhtasar is renowned but concise.
2
List the reasons for hesitation
The commentator mentions three obstacles: (a) sciences have fallen into disuse, schools abandoned, scholars hidden; (b) his own meager stock and negligence; (c) the general state of the times. Write them down.
These reflect both personal humility and a real crisis in Islamic scholarship.
3
Understand the decisive factor
He says 'the guide of success led me to this path'. Identify this as divine tawfiq (success from Allah) that overcame his reluctance.
Tawfiq is a key concept in Islamic ethics – success comes from Allah.
4
Analyze the hadith reference
The commentator cites the Prophet's statement: 'Indeed, knowledge is taken away from men.' Reflect on how this hadith relates to the decline he observes.
This hadith is often understood as a sign of the end times when scholars die and knowledge is lost.

Explanation of why the ruler's praise is included

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the commentator included praise of the ruler in his religious work and evaluate the ethical tension between scholarship and political patronage.

Core Idea
Scholars may include praise of rulers only when compelled by necessity, maintaining intellectual independence.

The commentator includes a lengthy praise of the ruler Mir Mahbub Ali Khan, but he also states that he is otherwise 'free of the princes of this time and their embellishments'. This lesson examines the delicate relationship between scholars and rulers in Islamic history. The commentator's approach illustrates how scholars navigated the need for patronage while preserving their integrity and the purity of religious works.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruler and his description
Find the passage that begins with 'I made it a display, nay, a humble offering for the presence of him...' and note the epithets used for the ruler (e.g., 'virtuous, perfect, devout scholar').

2
Understand the reason for inclusion

3

Immediately after, he says 'Otherwise, I am free of the princes of this time and their embellishments' and criticizes their preoccupation with singers.
This shows he does not generally approve of including rulers' names in religious books.
4
Analyze the historical context

The commentator is careful to distinguish this particular ruler from others.

Commentary
This passage is the opening preface of the commentary (sharh) by the commentator Abu Muhammad Abd al-Haqq ibn Muhammad Amin. It is an introduction to his commentary on the well-known usul al-fiqh text, the Mukhtasar of Imam Husam al-Din Muhammad ibn al-Akhsikathi. The commentator begins with the basmala and hamdala, praising Allah for establishing the Shariah on firm foundations. He sends blessings on the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) and his companions. He then states his purpose: to provide a commentary that clarifies the concise and sometimes difficult passages of the Mukhtasar, which is a revered work in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. He mentions that he was asked by some brothers to write this commentary, but he hesitated due to the decline of religious knowledge in his time and his own inadequacy. However, he was eventually guided to undertake it. He also explains that he included praise for the ruler of his time, Mir Mahbub Ali Khan Bahadur Sultan al-Dakan, as a necessary gesture, though he expresses reluctance to involve rulers in religious works. He concludes with a request for supplication and forgiveness for any shortcomings. The preface sets the stage for the commentary that follows, emphasizing both the importance of usul al-fiqh and the humility of the commentator.

The Foundations of Sharia: Book, Sunnah, and Ijma'
Defining the foundational evidences of Sharia and their scope.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the term 'usul al-shar' is used instead of 'usul al-fiqh', identify the three primary sources of Sharia, and describe the systematic classification of legal evidences.

Core Idea
Sharia encompasses belief and practice; fiqh only practice. Usul al-shar' covers all foundational evidences.

In this lesson, we examine the opening of Muntakhab al-Husami, where the author defines the foundational sources of Islamic law. The passage clarifies why these sources are called 'usul al-shar' rather than 'usul al-fiqh', highlighting the broader scope of Sharia compared to fiqh. Understanding this distinction is crucial for appreciating the full range of Islamic evidence, from theological beliefs to practical rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Find the definitions of 'usul' (foundations) and 'shar' (manifestation/path) as given in the passage. Note the linguistic and technical meanings.
'Usul' in this context means the evidences upon which the religion is built.
2
Understand the choice of 'shar' over 'fiqh'
The author explains that Sharia includes both theoretical ('itiqadiyyat) and practical ('amaliyyat) rulings, while fiqh is limited to practical rulings. This justifies using 'usul al-shar' to cover all evidence for both domains.
This distinction avoids the misconception that the foundations only pertain to legal practice.
3
List the three primary sources
The passage states the Book (Quranic legal verses, about 500), the Sunnah (legal hadith, about 3000), and Ijma' (consensus of mujtahids of the Ummah). Note the qualifications: 'some of it' for Book and Sunnah, and 'mujtahids' for Ijma'.
Ijma' is specifically the consensus of mujtahids, not just any Muslims.
4
Analyze the systematic classification
At the end of the passage, a method for controlling the four evidences is given: revelation that is recited and inimitable is the Book; revelation not recited is Sunnah; unanimous consensus of the entire Ummah is Ijma'; otherwise it is Qiyas. Also, laws of previous prophets are annexed to Book/Sunnah, customs to Ijma', and sayings of Companions to Qiyas or Sunnah depending on rationality.
This classification helps integrate various types of evidence into the four-source framework.
5
Recognize Qiyas as a fourth derived source
Although the focus is on the three primary, the passage notes that Qiyas is a fourth source derived from them. This will be explored in the next lesson.
Qiyas is both a foundation (as a source) and a branch (derived from the others).

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the foundational statement that the sources of Sharia are three (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) and that Qiyas is a fourth derived source. The commentator first defines the terms: 'usul' (foundations) linguistically means that upon which something is built, and here it refers to evidences. 'Shar' linguistically means manifestation, as in the Quranic verse, and also means path; technically it is synonymous with religion or Sharia. The writer explains why the author said 'usul al-shar' (foundations of the Sharia) instead of 'usul al-fiqh' (foundations of jurisprudence), because religion is broader than fiqh, covering both theoretical beliefs and practical rulings, while fiqh only covers the practical. By using 'shar', he includes all religious evidence. Some commentators interpreted 'shar' either as the Lawgiver (the Prophet) or as the legislated. The passage then explains that the Book refers only to the legal verses (about 500), the rest being stories etc., and the Sunnah similarly refers only to legal hadith (about 3000). Ijma refers to the consensus of the mujtahids of the Ummah, not just the Companions. Then the passage turns to Qiyas, noting that it is both a foundation (as a source) and a branch (derived from the others). Three examples of Qiyas are given: from the Quran (analogy between sodomy and intercourse during menstruation based on the cause of harm), from the Sunnah (analogy of plaster to the six commodities based on quantity and genus), and from Ijma (analogy of the mother of a fornicated woman to the mother of a female slave based on partial relation). The commentator critiques the first example, arguing that the prohibition of sodomy might already be established by text, which would invalidate the analogy. Finally, the passage provides a systematic categorization of all four evidences: revelation that is recited and inimitable is the Book; if not recited, it is Sunnah; the consensus of the entire Ummah is Ijma; otherwise it is Qiyas. It also places laws of previous prophets under Book/Sunnah, customs under Ijma, and sayings of Companions under Qiyas or Sunnah depending on rationality. This lays the groundwork for the detailed discussion of each source.

Qiyas: The Fourth Derived Source of Islamic Law
Explaining Qiyas as a fourth derived source with examples and a critical condition.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Qiyas is a derived source, analyze the three examples of Qiyas from the passage, and identify the condition that Qiyas requires the absence of a text in the branch.

Core Idea
Qiyas is valid only when there is no direct textual ruling for the branch case; otherwise, the text takes precedence.

In this lesson, we delve into the author's discussion of Qiyas as the fourth source of Sharia. The passage provides three concrete examples of Qiyas derived from each primary source: Quran, Sunnah, and Ijma'. It also includes a critical examination of the first example, emphasizing a crucial condition for valid Qiyas: the absence of a direct textual ruling in the branch case. Understanding this condition is essential for proper analogical reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand Qiyas as a derived source
The passage states that Qiyas is 'الأصل الرابع' (the fourth foundation) and that it is derived from the three primary sources. It is both a foundation (as a source) and a branch (derived).
Unlike the three primary sources, Qiyas relies on reasoning from existing texts.
2
Analyze the Qiyas derived from the Quran
The example: the prohibition of sodomy is analogized to the prohibition of intercourse during menstruation based on the cause (illa) of 'harm'. The commentator then critiques this example, arguing that the prohibition of sodomy may already be established by a text, which would invalidate the Qiyas.
The critique highlights the condition that Qiyas requires 'عدم النص في الفرع' – the absence of a text in the branch.
3
Analyze the Qiyas derived from the Sunnah
The example: the prohibition of exchanging one qafiz of plaster for two qafiz of the same plaster is analogized to the prohibition of riba in the six commodities (wheat, barley, etc.) based on the cause of quantity (qadr) and genus (jins).
This shows how Qiyas can extend a sunnah-based ruling to new items if the cause is present.
4
Analyze the Qiyas derived from Ijma'
The example: the prohibition of intercourse with the mother of a woman with whom one has committed zina is analogized to the prohibition of intercourse with the mother of one's female slave with whom one has had intercourse. The latter prohibition is established by Ijma', not a specific text.
This Qiyas is based on a ruling that itself is from Ijma', showing the layered nature of derivation.
5
Identify the condition for valid Qiyas
From the critique, we learn that a valid Qiyas requires that the branch case (far') not have a direct textual ruling. The commentator argues that if sodomy with men is the branch, its prohibition is already in the Quran, so Qiyas is unnecessary; if sodomy with one's wife is the branch, its prohibition is in the hadith. Thus the example fails the condition.
This condition prevents redundant Qiyas and ensures that Qiyas is only used when the law is truly silent.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the foundational statement that the sources of Sharia are three (Quran, Sunnah, Ijma) and that Qiyas is a fourth derived source. The commentator first defines the terms: 'usul' (foundations) linguistically means that upon which something is built, and here it refers to evidences. 'Shar' linguistically means manifestation, as in the Quranic verse, and also means path; technically it is synonymous with religion or Sharia. The writer explains why the author said 'usul al-shar' (foundations of the Sharia) instead of 'usul al-fiqh' (foundations of jurisprudence), because religion is broader than fiqh, covering both theoretical beliefs and practical rulings, while fiqh only covers the practical. By using 'shar', he includes all religious evidence. Some commentators interpreted 'shar' either as the Lawgiver (the Prophet) or as the legislated. The passage then explains that the Book refers only to the legal verses (about 500), the rest being stories etc., and the Sunnah similarly refers only to legal hadith (about 3000). Ijma refers to the consensus of the mujtahids of the Ummah, not just the Companions. Then the passage turns to Qiyas, noting that it is both a foundation (as a source) and a branch (derived from the others). Three examples of Qiyas are given: from the Quran (analogy between sodomy and intercourse during menstruation based on the cause of harm), from the Sunnah (analogy of plaster to the six commodities based on quantity and genus), and from Ijma (analogy of the mother of a fornicated woman to the mother of a female slave based on partial relation). The commentator critiques the first example, arguing that the prohibition of sodomy might already be established by text, which would invalidate the analogy. Finally, the passage provides a systematic categorization of all four evidences: revelation that is recited and inimitable is the Book; if not recited, it is Sunnah; the consensus of the entire Ummah is Ijma; otherwise it is Qiyas. It also places laws of previous prophets under Book/Sunnah, customs under Ijma, and sayings of Companions under Qiyas or Sunnah depending on rationality. This lays the groundwork for the detailed discussion of each source.

Key Terms
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical deduction: extending a ruling from an original case (asl) to a new case (far') based on a shared effective cause (illa).
العلة (al-illa): The effective cause or ratio legis that connects the original ruling to the new case.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the major sections and key topics of Hanafi usul al-fiqh as listed in the index of al-Husami, and use the index to locate specific discussions.

Core Idea
The index of al-Husami is a roadmap for the entire science of Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

This lesson uses the index of al-Husami to give you a bird's-eye view of the structure of Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The index reveals how classical scholars organized topics: from foundational sources (Kitab Allah, Sunnah) to linguistic categories (al-amm, al-khass, al-majaz) and then to specific legal principles (command, prohibition, performance, concession). By understanding this structure, you will be able to navigate any Hanafi usul text with confidence.

Guided Path
1
Locate the main divisions
Scan the index to identify the broad sections: Introduction, Foundations of Law (Kitab Allah), Divisions of Wording and Meaning, then topics on Command, Prohibition, Sunnah, etc.
This is the skeleton of the book.
2
Examine the linguistic categories
Under 'Divisions of Wording and Meaning', note terms like al-khass (special), al-amm (general), al-mushtarak (common), al-mu'awwal (interpreted), al-zahir (apparent). These form the core of Hanafi usul.
Hanafis treat al-amm as definitive (qat'i) until specification.
3
Identify the sections on command and prohibition
Find the entries for al-amr (command) and al-nahy (prohibition). Note subtopics like 'command after prohibition' and 'absolute command'.
These sections discuss the implications of imperative and prohibitive forms.
4
Locate the Sunnah and hadith classifications
Look for 'Sunnah and its types' and then detailed entries: mutawatir, mashhur, ahad, mursal, musnad, etc.
Hanafi usul has specific rulings for each hadith category.
5
Observe supplementary topics
Notice sections on performance (ada) and make-up (qada), resolve (azima) and concession (rukhsa), and contradiction (mu'arada).
These complete the usul framework.
