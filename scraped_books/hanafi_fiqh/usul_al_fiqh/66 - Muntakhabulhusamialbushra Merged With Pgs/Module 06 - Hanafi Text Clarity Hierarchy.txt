Distinguishing al-Nass and al-Mufassar in Hanafi Usul
Defines al-Nass and al-Mufassar with Quranic examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-Nass and al-Mufassar, identify their defining characteristics using Quranic examples, and explain the legal implications for tafsir and ta'wil.

Core Idea
Al-Nass clarifies the speaker's intent; al-Mufassar eliminates all alternative interpretations.

In Hanafi usul, texts are classified by clarity. Two important categories beyond al-Zahir are al-Nass, whose clarity comes from the speaker's context (siyaq), and al-Mufassar, which is so clear that no specification (takhsis) or interpretation (ta'wil) can alter its meaning. This lesson unpacks these definitions with Quranic examples and examines their practical consequences for deriving rulings.

Guided Path
1
Memorize the definitions
Internalise that al-Nass is clearer than al-Zahir because the speech is driven (siyaq) for that meaning. Al-Mufassar is clearer than al-Nass, cutting off all possibility of takhsis and ta'wil.
Focus on the key contrast: nass admits some possibility of specification/interpretation; mufassar does not.
2
Examine the Quranic examples
For al-Nass, study Quran 4:3 – 'marry what is good for you of women'. It is zahir for permissibility but nass for limiting the number of wives because the context (siyaq) is about the number. For al-Mufassar, study Quran 15:30 – 'the angels prostrated, all of them together'. The words 'all' and 'together' explicitly prevent takhsis and ta'wil.
Note how 'all of them' removes specification (only some angels) and 'together' removes interpretation (separate acts).
3
Analyze the defining criteria
Understand that al-Nass still allows room for takhsis and ta'wil, while al-Mufassar does not. This is because the wording of mufassar is so explicit that no alternative meaning can be entertained.
The text itself must leave no ambiguity; if any alternative reading is linguistically possible, it is not mufassar.
4
Apply the consequences
Because mufassar yields certainty (qat'), interpreting it by personal opinion (tafsir bil ra'y) is forbidden. However, ta'wil (conjectural interpretation) is permitted for texts that are not mufassar, as long as it is not claimed as certain.
This distinction impacts how a jurist derives rulings and justifies them.

Commentary
This passage defines two categories of clear texts: al-Nass and al-Mufassar. al-Nass is a text whose clarity is increased by the speaker's formulation—the speech is 'driven' (siyaq) for that meaning. The example is Quran 4:3, 'marry what is good for you of women', which is zahir (apparent) for permissibility (ibaha) but nass for the limitation to the number (two, three, four). The siyaq shows the purpose is to explain the number, so it is nass for that. al-Mufassar is even clearer: it eliminates all possibility of takhsis (specification) and ta'wil (interpretation). The example is Quran 15:30, 'the angels prostrated, all of them together'. The word 'all' removes the possibility of takhsis (that only some prostrated), and 'together' removes the possibility of ta'wil (that they prostrated separately). The commentator explains the derivation of mufassar from 'fasr' (to uncover), emphasizing that mufassar provides certainty (qat') regarding the intended meaning. This is contrasted with tafsir (exegesis) by personal opinion, which is forbidden because it does not yield certainty. Ta'wil, being conjectural, is not forbidden. The passage also notes that nass still admits takhsis and ta'wil, while mufassar cuts off those possibilities completely.

Key Terms
النص (al-Nass): A text that has increased clarity over al-Zahir because it is driven (siyaq) for that meaning by the speaker.
المفسر (al-Mufassar): A text that has increased clarity over al-Nass, removing all possibility of takhsis and ta'wil, yielding certainty of meaning.

Mufassar and Muhkam: The Highest Levels of Textual Clarity in Hanafi Usul
Defines mufassar and muhkam with examples and rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mufassar and al-muhkam, explain how they differ from nass, and apply the hierarchy when conflicting texts arise.

Core Idea
Mufassar removes all possibility of specification and interpretation; muhkam additionally removes the possibility of abrogation.

In the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity, we move from zahir to nass, and then to mufassar. This lesson explores the final two levels—mufassar and muhkam—which represent the highest degrees of certainty. Understanding these categories is crucial for resolving conflicting evidence, as the clearer text prevails.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definitions
Understand mufassar as a text that eliminates all possibilities of takhsis (specification) and ta'wil (interpretation), and muhkam as a text that additionally eliminates the possibility of naskh (abrogation).
Mufassar is stronger than nass; muhkam is the strongest.
2
Analyze the Quranic example
Examine verse 15:30. Notice how 'kulluhum' (all of them) removes the chance that only some prostrated, and 'ajma'un' (together) removes the chance of separate prostration.
The combination of both words is necessary to achieve mufassar.
3
Address the objection
Consider why the exception of Iblis does not destroy the mufassar quality: istithna' (exception) is not takhsis, as exception does not imply the text is capable of being specified.
The objection is answered by distinguishing exception from specification.
4
Distinguish mufassar from muhkam
Recognize that mufassar can still be abrogated by a later revelation, while muhkam is immune to abrogation and alteration.
Muhkam is reserved for texts like attributes of Allah or hadith that are confirmed against change.
5
Apply the hierarchy in practice
When two texts conflict, the one from a higher category (muhkam > mufassar > nass > zahir) is given preference.
This hierarchy helps resolve apparent contradictions in evidence.

Commentary
This passage defines the third and fourth levels of textual clarity: al-mufassar and al-muhkam. Mufassar is clearer than nass because it eliminates any possibility of specification (takhsis) or interpretation (ta'wil). The example is Quran verse 15:30: the angels prostrating. The word 'all of them' removes possibility that only some prostrated, and 'together' removes possibility that they prostrated separately. An objection is answered: the exception of Iblis is not specification because exception (istithna) does not imply the text is not mufassar; it is a separate category. The ruling of mufassar is that it decisively establishes the ruling, but it can still be abrogated by a later revelation. When a text gains further strength such that abrogation is also impossible, it becomes muhkam. Muhkam is the highest level, immune to abrogation. Examples include attributes of Allah and the hadith 'Jihad continues until the end.' The practical difference between these terms only matters when texts appear to conflict; then the higher category prevails.

Key Terms
المفسر (al-mufassar): A text that is so clear that no possibility of specification or interpretation remains, but it can be abrogated.
المحكم (al-muhkam): A text that is firm against abrogation and alteration, the highest level of clarity.

The Four Textual Categories: Definitions and Certainty
Definitions and hierarchy of textual categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define each of the four textual categories (Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, Muhkam) and distinguish them based on clarity, possibility of specification, interpretation, abrogation, and certainty.

Core Idea
Muhkam is immune to abrogation; Mufassar is definitive but still abrogable; Nass and Zahir yield certainty according to the majority Hanafi view.

This lesson introduces the foundational hierarchy of textual clarity in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. You will learn the precise definitions of Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, and Muhkam, and understand how each category relates to the others in terms of legal certainty. The passage explains that while all four categories establish rulings with certainty, they differ in their susceptibility to specification, interpretation, and abrogation.

Guided Path
1

Zahir and Nass are open to some possibility; Mufassar is clear and definitive but can be abrogated; Muhkam is firm and immune to change.
2

The probability arises from the evidence itself, not from external sources.

Both categories are definitive (qat'i), but Mufassar can be abrogated while Muhkam cannot. The passage explains that Muhkam may be 'li 'aynihi' (inherent) or 'li ghayrihi' (due to cessation of revelation).
'Muhkam li ghayrihi' includes all texts after the Prophet's death because abrogation ceased.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul work explains the hierarchy of textual categories used to determine the strength of legal evidence from the Quran and Sunnah. The author begins by defining the categories: Zahir (apparent), Nass (explicit), Mufassar (clearly explained), and Muhkam (firm, immune to change). The discussion includes how to resolve apparent conflicts between texts of different categories. The author provides detailed examples: the conflict between Zahir and Nass regarding polygamy (Quran 4:24 vs 4:3), the conflict between Nass and Mufassar regarding wudu for the mustahadah (using hadith with 'for every prayer' vs 'for the time of every prayer'), and the conflict between Mufassar and Muhkam regarding accepting testimony of those who repented from slander (Quran 65:2 vs 24:4). A key point of Hanafi internal dispute is presented: whether Zahir and Nass yield certainty (like Mufassar and Muhkam) or merely require action. The Iraqi Hanafis (Karkhi, Jassas, etc.) argue they yield certainty, while Shaykh Abu Mansur holds they only require practical adherence. The text sides with the Iraqi view, stating that probability from the evidence does not undermine certainty. The lesson concludes by noting that these categories are equal in establishing certainty but show difference in preponderance at conflict.

Key Terms
الظاهر (al-zahir): The apparent meaning that requires a ruling but is open to possibility of specification or interpretation.
النص (al-nass): Explicit text that does not allow for specification but may still be subject to abrogation.
المفسر (al-mufassar): A text clearly explained and definitive (qat'i), but can be abrogated.
المحكم (al-muhkam): A text that is firm and immune to abrogation and change; has two types: muhkam li 'aynihi (in itself) and muhkam li ghayrihi (due to external reason like demise of revelation).
محكم لغيره (muhkam li ghayrihi): A text that becomes muhkam due to the cessation of abrogation after the Prophet's death.

The Four Levels of Textual Clarity and Their Certainty
Explains the four levels of textual clarity and their role in establishing certainty

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the four levels of textual clarity (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) in a Quranic or hadith text, apply the Hanafi rule that each establishes certainty (qat‘i), and explain how precedence is determined when they conflict.

Core Idea
All four text levels yield certainty; their difference is only in precedence when they conflict.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, texts are classified by their clarity into four categories: zahir, nass, mufassar, and muhkam. A common misconception is that only the latter two deliver certainty. This lesson clarifies that the majority of Hanafi scholars (especially the Iraqi school) hold that all four give certainty; the distinction matters only when texts conflict, so the higher level prevails. We will walk through the definitions, a key internal dispute, and concrete examples of tarjih.

Guided Path
1
Define the four levels
Identify each category: zahir (apparent but open to an alternative), nass (clear with no plausible alternative), mufassar (explicitly clarified), and muhkam (definitive, cannot be abrogated). Understand that the first two are subject to debate on certainty, while the latter two are agreed upon.
The author gives examples: zahir (4:24), nass (4:3), mufassar (hadith on mustahadah with word 'time'), muhkam (24:4).
2
Locate the dispute on certainty
Identify the two positions: the majority (Iraqi Hanafis) say zahir and nass yield qat‘i; the minority (Abu Mansur et al.) say they only obligate action and belief. Note the majority's evidence: probability arising from evidence does not harm certainty.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf with practical implications for conflict resolution.
3
Understand the role of conflict (ta‘arud)
When two texts conflict and both are from the same or adjacent levels, the higher clarity level is preferred (tarjih). If both are from the same level, further rules apply. The passage gives three examples: nass over zahir (marrying up to four), mufassar over nass (wudu for mustahadah), and muhkam over mufassar (testimony of slanderers).
The difference in certainty only becomes operative in case of conflict.
4
Apply the Hanafi principle to a new text
Given a new Quranic or hadith text, classify its clarity level and determine whether it yields certainty. If it conflicts with another text, decide which level prevails using the hierarchy.
Practice with the example of the mustahadah: the mufassar (wudu for every prayer time) overrides the nass (wudu at every prayer).

Khafi: The Opposite of Zahir Defined
Defines khafi as the opposite of zahir, with example of theft verse.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define khafi in Hanafi usul and explain why the verse of theft (5:38) is khafi with respect to tarrar and nabbash.

Core Idea
Khafi: meaning hidden by an external factor, requiring ijtihad — illustrated by the theft verse and pickpockets.

In the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity, zahir is the most apparent, and its direct opposite is khafi. But khafi is not simply an unclear text; it is a text whose intended application is hidden due to something outside the wording itself, such as a specialized term. This classification is critical because it determines that the ruling is not self-evident and requires ijtihad to apply. The classic example is the theft verse, which is clear in its general meaning but becomes khafi when applied to pickpockets (tarrar) and grave-robbers (nabbash) — people known by different names.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of khafi
Identify the two key components: (a) the hiddenness is due to an external factor (bilâ arif), not the wording itself; (b) it cannot be known except by active seeking (talab).
Memorize the exact phrasing: 'ما خفي المراد منه بعارض غير الصيغة'
2
Distinguish khafi from other unclear texts
If the hiddenness were due to the wording itself, the text would be mushkil or mujmal, not khafi. The theft verse is khafi, not mushkil, because the wording is clear — only the application to tarrar/nabbash is hidden.
This distinction is essential for correct classification.
3
Identify the external factor in the theft example
Recognize that tarrar and nabbash are not called 'thieves' in common usage because they carry specialized names due to an increase or decrease in the meaning of theft.
The external factor is the specialized terminology (اسم آخر).
4
Apply the Hanafi method of ijtihad for khafi texts
For khafi, the jurist must perform ijtihad to see if the increase/decrease justifies inclusion or exclusion from the ruling. The Hanafi view: tarrar gets hadd (increase), nabbash does not (decrease).
This shows the practical consequence of the khafi classification.

Al-Khafi and Al-Mushkil: Categories of Hidden Texts in Hanafi Usul
Defines al-Khafi and al-Mushkil with rulings and an example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-khafi and al-mushkil, and apply the Hanafi method of resolving each by identifying the cause of hiddenness and using contextual indicators.

Core Idea
Al-khafi hides by surplus or deficiency; al-mushkil hides by resemblance – each requires a different level of reflection.

In the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity, some texts are not immediately clear. This lesson introduces two shades of hiddenness: al-khafi, where the meaning is hidden in some instances due to an increase or decrease in meaning relative to the apparent, and al-mushkil, where the meaning is obscured by resemblance to other possibilities. The passage provides definitions, examples, and the ruling (hukm) for each, along with a concrete method for resolving mushkil texts through reflection on contextual indicators (qara'in).

Guided Path
1
Identify the textual category
Determine whether the hiddenness is due to a surplus/deficiency in meaning (khafi) or due to the text entering into similars with multiple possible meanings (mushkil).
Khafi is hidden in some individuals, mushkil is hidden by nature of the wording.
2
Determine the ruling (hukm)
For khafi, the ruling is to reflect (al-nazar) to know the cause of hiddenness. For mushkil, the ruling is reflection after deliberate seeking (al-ta'mmul ba'd al-talab).
Mushkil requires more effort.
3
List possible meanings (for mushkil)
Enumerate the plausible interpretations based on linguistic usage. For example, 'anna shi'tum' can mean 'where you wish' or 'how you wish'.
Each meaning has a different legal implication.
4
Apply contextual indicators (qara'in)
Examine external evidence from the Quran, Sunnah, or reason to eliminate incorrect meanings. The word 'tilth' (haruth) and the restriction in 2:222 support the meaning of 'how' rather than 'where'.
Contextual indicators are essential to resolve mushkil.
5
Finalize the intended meaning and derive the ruling
Once the correct meaning is determined, derive the fiqh ruling accordingly. For 2:223, the ruling is that intercourse is permissible in various positions but only in the vagina.
The correct meaning avoids legal error.

Definition and Types of Mutashabih with Debate on Knowability
Defining mutashabih, its ruling, and the debate on its knowability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mutashabih, state its ruling of tawaqquf, explain the Hanafi position on its knowability, and identify its two types.

Core Idea
Mutashabih is a text whose meaning is known only to Allah, requiring suspension of judgment.

In the Hanafi usul hierarchy, texts vary in clarity. The opposite of muhkam (decisive) is mutashabih (ambiguous)—a text so obscure that its true meaning is beyond human grasp in this world. This lesson unpacks its definition, the ruling of tawaqquf (suspension), and the classical debate on whether the firmly grounded scholars (al-rasikhun) can know its interpretation. You will also learn the two subtypes and the practical ruling derived from mutashabih texts.

Guided Path
1
Define mutashabih and contrast with muhkam
Identify the defining feature: mutashabih has no path to grasp its meaning at all, so seeking it is dropped. Contrast with muhkam, which is clear and safe from abrogation.
Remember the simile: mutashabih is like a lost man whose trace is cut off.
2
Identify the ruling of mutashabih
The ruling is tawaqquf: perpetual suspension of judgment while believing the intended meaning is true. This applies to us (mufti), though the Prophet ﷺ knew it.
Suspension is not ignorance—it is active affirmation of an unknowable meaning.
3
Analyze the dispute over the rasikhun
The Hanafi position (majority of early scholars) holds that only Allah knows the interpretation, based on the reading of 3:7 stopping at 'إلا الله'. Later scholars and Mu'tazila hold the rasikhun also know. Evaluate the dalil and the Hanafi response.
The dispute hinges on waqf position in the verse.
4
Understand the benefit and the two types
The benefit is a test for scholars and ignorant alike. Then distinguish type 1 (utterly unknown, e.g., huruf muqatta'at) from type 2 (linguistically known but intended meaning unknown, e.g., 'yad Allah').
Type 2 potentially conflicts with muhkam verses of tanzih; later scholars resorted to ta'wil.
5
Derive the ruling of belief from mutashabih
Even though the specific meaning is unknown, mutashabih yields a ruling: obligation to affirm the attributes (e.g., hand, face) in a manner befitting Allah, without tashbih or ta'til.
This shows mutashabih is not idle; it has legal effect.

فيطلع على المراد منه في الآخرة. طلبه أي طلب ما يدل على المراد منه فخرج الخفي والمشكل والمجمل، فالمتشابه كرجل فقد عن الناس حتى انقطع أثره وانقضى جيرانه وأقرانه. وحكمه التوقف إلخ في حقنا؛ لأن النبي الله كان يعلم بالمتشابهات كما صرح به فخر الإسلام في أصوله. حقية المراد على سبيل الإجمال، وإن لم يعرف ما أراد الله به على سبيل التعين، فإنا نعلم يقينا أنه صادر من حكيم، فله معنى لطيف وليس بمهمل؛ لأن الحكيم لا يخاطبنا بالمهمل. فذهب عامة الصحابة والتابعين وعامة المتقدمين من أهل السنة من أصحابنا وأصحاب الشافعي إلى أنه لا يعلم المراد منه إلا الله، وهو مختار القاضي أبي زيد وفخر الإسلام وشمس العلماء وجماعة من المتأخرين، فيجب عندهم الوقف على قوله تعالى: {وَمَا يَعْلَمُ تَأْوِيلَهُ إِلَّا اللَّهُ} (آل عمران:7) كما هو قراءة ابن مسعود وأبي وابن عباس، ويجب الابتداء بقوله تعالى: {وَالرَّاسِخُونَ فِي الْعِلْمِ} الْعِلْمِ} لا على ما قبله. فإن قلت: فما الفائدة في إنزالها على المذهب الأول؟ قلتُ: الابتلاء، وهو إنما يكون على خلاف المتمني وترك الهوى، فهوى العالم الاطلاع على كل شيء، فابتلي بتركه، وهوى الجاهل ترك له صفة يعبر عنها باليد والوجه والاستواء مثلا وإن لم نعرف ما أريد منها. وقد ضل بعض الناس حيث ذهبوا إلى أن له تعالى يداً ووجهاً وهو كما قاعد على سريره هو شأن المجسمات، ولم ينظر آيات التنزيه من {لَيْسَ السور مثل: ألم، وحم، ونوع يعلم معناه لغةً لكن لا يعلم ما هو المراد منه؛ لأن ظاهره يخالف المحكم، كقوله تعالى: "وجه الله" و"يد الله" و"الرحمن على العرش استوى".
Opposite of the decisive (muhkam) is the ambiguous (mutashabih): it is that for which there is no way to grasp it at all, so seeking it has fallen away. Its ruling is perpetual suspension (tawaqquf) believing that the intended meaning is true. 'Its ruling etc.' means the ruling of mujmal is suspension in action until clarification from the speaker arrives, while believing the intended meaning is true. Opposite of muhkam: just as muhkam reached utmost clarity and strength, safe from abrogation, similarly mutashabih reached extreme obscurity where hope of clarification is completely cut off—neither by reason nor by transmission in this world, though its intended meaning may be known in the hereafter. 'Seeking it' means seeking what indicates its intended meaning; thus khafi, mushkil, and mujmal are excluded. Mutashabih is like a man lost from people until his trace is cut off and his neighbors and peers have passed. Its ruling is suspension for us, because the Prophet ﷺ knew the mutashabihs, as Fakhr al-Islam stated in his Usul. The truth of the intended meaning is in an overall sense, even if we do not know what Allah intended specifically. We know for certain it comes from the Wise, so it has a subtle meaning and is not meaningless, for the Wise does not address us with nonsense. The majority of the Companions, Successors, and early scholars of Ahl al-Sunna from our scholars and the Shafi'is held that only Allah knows its intended meaning—this is the choice of Qadi Abu Zayd, Fakhr al-Islam, Shams al-A'imma, and a group of later scholars. According to them, one must stop at His statement {And none knows its interpretation except Allah} (Surah Al 'Imran, 7) as in the reading of Ibn Mas'ud, Ubayy, and Ibn Abbas, and must begin with {And those firmly grounded in knowledge} and their statement {they say} which is praise introducing submission and faith that all is from Allah. Most later scholars and the majority of the Mu'tazila held that the firmly grounded also know its interpretation, and that one should pause at {And those firmly grounded in knowledge}, not before. If you ask: What benefit is there in revealing them according to the first view? I say: It is a test, which consists of opposing desire and abandoning caprice. The scholar's desire is to know everything, so he is tested by leaving it; the ignorant person's desire is to avoid study and investigation, so he is tested by that. If you ask: You are explaining the categories by which rulings are known; mutashabih, since its meaning is unknown, how is a ruling derived from it? I say: No—rather, a legal ruling is known from it, which is the obligation to believe that Allah has attributes expressed by 'hand', 'face', 'istawa', etc., even if we do not know what is intended by them. Some people went astray by claiming that Allah has a hand and face as if He is seated on His throne, which is the way of anthropomorphists, and they did not consider the verses of transcendence: {There is nothing like unto Him} (Surah al-Shura, 11) and {Is He who creates like one who does not create?} (Surah al-Nahl, 17). Hence later scholars closed this door and interpreted the mutashabihs. Then mutashabih is of two types: one whose meaning is not known at all, like the disconnected letters at the beginnings of surahs such as 'Alif Lam Mim' and 'Ha Mim'; and a type whose meaning is known linguistically but what is intended is not known, because its apparent meaning contradicts the decisive (muhkam) verses, like 'the face of Allah', 'the hand of Allah', and 'the Most Merciful established over the throne'.

Commentary
This passage defines 'mutashabih' (ambiguous/unclear) as the opposite of 'muhkam' (decisive). Mutashabih is a text whose meaning is completely inaccessible—there is no way to grasp it at all, so seeking its interpretation is not required. Its ruling is perpetual suspension (tawaqquf): one must believe that the intended meaning is true and that it comes from Allah, but cannot know it. The author distinguishes mutashabih from khafi, mushkil, and mujmal, because those can be understood through effort or clarification, whereas mutashabih has no hope of clarification in this world. The text then presents the famous debate about whether the 'rasikhun fi al-ilm' (those firmly grounded in knowledge) know the interpretation of mutashabih. The majority of early scholars (including Hanafis and Shafi'is) said only Allah knows; later scholars and Mu'tazila said the rasikhun also know. The first view is preferred, as it leads to the benefit of testing: the scholar is tested by refraining from seeking knowledge of things Allah has hidden. The author then addresses an apparent contradiction: how can mutashabih be used to derive rulings if its meaning is unknown? The answer: it yields a ruling of belief—obligation to affirm attributes like hand and face without likening Him to creation, and to avoid anthropomorphism. Finally, mutashabih is divided into two types: (1) utterly unknown, like the disconnected letters (huruf muqatta'at); (2) linguistically known but intended meaning unknown due to apparent contradiction with muhkam, like verses about Allah's hand or istiwa'.

Definition of 'Illah and Its Three Aspects
Defining 'illah and its three aspects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'illah in Hanafi usul and distinguish its three essential aspects: ism (name), ma'na (meaning), and hukm (ruling).

Core Idea
A legal cause is complete only when it has a name, is effective, and produces an immediate ruling.

The concept of 'illah (legal cause) is central to qiyas and legal reasoning in the Hanafi school. This passage provides a precise definition and breaks down the 'illah into three considerations: name, meaning, and ruling. Understanding these aspects will help you identify what qualifies as a valid cause and what does not, which is crucial for deriving rulings by analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of 'illah
Read the definition: 'illah is that to which the obligation of a ruling is attributed primarily (ibtida'). Note the examples: sale → ownership, marriage → permissibility, killing → retaliation.
The attribution is direct, without intermediary.
2
Extract the three considerations (i'tibarat)
The scholars considered three matters: (1) the 'illah must be established by the Lawgiver as a basis for the ruling; (2) it must be effective (mu'aththira) in proving the ruling; (3) the ruling must follow immediately without temporal gap.
These correspond to ism (name), ma'na (meaning), and hukm (ruling).
3
Distinguish complete vs. incomplete cause
If all three aspects are present, the 'illah is complete (tamm). If some are missing, it is incomplete (naqis). If none, it is not a cause.
Only a complete cause can be used for definitive qiyas.

As for the cause (al-'illah) in the Shari'ah, it is an expression for that to which the obligation of a ruling is attributed primarily, such as sale for ownership, marriage for marital permissibility, and killing for retaliation. So if the three descriptions are found in one thing, it is a complete perfect cause; if some are found without others, it is an incomplete cause; and if none are found, it is not a cause. According to the three matters, seven categories arise: first: what is name, meaning, and ruling; second: what is name only; third: what is name only; fourth: what is ruling only; fifth: what is name and meaning but not ruling; sixth: what is name and ruling but not meaning; seventh: what is meaning and ruling but not name. * Al-Ithbat: Some virtuosi said: The intended meaning of the effect of a thing is that the intellect judges that this ruling is established by it, and it is its inherent source. Commentary
This passage is a usul definition explaining the concept of 'illah (legal cause/ratio legis) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It begins with a clear definition: 'illah in the Shari'ah is that to which the obligation of a ruling is attributed directly (ibtida'), without any intermediary. Examples: sale causes ownership, marriage causes marital permissibility, killing causes retaliation. The commentary then elaborates that scholars have considered three essential aspects of the 'illah: (1) it must be established by the Lawgiver as a basis for the ruling, and the ruling is attributed to it directly (this is the 'illah as ism 'name'); (2) it must be effective (mu'aththira) in proving that ruling (this is the 'illah as ma'na 'meaning'); (3) the ruling must be established immediately upon the existence of the 'illah, without any temporal gap (this is the 'illah as hukm 'ruling'). If all three are present, the 'illah is complete and perfect (tamm). If some are missing, it is an incomplete cause (naqis). If none are present, it is not a cause. Based on these three aspects, seven categories of 'illah are derived: (1) ism + ma'na + hukm; (2) ism only; (3) something that appears to be ism only (possibly a repeat in OCR but logically should be 'ma'na only'); (4) hukm only; (5) ism + ma'na but no hukm; (6) ism + hukm but no ma'na; (7) ma'na + hukm but no ism. The marginal note clarifies that 'effectiveness' means the Lawgiver has considered the quality as a means to another thing, not necessarily rational causation.

Key Terms
العلة (al-'illah): In the Shari'ah, the 'illah is that to which the obligation of a ruling is attributed directly, without any intermediary. It is the ratio legis or legal cause.
اسم (ism): The first consideration of 'illah: that it be assigned by the Lawgiver as a basis for the ruling; the ruling is attributed to it directly (e.g., 'sale' is the ism for ownership).
معنى (ma'na): The second consideration: the 'illah must be effective (mu'aththira) in proving the ruling; it is the underlying meaning or purpose.
حكم (hukm): The third consideration: the ruling (hukm) must be established immediately upon the existence of the 'illah, without temporal interval.
