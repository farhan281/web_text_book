Definition and Ruling of Mushtarak (Common Word)
Parsing the definition of mushtarak and its ruling of tawaqquf.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mushtarak (the common word) according to the Hanafis, explain its ruling of tawaqquf, and apply the method of tarjih to a given common word.

Core Idea
A mushtarak word requires suspension of judgment until reflection (tarjih) determines the intended meaning.

In this lesson, we delve into the definition of al-mushtarak, a word that shares multiple meanings equally. The Hanafi school holds that such a word demands a pause (tawaqquf) before we can assign a specific meaning. We will explore how scholars, like in the case of 'qur'', used contextual evidence to preponderate one meaning over others.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definitional components
Parse the definition: 'what is shared in meanings or names, not comprehensively but alternatively.' Recognize 'genus' and 'differentia'.
'Not comprehensively' excludes umum (general).
2
Distinguish mushtarak from 'amm
Understand that mushtarak implies alternative sharing (badal), while 'amm implies comprehensive inclusion (shumul). The word 'nahl' for quenching/thirst is an example of mushtarak.
Also note that commonality can exist between two, unlike generality which requires at least three.
3
Examine the ruling: tawaqquf
The ruling (hukm) of mushtarak is suspension (tawaqquf): one must not commit to a meaning without reflection, because all meanings are equally probable.
This is based on the principle that a word equally bearing multiple meanings cannot determine intent.
4
Apply tarjih (preponderance) to the example of 'qur''
Analyze how Hanafi scholars used three indicators (plural form, number three, semantic sense of gathering/transfer) to preponderate menstruation over purity.
Tarjih is based on predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y).

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary on the definition of mushtarak (common word) and then presents its ruling and the definition of mu'awwal (interpreted word). The commentator begins by parsing the phrasing of the definition: 'it is what' (huwa ma) is the genus, and the rest is the differentia. The term 'sharing' (ishtirak) here refers to conventional (wad'i) sharing, meaning the word is equally likely to denote any of its meanings. The phrase 'not in a comprehensive manner' clarifies that the sharing is alternative (badal), not inclusive (shumul), thus distinguishing it from the general ('amm) which includes all individuals. The words 'meanings or names' divide the common word into two types: common in meaning (like 'nahl' meaning both quenching and thirst) and common in names (like 'ayn' referring to many external entities such as sun, knee, etc.). The commentator notes that the plural form 'meanings or names' is used to match the definition of general, not to imply a minimum of three, since commonality can exist between two (e.g., qur'). He then clarifies that 'al-mushtarak' in the definition is the terminological common word, while 'ma ishtaraka' refers to linguistic sharing, avoiding circularity. The ruling (hukm) of the common word is suspension (tawaqquf): one must not commit to a specific meaning without reflection (ta'mmul) to preponderate one over the others, because all meanings are equally probable. He illustrates with the example of 'qur'' (common between purity and menstruation), where Hanafi scholars determined it means menstruation through several contextual indicators (e.g., use of plural, the number three, and the sense of gathering and transfer). Finally, the mu'awwal is defined as the meaning that becomes preponderant from a common word through predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y). It is derived from 'aala/ya'ulu' meaning to return or preponderate. The commentator emphasizes that mu'awwal here is specifically the result of tarjih from mushtarak, not the broader category of mu'awwal from other ambiguous types (like khafi or mujmal), which belong to the division of bayan (clarification) not the wording itself. Sources of tarjih include solitary reports, analogy, reflection on wording or context, and examples are given from Quran.

Key Terms
المشترك (al-mushtarak): A word that shares multiple meanings or referents with equal probability, but alternatively (badal), not comprehensively (shumul).
التوقف (al-tawaqquf): Suspension of judgment regarding the intended meaning of a common word until after reflection and tarjih.
الترجيح (al-tarjih): Preponderance of one meaning over others based on evidence (dalil zanni) such as context, solitary reports, or analogy.

Definition of Mu'awwal and Its Relationship to Bayan
Defining mu'awwal and distinguishing it from mu'awwal under bayan.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mu'awwal (the interpreted word), distinguish it from mu'awwal under bayan, and identify examples of tarjih from context and wording.

Core Idea
Mu'awwal is the preponderant meaning after tarjih; it can result from any ambiguous text, not just mushtarak.

Having understood the suspension required for mushtarak, we now turn to the outcome of tarjih: al-mu'awwal. This lesson clarifies that while mu'awwal often arises from a common word, it can also emerge from other ambiguous types when clarified by a speculative proof. The commentator stresses that such cases belong to bayan (clarification), not the original wording. We will also explore examples from the Quran where context determines the meaning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic root of mu'awwal
The word mu'awwal is derived from 'aala ya'ulu' meaning to preponderate or return. 'Awwaltuhu' means I investigated and interpreted it.
This root highlights the act of turning towards a preponderant meaning.
2
Define mu'awwal in the context of mushtarak
Mu'awwal is the preponderant meaning obtained from a common word after applying tarjih (preponderance) using predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y).
This is a specific type of mu'awwal that belongs to the divisions of wording (aqsam al-sighah).
3
Distinguish mu'awwal under bayan
Other ambiguous words like khafi, mushkil, or mujmal, when clarified by a speculative proof, also become mu'awwal. However, in those cases, the mu'awwal is considered part of bayan (clarification), not part of the original wording's categories.
This distinction prevents confusion between categories of ambiguous texts.
4
Examine examples of tarjih from context
Analyze the Quranic examples: in 'Uhilla lakum laylata al-siyami...' (2:187), the word 'al-rafath' is understood as sexual intercourse from the word 'hill' (permissibility); in 'Ahalana dara al-muqamah' (35:35), 'ahalla' is understood as settling from 'hulul'. In the case of 'qur'', context (the preceding 'three') indicates menstruation.
These show how context (siyaq) and wording (lafz) provide clues for tarjih.

المؤول (al-mu'awwal): The preponderant meaning derived from a common word through predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y), or from other ambiguous types after clarification by a speculative proof (belonging to bayan).

Al-Mu'awwal: Definition, Classification, and Binding Force
Defines al-Mu'awwal and its binding legal effect.

Learning Objective

Defining al-Mujmal in Hanafi Usul
Definition and conceptual analysis of al-mujmal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mujmal and distinguish it from al-mufassar, al-mu'awwal, and al-mushkil by identifying the role of speaker clarification.

Core Idea
Al-mujmal requires the speaker's own clarification; reason alone cannot resolve its ambiguity.

In Hanafi usul, not all ambiguous texts are equal. Some can be resolved through reflection, but al-mujmal demands an external explanation from the original speaker. This lesson unpacks the precise definition in the passage, its etymology, and how it fits into the hierarchy of textual clarity.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of al-mujmal
The passage states: 'Al-mujmal is that wherein meanings crowd so the intended meaning is confused and cannot be grasped except by clarification from the speaker.' Break down this definition into its components: 'meanings crowd' and 'clarification from the speaker'.
Note the contrast with al-mufassar: the opposite extreme of clarity.
2
Analyze the restriction 'min jiha al-mutakallim'
The phrase 'from the side of the speaker' is a restrictive clause that excludes other ambiguous categories like al-khafi (hidden), al-mushtarak (common word when preference exists), and al-mushkil (problematic) because those may be resolved by reflection or seeking.
This restriction is key to the Hanafi definition.
3
Distinguish the three possible outcomes of clarifying a mujmal text
According to the passage, if the clarification is decisive and definitive (like explaining salah and zakat), the mujmal becomes mufassar. If the clarification is presumptive (like the extent of wiping with the hadith of al-Mughira), it becomes mu'awwal. If insufficient, it becomes mushkil.
This shows the dynamic nature of the category.

Commentary
This passage defines al-mujmal (the abridged/ambiguous text) in Hanafi usul. It is the opposite of al-mufassar (the clearly explained). The defining characteristic is that meanings crowd together so intensely that the intended meaning cannot be determined by reason alone; it requires clarification from the original speaker (al-mutakallim). The author traces the etymology: 'ajmala' means to consider important or to sum up. The simile is a foreigner lost in a crowd—he cannot be identified without asking people. The definition is analyzed to distinguish mujmal from other unclear categories: mushtarak (common word), khafi, and mushkil. For these, reflection and seeking (nazar wa talab) may suffice; they do not always require a speaker's explanation. The passage then discusses three types of mujmal: (1) the common word (mushtarak) when no preference exist, (2) a strange rare word (like 'halu' in Quran 70:19-20), and (3) a word transferred from its apparent meaning to a technical meaning (like salah, zakat, riba). The example of riba (usury/interest) is elaborated: the word 'riba' is a generic noun with the definite article, implying all types. The Prophet (PBUH) only specified six types (gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, salt) without limiting it, so consensus held that riba is not restricted to those six. This makes other types ambiguous (mushkil) because the analogy (qiyas) on the effective cause (illa) provides a presumptive clarification. The passage also critiques the author's definition, noting that 'crowding of meanings' literally applies only to the first type, but can be extended metaphorically. The correct definition according to the sharh is: that whose intended meaning is confused in a way that can only be clarified by inquiry from the speaker.

Opposite of the clarified (mufassar) is the ambiguous (mujmal): it is that in which meanings crowd together, so the intended meaning becomes unclear in a way that is not grasped except by a clarification from the speaker, like the verse on usury. The intended meaning of 'mujmal' is derived from 'ajmala al-amra' meaning he neglected it. It became opposite of mufassar because just as mufassar reached the utmost in clarity such that it cannot bear anything but abrogation, similarly mujmal reached the utmost in obscurity such that the intended meaning is not grasped by reason but only from the speaker. It is like a stranger who hides in a crowd of people; he cannot be identified without inquiry. 'Izdahamat' means they crowded, each meaning pushing aside the other. 'Al-ma'ani': the intended meaning here is the concept of the word, not that which opposes essence; the plural is used to include more than one, so that the definition includes the common term (mushtarak) when the door of preference is closed. This genus includes the common, the hidden, and the problematic. 'Fashtabaha': due to crowding, an added clarification of the reason for the unclarity. The phrase 'jihat al-mujmal' (with kasra on jim) excludes the hidden, the common, and the problematic, because their intended meaning can be grasped by consideration and investigation without needing the speaker's clarification. Then if the clarification is decisive and definitive like the clarification of prayer and zakat, the mujmal becomes mufassar; if it is conjectural like the clarification of the measure of wipes by the hadith of Mughira, it becomes mu'awwal; if the clarification is not decisive, it becomes mushkil, exiting from the category of mujmal to mushkil, and its ruling becomes that of mushkil — obligation of seeking and reflection — like the clarification of usury by the hadith on the six items. Since usury is a genus name defined by alif lam to encompass all its types, and the Prophet ﷺ did not clarify except the six items without limiting it to them, consensus held that usury is not confined to them, so the ruling beyond them remains unknown. Hence Umar رضي الله عنه said: 'The Prophet ﷺ left and did not explain the chapters of usury to us.' However, it is possible to arrive at it by considering what he clarified, as the mujtahids arrived at qiyas on the six items by identifying the common effective cause, so usury in the six items became mu'awwal and in others mushkil. Know that mujmal is of three types: first, what becomes ambiguous due to the crowding of equal meanings, like the common term when the door of preference is closed. Second, what becomes ambiguous due to the strangeness of the word without having commonality, like 'haloo' in {Indeed, mankind was created anxious} which Allah clarified by what follows. Third, what becomes ambiguous due to the speaker shifting from its apparent meaning to something unknown, like prayer, zakat, and usury. Now, the author's definition 'what crowds with meanings' only includes the first type, as if crowding only occurs there. Commentators struggled to explain it: some said the intended meaning of 'crowding of meanings' is general, whether due to wording as in the common term, or due to strangeness of the word or the speaker's implication, because in the other two types there is also a kind of estimated crowding: when the listener does not know the intended meaning, his mind moves from one meaning to another, so crowding occurs. The better view is that this phrase is superfluous in the definition, so the correct definition is: 'what is unclear in its intended meaning in a way that cannot be grasped except by inquiry' — reflect and be grateful. Like the verse on usury, you have known its ambiguity, so remember. Its ruling is suspension (tawaqquf) concerning it, believing that the intended meaning is true, until clarification comes.

Commentary
This passage defines 'mujmal' (ambiguous) as the opposite of 'mufassar' (clearly explained). The author explains that mujmal is a text whose meaning is unclear due to crowding of possible meanings, so it requires external clarification from the speaker. He distinguishes it from other obscure texts like khafi (hidden), mushkil (problematic), and mushtarak (common term) by noting that in those, the intended meaning can be reached through reasoning and investigation, whereas mujmal absolutely requires the speaker's clarification. The passage then elaborates on the types of clarification a mujmal may receive: if the clarification is definitive (qat'i), the text becomes mufassar; if conjectural (zanni), it becomes mu'awwal (interpreted); if insufficient, it becomes mushkil. The example of usury (riba) illustrates how a general term (riba) was clarified by the Prophet ﷺ only partially (six items), leaving the rest ambiguous. The three types of mujmal are: (1) due to homonymy (mushtarak) when no preference, (2) due to word strangeness (like 'haloo'), and (3) due to shift from literal to technical meaning (like salah, zakat). The author critiques the definition 'crowding of meanings' for excluding types 2 and 3, suggesting instead the definition 'what is unclear except by inquiry.' The ruling of mujmal is suspension (tawaqquf) — one must withhold action until clarification comes, while believing the intended meaning is true.

Classification of Kinayat for Divorce as Ba'in with Exceptions
Explaining why kinayat are ba'in and the exceptions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify kinayat for divorce, identify which yield ba'in versus raj'i divorce in the Hanafi school, and explain the linguistic reasoning for the exception of 'i'tiddi'.

Core Idea
Kinayat for divorce are metaphorically ba'in, except three words that imply divorce itself, yielding raj'i divorce.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text tackles a key puzzle: words like 'ba'in' and 'haram' have clear literal meanings, yet they are called 'kinayat' (allusive). The author resolves this by explaining that the ambiguity lies not in the word's meaning but in what it applies to. This lesson will guide you through the classification, the general rule, and the critical exceptions that change the type of divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic classification
Recognize that words like 'ba'in' and 'haram' are metaphorically called kinayat because their literal meanings are clear but ambiguous in application (e.g., from what is one ba'in?).
2
State the general Hanafi ruling
With intention, these metaphorical kinayat yield ba'in (irrevocable) divorce, as the author states 'we made them ba'in'.
3
Recognize the exceptions
Three words are excepted: 'i'tiddi', 'istibri' rahimaki', and 'anti wahida'. These yield raj'i (revocable) divorce.
4
Analyze the reasoning for 'i'tiddi'
Its literal meaning (counting) has no effect on marriage, so it cannot directly signify divorce. It must be interpreted as a command to observe the waiting period, implying a prior divorce.
This is a classic example of talil (reasoning) in Hanafi usul.
5
Understand implicit divorce
The command to observe the waiting period presupposes a divorce, and the necessity is satisfied by establishing one raj'i divorce without additional elements like baynuna.

Third section on the ways of using composition. Making kinayat as ba'in. Summary: The words 'ba'in', 'haram' and similar are called kinayat of divorce metaphorically; because their meanings are known, but there is ambiguity in what they relate to and affect, so they resemble kinayat, and are so named metaphorically. Due to this ambiguity, intention is needed; when the ambiguity is removed by intention, action according to their implications becomes obligatory without making them an expression of the explicit (sarih). Therefore we made them ba'in except in a man's saying 'I'tiddi' (observe waiting period), because its literal meaning is counting, and that has no effect on marriage. And 'i'tidad' (observing waiting period) can possibly mean what is counted other than menstrual periods. Like his saying: 'He does not put his foot in so-and-so's house' — it became widespread among people that the intended meaning is entering, so it became explicit. The author indicated that kinaya exists both in literal and metaphorical senses. 'And they are'... 'He called ba'in etc.' is an answer to an implicit question: you said that kinaya is that whose intended meaning is hidden, but words of ba'in divorce like 'you are ba'in', 'haram', 'batta', 'batla' etc. have known intended meanings and senses, for everyone knows that 'ba'in' comes from 'baynuna' which is separation, and similarly 'haram' from 'hurma' which is prohibition; these words are used in their meanings explicitly, so how do you call them kinaya? He answered that calling them kinaya is only by way of metaphor. 'Known meanings': because linguists know that 'ba'in' from 'baynuna', separation as you know. 'In what relates to it, etc.' means the ambiguity is only in the locus to which the word relates and affects; for it is not known from what something is ba'in — from wealth, beauty, husband, or tribe? By metaphor, therefore ba'in divorce occurs with them when there is intention. If they were literal kinayat, they would be like his saying 'you are divorced', and raj'i divorce would occur. Commentary
This passage discusses the classification of kinayat (allusive words) used for divorce in Hanafi fiqh. It begins by explaining that words like 'ba'in' (separated) and 'haram' (prohibited) are called kinayat metaphorically. Their literal meanings are known, but there is ambiguity regarding what they relate to—e.g., 'ba'in' could refer to separation from wealth, beauty, or the marriage bond. Therefore, intention is required to specify the meaning. Once intention is present, the ruling of divorce applies. These words yield a ba'in divorce (irrevocable separation) except for three specific expressions: 'i'tiddi' (observe the waiting period), 'istibri' rahimaki' (ascertain the purity of your womb), and 'anti wahida' (you are one). The last two are treated similarly due to implicit divorce. The passage then provides detailed reasoning for the exception of 'i'tiddi'. Its literal meaning is counting, which has no effect on marriage. However, if the husband intends by it the waiting period of divorce, it is interpreted metaphorically as a command to observe the waiting period after divorce. For a woman who has been consummated, the command implies that divorce has occurred prior, and only one raj'i (revocable) divorce is established. For a non-consummated woman, the word is interpreted as a metaphor for divorce itself, also yielding one raj'i divorce. The passage also defends the permissibility of using metaphor where the effect is specific to the cause. Finally, it notes that 'istibri' rahimaki' can mean seeking purity for a child or for another husband, and when divorce is intended, it results in raj'i divorce.

Time as a Measure (Mi'yar) in Fasting: Usul Analysis
Defining mi'yar and its fiqh implications for intention in fasting

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define mi'yar and zarf, apply the Hanafi principle that fasting time is a measure, and derive the rule that absolute intention suffices for Ramadan.

Core Idea
Fasting time is a measure, not a container — only Ramadan fasts are valid during Ramadan.

This passage from the usul text introduces the second type of time-bound worship: time as a measure (mi'yar). Unlike prayers whose time is a container (zarf) that can hold multiple acts, the time of Ramadan is coextensive with the fast itself. This distinction has practical consequences for intention: absolute intention suffices for Ramadan, but specification is needed for prayers.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of time-bound worship
Understand that time can be a container (zarf) for acts like prayers or a measure (mi'yar) for acts like fasting. The author distinguishes these in the text.
Prayer time is a container because it can accommodate multiple prayers (obligatory, supererogatory, etc.). Fasting time is a measure because it exactly coincides with the fast and cannot accommodate other fasts.
2
Analyze the ruling of 'nothing else remains prescribed'
The text states that during Ramadan, no other fast (obligatory, supererogatory, etc.) is prescribed. This is because the month is a measure for Ramadan fast only. The hadith 'When Sha'ban passes, there is no fasting except for Ramadan' supports this.
This implies that if one intends to fast for qada' or nafl during Ramadan, it will not count for those but for Ramadan instead, except for the traveler case.
3
Understand intention rules: absolute and mistaken intention
For a healthy resident, absolute intention (e.g., 'I intend to fast') or mistaken intention (e.g., intending qada') both result in the fast counting for Ramadan. This is derived from the exclusivity of Ramadan.
This contrasts with prayers where absolute intention would not suffice because the time is a container for multiple acts.
4
Examine the exception for the traveler
The text presents an intra-Hanafi dispute: According to Abu Hanifa, if a traveler intends another obligation (qada' or kaffarah) during Ramadan, it falls for that obligation. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, it still falls for Ramadan. The difference hinges on whether the traveler's witnessing of the month obligates Ramadan fast despite the concession.
The text does not prefer a view, but the dispute is a key example of ikhtilaf.
5
Contrast with the requirement of ta'yin in prayers
The end of the passage notes that for prayers (first type), specification of intention (ta'yin) is required because the time is a container suitable for multiple prayers. This highlights the practical difference between the two types.
Thus, for Zuhr prayer, one must specify 'Zuhr' unlike fasting where absolute intention works.

Bayan, Mujmal, Mushtarak, and Ta'khir al-Bayan in Hanafi Usul
Defining bayan and ruling on delaying explanation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the categories of ambiguous texts (mujmal and mushtarak) and evaluate the Hanafi position on delaying explanation (ta'khir al-bayan) with its proofs.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, delaying explanation is permissible until the time of need, as shown by the gradual revelation of prayer and zakah.

This passage introduces the concept of bayan (explanation) in Hanafi legal theory, focusing on how ambiguous (mujmal) and homonymous (mushtarak) texts are clarified. It then tackles a key dispute: can explanation be delayed until the time of action? The Hanafi school, supported by evidence from the Quran and the benefit of gradual belief, affirms this permissibility, while some other schools oppose it. Understanding this rule is crucial for grasping how the Shari'ah was progressively revealed and applied.

Guided Path
1
Define bayan and its scope
Read the opening line: bayan is the explanation of mujmal and mushtarak. Note that it also extends to mushkil (problematic) and khafi (obscure) texts.
The definition establishes that bayan is not for clear texts; it resolves ambiguity.
2
Identify the two Quranic examples
The first example is the command to pray and give zakah (2:43) – initially mujmal, later explained by the Prophet. The second is the word 'quru'' (2:228) – mushtarak between purity and menstruation, clarified by a hadith. Understand how each exemplifies the category.
The hadith about slave-girl's iddah shows the Prophet's explanation resolved the homonym.
3
State the core ruling on ta'khir al-bayan
The passage states: delaying explanation beyond the time of need is not permissible, but delaying until the time of need is permissible according to the majority (Hanafis). Identify the opposing groups: some Hanbalis, al-Sayrafi, Mu'tazilites.
The 'time of need' is when the obligation becomes incumbent (e.g., when prayer becomes due).
4
Examine the Hanafi proofs
Two proofs are given: (1) The verses of prayer and zakah were explained gradually, showing delay was allowed. (2) Delay has a great benefit: it permits summary belief first, then detailed belief, then action at the proper time.
The benefit argument is unique to Hanafis; it emphasizes the pedagogical value of gradual elaboration.
5

Opponents argue that delay harms the action because one cannot act on the unknown. Hanafi response: there is no obligation before explanation, so no harm in non-action. The benefit of gradual belief outweighs the objection.
This refutation preserves the coherence of Shari'ah: obligations only attach after clarification.

Bayan al-darurah is the fourth type of bayan. Recognize that it operates through non-explicit means (e.g., silence, context) and treats the implied ruling as if explicitly stated.
The text says 'bi ghayri ma wudi'a lahu' – other than what it is established for.

Focus on Q. 4:11. The verse declares both parents inherit but only specifies the mother's share (1/3). By necessity, the father must get the remainder; otherwise property would be left without an owner. This forced inference is treated as if it were uttered.

If the Prophet (peace be upon him) witnesses an action and stays silent without rebuke, his silence indicates approval. This silence becomes a source of permissibility.

Commentary
This passage introduces the fourth type of bayan: bayan al-darurah (necessity-based explanation). This is explanation that occurs through means other than explicit speech, such as silence or contextual indication. The text enumerates four sub-types derived by induction, but only two are detailed here. The first sub-type is explanation that is 'in the status of what is uttered,' exemplified by Q. 4:11 on inheritance. The verse states that both parents inherit but only specifies the mother's share as one-third. By necessity, the father gets the remainder; otherwise the property would go unallocated. This silence explains the father's share implicitly, and it is treated as if it were stated. The second sub-type is explanation through the state of the speaker (i.e., the Lawgiver). If the Prophet (صلى الله عليه وسلم) witnesses an action and does not rebuke it, his silence indicates approval and establishes the ruling of permissibility. The text also digresses into a discussion of the exception in Abraham's statement, arguing whether the exceptive phrase is connected or disconnected—showing that necessity-based explanation can also involve interpretation. The passage is cut off, but the main point is clear: when explicit textual explanation is absent, necessity or context can supply the ruling, and such implied explanations have the same legal force as explicit statements.

Key Terms
بيان الضرورة (bayan al-darurah): A type of explanation that is derived through non-explicit means like silence or contextual necessity, treated as if explicitly stated.
حكم المنطوق به (hukm al-mantuq bihi): The ruling of the uttered—i.e., an implied explanation is given the same legal effect as if it were spoken.
السكوت (al-sukut): Silence of the Lawgiver (Prophet) regarding an event; considered a form of approval and thus a source of ruling in necessity-based bayan.

Four Types of Bayan al-Darurah (Necessary Clarification)
Defines and exemplifies four types of bayan al-darurah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and classify the four types of Bayan al-Darurah according to the Hanafi school, and apply the principle that silence can constitute authoritative clarification in specific contexts.

Core Idea
Silence, necessity, and context clarify the law as if spoken—Bayan al-Darurah bridges gaps through implication.

In the Hanafi usul tradition, clarification (bayan) is not limited to explicit utterance. The Lawgiver's silence and other contextual indicators can serve as authoritative clarification. This lesson examines the four types of Bayan al-Darurah (necessary clarification), where the ruling is made clear by implication rather than direct speech. You will see how Quranic verses, Prophetic practice, and scholarly examples demonstrate this principle, including a key dispute with the Shafi'i school.

Guided Path
1
Define Bayan al-Darurah
State the definition: a type of bayan that occurs by other than what it is placed for (i.e., silence) but is treated as equivalent. Understand that it is a valid method of clarification in Hanafi usul.
The term 'darurah' indicates that this type of bayan is used when clarity is needed but explicit text is absent.
2
Identify the first type: In the ruling of the uttered (fi hukm al-mantuq bihi)
Analyze the Quranic example on inheritance (An-Nisa 11): Allah specifies the mother's third but remains silent about the father's share. By necessity, the father gets the remainder, making the silence equivalent to an explicit statement.
This shows that silence in a context requiring full clarification becomes binding.
3
Identify the second type: By the state of the speaker (hal al-mutakallim)
Consider the Prophet's silence upon witnessing customary practices like sales. His silence, given his role as lawgiver, indicates approval. This is known as taqrir (tacit approval).
The example of Walad al-Maghrur and the Companions' silence also falls under this type.
4
Identify the third type: Necessity to repel harm (daf' al-ghurur)
Examine cases where silence is taken as permission to avoid harm: the silence of the preemptor (shafi') after learning of a sale, and the silence of a master when his slave trades. Both are considered waivers or permissions because the opposite would cause detriment to others.
This type includes the Hanafi-Shafi'i dispute: Shafi'i denies that silence alone proves permission.
5
Identify the fourth type: Abundance of speech (kathrat al-kalam)
Understand that within a single utterance, a conjunction can indicate that two items are part of a single unit. Example: 'I owe him one hundred and a dirham' means one hundred and one dirham, not two separate debts.
This type clarifies through linguistic structure rather than silence.

Chapter of Bayan. 163. Sections of Bayan. Allah said: 'Indeed they are an enemy to me, except the Lord of the worlds' meaning 'but the Lord of the worlds'. As for Bayan al-Darurah (clarification of necessity), it is a type of bayan that occurs by other than what it is placed for, and this is of four aspects by induction: Among it is what is in the ruling of the uttered, like His saying 'And his parents inherit him, so for his mother is one third' [؟] the speech necessitated partnership, then specifying the mother with one third indicated that the father deserves the rest, so it became a bayan for the beginning of the speech not by mere silence. And among it is what is established by the indication of the state of the speaker, like the silence of the Lawgiver upon an event he witnesses about change indicates correctness. Allah said, narrating the saying of Ibrahim to his people: 'Indeed they (the idols you worship) except the Lord of the worlds': And since Allah was not included among them, the excluded becomes an independent statement, so its estimation is made clear by his saying 'i.e. etc.' The Lord of the worlds is not an enemy to me, so 'illa' means 'but'. And it is possible that it is connected, because the people used to worship Him and idols, so he said: 'Indeed they, i.e. all that you worship, are an enemy to me except the Lord of the worlds.' And when he finished from the third type of bayan, which is bayan al-taghayyur (change), he began the fourth section, which is bayan al-darurah, and said: 'As for etc.' 'By other than what it is placed for' i.e. for bayan, which is silence, for it is not placed for bayan, rather what is placed for it is speech. 'In the ruling of the uttered' i.e. a bayan obtained without speech but it is in the ruling of that obtained by speech, this is the first aspect. 'The beginning of the speech' i.e. His saying 'And his parents inherit him' (An-Nisa: 11) necessitated partnership between the parents absolutely, where He ascribed inheritance to both without stating the share of each. Then specifying the mother with one third by His saying 'so for his mother is one third', for it clarified the mother's share and remained silent about clarifying the father's share. 'He deserves the rest' because there is no other destination, so if the rest were not for him, some property would remain without destination, and the father's share would remain unknown, which contradicts the flow of speech, for it was brought to clarify the shares of the parents. So it became: specifying the mother with one third is for the beginning of the speech etc., as if it were said: 'so for his mother is one third and for his father the remainder', so the entitlement itself was obtained by the beginning of the speech, and by silence was obtained the clarification of the amount of the father's share, and this bayan is in the ruling of the uttered. And among it i.e. from bayan al-darurah and it is the second aspect: 'the state of the speaker' who is accustomed to speaking about the incident, so his silence upon that indicates he is pleased with it. 'Silence of the Lawgiver' i.e. the silence of the Prophet, peace be upon him, upon changing an event he witnesses of saying and deed in his time, until he did not speak and did not change, and the doer persisted in his action, indicates that matter is true. And that is like what the Prophet observed of sales and transactions that people dealt with among themselves from food, drink, and clothing that they persevered upon, so he remained silent about them and approved them, and did not reject them despite knowledge and ability, so his silence was a bayan that those matters are included in the known (ma'ruf) and excluded from the objectionable (munkar); for it is not the Prophet's way to leave people upon an objectionable matter, and Allah said about him: 'He commands them to the good and forbids them from the evil' (Al-A'raf: 157), and similarly silence. 'Walad al-maghrur': The maghrur is a man who has intercourse with a woman relying on ownership of the right hand (concubinage), then she gives birth, then it becomes manifest that she belongs to someone else, or upon marriage such that that woman said 'I am free', then she gives birth and it is established that she is a slave belonging to another, and he is called maghrur because that woman deceived him. So the child of the maghrur is free, and he is obliged to pay its value to the owner of the female slave. As for the benefits of his body that the father obtained, they are not valued, i.e. he is not obliged to pay their value to the father. That is because a woman absconded and married a man from Banu 'Udhrah, then she gave birth to children, then her master came and raised that case before 'Umar, may Allah be pleased with him, so he ruled in favor of her master and ruled against the father that he should ransom her children and take them by value, and he remained silent about guaranteeing her benefits and the benefits of her child, and that was in the presence of the Companions, so their silence about that indicates clarification that they were pleased with that. Razin narrated this story from Malik, and Malik mentioned some of these things in his Muwatta'. And among it: i.e. from bayan al-darurah, and this is a third aspect: 'necessity to repel harm' (daf' al-ghurur), and deception is haram. 'Silence of the preemptor (shafi') about demanding preemption after knowledge of the sale', for if his silence is not made a waiver of preemption, the buyer would be harmed because he is restrained from his dispositions in the sold item for fear of the preemptor, and likewise the seller would be harmed because perhaps no one would buy from him for fear of the preemptor, so he would be harmed. And 'silence of the master (mawla) etc.': for his silence at that time is a bayan because he gave him permission for trade to repel harm from the people. Al-Shafi'i, may Allah have mercy on him, said: 'His silence is not permission because it is possible that he remained silent out of anger and lack of concern for his action due to knowing that he is under interdiction, and the probable is not proof.' We said: 'His silence, although it is probable, but the prevailing custom is to favor the side of satisfaction due to the usual practice among people.' And among it i.e. from bayan al-darurah, and it is the fourth aspect: 'Abundance of speech' i.e. frequent usage or length of expression indicates the intended meaning. 'He owes me one hundred and a dirham' or 'one hundred and a qafiz of wheat'. Indeed the conjunction (al-'ataf) i.e. conjunction of dirham and qafiz of wheat.

This passage is a detailed commentary on the four types of Bayan al-Darurah (clarification of necessity). The author begins by quoting a Quranic verse about Ibrahim's statement to illustrate a type of bayan. Then enumerates four aspects of Bayan al-Darurah based on induction (istiqra').

First type: Bayan that is in the ruling of the uttered (fi hukm al-mantuq bihi). Example: Quran verse on inheritance (An-Nisa 11) where Allah mentions that parents inherit and then specifies the mother's share as one-third, remaining silent about the father's share. By necessity, the father gets the remainder, so the silence clarifies the father's share, which is treated as if it were explicitly uttered.

Second type: Established by the state of the speaker (hal al-mutakallim). This refers to the silence of the Prophet (peace be upon him) when witnessing practices of the people that he did not forbid. His silence indicates approval and that the practice is lawful. Example given: various transactions and customs that the Prophet saw and did not change. Interpreting 'A Hundred and a Garment' in Iqrar: The Role of Custom in Hanafi Usul
Ruling on interpreting 'miah wa thawb' in iqrar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of customary deletion to interpret ambiguous expressions in iqrar and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
When custom deletes the specifier, the hundred is interpreted as the conjoined type; otherwise, the muqir's explanation governs.

This passage examines a classic usul dispute: if someone confesses 'I owe you a hundred and a garment,' how is the hundred interpreted? The Hanafi school argues that due to widespread custom, the hundred is understood as the same type as the coordinated item (e.g., dirhams) when that item is frequently transacted, because people often delete the specifier for brevity. Al-Shafi'i objects that conjunction separates items. We'll break down the Hanafi proof, the role of custom, and the practical consequence in fatwa.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue
What exactly is being disputed in this passage? The issue is the interpretation of the phrase 'a hundred and a garment' (مائة وثوب) in a legal confession (iqrar). Does the hundred refer to a hundred of the same type as the garment, or is it a separate unspecified item?
The dispute is about whether the conjunction 'waw' can serve as a means of interpretation via custom.
2
State the Hanafi position and its basis
The Hanafi school holds that due to customary usage (urf), the hundred is interpreted as the same type as the conjoined item, provided that item is of a kind that is generally established in the dhimmah (e.g., dirhams, foodstuffs). The basis is that in transactions, people often say 'a hundred and ten dirhams' without repeating the unit, and it is understood that the hundred refers to dirhams.
The author argues that deletion of the specifier occurs out of necessity for brevity in frequent usage.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
The Hanafi proof relies on two pillars: (1) the observation of customary practice in buying and selling (e.g., 'I sold this for a hundred and ten dirhams'), and (2) qiyas (analogy) from sale to iqrar. The same logic applies: if the hundred is mentioned with a conjoined item in a sale, the hundred is understood as that item; similarly in iqrar.
The proof is based on urf and qiyas, not a direct text from Quran or hadith.
4
Examine the Shafi'i objection
Al-Shafi'i argues that the conjunction 'waw' indicates separation, so the hundred and the garment are independent. The hundred remains unspecified. He rejects the idea that custom can override the linguistic rule of conjunction.
For Shafi'i, the muqir's interpretation is not automatically accepted; the hundred must be clarified through other means.
5
Assess the Hanafi response and thamarah al-khilaf
The Hanafi response is that conjunction is not placed for interpretation linguistically; if it cannot serve as interpretation, the hundred remains ambiguous (mujmal). Therefore, the muqir's own explanation is accepted (القول قوله في بيانها). However, this acceptance is contingent: when the conjoined item is of a type that is frequently transacted (like dirhams), the custom removes the ambiguity. For garments, which are not generally established in dhimmah, the custom does not apply, so the hundred is ambiguous and the muqir's word is taken.
The practical difference (thamarah) is that in Hanafi fiqh, if the second item is dirhams, the hundred is immediately interpreted as dirhams; if it is a garment, the muqir can explain the hundred as he wishes. In Shafi'i, the hundred is always separate and not automatically clarified.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, precise terminology is crucial. We often encounter terms like 'condition' (shart) and 'cause' (sabab), but there is a distinct category: the sign ('alāmah). Matters that slavery contradicts. And this slavery negates ownership of property because [the slave] is himself property as wealth, so that the slave and the mukatab do not own concubines, and the Hajj of Islam is not valid from them because of the lack of original capacity, which is physical benefits; because they (the slave and mukatab) belong to the master, except in what is excluded regarding bodily devotions, i.e., on the master some of it [is exempt]. The ownership in the case where one manumits part of a slave: part of the cause has occurred, and the effect (freedom) is not realized unless the entire cause is realized. So if part of the cause occurs, it is pending completion. That is, the removal of ownership which is divisible for the establishment of freedom which is indivisible. Like washing limbs: washing limbs is divisible, so if one washes his hands or face, the ritual impurity is removed from them and purity is established, but permission for prayer is not established, which is indivisible, without washing all limbs. For prohibition, i.e., the severe prohibition; the numbers of divorce are divisible, and the severe prohibition, which is indivisible, is attached to them. So if one divorces his wife one or two times, the divorce is established but the severe prohibition is not established without completing the number. And when he finished explaining the difference among the scholars, he started on the rulings of slavery. He said: And this slavery (which we are discussing) and in it there is a caution regarding marriage, for although it is called 'riqq' (slavery), it does not negate ownership of property. Because [the slave] is property as wealth: the slave does not own property because he is property for his master and owned in terms of wealth, not in terms of humanity. The attribute of being owned is contradictory to the attribute of being an owner, so they cannot coexist in one person from one angle. There is discussion in this; because they can coexist from two angles: being owned in terms of wealth, and being an owner in terms of humanity. So understand. Thus the slave does not own [property] etc., even if the master permits him, just as they (slave and mukatab) do not own manumission because it is from the rulings of ownership. 'Tasarrī' means taking a female slave that one has set apart and prepared for intercourse; it is from 'sirr'. The author specifically mentioned the mukatab even though the mudabbar also does not own tasarrī, because the mukatab, since he is more entitled to his earnings and his freedom is close, might suggest that tasarrī is permissible for him. So he removed this illusion by mentioning him. 'Hajjat al-Islam' means the pilgrimage that is obligatory due to Islam. Even if they perform Hajj it is nafl (supererogatory) even if with the permission of the master. Because of lack of original capacity: capacity and ability are conditions for the obligation of Hajj, and the slave has no ability at all because ability consists of physical benefits, and all physical benefits belong to his master, as the author said regarding bodily devotions such as prayer and fasting. Commentary
This passage examines the legal effects of slavery (riqq) on a slave's capacity for ownership and religious obligations. The author states that slavery negates (yunāfī) the ability to own property because the slave himself is considered property (māl) from a financial perspective. Consequently, a slave or a mukātab (a slave contracted for manumission) cannot own concubines (tasarrī) nor perform a valid obligatory Hajj. The reason given for the invalidity of Hajj is the lack of 'aṣl al-qudra' (original capacity), which is constituted by physical benefits (manāfi' badaniyyah). Since all physical benefits of the slave belong to the master, the slave lacks the necessary capacity for Hajj. The passage also includes a discussion on the divisibility of ownership and manumission, linking back to the previous lesson. The author clarifies that marriage is an exception: although marriage is sometimes metaphorically called 'riqq', it does not negate ownership of property. The text later explores the possibility of the slave having ownership in terms of humanity versus being owned in terms of wealth, noting that these attributes can coexist from different angles. The passage ends abruptly, indicating continuation on the next page regarding the ability to earn (sa'yah) and concubinage. This analysis is based on the foundational texts (Zahir al-Riwayah) and reflects the Hanafi view that a slave lacks full legal capacity for property ownership and obligatory Hajj.

Key Terms
الرق (al-riqq): Slavery; a legal status that makes a person property of another, negating ownership and full legal capacity.
المكاتب (al-mukātab): A slave who has entered a contract of kitabah to earn his freedom; he is still a slave but has some rights over his earnings.
حجة الإسلام (hajjat al-islām): The obligatory Hajj; pilgrimage that is a duty upon every free Muslim with capacity.
