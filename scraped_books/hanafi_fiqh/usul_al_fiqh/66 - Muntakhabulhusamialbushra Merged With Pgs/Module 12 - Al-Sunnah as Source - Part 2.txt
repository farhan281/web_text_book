Legal Capacity for Minors: Benefit and Harm
Explanation of legal capacity for minors with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule of ahliyyah to determine which transactions are valid for minors based on benefit and harm, and cite the hadith evidence for inheritance.

Core Idea
Minors lack capacity for harmful acts; inheritance and loans by a judge are exceptions due to benefit and security.

This passage explores the concept of legal capacity (ahliyyah) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, focusing on minors. The author explains that while adults have full authority to handle both beneficial and harmful transactions, minors are protected from harm. However, certain beneficial acts like inheritance are valid even for minors, and loans by a judge are permitted to safeguard property. The text also addresses a subtle ikhtilaf among later scholars regarding the validity of divorce for a minor in cases of necessity. Understanding this ruling is crucial for correctly applying contracts and inheritances involving minors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concept of ahliyyah
Define legal capacity as the ability to acquire rights (ahliyyat al-wujub) and perform acts (ahliyyat al-ada'). Note that minors have partial capacity.
2
Analyze why inheritance is valid for minors
The text states inheritance was legislated as a benefit for the deceased, citing the hadith that leaving heirs wealthy is better. Since it is purely beneficial, it applies even to minors.
Refer to the dalil block for the exact hadith.
3
Contrast inheritance with bequest
Bequest is less preferred than inheritance because it transfers wealth to non-heirs; the hadith shows inheritance is better.
4
Examine harmful transactions for minors
Divorce, emancipation, gift, and loan (except by judge) are invalid for minors because they involve harm; guardians cannot perform them either.
The exception: loans by a judge are valid due to security against loss.
5
Explore the nuance on divorce in necessity
Shams al-A'imma argues that the right to divorce is inherent in marriage ownership, so if necessity arises, it can be valid for a minor, contra some earlier scholars.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on legal capacity (ahliyyah) for minors. The author explains that inheritance is legislated for the benefit of the deceased, even for a minor, as it transfers wealth to heirs, which is better than bequest due to the hadith encouraging leaving heirs wealthy. Bequest is less preferred because inheritance has more reward. For adults, actions like divorce, emancipation, gift, and loan are valid because they have full authority to handle both benefits and harms. However, for a minor, harmful transactions are not valid, neither by themselves nor by their guardian, except for a loan by a judge. This is because a loan secures the minor's property by creating a debt in the borrower's liability, preventing loss if deposited. The passage also notes a nuance: divorce and emancipation are not valid for minors without need, but in cases of necessity, they may be valid. Shams al-A'imma refutes a view that a minor's wife is absolutely not subject to divorce, arguing that the right to divorce is inherent in marriage ownership, though harm is only in execution; thus, if needed to repel harm, it is valid.

Key Terms
الأهلية (al-ahliyyah): Legal capacity to acquire rights and obligations (ahliyyat al-wujub) and to perform acts (ahliyyat al-ada').
الإرث (al-irth): Inheritance: transfer of property from deceased to heirs as legislated by Shari'ah.
الإيصاء (al-igsa'): Bequest: voluntary transfer of property after death.

Slave's Share of Spoils: Radhkh vs. Sahm
Explaining how slavery affects obligation to fight and share of spoils

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a slave receives only a token (radhkh) from spoils and derive this ruling from the hadith using the principle of karama.

Core Idea
A slave receives only radhkh, not a full sahm, because he lacks karama (honor status).

In this lesson, we examine how slavery affects a combatant's entitlement to spoils of war. The Hanafi school distinguishes between a full share (sahm) for free Muslims and a token gift (radhkh) for slaves, based on a hadith from the Prophet and the legal principle of honor. This lesson will guide you through the derivation of this ruling and the evidence supporting it.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Principle
The passage states that the full share is based on karama (honor), which a slave lacks. This is the key principle that determines the ruling.
Karama is a legal attribute that qualifies a person for full entitlement in certain matters.
2
Analyze the Hadith Evidence
The hadith from Tirmidhi shows that the Prophet gave a slave only a token, not a full share. This is the primary dalil for the Hanafi position.
The hadith is zanni al-thubut and zanni al-dalala, but it is relied upon in the absence of stronger evidence.
3
Distinguish Radhkh from Sahm
Radhkh is a discretionary token given by the Imam, while sahm is a fixed entitlement for free fighters. Understand the difference in legal effect.
The Imam may give radhkh from the khums or from the general spoils at his discretion.
4
Note the Minority View
Some scholars argue that the slave also receives a full share. Consider the basis for this opinion and why the Hanafi school rejects it.
The minority view may rely on general qiyas or a different interpretation of the hadith.

The Effect of Menstruation and Postpartum Bleeding on Legal Capacity and Worship
Explains that hayd and nifas do not remove legal capacity but condition prayer and fasting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hadith proofs for the Hanafi ruling on menstruation and explain why explicit text overrides qiyas in this case.

Core Idea
Purity from hayd/nifas is a condition for prayer and fasting; the text overrides qiyas.

This passage explores how menstruation and postpartum bleeding affect a woman's legal capacity. While these conditions do not eliminate capacity, they require purity for prayer and fasting. The Hanafi school relies on explicit hadith evidence to establish that fasting must be made up, but prayer is entirely waived—a ruling that contradicts pure analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general principle
Read the opening sentence: hayd and nifas do not remove ahliyya entirely. Understand that legal capacity for obligation and performance remains intact.
This is a core Hanafi position; capacity is not suspended.
2
Extract the hadith proof for prayer
Locate the hadith of Fatimah bint Qays and note the explicit command: when menstruation begins, leave prayer; when it ends, wash and pray. This establishes purity as a condition for prayer.
The hadith is from Bukhari and Muslim, indicating strong transmission.
3
Extract the hadith proof for fasting
Find the hadith of Aishah: women used to menstruate, then become pure, and the Prophet ordered make-up of fasts but not prayers. This shows purity is a condition for fasting too, and that make-up is required.
This hadith is classified as hasan (good) by Tirmidhi.
4
Compare the rulings for prayer and fasting
Note the distinction: prayer is entirely waived (no make-up) due to hardship of making up many prayers, while fasting is only deferred (make-up required) because it is less burdensome.
This difference is based on the explicit text, not analogy.
5
Understand why qiyas is set aside
Analogy with janabah would suggest that fasting is valid during menstruation, but the hadith overrides this. The commentator explains that qiyas is abandoned because of the explicit text (al-nass muqaddam 'ala al-qiyas).
This illustrates the usul principle that textual evidence takes precedence over reasoning.

As for menstruation and postpartum bleeding, they do not eliminate legal capacity entirely, but purity from them is a condition for the validity of performing fasting and prayer. For prayer: Bukhari and Muslim narrated that Fatimah bint Qays said: 'O Messenger of Allah, I am a woman who has istihadah (prolonged bleeding) and I do not become pure. Should I leave prayer?' He said: 'No, that is only a vein, not menstruation. When your menstruation comes, leave prayer, and when it ends, wash the blood from yourself and pray.' This is consistent with analogy. As for fasting: Tirmidhi narrated from Aishah that she said: 'We used to menstruate at the time of the Messenger of Allah, then we would become pure, and he would command us to make up the fasts but not command us to make up the prayers.' He said: this is a hasan hadith. Similarly, Muslim narrated from her its meaning. From this hadith, two matters are known: one is that women did not fast during menstruation, so it is established that purity from menstruation is a condition for fasting, which is contrary to analogy; for the analogy would be that fasting is performed with menstruation as it is performed with janabah. The second is that there is no make-up for prayer, but there is make-up for fasting. Since purity from menstruation and postpartum bleeding is a condition for performing prayer and fasting, performance is missed with them. And in making up prayer there is hardship because of its multiplicity, so the origin of prayer is dropped altogether after the days of menstruation; and there is no hardship in making up fasting, so its origin is not dropped.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling with proof regarding the effect of hayd (menstruation) and nifas (postpartum bleeding) on a woman's legal capacity. It clarifies that these conditions do not remove her capacity for obligation or performance entirely; they merely require purity from them as a condition for the validity of prayer and fasting. The commentator provides two hadith proofs: one for prayer (the hadith of Fatimah bint Qays about istihadah, which shows that actual menstruation requires leaving prayer) and one for fasting (the hadith of Aishah that the Prophet commanded making up fasts but not prayers). He notes that the ruling for fasting is contrary to qiyas (analogy) because qiyas would suggest that fasting is valid during menstruation just as it is valid during janabah. However, the hadith indicates that purity is a condition. The wisdom behind the difference is that making up many prayers would be burdensome, whereas making up a month of fasting is not; thus, prayer is waived entirely while fasting requires compensatory fasts.

أهلية (ahliyya): Legal capacity; hayd and nifas do not eliminate it either for obligation or performance.
قياس (qiyas): Analogical reasoning; here, the analogy between hayd and janabah is overridden by explicit text.

Debt and Suretyship After Death: Hanafi View
Ruling on suretyship for deceased's debt with ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the Hanafi position on kafala for the deceased and evaluate the hadith evidence used by opponents, identifying the key usul principle of dhimma weakening.

Core Idea
Death weakens the dhimma, making new surety invalid — a key usul distinction.

This passage presents a classic usul dispute: can a third party guarantee (kafala) the debt of a deceased person who left no assets? Abu Hanifa says no, because death weakens the dhimma (legal personality) to the point it cannot bear new obligations. The majority, including the Sahibayn, Shafi'i, Malik, and Ahmad, say yes, citing the famous hadith of Abu Qatada. This lesson walks through the arguments and reveals how Hanafi usul principles guide the interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Hanafi position and its usul basis
State the ruling and the principle: the deceased's dhimma is weakened by death (arid samawi), so no new obligations can attach.
2
Examine the opposing view and its dalil
The majority rely on the hadith of Abu Qatada, where the Prophet accepted the guarantee. Also the principle that death does not discharge debt.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to the hadith
The hadith is ambiguous (mujmal); it could be tabarru, 'idda, or acknowledgment, not necessarily kafala.
4
Compare with the restricted slave analogy
Why is kafala valid for a restricted slave but not for the deceased? Answer: slave's dhimma is complete; his lack of property is external.
5
Conclude the thamarah al-khilaf
Practical difference: for Hanafis, only voluntary payment (tabarru) is valid; for others, a binding guarantee can be made.

Commentary
This passage presents a complete inter-madhhab dispute regarding suretyship (kafala) for the debt of a deceased person. Abu Hanifa holds that such suretyship is invalid if the deceased left no assets or guarantor, because death weakens the dhimma (legal personality) to the point it cannot bear debt alone. The opposing view of Muhammad al-Shaybani, Abu Yusuf, al-Shafi'i, Ahmad, and Malik holds that it is valid, because death does not discharge debt; otherwise, voluntary payment and eschatological accountability would be meaningless. They cite several hadith where the Prophet refused to pray over a deceased with debt until Abu Qatada assumed responsibility. The commentator (probably the author of the hashia) argues that these hadith do not conclusively prove suretyship; they could be interpreted as a voluntary gift, a promise, or an acknowledgment of a prior suretyship. The passage then addresses an analogy: if suretyship is valid for a restricted slave (muhajjar) whose dhimma is weak, why not for the deceased? The answer distinguishes: the slave's dhimma is intrinsically complete; his lack of property is only vis-à-vis the master. In contrast, the deceased's dhimma is weakened by death itself. The ruling is a clear example of how usul principles (like the effect of 'arid samawi on dhimma) yield fiqh differences.

Key Terms
كفالة (kafālah): Suretyship or guarantee; a contract where a person assumes responsibility for a debt of another.
ذمة (dhimmah): Legal personality or capacity to bear obligations; weakens with death.
عبد محجور (ʿabd maḥjūr): A slave under legal interdiction, restricted from transactions, but his dhimma is still complete.

The Invalidity of Kafala for a Deceased Person: An Usul Analysis of Dhimmah and Dalil
Derivation of kafala invalidity from hadith and qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of dhimmah weakness to explain why kafala for a deceased is invalid, and identify the dalil (hadith) and qiyas supporting this ruling.

Core Idea
A deceased's dhimmah is too weak for new debts; kafala is invalid in temporal rulings.

In Hanafi usul, the dhimmah (legal capacity) is the foundation for incurring obligations. Death weakens this capacity, making it unable to bear new debts directly. This lesson examines the derivation of the ruling that kafala (suretyship) for a deceased is invalid in worldly matters. We will analyze the primary dalil from the hadith of Abu Qatadah, where the Prophet ﷺ deferred the funeral prayer until a surety stepped forward, indicating that the deceased's dhimmah could not bear the debt. Then we will explore a qiyas (analogical reasoning) that contrasts the deceased with an interdicted slave, whose complete dhimmah permits valid kafala.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue and target ruling
The passage states that kafala for a deceased person is invalid in temporal (dunyawi) rulings. Pinpoint the exact legal issue: whether a surety can guarantee a debt owed by a deceased person. The Hanafi position is that it is invalid.
2
Extract the primary naqli dalil (hadith)
Locate the hadith of Abu Qatadah in the text. The Prophet ﷺ asked about the deceased's debt and only prayed after Abu Qatadah assumed responsibility. Analyze wajh al-dalalah: the deferral indicates that without a surety, the deceased's dhimmah is insufficient to bear the debt; otherwise, prayer would have been allowed immediately.
This hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but accepted in Hanafi usul for deriving rulings.
3
Distinguish between types of obligations
The passage differentiates between obligations legislated 'by way of connection' (e.g. maintenance, expiations) and those based on need. The former become void at death unless there is a bequest; the latter persist for the deceased's benefit in the Hereafter. This distinction is crucial for understanding the scope of the ruling.
Kafala is not a need-based obligation for the deceased; it is a new debt imposed on the dhimmah.
4
Apply qiyas to clarify the illa (effective cause)
The text raises an objection: why is kafala valid for an interdicted slave but invalid for a deceased, if both have weak dhimmahs? The Hanafi response: the slave's dhimmah is complete in his own right (financial capacity is added only for the master's claim), while the deceased's dhimmah is fundamentally weakened. The illa is the presence of a complete dhimmah capable of bearing new obligations.
This qiyas is an example of qiyas al-shabah (analogy of similarity) where the distinguishing factor is the state of the dhimmah.
5
Recognize the implications for the Hereafter
The passage notes that the deceased has the ruling of the living in matters of the Hereafter (akhirah). Thus, certain obligations (e.g., debts, bequests) can be settled from the estate, and kafala is possible via bequest from one-third. This shows that the weakness of dhimmah is not absolute but relative to worldly judicial enforcement.
This nuance is important for understanding the full Hanafi theory of dhimmah.

Travel as a Cause of Legal Relief: The Hanafi View on Qasr
Defines travel and its legal effects, with a madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define travel as a cause of takhfif in Hanafi usul, apply the evidence to derive the obligation of qasr, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Shortening prayer in travel is obligatory; the traveler's prayer is two rak'ahs, not four.

This passage defines travel (safar) as one of the causes that bring about legal relief (takhfif) in Hanafi fiqh. The author focuses on two effects: the shortening of four-rak'ah prayers and the postponement of fasting. The central dispute with al-Shafi'i is whether qasr is an obligation or a license. We will examine the Hanafi proof from hadith and the school's usul principles that settle this ikhtilaf.

Guided Path
1

2

Travel affects salah (shortening of four-rak'ah prayers) and sawm (postponement of fasting). For salah, the Hanafi position is that the obligation becomes two rak'ahs; for sawm, it remains obligatory but may be postponed.
The ruling on sawm is different: fasting is not dropped but merely deferred.
3

The text cites the hadith of 'Aisha: 'Prayer was prescribed as two, then travel prayer was confirmed and resident prayer increased.' Also the constant practice of the Prophet and the caliphs. This indicates that the obligation for travel is two rak'ahs.
The dalil is qat'i in meaning and transmission, making qasr an obligation.
4

Al-Shafi'i analogizes qasr to fasting: since fasting is a license in travel, so is shortening. The Hanafi response: fasting can be performed in travel, but the ruling on prayer is fixed by explicit narration, not analogy.
This is a key usul point: dalil naqli overrides qiyas when the text is clear.
5

According to Hanafi, a traveler who prays four rak'ahs for Zuhr does not fulfill his obligation; the prayer is not legitimate. According to Shafi'i, such a prayer is valid but he missed the license.
The practical difference affects the validity of the prayer and the need for qada.

Qasr in Travel: Obligation or Concession?
Ruling with proof and inter-madhhab dispute on qasr

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on shortening prayer in travel, cite the primary hadith evidence, and explain the difference with the Shafi'i view.

Core Idea
Shortening prayer in travel is the obligatory form; the hadith prescribes two rak'ahs for travel, making four invalid.

The ruling on shortening prayer (qasr) is one of the most debated issues between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools. The Hanafi school holds that it is the obligatory form based on explicit hadith and the continuous practice of the Prophet and caliphs. This lesson examines the proofs and the method of istinbat.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that shortening the four-rak'ah prayers (zuhr, asr, isha) to two rak'ahs in travel is obligatory (fard). Praying four is not prescribed and is invalid.
This is based on explicit text and practice.
2
Identify the opposing view
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that the original obligation is four rak'ahs, and shortening is a concession (rukhsah) by analogy with fasting. One may choose to shorten or complete.
The thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference) is prayer validity.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof (dalil)
The hadith of Aisha: 'Prayer was prescribed as two rak'ahs; then travel prayer was confirmed, and residence prayer was increased.' This shows that the obligation for travel is two.
The hadith is qat‘i al-thubut (mutawatir) and zanni al-dalalah in Hanafi classification.
4
Analyze the opposing proof
Al-Shafi'i argues by qiyas: just as fasting in travel is voluntary (one may choose to fast or postpone), similarly, prayer should allow choice.
Hanafi refutes this because the texts for prayer are explicit (nass) and override qiyas.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
For Hanafi, praying four in travel is invalid and must be repeated if done intentionally. For Shafi'i, it is permissible but less virtuous.
This affects daily practice for travelers.

As for travel: it is linguistically the cutting of a distance, and legally it is the prolonged departure from the place of residence with the intention of traveling, and its minimum is three days and their nights with camel travel and walking on foot. It does not contradict legal capacity because of the retention of reason and ability, but it is a cause of concession as the author said. Causes of concession in rulings because it is a cause of hardship, whether actual or estimated. The four-rak'ah prayers: i.e., travel removes the latter half of the four-rak'ah prayers like zuhr, asr, and isha, such that completing them is not prescribed at all according to us. Al-Shafi'i said: its context is the obligation of four, and shortening is a concession, analogizing to fasting. So whoever prays four acts upon the resolution, and whoever shortens chooses the concession. Our proof is what the two shaykhs narrated from Aisha when she said: 'Prayer was prescribed as two rak'ahs; then travel prayer was confirmed, and residence prayer was increased.' (Muslim, no. 1570) Thus it is known that the obligatory prayer in travel is two rak'ahs. Therefore the Messenger of Allah constantly acted upon it as al-Bukhari narrated from Ibn Umar: 'I accompanied the Messenger of Allah in travel and he never increased beyond two rak'ahs until Allah took him. I accompanied Abu Bakr, he never increased beyond two; I accompanied Umar, he never increased beyond two; I accompanied Uthman, he never increased beyond two.' And Allah said: 'Indeed in the Messenger of Allah you have a good example' (al-Ahzab 33:21). The postponement of fasting to a number of other days is not in its cancellation, so it remains obligatory such that performing it in travel is valid, unlike half of the prayer.

Commentary
This passage is a legal definition followed by a ruling and proof. It defines travel (safar) linguistically as covering a distance and legally as prolonged departure from one's residence with intent to travel, with a minimum of three days and nights at a normal pace. Travel does not negate legal capacity (ahlīyah) because the traveler retains reason and ability, but it is a cause of concession due to hardship. The concession includes shortening the four-rak'ah obligatory prayers (zuhr, asr, isha) to two rak'ahs, and the author states that according to the Hanafi school, performing the full four rak'ahs in travel is not prescribed at all—shortening is the obligation. This is opposed to al-Shafi'i, who holds that the original obligation is four rak'ahs, and shortening is a concession (rukhsah) by analogy with fasting, meaning one may choose between the two. The Hanafi proof is the hadith from Aisha that prayer was originally prescribed as two rak'ahs, then the travel prayer was fixed at two and the residence prayer increased. This shows that the default for travel is two rak'ahs. Additional evidence from the practice of the Prophet and the caliphs, and the Quranic verse on following the Prophet's example. For fasting, travel allows postponement to other days but does not cancel the obligation, so fasting in travel is valid and carries reward, unlike the prayer where the obligation itself is reduced.

Key Terms
السفر (al-safar): Travel: linguistically covering a distance; legally, prolonged departure from residence with intent, minimum three days and nights at normal pace.
القصر (al-qaṣr): Shortening the four-rak'ah prayers to two in travel; for Hanafi, this is the obligatory (fard) form in travel.
النص (al-nass): Explicit text with clear meaning; in Hanafi usul, it is qat'ī al-dalālah (definitive in meaning) unless proven otherwise.
