Classification of a Discerning Minor's Acts by Benefit and Harm
Classification of minor's acts by benefit and harm

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four categories of a discerning minor's acts according to the Hanafi school and explain the role of the guardian for each category.

Core Idea
A minor's acts are valid without obligation if purely beneficial; require guardian if potentially harmful.

The Hanafi school classifies the acts of a discerning minor into four categories based on the nature of benefit and harm. This lesson unpacks each category and the legal consequences, including when the guardian's permission is necessary.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational principle
Locate where the text states that sound performance (sihhat al-ada') is based on deficient capacity (al-ahliyya al-qasira). This principle underlies all four categories.
2
List the four categories
From the text, extract the four types: (1) Rights of Allah that are purely beneficial (e.g., Islam), (2) Purely beneficial transactions (e.g., gift), (3) Physical acts of worship (e.g., prayer), (4) Transactions with potential benefit and harm (e.g., sale).
Note the order and the specific examples.
3
State the ruling for each category
For each category, determine whether the act is valid (sahih) and whether it is obligatory (wajib). Only acts in the fourth category require guardian involvement.
The text explicitly says for categories 1-3: valid without obligation; category 4: valid only with guardian.
4
Explain the guardian's role in the fourth category
The minor's deficiency in judgment is compensated by the guardian's opinion. The text also discusses gross deception (ghabn fahish) and different narrations from Abu Hanifa and his companions.
Abu Hanifa validates the sale even with ghabn fahish in one narration; his two companions do not.
5
Apply the hadith on prayer
The hadith 'Order your children to pray at seven' (from Musnad Ahmad) is used to support the validity of a minor's worship without obligation.
This is a key dalil for the third category.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed exposition on the legal capacity (ahliyya) of a discerning minor (ṣabī ʿāqil) in the Hanafi school. It opens by stating that the same rulings apply to a mentally deficient adult (maʿtūh) as to a minor. The key principle is that sound performance (ṣiḥḥat al-adāʾ) of an act is based on deficient capacity (al-ahliyya al-qāṣira), which every human possesses, while obligation (wujūb) and address of the Lawgiver (khiṭāb) require complete capacity (al-ahliyya al-kāmila) attained at puberty with sound intellect. The text then categorizes the minor's actions into four types: (1) Rights of Allah that are purely beneficial, like accepting Islam — valid in this world and the next without obligation, contrary to al-Shāfiʿī who does not validate it for worldly rulings. (2) Purely beneficial transactions not involving divine rights, like receiving gifts — valid without guardian permission. (3) Physical acts of worship like prayer and fasting — valid but not obligatory, training the child. A hadith commands ordering children to pray at seven. (4) Transactions involving potential benefit and harm, like sales — require the guardian's opinion because the minor lacks discernment. Regarding gross deception (ghabn fāḥish), Abū Ḥanīfa holds that a minor's sale to outsiders is valid even with gross deception, unlike his two companions. If the sale is with the guardian, two narrations exist from Abū Ḥanīfa: one permits, one rejects due to suspicion of collusion. The passage then discusses the minor's agency (wakāla) and will (waṣiyya). A minor agent does not bear liability (ʿuhda) unless with guardian permission. A minor's will for acts of charity is invalid according to the Hanafis because it is a gratuitous transfer after death, which is pure harm; the potential reward in the afterlife is disregarded because the owner no longer needs the property. Al-Shāfiʿī considers it purely beneficial and valid. The Hanafi position is that apparent benefit is outweighed by the harm of losing ownership.

Key Terms
الأهلية القاصرة (al-ahliyya al-qāṣira): Deficient legal capacity that permits valid performance of acts without obligation.
الغبن الفاحش (al-ghabn al-fāḥish): Gross deception where the price difference is so large that people would not normally accept it.

Disputed Rulings: Minor's Acceptance of Islam and Will
Inter-madhhab disputes on minor's Islam and will

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on the validity of a minor's acceptance of Islam and his will, and explain the legal reasoning (istidlal) behind each position.

Core Idea
Hanafis validate a minor's Islam as purely beneficial; they invalidate his will as pure harm.

This lesson examines two key inter-madhhab disputes regarding the discerning minor: the validity of his acceptance of Islam and the validity of his will. These disputes illustrate how the Hanafi school applies the principles of deficient capacity and the classification of acts by benefit and harm.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on minor's Islam
The Hanafis hold that a discerning minor's acceptance of Islam is valid in both this world and the Hereafter, without obligation (luzum). The basis is that Islam is a purely beneficial right (haqq mahd) that does not admit anything else.
2
Present the Shafi'i objection
Al-Shafi'i argues that the minor's Islam is not valid for worldly rulings (like inheritance and marriage dissolution) because the minor cannot bear obligation, and Islam implies commitment.
The text says the Shafi'i view is that it is valid only in the Hereafter for reward.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response is that deficient capacity (al-ahliyya al-qasira) suffices for valid performance of purely beneficial acts; obligation is not required. The practical consequence is that the Hanafi minor inherits from his disbelieving father, while the Shafi'i minor does not.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf mentioned in the madhhab dispute map.
4
Examine the will dispute
The Hanafis hold that a minor's will (wasiyya) is invalid, as it is a gratuitous transfer after death, which is pure harm (darar mahd). The Shafi'is consider it purely beneficial because it brings reward in the Hereafter after the owner no longer needs the property.
The text uses the analogy of selling a near-dead sheep to illustrate the Hanafi reasoning.

The Intra-Hanafi Dispute on the Interdicted Slave's Confession of Existing Theft
Explains the intra-Hanafi dispute on the interdicted slave's confession of existing theft.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the positions of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf, and Muhammad regarding an interdicted slave's confession of existing theft (sariqa qaima) when the master denies it, and evaluate the practical consequences (thamarah al-khilaf).

Core Idea
The dispute hinges on whether the slave's confession harms the master's property right, affecting both hadd and restitution.

The previous lesson established the general rule; now we delve into a specific ikhtilaf within the Hanafi school. When an interdicted slave confesses to stealing property that still exists in his possession, and the master denies it, the three founders of the school disagree. This lesson unpacks their arguments and the usul principles at play, showing how the same source text yields different rulings based on the weight given to the master's ownership versus the slave's confession.

Guided Path
1
State the disputed issue
Identify the exact scenario: an interdicted slave (mahjur) confesses to stealing an existing item (e.g., a sheep) that is still in his possession. The master denies the theft. What are the rulings of the three imams?
The master's denial is key because it triggers the dispute.
2
Identify each imam's position
List the three positions from the text: Abu Hanifa (cut hand + return property to victim); Abu Yusuf (cut hand + no return; slave owes equivalent after emancipation); Muhammad (no cut + no return; slave owes equivalent after emancipation).
Note the differences: hadd vs no hadd; immediate return vs delayed compensation.
3
Analyze the underlying usul reasoning
For each position, extract the usul principle: Abu Hanifa gives priority to the confession as proof against the slave (al-mar'u yu'khadhu bi-iqrarihi); Abu Yusuf balances confession with protection of master's property (confession harms master, so invalid as to property but valid for hadd); Muhammad gives full priority to master's ownership (confession is against master's property, so invalid entirely).
These reflect different weightings of competing principles: iqrar, harm (darar), and ownership (milk).
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: Under Abu Hanifa, the victim gets immediate restitution of property; under Abu Yusuf, the victim only gets compensation after the slave is free; under Muhammad, the victim also gets delayed compensation but the slave avoids amputation.
This affects the rights of the victim and the master in the short term.
5
Identify the mufta bihi position
The text does not explicitly state a preferred view, but later Hanafi authorities (e.g., Ibn Abidin) adopt Abu Hanifa's position as the mufta bihi. The reason is that the confession is stronger than the master's denial because the slave is the one who knows the truth.
This shows the later tarjih mechanism within the school.

Intoxication and Legal Capacity
States the rulings of the drunken person on legal capacity and liability.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the legal capacity of a drunken person and evaluate the counter-argument that the drunk is not mukallaf due to lack of understanding.

Core Idea
The drunk is legally obligated as a deterrent, but apostasy and confessions to pure hadd are not enforced by istihsan.

The passage examines how intoxication affects legal capacity (ahliyya) in Hanafi usul. The author affirms that the drunk is mukallaf (legally obligated) as a deterrent for drinking, but then presents two exceptions: apostasy and confessions to pure hadd penalties. A dispute is raised about whether lack of understanding negates taklif, and the Hanafi response relies on the deterrent rationale and the example of al-Shafi'i. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and the practical implications of this nuanced position.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi ruling on the drunk's legal capacity
Identify the author's claim: the drunk is obligated by all Sharia rulings and his dispositions are valid, except for apostasy (by istihsan) and confessions to pure hadd penalties.
2
Identify the opposing view and its basis
The opposing view (attributed to al-Taftazani) holds that the drunk is not mukallaf because he cannot understand the address, which is a condition for taklif.
The condition 'fahm al-mukallaf al-khitab' is central.
3
Follow the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response offers two arguments: (1) some scholars hold that reason does not completely disappear in intoxication; (2) even if reason is lost, the drunk is obligated as a deterrent for committing the prohibited act of drinking, supported by al-Taqi al-Subki and al-Shafi'i.
The deterrent argument (zajr) overrides the lack of understanding.
4
Apply the exceptions: apostasy and pure hadd confessions
Apostasy requires intention (qasd), which the drunk lacks, so it is not executed by istihsan. Confessions to pure hadd (e.g., zina, theft) are not accepted because the drunk is unstable and may retract; intoxication is treated as equivalent to retraction.
Contrast with confessions involving individual rights (qadhf, qisas), which are valid.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: If the drunk were not mukallaf, all his actions would be void; if he is mukallaf (Hanafi view), only specific exceptions apply. This affects rulings on divorce, sale, and other contracts.

عوارض مكتسبة من الأهلية وتلزمه أحكام الشرع، وتنفذ تصرفاته كلها إلا الردة استحساناً، والإقرار بالحدود الخالصة لله تعالى؛ لأن السكران لايكاد يثبت على شيء فأقيم السكر مقام الرجوع، فيعمل فيما يحتمل الرجوع والحد يسقط بالرجوع. وأما تفسيره: هو أن يراد بالشيء غير ما وضع له لغة وفي الاصطلاح كما مر. واجب شرعاً مقيدا بأمر غير واجب ينصرف إلى غير الواجب، فلايتم الاستدلال، فالاستدلال بالإجماع، فإن قيل: هذا مخالف لقولهم: "فهم المكلّف الخطاب شرط التكليف، والسكران لايفهم لتعطل عقله المميز بين الأمور الحسنة والقبيحة" على قول التفتازاني له أقول على قول من قال: إن العقل لا يزول في السكر وهو يفهم الخطاب فلايرد، وأما على قول غيره فالجواب أنه مكلف زجراً له لارتكابه المحظور، وعليه نص التقى السبكي في شرح المنهاج: أن العاصي بسكره يكلف تغليظاً عليه، وقد نص الشافعي على هذا، فإذا ثبت أن السكران مكلف كما مر، فلا يبطل شيئا من الأهلية.. أحكام الشرع من الصلاة والصوم وغيرهما. تصرفاته كلها كالطلاق والعتاق والبيع والشراء. إلا الردة استحساناً إلخ: فإنه إذا ارتد السكران وتكلّم بكلمة الكفر في حالة السكر لا يحكم بكفره استحساناً، وجه الاستحسان أن الردة تبنى على القصد، والسكران إن كان مخاطباً ومكلفا في الأحكام زجراً له ولكنه لا قصد له، والكفر إنما يتحقق ببدل الاعتقاد لا يرتفع ولا يثبت إلا بالقصد، وكذا إذا أقرّ بالحدود الخالصة لله تعالى كشرب الخمر والزنا والسرقة لايحد؛ لأن الحد إنما يجب إذا ثبت على إقراره والسكران لا يثبت على شيء. ألا ترى أن العلماء اتفقوا على أن السكر لا يتحقق إلا بزوال عقله حيث لا يميز بين الأشياء ولا يعرف الأرض من السماء، وفي هذه الحالة كيف يثبت على شيء؛ لأنه إنما يتحقق بالعقل والقصد فيعمل: السكر في إسقاط الحد بسبب الرجوع. يحتمل الرجوع وهو الحدود الخالصة لله تعالى بخلاف ما لو أقر بالحدود الغير الخالصة لله كالقذف والقصاص، فإنه لا يصح الرجوع لتعلقه بحقوق العباد، فالسكر لا يعلم فيها، فيؤاخذ بالحدّ والقصاص.
Acquired impediments from legal capacity: he is obligated by the rulings of the Sharia, and all his dispositions are executed except for apostasy by istihsan, and the confession to the pure hadd penalties of Allah because the drunken person hardly persists on anything, so intoxication is placed in the position of retraction (ruju'), so it operates in what admits retraction, and the hadd is dropped by retraction. As for its interpretation: it is that something is intended other than what it was placed for, linguistically and in terminology as previously. Wajib by Sharia bound by a non-obligatory matter diverts to the non-obligatory, so the inference is not complete, so inference is by consensus. If it is said: this contradicts their statement: 'The understanding of the obligated person of the address is a condition of obligation, and the drunk does not understand because his mind that distinguishes between good and bad things is disabled' according to the statement of al-Taftazani. I say: according to the one who said that reason does not disappear in intoxication and he understands the address, so it is not refuted. As for according to others, the answer is that he is obligated as a deterrent for committing the prohibited, and upon this is the text of al-Taqi al-Subki in Sharh al-Minhaj: that the sinner by his intoxication is obligated with severity upon him, and al-Shafii stated this. So when it is established that the drunk is obligated as mentioned, he does not invalidate anything of legal capacity. The rulings of Sharia like prayer, fasting, and others. All his dispositions like divorce, manumission, sale, and purchase. Except for apostasy by istihsan etc.: if the drunk apostatizes and speaks a word of disbelief in the state of intoxication, it is not judged that he is disbelief by istihsan. The aspect of istihsan is that apostasy is based on intention, and the drunk, although he is addressed and obligated in rulings as a deterrent, has no intention. Disbelief is only actualized by alternative of belief, not elevated nor established except with intention. Likewise, if he confesses to the pure hadd penalties of Allah like drinking wine, adultery, and theft, he is not subjected to hadd because hadd is only obligatory if he persists in his confession, and the drunk does not persist in anything. Do you not see that the scholars agreed that intoxication is only realized by loss of reason where he does not distinguish between things and does not know the earth from the sky? In this state, how can he persist in anything? Because it is only realized by reason and intention, so intoxication operates in dropping the hadd due to retraction. It admits retraction and it is the pure hadd penalties of Allah, contrary to if he confesses to the hadd that are not purely for Allah like qadhf and qisas, because retraction is not valid due to its connection to the rights of the servants, so intoxication does not operate in them, so he is punished with hadd and qisas.

Commentary
This passage discusses the rulings of a drunken person (sukran) regarding his legal capacity (ahliyya). The author begins by stating that the drunken person is obligated by the rulings of Sharia (like prayer, fasting, etc.) and his dispositions (like divorce, sale) are generally valid, except for apostasy and confession to pure hadd penalties (like zina, theft). This is because apostasy requires sincere intention (qasd), which the drunk lacks. The drunken person is still considered a mukallaf (legally obligated) as a deterrent (zajran) for committing the prohibited act of drinking, a view supported by al-Shafi'i and others. However, his apostasy is not executed by istihsan since it hinges on intention. Similarly, his confession to hadd penalties is not accepted because the hadd is only applied if he persists (thabat) on his confession, but the drunk is unstable and may retract. Intoxication is deemed equivalent to retraction (ruju') in such cases. In contrast, if he confesses to penalties involving individual rights (like qadhf or qisas), his confession is valid because these rights cannot be retracted due to the involvement of others' rights. The passage thus clarifies the nuanced Hanafi position on the legal effects of intoxication.

Key Terms
السكران (al-sukran): A person who is in a state of intoxication, having lost his discerning reason but not completely unconscious.
الأهلية (al-ahliyya): Legal capacity to acquire rights and obligations; the drunk retains ahliyya al-ada' (capacity to perform) but with some restrictions.
الردة (al-ridda): Apostasy: renouncing Islam. Its execution requires sincere intention, which the drunk lacks, so it is not enforced by istihsan.
الحدود الخالصة لله (al-hudud al-khalisa li-llah): Hadd penalties that are purely for the right of Allah, such as for wine drinking, zina, and theft; confessions to these by a drunk are not accepted because they may be retracted.
