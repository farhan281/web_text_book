The Principle: Duress Does Not Remove Legal Address
Establishes core principle and classifies duress types.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi principle that duress does not negate legal capacity and explain the justification using the concept of ibtila'.

Core Idea
Duress modifies rulings but does not remove the divine address from the coerced person.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi usul principle: even under duress, the coerced individual remains addressed by divine commands because they are in a state of affliction (ibtila'). The passage from Muntakhab al-Husami argues that duress in its entirety does not contradict legal capacity (ahliyyah) nor necessitate the removal of the address. Instead, the address is confirmed precisely because the person is tested. We will explore the reasoning and the Quranic evidence.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core rule
State the principle from the text: duress does not remove address (wad' al-khitab) nor negate legal capacity.
This is a general rule for all types of duress.
2
Understand the rationale: ibtila'
The text says the coerced person is 'afflicted' (mubtala), and affliction actually realizes the address. Without ibtila', there would be no accountability.
Ibtila' here means being in a state of trial under duress.
3
Locate the Quranic evidence
The text cites Quran 6:119 to show that necessity (idtirar) makes carrion permissible. This applies analogously to duress.
The analogy (qiyas) links duress to necessity.
4
Follow the derivation chain
The istinbat chain shows: takhsis of general prohibition by exception, then bayan of permissibility, then qiyas to duress, then talil resulting in obligation to preserve life.
This shows how usul principles generate the ruling.

ثم قال: وضع الخطاب: أي سقوطه عن المكره. بحال سواء كان الإكراه ملجئاً أو لا، لوجود الذمة والعقل الذي عليه مدار الخطاب. مبتلى في حالة الإكراه كما أنه مبتلى في حالة الاختيار. يحقق الخطاب: لأنه لا يثبت بدونه، ثم استدلّ على ثبوت الابتلاء وتحقق الخطاب به بين: فرض كما إذا أكره على أكل الميتة بالقتل فإنه حينئذ يفرض عليه أكلها، ولو صبر حتى مات يأثم؛ لأن أكلها كان مباحاً له؛ لأنه تعالى قال: ﴿إِلَّا مَا اضْطُرِرْتُمْ إِلَيْهِ﴾ (الأنعام: ۱۱۹)، فثبت الإباحة بالاستثناء، ومن أكره على مباح يفترض عليه فعله. وحظر أي ممنوع في حالة الإكراه أيضا كما إذا أكره على قتل مسلم بغير حق، فإن صبر حتى مات يؤجر، وإباحة كما أكره على الإفطار في نهار رمضان فحينئذ يباح له الفطر ورخصة كما إذا أكره على إجراء كلمة الكفر على لسانه، فحينئذ يرخص له ذلك بشرط أن يكون التصديق في قلبه والفرق بين الرخصة والإباحة هو في المباح ترتفع الحرمة، وفي الرخصة لا ترتفع الحرمة بل يرفع الإثم فقط، والأولى عدم ذكر الإباحة؛ لأنها إن كان مع الإثم في الصبر فهي الفرض، وإلا فهي الرخصة، فالحاصل أنها داخل في الفرض أو الرخصة. ويأثم فيه أي في الإقدام على ما أكره عليه إذا كان حراماً كقتل المسلم بغير حق. أخرى: في الإقدام على ما أكره عليه إذا كان فرضا كأكل الميتة إذا أكره عليه بالقتل، وكل من الأجر والإثم إنما يكون بعد تعلق الخطاب، فثبت أن المكره مخاطب مبتلى. ثم أشار إلى أمثلة الفرض والحظر والرخصة والإباحة بقوله: فلا رخصة إلخ. في القتل: أي قتل غيره إذا خاف على نفسه الهلاك؛ لأنهما في استحقاق العصمة سواء، فلايكون له صيانة نفسه بإتلاف غيره، فصار الإكراه في حكم العدم لتعارض الحرمتين مع عدم المرجح. والجرح وكذا لا رخصة له في جرح غيره إذا قيل له: اقطع يد فلان وإلا أقتلك، حتى لو قطع كان آثما؛ لأن أطراف المؤمن في الحرمة مثل حرمة نفسه في حق الغير، فتحقق التعارض بين حرمة نفسه وحرمة جرح غيره، فلم يترجح = القيد بالفتح بند والحبس باز داشتن وقيل القيد ما يوضع في الرجل.

Types of Duress
And duress in its entirety does not contradict legal capacity, nor does it necessitate removal of address in any case; because the coerced person is afflicted, i.e. with all the mentioned divisions, and affliction realizes the address. Do you not see that he is torn between an obligation, a prohibition, a permission, and a license, and the coerced person sins in doing what he is coerced to do in one case and is rewarded in another. So there is no license in killing and wounding for the coerced person = or by shackle, or by imprisonment for a long period, so in this type choice remains but he is not pleased with what he does. And a third type remains, which is one that does not eliminate consent nor spoil choice, such as threatening to imprison his father or his son or his wife or the like, so in this type both consent and choice remain; legal capacity is not capacity for obligation nor capacity for performance.

Then he said: Removal of address: i.e. its removal from the coerced person in any case, whether the duress is compelling or not, because of the existence of legal responsibility ('aql) upon which the address revolves. He is afflicted in the state of duress just as he is afflicted in the state of choice. It realizes the address: because it is not established without it. Then he argued for the establishment of affliction and realization of the address with it between: obligation, as when he is coerced to eat carrion by death, then it becomes obligatory upon him to eat it, and if he persists until death he sins; because its eating was permissible for him because Allah said: "Except what you are compelled to" (6:119), so permissibility is established by the exception, and whoever is coerced to do something permissible, it becomes obligatory upon him to do it. And prohibition: i.e. prohibited in the state of duress also, as when he is coerced to kill a Muslim unjustly, then if he persists until death he is rewarded. And permission: as when he is coerced to break the fast in Ramadan, then he is permitted to break the fast. And license: as when he is coerced to utter a word of disbelief on his tongue, then he is licensed to do that on condition that affirmation is in his heart. The difference between license and permission is that in permission the prohibition is lifted, while in license the prohibition is not lifted but the sin is removed only. And it is better not to mention permission; because if it involves sin in patience then it is obligation, otherwise it is license. So the conclusion is that it falls under obligation or license. And he sins therein: i.e. in proceeding with what he is coerced to do if it is forbidden, like killing a Muslim unjustly. Another: in proceeding with what he is coerced to do if it is obligatory, like eating carrion when coerced by death. And both reward and sin only occur after the address is connected, so it is established that the coerced person is addressed and afflicted. Then he pointed to examples of obligation, prohibition, license, and permission by saying: So there is no license etc. In killing: i.e. killing another if he fears destruction of himself; because they are equal in deserving inviolability, so he cannot protect himself by destroying another. So duress becomes like non-existent due to conflict of two prohibitions without a preponderant. And wounding: likewise, there is no license for him in wounding another if told: Cut off so-and-so's hand or I will kill you, such that if he cuts he is sinful; because the limbs of a believer in sanctity are like the sanctity of his own self in relation to others, so conflict arises between the sanctity of himself and the sanctity of wounding another, so no preponderance occurs = al-qayd: with fatha (band) and al-habs (imprisonment), and it is said al-qayd is what is placed on the leg.

In carrion etc., because the prohibition of these things is removed under complete duress; because it is not established by text except under choice, due to His saying: "And He has detailed for you what He has forbidden to you except what you are compelled to" (6:119), for the exception of the state of necessity from prohibited states necessitates permission. So those things become permissible for him. Nevertheless, if he drinks wine, he is not subject to the prescribed punishment by istihsan, because lesser duress becomes a doubt that averts the hadd punishment. He is a resident and healthy, and we only restricted by being a resident and healthy because if he was a traveler or sick and refrained from breaking the fast, he would be sinful wasting his blood without benefit, because breaking the fast was permissible for him in travel and sickness due to His saying: "So whoever among you is sick or on a journey, then a number of other days" (2:184). So this section is not included in examples of license but rather in examples of permission.

Commentary
This passage from Muntakhab al-Husami (a Hanafi usul work) provides a detailed analysis of the types of duress (ikrah) and their impact on legal rulings. It begins by stating a core principle: duress does not negate legal capacity (ahliyyah) nor remove the address (wad' al-khitab) from the coerced person. This means the coerced individual remains subject to divine address and is accountable, albeit with modified rulings. The reason given is that the coerced person is 'afflicted' (mubtala) by the duress, and this affliction actually confirms the address—without it, there would be no accountability. The text then categorizes duress into types based on whether choice and consent are eliminated. The first type is compelling duress (ikrah mulji') that eliminates choice (e.g., threat of death, severe beating, long imprisonment). The second is lesser duress (ikrah qasir) where choice remains but consent is absent (e.g., threat of minor beating). The third type involves threats to family (father, son, wife) where both consent and choice remain; this is considered a form of duress that does not affect rulings. The passage then elaborates on four possible rulings under duress: obligation (fard), prohibition (hazr), permission (ibaha), and license (rukhsa). It uses Quranic evidence (6:119) to show that under necessity, prohibited things become permissible, and with compelling duress, eating carrion becomes obligatory to preserve life. The text also distinguishes between permission (where prohibition is lifted) and license (where prohibition remains but sin is removed). Examples are given: killing and wounding others under duress are not excused because the inviolability of the victim is equal to one's own; thus, no license applies. Zina under duress for a man is also not excused due to the severe harm to lineage. However, a woman coerced into zina is granted license, as will be discussed later. For carrion, wine, and pork, complete duress lifts the prohibition entirely, making consumption permissible and even obligatory if necessary. For lesser duress, the prohibition remains, but if wine is drunk, the hadd punishment is averted by istihsan due to a doubt (shubha). The passage also covers license to utter words of disbelief (provided heart is firm), breaking prayer and fast (only for a healthy resident), and destroying property. The overall lesson is that duress modifies but does not nullify legal responsibility, and the specific ruling depends on the severity of the duress and the nature of the act.

Key Terms
الإكراه (al-ikrah): Compelling someone to perform an act against their will through threat of harm.
وضع الخطاب (wad' al-khitab): The removal of the address (i.e., the legal command) from the coerced person.
الابتلاء (al-ibtilā’): Affliction or trial that justifies the address and accountability.

The Effects of Coercion (Ikrah) in Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh
Explaining two effects of coercion and a dispute on hadd

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of coercion, explain their two effects (tabdil al-nisba and tafwit al-rida), and evaluate the intra-Hanafi dispute on hadd for a man coerced into zina.

Core Idea
Coercion changes attribution or removes consent but never nullifies the act itself; the hadd dispute shows istihsan overriding qiyas.

In Hanafi legal theory, coercion (ikrah) is not a blanket invalidator. Instead, it operates through two mechanisms: changing who the act is attributed to (tabdil al-nisba) and removing the required consent (tafwit al-rida). The source text explains these effects and then delves into a dispute within the school about whether a man coerced into zina receives the hadd punishment. This lesson unpacks these concepts and the methodological reasoning behind them.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish complete vs incomplete coercion
Identify ikrah kamil (mulji) – threat of death or severe harm – from ikrah qasir – threat of beating or shackles. Complete coercion shifts attribution; incomplete only affects consent.
Complete coercion must be imminent and unavoidable.
2
Understand tabdil al-nisba (attribution shift)
Under complete coercion, the act is legally attributed to the coercer, not the coerced. Conditions: the act must be suitable for transfer and no preventer (e.g., lineage in zina for women).
This applies to acts like killing, destroying property.
3
Understand tafwit al-rida (loss of consent)
Under any coercion, consent is lost but choice may remain. This affects contracts that require consent (sale, lease) – they become fasid (invalid), not batil. Unilateral acts like divorce are unaffected.
After coercion ends, if the coerced ratifies the contract, it becomes valid.
4
Apply to examples
Work through the examples given: sale under coercion is concluded but invalid; divorce is effective; acknowledgments are invalid because they contradict evidence.
See the text's discussion of khul' and minor's divorce.
5
Present the hadd dispute for men
State the disagreement: Zufar and earlier Abu Hanifa hold that a man coerced into zina is subject to hadd by qiyas; later Abu Hanifa and the two companions drop it by istihsan. Both views rely on valid methods.
The woman's case is distinct due to lineage.
6
Compare qiyas vs istihsan
Analyze the clash: qiyas says capacity remains, so hadd applies; istihsan says coercion creates a shubha that wards off punishment. The relied-upon position is istihsan.
This illustrates how istihsan can override qiyas in Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage from a commentary on usul al-fiqh explains the legal effects of coercion (ikrah) in Hanafi law. It begins by distinguishing between complete (kamil) and incomplete (qasir) coercion, using the example of a woman compelled to commit zina. The key principle is that coercion does not nullify the legal capacity of the coerced; actions and statements remain valid unless a specific legal changer (mughayyir) is present. The text then details two main effects: (1) Changing attribution (tabdil al-nisba) — under complete coercion, the act is attributed to the coercer rather than the coerced, but only if the act is suitable for such transfer and no preventer exists. (2) Loss of consent (tafwit al-rida) — coercion removes consent, which is required for contracts that can be rescinded, making them concluded invalidly (fasid) but not void. Acts like divorce and manumission, which do not require consent, are effective even under coercion. The passage also discusses the difference between men and women regarding zina: a woman may be excused from hadd due to lineage concerns, whereas a man generally is not. It notes an internal Hanafi dispute: Abu Hanifa's later opinion (with the two companions) holds that even complete coercion removes the hadd for a man by istihsan, while his earlier opinion and Zufar's view maintain the hadd by qiyas. The commentary follows the author's order, explaining each point with examples from Hanafi fiqh.

Key Terms
الإكراه الكامل (al-ikrāh al-kāmil): Coercion that compels a person to act against his will by a threat of death, severe bodily harm, or imprisonment that he cannot avoid.
تبديل النسبة (tabdīl al-nisbah): The legal effect where the act is attributed to the coercer rather than the coerced, removing liability from the coerced.
الرضاء (al-riḍā’): Consent, a necessary condition for the validity of contracts that are subject to rescission (e.g., sale, lease).

Complete Coercion: Tool-like Agency and Attribution of Acts
Explaining how complete coercion nullifies valid choice and attributes the act to the coercer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of complete coercion to determine whether an act is attributed to the coercer or the coerced, and identify cases where tool-like agency is impossible.

Core Idea
Complete coercion corrupts choice, making the coerced a tool; the act is attributed to the coercer except where tool agency is impossible.

In this passage, the author explains the legal effect of complete coercion (ikrah kamil) on actions. We will see how the Hanafi school distinguishes between actions that can be performed through a tool and those that cannot. This distinction determines who bears legal responsibility: the coercer or the coerced.

Guided Path
1
Define Complete Coercion
Identify the definition of ikrah kamil: coercion that corrupts choice entirely, making the coerced a mere tool (āla) of the coercer. Note the key phrase 'al-fāsid fī mu‘āraḍat al-ṣaḥīḥ ka al-‘adam' (the corrupt, in contest with the correct, is like non-existent).
The corrupt choice (ikhtiyar fāsid) is legally nullified when it opposes a valid choice.
2
State the General Rule
Apply the principle: For actions that can be performed through a tool (e.g., killing, destroying property), the act is attributed to the coercer, and he bears the legal consequences (qisas, diya). The coerced is like a sword or stick.
This is established by consensus (ijma‘) as stated in the text.
3
Identify the Exception
Recognize actions where tool-like agency is impossible: eating, intercourse, and all speech (including divorce, emancipation, marriage). In such cases, the act cannot be attributed to the coercer; the coerced retains a corrupt choice and bears the ruling.
The reasoning: one cannot eat with another's mouth or speak with another's tongue.
4
Apply to Scenarios
Test the rule: coerced killing → coercer liable; coerced eating during Ramadan → coerced's fast is broken; coerced divorce → divorce is effective because speech cannot be attributed to the coercer.
Compensation (ḍaman) for coerced eating of another's property is on the coerced because he receives the benefit.
5
Refine with Nuance
Note that in some cases the coerced may have recourse to the coercer for compensation if he did not benefit (e.g., if he was full when forced to eat his own food). See the references to al-Muhit and al-Khulasa.
This shows the depth of Hanafi juristic discourse.

The author establishes that complete coercion corrupts (yuFsid) the choice (ikhtiyar) of the coerced, making it like non-existent. Consequently, the coerced is considered a mere tool (āla) of the coercer, and the action is attributed to the coercer for rulings like retaliation and blood-money by consensus (ijma‘). However, this attribution applies only to actions that can logically be performed through a tool, such as killing or destroying property. For actions where tool-like agency is impossible—like eating, intercourse, and speech—the act cannot be attributed to the coercer. In those cases, the coerced retains a corrupt choice, and the ruling applies to him directly. For example, if a person is coerced into eating during Ramadan, his fast is broken; if coerced into eating another‘s property, he sins and must compensate (ḍaman) because he received the benefit. The text references various sources (Sharh al-Tahawi, al-Khulasa, al-Muhit) to show differences in detail, such as whether the coerced can seek reimbursement from the coercer depending on whether he benefited. This ruling demonstrates the Hanafi usul principle that complete coercion annuls valid choice, but the practical legal outcomes depend on the nature of the action and the possibility of agency. The discussion underscores the importance of distinguishing between actions that can and cannot be performed through a tool in determining legal liability.

Coercion and Choice: The Hanafi Definition of Ikrah
Explains Hanafi definition of coercion and its effect on choice.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle that coercion corrupts choice but does not eliminate it, distinguish between complete and incomplete coercion, and apply the attribution rule to determine liability.

Core Idea
Coercion corrupts choice but does not nullify it; attribution to the coercer determines liability.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, coercion (ikrah) is not a complete negation of human agency. Rather, it corrupts the quality of choice (ikhtiyar) while leaving its legal capacity intact. This lesson unpacks the definition, the condition of complete versus incomplete coercion, and the critical distinction between acts that can be attributed to the coercer versus those that become void. Understanding this principle is essential for correctly assigning responsibility in coerced actions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Foundational Principle
The passage begins by stating that coercion does not eliminate choice. Locate the Arabic phrase 'al-ikrahu la ya'damu al-ikhtiyara'. This is the core axiom: choice remains, but it is corrupt (yafsadu) and satisfaction (rida) is negated.
Contrast with the intuitive idea that coercion removes all responsibility.
2
Distinguish Complete vs. Incomplete Coercion
The text links completeness of coercion to permissibility of the coerced act. If the act is permitted (e.g., eating carrion under duress), coercion is complete (mulji') and indicates full excuse. If the act remains unlawful (e.g., murder, zina), coercion is incomplete and the coerced is still liable.
The permission of the act serves as evidence (dalil) of complete excuse.
3
Determine Attribution to the Coercer
When coercion is complete, the act is attributed to the coercer if possible (e.g., property destruction → coercer guarantees). If attribution is impossible (e.g., breaking a fast, which is inherently personal), the act is void and no one is held accountable; the coerced is treated like one who acted forgetfully.
Attribution requires the coercer to be the effective cause; personal acts cannot be transferred.
4
Apply the Principle to Examples
Work through the examples given: coercion into destroying property → coercer liable; coercion into breaking fast → fast void, no qada (like forgetful); coercion into murder or zina → coerced is punished (had/qisas) because the act is not permitted, proving incomplete coercion.
The examples illustrate the practical outcome of the definition.
5
Compare with the Shafi'i Position
The passage contrasts Hanafi with Shafi'i: the latter holds that coercion nullifies choice entirely, leading to total disregard of the coerced's dispositions. Recognize the thamarah al-khilaf in rulings on verbal acts like divorce or sale under coercion.
This contrast clarifies the distinctive Hanafi approach.
