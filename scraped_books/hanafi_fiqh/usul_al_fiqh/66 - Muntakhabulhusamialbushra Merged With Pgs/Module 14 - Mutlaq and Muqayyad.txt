Hanafi vs Shafi'i: Carrying Mutlaq to Muqayyad in Different Rulings
Dispute on carrying mutlaq to muqayyad in different rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that mutlaq is not carried to muqayyad when they appear in different rulings, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument based on analogy.

Core Idea
Hanafis apply mutlaq and muqayyad independently when rulings differ; Shafi'is carry them by genus analogy.

This lesson examines the usul al-fiqh dispute over whether an absolute term (mutlaq) is restricted by a qualified term (muqayyad) when they appear in different legal rulings. The Hanafi school maintains that each term operates independently because the Lawgiver may intend different scopes, while the Shafi'i school argues for analogy based on the shared genus. Understanding this difference is essential for deriving rulings from revealed texts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
State the dispute: when mutlaq and muqayyad appear in different rulings (e.g., expiation for killing vs. oath), should the absolute be carried to the qualified?
2
State the Shafi'i position and proof
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that the qualification (e.g., 'believing') acts like a condition, so its absence negates the ruling in all similar cases by analogy, because all expiations form one genus.
He relies on the principle that the understanding of a condition is evidence.
3
State the Hanafi position and proof
Hanafis argue that both texts can be applied independently because the Lawgiver may have intended expansion in one ruling and restriction in another. Example: freeing a believing slave for killing vs. any slave for oath.
Possibility of acting upon both prevents carrying.
4
Identify the thamarah (practical difference)
In practice, Hanafis do not require a believing slave for oath expiation, while Shafi'is do, because they carry the qualification from killing expiation.
5
Determine the tarjih (preferred view)
The Hanafi view is considered stronger because it respects both texts without forced interpretation.
This is the relied-upon position in Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage is an usul al-fiqh dispute regarding the interaction between absolute (mutlaq) and qualified (muqayyad) terms. The author presents the view of Imam al-Shafi'i that an absolute term should be restricted by a qualified term even when they appear in different legal incidents (like expiation for killing and other expiations). Al-Shafi'i argues that a qualification is analogous to a condition, and just as a condition negates the ruling in its absence, the qualified term should control all similar cases by analogy because they share the same genus (e.g., all expiations share the purpose of covering and deterring). The Hanafi position (the text says 'according to us') rejects this: if the absolute and qualified appear in different rulings, each is applied independently because the Lawgiver may have intended expansion in one and restriction in another. The author illustrates with the example of freeing a slave: in expiation for killing, the slave must be believing (qualified), while in expiation for an oath, the slave is absolute (no faith requirement). The Hanafis preserve both texts as they are, avoiding carrying one to the other. The passage concludes that even when the incident is the same but the rulings differ (as in the zihar example to follow), the absolute is not carried upon the restricted.

Key Terms
مطلق (mutlaq): A term applied to the essence (dhat) without any qualifying attribute, neither by negation nor affirmation.
مقيد (muqayyad): A term applied to the essence with an additional qualifying attribute (e.g., 'believing' restricts 'slave').
حقيقة شرعية (haqiqah shar'iyyah): Not explicitly defined here, but the principle of carrying relies on the legal understanding of terms as defined by the Lawgiver.

The Mutlaq-Muqayyad Principle Applied to Zihar Expiation
Analyzing when intercourse during the expiatory fast requires restarting.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that mutlaq is not carried upon muqayyad to a specific fiqh case involving zihar expiation.

Core Idea
In zihar expiation, fasting has a 'before touching' condition, but feeding does not — each proceeds independently.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text applies the key principle of mutlaq and muqayyad to a concrete fiqh dispute: the expiation for zihar. The ruling hinges on whether the Quranic condition 'before they touch' attaches to fasting and emancipation but not to feeding. By analyzing the text, we see how the Hanafi school derives different rulings for different expiation methods and why an intra-madhhab disagreement exists between Abu Hanifa/Muhammad and Abu Yusuf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case and the legal question
The scenario: a man declares zihar, chooses to expiate by fasting two consecutive months, and has intercourse with his wife during the fast. The key question: must he restart the fast?
2
Locate the Quranic evidence
The text cites Surah al-Mujadila (58:4): 'Fasting two consecutive months before they touch.' This verse attaches the phrase 'before they touch' to fasting. Similarly, emancipation has the same phrase. But the verse for feeding does not include this condition.
The verse for feeding is also in 58:4, but only says 'then feeding sixty poor persons.'
3
Understand the dispute
Abu Hanifa and Muhammad argue that since the condition 'before they touch' imposes both precedence and abstinence, any intercourse during the fast violates the condition, requiring restart. Abu Yusuf argues that partial fulfillment is better than none, so restarting is not needed.
Note that Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i hold the same view here.
4
Apply the mutlaq-muqayyad principle
The Hanafi principle: when a cause (zihar) has two rulings — one with a condition (fasting/emancipation: muqayyad) and one without (feeding: mutlaq) — each proceeds according to its own text. The mutlaq is not carried over to the muqayyad or vice versa.
This is a key usul rule: al-mutlaq yajri 'ala itlaqihi wa al-muqayyad 'ala taqyidihi.
5
Derive the final ruling
For fasting and emancipation: intercourse during the expiation requires restart (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad). For feeding: no restart required, by consensus of the three Hanafi imams. The distinction stems from the absence of the condition in the feeding verse.
Thus, the principle of mutlaq not being carried upon muqayyad directly determines the practical outcome.

Commentary
This passage is a fiqh ruling that applies the usul principle of mutlaq and muqayyad to the specific case of zihar expiation. The scenario: a man declares zihar and chooses to expiate by fasting two consecutive months or freeing a slave. If he has intercourse with his wife during the fast (at night, intentionally or forgetfully; during the day only forgetfully), what is the ruling? According to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad al-Shaybani, he must restart the entire fast because the Quranic condition 'before they touch' requires that the entire fast be completed before any sexual contact. They argue that the text implies two obligations: (1) the precedence of the fast over touching, and (2) abstinence from touching during the fast. If he fails in either, the fast is invalid. Abu Yusuf and Imam al-Shafi'i disagree: they hold that the condition is about the entire fast preceding the touching, but if he only fails partially, he should not restart because restarting would lose all progress. The passage then contrasts fasting/emancipation with feeding: if he chooses to feed sixty poor persons, intercourse during the feeding does not require restarting because the Quran does not mention the condition 'before they touch' for feeding. Thus feeding is an absolute term (no condition), while fasting and emancipation are qualified (with condition). Following the Hanafi principle, each proceeds independently, and the absolute is not carried upon the qualified.

مقيد (muqayyad): A qualified term, with an added condition or restriction.

The Hanafi Rule for Mutlaq and Muqayyad Sharing a Cause
Explaining the Hanafi rule for absolute and qualified texts in causes

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule for mutlaq and muqayyad texts when they enter upon the same cause, and determine whether to combine them or apply them independently.

Core Idea
When mutlaq and muqayyad share a cause (sabab), each text stands alone—no automatic restriction.

One of the most nuanced areas of Hanafi usul is the interaction between absolute (mutlaq) and qualified (muqayyad) texts. A common misconception is that the muqayyad always restricts the mutlaq. However, the Hanafi school makes a crucial exception: when both texts address the same cause (sabab), they are applied independently, each generating its own ruling. This lesson unpacks that rule through two key examples: zihar expiation and sadaqat al-fitr.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Cause and the Two Texts
First, locate the common cause (sabab) that triggers both the mutlaq and muqayyad texts. In the zihar example, the cause is the act of zihar; for sadaqat al-fitr, the cause is the head (the person). Then, note the two texts: one absolute, one qualified.
For zihar: Qur'an 58:4 (muqayyad with 'before they touch') vs. the feeding verse (mutlaq). For fitr: hadith with 'slave' (mutlaq) vs. 'Muslim slave' (muqayyad).
2
Determine if the Rulings Are the Same or Different
The next step is to see if the two texts command the same ruling (e.g., both require an expiatory act) or different rulings. In zihar, both fasting/emancipation and feeding are expiations, so rulings differ. In fitr, the ruling is identical: paying sadaqat al-fitr.
If rulings differ and one does not necessitate qualifying the other, no haml (feeding vs fasting example).
3
Check the Incident: Same or Different?
If the rulings are the same, check if the incident (al-haditha) is the same. In fitr, the incident is the same (obligation due to Ramadan). In the kaffarah of oath vs. killing, incidents differ. The Hanafi rule: if incident differs, no haml, contrary to al-Shafi'i.
If incident differs, the mutlaq and muqayyad remain independent.
4
If Incident Same: Is the Qualification in the Cause?
When the incident is the same, ask: does the qualification (qayd) relate to the cause (sabab) or to the ruling? In sadaqat al-fitr, the qualification 'Muslim' pertains to the cause (the head). In the Bedouin story, the qualification 'consecutive' pertains to the ruling (fasting).

Commentary
This passage explains a key Hanafi usul rule: when an absolute (mutlaq) and a qualified (muqayyad) text both apply to the same cause (sabab), they are not combined; each operates independently. The discussion begins with the example of kaffarah for zihar. A man who makes zihar must either free a slave, fast two months consecutively, or feed sixty poor, all before touching his wife (Quran 58:3-4). The text distinguishes between expiation by fasting/emancipation (where 'before touching' is mentioned) and feeding (where it is absent). For fasting and emancipation, the condition of 'being free from intercourse' is implied by the condition of 'preceding intercourse'. If intercourse occurs during the fast, Abu Hanifa and Muhammad say the fast must be restarted because both conditions are lost; Abu Yusuf and al-Shafi'i say no restart because only part of the preceding condition is lost. For feeding, all three Hanafi scholars agree no restart is needed because the condition of preceding is not mentioned. The second part uses sadaqat al-fitr as an example. One hadith says 'pay for every free and slave' (absolute), another says 'for every free and slave from the Muslims' (qualified). The ruling: both must be paid—for the Muslim slave due to the qualified text and for the non-Muslim slave due to the absolute text—because the cause (the head) is affected by both texts; there is no conflict. The passage then provides a detailed classification: (a) If rulings differ and one does not imply taqyid of the other, no haml (e.g., feed a man vs clothe a naked man). (b) If one implies taqyid, haml is required (e.g., free a slave vs do not free a disbelieving slave). (c) If the ruling is the same and negative, no haml (possible to obey both by not doing the act). (d) If the ruling is the same and positive, and the incident differs, no haml (Hanafi view, contrary to Shafi'i), as in kaffarah of oath vs kaffarah of killing. (e) If the incident is the same, and the absolute/qualified is in the cause, no haml (sadaqat al-fitr example). (f) If the incident is the same and the absolute/qualified is not in the cause, haml is obligatory (Bedouin story: fast 'two months' and 'two consecutive months' are combined). This rule ensures that each text is applied according to its own scope.

Key Terms
مطلق (muṭlaq): An expression that does not contain a qualifying restriction (qayd), such as 'شهرين' (two months) without 'متتابعين'
مقيد (muqayyad): An expression that includes a qualifying restriction, such as 'شهرين متتابعين' (two consecutive months)
سبب (sabab): The cause or occasion for a ruling, such as the 'head' (person) for sadaqat al-fitr, or the act of zihar for expiation
حمل (ḥaml): Carrying a mutlaq text to the meaning of a muqayyad text (i.e., restricting the absolute by the qualified)

Learning Objective

Core Idea

In Hanafi legal theory, time can play multiple roles for a commanded act. Prayer time is unique: it serves as a container (zarf), a condition (shart), and a cause (sabab). Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the rules governing the transfer of causality (sababiyya) in restricted commands (al-amr al-muqayyad), specifically regarding time-bound obligations like prayer. The author begins by stating that causality transfers to the last part of the time if the act is not performed in the first part. Four divisions are outlined: (1) performance connects to the first part, making it the cause; (2) performance connects to a later correct part; (3) time narrows and performance must be in deficient parts (e.g., the last part of Asr); (4) time expires and obligation attaches to the whole time because the impediment of commencement is removed. The text then explains why causality cannot be assigned to parts preceding the act—this would be a leap without proof. The discussion includes a dispute with Zufar regarding the measure of the deficient part: for the Hanafis, it is the time sufficient for the takbirat al-tahrimah (opening takbir), while Zufar requires time for four rak'ahs. The author further applies this to the status of the mukallaf: at the time when causality is determined, conditions like Islam, puberty, sanity, travel, and menstruation are considered. For example, if someone converts to Islam precisely at that deficient part, prayer becomes obligatory. The example of Fajr (complete time) vs. Asr (deficient end) illustrates that when the cause is complete (sound time), the obligation is complete.

Key Terms
السببية (al-sababiyya): The attribution of causality to a specific time part for the obligation of an act
التحريمة (al-tahrimah): The opening takbir (saying Allahu Akbar) that initiates prayer; the minimal action that constitutes commencement
الجزء الناقص (al-juz' al-naqis): The part of time that is insufficient to complete the full prayer but sufficient for takbirat al-tahrimah

Time as Standard and Cause: The Case of Fasting
Definition and rulings of time as standard and cause for fasting with intention details

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish time as a standard (mi'yar) from time as a container, apply the ruling that no other fast is valid during Ramadan, and explain the Hanafi position on a traveler's intention.

Core Idea
When time is both standard and cause, no other act of its kind is valid, and intention determines the fast's validity.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, time-bound acts (muqayyad) are divided into two types: time as container and time as standard. This lesson explores the second type—where time is the standard (mi'yar) and cause (sabab) for obligation, using the month of Ramadan as the primary example. We will see how this classification affects the permissibility of other fasts, the requirement of intention, and a key dispute concerning a traveling Muslim.

Guided Path
1
Define time as standard and cause
Identify the concept: time not only contains the act but measures it and is attributed to it, e.g., 'fast of Ramadan'. Contrast with time as mere container.
The key difference: container doesn't restrict other acts; standard prohibits other acts of the same kind.
2
Apply the ruling: no other fasting in Ramadan
Analyze the hadith 'When Sha'ban ends, there is no fasting except for Ramadan.' Understand how this proves that supererogatory and make-up fasts are impermissible during Ramadan.
This is a dalil for the exclusivity of Ramadan.
3
Examine intention rules for residents
See that a healthy resident who fasts with absolute intention (just 'I intend to fast') or even with a mistaken description (intending supererogatory) still has his fast count as Ramadan.
This is based on the idea that the time itself causes the act to be Ramadan.
4
Analyze the traveler's exception
According to Imam Abu Hanifa, a traveler who intends another obligatory fast (like make-up) during Ramadan, his fast counts for that intention, not Ramadan. Understand the reasoning: the traveler's license to break gives him a choice.
Contrast with the view of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) who hold it counts for Ramadan.
5
Connect to vowed fasts
Extend the concept: a vowed fast (nadhr) that is tied to a specific time also becomes like Ramadan—time is both standard and cause, so it becomes obligatory and no longer supererogatory.
This shows the generality of the principle beyond Ramadan.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the definition and rulings of the second type of muqayyad (restricted command) in usul al-fiqh, where time is made a standard (mi'yar) and cause (sabab) for the obligation of an act. The primary example is the time of fasting, specifically the month of Ramadan. The author explains that unlike the first type where time is merely a container (zarf), here time encompasses the act fully: it measures the act (length of fast increases with longer days) and is attributed to it (we say 'fast of Ramadan'). Consequently, no other fasting (like supererogatory or make-up) is permissible during Ramadan, corroborated by the hadith: 'When Sha'ban ends, there is no fasting except for Ramadan.' The passage then discusses the requirement of specific intention (niyyah al-ta'yin) for time-bound acts like prayer, but for fasting, a specific intention is not required for a healthy resident: if he fasts with absolute intention (just 'I intend to fast') or even mistakenly intends something else (like supererogatory), it counts as Ramadan fast. However, there is an exception for a traveler: according to Imam Abu Hanifa, if a traveler in Ramadan intends another obligatory fast (like make-up or expiation), his fast counts for that, not for Ramadan. This is because the traveler has the license to break his fast and thus can choose to fulfill another duty. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad (the Sahibayn) disagree, saying the fast counts for Ramadan because the month is present for him and the license is for ease; if he fasts without taking the license, it reverts to Ramadan. The text also notes two narrations regarding a traveler intending supererogatory fast. Finally, an analogy is drawn to vowed fasts (nadhr): a vowed fast in a specific time also becomes like Ramadan in that time is both standard and cause, so it is no longer supererogatory. The discussion emphasizes the Hanafi principle that time can be a cause for obligation and restricts other acts in that period.

Key Terms
نية التعين (niyyah al-ta'yin): The intention that specifies which particular act is being performed (e.g., intending to pray the noon prayer specifically).
مطلق الصوم (mutlaq al-sawm): Absolute intention to fast without specifying the type of fast (e.g., just 'I intend to fast').
الخطأ في الوصف (al-khata' fi al-wasf): Mistaking the description of the fast, e.g., intending it as supererogatory when it is Ramadan.
النذر المطلق (al-nadhr al-mutlaq): A vow to fast without specifying a particular time, e.g., 'I vow to fast a day'.

Defining Al-Hasan Li-Aynihi: The Three Acts and Their Ruling
Defining al-hasan li-aynihi through three examples and its legal consequence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-hasan li-aynihi according to Hanafi usul, identify the role of divine-created intermediaries, and state the ruling on the obligation (wujub) for such acts.

Core Idea
An act is good in itself when its apparent benefit is directly created by Allah; its obligation does not drop except by performance or an explicit dropping cause.

In this lesson, we examine a key usul distinction within al-hasan: the category of acts that are good in themselves (hasan li-aynihi) despite having external benefits. The classical Hanafi text gives three examples—zakah, fasting, and hajj—and explains why they are not considered good due to something else (hasan li-ghayrihi). Understanding this distinction is crucial for determining how obligations (wajibat) behave: once established, they remain binding until discharged or explicitly dropped.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three acts and their apparent negative aspects
List the acts: zakah, sawm, hajj. Note that each involves a loss (wealth, sustenance, effort) yet is considered good.
These are core acts of worship.
2
Recognize the intermediaries (wasait) that make them beneficial
For zakah, the need of the poor; for sawm, subduing the ego; for hajj, the honor of the place. These appear to be the source of goodness.
Intermediaries are external factors.
3
Determine the origin of those intermediaries
According to the text, these intermediaries are purely created by Allah; humans have no choice in their existence. For example, the poor's need is divinely decreed.
This is the key criterion.
4
Understand the annexation (ilhaq) to hasan li-aynihi
Because the intermediaries are divine creations, they are considered as if they do not intervene. Thus the acts are annexed to the category of goodness for themselves.
Contrast with hasan li-ghayrihi where intermediaries are human-chosen.
5
Note the ruling: wujub does not drop except by performance or a specific dropping cause
Once obligation is established, it remains until the act is performed or a cause like menstruation (hayd) explicitly drops it. This applies to all acts classified as hasan li-aynihi.
This distinguishes them from acts that are hasan li-ghayrihi.

Two Types of Hasan li-Ghayrihi
Classifying acts that are good due to something else into two types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two types of hasan li-ghayrihi, distinguish them using examples, and apply the classification to new cases.

Core Idea
Acts good due to something else are of two types: goodness after or by the act itself.

In Hanafi usul, acts commanded by the Lawgiver may be good either intrinsically (hasan li-aynihi) or due to an external meaning (hasan li-ghayrihi). This lesson unpacks the second category, showing how the external good is achieved—either through a subsequent intentional act or immediately by the commanded act itself. This distinction is crucial for understanding the nature of obligations like wudu, jihad, and funeral prayer.

Guided Path
1
Define hasan li-ghayrihi
Identify the definition: an act that is good not in itself but due to a meaning outside it. In Hanafi usul, this applies when the intermediaries are by human choice.
Contrast with hasan li-aynihi where the act is good in itself.
2
Identify the two subtypes
The passage divides hasan li-ghayrihi into two types: (1) the external good is achieved after the act by another intentional act, (2) the external good is achieved directly by the act itself.
Type 1: acts like wudu and going to Jumu'ah; Type 2: acts like funeral prayer, jihad, and hudud.
3
Analyze the examples
For each type, examine why the act is not good in itself and how the external good is obtained. Note the intermediaries (like prayer, Jumu'ah, fulfilling a right) and whether they require an additional act.
Wudu in itself is just cleaning; its goodness for prayer requires a separate prayer act. Funeral prayer inherently fulfills the right of the deceased.
4
Determine the role of intermediaries
Recognize that in both types, the intermediaries (e.g., the state of the deceased, the act of prayer) are by human action and choice, which is why the acts are classified as hasan li-ghayrihi.
If the intermediary were not by human choice, the act might be hasan li-aynihi.

بعده بفعل مقصود إلخ : يعنى النوع الأول هو ما لا يتأدّى الغير الذي حسن المأمور به لأجله بنفس فعل المأمور به، بل لا بد أن يوجد بعد المأمور به بفعل آخر مقصود كالوضوء والسعي فالوضوء في نفسه تدبير وتنظيف للأعضاء وإضاعة للماء، وإنما صار حسناً لأجل الصلاة، والصلاة لا تحصل بنفس الوضوء بل لا بد لها من فعل آخر قصداً توجـ توجد به الصلاة وكذا السعي إلى الجمعة عبارة عن المشي ونقل الإقدام، وليس في ذاته حسن، وإنما حسن لإقامة الجمعة، وهي لا تحصل بمجرد المشي بل بفعل آخر بعده. وما يحصل المعنى إلخ: يعني النوع الثاني ما يتأدّى الغير الذي حسن المأمور به لأجله بنفس فعل المأمور به، لا بفعل آخر بعده كالصلاة على الميت : فإنها في نفسها بدعة مشابهة بعبادة الأصنام، وإنما حسنت لقضاء حق المسلم وهو يحصل بمجرد الصلاة لا يحتاج إلى فعل آخر بعدها. والجهاد فإنه في نفسه تعذيب عباد الله وتخريب بلاده، وإنما صار حسناً لأجل إعلاء كلمة الله والإعلاء يحصل بمجرد الجهاد لا يحتاج إلى فعل آخر بعده.
وإقامة الحدود فإنها في الظاهر تعذيب، وإنما حسنت لزجر الناس عن المعاصي، وهو يحصل بمجرد إقامة الحدود لا يحتاج إلى فعل آخر بعده قضاء حق أي حق المسلم كما في صلاة الجنازة بنفس الفعل كما عرفت آنفا، ولما كانت هذه الوسائط وهي إسلام الميت وكفر الكافر، وهتك حرمة المناهي في النوع الثاني، والصلاة
والجمعة في النوع الأول بفعل العباد واختيارهم اعتبرت هذه الوسائط وجعلت داخلة في الحسن لغيره.
Section on the attribute of goodness of the commanded act, and it is the second division. That which is good due to a meaning in something else is of two types: one where the meaning is obtained after it by an intentional act, like wudu and going to Jumu'ah, and the other where the meaning is obtained by the very act of the commanded thing, like the funeral prayer, jihad, and establishing hudud. For the goodness in them, like fulfilling a right, subduing Allah's enemies, and deterring from sins, is obtained by the act itself. ... It (the obligation) drops with the dropping of that other and remains with its permanence. What is meant by 'wajib' is that which is established by a cause, but since the cause is known by the command, it is valid to attribute what is established by it to the command by its intermediary. So the objection is repelled: if what is meant by 'wajib' is that which is established in the conscience by a cause, which is the obligation itself, then his statement 'or by encountering something that drops it specifically' is valid because it may drop after obligation by incidental events in the time, but mentioning it here is inappropriate because the context is explaining the goodness of what is established by command, not the goodness of what is established by cause. And if what is meant is that which is established by command, which is the obligation of performance, then his statement 'or by encountering something that drops it' is not valid, because the obligation of performance after its establishment does not drop by an incident. 'After it by an intentional act, etc.' means the first type is that where the other for which the commanded act is good is not attained by the very act of the commanded thing, but must occur after the commanded act by another intentional act, like wudu and going to Jumu'ah. Wudu in itself is a disposal and cleaning of limbs and wasting water, but it became good for the sake of prayer, and prayer is not attained by the wudu itself, but must have another intentional act by which prayer is performed. Similarly, going to Jumu'ah is walking and moving feet, and not good in itself, but good for establishing Jumu'ah, and it is not attained by mere walking but by another act after it. 'And what obtains the meaning, etc.' means the second type is that where the other for which the commanded act is good is attained by the very act of the commanded thing, not by another act after it, like the funeral prayer: it is in itself an innovation resembling idol worship, but it became good for fulfilling the right of the Muslim, which is attained by the mere prayer and does not need another act after it. Jihad: in itself it is tormenting Allah's servants and destroying his lands, but it became good for exalting Allah's word, and exaltation is attained by mere jihad and does not need another act after it. Establishing hudud: in appearance it is torment, but it became good for deterring people from sins, which is attained by mere establishment of hudud and does not need another act after it. Fulfilling a right, i.e., the right of the Muslim as in funeral prayer, by the very act as understood earlier. Since these intermediaries—the Islam of the deceased, the disbelief of the disbeliever, and the violation of the sanctity of prohibitions in the second type, and prayer and Jumu'ah in the first type—are by the action and choice of the servants, these intermediaries are considered and made included in goodness due to something else.

Commentary
This passage defines the second major division of good acts: al-hasan li-ghayrihi (good due to something else). The commentator divides this into two types. The first type includes acts where the external goodness (the intended meaning) is achieved after the commanded act by another intentional act. Examples are wudu (which is good for prayer, but prayer requires a separate act) and going to Jumu'ah (which is good for the Friday prayer, but the prayer requires a separate act). The second type includes acts where the external goodness is achieved directly by the commanded act itself, without needing a subsequent act. Examples are the funeral prayer (which fulfills the right of the deceased by its very performance), jihad (which exalts Allah's word by its very act), and establishing hudud (which deters from sins by its very act). The passage then addresses a potential objection about the earlier ruling on obligation, clarifying that the context is acts established by command, not by cause. Finally, it notes that in both types of hasan li-ghayrihi, the intermediaries (such as the state of the deceased, the disbelief of the enemy, or the act of prayer) are by human choice, so the acts are rightly classified as good due to something else.

Key Terms
الحسن لغيره (al-hasan li-ghayrihi): Goodness of an act due to an external meaning, not intrinsic to the act itself.
النوع الأول (al-naw' al-awwal): The first type: external good achieved after the act by another intentional act.
النوع الثاني (al-naw' al-thani): The second type: external good achieved by the act itself without subsequent act.
الوسائط بفعل العباد (al-wasa'it bi-fi'l al-'ibad): Intermediaries that are by human action and choice, making the act hasan li-ghayrihi.

Key Takeaway
Hasan li-ghayrihi is divided into two types based on whether the external good is achieved after the act (type 1) or by the act itself (type 2).
