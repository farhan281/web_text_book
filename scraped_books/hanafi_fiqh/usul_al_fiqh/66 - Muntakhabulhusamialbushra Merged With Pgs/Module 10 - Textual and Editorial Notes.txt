Sarih and Kinayah: Explicit and Implicit Expressions
Defining explicit and implicit speech and their legal effects

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish explicit (ṣarīḥ) from implicit (kināyah) speech and apply the rule that explicit speech is effective without intention while implicit speech requires intention or circumstantial evidence.

Core Idea
Explicit speech binds legally without intent; implicit speech needs intention or context to take effect.

In Hanafi legal theory, the classification of speech as explicit or implicit determines how much weight the speaker's intention carries. This passage from a classical uṣūl al-fiqh text defines both categories and illustrates them with examples from oaths and divorce, highlighting disagreements between schools. Understanding this distinction is foundational for deriving rulings from texts and for evaluating speech acts in legal contexts.

Guided Path
1
Define explicit (ṣarīḥ) speech.
Identify the definition given: speech whose intended meaning is clearly apparent, whether ḥaqīqah or majāz. Its ruling attaches to the word itself without needing intention.
The text gives examples: 'I sold', 'I bought'.
2
Define implicit (kināyah) speech.
Identify the definition: speech whose intended meaning is hidden, requiring intention (niyyah) or circumstantial evidence (dalālat al-ḥāl) to be legally effective.
The text compares it to majāz before it becomes conventionally recognized.
3
Analyze the example of 'meat' (laḥm) in oaths.
The word 'meat' is explicit for land animals according to its linguistic derivation. The Qur'an calls fish 'meat' in Sūrat al-Naḥl, but the Ḥanafīs consider that a figurative usage, so an oath to avoid meat is not broken by eating fish unless intended. The Shāfiʿī and Mālikī schools consider the Qur'anic usage as making the word explicit for fish, breaking the oath.
This shows how the classification affects real fiqh rulings.
4
Examine the example of 'fruit' (fākihah).
Grapes and fresh dates are staple foods, not mere fruits, so according to Abū Ḥanīfa they are not included in an oath to avoid fruits unless the swearer intended them. His two companions and al-Shāfiʿī disagree, classifying them as fruits.
This again demonstrates the role of convention and intention.
5
Apply the distinction to divorce vocabulary.
Words like 'bā'in' (separated) and 'ḥarām' (forbidden) are known in meaning but ambiguous as to what they refer to, so they are kināyah metaphorically and require intention to effect irrevocable divorce. The word 'i'taddī' is an exception implying revocable divorce.
The practical consequence: intention determines the type of divorce.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the types of linguistic usage (الصريح والكناية) in Islamic legal theory. It begins by defining الصريح (explicit) speech as any utterance whose intended meaning is immediately clear, whether it is حقيقة (literal) or مجاز (figurative). Explicit speech has the legal effect that the ruling attaches to the word itself, without requiring the speaker's intention (النية). The author gives the example of a slip of the tongue: if someone intends to say 'Praise be to Allah' but instead says 'You are divorced', the divorce takes effect judicially regardless of his intention. This is because the words are صريح in divorce. In contrast, الكناية (implicit) speech is defined as an utterance whose intended meaning is hidden, requiring the speaker's intention or circumstantial evidence (دلالة الحال) to give it legal effect. The passage then applies these definitions to two controversial fiqh examples. First, the word 'meat' (لحم) in an oath: according to Abu Hanifa, if someone swears not to eat meat, he does not break his oath by eating fish because the Arabic root of 'meat' implies blood and strength, which fish lack. However, Imam Malik and al-Shafi'i argue that the Qur'an calls fish meat in Surah An-Nahl:14, so the oath is broken. Second, the word 'fruit' (فاكهة): Abu Hanifa holds that grapes and fresh dates are not classified as fruit because they are staple foods in some regions, so an oath to avoid fruit is not broken by them, unless the oath-taker specifically intended them. His two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) and al-Shafi'i hold that they are fruit and break the oath. The passage concludes with a discussion of why divorce words like 'bai'in' (intermediately divorced) are called كناية metaphorically: their meaning is clear (separation), but it is ambiguous whether it refers to separation from marriage or something else, so intention is needed. This makes them produce irrevocable divorce (طلاق بائن) except for the word 'اعتدى' (observe a waiting period) and similar phrases that imply revocable divorce due to the implicit presence of the word 'divorce'.

Key Terms
الصريح (al-ṣarīḥ): An utterance whose intended meaning is clearly apparent, whether ḥaqīqah or majāz, and which is effective in law without requiring the speaker's intention.
الكناية (al-kināyah): An utterance whose intended meaning is hidden, requiring the speaker's intention or circumstantial evidence to be legally effective.
النية (al-niyyah): The speaker's intention required to give legal effect to implicit (كنائي) speech.

Literal vs. Metaphorical Waw: Conjunction and State in Legal Interpretation
Textual analysis of waw as conjunction vs. hal

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when waw functions as waw al-hal in a legal statement and apply the correct ruling (condition vs. conjunction) using Hanafi usul principles.

Core Idea
Waw can be metaphorical for hal, making a condition out of a clause.

The Arabic particle waw is grammatically used for conjunction (al-‘aṭf), but in legal language it often indicates a concurrent state (al-ḥāl). Hanafi usul recognizes this metaphorical usage, which has direct impact on how obligations are conditioned. This lesson examines the evidence for waw al-ḥāl and its application in legal statements, using Quranic and classical examples.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish literal conjunction from metaphorical hal
Start by understanding the literal function of waw as a conjunction (عطف) that simply joins two items in a shared action. Then note that when this literal meaning leads to logical or legal absurdity, waw is interpreted metaphorically as a circumstantial clause (حال), meaning 'while' or 'whereas'.
The passage states that two beings cannot share one identical act, so waw in 'Zaid and Amr came' implies separate comings.
2
Identify the Quranic evidence for waw al-hal
The passage cites Q 39:73: 'Until when they come to it and its gates are opened.' Here, the waw cannot be simple conjunction because the opening of gates occurs simultaneously with coming, not sequentially. So it must be waw al-ḥāl: 'while its gates are open.' Recognize that this is a metaphorical use based on the shared notion of combination (جمع).
The metaphorical transfer is similar to calling a courageous man 'lion'.
3
Analyze the legal examples: slave and harbi
Consider the master's statement: 'Pay a thousand dirhams and you are free.' If waw were conjunction, it would mean simultaneous payment and freedom, which is legally ineffective (the slave cannot pay while a slave). If waw is hal, it means 'Be free while you are paying,' making payment a condition for freedom. Similarly, 'Descend and you are safe' means safety is conditional upon descending.
These examples show that waw al-ḥāl imposes a condition (شرط) on the main action.
4
Examine the objection and the Hanafi response
An objection arises: If waw is hal, and hal acts like a condition, then in 'Pay and you are free,' payment would depend on freedom, not freedom on payment. The Hanafi response uses inversion (قلب): the intended meaning is 'Be free while you are paying,' i.e., the hal clause qualifies the subject's state, reversing the apparent dependency. Another response is that conjunction would be grammatically invalid (conjoining imperative with declarative) leading to absurdity.
The inversion is like 'I presented the camel to the watering trough' meaning the opposite.
5
Apply the principle to a new case
Given this understanding, test a similar statement: 'Sell this for me and you are my agent.' Determine whether waw is conjunction (you are my agent from now) or hal (you are my agent while selling). Use the criteria: if literal conjunction renders the statement legally problematic, interpret waw as hal.
Hanafi fuqaha apply this principle to many contracts and oaths.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of the particle waw (واو) used in Arabic grammar and legal language. It begins by stating that in a sentence like 'Zaid and Amr came to me,' the waw is used for conjunction, but strictly speaking, two persons cannot share one identical act of coming, so the waw here implies separate comings. The main focus is on the metaphorical use of waw to indicate a concurrent state (hal). The author provides Quranic evidence from Surah Az-Zumar (39:73): 'Until when they come to it and its gates are opened' where waw means 'while', not 'and'. Legal examples include a master saying to his slave 'Pay a thousand dirhams and you are free' and to a harbi 'Descend and you are safe'. In these, the waw is interpreted as denoting the state, so that freedom is conditional upon payment and safety upon descent, not simultaneous. The passage then addresses an objection: if the waw indicates a state, which is like a condition, it would cause a logical reversal where the payment depends on freedom. The answer is that this is a case of inversion (qalb), similar to the Arabic expression 'I presented the camel to the watering trough' actually meaning 'I presented the watering trough to the camel'. So the intended meaning is 'Be free, while you are paying' and 'Be safe, while you are descending'. Another reason given is that interpreting waw as conjunction would be syntactically problematic because it would conjoin a declarative (you are free) to an imperative (pay), which is not standard and would lead to absurdity. Thus, necessity dictates the waw be for the state. This analysis is crucial for understanding how linguistic particles affect legal rulings in Hanafi fiqh.

Key Terms
واو العطف (wāw al-‘aṭf): The particle waw used for conjunction, connecting two items in a shared action or attribute.
واو الحال (wāw al-ḥāl): The waw used to indicate a concurrent state (hal), meaning 'while' or 'whereas'.
حال (ḥāl): A circumstantial clause describing the state of the subject at the time of the action.
قلب (qalb): Inversion, a rhetorical device where the order of elements is reversed in meaning to resolve a logical paradox.

The Dual Function of Fa: Immediate Sequence and Causation
Explaining the primary and secondary uses of fa'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between fa al-ta'qib (immediate sequence) and fa al-ta'lil (causation with permanent causes) and apply this distinction to conditional legal statements.

Core Idea
Fa connects instantly, but with permanent causes it lingers to indicate delay.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the particle fa is central to interpreting conditional legal statements. While its primary role is to signal immediate sequence (ta'qib) between two actions, it can also convey causation (ta'lil) when the cause is ongoing. This lesson unpacks both uses through examples of divorce, manumission, and Quranic evidence, showing how a single particle can shift the temporal relationship between conditions and their effects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary meaning
Recognize that fa fundamentally means 'and then immediately' (li al-wasl wa al-ta'qib) — the conjoined action follows the first without delay.
This is the default rule; any departure must be justified.
2
Learn the exception: fa on permanent causes
When fa enters upon a cause that is permanent (illa da'ima), it indicates delay and causation, similar to lam al-ta'lil. The permanence allows the cause to be considered as lagging behind the effect's beginning.
Example: 'Rejoice, for help has come' — help is an ongoing cause.
3
Apply to a conditional divorce
In 'If you enter this house then this house you are divorced', the condition requires entry into the second house immediately after the first. Any delay invalidates the conditional divorce.
This exemplifies fa al-ta'qib.
4
Apply to a manumission statement
In 'Pay a thousand dirhams and you are free' (ad ilayya alfan fa anta hurr), fa links freedom to payment. Using the concept of estimated states (ahwal muqaddara), freedom is deemed to occur at the moment of payment, not before.
This exemplifies fa al-ta'lil with a temporary cause, relying on estimation.
5
Evaluate the Quranic evidence
Verse 39:73 'So enter it, eternally abiding' (فَادْخُلُوهَا خَالِدِينَ) uses fa with an imperative. The hal 'khalidin' is an estimated state, supporting the Hanafi principle that fa can be followed by a state that is not actual but estimated.
This proof is used to justify the manumission analysis.

Thumma: The Scope of Tarākhī in Hanafi Usul
Analyzing thumma's scope of delay with intra-madhhab dispute and divorce examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the two Hanafi positions on thumma and apply them correctly in a conditional divorce case.

Core Idea
Thumma can either sever speech completely or merely delay effect — changing how many divorces fall.

The Arabic particle thumma (ثم) is commonly understood to mean 'then', but its precise legal implication has been debated within the Hanafi school. This lesson examines the disagreement between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two senior students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, regarding the nature of the delay (tarākhī) indicated by thumma. We will see how each position leads to different rulings in conditional divorce (ṭalāq mu‘allaq), a key area where this dispute has practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Define thumma and tarākhī
Understand that thumma is for conjunction with delay. The key term is tarākhī (delay).
Tarākhī can be temporal or conceptual.
2
Identify the two positions
Abu Hanifa: delay is complete, effectively restarting the sentence. Sahibayn: delay is only in existence, not in the speech itself.
This difference turns on whether the speech is severed.
3
Apply to conditional divorce (case 1)
Non-consummated wife, condition delayed: 'You are divorced, then divorced, then divorced if you enter the house.' Abu Hanifa: only first falls; Sahibayn: all conditional, fall in order if condition occurs.
The 'mahall' (place) for divorce is lost after first for non-consummated.
4
Apply to conditional divorce (case 2)
Condition advanced: 'If you enter, you are divorced, then divorced, then divorced.' Abu Hanifa: first suspended, second falls immediately, third void. Sahibayn: all conditional on condition, fall in order.
Trace the logic: suspension vs immediate effect.
5
Recognize metaphorical use
When literal delay is impossible, thumma is used metaphorically as waw (and), as in Quran 90:11-18.
Faith is prior to good deeds, so thumma cannot imply literal delay.

الاتصال في الكلام، ولما وقع الأول لم يبق المحل لما بعده؛ إذ المرأة غير مدخولة، فيلغو ما بعده ضرورةً كما إذا وجد السكوت حقيقة. هذا إذا أخر الشرط، ولو قدّم الشرط بأن قال: إن دخلت الدار فأنت طالق ثم طالق ثم طالق، تعلّق الأول بالشرط ووقع الثاني في الحال لبقاء المحل؛ إذ الأوّل تعلق بالشرط والمعلق لا ينزل في المحل، ولغا الثالث لعدم المحل. وفائدة تعلق الأول بالشرط أنه إذا نكحها ثانياً ووجد الشرط يقع الطلاق بسبب التعليق السابق. فإن قيل: إذا قلتم بالتراخي في التكلم حكماً انقطع الكلام الأول عن الثاني كأنه سكت حقيقةً ثم استأنف، فعلى هذا صار قوله: "ثم طالق" = خبراً بلا مبتدأ، وهو لا يفيد شيئاً، فلزم أن يلغو الثاني أيضاً. قلتُ: هب ولكن الاتصال بين المعطوف والمعطوف عليه صورة باق، وهو وإن لم يكف التعليق بالشرط الأول؛ لأنه أمر زائد مبني على الاتصال صورة ومعنى ولكن يكفي لصحة العطف لتقدير المبتدء؛ لأنه ضروري في الكلام. هذا في غير المدخول بها، وأما المدخول بها فإن قدّم الجزاء يقع الأول والثاني في الحال عليها؛ لأنها محل لهما، وتعلق الثالث بالشرط، فكأنه سكت على الأولين ثم قال: أنتِ طالق إن دخلت الدار، وإن قدّم الشرط تعلق الأول بالشرط ووقع الثاني والثالث كأنه سكت على الأول ثم تكلم بالأخيرين، وهي محل لهما. جملة: إذ لا فصل في التكلم فيتعلق الكل بالشرط، سواء قدّم الشرط أو أخر ولكن في وقت الوقوع على الترتيب لوجود التراخي في الوجود، أي كلمة "ثم" للعطف بصفة التراخي فلوجود معنى العطف والوصل في الكلام يتعلّق الكل بالشرط، ولوجود معنى التراخي يقع مرتباً، فإن كانت موطوءةً يقع الثالث وإلا يقع الأول فقط ويلغو الثاني والثالث بفوات المحل بالبينونة، كذا قيل. وقالا رحمهما الله: يتعلقن جملة وينزلن على الترتيب. وقد يستعار لمعنى الواو. قال الله تعالى: ثم كان من الذين آمنوا. وصدر الآية: فَلَا اقْتَحَمَ As for 'thumma', it is for conjunction with delay. According to Abu Hanifa, the delay is definitive, as if the speech is restarted anew, with complete delay. According to his two companions, the delay is in existence only, not in speech. Its clarification is in the case of a man who says to his non-consummated wife: 'You are divorced, then divorced, then divorced if you enter the house.' Abu Hanifa says: The first falls, and what follows is void, as if he kept silent after the first and then restarted speech. So the first falls because the beginning of speech does not depend on its end, even though a modifier exists, due to the loss of the condition of dependency, which is connection in speech. Since the first fell, no place remains for what follows, as the woman is not consummated, so what follows is void of necessity, just as if there were actual silence. This is if the condition is delayed. If the condition is advanced, saying 'If you enter the house, you are divorced, then divorced, then divorced,' then the first is suspended by the condition, and the second falls immediately due to the remaining place, because the first is suspended and a suspended matter does not descend into the place, and the third is void for lack of place. The benefit of the first being suspended is that if he marries her again and the condition occurs, the divorce falls due to the prior suspension. If it is said: You (Abu Hanifa) say that the delay is in speech in terms of ruling, so the first speech is cut off from the second as if he actually kept silent and then restarted; thus his saying 'then divorced' becomes a predicate without a subject, which conveys nothing, so the second must also be void. I (the commentator) say: Granted, but the connection between the conjoined and the conjoined-to remains in form, and although it does not suffice for suspension by the first condition because it is an extra matter built on connection in both form and meaning, it suffices for the validity of conjunction by supposing a subject, as it is necessary in speech. This applies to non-consummated wives. As for a consummated wife, if the apodosis is advanced, the first and second fall immediately on her because she is a place for both, and the third is suspended by the condition. It is as if he kept silent after the first two and then said: 'You are divorced if you enter the house.' If the condition is advanced, the first is suspended by the condition, and the second and third fall as if he kept silent after the first and then spoke the latter two, and she is a place for both. Summary: Since there is no separation in speech, all are connected to the condition, whether the condition is advanced or delayed, but in terms of occurrence they are ordered due to the existence of delay in existence. That is, the word 'thumma' is for conjunction with the attribute of delay; due to the presence of the meaning of conjunction and connection in speech, all are connected to the condition, and due to the presence of the meaning of delay, they occur in order. If she is a consumnated, the third falls; otherwise only the first falls, and the second and third are void due to the loss of the place by separation. Thus it is said. The two (companions), may Allah have mercy on them, said: They (the divorces) are all connected jointly and descend in order. And it may be used metaphorically in the sense of 'waw'. Allah Most High said: 'Then he became among those who believed.' The beginning of the verse: 'But he has not broken through the difficult pass. And what will make you know what the difficult pass is? (It is) freeing a slave, or feeding on a day of severe hunger, an orphan of near relationship, or a needy person in misery; then he became among those who believed' (90:11-18). In this context, it is impossible to apply 'thumma' in its true sense, because if it were applied, it would require that freeing slaves and feeding be considered before faith, which is corrupt, because faith is the foundation of all acts of obedience and the head of all worship, and the foundation comes first. This is understood from many verses indicating that faith is a condition for deeds. Allah said: 'Indeed, those who have believed and done righteous deeds' (2:277). There are various interpretations of this verse, which we have omitted for fear of lengthening. As for 'bala', it is placed to affirm what follows and to disregard what precedes. It is said: 'Zaid came, bala Amr', meaning the conjoined is Amr. And they all (the three imams) said concerning one who said to his non-consummated wife: 'If you enter the house, you are divorced once, no bala twice' = a predicate without a subject, which conveys nothing, so the second must also be void. If the condition is advanced, the first is suspended by the condition, and the second and third fall as if he kept silent after the first and then spoke the latter two, and she is a place for both. If she is consumnated, the third falls; otherwise only the first falls, and the second and third are void due to the loss of the place by separation. Thus it is said. For the meaning of 'waw': When it is impossible to work with its reality. The aspect of the metaphor is the connection between them in the meaning of conjunction. 'Waw' is for absolute gathering, and 'thumma' is for gathering with delay. By that appropriateness, when delay is impossible, it is used metaphorically in the sense of 'waw' from 'among those who believed' etc. To affirm what follows, i.e., the conjoined, by way of rectification of error. 'Zaid came, bala Amr' means: I was mistaken in saying (that) Zaid came.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul work analyzes two conjunctive particles: thumma (then) and bala (but/nay). The author first defines thumma as indicating conjunction with delay (tarākhī). The key legal dispute concerns the scope of this delay. According to Abu Hanifa, thumma implies a complete delay in both the ruling (ḥukm) and the speech (takallum) itself, meaning the speaker effectively restarts the sentence. His two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, hold that the delay is only in the existential effect (wujūd) of the conjoined items, while the speech remains connected. This difference has practical consequences in conditional divorce (ṭalāq mu‘allaq). The author illustrates with detailed examples of a man saying 'You are divorced, then divorced, then divorced if you enter the house' to a non-consummated wife. Abu Hanifa rules only the first divorce falls; the rest are void because the speech was cut off and the 'place' (mahall) for divorce is lost after the first. The companions argue that since the speech is continuous, all three are conditional on the condition, but due to the delay in existence, they fall in order if the condition occurs. The passage then discusses the metaphorical use of thumma in the sense of waw (and) when the literal meaning of delay is impossible, citing Quran 90:11-18 where freeing slaves and feeding the needy are mentioned before faith, yet faith is logically prior. Then the author turns to bala, defining it as a particle that affirms what follows and disregards what precedes. It is used to correct a mistake (tadāruk). The example given is a man saying to his wife: 'You are divorced once, no bala twice.' The three imams agree that this construction is invalid because 'once' is a predicate without a subject, rendering the second number meaningless. The author then repeats the same discussion about conditional divorce, possibly to show the application of the same principles to bala. The passage underscores the Hanafi approach to linguistic particles as legal causes with specific effects, and shows how intra-madhhab differences arise from different interpretations of the scope of those particles.

Bala: The Particle of Rectification in Hanafi Usul
Analyzing bala as a particle of rectification with conditions and divorce example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the definition of bala and identify the condition for its valid use in legal contexts.

Core Idea
Bala affirms what follows and discards the prior, but the second part must be a complete sentence.

The particle bala (بل) serves a unique function in Arabic: it turns away from a previous statement and rectifies it with a new one. This lesson explains the Hanafi classification of bala as a particle of iḍrāb (turning away) and tadāruk (rectification). It also discusses a crucial condition: the subsequent statement must be a full sentence with a subject and predicate; otherwise, the construction becomes invalid, as illustrated in a conditional divorce example.

Guided Path
1
Define bala and its function
Bala is placed to affirm what follows and disregard what precedes, used for rectification of a mistake.
It corrects the error by discarding the first statement.
2
Identify the condition for validity
The second part after bala must be a complete sentence with a subject and predicate; otherwise it is meaningless.
This condition emerges from the divorce example.
3
Apply to the divorce example
Husband says: 'You are divorced once, no bala twice.' The phrase 'twice' is just a number without a subject, so the construction fails.
All three imams agreed on this invalidity.
4
Contrast with valid usage
A valid example: 'Zaid came, bala Amr' – where 'Amr' is understood as the subject of the implied verb, forming a complete sentence in meaning.
The second part must be interpretable as a full predicate.

This lesson examines a subtle yet powerful instance of majaz in Hanafi usul: the reinterpretation of the conjunctive particle 'aw' (or) as 'ghaya' (until) when it joins a negative and affirmative clause in an oath. The ruling illustrates how context (qarinah) can force a metaphorical reading when the literal conjunction leads to absurdity. Understanding this principle is key to mastering Hanafi textual analysis.

Interpreting 'Hatta' in Hanafi Usul: Ghaya, Causation, and Conjunction
Analysis of the particle 'hatta' and its metaphorical uses in Hanafi usul al-fiqh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three meanings of the particle 'hatta' in Hanafi usul and determine which meaning applies based on the contextual relationship of the two actions.

Core Idea
Hatta shifts between limit, cause, and conjunction — context decides.

The particle 'hatta' is a versatile tool in Arabic, but its legal interpretation demands precision. In this lesson, we examine how Hanafi usul scholars distinguish its three possible meanings: ghaya (limit), lam kay (causation), and pure conjunction (atf). By analyzing examples from Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani's al-Ziyadat, you will learn the contextual clues that trigger each interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two actions linked by 'hatta'
Isolate the action before and after hatta, noting who performs each. For example, in 'beat you until you scream', the beating is by the speaker, screaming by the addressee.
2
Check if the second action can serve as the ghaya of the first
If the second action is a natural endpoint of the first (e.g., screaming as a limit to beating), then hatta is ghaya. This is the literal/default meaning.
3
If not ghaya, assess causation and agent identity
If the second action cannot be an endpoint but could be a cause or result, and the two actions are performed by different persons, hatta is interpreted as lam kay (causation). If both actions are by the same person, causation is rejected, and hatta becomes a conjunction.
One's own act cannot be a recompense for another of his acts (as per the passage).
4
For conjunction, prefer 'fa' over 'wa'
Hanafi scholars prefer interpreting hatta as 'fa' (immediate succession) over 'wa' (simple conjunction) because ghaya implies sequentiality, and 'fa' matches that better.
There is a legitimate ikhtilaf; the author deems fa as awjah (more sound).

Commentary
This passage is a detailed textual analysis of the particle 'hatta' and its multiple meanings in Hanafi usul. Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, one preposition carries distinct legal force—obligation, condition, or attachment—based on context.

This passage shows how the humble preposition 'alaa (على) holds multiple legal meanings. Hanafi jurists, unlike grammarians, expand its usage to achieve precision in contracts and oaths.
