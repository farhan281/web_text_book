Transactional Restrictions and the Intra-Hanafi Dispute on Insolvency
Explains transactional rules for the terminally ill and the intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two categories of transactions (rescindable vs suspended) for a terminally ill patient and evaluate the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding the slave's indemnity.

Core Idea
A patient's transactions are valid subject to rescission after death; manumission is suspended like a mudabbar. The Sahibayn differ on the slave case due to a different qiyas.

Building on the rule that illness does not negate capacity but requires guardianship, this lesson explores the specific rulings for transactions made during terminal illness. We will analyze the distinction between transactions that admit rescission and those that are suspended, and then dive into a classic intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn concerning the fate of a slave when the master becomes insolvent after choosing ransom.

Guided Path
1
Recall the principle of hajr
Hajr on the patient's wealth is to protect creditors and heirs. It applies from the start of terminal illness and covers up to two-thirds (or all if debt exhausts the estate).
This is the context for transaction rules.
2
Distinguish between two transaction categories
Read the text: 'Every transaction that admits of rescission (yahramilu al-faskh) is valid in the present, then rectification by annulment if needed. Every transaction that does not admit of rescission is made like suspended upon death.' Examples: gifts/sales with favor (rescindable), manumission (non-rescindable).
'Admits rescission' means it can be undone without inherent defect.
3
Apply the rule to rescindable transactions
Gifts and sales with favor are valid immediately because death is uncertain and there is no immediate harm. After death, they can be rescinded to the extent needed to satisfy creditors and heirs.
The rescission is only to the extent that protects rights, not full rescission necessarily.
4
Detail the slave dispute
The dispute arises when a master chooses to pay ransom for his slave's crime and then becomes insolvent. Abu Hanifa: the indemnity becomes a debt on the master (analogy to hawala), and the slave remains with the master; victims cannot take the slave. Sahibayn: if the master cannot pay, the slave is surrendered to the victims.
The thamarah al-khilaf: whether the slaves can be taken if master insolvent.
5
Note the exception for mortgaged slave
A pledgor can manumit his mortgaged slave immediately because the mortgagee's right is only in possession (milk al-yad), not ownership (milk al-raqaba). This is unlike the general rule where manumission is suspended.
The text says: 'unlike the manumission of the pledgor, which is effective because the mortgagee's right is in milk al-yad not milk al-raqaba'.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the section 'Heavenly Impediments' (al-'awarid al-samawiyya) focusing on terminal illness (marad al-mawt). It begins by addressing a previous discussion about a slave who commits a crime and the master chooses to pay ransom but then becomes insolvent. According to Abu Hanifa, the indemnity becomes a debt on the master, and the slave remains with the master; the victim's family cannot take the slave. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that if the master cannot pay, the slave is handed over to the victims. The author then moves to illness, defining it as a bodily state contrary to health where actions are inherently defective. Importantly, illness does not negate two key capacities: ahliyyat al-hukm (capacity to be addressed by divine law) and ahliyyat al-ibara (capacity to express legal acts). The patient is still obligated for prayers, fasting, and valid in divorce, manumission, sales. However, because illness leads to death, and death transfers wealth to heirs and creditors, the law imposes a form of guardianship (hajr) on the patient's wealth to protect their rights. This guardianship is traced back to the beginning of the terminal illness, but only to the extent needed to satisfy creditors and heirs: up to two-thirds for heirs, and all if debt exhausts the estate. The commentary then addresses a potential objection: if illness does not impair capacity, why restrict transactions? The reply is that the restriction is not because of lack of capacity but because the patient's wealth will soon belong to others. The author then discusses the rule for transactions: any transaction that admits of rescission (like gifts and sales with favor) is considered valid immediately, but can be rescinded after death if needed to protect rights. Transactions that do not admit of rescission (like manumission) are treated as suspended upon death, similar to a mudabbar. The exception is the manumission of a mortgaged slave: it is effective immediately because the mortgagee's right is only in the possession, not ownership. The passage concludes by clarifying that the patient's gift (sila) and performance of financial obligations like zakat are subject to the same rules.

Key Terms
محتمل الفسخ (muhtamil al-faskh): A transaction that can be rescinded without inherent defect, e.g., gift, sale with favor
المعلق بالموت (al-mu'allaq bi al-mawt): Suspended upon death; refers to transactions like manumission that take effect only after death, akin to mudabbar
الحوالة (al-hawala): Transfer of debt from one debtor to another; used as analogy for the slave's indemnity

Acts of a Sick Person: Revocable vs. Irrevocable – Freeing a Slave
Explains principle of revocable acts with qiyas to mudabbar

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of qiyas to the case of a sick person's freeing of a slave, and distinguish between acts subject to rescission and those that are suspended upon death.

Core Idea
A sick person's revocable acts are valid now but can be rescinded after death; freeing a slave is like a mudabbar – effective only after death and subject to payment.

This passage from a classical usul text explores the legal effect of a terminally ill person's (marid) transactions. The author establishes a general rule: any transaction liable to rescission (muhtamil al-faskh) is valid at the present moment to avoid harm, but may be revoked after death to protect creditors and heirs. He then applies this rule to freeing a slave, using qiyas (analogy) to the status of a mudabbar. Finally, he addresses an exception for a mortgaged slave, where freeing is immediately effective because the mortgagee's right is only over possession, not ownership.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
The passage states: any transaction that can be rescinded (e.g., gifts, sales at undervalue) is valid now, but can be rescinded later if needed after death to protect creditors/heirs.
This is because death is uncertain and no immediate harm exists.
2
Apply the rule to freeing a slave
When a sick person frees a slave and the estate is insufficient or the slave exceeds one-third, the freeing is not immediately effective; it becomes like a mudabbar.
The slave remains a slave during life, becomes free after death, but must pay value to creditors/heirs.
3
Trace the qiyas reasoning
The author compares the freed slave to a mudabbar: both remain slaves during the master's life, become free after death, and must pay their value if the estate is encumbered.
The 'illa (effective cause) is the attachment of third-party rights (creditors/heirs) that suspend immediate freedom.
4
Understand the exception for a mortgaged slave
Freeing a mortgaged slave by the mortgagor is effective immediately because the mortgagee's right is only over possession (yad), not ownership (raqaba).
This distinguishes it from the general rule where the right of creditors/heirs attaches to the ownership itself.

Commentary
This passage explains the legal ruling regarding the acts of a person who is terminally ill (marid). The key principle is that any act that is subject to rescission (like gifts and sales at undervalue) is considered valid at the present moment, because death is uncertain and no one is harmed immediately. However, if the sick person dies, these acts may be rescinded to the extent necessary to protect the rights of creditors and heirs. The text gives the specific example of freeing a slave. If a sick person frees a slave when the estate is insufficient to cover debts or when the slave's value exceeds one-third of the estate, the freeing is not effective immediately. Instead, the slave is treated like a mudabbar (a slave whose freedom is suspended upon the master's death). During the sick person's life, the slave remains a slave; after death, he becomes free but must work to pay his value to the creditors and heirs. This is derived by analogy (qiyas) to the mudabbar. The passage then distinguishes this from the case of a mortgaged slave (راهن). In that case, the slave can be freed by the mortgagor because the mortgagee's right is only over the possession (yad) of the slave, not over the ownership (raqaba). The ownership remains with the mortgagor, so the freeing is effective. The text also defines 'sila' as a transfer without compensation and notes that financial duties like zakat and expiations are also affected.

The Substitute Can Differ from the Original: Qisas to Diyah
A Hanafi usul rule demonstration using qisas-diyah conversion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a substitute (khalaf) may differ from the original (asl) in specific rulings when their contexts (ahwal) differ, using the qisas-diyah example and the analogy of wudu-tayammum.

Core Idea
A substitute differs from the original when serving different needs.

This passage explores a key Hanafi usul principle: while a substitute (khalaf) often follows the original (asl) in ruling, it may depart when the context or purpose changes. The author illustrates this with the conversion of qisas (retaliation) into diyah (blood money). Although diyah is a substitute for qisas, its inheritance treatment differs: qisas goes directly to heirs, while diyah first satisfies the deceased's debts. The analogy with wudu and tayammum—where tayammum requires intention but wudu does not—drives home the point that differences arise from the distinct natures of the original and substitute.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core principle
State the principle: the substitute (khalaf) may contradict the original (asl) when their situations (hal) differ. This is a Hanafi usul rule rooted in istihsan.
2
Examine the primary example
Analyze the case of qisas converting to diyah. Note that qisas is established for heirs directly because it serves vengeance and is unsuitable for the deceased's needs, whereas diyah (property) first meets the deceased's debts and bequests before passing to heirs.
3
Trace the derivation
The author derives this by comparing the natures: qisas cannot satisfy the deceased's needs (it is corporal), while diyah can. Hence the substitute differs from the original. This is justified by istihsan, not strict qiyas.
The argument uses a qiyas jaliy (clear analogy) but ultimately relies on istihsan.
4
Apply the supporting analogy
Consider the analogy of wudu (asl) and tayammum (khalaf). Water is inherently pure, so intention is not required; earth is impure, so intention is needed. This shows how differing contexts (hal) justify different rulings for the substitute.
Tayammum differs from wudu in requiring niyyah.
5
Recognize the thamarah (practical outcome)
The practical effect: in inheritance, diyah is first used to pay off the deceased's debts, while qisas is never subject to such deduction. This distinction has real-life implications for estate distribution.

Commentary
This passage is a usul rule discussion demonstrating the principle that a substitute (khalaf) can differ in ruling from the original (asl) when their respective contexts differ. The author uses the example of qisas (retaliation) converting into diyah (blood money) upon the death of the offender or other circumstances. The key point is that qisas, being a corporal punishment, serves the need of the heirs for vengeance and is established directly for them from the start; it does not benefit the deceased. When it converts into diyah (a property right), one might assume the same inheritance rule applies. However, the Hanafi position is that diyah first belongs to the deceased to satisfy his debts and needs, and only then passes to the heirs. The author defends this by arguing that the substitute (diyah) must serve the deceased's needs because property can do so, whereas qisas could not. He uses the analogy of wudu (ablution) and tayammum (dry ablution): tayammum requires intention while wudu does not, because water is inherently pure while earth is impure and requires intention to effect purification. Similarly, the difference in context (deceased's need for property vs. living heirs' need for vengeance) justifies the difference in inheritance rules between qisas and diyah. This is a fine example of Hanafi usul al-fiqh reasoning, where istihsan (juristic preference) plays a role in allowing the substitute to differ from the original when conditions change.

انقلاب (inqilab): The conversion of a legal right or obligation from one form to another due to a change in circumstances, such as qisas converting to diyah.

The Legal Effect of Jest (Hazl): Definition and Uṣūl Rule
Definition and effects of hazl in contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define hazl, apply the Hanafi rule that hazl negates ikhtiyār al-ḥukm but not riḍā’ bi al-mubāshara, and distinguish between contracts affected by hazl and those that are not.

Core Idea
Jest prevents the ruling but not the act; sale becomes corrupt, but divorce takes effect.

This passage from a classical Hanafi uṣūl text examines the concept of hazl (jest) as an acquired impediment (ʿāriḍ muktasab). The author explains how jest affects different legal acts: for acts that depend on consent and choice (ikhtiyār al-ḥukm), such as sale, jest renders the contract corrupt (fāsid) without transferring ownership; for acts that do not depend on consent, such as divorce and manumission, the effect arises from the mere utterance. The passage also draws an analogy with khiyār al-sharṭ (stipulated option) and records an internal Hanafi disagreement between Abu Hanifa and the two companions regarding the time limit for validation after jest.

Guided Path
1
Understand the definition of hazl
Define hazl as the utterance where the speaker intends neither the literal nor figurative meaning, only jest. Distinguish it from seriousness (jidd).
The definition 'an la yurād bi al-lafẓ maʿnāhu al-ḥaqīqī wa lā al-majāzī' is key.
2
Differentiate between riḍā' bi al-mubāshara and ikhtiyār al-ḥukm
Identify that hazl negates the choice and consent to the legal ruling (ikhtiyār al-ḥukm) but does not negate consent to the direct act of utterance (riḍā' bi al-mubāshara). This dual effect explains why apostasy by jest is valid (due to riḍā' bi al-mubāshara), while contracts requiring both are affected.
The jester is responsible for the utterance itself (cause of ruling) but not for the ruling's intended effect.
3
Categorize legal acts based on dependence on consent
Classify acts into three types: creation (inshā’), informing (ikhbār), and belief. Within creation, distinguish between revocable acts (like sale, which require consent) and irrevocable acts (like divorce, which do not).
Hazl only affects revocable creation contracts; irrevocable acts take effect even in jest.
4
Examine the analogy to khiyār al-sharṭ and the internal ikhtilāf
Understand that hazl is analogous to a perpetual option (khiyār al-mutabāyiʿain) in preventing ownership. Note the difference: Abu Hanifa limits the option to three days, while the two companions allow indefinite validation.
This ikhtilāf affects practical cases: after a jesting contract, when does it become binding?

Commentary
This passage is from a commentary on the chapter of Qiyas (analogy), specifically discussing acquired impediments (awāriḍ muktasaba). The author, a Hanafi jurist, explains the legal effect of jest (hazl) in contracts and other legal acts. The key principle: jest negates the intention and consent to the legal ruling (ḥukm) but does not negate the direct act (mubāshara) itself. Therefore, rulings that depend on consent and choice (such as the transfer of ownership in a sale) are not established by a jesting contract; such a contract is corrupt (fāsid) and does not transfer ownership. However, rulings that do not depend on consent (such as divorce and manumission) take effect even in jest because the utterance itself is the cause. The passage also compares jest to the option of stipulation (khiyār al-sharṭ), noting similarities and differences. It then categorizes jest into three types: creating a transaction, informing, and matters of belief. For the first type (creation), it distinguishes between what is revocable (like sale) and what is not (like divorce). For revocable contracts, jest can affect either the basis, the amount, or the type of consideration. The author details four possible scenarios after agreeing on jest: abandoning it, building upon it, silence, or disagreement. If the parties abandon jest and contract seriously, the contract is valid. If they build upon the jest, the contract is corrupt and does not transfer ownership, analogous to an option of stipulation that prevents ownership. A Hanafi internal difference appears: Abu Hanifa requires the option period to be limited to three days, while the two companions allow indefinite validation. This lesson is a thorough exposition of how jest interacts with contractual consent, a classic topic in Hanafi uṣūl al-fiqh.

Analogy between Khiyar and Hazl: The Hanafi Rule on Unlimited Options
Analogical reasoning linking khiyar to hazl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that an unlimited optional stipulation invalidates a contract by analogizing it to hazl (jest), and explain the intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf on the time limit for khiyar.

Core Idea
Hazl and unlimited khiyar both prevent ownership transfer; thus an indefinite option invalidates the contract.

This passage from a Hanafi usul commentary demonstrates how qiyas (analogical reasoning) is used to connect two seemingly different concepts: khiyar (the right to rescind) and hazl (jest). The commentator expands on the ruling that an unlimited option prevents the transfer of ownership, just as hazl does. This analogy is then used to derive a rule on the validity of contracts with indefinite options. The passage also highlights an intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two main disciples (Sahibayn) regarding the time limit for ratification when an option is stipulated indefinitely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases being compared
Note the two situations: (1) a contract made in jest (hazl) and (2) a contract with an unlimited optional stipulation (khiyar stipulated forever). Both are said to prevent the establishment of ownership.
Hazl negates serious intent; khiyar suspends transfer of ownership.
2
Understand the common effect (mana' al-mulk)
The commentary states that hazl prevents ownership transfer just like khiyar does. This shared effect is the basis for the analogy: if a valid contract with option prevents ownership, then a void contract (due to hazl) is even more deserving of preventing ownership.
The reasoning is a fortiori (qiyas aulawi) or analogical.
3
Apply the qiyas to derive the rule
Since an unlimited option (stipulated forever) prevents ownership, it must invalidate the contract, just as hazl does. Hence, a contract with an indefinite option is fasid (invalid) because it suspends ownership indefinitely.
This is the relied-upon Hanafi position (mufta bihi).
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on time limit
Abu Hanifa requires that such an option be limited to three days; otherwise the contract is invalid. The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani) allow ratification even after three days, analogizing to the general khiyar period. The text indicates the reliance on Abu Hanifa's view.
This ikhtilaf is rooted in how strictly they analogize to the standard khiyar.
5
Evaluate the practical consequence (thamarah)
In practice, according to Abu Hanifa, if parties stipulate an indefinite option, the contract becomes valid only if ratification occurs within three days. According to the Sahibayn, ratification can happen at any time.
The mufta bihi position is Abu Hanifa's three-day limit.

Core Idea
Jest does not invalidate three acts—marriage, divorce, and oaths—because their causes produce effects immediately and unconditionally.

This lesson unpacks a foundational Hanafi usul principle: when a person performs a legal act like marriage or divorce in jest, the act still takes effect. The author cites a hadith and provides a rational argument to show that these causes are binding regardless of intent. We will unpack both the textual proof and the logical reasoning, and see how this principle is derived.

The jester freely chooses the cause (sabab) but does not intend its ruling. For these particular causes, the ruling is inseparable from the cause—it occurs immediately and cannot be rejected. Hence, jest does not affect them.

3
Compare with khiyar al-shart
Just as stipulating an option of stipulation does not prevent the cause from operating (e.g., saying 'I divorce you, and I have the option for three days' still effects divorce), jest also does not prevent the cause from operating.
4
Distinguish from sale
Sale allows the ruling (transfer of ownership) to be postponed by stipulating an option of stipulation. These three acts do not admit such postponement; their rulings are immediate and unconditional.
5
Refine the concept of sabab vs illa
The author addresses an objection: if someone says 'You are divorced tomorrow', divorce does not occur today, seeming to show delay. The response: that utterance is not a cause (illa) today; it becomes a cause when tomorrow arrives, and then the ruling is immediate.
This shows that the principle is about the effective cause (illa), not the mere utterance.

And Abd al-Razzaq narrated from Ali and Umar as a saying of theirs: 'Three things in which there is no play: marriage, divorce, and freeing a slave.' The form of collusion here is that they collude to marry her, or to divorce her, or to pardon for retaliation, or to free a slave in front of people, while in reality it is not the case. And by oath is meant conditional suspension. The form of collusion is that he colludes with his wife or slave to suspend her divorce or his freeing upon something in a jesting manner, while it is not real. And the oath by Allah is not meant because collusion cannot be imagined in it, as has been said. In that form in all of the four states, the jest is nullified and the contract is binding. The textual proof for this is what has passed of the hadith. As for the rational proof, the author clarifies it with his saying: 'And because the jester... does not admit rejection...' The sum is that jest does not prevent the occurrence of the cause. So when the ruling occurs without delay and without the possibility of rejection, it is established that these causes do not separate from their rulings, so jest does not affect them just as the option of stipulation does not affect them. This is unlike sale where the ruling separates from it by the option of stipulation. If it is said: your statement that these causes do not separate from their ruling is denied; because if he says 'You are divorced tomorrow', divorce does not occur before tomorrow; is this not delay of the ruling from the cause? I say: we mean by causes the effective causes (illal). His statement 'You are divorced tomorrow' is not a cause at the moment; rather, it becomes a cause when what it is attached to (i.e., tomorrow) occurs. So when tomorrow comes and it becomes a cause, its ruling does not admit delay; rather, it is a causative cause. When the second section was finished, he began the third section, which is where wealth is intended: 'As for...' Wealth is intended there, and wealth becomes intended because wealth is not obligatory without mention. So when wealth is stipulated, it is known that it is intended. And settlement for intentional blood. The form of jest is that a woman seeks her divorce for compensation in a jesting manner, or he mentions divorcing his wife for compensation in a jesting manner, or he settles for intentional blood in a jesting manner. Jest in this section occurs either in its essence, or in the amount of the compensation, or in its type. Each of the three aspects is divided into four categories: that they agreed to build (on the jest), or to disregard, or remained silent, or differed. The ruling according to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) in this section is that jest is invalid in all aspects, has no effect, the transaction is binding, and all the named (compensation) in the contract is obligatory in all scenarios. According to Abu Hanifa, jest is effective in general, so he made the transaction suspend until the jest is removed, with the wealth being binding immediately in some scenarios. This difference is what he referred to by his saying: 'for it has been mentioned...' Clarification: They agreed that jest is like the option of stipulation. Then they differed as to whether khul' admits the option of stipulation on the part of the woman or not. For if a woman divorces herself by khul' for one thousand on the condition that she has the option for three days and the man accepts, then according to Abu Hanifa, she has the option; if she returns within three days, the khul' becomes void. The two said: she has no option. He alluded to this with his saying: 'because khul' does not admit...' It does not admit the option of stipulation because it is a transaction of an oath from the husband's side, as if he said: If you accept the named wealth, you are divorced. Therefore, he does not have the right to retract before her acceptance, and her acceptance is a condition for the oath. Since it is so, it does not admit the option of stipulation like other conditions. And if it does not admit the option, it does not admit jest because it is like the option of stipulation. So jest is void, having no effect in this section absolutely. This is as if he is refuting a hypothetical objection: We concede that jest does not affect the essence of khul', but it should affect the compensation because it is wealth, and jest affects wealth. The refutation: Jest only affects wealth if it is intended; here wealth is established as subsidiary within khul'. If it does not affect the main khul', then likewise it does not affect what is subsidiary to it, like the agency established within a pledge contract which is binding by its binding. If it is said: How do you say the wealth is subsidiary here while you previously said wealth in this section is intended? Even if conceded, we do not accept that jest does not affect it as it does not affect the main. Do you not see that wealth is subsidiary in marriage, and jest affected it such that the dower was one thousand when they jested about the amount, not two thousand as passed? I say: Our meaning of it being intended earlier is with regard to the contract, but it is subsidiary in effect to divorce and freeing because it is a condition, and conditions are followers; for that reason it is not obligatory without mention. So when it is subsidiary to divorce, its ruling is like its ruling, so jest does not affect it. Being intended from one aspect does not contradict being subsidiary from another aspect due to difference.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul work discusses the effect of jest (hazl) on legal transactions. The author begins by citing the hadith: 'Three things, their seriousness is serious, and their jest is serious: marriage, divorce, and oath.' This hadith, though not found in the major hadith collections, is reported by al-Tirmidhi, Abu Dawud, al-Daraqutni, and Ahmad. The author then provides a rational argument: the jester freely chooses the cause (sabab) but does not intend its legal effect. However, the effect of these particular causes (marriage, divorce, oath) cannot be separated from the cause—they occur immediately and cannot be rejected. Thus, jest does not invalidate them. The author compares this to the option of stipulation (khiyar al-shart), which also does not prevent the cause from occurring. For example, if someone says 'I divorce you' and adds 'I have the option for three days', the divorce still occurs immediately. This is unlike sale, where the ruling (transfer of ownership) can be postponed by stipulating an option. The passage then divides transactions into three categories: (1) those where jest is irrelevant (the three mentioned), (2) those where wealth is intended (such as khul', divorce for compensation, and settlement for intentional blood), and (3) others. In the third category, there is a dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). According to the two, jest is completely ineffective; the transaction is binding and the full named compensation is due. According to Abu Hanifa, jest has some effect, requiring the transaction to be suspended in some cases. The passage explains that this dispute stems from whether khul' admits the option of stipulation. The two companions argue that khul' is like an oath from the husband's side and therefore does not admit options or jest. Abu Hanifa allows the option, hence the difference. The passage also includes a detailed discussion of whether the compensation in khul' is considered subsidiary or intended, concluding that it is subsidiary to the divorce and thus not affected by jest.

Key Terms
الهزل (al-hazl): Jest or playful intent, where a person performs a legal act without intending its legal consequences.
السبب (al-sabab): The legal cause or occasion that triggers a ruling, such as the utterance of divorce words.
خيار الشرط (khiyar al-shart): The option of stipulation, a condition in a contract allowing one party to annul the contract within a specified period.
العلة (al-illa): The effective cause or ratio legis that necessitates a ruling, as opposed to merely the sabab.

The Effect of Jesting in Wealth-Intended Transactions: The Khul' Dispute
Examining the intra-Hanafi dispute on jest in khul' and similar wealth-intended transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi dispute regarding jest in khul' and similar transactions, and contrast the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
In transactions where wealth is intended, jest may affect the ruling, but the Sahibayn hold it ineffective because wealth is subsidiary to the main act.

This lesson delves into the intra-Hanafi dispute on whether jest affects transactions where wealth is a primary intended element, such as khul' (divorce for compensation), divorce for compensation, and settlement for intentional blood. The passage presents the views of Imam Abu Hanifa and his two companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and explores the underlying reasoning based on the nature of khul' as an oath-like transaction.

Guided Path
1
Identify the transactions in the third category
The text mentions khul', divorce for compensation (talaq 'ala mal), and settlement for intentional blood (sulh 'an dam al-amd). These are transactions where wealth is expressly intended.
Wealth is 'intended' because it is mentioned explicitly in the contract.
2
Understand the forms of jest in this category
Jest can occur in the essence of the transaction, in the amount of compensation, or in its type. And within each, there are four scenarios: both parties agree to build on the jest, both disregard it, both remain silent, or they differ.
3
State the position of the Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad)
According to them, jest is entirely ineffective in these transactions. The transaction is binding and the full named compensation is due, regardless of the form of jest.
They analogize khul' to an oath (yamin) from the husband's side, which does not admit conditions or jest.
4
State the position of Imam Abu Hanifa
According to Abu Hanifa, jest has some effect. In some scenarios, the transaction is suspended until the jest is removed; in others, the compensation may be reduced or the transaction may be void if the jest is not resolved.
He allows khul' to admit the option of stipulation, and thus jest can affect it.
5
Analyze the underlying reasoning
The dispute hinges on whether khul' admits the option of stipulation (khiyar al-shart) on the woman's part. The Sahibayn say no, because khul' is like an oath: the husband says 'if you accept, you are divorced'; the wife's acceptance is a condition, not an option. Abu Hanifa says yes, because khul' involves wealth and can include options.
The text also discusses whether the wealth in khul' is 'intended' or 'subsidiary'. The Sahibayn argue it is subsidiary to the divorce, so jest in the wealth follows the ruling of the main act (divorce).

ففي تلك الصورة في كل حال من الأحوال الأربعة يبطل الهزل ويلزم العقد، والدليل النقلي على هذا ما مرّ من الحديث، وأما العقلي فبينه المصنف بقوله : ولأن الهازل إلخ. لا يحتمل الردّ إلخ: والحاصل أن الهزل لا يمنع انعقاد السبب، فإذا وجد حكمه بلا تراخ وبغير احتمال الردّ ، فثبت أن هذه الأسباب لا ينفصل عن أحكام، فلا يؤثر فيها الهزل كما لا يؤثر خيار الشرط؛ لأن الهزل وخيار الشرط لايمنعان انعقاد السبب، وإليه أشار بقوله: ألا ترى إلخ. أنه: أي كل واحد من تلك الأسباب لا يحتمل إلخ حتى إذا طلق امرأته وقال: لي الخيار إلى ثلاثة أيام، يقع الطلاق بمجرد قوله: "طلقتك"؛ لأن خيار الشرط لايمنع انعقاد السبب، وهو قوله: "طلقتك"، فإذا وجد السبب وجد الحكم -وهو الطلاق ألبتة بغير تراخ، وهذا بخلاف البيع حيث ينفصل حكمه عنه بشرط الخيار، فإن قيل: قولكم: "إن هذه الأسباب لا ينفصل عنها حكمها" ممنوع؛ لأنه إذا قال: أنت طالق غداً، لا يقع الطلاق قبل مجيء الغد، وهل هذا إلا تراخي الحكم عن السبب. قلت: مرادنا بالأسباب العلل. وقوله: "أنت طالق غداً" ليس بعلة في الحال، وإنما يصير علة عند وجود ما أضيف إليه من الغد، فإذا جاء الغد وصار علةً لا يقبل حكمه التراخي بل هو سبب مفض. ولما فرغ عن القسم الثاني شرع في القسم الثالث، وهو ما كان المال فيه مقصوداً فقال: وأما إلخ. المال فيه مقصوداً وإنما صار المال في هذا القسم مقصوداً؛ لأن المال لا يجب بغير الذكر، فلما شرط المال فعلم أنه مقصود فيه. والصلح عن دم العمد وصورة الهزل أن المرأة طلبت طلاقها على مال بطريق الهزل، أو ذكر طلاق امرأة على مال بطريق الهزل، أو صالح عن دم العمد بطريق الهزل، والهزل في هذا القسم إما أن يقع بأصله أو في مقدار البدل أو في جنسه وكل واحد من الوجوه الثلاثة على أربعة أقسام مذكورة بأن اتفقا على البناء، أو على الإعراض، أو سكتا، أو اختلفا، فالحكم عندهما في هذا القسم أن الهزل باطل في جميع الوجوه، لا أثر له والتصرف لازم وجميع المسمّى في العقد واجب في الصور كلها، وعند أبي حنيفة الهزل مؤثر في الجملة، فأوجب توقف التصرف إلى إسقاط الهزل، ومع لزوم المال في الحال في بعض الصور، وإلى هذا الاختلاف أشار بقوله: فقد ذكر إلخ.

أن الطلاق إلخ: بيان ذلك أنهم اتفقوا على أن الهزل بمنزلة شرط الخيار، ثم اختلفوا في أن الخلع هل يحتمل شرط الخيار في جانب المرأة أم لا؟ حتى إن اختلعت المرأة بألف على أنها بالخيار ثلاثة أيام وقبل الرجل، فعند أبي حنيفة [؟] لها الخيار، فإن ردّت في الثلاث بطل الخلع، وقالا : لا خيار لها، وإليه أشار بقوله: لأن الخلع إلخ. لا يحتمل إلخ لأنه تصرف يمين من جانب الزوج كأنه قال: إن قبلت المال المسمّى فأنتِ طالق، ولهذا لايملك الرجوع قبل قبولها، وقبولها شرط لليمين، وإذا كان كذلك لا يحتمل خيار الشرط كسائر الشروط، وإذا لم يحتمل خيار الشرط لا يحتمل الهزل؛ لأنه بمنزلة خيار الشرط، فالهزل باطل لا أثر له في هذا القسم مطلقاً. أو بجنسه وسواء اتفقا على البناء، أو على الإعراض، أو سكتا، أو اختلفا عندهما في جميع الوجوه المذكورة لبطلان الهزل. لا يحتمل إلخ كأنه دفع لدخل مقدر تقريره أنا سلّمنا أن الهزل لا يؤثر بأصل الخلع لكن ينبغي أن يؤثر في البدل؛ لأنه مال، والهزل يؤثر في المال. تقرير الدفع أن الهزل إنما يؤثر في المال إذا كان مقصوداً، والمال هنا ثبت تبعا في ضمن الخلع، فإذا لم يؤثر في الخلع الذي هو متضمّن فكذا فيما هو في ضمنه كالوكالة الثابتة في ضمن عقد الرهن يلزم بلزومه.

فإن قيل: كيف قلتم: إن المال هنا تابع، وقد قلتم سابقا: إن المال في هذا القسم مقصود، وإن سلم فلا نسلّم أن الهزل لا يؤثر فيه كما لا يؤثر في أصله. ألا ترى أن المال تابع في النكاح، وقد أثر فيه الهزل حتى كان المهر ألفاً فيما هزلا بقدر البدل دون الألفين كما مرّ قلت إن مرادنا بكونه مقصودا فيما سبق باعتبار العقد ولكنه تابع في السيكوت للطلاق والعتاق؛ لأنه شرط والشروط أتباع، فلذا لا يجب بدون الذكر، فإذا كان تابعاً للطلاق فيكون حكمه كحكمه، فلايؤثر فيه الهزل، وكونه مقصوداً من جهة لا ينافي كونه تابعاً من جهة أخرى لاختلاف
Chapter of Analogy - Acquired Impediments. Because of his saying: 'Three things, their seriousness is serious, and their jest is serious: marriage, divorce, and oath.' And because the jester is free in choosing the cause, satisfied with it without its ruling, and the rulings of these causes do not admit rejection by the cause as has passed, nor delay. Do you not see that they do not admit the option of stipulation? And as for what wealth is intended in, such as khul', divorce for compensation, and settlement for intentional blood. His saying: 'Three things...' this hadith is not found in hadith books; rather, al-Tirmidhi, Abu Dawud, al-Daraqutni, and Ahmad narrated from Abu Hurayra that the Prophet said: 'Three things, their seriousness is serious, and their jest is serious: marriage, divorce, and return (raj'ah).' [Tirmidhi no. 1184] and al-Tirmidhi graded it good. And Abd al-Razzaq narrated from Ali and Umar as a saying of theirs: 'Three things in which there is no play: marriage, divorce, and freeing a slave.' [Musannaf Abd al-Razzaq no. 10247] And in another narration from them: 'Four,' adding 'vow.' As for oath and pardon for retaliation, they are established by indication, not by analogy. The form of collusion here is that they collude to marry her, or to divorce her, or to pardon for retaliation, or to free a slave in front of people, while in reality it is not the case. And by oath is meant conditional suspension. The form of collusion is that he colludes with his wife or slave to suspend her divorce or his freeing upon something in a jesting manner, while it is not real. And the oath by Allah is not meant because collusion cannot be imagined in it, as has been said. In that form in all of the four states, the jest is nullified and the contract is binding. The textual proof for this is what has passed of the hadith. As for the rational proof, the author clarifies it with his saying: 'And because the jester... does not admit rejection...' The sum is that jest does not prevent the occurrence of the cause. So when the ruling occurs without delay and without the possibility of rejection, it is established that these causes do not separate from their rulings, so jest does not affect them just as the option of stipulation does not affect them. This is unlike sale where the ruling separates from it by the option of stipulation. If it is said: your statement that these causes do not separate from their ruling is denied; because if he says 'You are divorced tomorrow', divorce does not occur before tomorrow; is this not delay of the ruling from the cause? I say: we mean by causes the effective causes (illal). His statement 'You are divorced tomorrow' is not a cause at the moment; rather, it becomes a cause when what it is attached to (i.e., tomorrow) occurs. So when tomorrow comes and it becomes a cause, its ruling does not admit delay; rather, it is a causative cause. When the second section was finished, he began the third section, which is where wealth is intended: 'As for...' Wealth is intended there, and wealth becomes intended because wealth is not obligatory without mention. So when wealth is stipulated, it is known that it is intended. And settlement for intentional blood. The form of jest is that a woman seeks her divorce for compensation in a jesting manner, or he mentions divorcing his wife for compensation in a jesting manner, or he settles for intentional blood in a jesting manner. Jest in this section occurs either in its essence, or in the amount of the compensation, or in its type. Each of the three aspects is divided into four categories: that they agreed to build (on the jest), or to disregard, or remained silent, or differed. The ruling according to the two (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) in this section is that jest is invalid in all aspects, has no effect, the transaction is binding, and all the named (compensation) in the contract is obligatory in all scenarios. According to Abu Hanifa, jest is effective in general, so he made the transaction suspend until the jest is removed, with the wealth being binding immediately in some scenarios. This difference is what he referred to by his saying: 'for it has been mentioned...' Clarification: They agreed that jest is like the option of stipulation. Then they differed as to whether khul' admits the option of stipulation on the part of the woman or not. For if a woman divorces herself by khul' for one thousand on the condition that she has the option for three days and the man accepts, then according to Abu Hanifa, she has the option; if she returns within three days, the khul' becomes void. The two said: she has no option. He alluded to this with his saying: 'because khul' does not admit...' It does not admit the option of stipulation because it is a transaction of an oath from the husband's side, as if he said: If you accept the named wealth, you are divorced. Therefore, he does not have the right to retract before her acceptance, and her acceptance is a condition for the oath. Since it is so, it does not admit the option of stipulation like other conditions. And if it does not admit the option, it does not admit jest because it is like the option of stipulation. So jest is void, having no effect in this section absolutely. This is as if he is refuting a hypothetical objection: We concede that jest does not affect the essence of khul', but it should affect the compensation because it is wealth, and jest affects wealth. The refutation: Jest only affects wealth if it is intended; here wealth is established as subsidiary within khul'. If it does not affect the main khul', then likewise it does not affect what is subsidiary to it, like the agency established within a pledge contract which is binding by its binding. If it is said: How do you say the wealth is subsidiary here while you previously said wealth in this section is intended? Even if conceded, we do not accept that jest does not affect it as it does not affect the main. Do you not see that wealth is subsidiary in marriage, and jest affected it such that the dower was one thousand when they jested about the amount, not two thousand as passed? I say: Our meaning of it being intended earlier is with regard to the contract, but it is subsidiary in effect to divorce and freeing because it is a condition, and conditions are followers; for that reason it is not obligatory without mention. So when it is subsidiary to divorce, its ruling is like its ruling, so jest does not affect it. Being intended from one aspect does not contradict being subsidiary from another aspect due to difference.

Commentary
This passage from a Hanafi usul work discusses the effect of jest (hazl) on legal transactions. The author begins by citing the hadith: 'Three things, their seriousness is serious, and their jest is serious: marriage, divorce, and oath.' This hadith, though not found in the major hadith collections, is reported by al-Tirmidhi, Abu Dawud, al-Daraqutni, and Ahmad. The author then provides a rational argument: the jester freely chooses the cause (sabab) but does not intend its legal effect. However, the effect of these particular causes (marriage, divorce, oath) cannot be separated from the cause—they occur immediately and cannot be rejected. Thus, jest does not invalidate them. The author compares this to the option of stipulation (khiyar al-shart), which also does not prevent the cause from occurring. For example, if someone says 'I divorce you' and adds 'I have the option for three days', the divorce still occurs immediately. This is unlike sale, where the ruling (transfer of ownership) can be postponed by stipulating an option. The passage then divides transactions into three categories: (1) those where jest is irrelevant (the three mentioned), (2) those where wealth is intended (such as khul', divorce for compensation, and settlement for intentional blood), and (3) others. In the third category, there is a dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad). According to the two, jest is completely ineffective; the transaction is binding and the full named compensation is due. According to Abu Hanifa, jest has some effect, requiring the transaction to be suspended in some cases. The passage explains that this dispute stems from whether khul' admits the option of stipulation. The two companions argue that khul' is like an oath from the husband's side and therefore does not admit options or jest. Abu Hanifa allows the option, hence the difference. The passage also includes a detailed discussion of whether the compensation in khul' is considered subsidiary or intended, concluding that it is subsidiary to the divorce and thus not affected by jest.

Key Terms
الخلع (al-khul'): Divorce initiated by the wife in exchange for compensation paid to the husband.
الصلح عن دم العمد (al-sulh 'an dam al-amd): Settlement in intentional homicide cases where the victim's family accepts blood money instead of retaliation.
التابع (al-tabi'): Subsidiary or subordinate element; in fiqh, a subsidiary follows the ruling of the main element.
اليمين (al-yamin): Oath; also used for conditional suspension (ta'liq).

Effect of Jest (Huzl) in Khul': Qiyas and the Hanafi Dispute
Explaining the ikhtilaf on huzl in khul' between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between Abu Hanifa's and the Sahibayn's positions on huzl in khul', identify the underlying qiyas reasoning, and apply the usul principle that khul' admits khiyar al-shart due to its exchange element.

Core Idea
Jest in khul' suspends the divorce for Abu Hanifa, but binds immediately for the Sahibayn — rooted in whether khul' is like a sale or a pure divorce.

This lesson examines a classic Hanafi usul dispute: does jest (huzl) in a khul' divorce affect its validity? The crux lies in whether khul' admits a condition of option (khiyar al-shart). Abu Hanifa analogizes it to a sale, allowing suspension; the Sahibayn see it as a pure divorce, voiding any condition. This passage from a usul text illustrates how differences in analogical reasoning yield divergent rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
What exactly is being disputed? The effect of huzl in khul' when jest occurs in the original contract, the amount of compensation, or its type. Both sides agree on some cases, but three specific scenarios are contested.
These three scenarios: jest in the original contract (asl al-'aqd), in the amount of the substitute (qadr al-badal), or in its type (jins al-badal).
2
Understand each school's analogical basis
How does each side analogize khul'? Abu Hanifa: khul' is like a sale (mu'awada) because it involves exchange of compensation for divorce, thus it admits khiyar al-shart. The Sahibayn: khul' is a talaq (divorce) from the husband's side, a verbal oath (yamin) that does not admit khiyar.
This difference in the asl (original case) drives the whole dispute.
3
Trace the qiyas structure
Identify the components of each qiyas: the original case (asl), the effective cause (illa), and the new case (far'). For Abu Hanifa: asl = sale (permits khiyar), illa = element of exchange, far' = khul' with huzl. For Sahibayn: asl = pure divorce (no khiyar), illa = being a verbal divorce act, far' = khul' with huzl.
The differing illa leads to opposite rulings on the effect of huzl.
4
Evaluate the thamarah al-khilaf
What is the practical difference? According to Abu Hanifa, the divorce is suspended on the woman's serious confirmation; according to the Sahibayn, both divorce and money are immediately binding. This difference applies only in the three contested forms when both parties agree to proceed (bana' ala al-muwada'a).
The remaining nine scenarios are undisputed within the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi dispute on the effect of jest (huzl) in divorce by khul', focusing on the ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani). Under the chapter of Qiyas, it illustrates how different views on the nature of khul'—whether it is like a sale (mu'awada) or a pure divorce (talaq)—lead to different rulings on the validity of a condition of option (khiyar al-shart) and consequently on the effect of jest. The Sahibayn argue that khul' is a form of divorce (talaq) from the husband's side, a verbal act (taghayyur yamin), which does not admit khiyar al-shart; therefore, jest (which is analogous to such a condition) has no effect, and the divorce and money are immediately binding. Abu Hanifa, however, considers that khul' has an element of exchange (mu'awada), allowing the woman to have an option; thus, jest suspends the divorce until she confirms her serious intent. The author explains that in khul', the money is subsidiary to the divorce (thubutan), whereas in marriage the mahr is not subsidiary in terms of existence, so effects differ. The fruit of the disagreement is limited to three scenarios—jest in the original contract, amount, or type—when both parties agree to proceed (banā' 'alā al-muwāḍa'a). In the other nine scenarios, there is no disagreement.

Voluntary vs Involuntary Concessions in Fasting
Expansion of the qiyās between travel and illness

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between voluntary (travel) and involuntary (illness) concessions to determine the permissibility of breaking a fast and the obligation of kaffarah.

Core Idea
Travel is a chosen act; its concessions are limited; illness is not chosen, so concessions are broader.

This passage from Muntakhab al-Ḥusāmī explains why travel and illness, though both valid reasons to break a fast, are treated differently in Hanafi fiqh. The key is whether the cause is a matter of choice (mukhtār) or an unavoidable necessity (ḍarūrah lāzimah). Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying rulings on breaking fast and kaffārah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two causes (travel and illness)
Both travel and illness are reasons for concession (rukhṣah) in fasting. The passage starts by acknowledging their apparent similarity.
Ruḵṣah means a relaxation of the original obligation.
2
Discover the distinguishing factor
Travel is an 'amr mukhtār' (chosen act) while illness is a 'ḍarūrah lāzimah' (unavoidable compulsion). This difference in nature leads to different rulings.
The voluntary nature of travel restricts the concession.
3
Apply to three scenarios
The passage gives three sub‑cases: (1) starting fast while already traveling, (2) starting fast as resident then traveling, (3) breaking fast before traveling. For each, determine if breaking is allowed or if kaffārah is affected.
The timing of travel relative to the fast is critical.
4
Contrast with illness scenarios
Illness, being involuntary, allows breaking the fast even if the fast was started while healthy. Kaffārah is not obligated if the fast is broken due to illness, and subsequent illness does not retroactively impose kaffārah.
Contrast the voluntary vs. involuntary nature.

Commentary
This passage clarifies a nuance: although both travel and illness are causes of concession in fasting, they differ because travel is a voluntary action while illness is involuntary. A traveler who starts fasting in the morning may not break his fast because he chose to travel and could have avoided it, whereas a sick person can break his fast after starting because illness is not his choice. Similarly, if a resident starts fasting and then travels, he may not break his fast. If he breaks fast without a valid excuse, kaffarah (expiation) is normally obligated, but if there is a doubt (shubhah) due to travel that permits breaking, the kaffarah may be negated. However, if he breaks fast before traveling (while still a resident), kaffarah is obligated and subsequent travel does not cancel it. This contrasts with illness: if one breaks fast due to illness, kaffarah is not obligated, and even if he later becomes ill after breaking, the ruling is based on the state at the time of breaking. The principle is that voluntary causes (like travel) do not create a doubt that removes kaffarah once the act is done, while involuntary causes do.

Qiyas on Duress: The Dual Aspect of Acts in Hanafi Usul
Applies qiyas to determine when mukrah is a tool for mukrih

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that duress splits an act into a sinful aspect (staying with the direct doer) and a destructive aspect (transferable to the coercer) using qiyas.

Core Idea
Sin requires personal intention; liability can follow causation.

This passage from Muntakhab al-Husami examines the legal status of acts performed under duress. The Hanafi school uses qiyas to distinguish between the sinful element (ithm) and the destructive element (itlaf) of an act. The key insight: while the direct actor retains moral responsibility, financial and penal liability may shift to the coercer. This lesson unpacks the reasoning applied to killing, sale, and emancipation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dual aspect
For any harmful act, separate the sinful aspect (requiring intention) from the destructive aspect (causing loss). The passage states that killing a Muslim has two considerations: it causes destruction (itlaf) and it incurs sin (ithm).
This dual nature is central to applying qiyas.
2
Apply to killing under duress
The direct killer bears the sin because intention is an act of the heart and cannot be transferred. However, the destructive act (death) is attributed to the coercer, so liability for qisas, diyah, and kaffara falls on him.
The passage: 'لا يصلح آلة في ذلك ... يبقى إثم القتل عليه وإن يصلح آلة له تفويت المحل'
3
Extend to sale and delivery
Delivery under duress completes the sale as an act of the seller; the seller is not a tool for the coercer in completing his own sale. But the financial loss (destruction of property value) may transfer, so the coercer can be made liable for the value.
The sale is fasid due to lack of consent, but ownership transfers to the buyer.
4
Extend to emancipation
Emancipation is a verbal act requiring the speaker's intention. The direct actor under duress performs the utterance, so wala' goes to him. However, the coercer must compensate the slave's value because the destructive aspect transfers.
The passage: 'المكره على الإعتاق ... هو المتكلم ... الولاء له'
5
Synthesize the general principle
Acts with a mental element (sin, intention) stay with the direct doer because they cannot be performed by another. Acts of destruction (itlaf) can be attributed to the cause (coercer) via hukmi attribution. This is the Hanafi way of balancing justice and causality.
This principle is derived from the qiyas presented.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul al-fiqh discussion applying qiyas to rulings on duress (ikrah). It distinguishes between two aspects of a harmful act: the sinful aspect (ithm) and the destructive aspect (itlaf). The direct actor (al-mukrah bi al-kasr) cannot be a mere tool for the coercer (al-mukrah bi al-fath) in matters of sin, because sin depends on intention and intention is an act of the heart which cannot be transferred. However, for liability (damages, qisas), the act can transfer to the coercer because destruction can be attributed to the one who caused it. The same principle is applied to sale/delivery and emancipation: in sale, the delivery is an act of the seller to complete his own sale, so it stays with him, making it a valid but defective sale (fasid); in emancipation under duress, the direct actor performs the utterance, and since emancipation is a purely verbal act, it cannot be transferred, so the wala' goes to the direct freed slave, but the coercer bears liability for the value. The text emphasizes that the transfer of an act from the coerced to the coercer is a legal (hukmi) attribution, not physical, and requires that the act be conceivable from the coercer and not be a direct sensory act of the coerced.

آلة (ālah): A tool or instrument; here, whether the direct actor can be considered a mere instrument of the coercer so that the act is wholly attributed to the coercer.
تغريم (taghrīm): The imposition of financial liability; here, for the coercer to compensate for the value of the slave or sold property.

The Legal Construct of Duress: Transfer of Physical Acts
Explaining the legal construct of transferring physical acts under duress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of amr ḥukmī to determine when a physical act under duress is attributed to the coersor.

Core Idea
Acts under duress transfer to the coersor as a legal construct, not physical reality.

When a person is forced to destroy another's property, who bears the liability? The Hanafi school uses a powerful tool: amr ḥukmī, a legal construct that attributes the act to the coersor even though the coerced person physically performed it. This lesson unpacks how this principle works for physical acts like delivery and destruction, setting the stage for the dispute on verbal acts.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharḥ) on the rulings of duress (ikrāh) in the Ḥanafī school, particularly addressing the legal attribution of actions. The author begins by analyzing the act of delivery (taslīm) in a sale under duress. He explains that delivery is a disposition completing the sale; in that capacity, the coerced seller is not a mere instrument of the coersor because the act is his own. However, in terms of destroying the buyer's ownership (itlāf), it can be attributed to the coersor as an act of usurpation (ghaṣb). This dual nature leads to a hybrid ruling: the contract is void due to lack of consent, but the destruction gives the seller a choice to claim compensation from either the coersor or the buyer. The author then introduces a key principle: the transfer of acts from the coerced to the coersor is a legal construct (amr ḥukmī), not a sensory one. It applies when the act is rationally conceivable from the coersor but not actually perceived from him. For example, in coerced emancipation, the utterance is from the coerced, but the destruction (loss of the slave) can be transferred to the coersor because destroying property is conceivable from him, even though he did not do it. The author contrasts the Ḥanafī view with that of al-Shāfi‘ī, who considers all verbal dispositions under unjust duress void, likening imprisonment to killing. The Ḥanafī school distinguishes between verbal and physical acts: verbal acts (like divorce, emancipation, sale) under duress are generally invalid because they lack intention, unless the duress is just (e.g., coercing a prisoner to embrace Islam). Physical acts under duress, however, are transferred to the coersor, who bears liability. The passage concludes with a detailed discussion of why emancipation under duress does not transfer the act itself, only the resulting destruction. Overall, this is a rigorous exercise in applying uṣūl principles to fiqh cases.

Core Idea
Hanafis split verbal and physical acts; Shafi'is void all acts under duress equally.

What happens when a man divorces his wife under threat of death? The Shafi'i school voids the divorce entirely. 1
State the Hanafi position on verbal acts under unjust duress
Identify that verbal acts (divorce, emancipation, sale) are invalid (lāghw) because they lack true intention (qaṣd). However, they are not entirely without effect: in some cases, the act is valid if the duress is just (bi-ḥaqq).
The text specifically mentions duress bi-ḥaqq (just) like coercion of a prisoner to embrace Islam.
2
State the Shafi'i position
Find where the text says 'qāla al-Shāfi‘ī ... al-tasarrufāt al-qawliyya takūnu laghwan idhā kāna al-ikrāhu bighayri ḥaqq' — all verbal acts under unjust duress are void, and duress by imprisonment equals duress by killing.
Their reasoning: speech requires free choice to express inner intention.
3
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
For coerced emancipation: Hanafi says the slave is freed and the coersor pays compensation; Shafi'i says the emancipation is void and the slave remains enslaved.
This is a clear practical difference.
4
Understand the Hanafi response to Shafi'i
The text explains that for verbal acts, the coerced cannot be an instrument for the coersor because speech cannot be uttered by another person. Therefore, the act stays with the coerced, but its destructive consequences can be transferred.
The qiyas on delivery is used to show this partial transfer.

Chapter of Analogy: Types of Duress. Because delivery (taslim) is a disposition in selling oneself by completion, and in that he is not suitable as an instrument for another. And if the coerced (maf‘ūl) were made an instrument for another, the locus would change, and the essence of the act would change; because then it becomes pure usurpation (ghasb). And we have attributed it to the coersor (mukrih) insofar as it is usurpation. And when it is established that it is a legal construct (ḥukmī), we adopt that view. That holds in what is rationally conceivable but not sensibly perceived. So we said: The one coerced into emancipation (itq) with full compulsion (iljā') is the speaker? Actually the instrument is for him. (Then follows discussion on the sale being invalid, etc.) And the meaning of destruction (itlāf) from him is transferred to the one who coerced him; because it is partially separate from him and bears the transfer in its origin. This is our position. And al-Shāfi‘ī (may Allah have mercy on him) said: The verbal dispositions of the coerced are void when the duress is unjust, because the validity of speech depends on intention and choice to be an expression of what is in the heart; so it becomes void when they are absent. According to him, duress by imprisonment is like duress by killing. And when duress occurs on an act, once duress is complete, the ruling of the act is removed from the agent; its completion is that it be made an excuse permitting the act. The act is transferred from him to the coersor, making him an instrument. But this transfer does not occur in emancipation because it cannot be uttered by the coersor with the tongue of the coerced. And the destruction is transferred separately.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharḥ) on the rulings of duress (ikrāh) in the Ḥanafī school, particularly addressing the legal attribution of actions. The author begins by analyzing the act of delivery (taslīm) in a sale under duress. He explains that delivery is a disposition completing the sale; in that capacity, the coerced seller is not a mere instrument of the coersor because the act is his own. However, in terms of destroying the buyer's ownership (itlāf), it can be attributed to the coersor as an act of usurpation (ghaṣb). This dual nature leads to a hybrid ruling: the contract is void due to lack of consent, but the destruction gives the seller a choice to claim compensation from either the coersor or the buyer. The author then introduces a key principle: the transfer of acts from the coerced to the coersor is a legal construct (amr ḥukmī), not a sensory one. It applies when the act is rationally conceivable from the coersor but not actually perceived from him. For example, in coerced emancipation, the utterance is from the coerced, but the destruction (loss of the slave) can be transferred to the coersor because destroying property is conceivable from him, even though he did not do it. The author contrasts the Ḥanafī view with that of al-Shāfi‘ī, who considers all verbal dispositions under unjust duress void, likening imprisonment to killing. The Ḥanafī school distinguishes between verbal and physical acts: verbal acts (like divorce, emancipation, sale) under duress are generally invalid because they lack intention, unless the duress is just (e.g., coercing a prisoner to embrace Islam). Physical acts under duress, however, are transferred to the coersor, who bears liability. The passage concludes with a detailed discussion of why emancipation under duress does not transfer the act itself, only the resulting destruction. Overall, this is a rigorous exercise in applying uṣūl principles to fiqh cases.

Key Terms
التصرفات القولية (al-tasarrufāt al-qawliyya): Verbal dispositions such as divorce, emancipation, and contracts.
الإكراه بالحبس (al-ikrāh bi-l-ḥabs): Duress by imprisonment; considered by al-Shāfi‘ī as equal to duress by killing.
