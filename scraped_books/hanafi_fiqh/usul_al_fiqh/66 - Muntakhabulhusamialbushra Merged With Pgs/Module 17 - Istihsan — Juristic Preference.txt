The Fourth Type of Metaphorical Concession (Rukhsa Majaziyya)
Defines fourth type of rukhsa as metaphorical and gives examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a metaphorical concession and distinguish it from abrogation by applying the condition that the ruling must remain legislated in some other context.

Core Idea
A concession is metaphorical when the original ruling is completely removed, leaving no 'azima to compare.

This passage clarifies a subtle but crucial distinction in Hanafi usul: not every easing of a ruling is a literal concession. When the original determination is entirely absent in a particular context, calling it a 'concession' is only metaphorical. Two vivid examples from fiqh—the salam contract and the compelled person's consumption of wine—illustrate how this concept works in practice.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish literal from metaphorical concession
Remember: a literal concession (rukhsa haqiqiyya) presumes an underlying 'azima that is lightened. A metaphorical concession occurs when the original ruling is completely dropped, so no 'azima remains in that context. The term 'rukhsa' is then applied figuratively.
This distinction prevents confusion with abrogation (naskh).
2
Analyze the condition: 'legislated in general' (jimla)
For the concession to be metaphorical, the dropped ruling must still be legislated somewhere else. If it is dropped everywhere, it is abrogation. Here, salam contract still exists in other types of sale, and prohibition of wine still applies normally; only the specific context is exempted.
The phrase 'مشروعاً في الجملة' (legislated in general) is key.
3
Examine the first example: 'ayniyya in salam
In a normal sale, the sold item must be specific and present ('ayniyya). In salam (forward sale), this condition is entirely dropped. In fact, requiring the specific item would invalidate the contract. Hence, the dropping of the condition is a metaphorical concession.
Salam is a sales contract for future delivery, so present specific object is impossible.
4
Examine the second example: prohibition of wine and carrion for the compelled
The general prohibition of wine and carrion is lifted entirely for a person coerced or compelled by necessity, based on the Quranic exception. Since the prohibition remains in effect for others, this is a metaphorical concession, not abrogation.
Reference: Quran 6:119, 'He has only forbidden you carrion, blood, pork... but whoever is compelled, without desire and without excess, then indeed, Allah is Forgiving and Merciful.'
5
Identify the epistemological weight
This definition is part of the Hanafi usul framework, governing how concessions are categorized. It is a foundational conceptual tool for deriving rulings in sales, dietary laws, and other areas where exceptions exist.
Authority: From the mutakallimun and usuliyyun of the Hanafi school.

Commentary
This passage discusses the fourth type of concession (rukhsa) which is metaphorical (majaz). The author explains that when an original ruling is completely dropped and no longer remains legislated in a particular context, calling it a concession is only metaphorical because there is no original determination ('azima) to compare it to. Two examples are given: first, the condition that a sold item be specific and present ('ayniyya) is dropped entirely in salam (forward sale), to the extent that requiring the specific object in salam would void the contract. Second, the prohibition of wine and carrion is dropped entirely for a person who is coerced or compelled by necessity, based on the Qur'anic exception in Surah al-An'am. These rulings are legislated in general (in other contexts) but completely lifted here, so the term 'rukhsa' is applied metaphorically.

Key Terms
الرخصة (al-rukhsa): A concession that deviates from the original determination due to a valid excuse.
مجاز (majaz): Metaphorical usage, where the term is applied to something other than its original meaning due to a relation.
العزيمة (al-'azima): The original determination that is the standard ruling.

Rulings on Reports of Impurity from a Fasiq
Expansion of rulings on reports of impurity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi ruling when a fasiq reports water impurity by determining the listener's obligation based on his conjecture, and distinguish it from reports of a kafir, boy, or insane.

Core Idea
A fasiq's report creates conjecture for tayammum, but the report itself has no authority.

This lesson explores the nuanced Hanafi position on accepting reports from a sinful narrator (fasiq) regarding the impurity of water. Unlike absolute acceptance or rejection, the Hanafi school prioritizes the listener's subjective certainty. We will examine the ruling of Imam Muhammad al-Shaybani and contrast it with the case of unreliability from kafir, boy, and insane narrators.

Guided Path
1
Identify the narrator type
The passage distinguishes between a fasiq (sinner) and a kafir (disbeliever), sabi (boy), or ma'tuh (insane). Note the difference in their capacity to impose compulsion.
Fasiq has some moral standing but his report lacks authority; kafir, boy, and insane lack any authority of compulsion.
2
Determine the listener's state
The ruling depends on the listener's subjective conjecture (ghalabat al-zann). Ask: does the listener feel strong certainty that the narrator is truthful?
Conjecture is key; the report itself is not binding.
3
Apply the ruling for each narrator
For a fasiq: if listener feels truthfulness, tayammum is wajib without pouring water. For kafir, boy, insane: even if truthfulness felt, ablution is performed, not tayammum.
Exception: For kafir etc., it is better to pour water and do tayammum, but not required.
4
Understand the underlying illa
The difference stems from the qiyas: fasiq's report can create doubt sufficient for tayammum because he has some authority? Actually the text uses qiyas khafi: lack of authority of compulsion is more pronounced for kafir etc., so their report cannot even trigger conjecture.
The illa is 'lack of authority of compulsion' (la wilayat al-ilzam).

Commentary
This passage discusses the rulings when a fasiq (sinner) or other narrators (kafir, boy, insane) report impurity of water, and also addresses non-binding transactions. The correct view (as per Muhammad al-Shaybani) is that if a fasiq reports that water is impure, the listener applies his own judgment: if he feels strong conjecture that the report is true, he must perform tayammum without pouring out the water; pouring it out and then doing tayammum is more precautionary. For the kafir, boy, and insane, even if the listener feels convinced of their truthfulness, he must perform ablution with the water (treating it as pure) and not tayammum; but if he pours out the water and then does tayammum, it is better. This is because their reports do not carry authority of compulsion, but the possibility of truth exists. In transactions that are non-binding (like agency, partnership, permission to trade), the report of any discriminating person (mumayyiz) is accepted, regardless of their faith, uprightness, age, or sanity, due to general necessity. This is an exception to the normal conditions for testimony. The passage contrasts binding transactions where full requirements apply.

Key Terms
التيمم (al-tayammum): Dry ablution with earth as a substitute for ablution when water is unavailable or cannot be used due to impurity
الإلزام (al-ilzām): Compulsion or binding force; in transactions, whether the matter is enforceable or merely informational
المميز (al-mumayyiz): A child who has reached the age of discernment (around 7 years) and can understand basic transactions
غلبة الظن (ghalabat al-zann): Preponderant conjecture; a strong suspicion that reaches near certainty

Acceptance of Reports from Any Mumayyiz in Non-Binding Transactions Due to General Necessity
Expansion of rulings on reports in non-binding transactions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule that in non-binding transactions (like agency, partnership, and permission to trade), the report of any discerning person (mumayyiz) is accepted, based on the principle of general necessity (umum al-darurah).

Core Idea
Necessity relaxes the strict conditions for testimony in non-binding transactions.

This lesson examines the second major ruling in this passage: the acceptance of reports from any discriminating individual (mumayyiz) in transactions that are free from compulsion (ilzam). The Hanafi school, citing general necessity, drops conditions such as Islam, uprightness, complete intellect, and precision. We will explore the scope and limitations of this principle.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category of transactions
The passage refers to transactions that 'are free from the meaning of compulsion' (tanfakku 'an ma'na al-ilzam). Examples: agency (wakalah), partnership (mudarabah), and permission to trade (idhn fi al-tijarah). These are not binding in the sense of enforceable obligations.
Contrast with binding transactions (e.g., sales, divorces) where strict testimony conditions apply.
2
Identify the narrators allowed
The passage says 'khabar kull mumayyiz' — the report of every discriminating person is considered. This includes fasiq, kafir, boy, Muslim, non-Muslim, as long as they can discern.
Mumayyiz is one who can distinguish and understand the transaction, typically a child around 7 years.
3
Understand the reason: general necessity
The passage gives the reason: 'li-umum al-darurah al-da'iyya ila suqut sa'ir al-shara'it' — due to the generality of necessity leading to the dropping of all other conditions (Islam, uprightness, intellect, precision).
Necessity here refers to the practical need for people to transact without always having reliable witnesses.
4
Know the exception: binding aspects
If a transaction has an element of compulsion (e.g., rights involving disputes), the full conditions of testimony (uprightness, number) apply. Also, mixed cases like dismissal of an agent require at least one condition (uprightness or number).
The passage specifically excludes 'rights that involve compulsion in some respects but not others'.

Chapter of Qiyas: Istiḥsān. Like the effect of minority in guardianship of property, and it is analogous to the truthfulness of a witness being known by the appearance of the effect of his religion in any partaking of any prohibited thing, that his truthfulness prevents him from engaging in what his religion prohibits. And when the cause ('illah) with us is a cause by its effect (athar), we have given precedence to istiḥsān over qiyās, which is the hidden analogy (qiyās khafī) when its effect is strong. And we have given precedence to qiyās due to the soundness of its inner effect over istiḥsān whose effect is apparent but whose corruption is hidden, because consideration is given to the strength and soundness of the effect, not to its appearance. And the clarification of the second is in the one who recites a verse of prostration. So we interpret it as causation to remove improbability. Analogies and examples in the books of uṣūl are many, so refer to them. This is a dissection of the speech; as for the verification of the truth, this place cannot accommodate it. Like the effect of minority etc., this is a second division of effect as mentioned. And it is: knowing the validity of the description by the appearance of the effect. The truthfulness of the witness: just as the truthfulness of a witness after eligibility is known by his restraint from sins and major offenses, so that acceptance of his testimony becomes obligatory thereafter, so too the validity of the description as a cause for the ruling after suitability is known only by effect, i.e., by the appearance of its effect by text and consensus in some place, so that acting by the description becomes obligatory thereafter. And when it was objected that qiyās is a legal proof and istiḥsān is something known only to Abū Ḥanīfah, and the Ḥanafīs may abandon qiyās for istiḥsān, and is this not abandoning a legal proof for a non-legal one? He pointed to its refutation and said: 'And when it became etc.' A cause by effect, not by conjecture and analogy as others hold. 'Istiḥsān which etc.' Linguistically, it is considering something good; you say: istahsantuhu kadhā, i.e., I considered it good. In terminological uṣūl: it is the hidden analogy when its effect is strong, because consideration is given to strength and weakness, not to visibility and hiddenness; for sometimes some things are apparent and some hidden, but the hidden outweighs the apparent if it is strong, like the Hereafter despite being hidden outweighing the apparent world due to strength, which is permanence and continuity. In this is an indication that istiḥsān is not outside the four proofs, but rather it is the stronger of the two types of qiyās, and it is given precedence over qiyās only due to the strength of its effect. It is objected to us that we abandon a legal proof for a non-legal one; but it is a legal proof, indeed stronger than qiyās which is a legal proof, so we have not acted upon anything other than the four proofs, because it is included in them. And the rule for the precedence of one over the other is this: 'And we gave precedence to qiyās etc.' even if outwardly it seems defective, because consideration is given to effect, etc. It is evidence for two matters: one is the precedence of istiḥsān over qiyās, and the second is the precedence of qiyās over istiḥsān. The second of these two. The one who recites a verse of prostration etc. Its summary is that a worshipper, if he recites a verse of prostration during prayer and intends to perform the prostration in the bowing (rukū‘), by intending integration between the bowing of prayer and the prostration of recitation, as is known among the memorizers, it is permissible by qiyās. In his prayer, he bows by it by qiyās, because the text has come with it. Allah said: 'and he fell down bowing and repented' (Q 38:24). And by istiḥsān, it does not suffice him, because the law commanded us to prostrate and bowing is different; i.e., it is not sufficient, and it is the ultimate veneration like the prostration of prayer. This is an apparent effect. As for the face of qiyās, it is pure metaphor, but qiyās is more deserving than istiḥsān by its inner effect. Its explanation: that prostration upon recitation was not legislated as an intended act of worship such that it would be obligatory by a vow; rather, the purpose is merely something that suffices as humility. One of them: i.e., it is not an act of worship in itself. The face of qiyās is that bowing and prostration are similar in humility, and therefore the name 'bowing' was applied to prostration in that verse, because falling, which is to fall to the ground, does not occur in the state of bowing but in the state of prostration. So it appears that what is meant by 'bowing' in that verse is prostration. Since it is established that bowing and prostration are similar in humility, and the purpose in prostration of recitation is humility, bowing is permissible by analogy to prostration due to the shared attribute of humility. Its opposite: i.e., less in veneration, and therefore one does not substitute for the other in prayer; likewise in prostration of recitation. And to this he pointed by his saying: 'Like the prostration of prayer is not performed by bowing, etc.' So this, i.e., that bowing is not prostration and the insufficiency of one for the other, is an apparent effect at first glance, but upon precise consideration there is corruption in it, and therefore he preferred qiyās over it. As for qiyās, at first glance it is weak, and therefore the qiyās opposite it is called istiḥsān, and to this he pointed by his saying: 'As for the face of qiyās, it is pure metaphor', i.e., its proof is by metaphor; because He Almighty made bowing stand in place of prostration considering similarity in seeking nearness, and metaphor in contrast to literal truth is weak. And qiyās was based on metaphor, while istiḥsān was based on literal truth. This is the face of the appearance of the effect of istiḥsān and corruption of qiyās at first sight. By its inner effect: and the conclusion is that if qiyās is corrupt at first sight and istiḥsān is sound, but upon precise consideration qiyās is more deserving than istiḥsān, because the effect of qiyās is strong inwardly and the effect of istiḥsān is weak. So he explained the first by his saying: 'Its explanation', i.e., explanation of the inner effect of qiyās. 'It is not obligated by a vow': for if it were an intended act of worship, it would be obligatory by a vow. This is proof that it is not an intended act of worship, but rather something that suffices as humility to distinguish the obedient, submissive person from the disobedient, arrogant one, as indicated by verses of prostration, including His saying: 'And to Allah prostrates whoever is in the heavens and the earth willingly or unwillingly' (Q 13:15), and including His saying: 'Do you not see that to Allah prostrates whoever is in the heavens and whoever is on the earth' (Q 22:18), i.e., the inhabitants of the heavens and earth humble themselves to Him. So it is known that the purpose of prostration in those contexts is humility. And that: i.e., that what is meant by 'bowing' in the verse of prostration [continues].

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of istiḥsān as a Hanafi uṣūl principle. It begins by defining istiḥsān: it is a hidden analogy (qiyās khafī) that is given precedence over explicit analogy (qiyās jalī) when its effect (athar) is strong. The author uses the example of a witness: just as the credibility of a witness is known by his avoidance of sins, the validity of a descriptive attribute as a legal cause is known by the appearance of its effect. The text then addresses a common objection: how can Hanafīs leave qiyās (a valid proof) for istiḥsān (which opponents claim is arbitrary)? The response is that istiḥsān is actually a type of qiyās—the hidden kind—and is still within the four proofs (Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijmā‘, Qiyās). In fact, it is the stronger type of qiyās because its effect is stronger. The passage then explains that sometimes istiḥsān is preferred over qiyās, and sometimes qiyās is preferred over istiḥsān, depending on the strength of the effect. The example of sajda tilāwa (prostration of recitation) during prayer is used to illustrate both. A worshipper recites a verse of prostration during prayer and intends to perform the prostration in his bowing (rukū‘). By qiyās, this is permissible because the verse 'and he fell down bowing and repented' applies the word 'bowing' to prostration, and both share the purpose of humility. However, by istiḥsān, bowing is not a substitute for prostration because the law commands prostration as a specific act of veneration; bowing is less in degree and does not suffice. At first glance, istiḥsān seems stronger because it relies on literal meaning, but upon deeper analysis, the qiyās has a strong inner effect: since the purpose of sajda tilāwa is merely humility, and bowing achieves that, the inner effect of qiyās is sound and strong. Therefore, in this case, qiyās is preferred over istiḥsān. The rule is that the strength and soundness of the effect (athar) determines precedence, not its outward appearance or hiddenness.

The Sajda Tilawa Example: Applying Qiyas and Istihsan
Application of qiyas and istihsan to sajda tilawa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to derive the ruling on performing sajda tilawa in bowing using both qiyas and istihsan, and determine which takes precedence based on strength of inner effect.

Core Idea
Qiyas allows sajda tilawa in bowing due to shared humility; istihsan requires literal prostration; but qiyas's inner effect is stronger.

The example of a worshipper reciting a verse of prostration during prayer and intending to perform the sajda in his bowing (ruku') illustrates how qiyas and istihsan conflict. The passage walks through both proofs and concludes that qiyas (based on metaphor) is stronger due to its inner effect.

Guided Path
1
Set the scenario
A person recites a sajda verse during prayer and intends to perform the prostration of recitation (sajda tilawa) within his bowing (ruku') by integrating the two acts.
This is called nawa al-tadakhul.
2
Apply qiyas
By qiyas, bowing suffices because the Quran used 'bowing' to mean prostration in verse 38:24. Both share the attribute of humility, which is the purpose of sajda tilawa.
The text says: 'He fell down bowing and repented' - the word 'bowing' is used for prostration.
3
Apply istihsan
By istihsan, bowing does not suffice because the law commands prostration specifically. Bowing is different in degree of veneration, as in prayer one does not substitute for the other.
This is the apparent effect (athar zahir).
4
Determine precedence
Although istihsan appears stronger at first, upon precise consideration, qiyas has a strong inner effect: sajda tilawa is not an intended worship but mere humility, and bowing achieves that. Therefore qiyas is preferred.
The inner effect of qiyas is sound and strong.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed discussion of istiḥsān as a Hanafi uṣūl principle. It begins by defining istiḥsān: it is a hidden analogy (qiyās khafī) that is given precedence over explicit analogy (qiyās jalī) when its effect (athar) is strong. The author uses the example of a witness: just as the credibility of a witness is known by his avoidance of sins, the validity of a descriptive attribute as a legal cause is known by the appearance of its effect. The text then addresses a common objection: how can Hanafīs leave qiyās (a valid proof) for istiḥsān (which opponents claim is arbitrary)? The response is that istiḥsān is actually a type of qiyās—the hidden kind—and is still within the four proofs (Qur'an, Sunnah, Ijmā‘, Qiyās). In fact, it is the stronger type of qiyās because its effect is stronger. The passage then explains that sometimes istiḥsān is preferred over qiyās, and sometimes qiyās is preferred over istiḥsān, depending on the strength of the effect. The example of sajda tilāwa (prostration of recitation) during prayer is used to illustrate both. A worshipper recites a verse of prostration during prayer and intends to perform the prostration in his bowing (rukū‘). By qiyās, this is permissible because the verse 'and he fell down bowing and repented' applies the word 'bowing' to prostration, and both share the purpose of humility. However, by istiḥsān, bowing is not a substitute for prostration because the law commands prostration as a specific act of veneration; bowing is less in degree and does not suffice. At first glance, istiḥsān seems stronger because it relies on literal meaning, but upon deeper analysis, the qiyās has a strong inner effect: since the purpose of sajda tilāwa is merely humility, and bowing achieves that, the inner effect of qiyās is sound and strong. Therefore, in this case, qiyās is preferred over istiḥsān. The rule is that the strength and soundness of the effect (athar) determines precedence, not its outward appearance or hiddenness.

Types of Istihsan and Their Transmissibility
Explains the four types of istihsan and their transmissibility

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four types of istihsan in Hanafi usul and state which type yields a transmissible ruling.

Core Idea
Only istihsan by hidden qiyas extends; the other three types are confined to their specific evidence.

Istihsan (juristic preference) is a key Hanafi source that sets aside manifest qiyas in favor of a stronger proof. This lesson unpacks the four categories of istihsan and a crucial rule: rulings based on text, consensus, or necessity are not extendable, whereas rulings based on hidden qiyas can be analogized to new cases. Understanding this distinction sharpens your ability to apply istihsan correctly.

Guided Path
1
Define istihsan and its role
Identify that istihsan is a proof that contradicts manifest qiyas and that the conflicting proof can be text, consensus, necessity, or hidden qiyas.
Istihsan is not arbitrary; it is always grounded in a valid source.
2
List the four types
Categorize istihsan as: (1) by text (nass), (2) by consensus (ijma), (3) by necessity (darurah), and (4) by hidden qiyas (qiyas khafi).
The first three are based on specific external evidence; the fourth is a subtle analogy.
3
Apply the transmissibility rule
Understand that rulings by manifest qiyas are generally transmissible, but rulings by istihsan of the first three types are not. Only istihsan by hidden qiyas is transmissible because it is essentially a valid analogy.
Transmissibility means the ruling can be extended to other similar cases via shared 'illa.
4
Examine the examples
Analyze the given examples: salam (text), istisna' (consensus), purification of wells (necessity), and the price dispute (hidden qiyas). Confirm why each belongs to its category and why only the last is transmissible.
The price dispute case shows both parties swear – a rule derived by hidden qiyas that can apply to similar disputes.

Commentary
This passage is an analytical expansion of the Hanafi usul principle concerning istihsan (juristic preference) and its types. The author first illustrates a case where hidden analogy (qiyas khafi) operates: bowing in prayer can substitute for prostration due to a shared cause (humility), contrasting it with the manifest analogy (qiyas jali) that would deny such substitution. The critical rule stated is: rulings established by manifest qiyas are generally transmissible (mutta'addi) to similar cases, but rulings established by the other three types of istihsan (text, consensus, necessity) are not transmissible, because they are based on specific evidences rather than a universal 'illa. He then reinforces the transmissibility of istihsan by hidden qiyas with a juristic example: a dispute over the price before delivery—by manifest qiyas only the buyer would be sworn (as he denies the higher price), but by istihsan (hidden qiyas) both parties are sworn because each is both claimant and denier from different angles. Types of Istihsān and Their Extensibility
Defines and illustrates types of istihsān and ta'diya rule

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify the four types of istihsān (based on athar, ijma', darura, and qiyās khafī) and determine which types allow ta'diya (extension) of the ruling.

Core Idea
Istihsān based on text, consensus, or necessity does not extend; only istihsān from hidden analogy does.

In Hanafi usul, istihsān (juristic preference) is a recognized source that can override qiyās. However, not all istihsān rulings are created equal. This lesson examines the four bases of istihsān according to the classical Hanafi text, and the crucial distinction between those which are confined to their specific case and those which can be extended to new situations via shared 'illa. Understanding ta'diya is essential for correctly applying istihsān in ijtihad.

The passage strictly adheres to the Hanafi method: identifying the source of istihsān determines its applicability and extensibility.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on a ruling about istihsan in a sale dispute. It explains that the rule of mutual oath (tahāluḥ) and rescission of sale, which is established by a hidden analogy (qiyas khafi) based on the hadith, applies not only to the original parties but also to their heirs and to ijarah contracts, provided the dispute occurs before possession. However, after possession has occurred, the situation changes: the direct analogy would require only the buyer to swear an oath, but the hadith (which speaks of 'returning' the goods) shows that mutual oath still applies between the original parties even after possession. This is an instance of istihsan overriding the apparent analogy. But according to the two shaykhs (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani), this istihsan ruling after possession is specific to the original parties and does not extend to heirs or to ijarah, because it is based on an exceptional tradition that contradicts the general analogy, and therefore it must be confined to its original case. Imam Muhammad, however, extends it to all cases. This illustrates how istihsan operates as a source of law and how its scope may be limited based on the strength of the evidence.

This passage is a detailed discussion of the role of intellect ('aql) in Islamic legal theory, specifically the dispute between the Mu'tazila, Ash'ariyya, and the Hanafi school. The Rule of Extended Insanity (Imtidad) and Its Effect on Obligation
Explanation of the limits of extension for insanity and its effect on obligation of worship

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi thresholds for extended insanity (imtidad) for fasting, prayer, and zakat, and apply the doctrine of istihsan to distinguish between extended and non-extended cases.

Core Idea
Extended insanity drops both performance and obligation; non-extended incidental insanity does not, by istihsan.

The Hanafi school treats insanity (junūn) as a celestial impediment that can, under certain conditions, remove the obligation of acts of worship. The key is the concept of ‘extension’ (imtidād): continuous insanity lasting specific durations. This lesson examines the thresholds set by the Hanafi imams and the reasoning behind them, particularly the use of istihsān over strict qiyās.

Guided Path
1
Identify the thresholds of imtidad for each worship
Find in the passage the specific continuous durations that define extended insanity for fasting (month), prayer (day+night), and zakat (year).
These thresholds are essential for determining whether obligation is dropped.
2
Distinguish extended vs non-extended insanity
Extended insanity (mumtadd) nullifies both the obligation of performance (wujūb al-adā') and the obligation itself (nafs al-wujūb). Non-extended insanity (ghayr mumtadd) does not drop obligation if incidental, by istihsān.
Note the subcategories: original (born insane) vs incidental (after puberty).
3
Understand the istihsān reasoning
The Hanafi use istihsān to analogize non-extended incidental insanity to sleep and fainting, which do not remove obligation. The basis is lack of continuous hardship (ḥaraj).
This is the key difference from the Shāfi‘ī/qiyās view.
4
Compare with the opposing school's qiyās
Al-Shāfi‘ī and Zufar hold that all insanity drops obligation because loss of intellect removes capacity for performance (qiyās). The Hanafi response: hardship is the criterion, not mere loss of intellect.
This illustrates the conflict between qiyās jaliy (explicit analogy) and istihsān.
5
Note intra-Hanafī ikhtilāf on original non-extended insanity
For original non-extended insanity (born insane), Abū Ḥanīfa drops the obligation (like minority), while Muḥammad does not (like incidental). The text says Muḥammad's view is ẓāhir al-riwāya.
Ponder this slippery slope (مزال الأقدام).

Death does not negate need; the deceased retains rights to fulfill his needs from his estate before heirs inherit.

While many obligations end, the law recognizes that the deceased retains certain rights and needs that must be addressed. Thus the remaining payment becomes inheritance for the slave's heirs, and his children born or bought during kitaba become free. This is the madhhab of Ali and Ibn Mas'ud. Because the husband is an owner: because the ownership of marriage cannot transfer to heirs, so it remains suspended until removal by end of waiting period. Washing is among his needs. When ownership is lost, marriage is lifted with all its ties, so touch and looking are not permissible.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a matn statement 'The deceased has the ruling of the living in the matters of the afterlife.' The commentator expands this principle by applying it to several fiqh rulings, demonstrating how qiyas (analogy) works in Hanafi usul. The core principle is that death does not eliminate all legal capacity; the deceased retains rights and obligations necessary for his needs—both worldly (e.g., washing, debt payment) and otherworldly (reward). The commentator first explains the priority order for spending from the estate: preparation (kafan, burying) precedes debts if debts are not tied to a specific asset; debts precede bequests; bequests (within one-third) precede inheritance. This order reflects the intensity of need: covering the body is more urgent than paying debts, and clearing liability is more urgent than voluntary charity. The benefit of these priorities to the deceased is outlined: immediate preparation, relief from liability, and reward for bequests. Even inheritance benefits his soul through his heirs' enrichment and their supplications or charity on his behalf. Next, the commentator applies the principle to the contract of kitaba (manumission by payment). If the master dies, the contract continues because the deceased master needs the reward from manumission. If the slave dies with sufficient funds, the contract also continues so that the slave is deemed free at the last moment, ensuring his children are free and any remaining payment becomes inheritance. This is the position of Ali and Ibn Mas'ud, contrasting with Zayd ibn Thabit and al-Shafi'i who hold the contract is annulled. Then the commentator discusses the ruling on a wife washing her deceased husband. Since the husband is the 'owner' (malik) in marriage, his ownership continues for his needs (including washing) during the 'idda period. Thus she may wash him. However, a wife is 'owned' (mamluka), and her capacity for ownership ceases at death, so her husband may not wash her. This distinction is rooted in the nature of marriage: the husband's right of ownership does not transfer to heirs but remains until the 'idda ends. Any act not needed by the deceased (like intimacy) is impermissible. The commentator then shifts to the conversion of retaliation (qisas) into blood money (diyah). The original (asl) is qisas, which benefits the heirs directly (they seek revenge or pardon). But when qisas converts to diyah (e.g., due to pardon or confusion), the diyah is treated as any other wealth: it first fulfills the deceased's needs (debts, bequests) and then passes to heirs by succession. This is because diyah, being property, can serve the deceased's needs, whereas qisas cannot. The commentator clarifies that this does not contradict the rule that the substitute follows the original; here, the original and substitute differ in their capacity to benefit the deceased, so the ruling differs—just as tayammum replaces wudu but requires intention because water is inherently purifying while earth is essentially impure and needs a devotional act.

Key Terms
الحاجة (al-hāja): The need of the deceased for worldly or otherworldly benefits that persist after death.
الخلافة (al-khilāfa): Succession: the heir takes the deceased's place after his needs are fulfilled.

Applications: Kitaba, Washing Spouse, and Conversion of Qisas to Diyah
Detailed applications of the principle of need to kitaba, spousal washing, and qisas conversion, including a madhhab dispute.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of need to three specific cases: the continuation of kitaba after death, the washing of the deceased spouse, and the treatment of diyah when qisas converts to property. You will also evaluate the madhhab dispute on spouse-washing.

Core Idea
The deceased's need for reward, clearance, and dignity shapes rulings on kitaba, spousal washing, and diyah distribution.

Having established the principle that the deceased retains rights for needs, this lesson applies it to three concrete cases. First, the contract of kitaba (manumission by payment) continues after the death of either party to benefit the deceased master or slave. Second, the washing of the deceased spouse is governed by the nature of marital ownership—only the husband (as owner) may be washed by his wife, not vice versa. Third, when retaliation (qisas) converts to blood money (diyah), the diyah is treated as property belonging first to the deceased for his needs before passing to heirs. Each application demonstrates how need (al-haja) serves as the effective cause (illah) in Hanafi legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Apply to Kitaba (Manumission Contract)
The principle that the deceased retains needs for reward and otherworldly benefit justifies the continuation of kitaba after death. If the master dies, the contract continues so he can earn reward from manumission. If the slave dies with sufficient funds, the contract continues so he is deemed free at the last moment. This is the position of Ali and Ibn Mas'ud, contrasting with Zayd ibn Thabit and al-Shafi'i who rule annulment.
2
Apply to Washing the Deceased Spouse
The husband is the owner (malik) in marriage, so his need for washing persists during the 'idda. The wife is owned (mamluka), so her capacity for ownership ends at death, meaning the husband cannot wash her. This leads to a dispute with the Shafi'i school, which relies on hadith reports.
3
Apply to Conversion of Qisas to Diyah
When qisas (retaliation) converts to diyah (blood money) due to pardon or confusion, the diyah is treated as any other property: it first satisfies the deceased's needs (debts, bequests) before passing to heirs by succession. This contrasts with qisas itself, which benefits heirs directly because it does not serve the deceased's need.
The commentator uses the analogy of wudu and tayammum to explain why the substitute (diyah) may differ from the original (qisas) in its ruling.
4
Synthesize the Common Principle
In all three cases, the key factor is whether the ruling serves a genuine need of the deceased. If it does (reward, clearance, dignity), the deceased retains capacity; if not (e.g., intimacy in marriage), capacity ceases.

For that: i.e., because the deceased retains from his estate what fulfills his need. His preparation means his shrouding and burial takes precedence over payment of debts if the right is not attached to a specific asset because the need for preparation is more intense. His bequests because his need for them is stronger than the right of heirs since their benefit returns to him in the afterlife. Then inheritance is obligated etc. i.e., these rights are established for his benefit. The benefit of preparation, debt payment, and bequest execution is apparent. As for the benefit of inheritance, his soul is delighted by their enrichment, and he obtains reward in the afterlife from their use of his wealth, and perhaps they supplicate for him and give charity. Kitaba remains etc.: If the master dies and the slave-in-kitaba is alive, the kitaba is ruled to continue due to the deceased's need for reward. The slave pays the kitaba to the heirs and becomes free, so the deceased gets reward for manumission and for the payment reaching his heirs—no dispute. Similarly, if the slave dies leaving enough to pay kitaba, the kitaba continues until the slave's heirs pay, because the master needs him to be free, severed from disbelief, remaining with the effect of freedom, so he is ruled free at the last part of his life. Thus the remaining payment becomes inheritance for the slave's heirs, and his children born or bought during kitaba become free. This is the madhhab of Ali and Ibn Mas'ud. Zayd ibn Thabit said the kitaba is annulled and all wealth goes to the master. Al-Shafi'i said similarly. And we said is connected to 'remains', i.e., for this. Because the husband is an owner: because the ownership of marriage cannot transfer to heirs, so it remains suspended until removal by end of waiting period. Washing is among his needs. As for what is not among his needs, he has no ownership over it after death because the deceased is no longer a locus for specific dispositions of ownership. When ownership is lost, marriage is lifted with all its ties, so touch and looking are not permissible.

For this: i.e., because what is legislated for the servant's need remains owned by him after death to the extent that fulfills his need. Commentary
This passage is a commentary (sharh) on a matn statement 'The deceased has the ruling of the living in the matters of the afterlife.' The commentator expands this principle by applying it to several fiqh rulings, demonstrating how qiyas (analogy) works in Hanafi usul. The core principle is that death does not eliminate all legal capacity; the deceased retains rights and obligations necessary for his needs—both worldly (e.g., washing, debt payment) and otherworldly (reward). The commentator first explains the priority order for spending from the estate: preparation (kafan, burying) precedes debts if debts are not tied to a specific asset; debts precede bequests; bequests (within one-third) precede inheritance. This order reflects the intensity of need: covering the body is more urgent than paying debts, and clearing liability is more urgent than voluntary charity. The benefit of these priorities to the deceased is outlined: immediate preparation, relief from liability, and reward for bequests. Even inheritance benefits his soul through his heirs' enrichment and their supplications or charity on his behalf. Next, the commentator applies the principle to the contract of kitaba (manumission by payment). If the master dies, the contract continues because the deceased master needs the reward from manumission. If the slave dies with sufficient funds, the contract also continues so that the slave is deemed free at the last moment, ensuring his children are free and any remaining payment becomes inheritance. This is the position of Ali and Ibn Mas'ud, contrasting with Zayd ibn Thabit and al-Shafi'i who hold the contract is annulled. Then the commentator discusses the ruling on a wife washing her deceased husband. Since the husband is the 'owner' (malik) in marriage, his ownership continues for his needs (including washing) during the 'idda period. Thus she may wash him. However, a wife is 'owned' (mamluka), and her capacity for ownership ceases at death, so her husband may not wash her. This distinction is rooted in the nature of marriage: the husband's right of ownership does not transfer to heirs but remains until the 'idda ends. Any act not needed by the deceased (like intimacy) is impermissible. The commentator then shifts to the conversion of retaliation (qisas) into blood money (diyah). The original (asl) is qisas, which benefits the heirs directly (they seek revenge or pardon). But when qisas converts to diyah (e.g., due to pardon or confusion), the diyah is treated as any other wealth: it first fulfills the deceased's needs (debts, bequests) and then passes to heirs by succession. This is because diyah, being property, can serve the deceased's needs, whereas qisas cannot. The commentator clarifies that this does not contradict the rule that the substitute follows the original; here, the original and substitute differ in their capacity to benefit the deceased, so the ruling differs—just as tayammum replaces wudu but requires intention because water is inherently purifying while earth is essentially impure and needs a devotional act.

Key Terms
الحاجة (al-hāja): The need of the deceased for worldly or otherworldly benefits that persist after death.
الخلافة (al-khilāfa): Succession: the heir takes the deceased's place after his needs are fulfilled.
انقلاب القصاص (inqilāb al-qiṣāṣ): Conversion of retaliation (qiṣāṣ) into property (diyah) due to pardon, confusion, or other causes.

Error (Khatta) as an Acquired Impediment in Hanafi Usul
Defines error as an impediment excusing from divine obligations

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define error (khatta) as an acquired impediment in Hanafi usul, identify the condition of ijtihad, and apply the distinction between divine and human rights to examples.

Core Idea
Error excuses divine obligations but requires compensation for human rights; it only becomes valid if preceded by ijtihad.

Error is a fundamental concept in Hanafi usul. It is considered an acquired impediment that can drop the right of Allah, but only when the person has engaged in ijtihad and still made a mistake. However, error does not excuse violations of others' rights, and there is a remaining element of negligence that calls for kaffara. This lesson unpacks the definition, the rational dispute with the Mu'tazila, and the practical applications.

Guided Path
1
Grasp the definition of khatta
Read the definition: error is doing an act without complete intent (qasd). Example: shooting at game but hitting a person. The intent is incomplete because the result was not intended.
Key: error is defined in contrast to 'sawab' (correctness).
2
Identify the condition of ijtihad
The text says error becomes an excuse only 'if it occurs after ijtihad'. This means the person must have made a sincere effort; mere negligence does not count.
Example: erring in the qibla direction after ijtihad makes the prayer valid; no sin.
3
Distinguish divine and human rights
Error drops the right of Allah (e.g., no hadd or qisas) but does not drop the rights of people. If you mistakenly destroy another's property, you must guarantee it.
The text uses phrases like 'right of Allah' and 'rights of people' (huquq al-ibad).
4
Analyze the rational possibility of punishment
The Mu'tazila argued that punishment for error is irrational because there is no intent. The Hanafi response uses the Quranic verse where believers ask not to be taken to task for error, implying it could happen. But the punishment has been lifted by the Prophet's supplication.
This shows the Hanafi view that error is a doubt (shubha) that wards off punishments.
5
Apply to practical cases
Study the applications: accidental killing (kaffara but no qisas), mistaken divorce (valid according to Hanafi), mistaken sale (fasid like the sale of one compelled).
These illustrate how the principles play out in concrete fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage defines error (khatta) as an acquired impediment in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author explains that error is a type of excuse that can drop the right of Allah (huquq Allah) when it occurs after ijtihad, i.e., after making a sincere effort to ascertain the correct ruling. Error also serves as a doubt (shubha) that averts punishment (hadd or qisas). The one who errs does not incur sin (ithm) and is not punished with hadd or qisas. However, there remains a form of negligence (taqsir) that calls for a lesser reparation (al-jaza' al-qasir), such as kaffara for accidental killing. The author then enters a discussion with the Mu'tazila: they argue that rational punishment for error is impermissible because there is no intent; the Hanafi position is that it is rationally permissible but has been dropped by a special divine grace due to the Prophet's supplication. The author also clarifies that error is not an excuse for violating the rights of others; if one mistakenly takes another's property, he must guarantee it. Finally, the passage applies the ruling: a divorce uttered by mistake is valid (contrary to Shafi'i), and a sale contract mistakenly concluded is valid but defective (fasid), like the sale of one compelled. This demonstrates that the ruling is based on the external cause (the utterance) and the capacity of the person, despite the lack of intent.

Key Terms
الخطأ (al-khata'): An act done without complete intent (qasd) to cause the result, such as shooting at game and hitting a person.
عذر ('udh'r): An excuse that waives divine liability.
اجتهاد (ijtihad): Exertion of effort to reach a correct ruling in matters where no clear text exists.

Key Takeaway
Error is an acquired impediment that excuses from divine punishment only if preceded by ijtihad, but it does not waive compensation for human rights nor does it completely remove the need for kaffara due to the element of negligence.
