The Three Primary Sources of Shari'ah
Defining the three primary sources and explaining the terminology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three primary sources of Shari'ah and explain why the author used 'uṣūl al-shar‘' instead of 'uṣūl al-fiqh'.

Core Idea
The foundations of Shari'ah are three: the Book, the Sunnah, and the consensus of the Ummah.

This lesson introduces the first part of the passage, where the author lays out the three primary sources of Islamic law: the Quran, the Sunnah, and the consensus of the mujtahids. The author deliberately chooses the term 'shar‘' over 'fiqh' to emphasize that these sources underpin both theoretical beliefs and practical rulings, not just jurisprudence.

Guided Path
1
Define 'aṣl' and 'shar‘'
Identify the linguistic and technical meanings of the two key terms in 'usul al-shar‘'. Note that 'aṣl' means foundation or evidence, and 'shar‘' means law or religion.
The plural 'usul' indicates multiple foundations.
2
Explain the choice of 'shar‘' over 'fiqh'
Analyze why the author says 'usul al-shar‘' and not 'usul al-fiqh'. The passage states that din (religion) includes both theoretical and practical rulings, while fiqh covers only practical ones.
Theoretical rulings include creed ('aqidah), which is outside fiqh.
3
List the three primary sources
Identify the three sources explicitly mentioned: al-Kitab (the Book), al-Sunnah, and Ijma‘ al-Ummah (consensus of the Ummah).
These are the foundations from every aspect.
4
Understand the scope of 'the Book' and 'the Sunnah'
The passage clarifies that 'the Book' refers only to the approximately 500 legal verses, and 'the Sunnah' to about 3000 legal hadiths, not the entirety.
This distinguishes the legal portions from narratives and parables.
5
Interpret 'Ijma of the Ummah'
The passage explains that 'Ummah' here means all mujtahids of the Ummah of Muhammad, not a specific group like the Companions or the people of Madinah.
This is the correct Hanafi position.

الشرع للعهد، أي أصول الشارع المعهود وهو النبي كبيت الله وناقة الله، وبمعنى المشروع، فاللام للجنس أي أدلة الأحكام المشروعة. ثم بين الثلاثة بقوله: الكتاب والمراد به بعضه وهو مقدار خمسمائة آية؛ لأنه أصل الشرع، والباقي قصص وأمثال وغيره والسنّة والمراد بها هنا أيضا بعضها ومقدار ثلاثة آلاف على ما قيل وإجماع الأمة: أي إجماع مجتهدي ** أمة محمد هلا والله لأن هذه الشرافة مخصوصة بأمته عليه، وفي لفظ الأمة إشارة إلى أنه لا خصوصية لإجماع الصحابة وأهل المدينة وعترة النبي على ما هو المذهب الصحيح، كما سيأتى إن شاء الله تعالى، ولما كان القياس أصلاً من وجه وفرعاً من وجه بخلاف الأصول الثلاثة؛ لأنها أصول من كل وجه، أفرده بالذكر. القياس المستنبط من هذه الأصول الثلاثة، أما نظير القياس المستنبط من الكتاب فقياس حرمة اللواطة على حرمة الوطي في حالة الحيض الثابتة بقوله تعالى: {قُلْ هُوَ أَذًى فَاعْتَزِلُوا النِّسَاءَ فِي الْمَحِيضِ} (البقرة: 222)، والعلة المشتركة بينهما هي الأذى، كذا قالوا أقول فيه نظر؛ إذ من شروط صحة القياس عدم النص في الفرع، فإن كان المراد من الفرع اللواطة بالرجال فحرمته ثابت بالكتاب، قال الله تعالى: {وَالَّذَانِ يَأْتِيَانِهَا} (النساء:16)،

للجنس: أي ليس للعهد لعدم المعهود والاستغراق؛ لأن هذه الثلاثة ليست بأصول بعض الأحكام، مثل وجوب الإيمان بالله وبالنبي؛ لأنه مثبت الثلاثة لا مثبت بها. مجتهدي: وذلك لأن المجتهدين هم المرادون من مطلق الأمة.
In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful. After praising Allah for His bestowal and sending blessings upon His Messenger Muhammad and his family, indeed the foundations of the Shari'ah are three: the Book, the Sunnah, and the consensus of the Ummah, and the fourth foundation is analogical reasoning derived from these foundations.
In the name of Allah, the Most Gracious, the Most Merciful.
"Uṣūl al-Shar‘": al-uṣūl is the plural of aṣl, which linguistically is that upon which something else is built, and here it means the evidences. Al-Shar‘ linguistically means manifestation; He said: {He has ordained for you of religion what He enjoined upon Noah} (Ash-Shura: 13). It can also mean the way. Technically, it is the religion, the Shari‘ah, the din, and the millah (all are one with only a conceptual difference). Thus the meaning becomes: the evidences of the religion are three. He did not say “uṣūl al-fiqh” because religion is more general than fiqh, as it includes theoretical and practical rulings, while fiqh includes only the latter. Since these three are the foundation of both theoretical and practical matters, he said “uṣūl al-shar‘” and not “uṣūl al-fiqh” to avoid misunderstanding that it is exclusive to fiqh. Some commentators strained, saying: “al-Shar‘” can mean the lawgiver, so the lam in “Allah” is for the three legislated things, and the idāfah is for glorification; or “al-Shar‘” means the legislated, so the lam is for the genus, i.e., the evidences of the legislated rulings. Then he explained the three by saying: “The Book” meaning part of it, namely five hundred verses because it is the foundation of the Shari‘ah; the rest are stories, parables, etc. “And the Sunnah” meaning here also part of it, amounting to three thousand (as is said). “And the consensus of the Ummah” i.e., the consensus of the mujtahids ** of the Ummah of Muhammad, because this honor is specific to his Ummah. And the word “Ummah” indicates that there is no specificity for the consensus of the Companions, the people of Madinah, or the Prophet’s family, according to the correct school, as will come, Allah willing. Since qiyas is a source in one respect and a branch in another, unlike the three sources which are sources from every aspect, he specified it separately. “Al-qiyās al-mustanbaṭ min hādhihi al-uṣūl al-thalāthah” – as for an example of qiyas derived from the Book, it is the analogy of the prohibition of sodomy (liwāṭah) to the prohibition of intercourse during menstruation established by His verse: {Say: it is harm, so keep away from women during menstruation} (Al-Baqarah: 222), and the common cause between them is harm. Thus they said. I say: there is consideration in this, because among the conditions for the validity of qiyas is the absence of a textual ruling on the branch. If the intended branch is sodomy with men, then its prohibition is established by the Book: Allah said: {And the two who commit it} (An-Nisa: 16).

[Footnote:] “For the genus”: meaning it is not for a specific covenant, due to the absence of a known specific, nor for universality, because these three are not the foundations of some rulings, such as the obligation of belief in Allah and the Prophet, because that is established by the three, not through them. “Mujtahidī”: because the mujtahids are meant by the absolute term “Ummah.”

Qiyas as the Fourth Source: Example and Critique
Explaining qiyas as a fourth source with an example and its critique

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the role of qiyas as a fourth source and identify the condition that invalidates a qiyas when a direct textual ruling exists.

Core Idea
Qiyas is valid only when the branch case lacks a direct textual ruling; a flawed qiyas example illustrates this condition.

This lesson focuses on the second part of the passage, where the author introduces qiyas as the fourth source and provides an example of qiyas derived from the Quran. However, he immediately criticizes the example because the supposed branch (sodomy with men) already has a direct textual ruling, making the qiyas unnecessary and invalid. This teaches a crucial condition for the validity of qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the status of qiyas
The passage states that qiyas is a source in one respect and a branch in another, unlike the three primary sources. Understand this dual nature.
Qiyas derives its authority from the primary sources.
2
Analyze the example of qiyas
The example given: analogy of sodomy (liwāṭah) to intercourse during menstruation, using the common cause of harm. Note the verse quoted: {Say: it is harm...}.
The verse prohibits intercourse during menstruation due to harm.
3
Understand the critique
The author says 'there is consideration' and points out that a condition for valid qiyas is the absence of a direct textual ruling on the branch. If the branch (sodomy with men) already has a text, qiyas is invalid.
The text for sodomy with men is from Surah an-Nisa:16.
4
Apply the condition
State the condition: qiyas is only permissible when the branch case is not already covered by a primary source (Quran, Sunnah, or Ijma).
This is a fundamental principle in Hanafi usul.

The Verbal Definition of the Book in Hanafi Usul
Parsing the verbal definition of the Book

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a verbal definition (ta'rif lafzi) and a real definition (ta'rif haqiqi) in Hanafi usul, and apply this distinction to the definition of the Book as the Quran.

Core Idea
The definition of the Book is a verbal definition because 'Quran' is more famous than 'Book'.

This passage introduces the primary source of Islamic law: the Book (al-Kitab), i.e., the Quran. The author defines it by using a more famous term, a technique called verbal definition. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correctly interpreting legal definitions in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify linguistic and technical meanings
First, note that 'kitab' linguistically means 'written' but technically refers to the Quran. Similarly, 'quran' means 'reading' but technically means the specific revelation. This dual usage is common in usul.
The passage gives examples like Sibawayh's book to show general custom.
2
Distinguish verbal vs. real definition
A verbal definition explains an unfamiliar term by a more familiar synonym. A real definition sets out the essential attributes. Here, 'Kitab' is defined by 'Quran', which is a synonym, so it is verbal.
The commentator explicitly says 'this is a verbal definition'.
3
Analyze the objection about definiendum in definition
Some object that since 'Quran' and 'Book' are synonyms, mentioning 'Quran' in the definition repeats the definiendum, a logical flaw. The commentator responds that 'Quran' here is not a synonym but a proper noun, and the additional words are part of a real definition of the Quran, not the Book.
This shows careful attention to logical structure.
4
Evaluate the alternative interpretation
Another view says 'Quran' here is a verbal noun meaning 'the read' (maqru'), which includes all speech. Then 'revealed' restricts it to divine revelation. This avoids the synonymy issue.
This illustrates how usul scholars consider multiple readings.

Definition of Command and the Dispute over Its Default Meaning
Defines amr and presents the debate on its default meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of the imperative form (amr) and apply the majority proof to justify that its default meaning is obligation (wujub).

Core Idea
The imperative form is khass for obligation until a qarīna redirects it.

This passage introduces a foundational usul dispute: when Allah or the Prophet says 'do', does it automatically obligate? The Hanafi school, with the majority of jurists, says yes. The imperative form ('if‘al') is a specific wording placed for a specific meaning—obligation. But other schools and scholars argue it could mean recommendation or permission. Let's trace the proofs that establish the default rule.

Guided Path
1
Define amr and its scope
Identify the definition of 'amr' as 'a specific wording from the derivations of the verb placed for demanding an action'. Note that this excludes nouns and particles.
The definition refutes those who include actions as commands.
2
Identify the main dispute
Recognize that scholars differ over the default meaning of the imperative form when free from context. Is it haqiqah in obligation, recommendation, permission, or a verbal homonym?
The text lists several views, including that of al-Ash'ari, al-Shafi'i, and Malikis.
3
State the Hanafi/majority position
The majority of jurists (jumhur) and the Hanafi school hold that the imperative is haqiqah (real) in obligation and majaz (metaphorical) in other meanings.
This is the view of Abu Hanifa and his companions.
4
Examine the proofs
The majority adduce three proofs: a Quranic verse (rebuking Iblis), consensus (ijma) of the community inferring obligation from commands, and linguistic practice (a master's command implies obligation).
The Iblis verse is the strongest proof; note that the rebuke would be unjust if the command were not obligating.
5
Understand the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: a command without context obligates action (Hanafi), recommends it (Shafi'i in one view), or permits it (some Malikis).
This affects how a mufti derives rulings from primary texts.

The Hanafi Principle of Shar' Man Qablana: When Previous Laws Bind Us
Determining which previous prophetic laws apply to Muslims

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi condition (narrated without disapproval) to determine whether a previous prophetic law is binding on Muslims.

Core Idea
A previous law binds us only if the Quran or Sunnah recounts it without disapproval.

This lesson introduces a key usul al-fiqh principle: how the Hanafi school determines which laws of previous prophets remain obligatory for Muslims. The passage explains that not all earlier sharai’ are binding; rather, only those that the Quran or authentic hadith narrate without any note of disapproval (inkar). Understanding this principle is crucial for correctly deriving rulings and avoiding the fallacy that all biblical laws are automatically applicable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the usul rule
Locate the core rule in the passage: 'ما قص الله أو رسوله منها من غير إنكار يلزمنا على أنه شريعة لرسولنا' — whatever Allah or His Messenger narrates from previous laws without disapproval is binding upon us as Sharia for our Prophet.
The rule is not that all previous laws are binding, but only those specifically affirmed.
2
Recognize the operative condition
The key condition is 'without disapproval' (min ghayri inkar). The passage contrasts two types of Quranic narrations: those mentioned neutrally (like qisas in 5:45) and those mentioned as punishments (like dietary restrictions in 4:160 and 6:146). The latter are not binding on Muslims.
Disapproval can be explicit (e.g., 'That We requited them for their transgression') or implied.
3
Apply the rule to the examples
Work through the examples: The law of qisas (retaliation) from 5:45 is binding because it is narrated without disapproval. The prohibitions of good foods and fats from 4:160 and 6:146 are not binding because they are presented as punishments for Jewish transgression.
This shows that the same source (Quran) can either ratify or abrogate previous laws based on context.
4
Grasp the evidentiary basis
The passage cites Quranic verses as the primary dalil. The Hanafi position is that previous laws known only through corrupted scriptures (Torah, Gospel) are not reliable; they must be confirmed by recited revelation (Quran) or non-recited revelation (Sunnah).
The condition of tawatur is not met for earlier books, so we cannot base rulings on them directly.
5
Understand the limitation
Even if a previous law is mentioned in the Quran without disapproval, it is binding only as part of our Sharia, not because it was revealed to an earlier prophet. The passage emphasizes that the binding force comes from its incorporation into Islamic revelation.
Thus, we do not follow the Torah; we follow the Quran's affirmation of a law originally given to Banu Isra'il.

The Lām al-'Āqibah Argument in Zakat Distribution
Refuting the Shafi'i position on mandatory distribution to all eight zakat categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul concept of lām al-'āqibah to distinguish the Hanafi position from the Shafi'i position on zakat distribution.

Core Idea
The lām in the zakat verse indicates consequence, not ownership, so zakat may be given to one category or individual.

This passage presents a critical Hanafi response to the Shafi'i interpretation of Quran 9:60. Imam al-Shafi'i argued that the lām indicates ownership and thus requires distribution to all eight categories. The Hanafi author refutes this by identifying the lām as lām al-'āqibah (consequence), demonstrating that zakat remains Allah's right until it reaches the recipient. By working through this dispute, you will learn how grammatical analysis in usul al-fiqh produces practical differences in fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Shafi'i dalil and its linguistic basis
The Shafi'i school argues that the lām in 'lil-fuqarā' indicates tamlīk (ownership), which would require distribution to all eight categories. They support this with an analogy: if someone bequeaths one-third of his wealth to multiple groups, it is divided among them by partnership.
Note the bequest analogy: the lām is taken to indicate shared ownership.
2
Analyze the Hanafi counter-argument: lām al-'āqibah
The Hanafi author provides two proofs: (a) Quranic usage in the Moses verse (Quran 28:8) where lām indicates consequence, not ownership, and (b) the fact that zakat is initially a right of Allah, so the lām can only indicate the ultimate consequence after payment.
Focus on how the Hanafi interpretation changes the ruling from mandatory sharing to need-based distribution.
3
Examine the refutation of the Shafi'i analogy
The bequest analogy is invalid because bequest transfers ownership from the outset, whereas zakat is Allah's right until payment. The Shafi'i analogy conflates a contract of ownership (bequest) with an obligation of servitude (zakat).
The Hanafi response highlights the difference in the nature of the obligation.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical difference)
The Hanafi position allows giving all zakat to one category (e.g., only the poor) or even one individual, provided need is fulfilled. The Shafi'i position requires distribution to all eight categories if possible. The underlying illah (cause) according to Hanafis is need (hajah), not ownership.
Practical consequence: Hanafis have flexibility in zakat distribution, while Shafi'is must ensure all eight categories receive a share.
5
Evaluate the tarjih (preferred view) and its usul basis
The text clearly favors the Hanafi position, supported by two proofs: the lām of consequence and the nature of zakat as Allah's right. The Shafi'i view is seen as a misapplication of the lām particle.
The tarjih here is based on linguistic analysis and the nature of the obligation.

Even if the lam is tamlik, ownership is deferred until after payment; the poor take on behalf of Allah initially. The Kaaba analogy shows they are channels, not entitled owners.
The Kaaba is a direction for prayer, not an entitled recipient.
5

Hanafi: permissible to give all zakah to one category even one person. Shafi'i: must distribute to at least three categories.
This is the thamarah al-khilaf (fruit of the difference).

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi disputation regarding the interpretation of Surah al-Tawbah 9:60. The author is responding to a Shafi'i objection that the Hanafi position of allowing zakah distribution to a single category contradicts the apparent meaning of the verse. The core of the dispute is the function of the lam in 'lil-fuqara'. The Hanafi author argues that it is lam al-aqibah (the lam of consequence), not lam al-tamlik (the lam of ownership). He provides two proofs: first, the payment to Allah precedes ownership; second, the term 'sadaqat' implies post-payment status. The analogy with the Kaaba illustrates that the poor are directions for worship, not possessors of a right. The Shafi'i view is refuted both by affirming the lam as aqibah and by conceding even if it were tamlik, the ownership is deferred. The discussion ends with the transition to the next topic: the rukn (pillar) of qiyas.

Key Terms
لام العاقبة (lam al-aqibah): The lam of consequence, indicating the final result or outcome of an action, not the immediate purpose.
لام التمليك (lam al-tamlik): The lam of ownership, indicating immediate transfer of ownership.
مصارف (masarif): Channels or categories for disbursement of zakah; not necessarily owners but recipients due to need.

The Hanafi View on the Insufficiency of Intellect for Obligation
Defends the Hanafi view that intellect alone does not obligate faith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on the role of intellect in legal obligation and apply the hadith 'رفع القلم عن ثلاث' to derive that obligation requires divine address or its substitute.

Core Idea
Intellect perceives good and evil, but only divine address obligates.

This passage defends the Hanafi doctrine that intellect alone is not sufficient to make a person legally obligated (mukallaf). The Hanafis steer a middle course between the Mu'tazila, who hold intellect sufficient, and some Ash'aris who dismiss intellect's role. The key evidence is the prophetic hadith that exempts children and the insane from accountability. From this, the Hanafis analogize to the case of adults who never received the Islamic call: they are excused until the call reaches them or they are given reasonable time to reflect. Understanding this ruling clarifies the nature of taklif in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the general rule
Recognize that the author states straight away: 'وما بالعقل كفاية' – intellect is not sufficient. This is the core Hanafi claim.
2
Extract the primary dalil
Locate the hadith 'رفع القلم عن ثلاث...' quoted in the commentary. Note its chain and the fact it is ahad but has clear meaning (dalala qati).
This hadith is the foundation of the Hanafi argument.
3
Analyze the analogy (qiyas)
The hadith exempts children (no puberty) and the insane (no intellect). By analogy, one who lacks the call (da'wah) is also exempt, since obligation requires either puberty or the arrival of the divine address.
The analogy is based on the shared illa: absence of the full cause of obligation.
4
Compare with opposing schools
Contrast the Hanafi position with the Mu'tazila (intellect suffices) and the Ash'ari view (intellect irrelevant). Understand why the Hanafi middle ground is preferred.
5
Apply to concrete cases
Work through the examples given: the adolescent girl under a Muslim husband, the isolated adult on a mountain, and the time-limit analogy of the foolish person's 25 years.
These cases illustrate how the rule operates in practice.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul al-fiqh explanation of the Hanafi position on the role of intellect (عقل) in legal obligation (تكليف). The author states the general rule: intellect alone is not sufficient for obligation. The main evidence is the hadith 'رفع القلم عن ثلاث' (the pen is lifted from three), which explicitly exempts the child, the sleeper, and the insane. From this, the Hanafis derive that obligation requires either puberty or the arrival of the divine call (دعوة). The passage then applies this to three cases:
1. An adolescent girl under a Muslim husband: if she cannot describe Islam, she is not an apostate and remains married, because she is not yet obligated.
2. A sane adult who has never received the call of Islam: he is not obligated by mere intellect; if he does not affirm faith, he is excused if he dies immediately after puberty without opportunity to investigate.
3. However, if he is given time and opportunity (like the foolish person given 25 years), then he is no longer excused; the time substitutes for the call.
The commentary by Nizam al-Din explains that intellect knows good and evil but is not independent; obligation comes from divine address. The Hanafi middle ground avoids the Mu'tazili extreme (intellect suffices) and the Ash'ari extreme (intellect irrelevant). The discussion also criticizes the Shafi'i view that killing such a person requires diyah (blood money), implying obligation on the victim, which the Hanafis reject because the victim is not obligated. Finally, the lack of definitive textual proof for a specific time limit for investigation is noted; the Hanafis rely on the analogy of the foolish person's 25-year period as a juristic estimate.

Key Terms
العقل (al-ʿaql): Intellect as a faculty of understanding and distinguishing good from evil, but not an independent source of obligation.
الدعوة (al-daʿwah): The call of Islam, whether through a prophet or a substitute (e.g., a scholar), which conveys the message to a person.
مكلف (mukallaf): A person legally responsible for fulfilling religious duties, requiring both capacity (intellect, puberty) and the arrival of the divine address.

Slavery's Effect on Dhimma and Hilla: The General Principle
Principle and proof: slavery's effect on dhimma and hilla

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state how slavery weakens legal liability (dhimma) and halves lawful marriage capacity (hilla), and apply the Hanafi proof from hadith against al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Slavery weakens dhimma and halves hilla, as proven by a direct hadith.

Slavery is a heavenly impediment that does not strip a person of all legal capacity but diminishes certain aspects. This lesson focuses on two key effects: the weakening of the slave's dhimma (legal liability) and the halving of hilla (lawful marriage rights). The Hanafi school relies on a specific hadith to establish that a slave-woman is divorced by two divorces, in contrast to the Shafi'i position.

Guided Path
1
State the Principle
Identify the overarching rule: slavery does not negate ownership of non-property (marriage, blood, life) but negates perfection of worldly dignities like dhimma, wilaya, and hilla.
This principle frames the discussion.
2
Examine Dhimma Weakening
Understand that a slave has an original dhimma from his humanity, but it is weakened so that debt cannot be claimed independently; instead, the slave's value and earnings are attached.
3
Analyze Hilla Halving
Note that hilla is halved: a male slave can marry two women, and a female slave is divorced by two divorces. This applies regardless of the spouse's status.
Here the hadith is directly cited.
4
Review the Hadith Proof
The hadith 'Divorce of a slave-woman is two divorces, and her iddah is two menstruations' is the primary textual proof.
This dalil is naqli (transmitted) and considered qat'i al-thubut? It is a hadith from al-Tirmidhi, considered hasan? The text treats it as hujjah.
5
Compare with Al-Shafi'i
Al-Shafi'i ties the number of divorces to the husband's status. The Hanafi response is that the hadith explicitly ties it to the woman's slave status.

Commentary
This passage from the chapter on Qiyas discusses the heavenly impediment of slavery (al-raq) and its effects on legal capacity (ahliyah) and specific rulings. The text begins by stating a principle: slavery does not negate ownership (mālikiyyah) of non-property matters like marriage (nikāḥ), blood (dam), and life (ḥayāt). However, it does negate the perfection of worldly dignities (karāmāt dunyawiyyah) such as dhimma (legal liability), wilāyah (guardianship), and ḥill (lawful enjoyment). For the slave's dhimma is weakened by his slavery, so it cannot independently bear debt; instead, the value of his slave-body (raqabah) and his earnings (kasb) are attached to it. The text then explains that just as weakness appears in dhimma, it appears in the halving of ḥill, which is the basis for the ownership of marriage. Therefore, a male slave can marry two women, and a female slave is divorced by two divorces. This is supported by the hadith: 'The divorce of a slave-woman is two divorces, and her iddah is two menstruations.' This hadith is presented as proof against al-Shafi'i, who considers the number of divorces and the iddah to be based on the husband's status. Thus his value (death compensation) is less than that of a free man, but not automatically half; it is reduced by a specific amount (ten dirhams less than 10,000 dirhams maximum). The Particle 'Thumma' and Its Metaphorical Use as Waw
Explaining the literal and metaphorical usage of 'thumma'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of metaphorical interpretation (majaz) to the particle 'thumma' when its literal meaning of sequence creates a contradiction with clear Quranic principles.

Core Idea
Thumma is not always sequential; when literal meaning contradicts clear principles, it becomes 'and'.

This lesson explores the linguistic principle that a word may shift from its literal (haqiqah) to metaphorical (majaz) meaning when the literal is impossible. Using the Quranic verse from Surah Al-Balad, the Hanafi usul tradition demonstrates that 'thumma' (then) must be understood as 'waw' (and) to avoid the contradiction of deeds preceding faith.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning of 'thumma'
Recall that 'thumma' indicates conjunction with delay (tarakhi) – it means 'then' in the sense of sequence after an interval.
This is the haqiqah (literal) meaning.
2
Examine the Quranic context
Read the verse from Surah Al-Balad (11-18) and note that it lists freeing slaves and feeding the needy before mentioning faith.
The actions are mentioned first, then 'thumma kana mina alladhina amanu'.
3
Test the literal application
Apply the literal meaning: if 'thumma' means 'then', the verse would imply that deeds come before faith. Evaluate whether this is acceptable.
This contradicts the clear principle that faith is the foundation and must precede deeds.
4
Apply the usul principle of majaz
When the literal meaning leads to contradiction, and there is a shared element (here, conjunction), the word is interpreted metaphorically. Conclude that 'thumma' here means 'waw' (and), making faith and deeds simultaneous.
The shared element is the function of conjoining; 'waw' is absolute conjunction, 'thumma' is conjunction with delay.

The Legal Meanings of Ba and Alaa in Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh
Analyzing legal meanings of prepositions ba and alaa

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the legal effects of the prepositions ba (al-ilṣāq) and alaa (al-ilzām wa al-shart) in oath and contract formulations, and apply the Hanafi rule to determine when an oath is broken or an obligation is incurred.

Core Idea
Ba requires truth for oath-breaking; alaa imposes obligation or condition.

In Hanafi usul al-Fiqh, prepositions carry legal weight. This passage examines 'ba' and 'alaa', showing how their literal meanings affect rulings on oaths and acknowledgments. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly interpreting oath clauses and contractual pledges.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two prepositions
The passage focuses on 'ba' (الباء) and 'alaa' (على). Identify their primary legal meanings: adhesion (ilṣāq) for ba, obligation (ilzām) and condition (shart) for alaa.
2
Analyze ba's adhesion effect
In the oath 'If you inform me of Zaid's arrival' (bi-qudūmi Zayd), 'ba' adheres the information to the actual event. The oath is broken only if the arrival truly occurred and the news is true. Contrast with using 'an' (that), where the oath is broken regardless of truth.
Adhesion requires a real connection to reality.
3
Apply the ba rule to a case
Given a false report of arrival, the oath is not broken because the adhesion is absent. This shows that 'ba' restricts oath-breaking to truthful reports.
4
Analyze alaa's dual role
In 'alayya alf' (upon me a thousand), 'alaa' indicates obligation (ilzām) — debt incumbent on the speaker. In the Quranic verse (60:12), it introduces a condition: 'upon that they do not associate' means 'conditioned that they do not associate'. Recognize that 'alaa' is used literally for obligation and by extension for condition.
The jurists treat this as real usage, not metaphor.
5
Connect to evidence
The Quranic verse serves as evidence that 'alaa' can introduce a condition. This is a key textual proof for the Hanafi position.

Commentary
This passage discusses the prepositions 'ba' and 'alaa' and their legal implications in oaths and acknowledgments. Starting with 'ba', the author states that 'ba' indicates adhesion (ilṣāq). This means that when someone says 'If you inform me of the arrival of Zaid' (bi-qudūmi Zayd), the information is literally 'adhered' to the arrival. Consequently, the oath is broken only if the arrival actually happened and the report is true; if the report is false (arrival did not occur), the oath is not broken because there is no adhesion to reality. This contrasts with using 'an' (that) instead of 'ba', where the oath is broken regardless of truth, because 'an' simply introduces a report without adhesion. The ruling is that 'ba' requires truth. Next, the author examines 'alaa', stating it is for obligation (ilzām). In oath phrases like 'Alayya alf' (upon me a thousand), it obligates payment. The explanation is that 'alaa literally means highness; but in legal context, it represents something being incumbent on one's liability (like debt overwhelming the debtor). This usage is so close to literal meaning that it is considered actual usage, not metaphor. Furthermore, 'alaa is used for condition (shart) as in the Quranic verse about pledging not to associate partners with Allah. Here, 'alaa means 'upon condition that'. The jurists treat it as a conditional particle; the pledge is conditioned on the action. The passage notes that this interpretation is the position of jurists and exegetes, emphasizing that 'alaa in such contexts conveys the meaning of condition, even though it literally links to the pledge verb.

الباء (al-bā): A preposition indicating adhesion (ilṣāq); when used in oaths like 'bi-qudumi' (of arrival), it requires the news to be true for the oath to be broken.
على (alā): A preposition indicating obligation (ilzām) or condition (shart); in 'alayya alf' it imposes debt, and in 'ala an la yushrikna' it introduces a condition.
الإلصاق (al-ilṣāq): Adhesion; the property of the preposition ba that connects the action of informing directly to the event, requiring its occurrence for the oath to take effect.
