Effect of Prohibition on Marriage vs Sale: A Hanafi Usul Rule
Analyzes how prohibition affects validity of marriage and sale

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to APPLY the Hanafi usul rule that prohibition nullifies validity only when it contradicts the primary purpose of an act, distinguishing between marriage and sale.

Core Idea
Prohibition invalidates marriage because its essence is permissibility; sale's permissibility is secondary, so prohibition does not invalidate.

This lesson explores a key Hanafi principle: the effect of a prohibition (nahi) on a legal act depends on whether the prohibition strikes at the act's core purpose. Using marriage and sale as contrasting examples, the text analyzes why a prohibited marriage is void while a prohibited sale remains valid. It also addresses objections from sensory acts like usurpation and adultery.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal issue and the Hanafi ruling
Determine the issue: Is a marriage without witnesses valid according to the Hanafi school? The ruling is that it is invalid (batil). This is based on the hadith and the inherent nature of marriage.
The key is that the ruling hinges on the purpose of marriage.
2
Analyze the proof: the hadith and its textual classification
Examine the hadith 'لا نكاح إلا بشهود'. In Hanafi usul, it is considered an abrogation (naskh) rather than a prohibition (nahi). Even if interpreted as a prohibition, the consequence is the same because marriage's essence requires permissibility.
The distinction between naskh and nahi is crucial for understanding the Hanafi position.
3
Distinguish marriage from sale using the talil (ratio legis)
Marriage is legislated for 'necessary ownership' (ملك ضروري) which is inseparable from permissibility. Sale is legislated for 'ownership of the asset' (ملك العين), with permissibility being a subordinate effect. Therefore, prohibition contradicts marriage's purpose but not sale's.
This ratio is applied by analogy to other contracts.
4
Address the objection from sensory acts (ghasb and zina)
How can prohibition not nullify validity when ghasb and zina are prohibited yet have legal effects? The response: the legal effects are not direct results of the prohibited act but arise from secondary causes (compensation for ghasb, the child for zina). The prohibited act itself is not the source of the ruling.
This preserves the consistency of the Hanafi principle.
5
Apply the rule to a new case
Given a prohibition on sale after the Friday adhan (Quran 62:9), apply the analogy: sale's primary purpose is ownership, not permissibility, so the sale remains valid but sinful. This contrasts with a prohibition on marrying a woman in iddah, which invalidates the marriage.
Practical application solidifies the understanding.

Commentary
This passage is an extended discussion on the effect of prohibition (nahi) on the validity of contracts, particularly comparing marriage (nikah) and sale (bay'), and addressing objections from usurpation (ghasb) and adultery (zina). The author, likely a Hanafi commentator, explains the ruling from the hadith 'لا نكاح إلا بشهود' (no marriage without witnesses) reported by al-Daraqutni. The Hanafi position is that marriage without witnesses is not valid because the hadith either negates its existence or, if considered a prohibition, it cannot coexist with the very purpose of marriage—permissibility (halal). Marriage is legislated for necessary ownership that is inseparable from permissibility, so prohibition directly contradicts its essence. In contrast, sale is for ownership of the asset, and permissibility is only a secondary effect; thus a prohibition on a sale does not nullify its validity. The text then anticipates an objection: if prohibition in sensory acts (like usurpation and adultery) can have legal effects despite being prohibited, why can't a prohibited marriage have effect? The answer: usurpation establishes ownership for the usurper only as a condition for the guarantee of compensation—a good legal ruling—not as an intended result. Adultery creates a prohibition of affinity because the child born is innocent and becomes the cause of marital prohibitions; the sinful act of adultery is not directly the cause but the means to the child. This preserves the consistency of the Hanafi usul principle that prohibition can negate validity only when it is inherent to the act's purpose, not in cases where the act's beneficial legal effect is separate from the prohibition.

Key Terms
النكاح (al-nikah): Marriage contract, legislated for necessary ownership (ملك ضروري) of the wife's rights, inseparable from permissibility of intercourse
البيع (al-bay'): Sale contract, legislated for ownership of the object (ملك العين), permissibility of use only secondary
التحريم (al-tahrim): Prohibition (hukm taklifi) that demands abstention; when applied to an act, it may or may not nullify its validity

Types of Majaz in Rukhsah: Complete Abrogation and Partial Suspension
Defines the third and fourth types of metaphorical rukhsah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the third and fourth types of metaphorical rukhsah and apply each to examples from Hanafi usul al-fiqh.

Core Idea
A rukhsah is literal only if the original ruling remains; if abrogated entirely, it is metaphorical.

In Hanafi usul, rukhsah (concession) is not always a true exception to an existing rule. Sometimes the so-called concession is simply the removal of a prior obligation (third type), or a partial suspension of a general requirement (fourth type). This lesson explores these two categories and their implications for legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of majaz in rukhsah
Locate in the passage the definitions of the third and fourth types. Note that both are called 'rukhsah' metaphorically, not literally.
The third type involves complete abrogation (naskh); the fourth involves partial removal while the ruling remains prescribed in principle.
2
Understand why the third type is a complete metaphor
The original ruling (al-asl) is dropped entirely and no longer prescribed. Calling it a rukhsah is a metaphor because there is no longer an existing stricter ruling to make an exception from.
Example: the removal of 'isr and aghlal (hardships) from previous nations.
3
Understand the fourth type as partial removal
The original ruling remains prescribed in general but is dropped in specific circumstances. It is a metaphor because the ruling is still valid elsewhere.
Example: the requirement of specification (al-'ayniyya) is dropped in salam contracts; prohibition of carrion is dropped for the compelled person.
4
Apply the distinction to examples
Classify given scenarios as third or fourth type based on whether the original ruling is completely abrogated or merely suspended in some cases.
Complete abrogation: third type; conditional suspension: fourth type.
5
Compare degrees of metaphor
The third type is more metaphorical because the original ruling is entirely absent; the fourth type is less metaphorical because the original still applies elsewhere.
The passage calls the third type 'the most complete of the two types of metaphor'.

Commentary
This passage continues the commentary on the types of rukhsah that are considered metaphorical (majaz). The third type is the complete removal of severe obligations from previous nations (like cutting off wrong body parts, etc.). These are called rukhsah only metaphorically because the original ruling is gone entirely; it is actually an abrogation (naskh). The fourth type is where a ruling is dropped in some situations while remaining valid in others. Examples: the requirement of the specific item ('ayniyya) in a sale is normally essential, but in the salam contract it is not only dropped but making it specific invalidates the contract. Similarly, the prohibition of wine and carrion is lifted for the one compelled by necessity (al-mudtar). This type is metaphorically called rukhsah because the original ruling remains prescribed elsewhere, so it is less metaphorical than the third type.

Key Terms
المجاز (al-majaz): Metaphorical usage; in this context, referring to calling something a 'rukhsah' when it is actually an abrogation or partial removal.

When Can a Mashhur Hadith Add to the Quran?
Explaining the Hanafi rule on ziyadah via mashhur and its examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi rule on ziyadah via mashhur hadith and identify the three categories of addition.

Core Idea
A mashhur hadith can add to the Quran only if explanatory or restrictive, not abrogating.

Commentary
This passage discusses the permissibility of adding a ruling (ziyadah) to the Quran via a mashhur hadith. The commentator distinguishes three cases: pure explanation (bayan), pure abrogation (naskh), and a mixture (bayan min wajh). Pure explanation can be done by any type of hadith (mutawatir, mashhur, ahad). Pure abrogation cannot be done by mashhur or ahad, only by mutawatir, because abrogation requires equal strength. The intermediate case—where the addition removes an absolute (itlaq) and replaces it with a restriction (taqyid)—is permissible via mashhur because it is mutawatir in its strength as proof but ahad in its actual transmission. Two examples are given: the addition of stoning for the muhsan (married) fornicator, and the permission to wipe over khuffs (leather socks) instead of washing the feet. However, the commentator notes a potential objection: the hadith of stoning is actually mutawatir in meaning, so the example may not be ideal. The hadith of wiping is also argued to be mutawatir in meaning, as multiple narrations support it.

Key Terms
الزيادة (al-ziyadah): Adding a new ruling not explicitly mentioned in the Quran, based on a hadith.
النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation of a Quranic ruling by a later revelation or hadith. Hanafis require mutawatir for naskh of the Quran.
البيان (al-bayan): Explanation of a Quranic ruling, e.g., clarifying ambiguous terms or specifying general commands.
التقييد (al-taqyid): Restriction of an absolute (mutlaq) command, e.g., limiting a general ruling to specific conditions.

Dropping Hadith Due to Narrator's Opposition
Rule on dropping a hadith due to narrator's or Companion's contradictory action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions under which a hadith is dropped due to narrator or Companion opposition and apply the Hanafi rule that such opposition indicates abrogation.

Core Idea
A hadith is dropped if the narrator or a Companion contradicts it with certainty after narration, and the hadith was apparent to them.

Usul al-Fiqh teaches us how to weigh conflicting evidence. One critical rule is that a narrator's or Companion's contradiction of a hadith they could not have been unaware of indicates the hadith is abrogated. In this lesson, we unpack the conditions and implications of this rule, using the famous hadith on flogging and banishment as a case study.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
Read the passage to understand the core rule: a hadith is dropped if opposition appears from the narrator or other Companions after narration, provided the hadith is apparent.
The rule has conditions: opposition must be certain and after narration.
2
Distinguish certain vs uncertain opposition
Note that opposition not certain (e.g., when the hadith admits multiple meanings and the narrator picks one) does not drop the hadith.
The phrase 'with certainty' is key.
3
Consider temporality of opposition
Opposition before narration or of unknown date does not harm; it is assumed the narrator later accepted the hadith.
4
Apply to the example
Examine the hadith on flogging and banishment. The Companions opposed it, so Hanafis consider it abrogated; Shafi'is follow the text.
The thamarah al-khilaf is that Hanafis do not include banishment in the hadd.
5
Synthesize the rule
Summarize: For a hadith to be dropped due to opposition, the opposition must be certain, after narration, and the hadith must be apparent.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary on the ruling that a hadith is dropped from consideration when the narrator himself or other Companions contradict it in word or deed, provided the contradiction is certain and occurs after narration. 

The author begins by discussing the status of the mustatar hadith: it is below wajib (obligation), meaning it yields knowledge where the probabilities are equal, so acting upon it is permissible but not obligatory. 

Then he moves to the main topic: the abrogation (suqut) of a hadith due to opposition. The opposition must be certain and after the narration. If a narrator contradicts his own narration, it indicates either that the hadith is fabricated or abrogated, or that the narrator is unreliable. In any case, the hadith is dropped. 

An important distinction: if the hadith admits of multiple interpretations and the narrator chooses one, that does not constitute opposition that drops the hadith. Similarly, opposition before narration or of unknown date does not drop it. 

The author also addresses opposition from non-narrator Companions. If a Companion opposes a hadith that is apparent and not possibly hidden, that indicates the hadith is abrogated. An example is the hadith on flogging and banishment for fornication. Al-Shafi'i held to it, but Hanafis argue that Companions acted contrary, proving abrogation.

Resolving Apparent Contradictions Between Quran and Sunnah
Usul rule on apparent contradiction and naskh

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why apparent contradictions between Quran and Sunnah are only apparent due to naskh and apply this principle to resolve a given conflicting pair.

Core Idea
True contradiction in revelation is impossible; apparent conflict arises only from ignorance of naskh.

Have you ever encountered two Quranic verses or a verse and a hadith that seem to say opposite things? At first glance, this might shake confidence in revelation. But Hanafi usul teaches that such contradictions are never real — they are only apparent, caused by our lack of knowledge about which text came later and abrogated the earlier one. This principle safeguards the integrity of divine speech and directs us to seek historical and contextual knowledge.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Two Conflicting Proofs
Locate the two texts (Quranic verses or hadiths) that appear to contradict each other on a ruling. Write them down side by side.
Ensure both are authentic and from accepted sources.
2
Establish Chronology
Determine which text was revealed or uttered first. The later text may abrogate the earlier one. Consult the science of al-nāsikh wa al-mansūkh.
The date of revelation or occasion (sabab al-nuzul) is key.
3
Apply the Principle of Naskh
If one text is clearly later and the rulings are incompatible, the later text abrogates the earlier. The earlier ruling is no longer operative.
Naskh only occurs in commands and prohibitions, not in beliefs or historical narratives.
4
If No Chronology Is Known, Suspend Judgment
If we cannot determine which is later, we do not assume real contradiction. Instead, we hold both as valid and seek reconciliation through other means (e.g., specification, contextual interpretation).
This reflects the Hanafi caution: genuine contradiction in revelation is impossible.
5
Conclude That Apparent Contradiction Is Resolved
State the operative ruling based on the later text, or if no chronology is known, state that both may be applied according to their contexts or that the issue remains open pending further evidence.
This step reinforces that the conflict was only in our understanding, not in the revelation.

Ruling on Conflicting Verses: Recourse to Sunnah
Rule and example for resolving ta'arud between two verses.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi hierarchy for resolving conflicting texts when tarih is unknown, and derive the ruling from Sunnah.

Core Idea
When two verses conflict and history is unknown, turn to Sunnah—not to a third verse.

This passage presents a key Hanafi usul rule: when two Quranic verses appear to conflict and we cannot determine which came later (tarih), we do not resort to a third verse for preponderance. Instead, we look to the Sunnah for resolution. The example of follower recitation in prayer illustrates how a hadith overrides both general and specific verses, settling the fiqh ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting texts
Find the two Quranic verses or statements that appear to give opposite rulings on the same issue. In the example, one verse commands recitation, the other commands silence during recitation in prayer.
These are both from the Qur'an, making them equal in authority.
2
Check historical order (tarih)
Determine if the chronology of revelation is known. If yes, the later abrogates the earlier (naskh). Here, tarih is unknown.
This is the first step in Hanafi method.
3
Resort to Sunnah if available
Apply the rule: when tarih is unknown and Sunnah exists, use it to resolve the conflict. The hadith of Abu Musa and Abu Hurayra commands silence when the imam recites.
A third verse is not used because that would be tarjih bi-kathrat al-adillah.
4
Verify the Sunnah's evidence
Check that the hadith is authentic and clearly indicates the ruling. The hadith is sahih (Muslim) and has clear imperative 'ansitu'. It specifies the situation.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but its dalalah is qat'i.
5
Derive the final ruling
Conclude that the follower must be silent during the imam's recitation, thus the general command to recite is suspended.
This resolves the ta'arud.

Definition of Naskh (Abrogation) in Hanafi Usul
Definition and conditions of naskh as a type of bayan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of naskh, identify its linguistic and terminological components, and explain why the Hanafi school considers naskh a type of bayan despite internal disagreement.

Core Idea
Naskh in Hanafi usul is not a cancellation but an announcement (bayan) of the preordained end of a ruling's duration.

In this passage, the author presents the definition of naskh (abrogation) as a type of bayan (clarification). He begins with linguistic meanings, then gives the precise terminological definition, and finally discusses an internal Hanafi dispute about whether naskh is truly part of bayan. Understanding this definition is crucial because it shapes how Hanafis determine when a ruling has been abrogated and by what evidence.

Guided Path
1
1. Identify the linguistic meanings
The passage states that naskh linguistically has two meanings: removal (izalah) and transfer (naql). Note the examples given for each. Scholars differed on which meaning is primary.
The preferred shari' meaning is removal because transfer is inconceivable for a ruling.
2
2. State the terminological definition
The definition is: 'announcement of the end of an absolute shari' ruling, free from perpetuity and timing, by a later text from its source.' Write this out and identify each key phrase.
This definition is a typical Hanafi usul definition, carefully worded to exclude other types of bayan.
3
3. Analyze each phrase of the definition
Work through the author's commentary: 'bayan' excludes other clarifications, 'intiha' excludes initial legislation, 'hukm shar'i' excludes rational rulings, 'mutlaq an al-ta'bid wa al-tawqit' excludes temporally limited or eternal rulings, 'bi-nass' excludes ijma and qiyas, 'muta'akhkhir an mawridih' excludes connected bayan like specification or exception.
Note that this definition makes naskh a very specific type of legal change.
4
4. Understand the internal Hanafi dispute
Shams al-A'imma argued that naskh is not bayan because bayan manifests a ruling initially while naskh removes it after establishment. Fakhr al-Islam argued that naskh is bayan because it manifests the duration of the ruling from Allah's perspective.
The author seems to side with Fakhr al-Islam, stating that naskh is bayan in relation to Allah and tabdeel in relation to us.
5
5. Apply the definition to a known example
Consider the abrogation of the qibla from Jerusalem to the Kaaba (Quran 2:144). Does it meet the conditions? It is announced by a later text, it ends a previous shari' ruling (absolute from perpetuity), and it is disconnected (the two verses are not in the same immediate context).
This example helps solidify the definition.

Bayan al-Tabdeel: Since this type of bayan has many investigations, he delayed it and made a chapter for it. In this place there are five investigations: first in its definition, second in its permissibility, third in its locus, fourth in its condition, and fifth in the abrogator and abrogated. The author left the second investigation due to its fame, and we will mention it if Allah wills. So he began the first investigation, which is al-tabdeel: al-naskh, for it is an expression of it; because Allah Most High said: 'Whatever verse We abrogate or cause to be forgotten' (al-Baqarah 2:106), and He said: 'And when We substitute one verse in place of another' (al-Nahl 16:101). So naskh is called tabdeel. Linguistically, naskh has two meanings: one is removal and elimination, it is said: 'the sun naskh'd the shadow' i.e., removed it, and 'the wind naskh'd the traces' if it eliminated them. The second is transfer, which is moving something from one place to another or from one state to another. It is said: 'The bees naskh'd the honey' when they transferred it from one hive to another. Then they differed: al-Qadi and al-Ghazali said it is shared between them. Abu al-Husayn al-Basri said it is haqiqah in removal, majaz in transfer, and the Imam preferred this. Al-Qaffal said the opposite. Then they differed: some said the preferred meaning in shari' is the meaning of transfer, for turning from the Kaaba to Jerusalem and then back is transfer. And it was said: the preferred meaning in shari' is removal, because it is more consistent with the shari' concept than transfer, for transfer of the abrogated ruling to the abrogator is inconceivable, while removal and elimination are conceivable. Terminologically, it is: 'announcement of the end of an absolute shari' ruling, free from perpetuity and timing, by a later text from its source.' So his saying 'announcement' is like the genus. His saying 'end' excludes the announcement of the ambiguous and other divisions of bayan, and the announcement of the original legislation of a ruling from naskh. His saying 'shari' ruling' excludes the announcement of a non-shari' ruling, which is the rational, like the end of original permissibility established by the original ruling before the coming of shari' by a later text; that is not naskh because it is not an announcement of a shari' ruling, for it is the speech of Allah as previously mentioned. And it includes the naskh of recitation without the ruling, because in its naskh is the announcement of the end of the ruling of its recitation. His saying 'absolute, free from perpetuity and timing' is a precaution against a ruling with a specific time, for its naskh is not valid before its end, and after it naskh is inconceivable due to the non-existence of its ruling. Likewise, an eternal ruling cannot be abrogated. His saying 'by a text' is a precaution against ijma and qiyas, for naskh is not permissible by them, and against the announcement of end that occurs by death, sleep, forgetfulness, incapacity, and lack of locus, for that is not naskh because it is not a text. His saying 'later than its source' meaning later than the time of revelation of the first ruling, is a precaution against the connected announcement (bayan muttasil) with the first ruling, whether independent like the specification of the general, for it is not later according to the majority of our companions, as his saying 'Do not kill the dhimmis' following his saying 'Kill the polytheists' connectedly, or dependent like the exception as in 'Kill the polytheists except the dhimmis', and the limit as in 'Complete the fast until the night' (al-Baqarah 2:187), and the condition as in 'Pray if you are healthy', and the description as in 'Honor the scholars', for these matters are only connected, so naskh is not established by any of them. This is as in al-Sharh al-Badi'. Know that Shams al-A'imma did not make naskh a part of bayan, for bayan is the manifestation of the ruling of an event at its occurrence initially, while naskh is removal after establishment. As for Fakhr al-Islam, he made it part of bayan, because naskh is the act of the Legislator, and its reality is the manifestation of the duration of the ruling for the servants. So naskh, with respect to Allah's knowledge and actual reality, is bayan, and with respect to us is tabdeel. The right of the Owner of the Law—that is, with respect to Him, it is pure bayan and manifestation of the duration of the legislation of the absolute ruling, and tabdeel with respect to us, because the ruling that was raised now was known to Allah that its time was such by the abrogator, because it was temporarily limited at that time with Him; He released it, meaning He did not show the servants the timing of the abrogated ruling and its end.

Commentary
This passage provides a detailed definition of naskh (abrogation) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It begins by stating that naskh is a type of bayan (clarification) and that it is called tabdeel (substitution) based on Qur'anic verses (2:106 and 16:101). The linguistic meanings are discussed: removal (izalah) and transfer (naql). Differences of opinion among scholars are noted: al-Qadi and al-Ghazali see it as shared, Abu al-Husayn al-Basri considers it haqiqah in removal and majaz in transfer, and al-Qaffal holds the opposite. The preferred view in shari' is removal because it is more fitting. The technical definition is presented: 'announcement of the end of an absolute shari' ruling, free from perpetuity and timing, by a later text from its source.' This definition is then analyzed word by word: 'bayān' (announcement) excludes other types of bayan like clarification of the ambiguous; 'intihā' (end) excludes initial legislation; 'ḥukm sharʿī' (shari' ruling) excludes rational rulings; 'muṭlaq ʿan al-taʾbīd wa al-tawqīt' (absolute from perpetuity and timing) excludes temporally limited or eternal rulings; 'binaṣṣ' (by a text) excludes abrogation by ijma or qiyas, and also excludes natural endings like death; 'mutaʾakhkhir ʿan mawridih' (later than its source) excludes connected bayan like specification, exception, or condition. The passage also discusses a dispute between Shams al-A'imma and Fakhr al-Islam about whether naskh is part of bayan: Shams al-A'imma says no because bayan is initial manifestation while naskh is removal after establishment; Fakhr al-Islam says yes because naskh, from the perspective of Allah's knowledge, is manifestation of the ruling's duration. The author concludes that naskh is bayan with respect to Allah and tabdeel with respect to us.

Key Terms
النسخ (al-naskh): Terminologically: announcement of the end of an absolute shari' ruling by a later text.
البيان (al-bayan): Manifestation or clarification of a ruling.
الإزالة (al-izalah): Removal or elimination, one linguistic meaning of naskh.
النقل (al-naql): Transfer, another linguistic meaning of naskh.

Defining naskh technically with conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi technical definition of naskh and identify each of its five conditions from the passage.

Core Idea
Naskh is a clarification of the termination of an absolute shari'a ruling by a later text.

This passage launches into the heart of Hanafi usul with a precise definition of naskh (abrogation). The definition is carefully worded to exclude everything that is not true naskh. By dissecting each term, you will learn exactly what qualifies as naskh and what does not according to the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the genus (jins)
The definition begins with 'bayan' (clarification). Naskh is a type of bayan. Understand what this implies about its function.
This directly ties naskh to the broader category of bayan.
2
Distinguish naskh from other bayan
The word 'intiha'' (termination) separates naskh from other clarifications like explaining a mujmal or initial legislation.
Naskh is about ending a ruling, not initiating or explaining it.
3
Examine the qualifications
The definition has three key qualifications: the ruling must be a shari'a ruling, it must be absolute (not eternal or time-bound), and the abrogating source must be a later text (nass).
Each qualification excludes certain invalid types of claimed naskh.
4
Understand the exclusions
By noting what is excluded (rational rulings, non-textual sources, connected clarifications), you see the boundaries of naskh.
This step reinforces the conditions.

Commentary
This passage is a classical Hanafi usul al-fiqh discussion on naskh (abrogation) as a form of bayan (clarification). The author first explains that from the perspective of Allah, the Lawgiver, naskh is a pure bayan because Allah's command was always appropriate for each time, while from our human perspective it appears as a change (tabdil). He analogizes this to killing: Allah knows the exact term of every person, so the killing is a bayan of the term from Allah's side but a change from the killer's side. The author then states that naskh is intellectually possible and has occurred in revelation, citing the Quranic verse, and responds to the Jewish objection that naskh implies ignorance or foolishness in Allah. The author provides two responses: first, an ilzami (pressing) argument showing that the Torah itself contains naskh with multiple examples (marriage with sisters, dietary laws, polygamy, and sacrificial rites), citing specific biblical passages. Then he extends the argument to Christians who have also denied naskh, showing from their own scriptures (Jeremiah, Paul's epistles) that the law of Isa abrogated the law of Moses. The passage is a robust defense of the Islamic concept of naskh as consistent with previous divine laws.

Key Terms
النسخ (an-naskh): Abrogation: a divine command that replaces a previous divine command, being a pure bayan from Allah's perspective and a change (tabdil) from human perspective.
البيان (al-bayan): Clarification: the act of making a divine ruling clear; naskh is a type of bayan because it clarifies the ruling for the new time.
الإلزامي (al-ilzami): Pressing argument: a method of debate that uses an opponent's own sources to prove a point.

Defending Naskh with Biblical Examples
Refutes objections to naskh using biblical and rational arguments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how Hanafi usul uses biblical examples to defend the validity of naskh and apply the rational distinction between temporary and eternal rulings.

Core Idea
Naskh is not abrogation of eternal decrees but a manifestation of incremental divine wisdom across revelations.

The objection that naskh (abrogation) implies ignorance or change in God is a common criticism of Islamic law. In this passage, a Hanafi scholar refutes that objection by demonstrating that abrogation occurred in previous scriptures—the Torah and the Gospels—and then provides a rational analogy that preserves divine perfection. You will learn how to articulate this defense using textual and rational arguments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objection to naskh
Recognize that opponents claim naskh implies God changed His mind or did not know the better ruling initially.
This objection is often based on a misunderstanding of God's attributes.
2
Trace the biblical examples of abrogation
The author cites three examples: (1) the sacrifice law in Leviticus abrogated in Deuteronomy, (2) the prophecy of a new covenant in Jeremiah, (3) divorce laws and dietary laws changed from Moses to Jesus/Paul.
These examples show that abrogation is not unique to Islam.
3
Analyze the rational response
The author compares naskh to a physician changing a prescription according to the patient's condition; God's eternal knowledge means the temporary nature of the first ruling was always known.
This preserves divine omniscience and wisdom.
4
Connect to Hanafi usul
In Hanafi usul, naskh is permissible for rulings that are not inherently eternal (mu'abbad), provided later revelation provides a clear abrogator.
This aligns with the idea that Shari'a unfolds progressively.
5
Apply the lesson to defend naskh
When confronted with objections, you can cite similar abrogations in previous scriptures and use the physician analogy to explain temporal vs. eternal rulings.
This strengthens interfaith dialogue and internal understanding.

Conditions for the Locus of Naskh
Defines the locus of naskh and its conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for a ruling to be abrogable and distinguish between rulings that can and cannot be abrogated according to the Hanafi school.

Core Idea
A ruling can only be abrogated if it is possible that it was not legislated and if it has no attached time limit or perpetual wording.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, not every ruling can be abrogated. This lesson clarifies the precise conditions that make a ruling eligible for naskh. By parsing the definition of mahall al-naskh, you will learn which types of rulings are subject to abrogation and which are permanently fixed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
The passage defines mahall al-naskh as a ruling with two essential conditions: it must be possible (muhtamil) for legislation and non-legislation, and it must not be attached to a preventing factor (tawqit or ta'bīd).
2
Understand the first condition: possibility
The ruling must be such that it could have been legislated or not. If it is eternally non-legislated (like disbelief) or eternally legislated (like belief in God), naskh does not apply because the ruling is not changeable.
This excludes matters of creed and necessary knowledge.
3
Understand the second condition: non-attachment
If a ruling is explicitly limited by a time (tawqit) or stated as perpetual (ta'bīd), it cannot be abrogated. Before the time expires, it remains; after expiration, the ruling ends naturally—not by naskh.
Examples from Quran: 2:109 and 4:15.
4
Note excluded categories
Naskh does not apply to reports (akhbar) about past, present, or future because that would imply falsehood. It also does not apply to rational or sensory matters (e.g., the laws of logic, physical facts) as that would lead to ignorance.
These are not shar'i rulings in the same sense.
5
Apply the conditions to examples
Given a ruling, check: Is it a shar'i ruling of obligation or prohibition? Is it possible that it was not legislated (e.g., not an eternal belief)? Does it have a time limit or perpetual statement? If yes to possibility and no to attachment, it may be abrogable.
Most ritual rulings (e.g., prayer direction, alcohol prohibition) satisfy the conditions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions a ruling must satisfy to be a valid subject of abrogation (mahall al-naskh) and classify rulings as abrogable or immune.

Core Idea
Only potentially legislative shar'i rulings not tied to timing or perpetuity can be abrogated.

The Hanafi usul defines the subject of abrogation precisely: not every ruling can be lifted. This passage delineates the criteria, excluding reports, rational truths, and explicitly timed or eternal rulings. Understanding this is key to correctly applying naskh in legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Read the Definition
Identify the core definition: mahall al-naskh is a ruling that is in itself potentially existent or non-existent and not joined by something that contradicts abrogation.
The ruling must be shar'i and legislative in nature.
2

The ruling must be capable of both existence (being legislated) and non-existence (being lifted). This excludes necessary truths (e.g., belief in Allah) and impossibilities (e.g., disbelief).

If a ruling is explicitly timed (e.g., 'until a certain event') or declared perpetual (e.g., 'forever'), it cannot be abrogated before the time ends, and after the time, the lifting is not called naskh.
Timing (tawqit) and perpetuity (ta'bid) are textual.

The author explains the Hanafi position that for abrogation to be valid, it is only necessary that the mukallaf (legally responsible person) has received the original ruling and has had enough time to mentally affirm it ('aqd al-qalb). It is not required that he has had time to physically perform the act. This is in contrast to the Mu'tazila, who demand that the mukallaf must have had the opportunity to physically act upon the ruling before it is abrogated. The basis for this disagreement lies in the nature of naskh: for the Hanafis, abrogation primarily clarifies the duration of a ruling for the heart (i.e., belief/awareness), and physical action is secondary. Therefore, the mental ability suffices. The Mu'tazila view naskh as clarifying the period for physical action, so they require physical ability. The proof for the Hanafi position is the famous hadith of the Night of Ascension, where the Prophet was commanded fifty daily prayers, and then within the same night, the obligation was reduced to five. The Prophet did not physically perform fifty prayers after the command; rather, he affirmed the command mentally before the abrogation occurred. The passage concludes by explaining why the heart is given primacy: the heart's actions (like belief) are stronger and cannot be excused, unlike bodily actions which may be excused due to inability.

Key Terms
تمكن (tamakkun): Possibility/ability; here, the condition for abrogation: the time needed for the mukallaf to be aware of the ruling.
عمل القلب (amal al-qalb): The action of the heart, i.e., belief, mental affirmation, and intention.

The Four Permissible Types of Naskh in Hanafi Usul
Classifying the four types of valid abrogation between Quran and Sunnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the four types of naskh between Quran and Sunnah according to the Hanafi school and provide a concrete example of each.

Core Idea
Naskh is a clarification of duration; it occurs in four types between Quran and Sunnah.

In this passage, the author enumerates the permissible categories of abrogation (naskh) according to the Hanafi school. Understanding these categories is essential for knowing which proofs can override others. The passage also addresses the objection of Imam al-Shafi'i, which we will explore in a subsequent lesson.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the four-fold division
The author states that naskh occurs in four divisions according to the Hanafis: Quran by Quran, Sunnah by Sunnah, Quran by mutawatir Sunnah, and Sunnah by Quran. Note that the third and fourth involve cross-abrogation, which is contested by al-Shafi'i.
The fourth type (Sunnah by Quran) is also considered cross-abrogation and is accepted by Hanafis.
2
Examine the example of Quran abrogating Quran
The waiting period for widows was originally one year (2:240) and was abrogated by the verse prescribing four months and ten days (2:234). This is a clear case of a later Quranic verse abrogating an earlier one.
Abrogation is always by a later revelation; the chronology is known.
3
Break down the sub-types of Sunnah abrogating Sunnah
Within Sunnah by Sunnah, there are four sub-cases: mutawatir by mutawatir, ahad by ahad, ahad by mutawatir (all agreed), and mutawatir by ahad (not permissible according to the majority). The example given is the hadith about visiting graves: 'I had forbidden you... so visit them now' – a sahih hadith abrogating an earlier prohibition.
The majority (jumhur) disallow abrogation of mutawatir by ahad because ahad gives only speculative knowledge.
4

The verse 'It is not lawful for you [to marry] women after that' (33:52) originally prohibited the Prophet from marrying more than nine wives. This was abrogated by a hadith (transmitted by Aisha) that Allah permitted him as many women as he wished. The author notes that some say this verse was abrogated by the previous verse (33:50).
The hadith is considered mutawatir by the Hanafis, so it can abrogate the Quran.
5
Analyze the example of Sunnah abrogated by Quran
Facing Bayt al-Maqdis (Jerusalem) in prayer was established by the Sunnah (the Prophet's practice and sayings), not by a Quranic verse. This was abrogated by the verse 'Turn your face toward al-Masjid al-Haram' (2:144).
This shows that the Quran can abrogate a ruling derived only from the Sunnah.

The Hanafi-Shafi'i Dispute on Cross-Abrogation between Quran and Sunnah
Classifying the four types of valid abrogation between Quran and Sunnah and addressing the Shafi'i objection

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi argument for cross-abrogation and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the concept of naskh as bayan (clarification).

Core Idea
If specification (takhsis) of the Quran by the Sunnah is allowed, then abrogation is also allowed — because specification is a form of partial naskh.

This lesson focuses specifically on the dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding whether the Quran and Sunnah can abrogate each other. The passage presents both sides: the Shafi'i objection that cross-abrogation leads to criticism, and the Hanafi response that naskh is merely a clarification of duration. The Hanafi argument also draws a powerful parallel to specification (takhsis), which the Shafi'is themselves accept.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi position on cross-abrogation
The Hanafis hold that the Quran and the mutawatir Sunnah can abrogate each other. This is based on the understanding that naskh is not an invalidation but a clarification of the duration of a ruling. The Prophet was sent as an explainer (mubayyin), so it is within his role to clarify how long a Quranic ruling lasts.
The Hanafis limit cross-abrogation to cases where the Sunnah is mutawatir, not ahad.
2
State the Shafi'i position and its apparent basis
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that abrogating the Quran by the Sunnah would give critics grounds to say that the Prophet lied against Allah. Conversely, abrogating the Sunnah by the Quran would make it seem that Allah lied to His messenger. Therefore, to protect the integrity of revelation, cross-abrogation is impermissible.
The Shafi'i position prioritizes preventing potential criticism and maintaining public trust in revelation.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response
The Hanafis respond that naskh is a clarification (bayan) of the term of the ruling, not a falsification of the earlier command. The Prophet, as the explainer of the Quran, is authorized to specify its duration. Just as the Prophet can explain the meaning of a verse, he can also clarify how long it remains in effect. This does not amount to lying; it is merely stating the effective period.
The key premise is that naskh is bayan, not ibtal (nullification).
4
Examine the analogy with specification (takhsis)
The Hanafi text points out that the Shafi'is themselves allow specification (takhsis) of a general Quranic verse by a hadith. For example, the verse 'The thief, male and female, cut off their hands' (5:38) is specified by a hadith that sets a minimum threshold. Since takhsis is a form of partial abrogation (it limits the scope of the ruling), if takhsis is allowed, full abrogation (which terminates the ruling entirely) should also be allowed.
This is a strong rebuttal because takhsis is widely accepted.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
The practical difference is that Hanafis can accept a later mutawatir hadith as abrogating a Quranic verse, while Shafi'is cannot. An example is the verse limiting the Prophet's wives (33:52): Hanafis hold it was abrogated by the hadith permitting unlimited wives, while Shafi'is would either reconcile or consider the hadith as not abrogating. This affects rulings on polygamy for the Prophet, though the issue is historical.
In practice, the dispute mainly affects theoretical usul principles; many specific cases have alternative resolutions.

Addition to the Text as Naskh: The Hanafi Proof
Arguments for Hanafi position that addition is naskh with proof and examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle that an addition to a divine command is naskh not bayan, and evaluate the Shafi'i counterposition using the two cited examples.

Core Idea
Adding a condition to a divine command changes the ruling, which is abrogation, not clarification.

In Hanafi usul, the addition of a new condition or restriction to a previously unqualified ruling is considered a form of abrogation (naskh), not mere clarification (bayan). This principle rests on the indivisibility of obligations owed to Allah. The following passage from a classical usul text builds the argument and draws out its practical consequences in two key areas: the recitation of Fatiha in prayer and the penalty for unmarried fornicators.

Guided Path
1
Understand the core claim
Identify the Hanafi position: addition to the text is naskh because the original ruling is partially replaced. The addition changes the composite obligation, and since Allah's rights are indivisible, the original ruling no longer stands alone.
Note the key term 'haqq Allah' – rights of Allah are indivisible.
2
Analyze the indivisibility argument
Examine the example: a person who expiates zihar by fasting one month and feeding thirty poor people does not fulfill the expiation. This shows that partial performance of an indivisible obligation is invalid. Similarly, a restricted ruling is a partial obligation.
The absolute ruling (e.g., 'recite what is easy') becomes part of a composite when a condition is added.
3
Apply to the Fatiha example
Follow the Hanafi reasoning: the Quranic command 'recite what is easy' (Muzammil 73:20) is general. The hadith 'No prayer without Fatiha' adds a condition. This addition restricts the generality, thus changing the ruling – it is naskh.
Hanafis classify the hadith as khabar wahid, which cannot prove abrogation.
4
Apply to the taghrib example
Similarly, the Shafi'i addition of exile (taghrib) to the flogging for virgin zina is seen as naskh of the original text (Surah Nur 24:2). Since it is based on a khabar wahid, Hanafis reject it.
Compare: Shafi'is view these additions as bayan (specification) and accept them via khabar wahid.
5
Identify the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: Hanafis do not require Fatiha in prayer (it is sunnah) and do not add exile for zina; Shafi'is require both. The underlying usul difference is about the epistemic threshold for naskh vs. bayan.
Hanafis require tawatur or mashhur for naskh; Shafi'is accept khabar wahid for bayan.

Addition to the Text as Abrogation: Hanafi vs Shafi'i
Derives Hanafi rule that addition to a text is naskh, not bayan

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school classifies addition to a text (ziyada 'ala al-nass) as abrogation (naskh) rather than clarification (bayan), and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Adding a condition to a divine command is abrogation, not clarification—it changes the ruling itself.

This lesson examines one of the most critical usul disputes between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools: whether an addition to a clear Quranic or sunnah text is an interpretive clarification (bayan) or an abrogation (naskh). The Hanafi position, grounded on the indivisibility of Allah's rights, has profound implications for many furu' rulings. We will work through the passage from Muntakhab al-Husami that lays out this principle and its four key applications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
Determine the exact point of contention: whether adding an extra condition, component, or punishment to a clear textual ruling is considered clarification (bayan) or abrogation (naskh).
The Shafi'i view sees it as bayan; the Hanafi view sees it as naskh.
2
State the Hanafi position and its rational basis
The Hanafis argue that the original ruling is a complete right of Allah (haqq Allah) that is indivisible. Adding a condition splits this right, effectively changing the ruling, which is the essence of naskh.
The example of zihar: if a man falls ill after fasting one month and feeds thirty poor, it does not suffice because the obligation of two months is an indivisible haqq Allah.
3
Recognize the Shafi'i counter-position
The Shafi'is view addition as mere specification (takhsis) of a general text, which can be established by khabar wahid or qiyas, not requiring the high evidentiary standard of naskh.
They argue that the original ruling's existence is not abolished; it is simply restricted to certain cases.
4
Trace the thamarah al-khilaf through the four examples
Identify the practical difference in rulings: (1) Fatiha in prayer, (2) exile for virgin fornicator, (3) purificatory state for tawaf, (4) faith condition for expiation slave. For each, Hanafis reject the addition; Shafi'is accept it.
In all four, the Hanafis see the original text as 'amm (general) and the addition as an abrogating change.
5
Evaluate the evidentiary requirement
Since addition is naskh, it requires the same evidence as naskh: either mutawatir or mashhur transmission, not khabar wahid or qiyas. This protects the integrity of the original text.
The Hanafis accept the hadiths cited but consider them too weak to abrogate the Quranic general commands.

It begins by noting that although the general principle is that a sick person cannot make free donations of his wealth due to the rights of heirs and creditors, the Shariah has permitted bequests from up to one-third of the estate as a concession (rukhsah). This concession is motivated by the need to allow the sick person to compensate for missed acts of worship, as narrated in the well-known hadith of Sa'd ibn Abi Waqqas (رضي الله عنه) where the Prophet (صلى الله عليه وسلم) fixed the maximum bequest at one-third. The text then addresses a possible misunderstanding: one might think that since the concession gives the sick person the right to one-third, he can bequeath it to anyone, even an heir. The commentator refutes this by invoking the verse of inheritance (4:11) and the hadith 'No bequest to an heir'. The passage explains that the original obligation of bequest (in Surah al-Baqarah) was abrogated by the inheritance verses, and the Prophet explicitly declared that no bequest is allowed to an heir because each right-holder has been given their due. The passage concludes with two scenarios: (a) 'Form' (surah): if the sick person sells an asset to an heir at market value, it is considered a bequest in form. Imam Abu Hanifa rules that this is invalid in all cases because the right of the heir attaches both to the value and the specific asset. The two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) allow it if the price is fair. (b) 'Meaning' (ma'nan): if the sick person acknowledges (iqrar) a debt or ownership in favor of an heir, it is considered a bequest in meaning and is invalid due to the suspicion of favoritism, unlike the Shafi'i school which accepts it.

Key Terms
صورة (surah): Literal form or outward appearance. Here, a transaction that appears to be a sale but functions as a bequest to an heir.
معنى (ma'nan): The underlying legal reality or meaning. In the context, an acknowledgment to an heir is considered a bequest in meaning because it transfers wealth without compensation.
وصية لوارث (wasiyyah li warith): A bequest to an heir. Prohibited by hadith and consensus.
نسخ (naskh): Abrogation – the replacement of a legal ruling by a later revelation.

Prohibition of Selling Ummahat al-Awlad: Naskh and Ijma
Refutes the permission of selling umm walad using naskh and ijma

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of naskh to resolve conflicting hadith and identify the role of ijma and famous sunnah in establishing a ruling.

Core Idea
A permissive hadith is abrogated if its own text reports a later prohibition, and famous sunnah plus consensus bind the ruling.

This passage addresses a classical usul problem: two conflicting hadith regarding the sale of an umm walad. The Hanafi position uses the concept of naskh (abrogation) and the authority of famous sunnah and consensus to override a solitary hadith. The student will learn how the Hanafi school evaluates evidence when direct texts appear contradictory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conflicting positions
Recognise that Dawud al-Isfahani permits sale based on the hadith of Jabir, while the Hanafis prohibit based on the hadith of Ibn Abbas and the practice of Umar.
2
Analyse the Hanafi objection
Understand that the hadith of Jabir is claimed to be abrogated because its own text reports that Umar later prohibited the practice.
3
Apply the concept of naskh
Distinguish the earlier ruling (permission) from the later ruling (prohibition). Note that abrogation is explicit in the hadith text.
4
Examine the strength of evidence
Compare the 'famous sunnah' (mashhur) and the consensus (ijma) of the second generation with the solitary hadith. Hanafis give priority to mashhur and ijma.
5
Derive the final ruling
Conclude that the Hanafi position of prohibition is established through naskh, famous sunnah, and ijma.

Commentary
This passage addresses the ruling on selling an umm walad (a slave woman who has borne a child to her master). The author presents the view of Dawud al-Isfahani and his followers who permitted its sale, relying on a hadith from Jabir and the principle that the slave remains property until proven otherwise. The Hanafi response refutes this by presenting the famous sunnah (hadith from Ibn Abbas and practice of Umar) which prohibits sale, and argues that the hadith of Jabir is abrogated (mansukh) because in its own text it mentions that Umar later prohibited it. The prohibition was widely accepted by the second generation of Muslims, forming a consensus. Thus the Hanafi ruling is that selling an umm walad is prohibited.

Key Terms
أم الولد (umm al-walad): A slave woman who has given birth to a child from her master, thereby acquiring special status.
النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation of a legal ruling by a later one; here the hadith of Jabir is abrogated by the later prohibition.
السنة المشهورة (al-sunnah al-mashhūrah): A sunnah that is well-known and widely accepted among scholars, carrying more weight than isolated reports.
