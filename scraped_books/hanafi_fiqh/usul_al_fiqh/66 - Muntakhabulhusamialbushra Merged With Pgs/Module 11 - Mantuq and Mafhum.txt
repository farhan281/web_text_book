Sarih vs Kinaya: The Role of Intention in Divorce and Hudud
Explains sarih vs kinaya with divorce and hudud examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify whether an expression is sarih or kinaya and apply the Hanafi rule that kinaya requires intention for legal effect, particularly in divorce and hudud cases.

Core Idea
Sarih needs no intention; kinaya needs it — hudud are averted by shubhat.

This lesson introduces the foundational distinction between explicit (sarih) and implicit (kinaya) expressions in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. Using examples from divorce law and the hadd punishments, the author demonstrates that the clarity of speech determines whether intention is necessary for legal consequences. The statement 'Observe 'idda' (as used by the Prophet) is shown to be sarih, while phrases like 'You are one' are kinaya. Understanding this difference is crucial for correctly applying rulings in marriage and penal law.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish sarih from kinaya
Grasp the definitions: sarih conveys its meaning without need for intention (e.g., 'You are divorced'); kinaya requires intention or context (e.g., 'You are one'). Note that the original in speech is sarih.
Kinaya has a deficiency in clarity.
2
Analyze the hadith proof
The Prophet told Sawda bint Zam'ah 'Observe 'idda' (اعتدي) and then took her back. This proves that 'idda is sarih for revocable divorce because the return is only valid after a revocable divorce.
The hadith is ahad (zanni al-thubut) but sufficient for this ruling.
3
Apply the rule to 'You are one'
The phrase 'anti wahida' (أنت واحدة) can be a descriptor of the divorce (meaning 'one divorce') or a compliment. Only when intended as a descriptor of divorce does it effect a revocable divorce. Without intention, it is kinaya and has no legal effect.
Intention removes ambiguity.
4
Extend to hudud and kaffarat
Hudud and kaffarat are averted by doubts (shubhat). Therefore, kinaya expressions (e.g., 'I had sex with her' without saying 'zina') do not establish hadd penalties. Only sarih words like 'I committed adultery' suffice.
Similarly, for qadhf, explicit words are required.

This passage is a ruling from the third section on ways of using speech. The author explains the distinction between explicit (sarih) and implicit (kinaya) expressions, particularly in the context of divorce. He first mentions a hadith where the Prophet told Sawdah to observe 'idda and then took her back, proving that the word 'idda can effect a revocable divorce. Key Terms
صريح (sarih): An explicit expression that conveys its meaning without need for intention, e.g., 'You are divorced'.
كناية (kinaya): An implicit expression that requires intention or context to convey the intended meaning, e.g., 'You are one' if intended as divorce.
شبهات (shubhat): Doubts that avert hadd punishments; here it refers to hudud and kaffarat which are not established by kinaya.

Dalalah al-Nass: Definition and Scope
Defining dalalah al-nass and its characteristics

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dalalah al-nass as a linguistic implication, distinguish it from ibarah and isharah, and apply the 'uff' example to identify rulings derived by dalalah.

Core Idea
Dalalah al-nass is a definitive linguistic implication, not a speculative analogy, known to all Arabic speakers.

In this lesson, we examine what the Hanafi school means by 'dalalah al-nass' (implication of the text). The text clarifies that it is not the apparent meaning (ibarah) or the indicated meaning (isharah), but rather the necessary implied meaning (iltizami). For example, the prohibition of saying 'uff' to parents implies the prohibition of beating them, as any Arabic speaker immediately understands. This type of inference is definitive (qat'i) and can even establish severe penalties like hadd.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three excluded categories
Understand why ibarah (explicit wording), isharah (indication), and mahzuf (omitted part) are excluded from dalalah. They are established by the wording itself, not by the meaning of the wording.
Ibarah gives the literal ruling; isharah requires additional context; mahzuf is treated as if mentioned.
2
Grasp the concept of iltizami meaning
The iltizami meaning is what is necessarily understood from the original meaning without any additional reasoning. For instance, 'uff' implies causing harm, which necessarily implies that greater harm is also prohibited.
3
Work through the 'uff' example
Trace from the explicit prohibition of saying 'uff' (established by ibarah) to the implied prohibition of beating. Note that this inference is immediate and requires no ijtihad.
Quran 17:23 is the proof text.
4
Recognize the epistemological status
Dalalah is qat'i (definitive) because it is known by any Arabic speaker without reflection. This is unlike qiyas which is often zanni (speculative).
Hadd penalties can be established by dalalah because it is definitive.
5
Note attribution to the text
Dalalah is attributed to the text itself, not to human opinion. This distinguishes it from qiyas, which is attributed to the mujtahid's reasoning.
This point leads into the contrast with qiyas.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion from the previous lesson by clarifying that 'dalalah al-nass' (implication of the text) refers to a ruling derived from the necessary implication (iltizami) of the text's linguistic meaning, not from the explicit wording or direct indication. The author explains that by 'meaning of the text' it does not mean the apparent linguistic meaning (which falls under ibarah), but an implied consequence. For example, the prohibition of saying 'uff' to parents (Quran 17:23) explicitly only forbids uttering that word, but through linguistic implication, it also forbids beating or harming them, because if a minor expression of annoyance is forbidden, then greater forms of harm are even more strongly forbidden. This is understood by any native speaker without need for ijtihad or qiyas, thus it is definitive (qat'i). The author distinguishes this from qiyas (analogy), which often yields speculative rulings and requires a mujtahid's deduction. The text also addresses a counter-claim that dalalah is merely qiyas jali (clear analogy), but the author refutes this by noting the differences: dalalah is known by all Arabic speakers without effort, while qiyas requires premises and is often speculative. Furthermore, dalalah is used to establish hadd penalties and expiations, which cannot be done with speculative analogy. The passage also clarifies that 'dalalah' is attributed to the text, whereas qiyas is attributed to human opinion. An example from hadd: the case of Ma'iz being stoned for zina because he was muhsan, which is understood by dalalah from the text of the hadith. The author also mentions that the muqtada (implied by context) is excluded from dalalah because it is directly from the text.

Key Terms
دلالة النص (dalalat al-nass): A ruling derived from the necessary implied meaning of the text's wording, without need for qiyas or ijtihad.
الالتزامي (al-iltizami): The necessary implication of a word; what is automatically understood from the original meaning.
القياس الجلي (al-qiyas al-jali): Clear analogy, where the 'illa is obvious and the similarity between cases is strong.

Dalalah al-Nass vs Qiyas: The Hanafi Refutation
Contrasting dalalah al-nass with qiyas and refuting the equating view

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to articulate at least three differences between dalalah al-nass and qiyas, and explain why the Hanafi school rejects the equation of dalalah with clear analogy (qiyas jali).

Core Idea
Dalalah is definitive and known by all Arabic speakers; qiyas is speculative and requires a mujtahid.

Some scholars have argued that dalalah al-nass is merely a form of clear analogy (qiyas jali). This lesson examines the Hanafi refutation of that claim. The text identifies fundamental differences: dalalah is definitive (qat'i) while qiyas is often speculative; dalalah is grasped by any Arabic speaker without reflection, whereas qiyas requires ijtihad; and dalalah is attributed to the text, while qiyas is attributed to human opinion. The practical consequence is that hadd penalties can be established by dalalah but not by qiyas.

Guided Path
1
State the opposing claim
The opposing view holds that dalalah al-nass is essentially clear analogy (qiyas jali) because it extends the ruling from one case to another based on a shared attribute (e.g., harm).
This claim is addressed as a zan (claim) that the author refutes.
2
Examine the Hanafi counter-arguments
The Hanafi school gives three main differences: (1) Certainty – dalalah is qat'i, qiyas is often zanni. (2) Accessibility – dalalah is known by all Arabic speakers, qiyas only by mujtahids. (3) Attribution – dalalah is attributed to the text, qiyas to the reasoner.
These are explicitly listed in the text.
3
Analyze the practical consequence
Because dalalah is definitive, it can establish hadd penalties (which require certain evidence). Qiyas, being speculative, cannot. This is a critical legal difference.
Example: The hadd of zina for a muhsan (stoning) is derived by dalalah from the hadith of Ma'iz.
4
Revisit the 'uff' example in this light
The inference from 'uff' to beating is immediate and does not require the identification of a common cause ('illa) – it is understood by any Arabic speaker. This shows it is not analogy.
5
Understand the author's conclusion
The author calls his statement 'not deduction by opinion' a refutation (radd) of those who equate dalalah with qiyas. The distinction is not merely terminological but affects the weight and applicability of the ruling.

Commentary
This passage continues the discussion from the previous lesson by clarifying that 'dalalah al-nass' (implication of the text) refers to a ruling derived from the necessary implication (iltizami) of the text's linguistic meaning, not from the explicit wording or direct indication. The author explains that by 'meaning of the text' it does not mean the apparent linguistic meaning (which falls under ibarah), but an implied consequence. For example, the prohibition of saying 'uff' to parents (Quran 17:23) explicitly only forbids uttering that word, but through linguistic implication, it also forbids beating or harming them, because if a minor expression of annoyance is forbidden, then greater forms of harm are even more strongly forbidden. This is understood by any native speaker without need for ijtihad or qiyas, thus it is definitive (qat'i). The author distinguishes this from qiyas (analogy), which often yields speculative rulings and requires a mujtahid's deduction. The text also addresses a counter-claim that dalalah is merely qiyas jali (clear analogy), but the author refutes this by noting the differences: dalalah is known by all Arabic speakers without effort, while qiyas requires premises and is often speculative. Furthermore, dalalah is used to establish hadd penalties and expiations, which cannot be done with speculative analogy. The passage also clarifies that 'dalalah' is attributed to the text, whereas qiyas is attributed to human opinion. An example from hadd: the case of Ma'iz being stoned for zina because he was muhsan, which is understood by dalalah from the text of the hadith. The author also mentions that the muqtada (implied by context) is excluded from dalalah because it is directly from the text.

Key Terms
دلالة النص (dalalat al-nass): A ruling derived from the necessary implied meaning of the text's wording, without need for qiyas or ijtihad.
القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning that extends a ruling from an original case to a new case based on a common 'illa, often speculative.
الالتزامي (al-iltizami): The necessary implication of a word; what is automatically understood from the original meaning.
الحدود (al-hudud): Fixed penalties prescribed by Islamic law for specific crimes (e.g., theft, zina, false accusation).

Defining al-Muqtada: The Implied Meaning in Hanafi Usul
Defines muqtada and its categories

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-muqtada in Hanafi usul, explain its function as a condition for textual validity, and identify its three types.

Core Idea
Muqtada is an implied addition required to make explicit text valid; it is both a condition and a ruling.

In Hanafi usul, not every legal ruling is derived from the explicit words of the text. Some rulings are based on an implied meaning (muqtada) that the text necessitates for its own validity. This lesson introduces the concept of al-muqtada, explaining why it is needed, how it functions as both a condition and a derivative ruling, and the three categories recognized by Hanafi scholars.

Guided Path
1
Identify the textual deficiency
Recognize that some explicit statements would be invalid without an implied addition. For example, 'ask the village' requires 'people of the village' to be understood.
This highlights the need for muqtada.
2
Understand the dual nature of muqtada
Note that muqtada is a condition for the text's validity (prior) but also a ruling dependent on the text (subsequent). The passage distinguishes two perspectives: as a condition it precedes, as a ruling it follows.
This dual aspect is crucial for correct application.
3
Learn the tripartite classification
Memorize the three types of muqtada: (1) for truthfulness of the speaker, (2) for rational correctness of speech, and (3) for legal correctness. Each serves a different purpose in validating the text.
These categories are specific to the Hanafi school.
4
Apply the definition to examples
See how the three types apply to specific hadith and Quranic verses. For instance, 'raise from my ummah' requires the muqtada of forgiveness for truthfulness.
Practice identifying the type in given texts.

Commentary
This passage defines the term 'muqtada' (implied meaning) in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. It explains that the muqtada is an addition to the explicit text (mansus) that is required to validate the explicit text; without it, the explicit text would be meaningless. Thus, the muqtada is a condition for the validity of the text, and since a condition must precede its conditioned, the muqtada is given priority. However, in terms of ruling, the muqtada is considered a branch (far') that follows the text (asl). The passage distinguishes between two perspectives: as a condition, it is a prerequisite (mutqaddim); as a ruling established through the text, it is a subsequent branch. The key point is that the ruling established by muqtada is equal in authority (qat'i) to the ruling established by the explicit dalala (textual indication) except when there is a conflict between the two, in which case the explicit dalala is given precedence. But the explicit dalala (the understood objective of removing impurity) allows using other liquids like vinegar, as the goal is removal of impurity, not the use of water per se. So the dalala overrides the muqtada. The passage also mentions that the majority of Hanafi scholars classify what is estimated to correct speech into three types: (1) for truthfulness of the speaker (e.g., 'pardon is raised from my ummah for mistakes'), (2) for rational correctness of speech (e.g., 'ask the village' meaning ask its people), and (3) for legal correctness (e.g., 'free your slave from me for a thousand'). All are called muqtada. Al-Qadi Abu Zayd inclined to this inclusive definition.

المنصوص (al-mansus): The explicit text (e.g., Quranic verse or hadith) that requires supplementation.
الاقتضاء (al-iqtida'): The textual indication that necessitates an implied meaning for correctness.

Muqtada vs. Explicit Dalala: The Rule of Precedence
Illustrates the precedence of explicit dalala over muqtada with a hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the rule that explicit textual indication (dalala) overrides muqtada when they conflict, using the hadith example from this passage.

Core Idea
When muqtada conflicts with explicit dalala, the explicit darala takes precedence.

While muqtada is a powerful tool for deriving rulings from texts, it is not absolute. The passage emphasizes that when the implied meaning (muqtada) contradicts the explicit indication (dalala) of the same text, the explicit meaning prevails. This lesson focuses on that rule, illustrated with the Prophetic command to wash menstrual blood with water and sidr. Understanding this hierarchy is crucial for accurate istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the explicit text and its apparent command
In the hadith, the Prophet (SAW) says 'wash it with water and sidr'. The explicit command is to use water and sidr as washing agents.
The explicit dalala is the understood objective: removal of impurity.
2
Determine the muqtada derived from the command
The command 'wash with water' by iqtida' implies that only water (and sidr) are permissible; other liquids are not allowed. This is the muqtada (implied prohibition).
This muqtada is based on necessity: without this implication, the command would be meaningless if any liquid could be used.
3
Recognize the conflict between muqtada and explicit dalala
The explicit dalala of the text is to remove impurity; this can be achieved by any liquid that removes impurity. Thus, the muqtada (only water) conflicts with the broader explicit objective.
The passage gives the example of throwing the garment into water, which cleans without scrubbing.
4
Apply the rule: explicit dalala overrides muqtada
Since the explicit dalala is more fundamental and universally understood, it takes precedence. Therefore, other liquids like vinegar are permitted for washing impurities.
This rule is stated explicitly: 'عند المعارضة يترجّح الدلالة على الاقتضاء'.

It explains that the muqtada is an addition to the explicit text (mansus) that is required to validate the explicit text; without it, the explicit text would be meaningless. Thus, the muqtada is a condition for the validity of the text, and since a condition must precede its conditioned, the muqtada is given priority. However, in terms of ruling, the muqtada is considered a branch (far') that follows the text (asl). The key point is that the ruling established by muqtada is equal in authority (qat'i) to the ruling established by the explicit dalala (textual indication) except when there is a conflict between the two, in which case the explicit dalala is given precedence. An example is given: the Prophet's command 'wash it with water and sidr' (hadith about menstrual blood on garment). But the explicit dalala (the understood objective of removing impurity) allows using other liquids like vinegar, as the goal is removal of impurity, not the use of water per se. The passage also mentions that the majority of Hanafi scholars classify what is estimated to correct speech into three types: (1) for truthfulness of the speaker (e.g., 'pardon is raised from my ummah for mistakes'), (2) for rational correctness of speech (e.g., 'ask the village' meaning ask its people), and (3) for legal correctness (e.g., 'free your slave from me for a thousand'). Distinction Between Muqtada and Mahzuf in Hanafi Usul
Explains the difference between implied meaning (muqtada) and omitted element (mahzuf) in textual interpretation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between muqtada and mahzuf, identify their different treatment of generality (umum), and apply the later Hanafi position to a given textual example.

Core Idea
Muqtada is estimated for shari'ah correctness; mahzuf is linguistic deletion — only muqtada sometimes accepts generality.

In Hanafi usul, not all implied meanings in a text are the same. Some are required for the text to be legally valid (muqtada), while others are simply omitted for linguistic brevity (mahzuf). This distinction affects whether the implied meaning can be generalized (umum). Early Hanafis denied generality in any muqtada, but later scholars refined the position, allowing generality only in muqtada estimated for legal correctness. Understanding this nuance is essential for correctly deriving rulings from the Qur'an and Sunnah.

Guided Path
1
Define Muqtada and Mahzuf
State the definitions: Muqtada is an implied element required for shari'ah correctness; Mahzuf is a purely linguistic omission indicated by context.
Muqtada is shar'i, mahzuf is lughawi.
2
Identify the Sign of Each
For mahzuf, when the omitted word is expressed, the grammatical attachment shifts to it. For muqtada, the attachment remains with the original expression.
Example: 'Ask the village' — expressing 'people' changes attachment.
3
Understand the Dispute on Generality
Early Hanafis: muqtada does not accept umum. Shafi'is: muqtada does accept umum. Later Hanafis: only muqtada estimated for legal correctness accepts umum.
This is a key ikhtilaf between the schools.
4
Apply the Later Hanafi Classification
Identify whether an implied element is muqtada (for shar'i correctness) or mahzuf (purely linguistic). Only the former may accept umum if the context allows.
Example: 'Free your slave from me for a thousand' — the implied sale is muqtada; generality is not accepted.
5
Analyze a Quranic Example
Consider Quran 2:60 'Strike the stone with your staff' — the implied actions (striking, splitting, gushing) are mahzuf. Their expression does not change grammar, confirming they are linguistic deletions.
This example tests the definition of mahzuf.

Descriptors and Conditions in Hanafi Usul
Hanafi analysis of descriptors and conditions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that a descriptor does not imply exclusivity and identify when a condition operates on the cause versus the ruling.

Core Idea
A descriptor's absence does not negate the ruling; a condition blocks the cause, not the ruling itself.

In this lesson, we analyze the Hanafi distinction between different levels of descriptive attributes (awṣāf) in legal texts and their implications. The key insight is that a descriptor serving as a cause does not imply that the ruling is exclusive to that descriptor, and conditions in legal speech (like oaths and suspended divorces) operate on the cause, not the judgment. We will work through the text to understand the reasoning and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three levels of descriptors
The text classifies descriptors into highest (cause), middle (condition-like), and lowest (no effect). Focus on the highest: a descriptor like 'zani' is a cause for the hadd punishment, but its absence does not prove absence of the ruling because a ruling can have multiple causes.
This is crucial for understanding Hanafi reluctance to infer exclusivity from cause-like descriptors.
2
Analyze the condition argument from the Shafi'i perspective
The Shafi'i opponent argues that if a descriptor is like a condition, its absence should imply absence of ruling. The text rebuts this by showing that in Hanafi usul, conditions in legal statements (e.g., 'If you enter the house, you are divorced') enter upon the cause (sabab), not the ruling (hukm).
The condition prevents the cause from being effective; thus absence of condition leads to absence of cause, not direct absence of ruling.
3
Understand the difference with option conditions in sale
The text contrasts divorce/manumission with sale: in sale, the option condition (khiyār al-sharṭ) enters upon the ruling because sale permits prohibition by necessity. This distinction supports the Hanafi position that in divorce and manumission, conditions operate on the cause.
This analogy (qiyās ma‘ al-fāriq) is a key rebuttal to the Shafi'i inference.
4
Apply the rule to practical examples
The text gives examples: a person who swears not to divorce and then says 'If you enter the house, you are divorced' does not break his oath until the condition occurs. Also, suspending divorce on future ownership is valid. These demonstrate the rule in operation.
Contrast this with the invalid advance expiation (takfīr) before violation, which the text discusses later.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul discussion on the 'invalid aspects' (al-wujūh al-fāsida), specifically focusing on the legal implications of descriptive attributes in the Quran and their relationship to conditions. The author explains that descriptors like 'zani' (fornicator) and 'sariq' (thief) are at the highest level: they serve as causes (ʿillal) for the ruling, but their absence does not prove the absence of the ruling, because a ruling can have multiple causes. The middle level is when a descriptor functions like a condition (sharṭ), meaning its absence might be taken to imply the absence of the ruling. However, the author argues that even in that case, the absence of a condition does not directly prove the absence of the ruling in Hanafi thought because conditions in legal statements (like oaths) operate on the cause (sabab) of the ruling, not the ruling itself. For example, in a conditional divorce (e.g., 'If you enter the house, you are divorced'), the condition prevents the cause (the statement of divorce) from taking effect; therefore, the absence of the ruling is due to the absence of the cause, not the condition. The author contrasts this with option conditions (khiyār al-sharṭ) in sales, where the condition operates on the ruling, not the cause, because sale permits the possibility of prohibition (for necessity), whereas divorce and manumission do not. The passage also addresses the validity of suspending divorce and manumission on future ownership, and the invalidity of performing expiation before an oath violation, with a detailed refutation of the distinction between wealth-based and bodily-based expiations in this context. The conclusion affirms the Hanafi position (madhhabunā).
