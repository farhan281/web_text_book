The Fourfold Classification of Word Usage: Haqiqah, Majaz, Sarih, Kinayah
Introduces the four-fold classification of word usage

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between haqiqah, majaz, sarih, and kinayah, and identify the type of placement (linguistic, legal, customary) that determines haqiqah.

Core Idea
A word's meaning is determined by its usage and its clarity.

This passage from Muntakhab al-Ḥusāmī introduces a fundamental classification of word usage in uṣūl al-fiqh. Understanding these categories is essential for interpreting legal texts correctly. The author explains that words can be classified along two dimensions: the type of usage (ḥaqīqah vs majāz) and the clarity of expression (ṣarīḥ vs kināyah). This four-fold division helps jurists determine the binding force of words in Qur'an, Hadith, and juristic statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two dimensions
Recognize that the classification has two independent dimensions: usage (haqiqah/majaz) based on original vs. non-original meaning, and clarity (sarih/kinayah) based on disclosure vs. concealment of meaning.
Sarih/kinayah can apply to both haqiqah and majaz.
2
Define haqiqah
Haqiqah is a word used in its original meaning according to its placement (wad'). The placement can be linguistic, legal, or customary (general or special).
The definition excludes words used figuratively (majaz) or without placement (mu'ammal).
3
Distinguish types of placement
Understand the four types: linguistic (language coiner), legal (Lawgiver), customary special (technical terms of a profession), and customary general (common usage). A word can be haqiqah by one or more placements.
If a word has haqiqah by all four, it is 'haqiqah mutlaqah' (absolute).
4
Relate sarih and kinayah
Sarih means the meaning is clearly disclosed; kinayah means it is concealed and requires context. This dimension applies to both haqiqah and majaz: e.g., a literal word can be explicit (sarih) or implicit (kinayah).
The author emphasizes this four-fold division to avoid confusion that sarih/kinayah are subsets.
5
Apply to a sample text
Take a word like 'salah' (prayer): in the legal context, it is haqiqah shar'iyyah (placed by the Lawgiver) and sarih (explicit). A phrase like 'You are divorced' (in certain indirect forms) may be kinayah if the intended meaning is not immediately clear.
This classification guides how a mujtahid derives rulings from texts.

Commentary
This passage introduces the third division of word classification in usul al-fiqh: haqiqah (literal), majaz (figurative), sarih (explicit), and kinayah (implicit). The author clarifies that this is a four-fold division with two dimensions: usage (haqiqah vs majaz) and clarity (sarih vs kinayah). The commentary explains that haqiqah is when a word is used in its original meaning, and it can be further categorized by type of placement (linguistic, legal, customary general/special). The Hanafi approach emphasizes that haqiqah and majaz are properties of words, not meanings. This classification is foundational for interpreting legal texts: word-of-mouth evidence requires knowing which sense is intended.

Key Terms
الحقيقة (al-ḥaqīqah): A word used in its original meaning according to its placement (linguistic, legal, or customary).
المجاز (al-majāz): A word used in other than its original meaning due to a contextual clue.
الصريح (al-ṣarīḥ): A word whose meaning is clearly disclosed without any concealment.
الكناية (al-kināyah): A word used in a way that the meaning is concealed, requiring context to be understood.

Defining Majaz: The Need for the Perspective Qualifier
Defining majaz and the necessity of the perspective qualifier

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define majaz in Hanafi usul, explain the necessity of the qayd al-haythiyya, and distinguish majaz from ghalat, hazl, and irtijal.

Core Idea
A word can be both haqiqa and majaz from different perspectives; definitions must include 'from the perspective that it is placed for'.

Having completed the previous divisions of usage, we now turn to the third: the definition of majaz (metaphorical usage). The central challenge is that a single word, like 'salat', can be haqiqa in its original linguistic sense (supplication) and majaz in the legal context (prescribed prayer). To avoid contradictions, the definition must be qualified by the perspective (haythiyya). This lesson unpacks that concept, clarifies the conditions for majaz, and distinguishes it from mere error or jest.

Guided Path
1
Identify the object of definition
Locate the definition of majaz in the text: 'majaz is a term for every word used for other than what it was originally placed for, due to a connection between them in meaning.' Note the key components: word, other-than-original meaning, and connection.
2
Understand the problem of dual perspective
Examine the example of 'salat': it is haqiqa in the linguistic sense and majaz in the legal sense. This shows that a word can be both simultaneously from different perspectives. Realize that without a qualifier, the definitions of haqiqa and majaz would conflict.
The term 'perspective' (jiha/haythiyya) is crucial.
3
Recognize the need for the qualifier (qayd al-haythiyya)
See how the author insists that definitions must include 'min haythu' (from the perspective that...). This prevents counterexamples like 'salat' and 'dabba' from breaking the definition.
The qualifier ensures consistency across different contexts.
4
Distinguish majaz from similar phenomena
Go through the three exclusions: ghalat (error) – no relationship ('alaqa); hazl (jest) – intentional but without a real connection; irtijal (improvisation) – a new coinage without a link to the original. Only majaz has a proper 'alaqa.
Irtijal is actually considered haqiqa by a second placement, but the author excludes it because from the first placement it appears as majaz.
5
Note the condition of 'alaqa and its enumeration
The author states that a word is not used in a non-literal sense without a 'alaqa. Various scholars count the 'alaqat differently (e.g., 25, 12, 9, 5, 4). Recognise that 'alaqa is the essential link (e.g., similarity, causality).
Common examples: calling a brave man 'lion' due to shared bravery, or rain 'sky' because it comes from the sky.

Meaning-Based vs Form-Based Connection in Majaz
Defines two types of ittisal in majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between meaning-based (ma'nawi) and form-based (suri) connections in majaz and classify given examples accordingly.

Core Idea
Majaz has two types of connection: a shared attribute (ma'nawi) or physical contiguity (suri).

This passage introduces a key classification in the study of majaz (figurative language) used in usul al-fiqh. Understanding this distinction helps when interpreting legal texts that employ metaphorical language, such as using words of divorce for manumission.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of connection
The text states that connection between literal and figurative meanings can only be of two types: by meaning (ma'nawi) or by form (suri). There is no third type.
This binary classification is crucial for analyzing majaz.
2
Understand meaning-based (ma'nawi) connection
This occurs when the literal and figurative meanings share a well-known attribute, e.g., calling a brave man 'lion' due to shared bravery. This type is called isti'ara by rhetoricians.
The shared attribute must be commonly recognized in custom ('urf).
3
Understand form-based (suri) connection
This occurs when the form or image of the figurative meaning is physically contiguous with the literal meaning, e.g., calling rain 'sky' because rain comes from the sky. This type is called majaz mursal.
Contiguity includes cause-effect, adjacency, etc.
4
Apply the classification to legal terms
The author notes that form-based connection is especially relevant in legal (shar'i) usage, such as using divorce words for manumission, where the connection is formal, not meaning-based.
Causal connections fall under form-based, as there is no inherent meaning relationship.

Applying the Rule Against Combining Haqiqah and Majaz to Bequests to Mawali
Applies the usul rule against combining haqiqah and majaz to a bequest interpretation.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that haqiqah and majaz cannot be combined in a single expression, by analyzing a bequest case involving the ambiguous term 'mawali'.

Core Idea
Haqiqah and majaz cannot be combined; context can determine the literal meaning of an ambiguous word.

This lesson demonstrates how a core usul principle—the impossibility of combining haqiqah and majaz—resolves an ambiguity in wills. The word 'mawali' can refer to either the manumitter or the manumitted, but in the specific case of an Arab with no wala', context forces the literal meaning. We will walk through the reasoning step by step, see the derivative ruling, and contrast with a scenario where figurative extension is allowed.

Guided Path
1
Identify the source of ambiguity
Recognize that the word 'mawla' (plural mawali) is a homonym with multiple literal and figurative meanings, including the manumitter (mu'tiq bi'l-fath) and the manumitted (mu'tiq bi'l-kasr).
The Hanafis treat it as mushtarak (shared homonym) in general.
2
Determine the context of the testator
Note that the testator is an Arab with no wala' upon him. This means he has never been enslaved and no one holds wala' over him. Thus the possibility of him being a freedman (mu'tiq bi'l-kasr) is negated.
Rare exceptions exist (e.g., Arab child born to a slave mother), but the condition 'la wala' alayhi' excludes them.
3
Apply the haqiqah-majaz rule
Since the literal meaning (testator's own freedmen) is determined, intending the figurative meaning (mawali of the freedman) would combine haqiqah and majaz, which is impermissible. Therefore only the literal meaning is valid.
The rule is: لا يجوز الجمع بين الحقيقة والمجاز في التعبير الواحد.
4
Derive the distribution of the bequest
If the testator has one freedman, that freedman receives half the bequest (because the minimum of plural is two, but only one exists); the other half returns to the heirs. If two or more freedmen, they share the entire third. Nothing goes to the mawali of the freedman.
This distribution follows from the determination of the literal meaning and the rule against combining.
5
Contrast with the aman case
In the aman request, 'our children and mawali' includes children of children figuratively, but this is allowed because the entire phrase is interpreted as a single intended coverage, not a combination of separate meanings.
The difference: in the will, two distinct groups are intended; in aman, one group is intended with figurative extension.

Prioritizing Haqiqah over Majaz: The Hanafi Rule
Stating the usul rule that literal meaning is used when possible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that literal meaning (haqiqah) is used when possible, and identify when figurative meaning (majaz) is used due to impossibility or abandonment by Shariah or custom.

Core Idea
Literal is default; figurative is used only when literal is impossible or abandoned.

This lesson introduces a foundational Hanafi usul principle: when interpreting a word, the literal meaning (haqiqah) is always preferred as long as it is possible. Only when the literal is impossible (mut'adhdhirah) or abandoned (mahjoorah) do we turn to the figurative (majaz). We will see this rule applied to two scenarios: oaths and agency for litigation, including a madhhab dispute with Zufar and al-Shafi'i.

Guided Path
1
State the rule
Identify the core principle: haqiqah takes priority over majaz. The figurative does not compete with the original as long as the literal is accessible.
2
Define impossibility and abandonment
Impossibility (ta'addhur) means the literal cannot be reached without extreme hardship. Abandonment (hajr) means the literal is possible but people or Shariah have left it.
Abandonment by Shariah (e.g., prohibitory verse) is treated like abandonment by custom.
3
Apply to oath examples
Analyze the two oath cases: 'not eat from this palm tree' (literal impossible) → figurative: eat fruit or value. 'Not place foot in house' (literal abandoned) → figurative: enter the house.
Intention can alter the ruling if specified.
4
Apply to agency for litigation
Examine the Hanafi position: agency for litigation (tawkeel bil-khusoomah) is interpreted figuratively as absolute response (denial or admission) because the literal meaning (denial) is prohibited by Quran 8:46 and thus abandoned.
This differs from Zufar and al-Shafi'i who restrict agency to denial only.
5
Note the role of intention
The default interpretation applies when no specific intention is expressed. If the principal had a specific intention, that governs.

Commentary
This passage establishes a core usul principle: when the literal meaning (haqiqah) of a word is possible, the figurative (majaz) is not used. If the literal is impossible (mut'adhdhirah) or abandoned (mahjoorah), then the figurative is employed. Two examples illustrate: swearing not to eat from a palm tree (impossible to eat the tree itself) leads to the figurative meaning of its fruit or value. Swearing not to place a foot in a house (abandoned custom) leads to the figurative meaning of entering. The ruling applies unless the swearer had a specific intention. The passage then applies this rule to agency in litigation: the literal meaning of litigation (denial) is prohibited by Shariah (Quran 8:46), so it is considered abandoned, and the agency is interpreted figuratively as covering any response (denial or admission). This contrasts with the views of Zufar and al-Shafi'i. The commentary also clarifies the impossibility and abandonment conditions with further explanations, emphasizing that intention can affect the ruling.

Key Terms
حقيقة (haqiqah): The literal meaning for which the word was originally placed.
مجاز (majaz): A figurative meaning derived from the literal by usage, used when literal is impossible or abandoned.
متعذرة (mut'adhdhirah): Impossible to achieve except with hardship, such that it is not reasonably attainable.
مهجورة (mahjoorah): Abandoned by custom or law, even if technically possible.
توكيل بالخصومة (tawkeel bil-khusoomah): Appointing another person to litigate on one's behalf.

Shifting from Literal to Metaphorical: Impossible or Abandoned Meanings
Rule with examples on shifting from literal to metaphorical meaning

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule for when a literal meaning is impossible or abandoned, and apply it to example oaths and agency in litigation.

Core Idea
When a literal meaning is impossible or abandoned, the metaphorical takes over — but when both are in use, priority is debated among Hanafi imams.

This passage from a Hanafi usul work explains a key interpretive principle: how to shift from literal (haqiqa) to metaphorical (majaz) when the literal is not feasible or has fallen out of use. You will see this applied to oaths, agency contracts, and the legal abandonment (hajr) of certain meanings. A notable intra-Hanafi dispute arises when both meanings are still used.

Guided Path
1
Identify the principle
The core rule: literal meaning is given priority as long as it is possible to act upon it. If the literal is impossible (muta'addhir) or abandoned (mahjur), one resorts to the metaphorical.
This rule governs interpretation of words in oaths, contracts, and legal texts.
2
Examine the impossible case
In an oath 'I will not eat from this palm tree', the literal meaning (eating the tree itself) is impossible. Therefore, the metaphorical meaning applies: eating its fruit or its monetary value if no fruit.
If the tree has no fruit, the oath is broken by consuming its price.
3
Examine the abandoned case
In an oath 'I will not place my foot in this house', the literal act (placing the foot without entering) is possible but customarily abandoned. Thus, the metaphorical meaning (entering) is used. Similarly, legally abandoned meanings like 'litigation' (khusumah) are also replaced by metaphor (any response).
Legal abandonment is treated like customary abandonment.
4
Contrast when both meanings are used
When both literal and metaphorical meanings are common, Abu Hanifa prioritizes the literal, while the Sahibayn prioritize the metaphorical if it is more frequent or better understood. Example: oath not to eat 'from this wheat' — Abu Hanifa: breaking only by eating the grain; Sahibayn: breaking by eating bread.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf with practical consequences.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Consider an oath 'I will not speak to this child'. The attribute 'child' may be a motive, but because abandoning speech to a child is legally discouraged, the oath applies to the entity irrespective of age. If he speaks after the child becomes an adult, he breaks the oath.
The principle of legal abandonment overrides the linguistic consideration of the attribute.

If it is said: On this, his statement 'I will not speak to this child' becomes like his statement 'I will not speak to a child' in the indefinite form, and they said if he says it indefinitely, the attribute of childhood is considered such that his oath is restricted to children, so if he speaks after childhood he does not break the oath? The verification of that is: When an oath is taken on something with an attribute, if mentioned indefinitely, it is considered absolutely, whether the attribute is a valid motive for the oath or not; if mentioned definitively, if the attribute is a valid motive, it is considered too—as in his saying 'I will not speak to this child'—otherwise not. 'If the word etc.' means what we mentioned earlier was only in the case of the abandoned literal meaning. Commentary
This passage is an explanation of the principle that when the literal meaning (haqiqa) of a word is impossible (muta'addhir) or abandoned (mahjur), one must resort to the metaphorical meaning (majaz). The author first defines the terms: 'musta'ar' (metaphorical) is the word used figuratively, and it does not compete with the original (the literal meaning) as long as the literal can be applied. This is contrasted with the views of Zufar and al-Shafi'i who do not consider the legal abandonment. The passage also examines oaths involving children, explaining that if one swears not to speak to 'this child', the attribute of childhood is considered only if it is a valid motive; otherwise, the oath applies to the entity regardless of age. Finally, the author discusses a case where both literal and metaphorical meanings are used, e.g., an oath about eating wheat. Here Abu Hanifa gives priority to the literal (eating the grain), while the two students (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) give priority to the common metaphorical usage (eating bread). The passage demonstrates how Hanafi usul al-fiqh applies linguistic principles to derive rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn), regarding the nature of majaz (metaphorical usage). The starting point is that majaz is a substitute (khalaf) for haqiqah (literal meaning). All agree that haqiqah is the original and majaz is secondary, resorted to when haqiqah is impossible or not intended. However, they differ on what aspect of substitution applies. Abu Hanifa holds that majaz is a substitute in the speech act (takallum). This means that as long as the speech is grammatically and syntactically correct, one can use it metaphorically even if the literal referent is nonexistent, provided the speech can be considered a valid utterance. For example, a man says about his female slave who is older than him, 'This is my son,' intending to free her. The literal meaning (sonship) is impossible, but the speech is grammatically sound (subject and predicate), so Abu Hanifa regards it as a valid metaphorical statement meaning freedom, and the slave is freed. Similarly, in an oath not to drink from the Euphrates, Abu Hanifa interprets the oath literally (lapping from the river), so the oath is only broken if one laps directly. The Sahibayn, on the other hand, hold that majaz is a substitute in the ruling (hukm). The original literal meaning must be possible in the context for majaz to work; if the literal meaning is impossible ab initio, the metaphor fails. In the slave example, since the literal meaning (sonship) is impossible (older cannot be son of younger), the speech is meaningless and does not free the slave. In the Euphrates oath, they consider the common metaphorical usage (drinking from a vessel or by scooping) as the intended meaning, so the oath is broken by that as well. The key difference is that Abu Hanifa focuses on the speech's formal validity, while the Sahibayn focus on the existence of a possible literal meaning that can be substituted. Accordingly, when haqiqah is commonly used and majaz is also common, Abu Hanifa prefers the literal meaning because speech with haqiqah is preponderant over speech with majaz, whereas the Sahibayn prefer the metaphorical meaning because the metaphorical usage carries the ruling of haqiqah more strongly.

Abandoning Haqiqah: Context and Custom

Learning Objective

Core Idea
When literal meaning is impossible or customarily abandoned, metaphor prevails – but only if haqiqah itself is not commonly used.

This passage from al-Muntakhab explains two of the five ways the literal meaning (haqiqah) can be abandoned in favor of metaphorical meaning (majaz). The first is through the context of speech (mahall al-kalam) – if the literal meaning is impossible or leads to falsehood, metaphor is necessary. The second is through customary usage ('adah) – if a word is customarily used for a non-literal meaning, that meaning is intended. However, an important dispute arises between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding which takes precedence when both haqiqah and majaz are in use.

2

For Abu Hanifa, the consideration is the speech itself; if haqiqah is used in speech customarily, there is no need for majaz. For the Sahibayn, the consideration is the ruling; if the metaphorical meaning is more customary, the ruling in majaz is preponderant.
3

The first qarinah is mahall al-kalam (context of speech): if the literal meaning is impossible or would entail falsehood for an infallible speaker, majaz is used. The second is 'adah (custom): if a word is customarily used for a non-literal meaning, that meaning is intended, provided haqiqah is not also commonly used.

The discussion begins by reiterating the earlier principle: when haqiqah is commonly used and not obsolete, Abu Hanifa prefers it over majaz even if majaz is also common, because his criterion is the speech act. For the Sahibayn, the criterion is the ruling, so if the metaphorical meaning is more customary, they prefer majaz. The author then notes that the author of the matn (al-Muntakhab) should have used 'predominance of usage' instead of 'inclusion of the ruling of haqiqah' to cover all types of majaz. Then the passage explains two qara'in (indicators) for abandoning haqiqah: (1) context of speech: if the literal meaning is impossible or leads to falsehood for an infallible speaker (like the Prophet), then majaz is used. Example: 'Do not eat from this date-palm' — the palm tree itself cannot be eaten, so it means the fruit or its value. (2) custom: if the word is used customarily for a non-literal meaning, that meaning is intended. Example: 'Do not place your foot in the house' means 'do not enter,' because the literal act of placing the foot is not what is meant by custom. The same applies to shari'i terms like salah, zakat, hajj, which have transferred meanings from their linguistic origins to specific legal acts. The key point is that haqiqah is only abandoned when it is not commonly used or when context makes it impossible; otherwise, according to Abu Hanifa, the literal meaning takes precedence.

محل الكلام (mahall al-kalam): The context in which speech occurs, including its subject matter and related circumstances.
العادة (al-'adah): Customary usage of words; what is commonly understood by a particular community.
القرينة (al-qarinah): An indicator that guides interpretation from haqiqah to majaz.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Impediments to Legal Capacity: Types and Effects
Definition and classification of impediments (al-awarid) to legal capacity.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between heavenly and acquired impediments to legal capacity and identify their effects on ahliyyat al-wujub and ahliyyat al-ada'.

Core Idea
Impediments (awarid) are either heavenly (outside control) or acquired (chosen), and they either remove or modify capacity.

Legal capacity (ahliyyah) is not absolute; it can be affected by various impediments. The Hanafi jurists divided these impediments into two fundamental categories: heavenly, which come from the Lawgiver without our choice, and acquired, which result from our actions. This lesson unpacks this classification and explains how different impediments impact the two types of capacity: capacity to acquire rights and obligations (ahliyyat al-wujub) and capacity to perform acts (ahliyyat al-ada').

Guided Path
1
Understand the two major categories
Identify heavenly impediments (e.g., death, sleep, insanity) as those decreed by Allah without human choice. Identify acquired impediments (e.g., apostasy, slavery, ignorance) as those involving human choice.
Heavenly are also called 'divine' or 'involuntary'.
2
Memorize examples of each
List examples from the text: heavenly: death, sleep, fainting; acquired: apostasy, travel. Note that travel is an acquired impediment that modifies rulings without removing capacity.
Travel is a special case – it changes rulings (e.g., prayer shortened) but does not remove capacity.
3

Indefinite vs Definite Accidents in Presenting Islam
Explaining the difference between indefinite and definite delays in presenting Islam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that distinguishes between indefinite and definite accidents ('awāriḍ samāwiyya) to determine the timing of presenting Islam to a non-Muslim husband when the wife converts.

Core Idea
A definite accident allows delay; an indefinite accident mandates immediate action to protect the right.

In Hanafi usul, not all legal incapacities are treated equally. When a woman converts to Islam, the obligation to present Islam to her non-Muslim husband depends on whether the husband's incapacity (insanity, minority, imbecility) has a known end or not. This passage illustrates how the indefinite nature of insanity forces immediate action, while the definite end of minority permits delay. Understanding this distinction is key to deriving rulings for similar 'awāriḍ samāwiyya.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of accidents
The passage divides divine accidents into 'ghayr mahdūd' (indefinite, no known end) and 'mahdūd' (definite, known end). Insanity (junūn) is indefinite; minority (ṣighar) is definite because reason typically appears by a certain age.
"غير محدود" and "محدود" are technical terms in Hanafi usul.
2
Apply the rule to the insane husband
For an insane husband (indefinite), Islam is presented immediately to his parents because waiting would harm the wife's right indefinitely. If they refuse, separation occurs.
The madman himself cannot be addressed; guardians stand in.
3
Apply the rule to the minor husband
For a minor husband (definite), presentation is delayed until he reaches the age of reason (ya'qil). The delay is permissible because the end is known, and the woman's right is not harmed indefinitely.
The minor's own Islam is valid when he is rational, so his acceptance or refusal can be awaited.
4
Handle the imbecile (ma'tuh) case
An intelligent imbecile (ma'tuh 'āqil) is treated like a rational minor: Islam is presented immediately because his conversion is valid. He is not like the insane, despite the overlap in terminology.
The text explicitly restricts the ruling: 'لا يفترقان' – they do not differ from the rational minor.
5
Derive the underlying principle
The ratio (ʿilla) is the certainty of the accident's end. If the end is known, the obligation can be suspended until the impediment ends. If unknown, immediate mandatory action is required to avoid harm (ḍarar).
This principle extends to other accidents like forgetfulness or sleep with known durations.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed analysis of the ruling on fainting (ighma') during prayer within the Hanafi school. The text begins by distinguishing fainting from sleep: while sleep is a natural and common state (tabi'iyya), fainting is an accidental impediment that causes a more severe loss of sensation and ability to use capacity. Consequently, fainting is considered a hadath (event that invalidates wudu) in all positions (standing, sitting, etc.) because it involves relaxation of joints, whereas sleep only becomes a hadath if the person is lying down or leaning. The next point addresses the permissibility of 'building' (al-bina) on one's prayer after an interruption due to fainting. The text rejects this by citing a hadith from 'Ali (RA) that allows building for habitual events like vomiting or nosebleed, but explains that fainting is not a habitual event and causes complete loss of consciousness, so the same rule does not apply. Al-Shafi'i is reported to consider only the encompassment of an entire prayer time. The text concludes with an istihsan (juristic preference) in favor of the ruling based on these reports, even though qiyas would not exempt missed prayers. The passage thus clarifies that riqq is a singular, indivisible legal status affecting the entire person.

As for riqq (slavery), it is a legal incapacity legislated as a punishment in origin, but in a state of continuance it became among the legal matters by which a person becomes subject to being owned and degraded, and it is a description that does not admit of divisibility. Indeed, Muhammad said in al-Jami' regarding a person of unknown lineage: if he acknowledges that half of him is a slave of so-and-so, then he is made a slave in his testimony and in all his rulings, and similarly 'itq (emancipation) which is its opposite. Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: i'taq (the act of emancipating) does not admit of divisibility because its effect, which is 'itq, does not admit of divisibility. Abu Hanifa said: i'taq is removal of ownership, and it admits of divisibility; it is attached to the complete removal of the ruling (of slavery) from the subject, which is 'itq and does not admit of divisibility. Riqq in language means weakness, and in law it is a legal incapacity: that the Lawgiver did not make him an owner nor eligible for what a free person owns, such as testimony, judgeship, guardianship, and the like. Riqq is a right of Allah initially in the sense that it is a punishment for disbelief, because the disbelievers were arrogant in worshipping Him, so Allah punished them in this world by making them slaves of His slaves, owned and degraded. Hence riqq is not established upon a Muslim initially. And it is a right of the people ultimately and in continuance, meaning that the Lawgiver made it ownership apart from the aspect of punishment and retribution, so that he remains owned even if he embraces Islam. 'Legal incapacity': i.e., not physical, established by legal ruling, so the slave cannot perform legal dispositions even if he is physically stronger than a free person. 'Does not admit of divisibility': in establishment and removal, because it is an effect of disbelief and a result of subjugation, so divisibility is inconceivable in them, just as it is not valid with knowledge and ignorance. Thus the subject is not susceptible to divisibility, so a slave cannot be described as partially enslaved, contrary to the ownership attached to him, for a slave can be described as partial in ownership in establishment and removal; so if he sells his slave to two persons, it is permissible by consensus. One deduces from this issue that non-divisibility is the doctrine of our three imams since none opposed it. That is: a man of unknown lineage, if he acknowledges that half of him is a slave of so-and-so, he becomes by his acknowledgment a slave in his testimony against others, as his testimony against others is like the testimony of a full slave. So it is known that the riqq established by his acknowledgment is complete and non-divisible. For if it were divisible, he would, by joining his like to him, be like one free person in testimony, just as two women are considered like one man in it. Similarly, he is a slave in all his rulings, such as prescribed punishments, inheritance, pilgrimage, and zakat, but separate ownership is not established except by text, because ownership is divisible by agreement, just as riqq and 'itq are non-divisible by agreement. Similarly, 'itq, which is the opposite of riqq, does not admit of divisibility, because 'itq in law is a legal capacity by which a person becomes eligible for ownership, testimony, and guardianship; the establishment of such a capacity is inconceivable in some undivided part without the other. So just as they agreed on the non-divisibility of 'itq and riqq, they agreed on the divisibility of absolute ownership for disposition, because a man selling his slave to two persons is permissible by consensus, and each is established ownership of half; similarly, if he sells half his slave, his ownership remains in the other half by consensus. However, they differed regarding the divisibility of i'taq, which is the act of the emancipator. The summary is that according to Muhammad and Abu Yusuf, divisibility of i'taq is not permissible, meaning that freeing part frees all, because 'itq is a necessary consequence of i'taq, since it is its effect, and since it is agreed that 'itq is non-divisible, i'taq is also non-divisible. Abu Hanifa said: i'taq is divisible, and it does not necessitate the divisibility of 'itq, so that if he frees a share of his slave, neither the whole nor a part is freed; rather, he remains a slave in testimony and all rulings, but he removes ownership in what he freed, because the effect of i'taq is removal of ownership, which is divisible. So the ruling of 'itq is suspended until he performs sa'yah (earning to pay the value); if he pays, ownership is completely removed, and he becomes entirely free.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and elaboration of riqq (slavery) as a legal impediment. The author begins by defining riqq as a legal incapacity (ajz hukmi) — a legally imposed disability, not a physical one. Originally, it was legislated as a punishment for disbelief, but once established, it becomes a purely legal status that persists even if the slave embraces Islam. The key attribute emphasized is that riqq is indivisible (la yahtamil al-tajazzi): one cannot be half-slave and half-free in terms of legal personality. This indivisibility applies to both riqq and its opposite 'itq (emancipation). To prove this, the author cites a case from al-Jami' al-Kabir: a person of unknown lineage who acknowledges that half of him is a slave of someone; the ruling is that he is considered a full slave in all legal matters, including testimony. If riqq were divisible, his testimony would be like that of a free person when combined with another half, but it is not. The passage then distinguishes ownership (milk), which is divisible by consensus — e.g., selling half a slave is valid. The main dispute arises regarding the act of emancipation (i'taq). Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that since 'itq (the effect) is indivisible, the act (i'taq) must also be indivisible, so freeing part frees the whole. Abu Hanifa argues that i'taq is merely removal of ownership, which is divisible; thus freeing a share only removes ownership from that share, and full freedom is postponed until the slave performs sa'yah to compensate the owner.

The Hanafi Principle of Tanṣīf: Halving Rulings Due to Deficiency in Ownership

Core Idea

This passage from Muntakhab al-Husami explains a core Hanafi usulī principle: the halving (tanṣīf) of certain legal rulings for slaves and women is rooted in the degree of their legal capacity (malikiyya). This passage is a commentary explaining the principle of tanṣīf (halving) of certain legal rulings for slaves and women, based on the Hanafi classification of ownership (mālikiyya) into two types: ownership of wealth (mālikiyyat al-māl) and ownership of enjoyment (mālikiyyat al-mut'a) such as marriage. Ignorance as a Valid Excuse (Udhur) in Hanafi Usul
Explaining when ignorance excuses from liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for ignorance as a valid excuse in Hanafi usul and apply the criterion of obscurity of evidence to determine liability.

Core Idea
Ignorance excuses when the evidence is obscure and the person is not negligent.

In Hanafi usul, not all ignorance is equal. This lesson explores the fourth category of acquired impediments: ignorance that qualifies as a valid excuse (udhur). The central idea is that when legal evidence is genuinely hidden or inaccessible, and the person bears no fault for ignorance, the law excuses them from liability. We will examine several concrete examples from the text, including a new Muslim in enemy territory, an unaware agent, and a pre-emptor who learns of a sale late.

Guided Path

Identify the condition for udhur
The text states that ignorance is an excuse when the person is not negligent (muqassir) due to obscurity of evidence (khafa' al-dalil). Look for those two elements in each example.

A person converts to Islam in enemy territory and does not migrate; he misses prayers and fasting. The Hanafi ruling: no make-up required because the evidence is not widespread there.
This is the clearest case of khafa' al-dalil.
3
Extend to agency and pre-emption
An agent or authorized slave who acts before learning of his appointment is not bound; the pre-emptor who discovers a sale later keeps his right. The common cause: the information was not readily available to them.
Prevents hardship (darar).

Examine the hadith proof
The text cites the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father' to create a shubha in the case of a father's intercourse with his son's slave-girl, excusing him from hadd. This shows how a legal text can cause obscurity even if the person knows the literal prohibition.
Shubha fi al-mahall due to a conflicting dalil.
5
Synthesize the principle
All examples share: genuine obscurity of evidence + non-negligence → no liability. The mufta bihi is that such ignorance excuses both in acts of worship and in contractual/penal matters.
Contrast with clear evidence: if the person should have known, no excuse.

Excusable Ignorance (al-Jahl al-'Udhr) in Hanafi Usul
Defines excusable ignorance with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the conditions for excusable ignorance (al-jahl al-'udhr) and apply the distinction between khafa' al-dalil and shubha al-dalil.

Core Idea
Ignorance excuses only when evidence is hidden and the person is not negligent.

This passage introduces a key concept in Hanafi usul: ignorance can be a valid legal excuse if it arises from hidden evidence (khafa' al-dalil). It distinguishes such excusable ignorance from mere negligence. By understanding this, you will see how Hanafi jurists protect those who could not have known the law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of al-jahl al-'udhr
Extract the two conditions: the person is not negligent (ghayr muqassir) and the evidence is concealed (khafa' al-dalil).
Non-negligence is key—if the person could have easily learned the ruling, ignorance is not excused.
2
Examine the paradigm case: a new Muslim in dar al-harb
Such a person is excused for missed prayers and fasts because Islamic rulings did not reach him. The cause is that dar al-harb is not a place where Islamic law is publicized.
This shows how the legal cause (illa) of khafa' al-dalil operates.
3
Trace the underlying reasoning (ta'lil): khafa' al-dalil
The text states the rationale explicitly: 'due to concealment of evidence' (li-khafa' al-dalil). Understand that this refers to actual non-receipt (bil-sama') and constructive non-receipt (bi al-istifada wa al-shuhra).
Constructive receipt occurs when news spreads widely; if it does, ignorance is not excused.
4
Apply to other cases: agent, pre-emptor, master
The same illa (khafa' al-dalil) is extended to an agent who does not know of his appointment, a pre-emptor who does not know of a sale, and a master who frees a slave before learning of the slave's crime.
In each, the person is unaware due to lack of normal channels of information, so liability is suspended.
5
Distinguish from shubha al-dalil
Shubha al-dalil occurs when a legal proof exists that negates the ruling, but the ruling is not applied due to an obstacle. Example: a father who sleeps with his son's slave-girl is not punished because of the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father.'
This is different from excusable ignorance; here the person might know it is forbidden, but the evidence creates a doubt that prevents the hadd penalty.

Commentary
This passage defines the fourth type of acquired impediment: ignorance that is a valid excuse. The author first states the general ruling: ignorance is an excuse when the person is not negligent because the evidence is hidden (khafāʼ al-dalīl). He gives the paradigm case of a person who accepts Islam in dār al-ḥarb (non-Muslim territory) and does not emigrate; such a person is excused for not performing prayers, fast, etc., because the laws of Islam have not reached him. The reasoning is that dār al-ḥarb is not a place where Islamic rulings are publicized. The same principle applies to an agent (wakīl) who does not know he has been appointed: his actions before knowledge are not binding on the principal, and he is not liable for damage. Similarly, a pre-emptor (shafīʻ) who does not know of a sale retains his right of pre-emption even after a delay. A master who frees a slave before learning of the slave's crime is not liable for the full ransom but the lesser of value or damages. For a virgin given in marriage, her silence before knowledge is not consent. A married slave-woman who does not know she has been emancipated retains her option to annul the marriage. However, ignorance of the option of puberty (khiyār al-bulūgh) is not an excuse because such knowledge is widespread in dār al-islām and the person is free and able to learn. The passage also discusses a related type: shubha al-dalīl (doubt based on evidence), where a legal proof exists that negates the ruling but the ruling is not applied due to a preventing factor. Example: a father who has intercourse with his son's slave-girl; he is not subject to the ḥadd punishment even if he knew it was forbidden, because of the hadith 'You and your property belong to your father', which serves as a legal indicator (shubha) that prevents the penalty.

Key Terms
الجهل العذر (al-jahl al-ʻudhr): Ignorance that excuses the ignorant person from liability because the evidence is hidden and the person is not negligent.
خفاء الدليل (khafāʼ al-dalīl): Concealment of the legal evidence such that it does not reach the person through normal channels.

Types of Coercion (Ikrah) and Their Effects: Definition and Application
Defining and distinguishing complete and deficient coercion, their effects on choice and consent

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between complete and deficient coercion and apply the Hanafi rule on consent and choice to example scenarios.

Core Idea
Coercion is of two types: complete (voids choice) and deficient (voids consent only) — their effects differ.

In this passage, the Hanafi usuli author defines coercion and divides it into two categories. Understanding this distinction is key to evaluating the validity of actions performed under duress. The text then applies these categories to the case of breaking a fast, showing how the doubt created by travel affects the obligation of kaffarah.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of coercion
From the definition, name the two types: complete (ikrāh kāmil) and deficient (ikrāh qāṣir). Note that complete coercion corrupts choice and necessitates compulsion, while deficient coercion only removes consent.
Complete coercion involves threats of death or limb loss; deficient involves lesser threats like beating.
2
Analyze the effects on choice and consent
Map the effects: complete coercion corrupts both choice (ikhtiyār) and consent (riḍāʾ); deficient coercion removes consent only, leaving choice partially intact.
This distinction determines whether an act under coercion is void (no legal effect) or valid but subject to options.
3
Trace the application to travel vs. illness
Travel is a chosen act (ikhtiyārī), so the traveler cannot break his fast after starting without a necessity. Illness is involuntary, so the sick person may break. This illustrates how the classification of an act as chosen or coerced affects the ruling.
The text uses this contrast to emphasize that coercion (from an external agent) similarly affects consent and choice.
4
Apply to kaffarah scenarios
Analyze the three scenarios: (1) traveler starts and breaks, (2) resident starts, travels, then breaks – no kaffarah due to shubhah (doubt) from travel; (3) resident starts and breaks before traveling – kaffarah is due because no shubhah. Understand that the doubt acts like a deficient coercion, removing the full legal blame.
The presence of shubhah (doubt) negates the obligation of kaffarah, similar to how complete coercion negates liability.
5
Formulate the underlying principle
The principle: kaffarah is only due for breaking a wajib fast without a legally recognized doubt or coercion. This connects back to the definition: when choice is compromised (by coercion or doubt), the strict ruling is lifted.
This is an example of how usul al-fiqh provides principles to derive fiqh rulings.

Commentary
This passage is a definition and classification of ikrah (coercion). It begins the chapter of Qiyas (analogy) but immediately discusses types of coercion as part of the second section of acquired accidental qualities (al-‘awāriḍ al-muktasabah). Coercion is divided into two types: (1) Complete coercion (ikrāh kāmil): it corrupts choice (ikhtiyār) and necessitates compulsion (iljā'), such as a threat of death or loss of limb. This eliminates both consent and choice. (2) Deficient coercion (ikrāh qāṣir): it removes consent (riḍā') but does not remove choice; the person still has some ability to choose, though under pressure. An example is beating without fear of death. The text then revisits the distinction between travel and illness: travel is a chosen act, so the traveler cannot break his fast after starting, unlike the sick person whose illness is involuntary. Then it details three scenarios regarding kaffarah for breaking fast: (1) traveler starts fast and breaks – no kaffarah (doubt due to travel). (2) resident starts fast, travels, then breaks – no kaffarah. (3) resident starts fast and breaks before traveling – kaffarah is obligatory. This shows how the doubt (shubhah) created by travel prevents kaffarah. Finally, the author defines coercion technically as one person forcing another by threat of harm.

الإلجاء (al-iljā'): Compulsion: forcing someone to act with no possibility of refusal.

The Wāw Indicates Absolute Conjunction, Not Order
Defining and proving the Hanafi position on the wāw of conjunction, with intra-school and inter-school disputes.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the meaning of the wāw of conjunction, apply it to Quranic verses and conditional divorces, and evaluate the dispute with the Shafi'i school regarding its implication of order.

Core Idea
The wāw of conjunction indicates mere sharing in a ruling, not order or simultaneity.

This passage from Muntakhab al-Ḥusāmī delves into one of the most important particles in Arabic for usul al-fiqh: the wāw of conjunction. Understanding its default meaning is crucial because many fiqh rulings—especially in oaths, divorces, and acts of worship—depend on whether wāw implies order or mere conjunction. The Hanafi school, backed by majority linguists and jurists, holds that wāw indicates absolute conjunction without ordering. In this lesson, you will examine the proofs and the practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the default meaning of wāw according to Hanafi usul.
According to the text, the wāw is for absolute conjunction (mujarrad al-jam‘) without indicating simultaneity or order. This is the view of the majority of linguists and the imams of fatwa.
2
Analyze the Hanafi proofs against wāw implying order.
The passage presents Quranic evidence (2:58, 3:43) and a linguistic proof from 'Zayd and Amr met' to show that if wāw implied order, logical contradictions would arise. Study each proof.
Focus on why the order of entering and saying in 2:58 cannot be fixed, and why prostration before bowing in 3:43 is impossible.
3
Examine the Shafi'i counter-argument and the Hanafi response.
Some Shafi'is cite the hadith 'Begin with what Allah began with' and verse 22:77 to claim order. The Hanafis respond that the Prophet's order came from independent revelation and that verse 3:43 contradicts the claim. Understand the rebuttal.
The Hanafi response uses qiyās awlā: if wāw meant order, then 3:43 would demand the impossible, so it cannot mean order.
4
Apply the dispute to a conditional divorce case.
The case: 'If I marry her, she is divorced, and divorced, and divorced.' Abu Hanifa rules one divorce; his two disciples rule three. The passage explains that the divergence stems from whether the wāw makes all three share equally in the condition (disciples' view) or whether the grammatical structure imposes sequentiality (Abu Hanifa's view).
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf that shows the practical thamarah al-khilaf of the wāw analysis.

Section on Particles of Meaning. Conjunction Particles. A portion of fiqh issues is built upon them. The most frequently occurring are the conjunction particles, and the foundational one among them is wāw. It is for absolute conjunction according to us, without indicating simultaneity or order, and this is the view of the majority of linguists and the imams of fatwa. Order is only established in his statement: 'If I marry her, she is divorced, and divorced, and divorced,' regarding a non-mahram woman, so that only one divorce occurs according to Abu Hanifa, contrary to his two companions, necessarily because the second [divorce] is connected to the condition through the medium of the first, not due to the wāw. And in the statement of a master: 'I have freed this one and this one.' It is responded to the first: perhaps order was established with him (peace be upon him) from unrevealed inspiration, or reference to the verse considering that precedence in mention indicates importance and preference. And regarding the second: it is contradicted by the Almighty's statement: 'And prostrate and bow' (Quran 3:43), for precedence of prostration over bowing is contrary to consensus. The imams of fatwa also argued that if wāw indicated order, inconsistencies would follow, including contradiction between the Almighty's statements: 'Enter the gate prostrating' (Quran 2:58) and 'And say: Hittah' (Quran 2:58), with another verse: 'And say: Hittah, and enter the gate prostrating' (Quran 2:58), while the story is one, and contradiction in His speech is impossible. And also that our saying: 'Zayd and Amr met' would not be valid, because meeting necessitates simultaneity, which is inconceivable with order, yet it is valid by agreement. And since it can be objected that if someone says to a non-mahram: 'If I marry her, she is divorced, and divorced' as if he said to a woman not yet touched: 'You are divorced, and divorced,' then according to Abu Hanifa one divorce occurs, and according to the two companions three. So it is known that according to him wāw indicates order, so the first occurs alone, and no room remains for the second and third; and according to them it indicates simultaneity since all occur at once. He refutes this by saying: 'Order is only established, etc.' [This means] there is no implication from wāw. The summary of the response is that order according to him is not established due to wāw as you claim, but due to the speech itself and its necessity: his statement 'If I marry her, she is divorced' is a complete sentence that does not need what follows it. As for his statement 'and divorced and divorced,' it is an incomplete sentence, so it necessarily depends on the first sentence, because the incomplete is in need of the complete to convey meaning; if not for the conjunction, the incomplete would convey nothing. So when it is conjoined to his statement 'she is divorced,' it is connected to the condition—his saying 'if I marry her'—through a medium. Thus the first is connected to the condition directly, the second through one medium, and the third through two media in order. When the condition occurs, they descend in the previous order: the first occurs first, then the second. When the first occurs, no room remains for the second and third because the woman is not [yet] consummated, so only one divorce takes place. This is the response from his side. As for from their side, the import of the speech is togetherness and sharing—i.e., the conjoined and the base share in the condition—so the second and third equal the first in being conditioned by the condition directly, as if he repeated the condition saying: 'If I marry her, she is divorced, and if I marry her, she is divorced, and if I marry her, she is divorced.' So when the condition occurs, they all fall together. All this is if the condition is mentioned first. If he delays it, saying: 'She is divorced and divorced if I marry her,' then three occur by agreement, because something appears at the end of the speech that changes its beginning, so they all become connected to the condition together; thus according to him, when the condition occurs, they fall as a group.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed Hanafi analysis of the conjunction particle wāw (و). The author states at the outset that the chapter on Huruf al-Ma'ani (Particles of Meaning) is included because many fiqh rulings depend on these particles, especially the conjunctions. The position of the Hanafi school, along with the majority of linguists and jurists, is that wāw indicates absolute conjunction (mujarrad al-jam‘) without signifying either simultaneity (muqārana) or order (tartīb). This is the default meaning. The author then presents several proofs. First, the Shafi'i argument that wāw indicates order, based on the hadith 'Begin with what Allah began with' and the verse 'Perform rukū' and sujūd' (22:77), is refuted. The response: the Prophet's order may have come from divine inspiration beyond the Quran, and the precedence of rukū' over sujūd is derived from other evidence, not from the wāw itself. Moreover, the verse 'Prostrate and bow' (3:43) would contradict if wāw implied order, since prostration before bowing is impossible in prayer. Second, internal contradictions in the Quran would arise if wāw implied order, as in the two verses about entering the gate (2:58). Third, the validity of the phrase 'Zayd and Amr met' (which requires simultaneity) shows wāw does not imply order. The passage then delves into a fiqh example: 'If I marry her, she is divorced, and divorced, and divorced' spoken to a non-mahram woman. Abu Hanifa rules that only one divorce occurs, while his two disciples (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) say three. The author explains that the disciples' view does not come from wāw itself, but from the structure of the conditional sentence. According to Abu Hanifa, the first 'divorced' is a complete statement tied directly to the condition; the second and third are incomplete and depend on the first, so they take effect sequentially. Once the first divorce occurs, the woman becomes non-mahram, so the latter two cannot take effect. According to the disciples, the wāw makes all three share equally in the condition, so they all fall simultaneously. The passage also notes that if the condition is delayed (e.g., 'she is divorced and divorced if I marry her'), all three occur by consensus, because the end of the speech retroactively establishes the condition for all. This passage demonstrates how usul al-fiqh analyses of particles directly impact concrete fiqh rulings.

Key Terms
الواو (al-wāw): حرف عطف يدل على مطلق الجمع من غير تعرض لمقارنة ولا ترتيب (a conjunction particle indicating absolute conjunction without implying simultaneity or order)
حروف العطف (ḥurūf al-‘aṭf): الحروف التي تدخل على الأسماء والأفعال للربط بين المعطوف والمعطوف عليه (particles that enter upon nouns and verbs to link the conjoined to the base)
لمطلق الجمع (li-muṭlaq al-jam‘): مجرد الاشتراك في الحكم بدون زيادة (mere sharing in the ruling without additional qualification)

The Hanafi Dispute on Waw: Does It Imply Order or Concurrence?
Analyzing the Hanafi dispute on waw's implication in conditional sentences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the Hanafi position on waw from the Sahibayn's position and derive the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf) in conditional divorce cases.

Core Idea
Waw does not inherently imply order or simultaneity; any sequence arises from the structure of speech, not from the conjunction itself.

This passage tackles a foundational usul dispute among Hanafi masters: does the Arabic letter waw (و) indicate sequential order (tartib) or mere conjunction without sequence? Imam Abu Hanifa argues that waw is a neutral connector; any perceived order stems from sentence dependency. His companions, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, hold that waw can imply order in specific contexts. We'll dissect their arguments and see how this dispute impacts actual fiqh rulings on conditional divorce.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
What exactly is contested? The implication of the conjunction waw in conditional sentences: does it impose order (tartib) or merely join without order?
2
State Abu Hanifa's position and his evidence
Abu Hanifa holds that waw does not imply order. He cites Quranic verses (22:77 and 3:43) where bowing and prostrating are mentioned in different orders, proving waw allows reversal. Also, the phrase 'Zaid and Amr met' is valid despite requiring simultaneity, showing waw does not enforce sequence.
3
State the Sahibayn's position and their evidence
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani hold that waw can imply order or simultaneity, depending on context. In the conditional divorce case, they argue that waw makes each 'divorced' independent, so all three occur simultaneously.
4
Analyze the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: In a conditional divorce like 'If I marry you, you are divorced, and divorced, and divorced,' Abu Hanifa rules one divorce occurs (since the woman becomes non-virgin, preventing subsequent divorces), while the Sahibayn rule three occur. This affects remarriage possibilities.

The Waw Coordinating Complete Sentences: Independence in Predication
Establishing the rule when waw coordinates two complete sentences on legal speech

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that when waw joins two complete sentences, the second sentence does not inherit the predicate of the first, and identify whether a given coordinated sentence is complete or incomplete.

Core Idea
A complete sentence with its own predicate does not borrow another predicate via waw.

This lesson clarifies a key rule in Hanafi usul al-fiqh regarding the conjunction waw. When two sentences are both complete—each having its own subject and predicate—the waw does not force the second to share the predicate of the first. This principle has direct consequences in legal statements, particularly in divorce pronouncements, where the number of divorces depends on whether the second clause is independent or dependent.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two coordinated sentences
Look at the phrase introduced by waw. Determine whether the second part has its own predicate or is a fragment that must borrow the predicate from the first.
A complete sentence has both a subject and a predicate (e.g., 'this is divorced'). An incomplete sentence lacks a predicate (e.g., 'and this' without a following verb).
2
Apply the rule: complete sentences do not share the predicate
If the second sentence is complete, the waw does not import the predicate of the first. The second sentence stands alone with its own meaning.
The evidence for sharing is the need (iftiqar) of an incomplete sentence. When the need is absent, sharing is not required.
3
Test with a divorce example
Consider the statement 'This is divorced three times and this is divorced.' The second sentence 'this is divorced' is complete; it does not borrow 'three times' from the first. Therefore the second wife receives only one divorce.
4
Extend to conditional statements
In 'If you enter the house, you are divorced and divorced,' the second 'divorced' is attached to the same condition (entering the house) without needing a new condition. This is because the condition is shared, not the predicate.
The waw of initiation (waw al-ibtida') introduces an independent sentence, but the condition remains the same.

A complete sentence stands alone; waw only forces predicate sharing when the second sentence is incomplete.

The Waw for Circumstantial Clause and Its Legal Effect
Explanation of waw for ḥāl with Quranic and legal examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when the waw indicates a circumstantial clause (ḥāl) and apply the Hanafi principle of linguistic inversion (qalb) to derive the correct legal ruling.

Core Idea
The waw in 'Pay me one thousand and you are free' creates a condition, not a conjunction—freedom requires payment first.

This lesson explores a key interpretive tool in Hanafi usul: the recognition of waw as a marker of a circumstantial clause (ḥāl) rather than a simple connector. Understanding this distinction is crucial when analyzing legal statements involving manumission, safe conduct, or other conditional promises. The Hanafi method employs linguistic inversion (qalb) to resolve apparent logical contradictions, ensuring that the intended conditionality is preserved.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic structure
Spot the waw followed by a nominal sentence (e.g., 'wa-anta ḥurr'). Determine whether it appears to be a simple conjunction or a description of a concurrent state.
In 'Pay me one thousand and you are free', the phrase 'and you are free' is a nominal sentence.
2
Classify the waw: conjunctive or circumstantial?
Apply the Hanafi principle: if a literal conjunction leads to a logical or legal absurdity (e.g., a slave must pay while still a slave), the waw is for ḥāl, indicating the circumstance of the action.
The absurdity here is that the slave cannot pay as a slave if freedom is immediate.
3
Handle the objection using qalb (inversion)
The objection states that ḥāl is like a condition, so payment would depend on freedom, contradicting the intended meaning. Respond by inverting the word order: 'Be free while paying' becomes the intended reading.
Qalb is a recognized linguistic tool in Hanafi usul to rearrange terms for correct interpretation.
4
Derive the legal ruling
Conclude that the act (payment or descent) is a condition for the outcome (freedom or safety). The slave becomes free only after payment; the enemy becomes safe only after descending.
This ruling affects contracts, oaths, and conditional statements in daily legal practice.
5
Apply to other similar texts
Use the same method for any statement where waw precedes a result clause. Test for absurdity and apply qalb if needed.
Example: 'Sell this and you are pardoned' – the pardon is conditional on the sale.

The Conjunction 'Bal' in Insha' and Its Effect on Conditional Divorces
Analyzing the conjunction بل and its impact on conditional divorces

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the legal effect of 'bal' (بل) in performative utterances (insha') versus reports (khabar), and apply the Hanafi ruling that in conditional divorces 'bal' combines both divorces without order, while 'waw' may limit the effect according to Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
In performative utterances, 'bal' does not revoke the first statement but combines both; only in reports does it correct by canceling the first.

The conjunction 'bal' (بل) is often used in Arabic to correct or turn away from a previous statement. However, in Islamic legal theory (usul al-fiqh), its effect depends on the type of speech: reports (akhbar) allow correction, but performative utterances (insha') like divorce do not. This lesson explores how 'bal' functions in a conditional divorce and contrasts it with the conjunction 'waw', where a dispute between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn emerges.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of speech
Distinguish between khabar (reports) and insha' (performative utterances). Recognize that khabar can be true or false and can be corrected, while insha' creates legal effects instantly and cannot be revoked.
This distinction is foundational for understanding the effect of 'bal'.
2
Analyze the effect of 'bal' in khabar
In khabar, 'bal' indicates turning away (i'rad) from the first statement, invalidating it and affirming the second. This is only possible because khabar admits correction.
Example: 'ما جاءني زيد بل عمرو' – 'Zayd did not come to me, but 'Amr did.'
3
Apply the same to insha'
In insha', 'bal' cannot revoke the first statement because performatives do not admit correction. Therefore, 'bal' functions as a pure conjunction (عطف محض), combining both parts without order.
This is the madhhab of the muhaqqiqin.
4
Apply to a conditional divorce scenario
If a husband says before consummation: 'If you enter the house, you are divorced once, bal twice,' both statements take effect together upon condition fulfillment, resulting in three divorces.
The condition is applied once, and both numbers combine.
5
Contrast with conjunction using 'waw'
When using 'waw' (e.g., 'once and twice'), Abu Hanifa holds that only one divorce occurs because the second is attached via the first as an intermediary. The first occurs first, and since she is non-ma'tua, the place (mahall) for further divorces is lost. The Sahibayn disagree, holding that all occur together.
Note the practical difference: with 'bal', always three; with 'waw', only one according to Abu Hanifa before consummation.

The Conjunction 'Lakinna' for Istidrak After Negation
Explaining the rules of 'lakinna' for correction after negation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that 'lakinna' after negation functions as a conjunction only when the speech is coherent (connected and with different referents for negation and affirmation).

Core Idea
'Lakinna' after negation: conjunction if speech is coherent, else a new statement.

This lesson explains the particle 'lakinna' used for istidrak (correction) after a negated statement. In Hanafi usul, the ruling depends on the coherence of the speech: whether the parts are connected and whether the negation applies to one thing and the affirmation to another. Two examples from fiqh illustrate the difference: an acknowledgement about a slave and a marriage contract.

Guided Path
1
Identify the particle and context
Recognize that 'lakinna' appears after a negated statement to correct a misunderstanding (istidrak). The passage focuses on its function after negation.
The particle can be lightened or emphasized without changing the rule.
2
Understand the condition for conjunction
For 'lakinna' to be a conjunction (عاطفة), the speech must be coherent (اتساق الكلام). This requires two sub-conditions: (1) the part after 'lakinna' must be connected to the preceding part, not separated; (2) the negation must refer to one thing and the affirmation to a different thing, so that the end does not contradict the beginning.
Coherence ensures that 'lakinna' functions as an effective correction.
3
Apply to the first example: acknowledgement of a slave
In the scenario, the acknowledgee says: 'It was never mine, but it is so-and-so's.' The negation applies to himself, and the affirmation to another person. The statements are connected. Thus, coherence is achieved, and 'lakinna' is conjunctive, transferring ownership.
The second person deserves the slave because the correction is valid.
4
Apply to the second example: marriage contract
The woman says: 'I do not accept it, but I accept it for one hundred and fifty.' Here, she negates the marriage itself and then affirms the same marriage with a different dowry. The negation and affirmation refer to the same action (the marriage), so the end contradicts the beginning. Coherence fails, and 'lakinna' is isti'nafi (independent), not conjunctive.
The correct formulation would be to negate the restrictive condition, not the action itself.
5
Conclude the rule
If the two conditions are met, 'lakinna' is a conjunction; otherwise it begins a new independent statement. The Hanafi school applies this linguistic principle consistently in legal texts.
This rule governs interpretation of contracts, acknowledgements, and other legal speech.

Conditions for 'Lakinna' as a Conjunction
Conditions for 'lakinna' as a true conjunction

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the condition of coherence (ittisāq) to determine whether 'lakinna' functions as a conjunction or a new beginning in a given legal statement.

Core Idea
Lakinna is a conjunction only when speech is coherent: negation on one thing, affirmation on another.

The particle 'lakinna' (but) is a common conjunction in Arabic legal texts. However, not every occurrence of 'lakinna' after a negation creates a true conjunction. Hanafi jurists and usulis have laid down a precise condition: coherence (ittisāq) between the two parts. This lesson unpacks the two sub-conditions of coherence and illustrates them with classical examples from acknowledgment and marriage cases.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the two conditions for coherence
The passage states that coherence requires (1) the part after 'lakinna' to directly correct the part before, with connected speech, and (2) the negation refers to one subject and the affirmation to a different subject so that no contradiction arises.
If either condition is missing, 'lakinna' is not a conjunction but a new beginning (istifna').
2
Apply to the first example: acknowledgment of slave
In the example 'It has never been mine but it is so-and-so's', the negation refers to self, affirmation to another. Speech is connected, so coherence holds and 'lakinna' is a conjunction.
The second person deserves the slave because negation is transferred to affirmation.
3
Apply to the second example: marriage rejection
The woman says 'I do not accept it but I accept it with one hundred and fifty'. Here negation and affirmation both apply to the same act (acceptance), so coherence fails. 'Lakinna' becomes a new beginning, and the marriage is dissolved.
The correct coherent version: 'I do not accept it with one hundred but I accept it with one hundred and fifty'.
4
Analyze the rebuttal of Ghāyat al-Taḥqīq
The author of Ghāyat al-Taḥqīq argued that even the corrected version lacks coherence because it still negates and affirms the same act. The passage refutes this by noting that in restricted speech, negation applies only to the restriction, not the act itself.
Thus, 'I do not accept it with one hundred' negates the restriction of one hundred, not the acceptance.
5
Summarize the rule for 'lakinna' in legal texts
When encountering 'lakinna' after a negation in a legal statement, check coherence: if the negation and affirmation refer to different aspects (different subjects or different restrictions), then 'lakinna' is a conjunction joining the second part to the first in the ruling. Otherwise, treat it as a new sentence.
This rule prevents misinterpretation of contracts, acknowledgments, and rulings.

الاستدراك. اتساق الكلام والمراد بالاتساق أن يصح ما بعد "لكن" تداركاً لما قبلها، بأن يكون الكلام متصلا بعضه ببعضه غير منفصل ليتحقق العطف، وأن يكون النفي راجعاً إلى شيء، والإثبات إلى شيء آخر ليمكن Chapter on Particles of Meaning - Conjunction Particles. 'Lakinna' (but) for 'Amr'; however, conjunction with it is only valid when the speech is coherent. When the speech is coherent, as in the one affirmed regarding the slave: 'It has never been mine but it is so-and-so's', the negation attaches to the affirmation so that the second person deserves it. Otherwise, it is a new beginning, like the married woman with one hundred saying: 'I do not accept it but I accept it with one hundred and fifty', then the contract is dissolved because it is negation of an act and affirmation of the same act, so the speech is not coherent. This statement... between them, so you correct by saying 'but 'Amr' or restrict the correction after negation in conjoining a single to a single. If the speech has two sentences differing in negation and affirmation, correction with 'lakinna' is permissible in both affirmation and negation like 'bal'. Whether it is lightened, it is a conjunction, and if it is emphasized, it resembles a verb sharing in correction with the conjunction. Coherence of speech: what is meant by coherence is that what comes after 'lakinna' is a correction of what came before, so that the speech is connected part to part not separated to achieve conjunction, and that the negation refers to one thing and the affirmation to another so that both can be combined, and the end of the speech does not contradict its beginning. If one of the two conditions is missing, coherence is not achieved, so correction is not valid; then the speech is a new beginning, not conjoined. Like the one affirmed for him: i.e., like the coherence of the speech of the one affirmed for him. The picture of the issue is that a man has a slave in his hand, so he acknowledged the slave, i.e., that slave belongs to so-and-so, then the one affirmed for him said: 'It has never been mine...' to another so-and-so. When he connects his statement 'to another so-and-so' with his statement 'It has never been mine', the negation - his statement 'It has never been mine' - attaches to the affirmation mentioned after 'lakinna', which is his statement 'to another so-and-so', so coherence is achieved in two senses. As for connection, because his statement 'but it is for another so-and-so' came connected to his statement 'It has never been mine'. Indeed, if it had not come connected, it would be possible that his statement 'It has never been mine' was a denial from himself originally and a rejection of his acknowledgement. When it was connected by 'but it is for another so-and-so', it is known that he intended transfer from himself to another man. He deserves it etc. As for the consideration that the negation refers to one thing and the affirmation to another, it is clear; because he negated ownership from himself and affirmed it for another where he said: 'but it is for another so-and-so'. Otherwise: i.e., if coherence is not found due to loss of one of the two meanings. Like the married woman with one hundred: i.e., like the speech of the woman who was married by a guardian to a man with a dowry of one hundred, then she said after... so the speech was not coherent due to loss of one of the two meanings, which is that the negation refers to one thing and the affirmation to another; because when she said: 'I do not accept it', she uprooted the marriage from its origin until no aspect of its validity remained, then she said afterwards: 'but I accept it with fifty', it necessarily follows that it is an affirmation of that very negated act, and the dowry in marriage is subsidiary, not considered, so the beginning of the speech contradicts its end. So we consider it as the beginning of speech, so 'lakinna' is for beginning, not conjunction. The speech would be coherent only if she said: 'I do not accept it with one hundred but I accept it with one hundred and fifty' so that the correction is in the amount of the dowry, not in the basis of the marriage, so the negation refers to the restriction of one hundred, and the affirmation to the restriction of one hundred and fifty; due to what is established among them that negation in restricted speech refers only to the restriction, thus what the author of 'Ghāyat al-Taḥqīq' said is refuted: that in that case also the speech is not coherent because it is negation of an act and affirmation of the same act, so reflect.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of the conjunction particle 'lakinna' (but). It explains the condition for 'lakinna' to function as a true conjunction (عطف), which requires coherence (اتساق) in the speech. Coherence has two conditions: (1) The speech must be connected such that the part after 'lakinna' directly corrects the part before, and (2) the negation must refer to one subject and the affirmation to a different subject, so that both can be true without contradiction. If either condition is missing, 'lakinna' is not a conjunction but indicates a new beginning (استئناف). The passage provides two examples: first, a man acknowledges a slave belongs to someone else; his words 'it has never been mine but it is for so-and-so' are coherent because negation applies to himself and affirmation to another. Second, a married woman says 'I do not accept the marriage but I accept it with one hundred and fifty' – this is incoherent because she negates and affirms the same act (the marriage itself), so 'lakinna' is not a conjunction and the marriage is dissolved. The correct way would be to say 'I do not accept it with one hundred but I accept it with one hundred and fifty', where negation and affirmation apply to different restrictions. The passage also rebuts an objection from the author of 'Ghāyat al-Taḥqīq' by clarifying that negation in restricted speech applies only to the restriction.
