Abandoned Literal Meaning (al-Mahjurah): When Custom Overrides Literal Sense
Defining the concept of abandoned literal meaning with examples and hadith proof.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a literal meaning is considered mahjurah and apply the Hanafi rule that the customary metaphorical meaning takes its place in legal rulings.

Core Idea
When a word's literal sense is abandoned in custom or law, the metaphorical usage becomes legally operative.

This passage introduces the Hanafi principle of al-mahjurah (abandoned literal meaning). It shows that when common usage or Islamic law no longer employs a term in its literal sense, the metaphorical or customary meaning becomes the basis for legal rulings. The examples of oaths and agency for litigation illustrate how this principle works in practice, and a hadith strengthens the argument regarding the legal abandonment of avoiding children.

Guided Path
1
Identify the concept and its conditions
Define al-mahjurah as a literal meaning that has fallen out of use in custom or law. Note that the meaning is not abrogated but simply no longer operative.
Distinguish from naskh.
2
Analyze the first example (oath)
Consider the oath 'I will not place my foot in this house.' Explain why the literal act of placing the foot is abandoned and why 'entering' becomes the effective meaning.
The Hanafi rule: yahinthu kayfa dakhala.
3
Analyze the second example (agency)
For agency in litigation, explain why the agent's authority covers denial and confession, even though the literal meaning of 'response' is specific.
The literal meaning is abandoned legally because the agent's role is to respond on behalf of the principal.
4
Examine the hadith proof
The hadith 'He is not of us who does not have mercy on our young' shows that avoiding children is legally disapproved. Therefore, an oath 'I will not speak to this child' applies at all times, not just childhood.
The hadith provides a legal basis for the abandonment.
5
Apply the principle to new cases
Given the rule that legally abandoned meanings are like customarily abandoned ones, think of a new scenario where a literal meaning is no longer used in Islamic law and determine the operative meaning.
For example, the word 'salah' originally means supplication but is now used exclusively for the ritual prayer.

Commentary
This passage defines 'al-mahjurah' (the abandoned literal meaning). When the literal sense is no longer used in custom or law, the metaphorical (customary) meaning takes effect. Examples: an oath not to put one's foot in a house—the literal act of placing the foot is abandoned; the customary meaning is entering, so any entry breaks the oath. Similarly, in legal agency for litigation, the agent's authority covers all responses (denial or confession) metaphorically, not just the literal act of speech. The principle is that legal abandonment is equivalent to customary abandonment. The hadith about mercy towards children illustrates that avoiding children is legally discouraged, so an oath not to speak to a child applies at all ages, not just childhood.

Key Terms
المهجورة (al-mahjurah): The literal meaning of a word that has been abandoned in common usage or Islamic law
المجاز (al-majaz): The metaphorical meaning that becomes the customary usage
التوكيل بالخصومة (al-tawkīl bi-l-khuṣūmah): Agency for litigation

Jest and Collusion in Contracts: The Hanafi Dispute on Intent
Analyzing the Hanafi dispute on jest and collusion in contracts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the positions of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn regarding jest and collusion, apply the principle 'al-akhir nasikh' to contract scenarios, and evaluate the practical consequences of each view.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, the contract's apparent seriousness overrides prior collusion; the later act abrogates the earlier.

This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul presents a key intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two students regarding the legal effect of jest and collusion on contracts. The discussion revolves around whether custom (al-'ada) or the default seriousness of the contract should govern. We will parse the arguments and apply them to concrete scenarios.

Guided Path
1
State the Positions
Identify the two conflicting views: Abu Hanifa holds that the contract's seriousness (jidd) prevails; the Sahibayn hold that custom (al-'ada) demands giving effect to prior collusion where possible.
Note that both use the same source material but apply different usul principles.
2
Identify the Proofs
Abu Hanifa relies on the principle 'al-akhir nasikh' (the later act abrogates the earlier) and the lack of textual connection of jest. The Sahibayn rely on the validity of custom (urf) as a source for interpreting contracts.
3
Apply to Scenarios
Work through the scenarios: (a) parties agree on seriousness but collude on price with jest; (b) parties build on the collusion; (c) collusion on the entire contract as a cover-up (talji'a). For each, derive the ruling according to both positions.
Pay attention to the distinction between jest in the price and jest in the contract itself.
4
Analyze the Distinction
Understand why the Sahibayn differentiate between jest in the price (possible to give effect to both collusions) and collusion on the contract (impossible because the contract needs a price). This reveals the limits of custom in contract law.

Commentary
This passage addresses a key usul dispute within the Hanafi school regarding the impact of jest (hazl) and collusion (muwada'a) on contracts. The two students of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, hold that when parties are silent or differ on intent, the default is to consider prior collusion (muwada'a) unless explicit text overrides it. Abu Hanifa, however, prioritizes the apparent seriousness of the contract (al-jidd) because jest is not textually connected to the contract; thus, the contract is more deserving of enforcement. He also invokes the principle that the later act (the serious contract) abrogates the earlier (the collusion). The text then applies these principles to several scenarios: when both agree on seriousness but collude on a price with jest, when they agree on no intent or differ, and when they deliberately build on the collusion. Abu Hanifa consistently upholds the seriousness of the contract, while the students give effect to custom (al-'ada) by enforcing the collusion where possible. The passage also touches on the legal effect of the phrase 'as far as I know' (fima a'lam) in rulings and testimony, clarifying that in Abu Yusuf's view, it does not introduce doubt in narration. The discussion concludes with a distinction between jest in the price versus jest in the contract itself, where the impossibility of a sale without a price leads to prioritizing the collusion in the contract. The final sentence signals a new topic on matters that do not admit of reversal.

Key Terms
الهزل (al-hazl): Jest or mockery—a statement made without serious intent to create legal effects.
المواضعة (al-muwada'a): Prior collusion or agreement between parties about a term or condition that contradicts the apparent contract.
الجد (al-jidd): Seriousness—the default legal intent required for a valid contract.
الآخِرُ ناسخ (al-akhir nasikh): A principle meaning that the later act or statement abrogates the earlier one in legal effect.
