Al-Mahkum Bih — The Object of Ruling
intermediate
10 min
The Tripartite Structure of Faith (Aṣl, Mulḥaq, Zawāʾid)
Taxonomy of worship into faith and its branches

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the original (aṣl), annexed (mulḥaq), and additional (zawāʾid) components of faith (īmān) as defined in Hanafi uṣūl, and explain why the original component cannot be dropped even under duress.

Core Idea
Faith has an original core (taṣdīq) that never drops and an annexed element (iqrār) that becomes a pillar for worldly rulings.

This passage from al-Bazdawī’s Uṣūl introduces the fundamental classification of faith (īmān) into three layers: original, annexed, and additional. Understanding this taxonomy is essential for grasping how the Ḥanafī school treats the inviolability of faith under compulsion and the role of verbal confession. We will unpack the text’s key statements and see how they form the basis for legal rulings on apostasy and duress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three categories of faith
Read the opening part of the passage that states: ‘And acts of worship are of two types: belief in Allah and its branches, and they are of three types: original, annexed to it, and additional.’ Locate the definitions of each category in the subsequent lines.
The original (aṣl) is taṣdīq, the annexed (mulḥaq) is iqrār, and the additional (zawāʾid) are the branches of faith like prayer, zakat, etc.
2
Understand why the original cannot be dropped
Find the phrase ‘أصل محكم لا يحتمل السقوط بحال’ (an original, firm [element] that cannot be dropped under any circumstance). Note that it applies to taṣdīq (affirmation of the heart). This is an absolute rule, even under duress.
This principle distinguishes the Ḥanafī view from others that might allow dispensation in extreme duress.
3
Analyze the role of the annexed element (iqrār)
The text explains that iqrār was originally evidence for taṣdīq but became a pillar (rukn) in the rulings of this world and the Hereafter. Note its dual nature: it is annexed to the original but still essential for legal effects.
Unlike taṣdīq, iqrār can be affected by duress? The passage later discusses apostasy under duress, showing that iqrār alone may suffice to establish iman in some cases.
4
Apply to the case of duress
Read the example: if a disbeliever is forced to faith and he verbally confesses, his faith is valid based on the existence of one of the two pillars (i.e., iqrār). This shows that iqrār can substitute for taṣdīq in worldly rulings under duress, but taṣdīq itself is never negated.
Contrast with apostasy under duress: the act of apostasy is pure evidence (dalīl maḥḍ), not a pillar, so it does not affect the original faith.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul presents a detailed classification of acts of worship (عبادات) as part of the subject of the ruled act (المحكوم فيه). The author first lists several categories (5–8) that seem to be subcategories of more comprehensive classifications, then focuses on faith (الإيمان) and its branches. Faith has an original element (تصديق, affirmation of the heart) which can never be dropped, and an annexed element (إقرار باللسان, verbal confession) which originally was evidence but became a pillar in worldly and afterlife rulings. The branches of faith (فروع الإيمان) are then ordered in hierarchy: prayer (صلاة), zakat (زكاة), fasting (صوم), Hajj (حج), Umrah (عمرة), jihad (جهاد), and i'tikaf (اعتكاف). Each is evaluated in terms of its mediations (واسطة) and its status as original, annexed, or additional. Then the passage turns to mixed categories: acts of worship that have the meaning of maintenance (مؤنة) like sadaqat al-fitr, maintenance that has the meaning of nearness (قربة) like 'ushr, and maintenance that has a semblance of punishment like kharaj. The distinctions are based on the nature of the act, its cause, and its effect on the performer. This taxonomy is fundamental in Hanafi usul for understanding the legal character of different acts of worship and their conditions.

Key Terms
أصل (asl): The foundational element that cannot be dropped under any circumstance, like affirmation (تصديق) in faith.
ملحق (mulhaq): Annexed to the original, sharing its ruling but not originally part of it, like verbal confession annexed to affirmation.
زوائد (zawa'id): Additional elements beyond the original and annexed, possibly optional or supplementary.
واسطة (wasita): A mediation or intermediary through which an act becomes a means of nearness to Allah, e.g., Ka'ba for prayer, poor for zakat.
مؤنة (mu'na): Maintenance cost or burden; here used to describe acts that have a nature of financial obligation rather than pure worship.
