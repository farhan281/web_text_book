The Rules of Tahri and When a Ruling Can Be Overturned
Explanation of tahri rules with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rules of tahri (personal judgment) to determine when it is obligatory, when it is overridden, and when a prior tahri ruling can be changed in future practice.

Core Idea
A tahri ruling stands until overturned by stronger evidence; it can be changed in future only if the ruling concerns a mutable matter like direction, not a fixed attribute.

This passage from the sharh of Usul al-Bazdawi addresses a critical dimension of ijtihad: the use of personal judgment (tahri) when external evidence is absent. The author walks through several scenarios—doubtful garments, ambiguous Qibla—to illustrate when tahri becomes obligatory and when it must be substituted (e.g., by tayammum). The text then explores the binding nature of a tahri ruling: once you act on it, you cannot overturn it by another tahri of equal weight; you need certainty, a text, or consensus. However, for mutable matters (like Qibla direction), a new tahri can guide future acts. This distinction between mutable and immutable rulings is a key contribution of Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify when tahri is required
Scenarios: when there is no external evidence for purity or direction, but there is some basis for judgment (e.g., one pure and one impure garment). Note that tayammum is used when there is complete inability to use water, not for resolving conflicting evidence.
The key is necessity: if you must choose and have no other recourse, tahri is obligatory.
2
Recognize the role of shahadat al-qalb
The testimony of the heart is the tool used when no external sign exists; it is not a mere choice but an obligation to arrive at the most likely ruling.
Shahadat al-qalb is the inner conviction of the mujtahid, akin to zann ghalib.
3
Understand the stability of a tahri ruling
Once a ruling is reached and acted upon, it cannot be overturned by another tahri of equal strength. Only evidence of higher authority (text, consensus, certainty) can abrogate it.
The first ruling is strengthened by being put into practice.
4
Distinguish mutable vs immutable rulings
For mutable matters (like Qibla direction, which changed historically), a new tahri can change future practice. For immutable attributes (like the purity of a specific garment), the original tahri's conclusion is fixed because the attribute is attached to the object.
This distinction determines whether a later tahri can override the earlier one for future cases.

Commentary
This passage from the sharh explains the rules of tahri (personal judgment in cases of doubt) and when a previous ruling based on tahri can be changed. The author presents several scenarios: First, if someone has two garments but neither is known to be pure and he is unable to determine, he falls back on tayammum. If he has one pure and one impure garment, he must use tahri to choose. If the Qibla is ambiguous with no external signs, he relies on his inner conviction. Once he acts on his tahri, that ruling cannot be overturned by another tahri of equal weight; only evidence of higher authority (like a decisive text or consensus) can overturn it. However, for matters that naturally change over time or context (like Qibla direction), a new tahri can change the future practice. But for attributes inherent to a specific object (like the purity of a particular garment), once a ruling is established by tahri, it cannot be changed by another tahri; only certainty can override it. This distinguishes between rules that are mutable and those that are fixed to an entity.

Key Terms
تحري (tahri): Personal ijtihad and judgment to determine the correct ruling in cases of uncertainty, based on available signs and inner conviction.
شهادة القلب (shahadat al-qalb): The testimony of the heart, i.e., one's inner conviction or intuitive judgment when no external evidence is available.

The Prophet's Ijtihad: Preferring Revelation Over Opinion
Argues for preferring revelation over ijtihad using analogy to tayammum

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that the Prophet's ijtihad is valid only when revelation is absent, and apply the analogy of seeking water before tayammum to justify the obligation of seeking revelation first.

Core Idea
The Prophet waits for revelation before ijtihad, as one seeks water before tayammum.

In this passage, al-Bazdawi addresses a key usul question: Did the Prophet use personal opinion (ra'y) and consultation? The answer is yes, but only when no revelation was available. The Hanafi school emphasizes that the Prophet was honored with revelation, making waiting for it the preferred course. This lesson explores the proof for this rule through an analogy to tayammum.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The passage discusses whether the Prophet engaged in ijtihad and consultation. Identify the specific ruling: the Prophet used ijtihad and consultation only in the absence of revelation.
Note the distinction: when revelation exists, it takes precedence.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi position is that the Prophet is infallible (ma'sum) from persisting in error, so his ijtihad is always correct. Nevertheless, waiting for revelation is preferred because revelation is more honored and sufficient.
This preference is a tarjih, not a prohibition of ijtihad.
3
Examine the proof (qiyas)
The passage draws an analogy: just as tayammum is not permissible where water is usually present without first seeking water, a mujtahid must first seek the revealed text before resorting to opinion. Identify the illa: the general presence of the object (water/revelation) necessitates searching.
The dalil is a qiyas, which is zanni in thubut and dalala.
4
Consider the scope and infallibility
The Prophet is infallible, so his ijtihad is binding. For other mujtahids, their ijtihad may be correct or erroneous, but they must still seek the text first.
This distinguishes the Prophet's ijtihad from that of others.
5
Apply the rule in practice
For a mujtahid facing a new issue, the rule is: search for a revealed text (Quran, Sunnah) first. If found, follow it. If not, perform ijtihad. The waiting period extends until one hopes for revelation, unless delay would cause loss of the issue.
This is analogous to the obligation of seeking water before tayammum.

Commentary
This passage is a discussion in Hanafi usul al-fiqh about the nature of the Prophet's ijtihad and the role of consultation. The commentator begins by noting that the Prophet (peace be upon him) took the opinion of Usayd ibn Hudayr in a military matter, but he would decide matters himself when revelation came. The Prophet told his senior companions that in matters without revelation, he is like them—implying his own ijtihad. However, consultation is only permissible when revelation is absent; when revelation exists, it takes precedence. The Prophet is infallible (ma'sum) from persisting in error, unlike others. Therefore, his ijtihad is always correct. Nevertheless, the Hanafi preference is to wait for revelation because the Prophet is honored with revelation that obviates the need for opinion. This waiting is similar to the obligation of seeking water before tayammum. The passage then transitions to the topic of laws of previous prophets (shara'i' man qablana), noting that it is a separate chapter that was delayed due to disagreement about their applicability to our Prophet's community.

Key Terms
الوحي (al-wahy): Divine revelation sent to the Prophet, which is the primary source of knowledge and takes precedence over opinion.
الرأي (al-ra'y): Personal opinion or ijtihad used when no specific revelation or text is available.
المشورة (al-mushawara): Consultation with companions, permissible only in matters where no revelation exists.

Conditions of Ijtihad and the Debate on Taswib vs Takhti'a
Conditions and ruling of ijtihad, and the dispute on taswib

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three conditions of a mujtahid according to the Hanafi school and identify the Hanafi position on whether every mujtahid is correct.

Core Idea
The mujtahid must know Quran, Sunnah, and qiyas; truth is one, but a mistaken mujtahid is rewarded.

This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul al-Fiqh introduces the essential qualifications of a mujtahid—what knowledge he must possess—and then addresses a foundational theological dispute: are all mujtahids correct? The Hanafi school holds that only one interpretation is correct in God's sight, but the mujtahid is rewarded even when mistaken. Understanding this distinction is key to appreciating the Hanafi theory of ijtihad and its approach to legal pluralism.

Guided Path
1
Identify the conditions of ijtihad
Read the passage and list the three areas of knowledge a mujtahid must master: the Book (Quran) with its meanings and aspects, the Sunnah with its chains and texts and meanings, and the types of qiyas (analogy) as outlined in this book.
The text says 'أما شرطه : فأن يَحوي علم الكتاب بمعانيه ووجوهه... وعلم السنة بطرقها ومتونها... وأن يعرف وجوه القياس.'
2
Understand the ruling of ijtihad
The passage states that ijtihad yields 'correctness by predominant opinion' (al-isaba bi ghalib al-ra'y). This means the mujtahid does not attain certainty but a strong probability, and may err or be correct.
The term 'ghalib al-ra'y' is key: it distinguishes ijtihad from definitive knowledge.
3
State the Hanafi position on taswib vs takhti'a
The Hanafi school (and Ahl al-Sunna) holds that the truth is one in matters of ijtihad. A mujtahid may be correct (musib) or mistaken (mukhti'), but if mistaken he is still rewarded for his effort. The chosen view is that he is correct in the beginning of his ijtihad (i.e., his act of ijtihad is valid and rewarded) but mistaken in the end regarding the actual divine ruling.
The text says: 'عندنا الحق واحد... والمختار عندنا هو أنه مصيب في ابتداء اجتهاده، لكنه مخطى انتهاء.'
4
Contrast with the Mu'tazila view
The Mu'tazila argue that every mujtahid is correct and that truths are multiple. Among them, some hold all correct opinions are equal, while most hold one is more correct. The Hanafi response is that the truth is singular.
This is a theological dispute with practical implications for the binding nature of fatwas.
5
Recognize the internal Hanafi disagreement
Even among Ahl al-Sunna, there are two views: (1) the mujtahid if mistaken is mistaken from start to finish, and (2) he is correct in his ijtihad process but mistaken in the end. The latter is the chosen view.
This nuance affects how we view the status of differing rulings within the school.

Commentary
This passage is a usul al-fiqh dispute on the nature of ijtihad and whether the truth (haqq) sought by mujtahids is one or multiple. The author, al-Bazdudi, begins by stating the conditions for a mujtahid: comprehensive knowledge of the Quran and Sunnah, and mastery of qiyas. The ruling on ijtihad is that it leads to correctness by predominant opinion (ghalabat al-ra'y), meaning a mujtahid can sometimes be correct (musib) and sometimes mistaken (mukhti). This is the Hanafi position. In contrast, the Mu'tazila hold that every mujtahid is correct (musib) and that truths are multiple. The passage then subdivides the opinions: among those who affirm multiple truths, some say they are equal, others say one is superior. Among the 'people of the correct doctrine' (ahl al-maqalat al-sahiha, i.e., the Ahl al-Sunnah), there is a further split: some say the erring mujtahid is mistaken from start to finish; others (the chosen view) say he is correct in his effort but mistaken in the outcome. The narration from Abu Hanifa – 'every mujtahid is correct, and the truth with Allah is one' – is interpreted to mean that the mujtahid is correct in his ijtihad (as an act of worship) but the truth itself is singular and known to Allah. The Mu'tazila's proof is based on the obligation to hit the truth: since Allah obliges all mujtahids to seek the truth, they must all be considered correct in their results, otherwise the obligation would be impossible. They analogize to the qibla where all who pray according to their ijtihad are correct and the duty is fulfilled, even if some face the wrong direction. They also argue that multiple truths are permissible in matters of permission and prohibition, as seen with different prophets and times. The Hanafi response, presented as the correct view, is that multiple truths are not necessary; the mujtahid may err. The proof from the Quran is the story of Prophet Sulayman (AS) and Dawud (AS) where Allah says 'We gave understanding to Sulayman', implying that one was correct and the other, though a prophet, did not hit the correct ruling. This supports that even prophets can err in ijtihad, so ordinary mujtahids certainly can. The Hanafi view maintains the singular truth while acknowledging the sincerity and reward of every mujtahid.

Core Idea
Tarjih is the tipping of the scale by a subsidiary attribute, not by an independent cause.

In this passage from the chapter on tarjih, Imam al-Bazdawi introduces the concept of preponderance as a method for resolving conflicts between two equivalent evidences. He uses the analogy of a scale and the Prophet's instruction to 'weigh and give extra' to illustrate that tarjih occurs through a subsidiary description that reinforces meaning, not through an additional independent cause. This lesson unpacks that definition and its key conditions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the scope of the chapter
The passage opens by stating four topics in this chapter: definition of tarjih, ways of tarjih, resolution of conflicting ways, and invalid ways. Focus on the first topic.
This lesson only covers the definition.
2
Extract the linguistic and legal definition
Find the sentence defining tarjih: 'الترجيح عبارة عن فضل أحد المثلين على الآخر وصفاً'. Note that it is based on equivalence (مماثلة) and conflict (تعارض).
The definition has linguistic roots in the scale (رجحان الميزان).
3
Analyze the scale analogy
Understand the analogy: two scale pans equal, then a small weight (daniq) tips the balance. This small weight is subsidiary—it does not establish the weight independently. Contrast with adding six or seven, which would be excessive.
The opposite is tatfif (deficiency), which also does not establish conflict.
4
Examine the legal example from the hadith
The Prophet (صلى الله عليه وسلم) said to a weigher: 'زن وأرجح' (Weigh and give extra). This surplus is a valid tarjih, not a gift, as long as it is small. If excessive, it becomes a gift and is invalid.
This hadith is the dalil for permissibility of a small extra in debt repayment.
5
Identify what does NOT constitute tarjih
Tarjih cannot occur by an independent cause. Examples: four witnesses vs. two, a third witness vs. two, multiple qiyas, multiple hadiths. These do not increase evidentiary strength because they are independent causes, not subsidiary attributes.
Fame (shuhra) can cause tarjih because it strengthens connection to the Prophet.

Third and Fourth Types of Tarjih: Kathrat al-Usul and al-Aks

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Al-aks is the weakest tarjih, but it validates a description when the ruling follows its presence and absence.

Commentary
This passage is from al-Bazdawi's work on usul al-fiqh, specifically discussing the types of tarjih (preponderance) in legal reasoning. The author presents two types: the third type (kathrat al-usul, 'abundance of sources') and the fourth type (al-aks, 'the inverse'). Rulings on the Rebel (Bāghī) in Hanafi Law
Defines the baghi and gives his rulings based on man'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two categories of ignorance in Hanafi usul, state the conditions for treating a baghi as a combatant, and derive the ruling on liability and inheritance based on the presence of man'ah.

Core Idea
The baghi's liability turns on man'ah: power transforms a criminal into a legitimate combatant subject to jihad.

This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul al-Fiqh addresses a crucial legal category: the baghi (rebel) who opposes the just imam with a faulty interpretation. The Hanafi school carefully distinguishes between a rebel without power—treated as a common criminal—and one with organized might, whose faulty interpretation becomes operative in worldly matters, triggering the rules of jihad. This lesson unpacks the reasoning behind that distinction and its implications for liability, inheritance, and the difference from warfare against non-Muslims.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category of ignorance
Recognize that the baghi belongs to the second category of ignorance: he has a faulty interpretation (ta'wil fasid) based on Quranic evidence, but opposes clear proof. This places him below the first type (rejection of obvious truth) yet above pure error because he claims Quranic support.
This classification matters for the degree of culpability and the method of response (debate before combat).
2
Isolate the condition of man'ah
Focus on the pivot: whether the baghi has man'ah (power, strength, ability to resist the authority). Without man'ah, he is fully liable for damages and all normal Islamic rulings apply. With man'ah, his status changes fundamentally.
Man'ah is defined as organized military power, not merely individual strength.
3
Trace the legal effects of man'ah
With man'ah: (a) his faulty interpretation is given effect in worldly matters, so he is not liable for damages; (b) jihad against him becomes obligatory, including killing captives and finishing off the wounded; (c) inheritance between the two sides is not barred because Islam unites them (Abu Hanifa and Muhammad's view).
The justification is that power removes the ability to compel, so the rules of jihad replace criminal liability.
4
Understand the rationale for the shift
The passage explains that when the baghi has power, the authority to compel falls away, so we act upon his faulty interpretation (i.e., treat him as a legitimate adversary). The legal consequences follow from the condition of power and the principle that Islam is preserving unity despite dissension.
This reflects the usul principle that legal rulings can change based on the capacity to enforce them.
5
Contrast with the people of war (harbis)
The passage explicitly distinguishes ahl al-harb: different abode (dar), no inviolability either way. For baghi, the abode is one (Dar al-Islam) but differs in religious ruling, so inviolability is partial, leading to different rules on property and inheritance.
This contrast sharpens the unique Hanafi treatment of the baghi.

And we are not liable for their property and lives, nor are we prevented from inheritance by killing them, because Islam unites, and the killing is justified. And they are not prevented (from inheritance) either if they kill, according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad (may Allah have mercy on them), because their killing is in the worldly ruling, with the condition of power, in the ruling of jihad, based on their religious conviction (diyaanah), even if it is false in reality. And it is obligatory to detain their property as a deterrent, and we do not own their property, because the original abode (dar) is one, and it differs by virtue of religious law; so inviolability is established from one aspect and not from another, so liability is not necessitated by doubt, and ownership is not necessitated by ambiguity. Unlike the people of war (ahl al-harb), because the abode is different and power is separate from every aspect, so inviolability is nullified for us with respect to them, and for them with respect to us from every aspect. Likewise, the ignorance of one who opposes in his ijtihad the Book, the noble Sunnah, and the imams of fiqh, or acts upon a strange (gharib) narration from the Sunnah contrary to the Book or the well-known Sunnah: it is rejected and invalid, not an excuse at all. (1) His statement: 'or acts upon a strange narration from the Sunnah contrary to the Book or the well-known Sunnah' – from the strange (reports) is what al-Nasa'i, Ibn Majah, and al-Daraqutni narrated from Jabir (may Allah be pleased with him): 'We used to sell ummahat al-awlad (slave mothers of children) while the Messenger of Allah (peace be upon him) was alive, and he saw no problem with that.'

Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul defines the second category of ignorants: those with whims regarding divine attributes and the afterlife, and the rebel (bāghī). The bāghī is one who rebels against the just imam with a faulty interpretation (ta'wil fasid) based on the Quran, but their interpretation is invalid because it contradicts clear proofs. However, since they are Muslims, we must debate and compel them. The key legal distinction lies in their power (man'ah): without power, they are liable for damages like a common criminal; with power, their faulty interpretation is given effect in worldly matters—they are not liable for damages, and jihad against them becomes obligatory, including killing captives and finishing off the wounded. Notably, both parties inherit from each other because Islam unites them, and the killing does not bar inheritance (according to Abu Hanifa and Muhammad). This differs from warfare with harbis, where no inviolability exists. The passage ends by rejecting the excuse of those who act on strange hadiths contrary to established texts.

Key Terms
الباغي (al-bāghī): A Muslim who rebels against the just imam with a faulty interpretation and has man'ah (power to fight).
المنعة (al-man'ah): Power, strength, or ability to resist the authority; the condition for treating the baghi as a legitimate combatant in jihad.
التأويل الفاسد (al-ta'wil al-fasid): An interpretation of the Quran that is incorrect due to conflicting with clear proof; it is the basis for the baghi's actions.

Jahl in Ijtihad Excuses from Hadd and Kaffarah
Examples demonstrating the rule of jahl in ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ignorance in matters of ijtihad and ignorance of clear prohibitions, and apply the rule that jahl in ijtihad areas drops hadd and kaffarah due to shubha.

Core Idea
Ignorance in ijtihad creates shubha that drops hadd/kaffarah; ignorance of clear text does not.

The Hanafi school grants an excuse for ignorance (jahl) in matters where there is room for ijtihad—legal reasoning based on ambiguous or contested evidence. This lesson explores al-Bazdawi's examples illustrating when jahl prevents the application of fixed penalties (hadd) and expiation (kaffarah), and when it does not. Understanding this distinction is critical for applying the concept of shubha in criminal and ritual law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the rule
The passage begins by stating the first category: ignorance in matters subject to ijtihad. Read the examples to infer the underlying principle: that such ignorance creates a shubha (doubt) which negates hadd and kaffarah.
The rule is a memorisation anchor; focus on the pattern, not the details first.
2
Analyse the first example (prayer order)
Compare two scenarios: ignorance contrary to ijma (invalid Asr) vs ignorance in ijtihad (valid Maghrib after making up Zuhr). Note that the second scenario is excused because the order of missed prayers is a matter of ijtihad.
3
Analyse the second and third examples (murder and cupping)
In the murder case, the second heir's ignorance that his right to retaliation has been reduced is in an ijtihad area, so no retaliation. The cupping example: thinking cupping breaks the fast is an ijtihad matter, so no kaffarah.
Both show that shubha drops the penalty, not the underlying obligation (e.g., qada of fast still required).
4
Analyse the fourth example (adultery with slave girl)
The hadd is dropped if the man mistakenly believes the slave girl is permissible (e.g., wife's or father's). This is a matter of ijtihad because permissibility of such slaves is debatable. Contrast with clear prohibition of a brother's slave girl.
Note: this shubha does not affect lineage or idda; those are based on clear texts.
5
Analyse the fifth example (harbi vs dhimmi)
A harbi convert who claims ignorance of wine prohibition is not flogged because he came from a land where the law may not have reached him (ijtihad area). But a dhimmi who converts is flogged because as a resident he is expected to know the clear prohibition. Same reasoning for adultery: universally known, so ignorance no excuse.
The rule hinges on the clarity of the prohibition in the person's context.

أما الأول: فإن مَن صلى الظهر على غير وضوء، ثم صلى العصر بوضوء وعنده أن الظهر قد أجزأه : فالعصر فاسد؛ لأن هذا جهل على خلاف الإجماع. وإن قضى الظهر، ثم صلى المغرب وعنده أن العصر قد أجزأ عنه: جاز ذلك؛ لأنه جهل في موضع الاجتهاد في ترتيب الفوائت. وقال أصحابنا رحمهم الله فيمن قتل وله وليان، فعفا أحدهما عن القصاص، ثم قتله الثاني وهو يظن أن القصاص باق له على الكمال، وأنه وجب لكل واحد منهما قصاص كامل : فإنه لا قصاص عليه ؛ لأن جهله حصل في موضع الاجتهاد وفي حكم يسقط بالشبهة. وكذلك صائم احتجم ، ثم أفطر على ظن أن الحجامة قد فطّرته. وعلى ذلك التقدير : لم تلزمه الكفارة؛ لما قلنا، ومثله كثير. ومَن زنى بجارية امرأته أو جارية والده، وظن أنها تحل له: لم يلزمه الحد، فيصير الجهل والتأويل في موضع الاشتباه شبهةً في الحد، دون النسب والعدة. بخلاف ما إذا وطئ جارية أخيه، أو أخته. وكذلك حربي أسلم، ودخل دارنا، فشرب الخمرَ ، وقال : لم أعلم بالحرمة : لم يُحد. بخلاف ما إذا زنى. وبخلاف الذمي إذا أسلم ثم شرب الخمر، وقال: لم أعلم بحرمتها : فإنه يُحدُّ هذا ؛ بناءً على هذا الأصل الذي ذكرنا.
As for the first: Whoever prays Zuhr without wudu, then prays Asr with wudu while thinking that Zuhr was valid for him: then Asr is invalid; because this is ignorance contrary to consensus. And if he makes up Zuhr, then prays Maghrib while thinking that Asr was valid for him: that is permissible; because it is ignorance in a place of ijtihad concerning the order of missed prayers. And our companions (may Allah have mercy on them) said concerning someone who kills and has two heirs, one of whom forgives retaliation, then the second kills him while thinking that complete retaliation remains for him, and that a full retaliation was obligatory for each of them: then there is no retaliation upon him; because his ignorance occurred in a place of ijtihad and in a ruling that falls due to doubt. Similarly, a fasting person who cupped himself then broke his fast thinking that cupping had broken his fast: on that basis, no expiation is required of him; because of what we said, and many similar examples. And whoever commits adultery with his wife's slave girl or his father's slave girl, thinking she is permissible for him: the hadd penalty is not imposed on him; thus ignorance and interpretation in a place of ambiguity become a doubt (shubha) in the hadd, but not in lineage and waiting period. Unlike if he had intercourse with his brother's or sister's slave girl. And similarly, a harbi who converts and enters our land, then drinks wine and says: 'I did not know of its prohibition': he is not subject to the hadd. Unlike if he commits adultery. And unlike a dhimmi who converts then drinks wine and says: 'I did not know of its prohibition': he is subject to the hadd; this is based on the principle we have mentioned.

Commentary
This passage is a statement of a usul rule concerning the effect of ignorance (jahl) on penal and expiatory obligations. The author, al-Bazdawi, explains the first category: ignorance that occurs in matters open to ijtihad. He provides several examples. First, if someone prays Zuhr without wudu (invalid), then later prays Asr with wudu thinking his Zuhr was valid, his Asr is invalid because this ignorance is contrary to ijma (no one considers such prayer valid). But if he subsequently makes up Zuhr and then prays Maghrib thinking his earlier Asr was valid, that Maghrib prayer is valid because the order of missed prayers is a matter of ijtihad; his mistaken belief is excused. Second, in a murder case with two heirs, if one heir forgives retaliation, the other heir who kills the murderer thinking he still has a right to full retaliation is not liable for killing because his ignorance is in an ijtihad area and the ruling (retaliation) falls due to doubt (shubha). Third, a fasting person who cupped himself and then broke his fast thinking cupping invalidates the fast is not required to make expiation (kaffarah) because the effect of cupping on fasting is subject to ijtihad. Fourth, adultery with a slave girl whom one mistakenly believes is permissible (e.g., one's own wife's or father's slave) does not incur the hadd penalty, because the permissibility is ambiguous; however, this ignorance does not affect lineage or waiting period. This is contrasted with intercourse with a brother's or sister's slave girl, where ignorance is not excused because the prohibition is clear. Fifth, a harbi (non-Muslim from enemy territory) who converts to Islam, enters Muslim land, and drinks wine claiming ignorance of its prohibition is not flogged, because the prohibition of wine may not have reached him. But if he commits adultery, he is flogged because its prohibition is universally known. A dhimmi (protected non-Muslim) who converts then drinks wine claiming ignorance is flogged because as a resident of Muslim land, he should know the prohibition. The underlying principle is that ignorance in ijtihad matters creates a shubha that drops hadd and kaffarah, but ignorance of clear, universally known prohibitions does not excuse.

Key Terms
جهل (jahl): Ignorance of a legal ruling; in this passage, ignorance that occurs in matters open to ijtihad creates a shubha (doubt) that prevents the application of hadd and kaffarah.
اجتهاد (ijtihad): Independent legal reasoning; matters subject to ijtihad are those where there is no clear text, allowing for valid difference of opinion.
شبهة (shubha): A doubt or ambiguity that prevents the application of a fixed penalty (hadd); in this passage, ignorance in ijtihad matters creates a shubha that drops the hadd.
