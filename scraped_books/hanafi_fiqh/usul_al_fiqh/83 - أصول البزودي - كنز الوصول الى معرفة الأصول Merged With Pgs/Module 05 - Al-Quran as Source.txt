Al-Bazdawi's Three-Dimensional Definition of Fiqh
Definition of fiqh with three components and Quranic evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state al-Bazdawi's three components of fiqh and identify the Quranic proof he uses.

Core Idea
Fiqh is not mere knowledge; it requires mastery and practice to be complete.

Al-Bazdawi opens his discussion of fiqh by defining it as a three-dimensional science. He insists that true fiqh integrates knowledge of the law, deep understanding that ties principles to branches, and active practice. This definition challenges the notion that fiqh is only theoretical and is supported by Quranic verses about wisdom.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components
List the three divisions al-Bazdawi mentions: knowledge of what is legislated, mastery of understanding texts and binding principles to branches, and acting upon it.
Each division is essential; missing one reduces the status.
2
Understand the Quranic proof
Al-Bazdawi cites two verses where 'hikmah' appears, and Ibn Abbas' tafsir equating hikmah with knowledge of halal and haram. Linguistically, hikmah means knowledge and action, supporting the inclusion of practice in fiqh.
The proof relies on linguistic haqiqah and authoritative tafsir.
3
Apply the definition
Determine whether a person who knows rulings but does not practice is a 'faqih mutlaq' or 'faqih min wajh' based on al-Bazdawi's conclusion.
Practice is necessary for absolute fiqh; without it, one is only a faqih in one aspect.

Coercion and the Heart's Steadfastness: Ammar's Hadith
Examining Hadith of Ammar and Quranic verse on coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that coerced utterances do not nullify faith when the heart remains firm, using the Hadith of Ammar and Quran 16:106 as evidence.

Core Idea
Coerced disbelief under duress is excused if the heart remains steadfast in faith.

In this lesson, we examine a foundational hadith and its corresponding Quranic verse that establish the Hanafi principle regarding coercion (ikrah). The incident of Ammar ibn Yasir, approved by the Prophet ﷺ, demonstrates that outward utterances under duress do not affect the status of a believer as long as the heart is assured with faith. This textual analysis reveals how the Hanafi school derives a nuanced ruling on apostasy and duress.

Guided Path
1
Locate the textual evidence
Identify the two primary texts: the Hadith of Ammar and Quran 16:106. Read the passage to understand the incident and the verse's context.
2
Assess transmission and meaning
Classify the hadith as ahaad (zanni al-thubut) but note its strong indication (qati al-dalala) regarding the core principle. The verse is mutawatir (qati al-thubut) and clear in meaning.
The hadith's chain is reliable, though ahaad, and its meaning is unequivocal in allowing compliance under duress.
3
Apply the Hanafi istinbat principle
The Prophet's command 'If they return, return' is an amr (imperative). In Hanafi usul, amr indicates wujub unless there is a qarina (contextual evidence). Here, the qarina of coercion and Ammar's firm heart shifts the meaning to ibaha (permissibility).
This principle is key: the amr is not taken literally as obligation because of the duress context.
4
Derive the legal ruling
From the texts, derive that a person under coercion does not become a disbeliever if the heart remains firm. Outward compliance does not invalidate iman.
This ruling applies to all acts of worship and statements of faith under duress.
5
Compare cross-madhhab readings
Note that the Shafi'i school also uses these texts but holds that ikrah does not change the ruling of kufr; the person is considered a disbeliever outwardly unless the heart is firm, but they differ on the legal consequences.
The practical difference (thamarah al-khilaf) affects inheritance, marriage, and funeral rites.

The Story of 'Ammar: Proof that Ikrah Excuses Uttering Disbelief
Proof from the story of 'Ammar for the exception of ikrah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the dalil from the hadith of 'Ammar ibn Yasir and apply the Hanafi usul principle that ikrah with a firm heart removes the ruling of kufr from coerced speech.

Core Idea
The Prophet's instruction 'If they return, then repeat' proves that forced utterances do not affect iman when the heart is firm.

This lesson examines the narration of 'Ammar ibn Yasir, which classical exegetes agree was the occasion for revealing Quran 16:106. This hadith serves as a key textual proof (dalil) for the Hanafi rule that compulsion (ikrah) can render an otherwise invalidating act permissible. We will analyse the text, identify its legal implication, and derive the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary texts
Locate both the Quranic verse (16:106) and the hadith of 'Ammar that serve as the main proof for ikrah. The verse provides the general rule, the hadith provides the specific application.
The verse is Qur'anic (qat'i thubut), the hadith is ahad (zanni thubut).
2
Classify the hadith
Determine the category of the hadith in terms of transmission and meaning. It is a hadith ahad (single transmission) but its meaning is explicit and supported by the consensus of exegetes.
Hanafi usul: ahad hadith can be used for ahkam when the meaning is clear (qati al-dalala).
3
Extract the operative phrase
Identify the key part of the hadith that indicates the ruling. The Prophet's question 'How do you find your heart?' and his response 'If they return, then repeat' are the operative phrases.
These phrases show that the outward utterance is not the criterion; the inner state is.
4
Apply the Hanafi usul principle
The Prophet's tacit approval (taqrir) of 'Ammar's action indicates permissibility. His instruction to repeat under similar conditions confirms that the ruling is not a one-time exception but a general principle.
Taqrir is a recognised source of sunnah in Hanafi usul.
5
Derive the ruling
From the above, the ruling is that uttering kufr under duress with a heart firm in iman does not nullify one's Islam. The outward act is excused because of coercion.
This is the mufta bihi position in the Hanafi school.

Rulings Established by the Exact Wording (Nass)
Examples of direct textual inferences (nass) from Quran and Hadith.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a nass text, classify it as a direct dalil, and explain how the ruling is derived from the wording without qiyas.

Core Idea
A ruling established by nass is derived directly from the text's wording, requiring no analogical reasoning.

In Hanafi usul, rulings can be derived directly from the Quran and Sunnah through the exact wording (nass). This lesson examines three examples where the text itself, without any analogical extension, yields a clear legal ruling. By parsing these examples, you will learn to distinguish nass from other types of textual evidence and understand the strength of direct inference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the textual source
For each example, determine whether the source is a Quranic verse or a prophetic hadith. Note the specific reference.
The three sources are Quran 2:233, a hadith, and Quran 46:15.
2
Isolate the key phrase
Find the exact wording that carries the ruling. In nass, the ruling is embedded in the phrasing itself.
E.g., 'upon the one to whom he is born' (Quran 2:233) points to the father.
3
Analyze the direct inference
Explain how the phrase directly indicates the ruling without additional reasoning. Is it by explicit mention or by necessary implication?
Quran 46:15 states total gestation and weaning is thirty months; using known weaning period (24 months), the minimum pregnancy is necessarily six months.
4
Verify no qiyas is needed
Confirm that the ruling is not derived through analogical reasoning. If the text itself gives the ruling, it is nass.
These examples are self-contained; no qiyas is required.
5
Distinguish from rulings by qiyas
Contrast with a case where the text does not explicitly rule and qiyas would be used. This reinforces the strength of nass.
For example, the prohibition of wine is nass; the prohibition of narcotics is by qiyas.

Interpreting 'Nikah' in Quran 2:230: Contract or Intercourse?
Linguistic analysis of 'nikah' and the complementary role of Sunnah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a word's contextual indicators (qara'in) can specify its meaning, and identify how the Sunnah provides a complementary ruling (hukm zaaid) not derived from the Quranic text alone.

Core Idea
In Quran 2:230, 'nikah' means the contract, but intercourse is required by the Sunnah — showing the Quran-Sunnah complementary relationship.

This passage from a Hanafi usul al-fiqh work examines the word 'nikah' in Quran 2:230. The verse conditions a divorced woman's return to her first husband on her marrying another. Does 'nikah' mean the marriage contract or actual intercourse? The author first presents the argument of Muhammad al-Shaybani and al-Shafi'i that the particle 'hatta' (until) indicates a limit and does not itself create a new ruling. Then the Hanafi response uses linguistic analysis to show that 'nikah' here means the contract because it is attributed to the woman. Crucially, the requirement of intercourse (dukhul) is not from the verse but from the Sunnah — a key example of how the Sunnah supplements the Quran.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic issue
The word 'nikah' in Quran 2:230 can mean either the marriage contract (al-aqd) or intercourse (al-wat'). The Hanafi analysis uses the attribution of the verb to the woman as a contextual indicator (qarinah) to determine which meaning applies. Because women directly perform contracts but not the act of intercourse, 'tankiha' here must refer to the contract.
Attribution to the woman is crucial: 'tan-kiha' implies she performs the action, which she can do for a contract but not for intercourse.
2
Understand the role of 'hatta' (حتى)

This shows the precision of usul: particles have fixed functions and cannot be arbitrarily reinterpreted.
3

The author argues that 'nikah' originally means intercourse (its 'asl'), but it can also be used metaphorically for the contract. In this verse, the attribution to the woman (mudhaf ilayha) makes the contract meaning dominant, because she can only directly perform the contract, not intercourse.

4

Even after establishing that 'nikah' means the contract, the verse does not explicitly require intercourse. The requirement of dukhul (intercourse) is proven by the hadith of Rifa'a's wife, where the Prophet (PBUH) indicated that she could not return to her first husband without having had intercourse with the second. Thus, the Sunnah adds a condition not found in the Quranic text.
This illustrates the Hanafi principle that the Quran and Sunnah together form a single source of law.

The verse is 'amm' (general) in that 'nikah' could include both contract and intercourse. The linguistic indicator (attribution to woman) specifies (takhsis) it to the contract. Then the Sunnah further specifies that the contract alone is insufficient — intercourse is required. This shows how multiple levels of specification operate.
The Hanafi school holds that amm can be specified by a qarinah or by a hadith.

The letter 'fa' in the Qur'an links the third divorce to khul', not to the first two divorces.

The Facilitating Ability (Qudrah Muyassirah) in Kaffarah
Expansion of the usul rule on ability in expiations.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that ability in kaffarah is facilitating (qudrah muyassirah) and distinguish it from the condition for zakah, using the Quranic verse (5:89) as evidence.

Core Idea
In kaffarah, ability is facilitating: present inability permits fasting, unlike zakah which requires continuous wealth.

This passage from Al-Bazdawi's Kanz al-Wusul expands a key usul rule: the condition of ability (qudrah) for obligations does not always require continuous wealth. In expiations like kaffarah, the Law considers present inability – if you are poor at the time of performance, fasting suffices. This is derived from the Quranic phrase 'lam yajid' (does not find) in Surah al-Ma'idah (5:89), interpreted as present inability. The passage contrasts this with zakah, where wealth must be specified and debt nullifies the obligation, and discusses a nuanced intra-Hanafi difference regarding debtors.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Rule
The passage states that for kaffarah, the obligation is attached to 'al-qudrah al-muyassirah' (facilitating ability). This means if a wealthy person breaks an oath and then becomes poor before expiating, he may fast. The rule is that ability is assessed at the time of performance, not continuously.
This is a usul rule about the condition of obligation (shart al-wujub) vs. condition of performance (shart al-ada).
2
Locate the Dalil
The primary evidence is the Quranic verse 5:89: 'Whoever does not find [an animal for sacrifice], then a fast of three days.' The word 'lam yajid' (does not find) is the operative phrase.
The dalil is naqli (textual) from the Quran.
3
Analyze the Wajh al-Dalalah
The Hanafi interpretation holds that 'lam yajid' refers to current inability (al-‘ajz al-hali), not inability for the entire lifetime. If lifetime inability were required, fasting would never be valid for someone who might become wealthy later. Thus present inability suffices to permit the badal (substitute).
This is based on the principle that absolute (mutlaq) expressions run on their absoluteness.
4
Contrast with Zakah
In zakah, wealth must be specified ('ayn al-mal) and debt nullifies the obligation because it contradicts wealthiness (ghina). In kaffarah, wealth is not specified (ghayr 'ayn), so consuming it before expiation is not transgression, and ability continues if any wealth is later obtained.
This distinction shows that the condition of ability varies by obligation type.
5
Apply to the Debt Scenario
The passage mentions an intra-Hanafi difference: if a debtor expiates by fasting, some Hanafi teachers allow it because debt negates the quality of ease (yusr), so wealth is treated as non-existent. This shows the practical application of the rule.
The mufta bihi (operative view) is that the debtor's expiation by fasting is valid.

Core Idea
Prohibition necessarily implies ugliness, negating legal goodness; an act cannot be both legally sanctioned and prohibited.

In this lesson, we delve into a foundational principle of Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the implication of prohibition. Just as a command (amr) necessarily implies the goodness (husn) of the commanded act, a prohibition (nahy) necessarily implies its ugliness (qubh). This has far-reaching consequences for how we classify acts that are simultaneously prohibited yet legally binding. We will explore the textual basis, refute an opposing view, and trace the application through several branches, including zina, usurpation, ihram violations, and divorce during menstruation.

Commentary
This passage is a commentary expanding on the principle that prohibition (nahy) necessarily implies ugliness (qubh), just as command (amr) necessarily implies goodness (husn). The author argues that the absolute meaning of any term, including 'prohibition', indicates its perfect form; for prohibition, perfection is ugliness. He refutes those who claim a prohibited act is legally good in origin but ugly in description, calling this a reversal of literal and metaphorical meaning. He then establishes two ways to derive further rulings: either the act ceases to be legally sanctioned due to the very essence of prohibition, or it ceases due to its ruling. The first way is explained by citing the Quranic verse 'He has ordained for you...' showing that legally sanctioned acts must be pleasing to Allah. A prohibited act, being ugly, contradicts this pleasure, even though it may occur by Allah's will and decree (like disbelief and sins). Thus, prohibition effectively abrogates any prior legal goodness. The second way is that prohibition necessitates desisting, making the act a sin, which is contradictory to being a legally sanctioned act of obedience. Several examples illustrate this: zina does not create marriage prohibition (musahara) because it is pure sin and cannot be a cause for a blessing; usurpation (ghasp) does not transfer ownership. The author then addresses objections: ihram during intercourse is not corrupt in its essence; it remains legally binding but is invalidated due to the prohibition of intercourse, but does not cease to exist as a legal act (it is not like the case where the act itself vanishes by prohibition). Similarly, divorce pronounced during menstruation or after intercourse is still valid despite being prohibited. The passage concludes with a supporting hadith about a man who had intercourse while in ihram, showing that his ihram remains valid but is corrupted, requiring a sacrifice and an additional pilgrimage. The principle throughout is that prohibition eliminates the legal goodness of an act but not necessarily its legal existence if the act is binding.

القُبْح (al-qubh): The attribute of being ugly or displeasing, which is inherently implied by prohibition (nahy).
الحَقِيقَة (al-haqiqah): The literal meaning of a word; for nahy, its haqiqa is to imply ugliness.

Prohibition as Negation: Marriage to Mahrams
Usul analysis of nahy as nafy due to absent mahall

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a Quranic prohibition is interpreted as a negation and explain the role of the subject (mahall) in that interpretation.

Core Idea
A prohibition where the subject is absent is actually a negation of the act itself.

In Usul al-Fiqh, the linguistic form of a command or prohibition does not always yield its literal meaning. This passage from al-Bazdawi's Kanz al-Wusul illustrates a key principle: when the subject (mahall) of an act is non-existent, the prohibition (nahy) is reinterpreted as a negation (nafy) of the act's validity. The Quranic verse forbidding marriage to stepmothers is used as evidence—the prohibition is a metaphor for negating the contract entirely.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling
Read the passage and note the ruling: marriage to mahrams is 'negated' (manfiyy). This means the contract is legally non-existent, not merely prohibited.
Distinguish between 'negation of validity' and 'prohibition with validity'.
2
Locate the dalil
The author cites Quran 4:22: 'Do not marry those whom your fathers married.' Identify the dalil as a Quranic verse (naqli proof).
Verse is mutawatir in transmission (qat'i thubut) but its interpretation (dalala) is zanni.
3
Analyze the linguistic mechanism
The author states that the prohibition (nahy) is 'metaphorically used for negation' (musta'ar 'an al-nafy). Why? Because the mahall (subject) is absent—these women are intrinsically unmarriable (mahram).
When the subject is impossible, the prohibition cannot be a mere prohibition; it must indicate impossibility.
4
Apply the usul principle
The Hanafi usul principle: An imperative or prohibition that applies to an impossible subject indicates negation of the act's validity. Here, the prohibition negates the marriage contract.
This is a form of majaz (metaphorical usage) in Quranic language.
5
Derive the fiqh ruling
The derived ruling: Marriage to a mahram is batil (void) from the outset, not simply sinful. There is no legal effect.
Contrast with a valid but sinful marriage (e.g., marrying during ihram) where the contract is sahih but makruh.

Dalala Min Qabl al-Mutakallim: Reinterpreting Commands When Literal Meaning Is Impossible
Reinterpreting commands when literal meaning is impossible

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a case where the literal meaning of a command is impossible, apply the Hanafi principle of dalala min qabl al-mutakallim, and derive the proper ruling.

Core Idea
When literal meaning is impossible, the command is reinterpreted according to the speaker's known purpose.

Sometimes a text’s literal meaning contradicts what we know about the speaker or context. The Hanafi school uses the principle of dalala min qabl al-mutakallim to resolve such conflicts. This lesson walks through the Quranic example of Allah’s command to Satan and two fiqh cases, showing how scholars derive rulings from the speaker’s intent rather than the bare words.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal impossibility
Examine the text (e.g., command to Satan) and determine why the literal meaning cannot be intended. Consider the nature of the addressee and the act commanded.
Satan cannot command sin and disbelief because that authority belongs to Allah alone.
2
Determine the speaker's plausible purpose
Based on context and the impossibility, infer what the speaker likely intended. Is the command a granting of permission, empowerment, or a conditional threat?
In the Quranic example, the command is reinterpreted as granting Satan the ability to tempt (imkan al-fi'l).
3
Derive the ruling by analogy
Apply the same reasoning to similar textual problems. For instance, a man swearing 'I will not have lunch' when invited is understood as refusing that specific invitation, not all lunches.
The speaker's intent (rejecting the call) overrides the literal generality.
4
Test with the conditional divorce example
A husband says 'If you go out, you are divorced' just as his wife stands to leave. The literal condition is 'going out', but the speaker's purpose is to prevent her immediate departure, so the divorce takes effect at once.
The condition is fulfilled instantly due to the contextual indication.

The Meaning of the Letter Ba' in the Qur'anic Verse of Wiping the Head
Dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is/Malikis on the function of ba' in wudu verse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi position on the function of the letter ba' in the wudu verse and explain how it differs from the Shafi'i and Maliki positions, using linguistic arguments.

Core Idea
The Hanafi school holds that the ba' in the wudu verse indicates ilṣāq (adhesion), not tab'īḍ (partiality) or zā'ida (pleonastic).

This passage presents a foundational usul dispute about the meaning of a single preposition. The Hanafi position on the function of the letter ba' in the Qur'anic verse "wa amsaḥū bi-ru'ūsikum" has far-reaching implications for the obligatory act of wiping the head in wudu. This lesson will guide you through the linguistic analysis that determines whether the head must be fully wiped or only partially, and how this dispute exemplifies the Hanafi method of usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute and its sources
Identify the contested linguistic element: the letter ba' in the wudu verse (al-Mā'idah 6). Note the three schools involved: Hanafi (ilṣāq), Shafi'i (tab'īḍ), Maliki (zā'ida). Recognize the practical consequence: the obligatory extent of wiping the head.
This is a classic example of ikhtilāf arising from linguistic analysis.
2
State the Hanafi position and its linguistic basis
The Hanafi position: ba' indicates ilṣāq (adhesion). Analyze the key example: 'masahtu al-ḥā'iṭa bi-yadī' (I wiped the wall with my hand) implies full coverage of the wall because ba' is attached to the instrument (yadī). In the verse, ba' is attached to the locus (ru'ūsikum), so the verb remains transitive to the instrument (hands), meaning the hands are stuck to the head without requiring full coverage.
The distinction between ba' attached to the instrument versus the locus is critical.
3
Analyze the Hanafi response to opponent proofs
The Hanafi response rejects the Shafi'i claim of tab'īḍ (partiality) as having no basis in the language; partiality arises only from custom (the hand does not cover the whole head). The Maliki claim of ba' being zā'ida (pleonastic) is also rejected because the primary meaning of ba' is al-ṣila (attachment), and abandoning it for emphasis is unnecessary.
The Hanafi argument relies on the principle that words have fixed primary meanings and only shift by necessity.
4
Derive the thamarah al-khilaf
Based on the Hanafi analysis, wiping the head does not require full coverage; the obligatory extent is what is customary with the hand, which is at least a quarter of the head. For Shafi'i, any part of the head, even a hair, suffices because ba' indicates partiality. For Maliki, full head wiping is required because ba' is redundant, making the verb directly transitive.
This practical difference affects the validity of wudu across schools.
5
Apply the method to a related case
The passage applies the same linguistic analysis to conditional statements about divorce and permission. For example, 'You are divorced by the will of Allah' is interpreted as conditional because ba' as ilṣāq connotes attachment to a condition. Similarly, 'If you leave except with my permission' requires repetition of permission because ba' as ilṣāq attaches the exception to the permission, not to the leaving.
This shows the consistency of Hanafi usul across different legal contexts.

Core Idea
Dalalat al-Nass derives rulings from linguistic meaning, not juristic reasoning—stronger than qiyas.

In Hanafi usul, the text (nass) can indicate a ruling in multiple ways: explicit statement (ʻibārah), allusion (ishārah), and implication (dalālah). This lesson focuses on dalālah, the meaning that is linguistically implied by the wording even if not directly stated. Understanding this category is crucial because it carries higher authority than analogy (qiyas), allowing it to establish even hudud and kaffarat. Classification of Legal Evidence: Binding vs Non-Binding Reports
Explains conditions for three types of testimony and report acceptance.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the third, fourth, and fifth categories of legal evidence (bayyinah) and apply the Hanafi conditions for accepting testimony versus reports.

Core Idea
Binding evidence requires strict conditions; non-binding reports require only discernment (tamyiz).

This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul classifies legal evidence into five categories, focusing on the conditions for accepting testimony (shahada) versus reports (akhbar). It illustrates how the Hanafi school calibrates evidentiary requirements based on the binding nature of the outcome. We will walk through the key distinctions between the third, fourth, and fifth categories.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational proof for bayyinah
The author begins by stating that evidence (bayyinah) is only valid by a text that has no ambiguity, citing Quran 4:15 which commands calling four witnesses for accusations of lewdness. This establishes testimony as a valid legal proof.
The verse is used as the basis for the general category of evidence.
2
Observe Abu Hanifa's strict approach to hudud
The passage notes that Abu Hanifa did not establish the hadd punishment for sodomy by analogy (qiyas) nor by a solitary strange report (khabar gharib). This illustrates that for hudud, only the strongest evidence (clear text with high certainty) is accepted.
Khabar gharib is a hadith reported by a single narrator at some level; it is not considered sufficient for mandatory punishments in the Hanafi school.
3
Examine the third category (testimony / shahada)
Testimony is binding (ilzam) and requires: the specific wording of shahada, the prescribed number of witnesses, and capacity through guardianship (wilayah) when possible. These strict conditions safeguard against forgery and protect inviolable rights. Example: testimony for the crescent of Eid al-Fitr.
This category is the most rigorous because it imposes obligations on others.
4
Examine the fourth category (reports / akhbar)
Reports are accepted with only the condition of discernment (tamyiz), not moral probity ('adala). Thus, reports from children and non-Muslims are allowed in matters such as agency (wakalah), mudarabah, messages, and permissions. The reasons: necessity and the fact that these reports are non-binding.
Contrast: in matters of religion (e.g., purity of water) stricter conditions apply because they involve binding religious duties.
5
Note the fifth category (knowledge by other means)
This includes cases where knowledge is attained through other indicators, such as the dismissal of an agent, restriction of a minor, or a virgin's silence regarding her guardian's marriage arrangement. These are situations where the legal effect is known through contextual evidence.

Categories of the Prophet's Actions and the Default Ruling of Ibaha

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the four rooted categories of the Prophet's actions, define zalla and distinguish it from sin, and explain why the Hanafi school adopts ibaha as the default ruling for his actions.

Core Idea
Not all Prophet actions are binding: lapses are excluded, and permissibility is the default until evidence proves obligation or merit.

In this lesson, we examine how Hanafi usul categorizes the actions of the Prophet Muhammad (saw). Beyond the well-known four types (mubah, mustahab, wajib, fard), there is a special category called zalla (lapse) that is deliberately excluded from emulation. Critically, we explore the scholarly disagreement about the default ruling on his other actions and why the Hanafi school (via al-Karkhi) holds that permissibility (ibaha) is the baseline.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four main categories
The text lists four types: mubah (permissible), mustahab (recommended), wajib (obligatory), and fard (mandatory). Note that in Hanafi usul, wajib and fard are distinct levels.
These are the usual shari'a categories applied to the Prophet's actions.
2
Understand the zalla and its exclusion
Zalla is a fifth category but is excluded from legal emulation because it is an unintended slip from a permissible act into a forbidden one. The Prophet's zalla are not a source of law.
Examples from the Quran: Adam's disobedience and Moses' killing of the Copt.
3
Distinguish zalla from sin (ma'siya)
Sin is an intended forbidden act, while zalla is unintentional. Neither is suitable for emulation, but zalla is not sinful in the same sense.
This distinction is crucial for understanding the Prophet's infallibility.
4

For actions not due to forgetfulness or innate nature, some scholars suspend judgment (wuqoof), others require following (ittiba'). Al-Karkhi (Hanafi) asserts ibaha (permissibility) as the default.
The dispute revolves around whether an action is automatically meritorious or obliging.
5
Recognize the Hanafi position and its evidence
Al-Karkhi states that we hold ibaha; neither merit nor obligation to follow is established without independent evidence. This is the relied-upon view in the Hanafi school.
This principle restrains unwarranted obligations and allows flexibility.

Refuting the Denial of Rational Causes in Legal Causation
Usul dispute: must causes be rational?

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi argument that legal causes must be rationally understandable and evaluate the insufficiency of mere consistency (ṭard) as proof of causation.

Core Idea
A legal cause must have a rational meaning; mere consistency and absence of ruling cannot distinguish cause from condition.

This passage tackles a fundamental usul dispute: whether the ʿillah (legal cause) must be maʿqūl (rationally understandable) or can be a mere sign without rational meaning. The Hanafi school insists on rational meaning, drawing on the practice of the salaf and Quranic evidence. The author critiques the opposing view that consistency (ṭard) alone suffices, showing that it leads to uncertainty and fails to exclude conditions. Finally, the wudu verses are analyzed to demonstrate how the cause (ḥadath) is established through textual wording and rational inference.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core dispute
The opposing claim is that causes are mere signs (amārāt) that require no rational meaning. The Hanafi position is that they must have rational meaning because humans are obligated to relate rulings to causes in a rational manner.
The author begins: 'They are not necessitating, so we have no need for a rational meaning.' This sets up the refutation.
2
State the Hanafi response
The Law (sharʿ) made the original a witness, but that does not mean every word is a witness without rational meaning. Examples from practice: sale implies ownership, retribution implies killing. These relationships are rationally understood, even though the legal cause is made effective by divine law.
The author says: 'they were not necessitating in principle, but they were made necessitating legally for us, in a manner befitting them, which is relation.'
3
Critique of the consistency argument
The opponent relies on ṭard (consistency: the cause being present whenever the ruling is present) and ʿadam (absence of ruling when cause absent) to prove causation. The author refutes: ṭard does not distinguish cause from condition; it leads to ignorance because it cannot rule out a contradictory principle. ʿadam is nothing—absence of ruling may be due to another cause or condition.
The author asks: 'what tells you that there remains no contradictory or opposing principle?'
4
Respond to the angry judge hadith
Opponents cite the hadith 'The judge should not judge while angry' as evidence that the cause (preoccupation of heart) is not rationally known. The author responds: this is a rare condition, not a general principle, and the cause there is still rationally understood (preoccupation distracts).
The hadith is not a general rule; it is a specific condition.
5
Analyze the wudu verses
The cause of wudu (ḥadath) is established through indication of the text (dalālat al-naṣṣ) and phrasing, not through mere causation. The verse on tayammum links substitution to the original cause, and the phrase 'when you rise to prayer' is interpreted as 'from your sleeping places', which indicates sleep as a sign of ḥadath.
The author says: 'the text in the substitute is text for the original, because it differs from it in its state, not in its cause.'

Commentary
This passage from al-Pazdawi's Usul classifies acts of worship (ibadat) based on their relationship to the body, place, and purpose, and then discusses how mixing with worldly burdens (mu'nah) affects legal rulings, particularly regarding 'ushr and kharaj. Then it discusses worship with a burden aspect: Zakat al-Fitr is not pure worship, so capacity is not required. Conversely, 'ushr is a burden with worship meaning, so it is not initially imposed on a kafir, but Muhammad al-Shaybani allowed it to continue if the land was under Muslims. Kharaj is a burden with punitive meaning, so it is not imposed on Muslims initially but can remain. The dispute arises regarding the 'ushr on a kafir: Abu Hanifa holds it becomes kharaj (land tax) due to the lack of worship capacity; Abu Yusuf says it should be doubled (tada'uf) because kufr negates worship; Muhammad has two narrations. The preferred view (al-sahih) is Abu Hanifa's because doubling is a necessity not resorted to when the original (kharaj) is possible. Finally, khums (the fifth of spoils) is a right of Allah that remains His; the ruler distributes it, and it is permissible for Banu Hashim because it is not from impurities, and support (nusra) is the cause of entitlement.

The condition 'if you fear' is a real condition for qasr al-ahwal, not a customary tag to travel.

In the analysis of the Quranic verse on shortening prayer, a common mistake is to assume that the condition 'if you fear' is merely a reference to the context of travel. The Hanafi usul analysis reveals that this condition is intended in its true sense, and the ruling of shortening by gestures or lightening recitation is tied directly to the presence of fear, not to travel. This lesson will walk you through the textual evidence and derivation.

Descriptive Phrase vs. Legal Condition in Quranic Text
Identifying real condition in step-daughter marriage prohibition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a descriptive phrase and a legal condition in a Quranic verse and apply this distinction to derive the correct Hanafi ruling.

Core Idea
The phrase 'who are in your care' describes, not conditions; the real condition is consummation of the mother.

In this lesson, we examine a key usul al-fiqh skill: differentiating between a descriptive attribute and a legal condition in a revealed text. The Quranic verse on step-daughters (4:23) contains both elements, and the Hanafi analysis shows how careful attention to wording determines the ruling.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two key phrases in the verse
Locate the two relevant portions: (1) 'wa raba'ibukum allati fi hujurikum' (and your step-daughters who are in your care) and (2) 'fa in lam takunu dakhaltum bihinna' (if you have not entered into them).
The first phrase describes the step-daughters; the second introduces a condition.
2
Classify each phrase as description or condition
Determine whether the phrase imposes a legal effect or merely provides additional information. A condition must explicitly link the ruling to an event or state.
In Hanafi usul, a condition (shart) is what makes the ruling contingent; a description (wasf) does not alter the ruling by its presence or absence.
3
Apply the Hanafi principle for identifying conditions
The Hanafi rule: 'Al-amr bi al-shay' amr bi wasa'ilihi' is not relevant here; instead, use the principle that a true condition is indicated by a conditional particle like 'in' or 'law'.
The verse uses 'in' in 'fa in lam takunu', clearly indicating a conditional structure.
4
Derive the ruling
If the condition (no consummation with mother) is met, the step-daughter is permissible (halaal). If the mother was consummated, the step-daughter is prohibited (haraam).
The care (hujur) is irrelevant; it is simply a common scenario, not a legal requirement.

The Legal Effect of Prohibited Intoxication on Legal Capacity
Definition and usul rule on sukr mahzur

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that prohibited intoxication does not negate legal capacity (khitab) and apply the derived rule to the validity of legal utterances.

Core Idea
The drunkard remains addressable by the Law; his statements are valid unless intention is specifically required.

This passage from al-Bazdawi's usul defines prohibited intoxication and its legal effect. It clarifies that even in a state of prohibited intoxication, a person remains a fully accountable legal subject (mukallaf). The Qur'anic verse forbidding prayer while intoxicated proves that the drunkard is still addressed by divine commands. Consequently, all his legal acts — divorce, manumission, contracts, acknowledgments — are valid, with only intention (qasd) being negated.

Guided Path
1
Define prohibited intoxication (sukr mahzur)
Identify what constitutes sukr mahzur according to the text: intoxication from any prohibited drink, and from permissible drinks when consumed to intoxication (e.g., nabidh al-muthallath if drunk to intoxication). Note the condition.
2
Establish the consensus
The text states that by consensus (ijma), prohibited intoxication does not negate khitab (divine address). Understand that this is a settled usul principle.
3
Examine the Quranic proof
Analyze Surah al-Nisa' 4:43: 'Do not approach prayer while you are intoxicated.' The verse addresses the intoxicated person, proving he is still legally addressed. If it were addressed to the sober, the analogy with insanity shows that addressing the insane is meaningless.
4
Derive the ruling on legal capacity
Since the drunkard is addressed, he retains full ahliyyah. All his utterances (divorce, manumission, sales, acknowledgments) are valid. Only intention (qasd) is absent.
5
Apply to specific cases
Note exceptions: utterance of disbelief does not dissolve marriage (by istihsan), but forced conversion is valid. Hadd punishments for qadhf and zina are enforced; acknowledgment of drinking wine requires sobriety for punishment except for qadhf.

So all rulings of the Shari'ah are binding upon him, and all his expressions are valid with respect to divorce, manumission, sale, purchase, and acknowledgments. Only intention is negated by intoxication, not the expression itself. Hence if the intoxicated person utters a word of disbelief, his wife does not become separated from him, by istihsan. And if he embraces Islam, his Islam must be valid, like the Islam of one coerced. And if he acknowledges retaliation (qisas) or directly causes its cause, its ruling applies to him. If he makes a false accusation of adultery (qadhf) or acknowledges it, the hadd punishment is necessary for him, because intoxication is evidence of withdrawal, but that does not invalidate express withdrawal, so by its evidence even more so. If he commits zina while intoxicated, he is punished when he becomes sober. If he acknowledges that he drank wine willfully, he is not punished until he becomes sober, then acknowledges, or evidence is established against him. And if he acknowledges anything of the hudud, it is not enforced except for the hadd of qadhf.

Commentary
This passage defines prohibited intoxication (sukr mahzur) as intoxication from any forbidden drink, including from date wine or raisin wine that is drunk to intoxication. The author notes that Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf consider such drinks permissible in principle, but only if not drunk to intoxication; if they are, the resulting intoxication is treated legally like that from explicitly forbidden wine, as it incurs the hadd punishment. By consensus, this type of intoxication does not negate the divine address (khitab); the drunkard remains a legal subject. The Quranic verse forbidding prayer while intoxicated is cited as proof: whether addressed to the drunk or the sober, it shows legal address remains. Consequently, all rulings apply, and all legal utterances (divorce, manumission, contracts, acknowledgments) are valid. However, intention (qasd) is absent, so in a case of uttering disbelief, the wife does not become separated (by istihsan). Other rulings follow: forced conversion is valid, retaliation applies if he acknowledges or causes it, qadhf carries hadd, zina is punished upon sobriety, and acknowledgment of drinking requires sobriety for punishment except for qadhf which is enforced immediately.

Key Terms
خطاب (khitab): The divine address or command; the state of being legally addressed by the Shari'ah.
أهلية (ahliyyah): Legal capacity to acquire rights and obligations.
قصد (qasd): Intention or deliberate will; the text says it is negated by intoxication, not the expression itself.

Coercion and Its Effect on Permissibility: Between Rukhṣah and ʿAzīmah
Distinguishes coercion that permits a license from coercion that does not

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between coercion that permits a license (rukhṣah) and coercion that does not, and apply the Hanafi usul principle that determines when a primary ruling (ʿazīmah) may be overridden.

Core Idea
Coercion does not automatically permit the prohibited; it depends on whether the act involves harm to others or mere speech.

Coercion (ikrāh) is a recognized excuse in Hanafi law, but its effect varies. This passage from Usul al-Bazdawi explains why coercion to murder does not lift liability (qisas), whereas coercion to utter kufr permits a license. The key lies in the absolute sanctity of life versus the preservation of faith under duress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two cases contrasted
Notice the author contrasts coercion to kill (by imprisonment) with coercion to utter words of disbelief. For killing, the ruling is qisas (retaliation) even under coercion; for kufr, a license (rukhṣah) is granted.
This contrast is the core of the lesson.
2
Analyze the rationale for the difference
For killing, the prohibition of murder is absolute (ḥurma lā tuḥtamal) – it cannot be suspended by any excuse. For kufr, the license is granted because the heart remains faithful, and life is preserved.
The passage says: 'if he did not intend, but he moved away from it, then if he did it, it did not move, and it did not become a shubha.'
3
Locate the textual evidence
The Quranic verse (al-Nahl 16:106) and the hadith of ʿAmmār ibn Yāsir provide the basis for the license. The verse explicitly excludes coerced utterances from sin; the hadith shows the Prophet permitting repetition if coerced again.
These are the primary naqlī proofs.
4
Understand the scope of rukhṣah
The license does not abolish the original ruling (ʿazīmah). The story of Ḥubayb, who chose martyrdom over uttering kufr, shows that the license is not obligatory; one may choose to endure harm.
This preserves the principle that ʿazīmah remains the default.

Commentary
This passage from the commentary on Usul al-Bazdawi discusses the Hanafi principle that coercion (ikrāh) does not uniformly affect the legal ruling. It contrasts two cases: coercion to kill, where the prohibition of murder is absolute and cannot be overridden—hence the coerced killer remains liable for qisas—and coercion to utter words of disbelief (kufr), where a license (rukhṣah) is granted due to the overwhelming threat to life, as established by the Quranic verse (al-Nahl 16:106) revealed about ʿAmmār ibn Yāsir. The passage notes that the original ruling (ʿazīmah) of prohibiting kufr remains, but the license is exceptional. The footnotes provide the full narrations: the story of ʿAmmār, where the Prophet instructed him to repeat if coerced, and the story of Ḥubayb, who chose martyrdom over uttering kufr, showing that the license is not obligatory. The passage thus defines the boundary of ikrāh in Hanafi usul: coercion that eliminates consent (ikrāh muljiʾ) permits speech acts but not acts that harm others, like murder.

Key Terms
الإكراه (al-ikrāh): Coercion that compels a person to act against their will, typically under threat of death or severe harm.
الرخصة (al-rukhṣah): A dispensation or license that permits an otherwise prohibited act due to necessity or coercion.
العزيمة (al-ʿazīmah): The original, general ruling that applies without excuse.
