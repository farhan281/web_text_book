Time as Criterion vs Cause in Non-Determined Obligations
Explains the category of muwaqqata where time is criterion.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between time as a criterion (miyar) and time as a cause (sabab) in muwaqqata obligations, and derive the ruling on intention and loss.

Core Idea
When time is a criterion, not a cause, intention is required and the obligation has no loss.

In Usul al-Fiqh, obligations (wajibat) are sometimes bound to a specific time (muwaqqata). But not all time-bound obligations treat time as the cause of the obligation. This passage from al-Bazdawi explains a crucial distinction: when time is merely a criterion for performance, the rulings differ. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying intention requirements and determining whether an obligation can be made up later.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of time-bound obligations
Distinguish between time as cause (sabab) and time as criterion (miyar). The passage focuses on the latter.
Time as cause triggers obligation; time as criterion merely measures performance.
2
Understand the examples
Examples include making up missed Ramadan fasts and absolute vows (nadhr mutlaq). These have non-determined times.
Non-determined means any day can be chosen.
3
Analyze the legal effect: intention requirement
Because the time is only a criterion, intention (niyah) at the beginning is a condition (shart) for the act to count as the intended obligation.
Without intention, the fast may default to supererogatory.
4
Analyze the legal effect: no loss
Since the time is non-determined, there is no loss (fawat) if delayed; the obligation can be fulfilled later.
Loss (fawat) applies only when time is a cause and expires.

Core Idea
When default intention arises from the performer's obligation, explicit contrary intention invalidates it.

In this lesson, we examine a nuanced usul discussion from the Hanafi school: how default intention (ta'yin) operates in ihram when one owes the obligatory hajj. The text argues that the default specification arises from the performer's circumstance (owing Hajj al-Islam), not from the act itself. This principle explains why explicitly intending supererogatory invalidates the ihram, contrasting with Ramadan where the day is inherently specified. We also explore an analogy to local currency and a disagreement with al-Karkhi.

The Hanafi Rule on the Conjunction 'Waw': Absolute Conjunction Without Sequence
Determining the legal meaning of the conjunction 'waw'

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that 'waw' indicates absolute conjunction (mutlaq al-'atf) and distinguish it from interpretations that impose sequence or concurrence.

Core Idea
The 'waw' is legally neutral: it joins without specifying order or simultaneity.

In Arabic legal texts, the conjunction 'waw' (واو) appears frequently, but its precise legal meaning is a matter of usul al-fiqh. This passage from Kanz al-Wusul clarifies the Hanafi position that 'waw' is placed for absolute conjunction, akin to a generic noun, and does not inherently imply sequence or concurrence. Understanding this principle is crucial for correctly interpreting conditional statements and legal texts, as illustrated by the famous disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his students regarding the number of divorces in a conditional divorce formula.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic principle
Recognize that each conjunction particle (fa, thumma, waw) has a unique default meaning. The author states that 'fa' is for sequence, 'thumma' for delay, and 'waw' for absolute conjunction.
This sets the foundation for the Hanafi position.
2
Understand 'mutlaq al-'atf'
The 'waw' is placed for absolute conjunction, meaning it simply links two items without specifying any particular relationship. It is like a generic noun (e.g., 'raqaba') that is neither general nor ambiguous.
This is the key doctrine: 'waw' does not indicate sequence or concurrence by itself.
3
Analyze the divorce example
Consider the case: 'If you enter the house, you are divorced and divorced and divorced.' According to Abu Hanifa, only one divorce occurs because the second and third utterances are connected to the condition through intermediaries (the first utterance). The 'waw' does not alter this structural reality.
The disagreement with the Sahibayn is structural, not about the meaning of 'waw'.
4
Apply the principle to other cases
The author shows that in the verse of ablution, the 'waw' does not impose order or simultaneity. Similarly, in the case of freeing two slave women, the 'waw' does not determine the outcome; the structure of conditions does.
This demonstrates consistency across different fiqh chapters.
5
Conclude that 'waw' does not overrule context
The 'waw' is neutral; its presence does not change the underlying legal reasoning based on other factors (like intermediaries or incomplete sentences). The restricted (muqayyad) is not abandoned for the unrestricted (mutlaq).
This is a fundamental principle: the 'waw' does not add new meaning beyond conjunction.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed usul rule discussion about the meaning of the conjunction 'waw' (واو) in Arabic and its legal implications. The author begins by stating that each conjunction particle has its own distinct meaning: 'fa' (ف) for sequence, 'ma'a' (مع) for accompaniment, 'thumma' (ثم) for deferral and delay. The 'waw' is placed for absolute conjunction (مطلق العطف), covering all possible meanings without specifying any. To illustrate, the author compares 'waw' to a generic noun like 'raqaba' (neck) which is absolute—neither general (عام) nor ambiguous (مجمل). This is the basis for interpreting the verse of ablution (آية الوضوء) as merely requiring the actions without specifying whether they must be performed simultaneously or in sequence.

The author then addresses a misconception: some Hanafi scholars thought that 'waw' indicates concurrence (مقارنة), but that is incorrect. Others claimed that Abu Yusuf and Muhammad considered 'waw' to indicate concurrence, citing a divorce example. If a man says to his wife before consummation: 'If you enter the house, you are divorced and divorced and divorced' (فأنت طالق وطالق وطالق), the three divorces are effective upon entry. Abu Hanifa rules that only one divorce occurs, while Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule that three occur. The objectors thought this shows Abu Hanifa interprets 'waw' as sequential (الترتيب), but the author explains that the real difference lies elsewhere. According to Abu Hanifa, the second and third utterances are connected to the condition through intermediaries, so they do not produce new divorces; the 'waw' does not affect this. According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, the second and third utterances are incomplete sentences that combine with the first into a single declaration, so all three take effect. The 'waw' itself does not impose sequence or concurrence; the difference stems from how the utterances are structured.

The author reinforces that the 'waw' does not specify sequence or concurrence, so it does not override the underlying principle. The principle is that the restricted (المقيد) is not abandoned for the unrestricted (المطلق). Additional examples from marriage law are provided: if a man says to his wife 'You are divorced, and divorced, and divorced' before consummation, the ruling is separation by one divorce (this is consistent with Abu Hanifa's view). In the case of marrying two slave women without permission, if the master frees them simultaneously in one utterance (e.g., 'This one is free and this one is free' connected by 'waw'), the second marriage is invalidated. This shows that even when the 'waw' connects two emancipations, it does not necessarily indicate sequence but rather the underlying structure of the conditions determines the outcome. The author concludes that the 'waw' is neutral and does not alter the legal consequences beyond what the context and structure require.

ترتيب (tartib): Sequence or order; the passage discusses whether 'waw' necessitates a sequential order between the conjoined items.
مطلق العطف (mutlaq al-'atf): Absolute conjunction; the 'waw' simply joins two items without specifying the nature of the relationship.

The Waw in Utterances of Combined Items
Clarifying that certain waw constructions are not based on conjunction rules

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when a waw conjunction in a number+item phrase is not a true conjunction and apply the Hanafi principle that such phrases denote a single unit.

Core Idea
A hundred and a dirham means one hundred dirhams—the waw is not conjunctive but part of a unitary expression.

Commentary
This passage discusses the Hanafi doctrine of khilafah (substitution) in the context of tayammum. The author states the preferred Hanafi view that tayammum is an absolute substitute (khalaf mutlaq), meaning it can replace wudu for all prayers until the excuse is removed. Hence one may pray multiple obligatory prayers with a single tayammum. Regarding the two vessels (a scenario where one has both water and earth but cannot determine which is ritually pure), the Hanafi ruling is not to speculate (taharri) because earth is considered a pure purifier only when water is unavailable, and the inability due to uncertainty is established by conflicting evidence. The key ikhtilaf (disagreement) between Imam Abu Hanifa and Imam Abu Yusuf on one side, and Imam Muhammad and Imam Zufar on the other, concerns the exact nature of the substitution.
