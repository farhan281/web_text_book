Did the Prophet Use Ijtihad? The Hanafi Position
Debates whether the Prophet used personal opinion in legal rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three opinions on the Prophet's ijtihad, identify the Hanafi position, and explain its proof, distinguishing it from the other views.

Core Idea
The Prophet used ijtihad only after waiting for revelation — a unique Hanafi reconciliation.

This lesson examines a foundational debate in usul al-fiqh: did the Prophet Muhammad (SAW) engage in ijtihad (independent reasoning) to derive legal rulings? The passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul presents three opinions, and the Hanafi school adopts a nuanced middle position. By understanding this debate, you will grasp how the Hanafi school validates analogical reasoning (qiyas) while preserving the primacy of revelation.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish the two types of revelation
Identify the difference between manifest revelation (al-wahy al-zahir) and inner revelation (al-wahy al-batin). Manifest revelation is directly communicated; inner revelation is attained through ijtihad and reflection on texts.
This distinction is key to understanding the dispute.
2
State the three opinions
List the three views: (1) The Prophet did not use ijtihad at all; (2) The Prophet used both revelation and ijtihad without restriction; (3) The Prophet used ijtihad only after a waiting period for revelation.
The third is the Hanafi position.
3
Analyze the proof of the first opinion
Examine the verse 'Nor does he speak from desire' (53:3-4) and the argument that ijtihad is prone to error. Understand why this group denies ijtihad entirely.
This group restricts the Prophet solely to manifest revelation.
4
Analyze the proof of the second opinion
Examine the verse commanding 'consideration' (59:2), the story of Solomon (21:79), and the hadith of al-Khath'amiyyah. Understand how they argue that the Prophet used both revelation and opinion freely.
This group sees no need for a waiting period.
5
Synthesize the Hanafi position and its tarjih
Understand how the Hanafi view reconciles the two extremes: the Prophet is commanded to wait for revelation first, and only after a period does he exercise ijtihad. This preserves infallibility and validates ijtihad for later scholars.
The author states this is the correct view in our school.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul discusses the nature of revelation (wahi) and whether the Prophet Muhammad (SAW) engaged in ijtihad (independent legal reasoning). The passage first distinguishes between two types of revelation: manifest revelation (wahi zahir), which is directly conveyed and requires reflection to perceive its truth, and inner revelation (wahi batin), which is attained through ijtihad and reflection on textual rulings. The author then presents three opinions on whether the Prophet used ijtihad. The first opinion denies it entirely: the Prophet only received manifest revelation, and ijtihad is for his community. They cite the verse 'Nor does he speak from desire' to argue that all his speech is revelation, and that ijtihad is prone to error, thus unsuitable for establishing divine law. They distinguish matters of war, which involve human interests and can be decided by opinion. The second opinion affirms that the Prophet used both revelation and opinion. They cite the verse commanding 'consideration' (i'tibar), arguing that the Prophet is most entitled to it, as well as stories of Prophet Solomon judging by opinion and the hadith of al-Khath'amiyyah where the Prophet analogizes debt to religious obligations. The third opinion, which the author states is the correct view in the Hanafi school, is that the Prophet was commanded to wait for revelation regarding new incidents, and only after the waiting period did he act by opinion. This view reconciles the other two: the Prophet did not initiate legislation by opinion, but in the absence of revelation, after a period of waiting, he could use ijtihad. The proofs for the second opinion are also considered relevant, but the key point is the sequence: waiting first, then ijtihad. This discussion is foundational in Hanafi usul al-fiqh, highlighting the role of the Prophet's ijtihad and its limits, and contrasting with those who deny it entirely. It also relates to the broader debate about the role of reason (ra'y) in Islamic law.

Key Terms
الوحي الظاهر (al-waḥy al-ẓāhir): Manifest revelation directly communicated to the Prophet, clear and perceived through reflection.
الوحي الباطن (al-waḥy al-bāṭin): Inner revelation attained through ijtihad and reflection on textual rulings.
الاجتهاد (al-ijtihād): The exercise of personal opinion (ra'y) to derive rulings when no explicit text is available, after waiting for revelation.

The Rule of Consultation and Ijtihad in the Absence of Revelation
Stating the permissibility and conditions of consultation and ijtihad

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify when consultation (shura) is permissible according to Hanafi usul, explain the role of the Prophet's infallibility in ijtihad, and apply the principle that revelation overrides opinion.

Core Idea
Consultation is permissible only when no revelation exists; the Prophet's ijtihad is infallible, but waiting for revelation is preferred.

This passage from Usul al-Bazdawi clarifies the scope of consultation (shura) in Islamic law. It establishes a clear principle: consultation is permissible only in matters where there is no direct divine revelation (wahi). The Prophet's own examples illustrate that even he engaged in consultation with his companions on matters of opinion (ra'y), such as the captives of Badr and the defense of Medina. The passage also addresses the Prophet's infallibility (isma) in his ijtihad decisions, emphasizing that while his ijtihad is always correct, the preferred approach is to wait for revelation when possible.

Guided Path
1
Identify the examples of consultation from the Prophet's life
The passage cites the Prophet consulting the companions about the captives of Badr and about giving half of Medina's dates to the attacking tribe during the Battle of the Confederates. List these examples and note what the Prophet did after consultation.
The examples show the Prophet engaging in shura in matters of human judgment.
2
Extract the general principle about when consultation is permissible
Look for the explicit statement: 'Consultation is not permissible when revelation is present; rather, consultation is permissible only in acting by opinion.' State this principle in your own words.
This is the core rule.
3
Analyze the Quranic evidence cited (Surah al-Anfal 8:68)
The verse indicates that the Prophet's decision to pardon the captives was not a mistake because a prior divine decree made it valid. This shows that the Prophet's ijtihad (based on consultation) was aligned with divine will, but not because it was a substitute for revelation—rather, because there was no specific revelation on that matter.
The verse is used to demonstrate that consultation in matters of opinion can be correct.
4
Understand the exception for the Prophet: his infallibility in ijtihad
The passage states that the Prophet is ma'sum (protected) from error in his decisions, so his ijtihad is always correct. For others, ijtihad may contain error. However, this does not negate the principle that consultation is only for matters without revelation.
This is a key distinction between the Prophet and others.
5
Determine the preferred approach (mufta bihi): prioritizing waiting for revelation
Despite the Prophet's infallibility, the preferred practice (اخترنا تقديم انتظار الوحي) is to wait for revelation when possible, because revelation is superior and more certain. This shows the high status of wahy over ra'y.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on textual evidence.

Commentary
This passage is a usul rule discussion from Usul al-Bazdawi. It argues that consultation (shura) is only valid when there is no direct revelation (wahi). The Prophet's consultation with his companions about the captives of Badr and about the Confederates shows that even the Prophet engaged in consultation in matters of opinion. The Quranic verse (8:68) indicates that the Prophet's decision to pardon the captives was not wrong, but a decree from Allah. The principle is stated: 'Consultation is not permissible when revelation is present; rather, consultation is permissible only in acting by opinion.' However, the Prophet is infallible (ma'sum) from error in his ijtihad, while others are not. Therefore, the Prophet's ijtihad is always correct. Despite this, the preferred approach (اخترنا تقديم انتظار الوحي) is to wait for revelation because the Prophet is honored with it and it suffices over opinion. The passage cites examples from the Prophet's life and provides hadith references via footnotes.

Key Terms
الشورى (al-shura): The act of consultation in matters where there is no direct revelation; permissible only in the realm of opinion (ra'y).
الرأي (al-ra'y): Personal opinion or ijtihad based on reasoning, used when no explicit textual evidence is present.
الوحي (al-wahi): Divine revelation to prophets; the primary source of Islamic law that takes precedence over opinion.
المعصوم (al-ma'sum): One who is divinely protected from error, specifically referring to the Prophet regarding his decisions in religious matters.

Proof for the Specific Authority of the Companions (Sahabah)
Dispute over the specific authority of the Sahabah with hadith evidence.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the hadith evidence that the Hanafi school uses to establish the specific authority (khusus) of the Sahabah, and explain the thamarah al-khilaf of this dispute.

Core Idea
The elevated authority of Abu Bakr and Umar is proven by explicit prophetic commands, binding in usul.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, the statements and actions of the Companions (Sahabah) carry special weight, especially those of the first two caliphs. This passage from al-Bazdawi's Kanz al-Wusul presents multiple hadiths as evidence for this specific authority (khusus). Understanding this evidence is crucial for grasping why the Hanafi school prioritizes qawl al-sahabi as a source of law when no clear text exists.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
State the core dispute: whether the Sahabah have a specific, elevated authority (khusus) beyond general believers in matters of religion and leadership.
2
Locate the Hanafi proof (dalil)
Identify the primary hadith cited: 'اقتدوا بالذين من بعدي أبي بكر وعمر' (Follow those after me: Abu Bakr and Umar). Note its authenticity and chain.
The hadith is narrated by Hudhayfah and declared hasan sahih by al-Tirmidhi.
3
Examine additional supporting hadiths
Consider the other hadiths presented: the 'stars' hadith, the hadith on the rightly-guided caliphs, and the numerous virtues of individual Companions. Understand how each reinforces the special status of the Sahabah.
4
Analyze the Hanafi response to opponents
Contrast the Hanafi position with those who deny specific authority. The Hanafi response relies on the abundance of explicit commands and the consensus of the Ummah.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
State the legal impact: accepting the qawl of a Sahabi as hujjah (proof) when there is no clear text, versus not accepting it.

Textual Analysis of Riba Hadith and Derivation of Ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi method of istinbat to derive the ruling that exchanging like commodities requires equality in kind and measure, and identify the textual operations (takhsis, bayan, taqyid) used.

Core Idea
The command to exchange like for like is obligatory; the condition 'like for like' is a binding requirement, and any excess is prohibited riba.

In this passage, al-Pazdawi demonstrates the Hanafi method of istinbat (deriving rulings) from a prophetic hadith. By analyzing the linguistic structure of the riba hadith—especially the implicit command and the circumstantial condition—he extracts a detailed ruling that governs exchanges of like commodities. This lesson will walk you through each step of his textual analysis, showing how commands, conditions, and definitions yield fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
Identify the implicit command
The phrase 'al-hintatu bil-hintati' (wheat for wheat) contains the letter ba', which indicates attachment and implies an implicit verb. The author argues this verb is an imperative: 'sell wheat for wheat'. Recognize that the ba' triggers an implicit command.
The author cites corroborating hadiths that use explicit prohibitions (e.g., 'do not sell') to reinforce the imperative reading.
2

The Principle of Tarjih: Preponderance by Strengthening Attribute
Stating the rule that preponderance does not occur by an independent cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi rule that preponderance (tarjih) requires a strengthening attribute (wasf) and not an independent cause (illa), and apply it to examples from witness testimony, wounds, pre-emption, and inheritance.

Core Idea
Preponderance requires qualitative strength, not mere quantity: an independent cause cannot outrank another.

In Hanafi usul, when two pieces of evidence conflict, we do not simply count which has more supporting factors. The key is whether one factor is an attribute that strengthens the core meaning without being able to stand alone. This passage, from a classical usul work, explains this rule through vivid examples like witnesses, wounds, and pre-emption. It then examines the dispute with al-Shafi'i and shows how the rule governs inheritance cases. Mastering this principle is essential for correctly weighing evidence in legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Extract the core principle
Identify the rule stated: preponderance does not occur by what is suitable as an independent cause (illa), but only by a description (wasf) that strengthens the meaning of the pillar (rukn).
This is the foundational premise of the passage.
2
Analyze the hadith proof and its implication
The Prophet's command to 'weigh and give extra' (zin wa arjih) shows that the extra weight is a preponderating description, not a separate gift (hiba). This illustrates that a factor that could serve as an independent cause (the extra weight as a gift) is not used for tarjih.
The hadith is a dalil from Sunnah ahad, transmitted with zanni thubut.
3
Apply the principle to quantitative examples
In the examples of witnesses (two vs. four) and wounds (one vs. multiple), adding more of the same kind does not create preponderance because each unit is an independent cause (illa). They do not become a strengthening attribute (wasf) for the other.
This shows that numerical increase does not produce qualitative strengthening.
4
Examine the dispute with al-Shafi'i on pre-emption
In pre-emption, two pre-emptors with unequal ownership are equal in entitlement under Hanafi law because each part of the share is an independent cause. Al-Shafi'i disagreed, analogizing pre-emption to appendages of ownership. The passage critiques this as an error.

This ruling is attributed to al-Muntaqa, a work of the Hanafi school, without distinguishing the remoteness of time.

Nature of Qasr: Original Ruling or Concession?
Dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is on whether qasr is original or a concession

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the Hanafi and Shafi'i positions on whether qasr is an original ruling (asl) or a concession (rukhsa) by analyzing the hadith of Aisha and the usul principles involved.

Core Idea
Qasr is not a concession — it is the original duty; travel simply maintains the two-rak'ah baseline.

This lesson examines a classic usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools over the legal nature of shortening prayer during travel. The Hanafi school argues that qasr is the original ruling (asl), supported by the hadith of Aisha indicating prayer was initially prescribed as two rak'ahs. The Shafi'i school views it as a concession (rukhsa) that does not abrogate the original four-rak'ah obligation. Understanding this distinction has practical implications for when and how the traveler prays.

Guided Path
1
State the central issue
Identify the exact point of disagreement: Is the shortening of prayer during travel an original ruling (asl) that always applies, or a concession (rukhsa) that temporarily lightens the original obligation?
This is a dispute over legal categorization, not over the permissibility of shortening.
2
Articulate the Hanafi position and proof
According to the text, travel is a cause for reduction originally (sabab li'l-wad' aslan). The Hanafis cite the hadith of Aisha that prayer was prescribed as two rak'ahs and maintained in travel, increased in residence. This shows qasr is the original (asl).
The hadith is considered zanni al-thubut but qati al-dalala.
3
Present the Shafi'i counter-position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that travel is a cause for concession (rukhsa), so the original obligation of four rak'ahs remains but is temporarily waived. He analogizes this to the fasting traveler who may defer fasting.
The Shafi'i view relies on qiyas between prayer and fasting.
4
Analyze the Hanafi dalil
Examine the hadith: 'فرضت الصلاة ركعتين ركعتين' (Prayer was prescribed as two rak'ahs each). The word 'فرضت' indicates the original fard. The phrase 'فأُقِرَّت في السفر' shows that in travel this original was kept, while 'وزيدت في الحضر' shows increase was added only in residence. Thus qasr is the asl.
The wajh al-dalalah is the explicit mention of the original prescription and its maintenance in travel.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence)
For Hanafis, the traveler must shorten (it is wajib), because it is the original ruling regardless of hardship. For Shafi'is, shortening is a concession that is permissible but not mandatory; the traveler may choose to complete four if able.
This difference affects the traveler's default practice.

Prohibitions That Do Not Lift but Allow Concession
Distinguishing prohibitions that allow concession without lifting the original ruling

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between prohibitions that are lifted under duress and those that only allow concession, and apply the Hanafi rule to classify a given scenario.

Core Idea
A prohibition that allows concession is not lifted; the original ruling remains binding in principle.

In Hanafi usul, compulsion (ikrah) does not automatically make the prohibited permissible. Some prohibitions are so severe that even under threat, no concession is given. Others allow a temporary concession for the outward act while the original ruling stands. This passage explains the second category using the cases of uttering disbelief under duress and the martyrdom of Habib.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Note that the passage distinguishes between prohibitions that are 'lifted' (yasqut) and those that 'do not lift but allow concession' (la yasqut wa yahtamil al-rukhsa). The first category is exemplified by compelled killing (qisas applies), the second by uttering kufr under duress.
In the first category, the prohibition transfers (intaqala) to the compelled person but does not become permissible.
2
Locate the evidence for the concession
Read the story of Ammar ibn Yasir (ra) and the Quranic verse (16:106) cited. The Prophet (saw) told Ammar: 'If they return, then return' – indicating a concession for outward utterance when the heart is firm.
The verse explicitly makes an exception for those compelled while their hearts are tranquil in faith.
3
Locate the evidence for the remaining azima
Read the hadith of Habib (ra) who chose martyrdom over uttering kufr. This shows that the original prohibition (azima) remains in effect and is the ideal, even though a concession exists.
Habib's act is praised, proving that the prohibition is not lifted – otherwise his martyrdom would not be virtuous.
4
Apply the distinction
Contrast with the case of compelled killing: there, no concession is given at all; the prohibition is absolute and qisas applies. Understand why the two cases differ (the nature of the act and the severity of harm).
Killing an innocent person cannot be allowed even outwardly because it involves harm to others, whereas uttering kufr involves only one's own speech.
5
State the Hanafi rule
Formulate the rule: For certain prohibitions, the original ruling (azima) remains binding in principle, but a temporary concession (rukhsa) is granted for the outward act under duress. The concession does not nullify the prohibition.
This is a key usul principle that balances necessity and preservation of the Shari'ah.
