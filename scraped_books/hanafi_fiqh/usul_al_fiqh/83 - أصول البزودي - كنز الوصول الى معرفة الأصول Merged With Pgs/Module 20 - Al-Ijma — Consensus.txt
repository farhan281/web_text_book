Unacceptable Objections (Ta'n) in Hanafi Usul
Classifying unacceptable objections to narrators/scholars

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify which types of objections against narrators/scholars are invalid in Hanafi usul and explain why they are rejected.

Core Idea
Objections based on ijtihadi matters or personal bias are rejected; only specific, unbiased accusations matter.

In Hanafi usul, not every criticism of a narrator or scholar is accepted. The school carefully distinguishes between valid grounds for rejecting a narrator (fisq, jarh) and subjective or ijtihadi matters that do not affect reliability. This lesson unpacks the two main categories of unacceptable ta'n: objections based on matters open to ijtihad (like irsal) and objections driven by partisanship or enmity. Understanding this principle is crucial for evaluating hadith and scholarly authority within the Hanafi method.

Guided Path
1
Classify the two categories of unacceptable ta'n
Identify the two broad reasons an objection may be rejected: (1) the objection is based on a matter of ijtihad (e.g., irsal), and (2) the objection, even if specific, is motivated by bias or enmity.
The text gives examples: irsal and excessive derivation of fiqh branches are ijtihadi; objections of atheists or partisans are biased.
2
Apply the rule to the ijtihadi objection
Recognize that Hanafi usul considers matters like irsal as non-defects because the school accepts mursal hadith under conditions. Therefore, objecting on that basis is invalid.
Compare with Shafi'i position that often treats irsal as a defect – we'll note that as ikhtilaf.
3
Apply the rule to biased objection
Even if the objection is specific and involves fisq/jarh, if the objector is motivated by asabiyya or personal enmity, it is not heard. The example given is Shafi'i partisans attacking Hanafi scholars.
This ensures that personal vendettas do not corrupt the process of jarh wa ta'dil.
4
Contrast with acceptable ta'n
Acceptable objections must be specific (mufassar), based on clear fisq or jarh, and made without bias. The text implies this by setting the condition for rejection.
This step shows the positive condition: valid ta'n must be mufassar and free from bias.
5
Understand the scope of the rule
The author notes that there are many possible grounds for valid objections (up to forty), but this book cannot cover them all. The student should seek them in specialized works.
This indicates that the principle here is a meta-rule for filtering objections, not an exhaustive list.

Commentary
This passage from Usul al-Bazdawi discusses two core principles. First, it outlines when objections (ta'n) against narrators or scholars are unacceptable in the Hanafi madhhab. Key Terms
الطعن (al-ta'n): Objection or criticism against a narrator's reliability or a scholar's methodology; must be specific and not based on ijtihadi matters or bias.
الإرسال (al-irsal): Narrating a hadith with a missing link between the Successor and the Prophet (i.e., omitting the Companion).
المفسر (al-mufassar): Specific, detailed accusation; as opposed to a general or vague objection.
العصبية (al-'asabiyya): Partisanship or group loyalty that biases judgment.

The Principle of Non-Contradiction in Shari'ah Proofs
Asserting the coherence of Shari'ah proofs and introducing abrogation as the key to resolving apparent conflicts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the principle that Shari'ah proofs do not contradict, and identify the proper resolution of apparent contradictions through abrogation.

Core Idea
Shari'ah proofs never truly contradict; apparent contradiction arises from human ignorance of abrogation.

A common misunderstanding among students of usul is that Quranic verses or hadith sometimes conflict. This lesson, drawn from Usul al-Bazdawi, clarifies that such contradiction is only apparent. The author asserts that proofs from the Book and Sunnah cannot contradict each other in themselves, because that would imply divine incapacity—a notion impossible for Allah. Rather, any perceived contradiction stems from our ignorance of which ruling is abrogated (nasikh) and which is abrogated (mansukh). Thus, the proper response is to study the science of abrogation. This principle is foundational for reconciling seemingly conflicting texts.

Guided Path
1
Recognize the claim: proofs do not contradict
The passage opens by stating that the proofs whose aspects were mentioned do not contradict each other in themselves (في أنفسها) and do not conflict in placement. This is a strong assertion of coherence.
The wording 'في أنفسها' means in their own essence, not just in our understanding.
2
Understand the reason: contradiction would imply divine incapacity
The author gives a rational argument: contradiction would be a sign of incapacity (من أمارات العجز), and Allah is exalted above that. Therefore, true contradiction cannot exist.
This is an aqli (rational) dalil, not naqli.
3
Identify the actual cause of apparent contradictions
Despite the principle, we do observe texts that seem to conflict. The author attributes this to 'our ignorance of the abrogating and the abrogated' (جهلنا بالناسخ من المنسوخ).
This shifts the problem from the texts to our knowledge: reconciliation requires identifying which text came later and supersedes the earlier.
4
Conclude the necessity of studying nasikh wa mansukh
Because the solution lies in knowing abrogation, the author announces that it is necessary to explain this topic (فلا بدَّ من بيان هذه الجملة). This leads to the upcoming chapter on contradiction (باب المعارضة).
The passage serves as an introduction to the study of ta'arud and its resolution.
5
Apply the principle to a sample apparent contradiction
For example, the verses on gradual prohibition of alcohol. The earlier verses allowing it are abrogated by the later verse of absolute prohibition. Without knowledge of naskh, one might think the texts contradict.
This step grounds the abstract principle in a classic example.

Bayan al-Taghyir: Definition and Two Types
Defines bayan al-taghyir and its two types

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define bayan al-taghyir, identify its two types (ta'liq bi'l-shart and istithna'), and state the condition of connectivity that distinguishes it from naskh.

Core Idea
Bayan al-taghyir modifies rulings at inception; it is valid only when connected, otherwise it becomes naskh.

In the previous lesson, we learned about bayan (clarification) as a broad category. Now we delve into a specific subtype: bayan al-taghyir (modificatory clarification). This passage from al-Bazdawi defines its two forms and sets the critical condition that separates it from naskh. Understanding this distinction is essential for properly applying conditional clauses and exceptions in legal texts.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition of bayan al-taghyir
Read the first line: 'Bayan al-taghyir is of two types: suspension by condition, and exception.' Note that the author defines it by its types, not by a single sentence.
The name 'taghyir' (change) hints at its effect: modifying the ruling at its inception.
2
Identify the two types and their examples
For ta'liq bi'l-shart, consider the example: a master says to his slave 'You are free' but adds a condition (e.g., 'if I enter the house'). The emancipation is suspended until the condition occurs. For istithna', consider 'I owe you a thousand dirhams except five hundred.' The exception modifies the amount owed.
Both types prevent the full legal effect of the initial utterance from the very beginning.
3
Analyze the condition of connectivity (ittisal)
The passage states that both types are valid only when connected (mawsulan) to the original speech. If disconnected (munfasil), they would become naskh (abrogation), not bayan. This is based on consensus (ijma') of the jurists.
Connectivity means the condition or exception must be uttered immediately after or within the same context as the main speech; a delay breaks the connection.
4
Distinguish bayan al-taghyir from naskh
The key difference: taghyir modifies the ruling at the moment the speech is uttered, preventing full effect; naskh abrogates a ruling after it has been in effect. The author emphasizes: 'modification after existence is naskh, not bayan.'
Taghyir is a kind of bayan; naskh is not bayan but an independent operation.
5
Understand the rationale for the name
The author explains that both types are called 'taghyir' because they change the initial meaning or effect of the speech. They prevent the speech from being an immediate, unconditional enactment. This is opposed to 'tabdil' (substitution), which would replace the ruling entirely.
The term 'taghyir' encompasses both suspension and exception as modifications at inception.

Commentary
This passage defines a specific type of bayan (clarification) called bayan al-taghyir (modificatory clarification). The author states it has two forms: ta'liq bi'l-shart (suspension by a condition) and istithna' (exception). Both are valid only when connected (mawsulan) to the original speech; if disconnected, they would constitute naskh (abrogation) rather than bayan. The term 'taghyir' refers to the effect of modifying the initial ruling at its inception, unlike naskh which changes it after establishment. For ta'liq, the example is a master saying to his slave 'You are free' conditioned on something; the condition prevents immediate emancipation. For istithna', the example is 'I owe you a thousand dirhams except five hundred'; the exception modifies the number. Both operations prevent the full effect of the speech from the beginning, so they are called taghyir, not tabdil (substitution). The passage also notes consensus (ijma') that connection is required.

Key Terms
التغيير (al-taghyir): Modification of speech at its inception, preventing full effect without revoking after existence.
التعليق بالشرط (al-ta'liq bi'l-shart): Suspending a ruling on a condition, so the ruling does not take effect until the condition occurs.
الاستثناء (al-istithna'): Exception from a general statement, removing part of the intended subject.
النسخ (al-naskh): Abrogation of a ruling after it has been established, as opposed to taghyir which prevents establishment.
البيان (al-bayan): Clarification that manifests the intent of speech; includes both initial statement and modificatory types.

Ijmāʿ Sukūtī: Tacit Consensus in Hanafi Usul
Praise of Hanafi school and introduction of ijma suktuti

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define ijma suktuti, state its conditions, and explain why Hanafis accept it as a valid proof while Shafi'is do not.

Core Idea
Silence of other Companions to a known statement indicates tacit consent (ijma suktuti) in Hanafi usul.

This passage from the Usul of Bazdawi extols the Hanafi school as the perfect methodology in both usul al-fiqh and furu'. It then introduces a key methodological principle: when a Companion's opinion is known and no other Companion objects, their silence is understood as agreement, forming a form of consensus (ijma). This tacit consensus (ijma suktuti) is accepted as a binding proof in the Hanafi school, distinguishing it from other schools.

Guided Path
1
Identify the claim about the Hanafi school
Read the first part where the author praises the Hanafi path as the ultimate one. Recognize that this is a foundational claim about the school's completeness.
This sets the context for the methodological discussion.
2
Extract the scenario for tacit consensus
Look for where the passage describes a situation: a Companion's statement is known, no disagreement is recorded, and it is not confirmed that the statement reached another Companion who remained silent.
This scenario is the basis for ijma suktuti.
3
Analyze the meaning of silence
The passage says 'so he remained silent as a submission to it' (فسكت مسلماً له). Understand that silence here indicates consent, not rejection.
This is the core difference with other schools.
4
Connect to Hanafi usul
Recognize that this principle is called ijma suktuti and is considered a valid source of law (dalil) in the Hanafi school.
Contrast with the Shafi'i position that requires explicit consensus.

Core Idea
Silence of scholars after presentation and reflection is consent by necessity; requiring speech from all is impossible.

In the Hanafi school, establishing ijma (consensus) does not always require explicit verbal agreement from every scholar. This lesson explores the rational proof behind this principle and the conditions that make silence a valid indicator of agreement.

Identify the core claim
The Hanafi position: silence of scholars can constitute consent for ijma under certain conditions. The passage argues that requiring speech from all scholars is impractical, so silence implies consent by necessity.

Analyze the rational proof
The proof is based on custom (urf) and necessity. The author states: 'requiring speech from all is impossible and not customary; the custom is that senior scholars give fatwa while others remain silent.' This is a qiyas from custom: since the norm is that silence follows, it is treated as consent.

The silence of Ali (ra) during Umar's ruling is addressed. The passage explains that Ali's silence was not due to disagreement but because both opinions were valid; his silence indicated acceptance, not objection.

The author also includes cases where desire leads to insanity or even disbelief, such as the Rafidah and Khawarij who are considered outside the ummah due to their extreme views on imamah. Then the author makes an important distinction: the condition of ijtihad is not required in all matters. In foundational religious matters (أصول الدين الممهدة) like the transmission of the Quran and the basic laws, all Muslims, including laymen, are part of the ijma because these are known by necessity. However, in matters that require opinion and deduction (الرأي والاستنباط), only those who are capable of ijtihad are considered. Similarly, scholars who are not mujtahids, like muqallids, are not considered in such matters unless the matter does not require opinion (i.e., is known through transmission).

هوى (hawa): Personal desire or bias that leads to partisanship and undermines impartiality.
فسق (fisq): Immorality, particularly commission of major sins or persistence in minor sins, which disqualifies one from being upright (adl).

Prior Dissent and Later Consensus: Does Ikhtilaf Block Ijma?
Intra-Hanafi dispute on whether prior ikhtilaf blocks later ijma.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to evaluate the two Hanafi interpretations regarding whether a prior dissent prevents later consensus, and identify the relied-upon position with its proof.

Core Idea
A prior dissenter's evidence is not a permanent block: it is abrogated by subsequent consensus.

One of the delicate questions in Hanafi usul is whether the existence of a previous dissenting opinion (from a deceased scholar) prevents the formation of a later consensus (ijma) that contradicts it. This lesson unpacks two interpretations of Abu Hanifa's position, exploring the evidentiary reasoning on both sides. Understanding this dispute sharpens your grasp of ijma's conditions and the mechanism of naskh (abrogation) in usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
State the question precisely: Does the dissenting opinion of a deceased mujtahid block the formation of a later consensus on the opposite ruling?
The dispute is intra-Hanafi, not between madhhabs.
2
Examine the two interpretations of Abu Hanifa's position
Some Hanafi scholars interpreted Abu Hanifa's ruling on the children of a doubtful marriage as evidence that he considered prior dissent a barrier. Others said his ruling was based on shubha (doubt), not on the persistence of the dissent.
The first view: dissent blocks ijma. The second: the ruling is executory due to doubt, but the ijma is valid.
3
Analyze the argument for dissent blocking ijma
Proponents argue that if the dissenter were alive, consensus would not be valid without him. Since his evidence remains after death, the dissent continues. Also, validating a later consensus would imply accusing the dissenter (like Ibn Abbas on 'awl) of error.
They cite Imam Muhammad's divorce case: a man who uttered ambiguous divorce terms with intent of three avoids hadd because Umar's view creates shubha.
4
Analyze the counter-argument: dissent does not block ijma
The basis of ijma's authority is the honor of commanding good and forbidding evil, which applies only to the living. The dissenter's evidence, though still existing, is abrogated (naskh) by the later consensus, just as a textual ruling abrogates a previous analogy. Accusation of error is not necessary because the pre-ijma opinion was valid in its own time.
Analogies: Companions' disagreements resolved by the Prophet without blame; people of Quba praying to the wrong direction before the abrogating revelation reached them.
5
Determine the relied-upon position and its condition
The text indicates that the correct view is that prior dissent does not block later consensus. Additionally, a condition for valid ijma is the agreement of all qualified mujtahids (not just a majority), because the honor of ijma is based on complete consensus.
This condition is derived from the nature of ijma as a collective honor.

In Hanafi legal theory, not all ignorance carries the same consequence. Complete and Lesser Duress in Tort Liability: The Instrument Principle
Equal treatment of duress types in tort liability with ijma proof

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi principle that both complete and lesser duress render the coerced person an instrument (aalah) in tort liability, and explain the ijma-based proof for qisas on the coercer.

Core Idea
Complete and lesser duress are equal in tort liability: the coerced is a mere instrument, and the coercer bears full responsibility.

This passage from the Hanafi usul text addresses a key distinction: whether the degree of duress affects liability for torts. The author states that both complete duress (ikraah kamil) and lesser duress (ikraah qasir) lead to the same ruling when the coerced person becomes an instrument (aalah) for the coercer. The examples provided—destruction of property and life—clarify the scope, and the ruling is supported by consensus (ijma). As you work through this lesson, you will learn to apply this principle and understand its proof.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of duress
The passage mentions 'al-kamil min al-ikraah' (complete duress) and 'al-qasir min al-ikraah' (lesser duress). First, locate these terms in the Arabic text and note their definitions: complete duress leaves no choice, lesser duress leaves some choice.
In Hanafi usul, complete duress typically involves a threat to life or bodily integrity, while lesser duress involves threats to property or reputation.
2
Understand the concept of instrument (aalah)
The passage states that in the category where the coerced person can be an instrument for another, the duressed person is used as a tool. Focus on the phrase 'آلةً لغيره' (an instrument for another). This legal fiction shifts liability from the coerced to the coercer.
The instrument principle applies when the coerced person has no meaningful choice or is merely a physical conduit.
3
Analyze the examples given
The author gives two examples: destruction of property (إتلاف المال) and destruction of life (إتلاف النفس). Consider how the instrument principle applies: if A forces B to strike C's property or person, B is the instrument, and A is liable.
These examples fall under tort (damān) and criminal (jināyāt) law respectively.
4
Recognize the proof: ijma (consensus)
The passage explicitly says that retaliation (qisas) is obligatory 'بالإجماع' (by consensus). Identify this as a dalil (proof) from ijma. Note that ijma is a primary source of law in Hanafi usul.
The consensus cited here establishes that the coercer bears the penalty, not the coerced.
5
Note the incomplete nature of the passage
The Arabic text ends mid-sentence. However, the key principle is clear: both duress types are equal in making the coerced an instrument. The missing part likely continues the ruling, but the main legal point is captured.
