Commentary
This passage presents a foundational classification in Hanafi usul al-fiqh. The author enumerates several four-fold divisions of textual categories. First are the levels of textual clarity: zahir (apparent), nass (explicit), mufassar (detailed), and muhkam (firm). Their opposites are khafi (hidden), mushkil (problematic), mujmal (summary), and mutashabih (ambiguous). A second division concerns modes of expression: haqiqah (literal), majaz (metaphorical), sarih (plain), and kinayah (allusive). A third division covers methods of derivation from text: by explicit statement, by indication, by implication, and by requirement. Finally, a fifth division encompasses the knowledge of positions, order, meanings, and rulings of these categories. The author then emphasizes that the foundation of all Sharia is the Quran and Sunnah, and that mastery of these textual divisions is obligatory for anyone engaging in ijtihad. Importantly, this enumeration sets the stage for subsequent detailed analysis of each term.

Key Terms
الظاهر (az-zahir): A text whose meaning is apparent but may require specification or interpretation.
النص (an-nass): A text whose meaning is explicit and unambiguous, not subject to further interpretation.
المفسر (al-mufassar): A text that has been explained in detail by Allah or the Prophet, leaving no hidden meaning.
المحكم (al-muhkam): A text that is definitive and firm, with multiple lines of evidence, and cannot be abrogated or changed.
الخفي (al-khafi): A text whose meaning is hidden or ambiguous due to some external factor.
المشكل (al-mushkil): A text whose meaning is problematic, requiring outside clarification.
المجمل (al-mujmal): A text that is summarized or ambiguous, needing elaboration.
المتشابه (al-mutashabih): A text that is completely unclear, known only to Allah (e.g., certain Quranic verses).

The Hanafi Clarity Hierarchy: Mushtarak, Mujmal, Mu'awwal, Mufassar, Zahir
Defining key Hanafi usul categories: mushtarak, mujmal, mu'awwal, mufassar, zahir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between al-mushtarak, al-mujmal, al-mu'awwal, al-mufassar, and al-zahir, and apply the Hanafi rule that mushtarak has no 'umum and requires tarjih.

Core Idea
Homonyms require preponderance; ambiguous requires clarification; interpretation is speculative; explicit is definitive; apparent is clear.

In Hanafi usul, not all texts are equally clear. Some words carry multiple meanings, some are ambiguous due to legal additions, and some are definitively clear. This passage from Imam al-Bazdawi’s Kanz al-Wusul lays out a systematic hierarchy of textual clarity, from homonym (mushtarak) to explicit (mufassar) and apparent (zahir). Understanding this classification is essential for deriving rulings correctly, as each category demands a different epistemological treatment and determines whether a ruling is definitive (qat'i) or speculative (zanni).

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
For each term (mushtarak, mujmal, mu'awwal, mufassar, zahir), isolate the definition given by the author and note its key characteristics.
The author also provides linguistic derivations for some terms.
2
Analyze how each term differs from adjacent categories
Mushtarak differs from mujmal because mushtarak can be resolved by linguistic reflection (tarjih), while mujmal cannot. Mu'awwal differs from mufassar because mu'awwal is based on predominant opinion (ghalib al-ra'y), while mufassar is based on a decisive proof (dalil qati').
Zahir is the simplest category: speech clear from its wording.
3
Determine the epistemological weight
Mushtarak before tarjih is ambiguous; after tarjih it becomes mu'awwal (zanni). Mufassar is qat'i. Zahir is also qat'i in its apparent meaning but admits probability of a less likely meaning.
This affects whether a ruling can be abrogated or must be followed definitively.
4
Examine the proof cited (hadith)
The author cites the hadith 'Whoever interprets the Quran by his opinion, let him take his seat in the Fire' to support the definition of mu'awwal and refute the Mu'tazila. Identify how the hadith is used as a dalil.
The hadith is ahad, so it is zanni al-thubut but its meaning is clear (qat'i al-dalalah).
5
Apply the categories to a sample text
Take a Quranic word like 'qur'' (al-Baqarah 228). Classify it according to the hierarchy: it is mushtarak between menstruation and purity. Then apply tarjih to reach the mu'awwal (preponderant meaning) used in Hanafi fiqh.
This shows the real-world impact of these abstract definitions.

Then it defines al-mushtarak (homonym) as a word with multiple possible meanings, where only one is intended, and it has no generality (la 'umum). Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul defines three key terms in the Hanafi hierarchy of textual clarity: zahir, nass, and mufassar. The author begins with a discussion of 'mufassar' (explained), deriving the term linguistically from the idea of clear dawn or unveiling. He then connects this to the hadith warning against interpreting the Quran by personal opinion, emphasizing that claiming knowledge of Allah's intent without decisive proof is erroneous. This refutes the Mu'tazili position that every mujtahid is correct in his interpretation.

Next, he defines 'zahir' (apparent): speech whose meaning is clear from its wording to the listener. Examples: the verse about marrying women (4:3) is zahir in permissibility (itlaq), meaning it allows marriage without specifying numbers; the verse about trade (2:275) is zahir in legality (ihlal).

Then 'nass' (textually clear): speech that is clearer than zahir due to the speaker's intent, not the wording itself. The term derives from 'nasas' meaning to urge a mount beyond its usual pace. Examples: the same marriage verse with numbers (4:3) is nass in specifying the number because the context is directed to that purpose; the trade/usury verse (2:275) is nass in distinguishing between the two contracts because the speech is intended for that distinction. The ruling is that both zahir and nass give certainty, but nass takes precedence in case of conflict.

Finally, 'mufassar' (explicitly explained): what is clearer than nass, either by a meaning within the nass or by an external decisive clarification (bayan qati'). If the text was ambiguous (mujmal), a decisive explanation closes the door of ta'wil (interpretive speculation); if it was general ('amm), decisive specification (takhsis) closes the door of possible exceptions. Example: 'the angels prostrated, all of them together' (15:30) — 'the angels' is a general plural that could be specified, but the added 'all of them' makes it mufassar, closing the door of takhsis.

In Hanafi usul, this hierarchy is crucial for legal reasoning: a text's clarity determines its evidentiary weight and the possibility of interpretation or specification.

المفسر (al-mufassar): Speech that is clearer than nass, either by inherent meaning or by an external decisive clarification that closes the door of ta'wil or takhsis.

Zahir is an apparent meaning that does not require investigation; nass is clearer because the speaker intended a specific meaning even though the wording itself is not explicit; mufassar is even clearer because it closes the door of ta'wil or takhsis through a definitive clarification; and muhkam is the highest degree, immune even to abrogation. The author also clarifies that at each level, the ruling is certain, but when conflicting, the stronger prevails. He then introduces the three opposite categories—khafi (hidden due to a circumstantial barrier), mushkil (confusing due to resemblance to other terms), and mujmal (ambiguous due to multiple possible meanings). These categories are used to classify texts that require additional effort to interpret. The passage provides Quranic examples for each category, grounding the definitions in scripture. This understanding is crucial for the Hanafi method of istinbat, as it determines how much interpretative leeway a text allows.

Key Terms
الظاهر (al-zāhir): ما دل على معناه بنفس الصيغة من غير احتياج إلى قرينة (that which indicates its meaning by its own wording without need for a context)
النص (al-naṣṣ): ما ازداد وضوحاً على الظاهر بمعنى من المتكلم لا في الصيغة (that which has increased clarity over zahir by a meaning from the speaker, not in the wording)
المفسر (al-mufassar): ما ازداد وضوحاً على النص، وسد باب التأويل أو التخصيص ببيان قاطع (that which has increased clarity over nass, closing the door of ta'wil or takhsis by definitive clarification)
المحكم (al-muḥkam): ما ازداد قوةً على المفسر، وأحكم عن احتمال النسخ والتبديل (that which increased in strength over mufassar and made firm against possibility of abrogation and replacement)
الخفي (al-khafī): ما اشتبه معناه وخفي مراده بعارض غير الصيغة (that whose meaning is ambiguous and intended meaning hidden by a circumstantial obstacle not from the wording)
المشكل (al-mushkil): الداخل في أشكاله وأمثاله، لا يُنال بالطلب بل بالتأمل بعد الطلب (that which enters into its forms and similars, not reached by seeking but by reflection after seeking)
المجمل (al-mujmal): ما ازدحمت فيه المعاني واشتبه المراد (that in which meanings are crowded and the intended meaning is ambiguous)

Commentary
This passage from Imam al-Bazdawi's 'Kanz al-Wusul' defines the four categories of textual ambiguity that are the counterparts to the four clear categories (Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, Muhkam). The author explains each term with etymology and examples. The author notes that scholars differ in their obligation: some must strive to understand the ambiguous texts, while others, out of honor, must stop and submit.

The Four Levels of Textual Clarity: Zahir, Nass, Mufassar, and Muhkam
Defines the rulings for zahir, nass, mufassar, and muhkam

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish the four levels of textual clarity (zahir, nass, mufassar, muhkam) in Hanafi Usul al-Fiqh and apply the hierarchy of obligation when texts conflict.

Core Idea
The strength of a text is measured by what possibilities are excluded: zahir allows ta'wil, nass allows only far-fetched ta'wil, mufassar allows naskh, muhkam allows nothing.

In Hanafi Usul, not all clear texts are equal. Al-Bazdawi's Usul presents a ladder of clarity: each level obligates action, but differs in what possibilities remain open. This lesson will unpack the rulings and show how these categories resolve conflicts between evidences.

Distinguish between reasonable and far-fetched ta'wil
Zahir's ta'wil must be plausible in language and context. Nass's ta'wil is strained and unlikely, yet not impossible.

The Sahibayn's View: Majaz as Substitute (Khalaf)
Presenting the disagreement between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples regarding priority of haqiqah and majaz

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why Abu Yusuf and Muhammad prefer acting on the general figurative meaning, and identify how the concept of majaz as a substitute (khalaf) underlies their disagreement with Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
The Sahibayn treat majaz as a substitute for haqiqah, giving it precedence; Abu Hanifa does not.

While Abu Hanifa prioritized the literal meaning, his two foremost students held a different view. They considered the figurative meaning as a valid substitute (khalaf) for the literal, and thus gave it priority when both are in common use. Understanding this ikhtilaf reveals a deeper usul principle about the nature of language and legal interpretation.

Guided Path
1
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad said: 'al-amal bi-umum al-majaz awla' (acting upon the general figurative meaning is preferable). This is directly from the text.
They do not reject literal meaning entirely, but prioritize the common figurative.
2
Identify the basis: khalaf
The text says: 'This goes back to the principle that figurative meaning according to them is a substitute (khalaf) for literal meaning.' A substitute takes the place of the original when used. Thus, majaz is not secondary but an alternative that can be given precedence.
This is the crux of the ikhtilaf.
3
Compare with Abu Hanifa
Abu Hanifa does not consider majaz as a substitute. He sees it as a departure from the original meaning, only used when literal is impossible or there is a strong indicator. Thus he gives priority to haqiqah.
The difference is whether majaz is a legitimate independent category or a deviation.
4
Explore thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: in cases where both meanings are common, Abu Hanifa will apply the literal, while the Sahibayn will apply the figurative. This can lead to different rulings in issues like acknowledgment of lineage and emancipation.
For example, in the older child case, Abu Hanifa offers two methods; the Sahibayn might directly apply emancipation.
5
Recognize the intra-madhhab ikhtilaf
Both positions are within the Hanafi school. Later scholars have preferred one over the other depending on context. The mufta bihi often follows Abu Hanifa in most matters due to his higher rank, but the Sahibayn's view is considered in many fatwas.
This ikhtilaf is legitimate and both are valid.

Preference of Haqiqa over Majaz in Hanafi Usul
Usul rule with internal Hanafi ikhtilaf

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule on preferring haqiqa over majaz muta'arif, identify the Sahibayn's counter-position, and apply the rule to oath examples.

Core Idea
Abu Hanifa prefers literal meaning; Sahibayn choose general metaphor - a key usul ikhtilaf.

In usul al-fiqh, when a word has both a literal and a commonly known metaphorical meaning, which should be given priority? This question arises in oaths and contracts. Imam Abu Hanifa and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, hold different views. Understanding this ikhtilaf illuminates Hanafi methodology and its practical impact on legal interpretation.

Guided Path
1
Define haqiqa and majaz muta'arif
Identify the literal (haqiqa) and the common metaphorical (majaz muta'arif) meanings of a word. Haqiqa is the originally intended meaning; majaz muta'arif is a metaphor widely understood.
e.g., 'wheat' can mean the grain (haqiqa) or bread made from it (majaz).
2
State the two positions
Recall: Abu Hanifa gives priority to haqiqa; the Sahibayn give priority to acting upon the general metaphorical meaning (shumul al-majaz).
3
Explain the reasoning (khalaf in speech vs ruling)
For the Sahibayn, majaz is a substitute (khalaf) for haqiqa in the legal ruling (hukm), so it encompasses both. For Abu Hanifa, it is a substitute only in speech (takallum), not in ruling, so preference remains on the original speech form.
This is the core talil of the dispute.
4
Apply to examples: wheat and Euphrates oaths
Manually apply the rule: for 'I will not eat this wheat,' Abu Hanifa restricts to grain; Sahibayn extend to products. For 'I will not drink from the Euphrates,' Abu Hanifa restricts to cupping hands; Sahibayn extend to any water from that river.
Observe the practical thamarah al-khilaf.

The passage provides two illustrative examples from oaths: (1) If someone swears not to eat 'from this wheat' (hantha), Abu Hanifa says the oath applies only to the grain itself, not to products made from it (like bread). The Sahibayn say it applies to the general content (madhmun) metaphorically, so it includes bread. (2) If someone swears not to drink 'from the Euphrates' (al-Furat), Abu Hanifa restricts it to drinking by cupping hands (al-kar'), while the Sahibayn extend it to any drinking of water adjacent to the river, even if by vessels, because the vessels hold less than the river. This principle has practical implications in oaths and contracts.

مجاز متعارف (majaz muta'arif): A commonly known metaphorical meaning that is widespread in usage.
خَلَف (khalaf): A substitute or successor; here it refers to the metaphorical meaning standing in for the literal.

This lesson explores a foundational principle of Hanafi usul: the default in speech is explicitness (al-asl fi al-kalam huwa al-sarih).
