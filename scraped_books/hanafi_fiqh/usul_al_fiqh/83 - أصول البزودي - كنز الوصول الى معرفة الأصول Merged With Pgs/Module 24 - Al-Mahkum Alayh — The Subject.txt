Types of Legal Capacity: Ahliyyat al-Wujub and Ahliyyat al-Ada
Introduces and defines the two types of legal capacity

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between ahliyyat al-wujub and ahliyyat al-ada, identify their basis in the dhimmah, and cite the Quranic and Ijma proofs.

Core Idea
Legal capacity splits into two: being subject to obligations (wujub) and performing them (ada); both rely on dhimmah from birth.

This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul lays the foundational distinction between two types of legal capacity. Ahliyyat al-wujub is the suitability for having rights and obligations imposed, rooted in the dhimmah (covenant) present from birth. Ahliyyat al-ada is the capacity to perform actions and incur liability, either complete (kamil) or deficient (qasir). The author supports these concepts with Quranic verses and juristic consensus. Understanding this dual classification is key to grasping how Shariah addresses human capacity across all stages of life.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types of ahliyyah
Scan the text for the explicit division: 'الأهلية ضربان: ١ - أهلية وجوب، ٢ - وأهلية أداء'. Note that the author then explains each type separately.
2
Define ahliyyat al-wujub
The text defines it as 'الصلاح للحكم' (suitability for ruling). It is a single, unified quality: whoever is suitable for a ruling of obligation in any respect has ahliyyat al-wujub.
This is not specific to a particular age; it is a yes/no condition.
3
Identify the basis of ahliyyat al-wujub
The text states it is based on 'قيام الذمة' (existence of the dhimmah). This is established from birth by the primordial covenant, as proven by Quranic verses and consensus.
The word ذمة is defined as العهد (covenant).
4
Define ahliyyat al-ada and its subtypes
The text divides it into two: كامل (complete) and قاصر (deficient). Complete capacity makes one suitable for 'لزوم العهدة' (incurring liability), while deficient does not.
Complete capacity typically comes with puberty and sanity, but deficient applies to minors and the insane.
5
Trace the proofs
The passage cites two Quranic verses: (1) al-A'raf 7:172 on the primordial covenant, and (2) al-Isra 17:13 on obligation imposed on every human. It also mentions 'إجماع الفقهاء' (consensus of jurists) as additional proof for the existence of dhimmah from birth.
These proofs are explicit in the text; note that the second verse uses 'كُلَّ إِنسَنِ' (every human), supporting generality.

Commentary
This passage begins the section on legal capacity (ahliyyah). The author defines two primary types: ahliyyat al-wujub (capacity for obligation) and ahliyyat al-ada (capacity for performance). Ahliyyat al-wujub is the suitability for having rights and obligations imposed; its basis is the single quality of being 'suitable for a ruling' (salih li al-hukm). This capacity arises from the dhimmah, a covenant established at birth, as proven by the Quranic verses on the primordial covenant (mithaq) and the obligation tied to every person. Ahliyyat al-ada is divided into complete (kamil) and deficient (qasir). Complete capacity makes one liable for obligations (luzum al-'uhda), while deficient does not. The author cites consensus of the jurists for the existence of dhimmah from birth. The text emphasizes that dhimmah is a covenant (‘ahd) inherent in every human being, enabling them to bear obligations.

Key Terms
الأهلية (al-ahliyyah): Legal capacity, divided into ahliyyat al-wujub (capacity for obligation) and ahliyyat al-ada (capacity for performance).
الذمة (al-dhimmah): The covenant or pledge (عهد) that makes a person suitable for obligations; it is established at birth by the primordial covenant.

Defining deficient and complete capacity for performance

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between deficient and complete capacity for performance and explain how intellect and puberty determine capacity.

Core Idea
Capacity for performance depends on physical ability and intellect; puberty marks the standard for complete capacity.

In this passage, the author defines ahliyyat al-ada (capacity for performance) and divides it into two types: deficient (qasir) and complete (kamil). Understanding this distinction is foundational for determining which acts of worship are valid from a person who is not yet a fully responsible mukallaf.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two types
Read the first sentence to see that the author explicitly names two types: deficient and complete.
These categories apply to physical ability and intellect.
2
Understand deficient capacity
Note that deficient capacity is established by limited physical ability before puberty, and after puberty for the ma'tuh (intellectually impaired). The key is that the person is rational but lacks balanced intellect.
Capacity is defined here by capability, not obligation.
3
Understand the standard for complete capacity
The author explains that balanced intellect (i'tidal) varies among humans. When it rises above deficiency, puberty is established as the legal standard for balance in Shariah.
Puberty is a proxy for i'tidal in legal rulings.
4
Relate to division of rulings
The author hints that rulings in this chapter are divided according to what has passed—likely referring to earlier sections on rights of Allah and rights of people.
The division affects which acts are considered valid.

Validity of Iman from a Minor
Applying capacity for performance to the case of a minor's iman

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the principle of capacity for performance to determine that a minor's faith is valid and explain the reasoning based on the nature of iman.

Core Idea
Iman from a minor is valid because it is purely good and there is no legal impediment against it.

After establishing the two types of capacity for performance, the author applies this to one of the rights of Allah: iman (faith). He argues that iman is entirely good and does not admit of anything else, so when a minor performs it with the necessary capacity, it is valid. This is a key Hanafi position with practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the legal question
The passage asks: Is a minor's declaration of faith (iman) valid? The ruling is that it is valid.
This is an application of the earlier classification of capacity.

This purity means there is no inherent reason to invalidate it.
3
Apply the principle of valid occurrence
When a thing occurs in its true reality (bi-haqiqatihi), it is not invalidated except by a legal impediment (hajr). For iman, no such impediment exists because it is purely good.
The only liability is in lizum al-ada' (necessity of performance), which can be waived.

Note the partial incomplete text
The passage ends with a mention of deprivation of inheritance, suggesting that the validity of iman has legal consequences like preventing inheritance differences.
The text is cut off, but the principle is clear.

Ruling on a Child's Apostasy: Valid in Afterlife, No Execution
Ruling on child apostasy and why execution is not applied

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that a child's apostasy is valid for the afterlife but does not incur worldly execution, and identify the usul principle that execution is tied to waging war (muharaba) rather than to disbelief itself.

Core Idea
A child's apostasy is valid in the afterlife, but execution is linked to rebellion, not to disbelief.

This passage from Usul al-Bazdawi addresses a nuanced aspect of mahkum alayh: the capacity of a child. While a child's apostasy is effective for the afterlife, the Hanafi school does not apply the death penalty because the penalty for apostasy is actually tied to waging war against the state, not to the mere act of disbelief. Understanding this distinction is crucial for grasping Hanafi usul on the relationship between internal belief and worldly punishments.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish between afterlife and worldly rulings
The text separates the validity of apostasy in the afterlife from its worldly consequences. The child's apostasy is 'sahih' for the afterlife, meaning it incurs Allah's punishment, but worldly rulings like execution do not follow.
This distinction is based on the child's incomplete legal capacity (ahliyya naqisa).
2
Identify the underlying cause (illa) for execution
The author states that execution is obligated by muharaba (waging war), not by apostasy itself. This is a key usul point: the same act (apostasy) can have different rulings depending on capacity.
The text explicitly says: 'because killing is obligated by waging war, not by the essence of apostasy.'
3
Apply the analogy to a woman's apostasy
The child's case is compared to a woman's apostasy, which also does not carry the death penalty in the Hanafi school. This qiyas reinforces the principle that execution requires muharaba.
The passage ends: 'so it resembles a woman's apostasy.'
4
Recognize the role of capacity (ahliyya)
The child lacks full ʿuhda (legal responsibility), so acts are valid but not binding. This explains why no worldly penalty is applied.
Earlier in the passage, the author discusses that a child's acts of worship are valid without obligation.

Riqq as Legal Incapacity and the Dispute on Partial Manumission
Definition of riqq and dispute on partial manumission via qiyas.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of riqq as legal incapacity and apply the qiyas analogy to explain why partial manumission results in mukatab status according to Abu Hanifa.

Core Idea
Riqq is an indivisible legal incapacity; partial manumission creates a mukatab, not a free person.

This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul defines riqq (slavery) as a legal incapacity that is indivisible. It then presents the intra-Hanafi dispute between Abu Hanifa and the two Sahibayn over partial manumission. The key is understanding how qiyas (analogical reasoning) is used: Abu Hanifa compares to wudu limbs (divisible act, indivisible effect), while the Sahibayn compare to talaq (indivisible cause and effect).

Guided Path
1
Define riqq and its legal characteristics
Identify the definition of riqq as 'ajz hukmi and note that it is originally a penalty but now a legal construct. Recognise that riqq is indivisible: even partial acknowledgment makes one a full slave in legal effects.
2
State the dispute on partial manumission
Abu Hanifa says a partially freed person becomes a mukatab (not free); Sahibayn say manumission must be fully effective for freedom.
3
Analyse the Sahibayn's qiyas
They compare manumission to talaq: if the effect (freedom, divorce) is indivisible, the act must be indivisible.
4
Analyse Abu Hanifa's counter-qiyas
He compares to wudu limbs: the acts (washing limbs) are divisible, but the permission for prayer is indivisible. Similarly, ownership removal is divisible but the ruling of freedom is indivisible; partial removal yields intermediate mukatab status.
5
Understand the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
According to Abu Hanifa, partial manumission results in mukatab status; according to the Sahibayn, the act may be invalid or require full freedom. The mufta bihi position follows Abu Hanifa.

Classification of Acquired Impediments in Hanafi Usul
Chapter heading and classification of acquired impediments.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to list the seven acquired impediments in Hanafi usul and classify each as self-originating or other-originating.

Core Idea
Acquired impediments are barriers to legal capacity from oneself or others; knowing them is key to assessing the validity of acts.

In Hanafi legal theory, not all barriers to legal capacity are permanent or inherent. Some arise from a person's own actions or circumstances, while others are imposed externally. This lesson introduces the foundational classification of these 'acquired impediments' (ʿawāriḍ muktasaba) as a memorization anchor for further study.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main categories
The text divides acquired impediments into two types: those that come from the person himself (min nafsihi) and those that come from others (min ghayrihi). Commit this binary classification to memory.
This is a fundamental taxonomy in Hanafi usul.
2
List the self-originating impediments
Memorize the six impediments from oneself: ignorance (jahl), intoxication (sukr), jest (hazl), foolishness (safah), mistake (khaṭa), and travel (safar).
Each has specific fiqh implications studied later.
3
List the other-originating impediment
Only one impediment comes from others: coercion (ikrāh). Understand that this includes duress that invalidates consent.
Coercion is a single category but covers various forms of duress.
4
Understand the legal effect distinction
These impediments do not necessarily remove legal capacity entirely; they affect the validity of specific acts (e.g., contracts, divorce). Some are partial (e.g., travel allows concessions) while others nullify consent (e.g., coercion).
The effect varies by context and is detailed in fiqh texts.

The Legal Effect of Intoxication on Statements and Hudud
Analyzing when intoxication excuses legal liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule on intoxication to determine when a statement or acknowledgment is valid and when hudud are imposed.

Core Idea
Intoxication does not remove legal capacity, but its effect depends on the cause and the type of act.

This passage from Usul al-Bazdawi explores a crucial nuance in legal capacity (mahkum alayh): intoxication does not strip one of legal responsibility entirely, but its impact differs based on the source of intoxication and the nature of the action. Understanding this distinction is key to correctly applying rulings on statements, contracts, and hudud punishments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause of intoxication
The passage distinguishes between sinful causes (e.g., drinking alcohol) and permissible causes (e.g., accidental consumption). Only permissible causes may excuse certain actions.
This is a fundamental distinction in Hanafi usul for excusing acts.
2
Categorize the act type
Acts are divided into three categories: belief-based (e.g., apostasy), expression-based (e.g., contracts, acknowledgments), and hudud punishments. Each category receives different treatment.
Belief-based acts rely on inner conviction; expression-based acts depend on verbal validity.
3
Apply the rule for belief-based acts
For acts like apostasy, intoxication from a permissible cause excuses the act because the pillar (inner conviction) is absent.
Istihsan prevents establishing apostasy when the person is intoxicated from a valid cause.
4
Apply the rule for expression-based acts
For acts depending on valid speech, intoxication does not excuse because the pillar (expression) is present. Statements are generally valid, unless the intoxication undermines the speech itself.
The exception is when speech becomes incoherent—then it may be treated as retraction.
5
Analyze hudud and Abu Hanifa's refinement
Hudud are delayed until sobriety. Abu Hanifa added that incoherent speech (ikhtilat al-kalam) establishes intoxication as an equivalent to actual retraction, affecting acknowledgments for qadhf and qisas differently.
This refinement creates a delicate balance between justice and mercy.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the legal effect of intoxication. It clarifies that intoxication does not remove legal capacity entirely because the intellect is not removed, only overwhelmed. The ruling distinguishes based on the cause of intoxication: if it stems from a sin (like drinking alcohol), it is not an excuse; if it is from a permissible cause (like eating something that accidentally intoxicates), it is an excuse. However, this excuse does not apply to matters of belief (like apostasy) because those depend on inner conviction, not external expression. Matters dependent on verbal expression (like contracts) require valid speech, which intoxication undermines, so the intoxicant's statements are not excused. For hudud punishments, they are delayed until sobriety. Abu Hanifa refined the standard: incoherent speech (ikh-tilat al-kalam) suffices to establish intoxication as an excuse. In acknowledgments (iqrar), intoxication operates like retraction for statements that can be retracted (e.g., acknowledging a debt) but not for in-retractable ones (hudud for qadhf and qisas).

Types of Legal Interdiction (Hajr) According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
Enumeration of three hajr types with intra-madhhab dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three types of legal interdiction (hajr) according to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, and explain the intra-madhhab disagreement regarding the necessity of a judge's ruling for interdiction due to insanity.

Core Idea
Interdiction can be automatic for insanity (Muhammad) or require a judge (Abu Yusuf).

Legal interdiction (hajr) restricts a person's capacity to protect rights. The Hanafi school, through its two primary disciples, recognizes three grounds for interdiction. However, on the first ground—insanity—the two Imams differ: one holds it takes effect immediately, the other requires a judge's ruling. This lesson parses their positions and the underlying principle.

Guided Path
1
List the three types of hajr mentioned in the passage
Identify each type: interdiction due to insanity, refusal to pay debt, and fraudulent disposal of property.
2
Isolate the dispute on the first type
Note that Muhammad and Abu Yusuf disagree on whether a judge's ruling is needed for interdiction due to insanity.
This is an intra-Hanafi ikhtilaf.
3
Analyze each Imam's dalil
Muhammad relies on the automatic effect of insanity; Abu Yusuf relies on the judge's authority over guardianship.
The text does not provide a full argument for either side.
4
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Under Muhammad, an insane person is interdicted immediately; under Abu Yusuf, his transactions remain valid until a judge rules.
5
Identify the underlying principle
The passage states that the basis of hajr is consideration for Muslims, and insanity is analogous to a guardian's refusal.
This reflects the Hanafi emphasis on maslahah.

Commentary
This passage presents three types of legal interdiction (hajr) according to the two primary disciples of Abu Hanifa, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani. The first type is interdiction due to insanity (safah). Imam Muhammad holds that interdiction becomes effective immediately upon the occurrence of insanity after puberty, or if one reaches puberty already insane, without need for a judge's ruling. Imam Abu Yusuf, however, requires a judge's ruling because the authority to restrict a person's rights belongs to the judge. The second type is when a debtor refuses to sell his property to pay debts; the judge may sell his property, and both movable and immovable assets are treated equally. The third type is preventive interdiction when it is feared that the debtor may fraudulently dispose of his property; he is interdicted such that his transactions are valid only with the creditors' consent. The text clarifies that the basis for interdiction according to the two Imams is the welfare of Muslims, and insanity is not a direct cause but is analogous to a guardian's refusal (to marry a qualified woman).

سفه (safah): Insanity or foolishness that impairs sound judgment, a ground for interdiction.
