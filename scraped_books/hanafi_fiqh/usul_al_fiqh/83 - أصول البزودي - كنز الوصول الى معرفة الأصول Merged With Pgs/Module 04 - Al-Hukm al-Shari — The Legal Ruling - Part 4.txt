Intoxication and Legal Acts: Validity of Iqrar and Effect on Apostasy
Comparison of intoxication's effect on iqrar and apostasy

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that intoxication does not invalidate iqrar, but it casts doubt on apostasy, and explain the reasoning based on the distinction between expression-based and belief-based acts.

Core Idea
Intoxication is no excuse; iqrar stands, but apostasy requires pure belief, so doubt blocks it.

This lesson examines the contrasting rulings for an intoxicated person's iqrar and his apostasy. While duress invalidates iqrar, intoxication does not, because it is not a valid excuse. However, in apostasy, intoxication prevents the legal effect (marriage not dissolved) because apostasy depends on pure belief, which is clouded by intoxication. This demonstrates a key usul principle: acts based on expression are not invalidated by doubt, but acts based on belief are affected.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling for iqrar of an intoxicated person
Iqrar of a drunkard is valid, unlike under duress.
The passage says 'this is unlike the acknowledgments of an intoxicated person, for they are valid'.
2
Identify the rationale for iqrar
Intoxication is not a valid excuse, so it does not serve as evidence that the confessed matter does not exist. It only indicates possible retraction.
3
Contrast with apostasy
If an intoxicated person apostatizes, his wife does not become separated. Here, intoxication is taken as evidence that the apostasy is not genuine.
Because apostasy depends on pure belief, and intoxication introduces doubt.
4
Derive the general principle
Acts that depend on expression (like iqrar) are not invalidated by doubt; acts that depend on belief (like apostasy) are affected by doubt.
The passage concludes: 'what depends on expression is not invalidated by doubt either.'
5
Apply the principle to other acts
Consider other verbal acts like divorce or marriage. According to this principle, intoxication would not invalidate them because they are expression-based, unless they require intent.
This is an extension not in the text but follows from the principle.

Non-Compelling Duress and Inherently Personal Acts
Explains first two exceptions to transfer of liability under duress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify two categories of duress that do not transfer liability and apply the Hanafi principle that duress which preserves choice leaves the act with the coerced.

Core Idea
Non-compelling duress transfers no liability; acts like eating and zina are inherently personal.

In Hanafi usul, not all duress transfers legal responsibility from the coerced to the coercer. This passage from al-Bazdawi's Kanz al-Wusul distinguishes three exceptional categories. The first two are straightforward: duress that leaves some choice, and acts that cannot be performed vicariously. Understanding these exceptions helps clarify the conditions under which a coerced person remains fully liable.

Guided Path
1
Identify the first category of duress
Read the opening line: 'As for duress that does not necessitate compulsion, it does not necessitate transfer.' Recognize that this is called ikrah ghayr mulji'. Note that it removes consent (rida) but not choice (ikhtiyar) and will (mashi'a).
2
Explain why no transfer occurs
Since the coerced retains some volition, he is not a mere instrument (ala) for the coercer. The act is still his own, so liability stays with him.
The key term is 'instrument' – if the coerced is not a passive tool, duress does not transfer.
3
Identify the second category
Look for acts that are inherently personal: eating, intercourse, adultery. The text says they are 'inconceivable as an instrument' because they require the direct physical involvement of the actor's own body.
One cannot eat with another's mouth; similarly, zina requires personal physical participation.
4
Apply the principle to a new case
Consider if a person is coerced to commit theft. Is theft an inherently personal act? Theft can be done through an agent (e.g., ordering someone to steal). So it does not fall under the second exception; it is not inherently personal like eating. Thus, theft under compelling duress may transfer liability.
This comparison shows the illa: if the act can be done by an instrument, duress can transfer; if not, it stays with the coerced.

وأما الإكراه الذي لا يوجب الإلجاء: فلا يوجب النقل؛ لأنه يُعدم الرضا، ولا يُفسد الاختيار والمشيئة، فلذلك لم يُجعل آلة له.

As for duress that does not necessitate compulsion: it does not necessitate transfer, because it removes consent but does not spoil choice and will; therefore, he is not made an instrument for him. As for the category in which the doer cannot be made an instrument for another: that is like eating, intercourse, and adultery, because eating with another's mouth is inconceivable, and likewise adultery. Also, if the very act is such that the doer could conceivably be an instrument for another in form, but the locus is not the one that the destruction contacts in form, and that locus changes, then if he is made an instrument, that is invalid, and the act is limited to the coerced person. Because when the locus changes, in its change lies the invalidity of the duress, and duress has no effect in changing the locus. Changing the locus opposes the intention of the coercer, and in opposing him, the duress becomes invalid. When it becomes invalid, the act is limited to the doer, the matter returns to the original locus, and the substitution is void. This is like coercing a muhrim to kill game, or coercing a non-muhrim to kill game in the Haram: that killing is limited to the doer, because the coercer only impels him to transgress against his own ihram or his own religion, and in that he cannot be an instrument for another. If he were made an instrument, the locus of the tort would change, becoming the ihram and religion of the coercer. Hence we said: the coerced killer sins, because killing, insofar as it causes sin, is a tort against the religion of the killer, and in that he cannot be an instrument for another.

Commentary
This passage delineates three categories where duress does not transfer liability to the coercer. First, non-compelling duress (ikrah ghayr mulji'): it removes consent (rida) but does not spoil choice (ikhtiyar) and will (mashi'a). Since the coerced retains some volition, he is not a mere instrument, and the act remains his own. Second, acts that are inherently personal and cannot be performed vicariously: eating, sexual intercourse, and adultery (zina) are inconceivable as instrumental acts because they require the direct physical involvement of the actor's own body (e.g., one cannot eat with another's mouth). Third, a subtle category: the act itself could be instrumental (like killing), but the locus (mahall) of the tort changes when considered for the coerced vs. the coercer. For example, a muhrim (person in ihram) is coerced to kill game: killing game is a normal act, but for a muhrim, the locus of the prohibition is his own ihram. If we attributed the act to the coercer, the locus would become the coercer's ihram, which is not the same. Since duress cannot change the locus (it is a quality attached to the person), the attribution fails. Similarly, coercing a non-muhrim to kill game in the Haram: the prohibition attaches to the sanctity of the place, which is a personal obligation (dīn) of the doer. Changing the locus to the coercer's sanctity is not permissible. Hence the act remains with the direct perpetrator. Finally, the passage concludes that the coerced killer is sinful (āthim) because the sinfulness of killing is a harm against one's own religion (dīn), and in that regard, he cannot be an instrument for another.

Key Terms
المحل (al-mahall): The legal locus or subject matter of a prohibition (e.g., ihram, sanctity of the Haram) that attaches to a specific person and cannot be transferred to another.

Change of Locus as a Barrier to Transfer of Liability
Explains the exception of locus (mahall) change preventing transfer under duress

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why a change in the legal locus (mahall) prevents the transfer of liability under duress and identify cases like coercing a muhrim or a non-muhrim in the Haram.

Core Idea
When duress changes the locus of prohibition, liability stays with the coerced; the coercer's intent fails.

The third exception in al-Bazdawi's treatment of duress is more subtle: even if an act could be performed by an instrument in form, if the locus of the prohibition changes when attributed to the coercer, the duress fails and the act remains with the coerced. This lesson unpacks the concept of mahall and its critical role in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Understand the third category's condition
The text says: 'the act could be performed by an instrument in form, but the locus is not the one that the destruction contacts in form, and the locus changes.' Identify that 'locus' (mahall) refers to the subject of the prohibition, like ihram or sanctity.
2
Apply the example: muhrim coerced to kill game
Killing game is normally a permissible act for a non-muhrim, but for a muhrim it is prohibited because the mahall is his ihram. If duress is applied, can the act be attributed to the coercer? The text says no, because the coercer does not have ihram; the prohibition attaches only to the coerced.
The locus mahall changes from the coerced's ihram to the coercer's (which does not exist).
3
Apply the example: non-muhrim in Haram coerced to kill game
Similarly, the sanctity of the Haram (hurmah) is a mahall that attaches to the person present. The coercer is not in the Haram, so the locus changes.
The text says 'upon his own religion' (dīn), meaning the prohibition is personal.
4
Derive the principle
The principle: duress cannot change the mahall of a prohibition because the mahall is personally attached. Therefore, if attribution to the coercer would change the mahall, the duress is invalid (bātil) and the act remains with the doer.
This is a key usul principle: al-ikrāh lā athar lahu fī tabdīl al-mahall.
5
Conclude with the ruling on sin
The text ends by stating the coerced killer sins because the sin (ma‘tham) is a tort against his own religion, which cannot be transferred. This reinforces that certain liabilities are personal.
The sin attaches to the doer's dīn, not transferable.

The Tool Analysis in Coercion: Attributing Actions under Duress
Analyzing when a coerced person is considered a mere tool of the coercer

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi tool (ālah) analysis to determine the attribution of an action under duress, distinguishing between acts where the coerced person is a mere tool and acts where they are not.

Core Idea
A coerced person is a tool only if the act can be performed by the coercer through the coerced without changing the act's nature or locus.

This passage from Al-Bazdawi's usul al-fiqh introduces a critical principle for attributing actions under coercion. The key concept is whether the coerced individual can be regarded as a mere tool (ālah) of the coercer. Understanding this tool analysis is essential for determining legal responsibility in cases of duress, as it distinguishes between acts that transfer liability to the coercer and those that remain with the coerced.

Guided Path
1
Identify the type of coercion
Distinguish between mulji' (compelling, eliminating choice) and ghayr mulji' (non-compelling, eliminating consent only). Only mulji' can potentially transfer attribution.
The passage states that only coercion that compels (iljā') effects transfer.
2
Check if the act can be performed by the coercer using the coerced as a tool
Ask: Is the act conceivable as being performed by the coercer's will through the coerced's body? If not (e.g., eating, zina), the act stays with the coerced.
Acts like eating by another's mouth are inconceivable.
3
Analyze the locus (maḥall) of the injury
If the act is conceivable as a tool, check whether the locus of the injury would change if attributed to the coercer. A change in locus (e.g., ihram to non-ihram) prevents tool status.
The example of a muhrim coerced to kill game: the locus would become the coercer's ihram, which is impermissible.
4
Apply to the case of sale and manumission
For sale delivery, the act is a personal disposal (tasarruf) that cannot be transferred. For manumission under compulsion, the coerced is the speaker but the legal effect transfers because the act is separable.
Manumission involves a verbal act that can be transferred in legal effect.
5
Determine sin and legal liability
Even when the act is attributed to the coercer for worldly rulings, the coerced may still be sinful if they chose the act (e.g., killing) because sin depends on intention and choice.
The passage emphasizes that sin afflicts the coerced because they actualized the act with their own will.

The overall principle is that attribution depends on whether the coerced can be a neutral instrument.

Core Idea
Acts causing equal harm are never permitted by duress – only where self-harm outweighs the sin may be allowed.

This passage from Al-Bazdawi’s Uṣūl al-Fiqh defines the limits of duress (ikrāh). While many prohibitions are lifted under duress, certain acts—zina, killing, and wounding—remain absolutely forbidden. The reasoning is based on the principle of taʿāruḍ: the basis for any concession is fear of death, but here the coercer and coerced share that fear equally, so no concession is granted. The passage also distinguishes cases of unequal harm, such as cutting one’s own hand versus another’s.

The Legal Effect of Coercion in Crimes vs. Contracts: A Hanafi Qiyas
Qiyas-based usul rule on coercion

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between coerced destructive acts and coerced dispositions, and derive the ruling for a new scenario using qiyas.

Core Idea
In coercion, the legal doer differs from the moral doer; the act's nature determines whether the coerced is a tool or a real agent.

This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul lays out a key Hanafi principle: coercion does not uniformly transfer liability. In crimes, the coercer is the legal actor; in contracts, the coerced remains the actor. This distinction rests on a qiyas that considers whether the coerced can serve as a 'tool' (āla) or must exercise his own disposition. Understanding this qiyas is essential to correctly attribute legal consequences in cases of duress.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories of coerced acts
The text discusses two types: destructive acts (jīnāyāt, e.g., murder) and dispositional acts (contracts like sale and emancipation). Note the different outcomes for each.
Destructive acts involve taking life or property; dispositions involve transferring ownership or rights.
2
Analyze the legal attribution for each category
For murder: the coercer (mukrih) is the legal doer (fā'il), while the coerced (mukrah) bears sin (ithm). For sale: the coerced himself is the legal doer of the delivery (taslīm).
Sin attaches to the coerced because he chose his life over another's; in contracts, no such choice of harm is present.
3
Examine the qiyas reasoning
The author uses a qiyas (analogy) to explain why the coerced can be a 'tool' in murder but not in sale. In murder, the coerced's action is pure destruction, so he can be a tool. In sale, the act is a disposition (tasarruf) that must be attributed to the owner; transferring it would change the act to usurpation (ghasb).
The key distinction is whether the act can be separated from the agent's intention.
4
Apply the same reasoning to emancipation
In coerced emancipation, the coerced is the speaker (the one who utters the divorce/emancipation), but the destruction (property loss) is transferred to the coercer because it is separable from the speech act.
This shows the Hanafi principle that some effects of an act can be transferred even if the act itself is attributed to the coerced.
5
Formulate the underlying usul rule
The rule: The effect of coercion on legal attribution depends on whether the coerced act is a pure destruction (where the agent can be a tool) or a disposition (where the agent must be the principal). This rule is derived by qiyas from the known case of murder.
This is a jaliy (clear) analogy because the ratio legis (illa) is the nature of the act itself.

And the prohibition that is capable of being entirely lifted: it is the prohibition of wine, carrion, and pork, for compelling duress (ikrah mulji') necessitates its permissibility; because the prohibition of these things is not established by the text except under choice. Allah Almighty said: 'And He has detailed to you what He has forbidden to you except what you are compelled to' (6:119). And He said: 'So whoever is compelled, without desiring or transgressing, there is no sin upon him' (2:173). And if the prohibition in origin is established as restricted by an exception, then the case of exception is outside the prohibition, so it remains on absolute permissibility, like one who is compelled to it due to hunger or thirst. Do you not see that the wisdom of the prohibition returns to the consumer due to vileness in the food and drink? Allah Almighty said: 'And to turn you away from the remembrance of Allah and from prayer, so will you not desist?' (5:91). And He said: 'And He makes unlawful to them the evil things' (7:157). So if that leads to the loss of everything, then losing a part is better than losing everything, on the example of our saying: 'Either you cut off your hand or we will kill you.' So if the prohibition falls entirely, then the one who refrains from consuming it, while being compelled, is wasting his own life, thus becomes sinful. This is when compulsion is complete. But if it falls short, consumption is not permissible for him due to lack of necessity, but if he consumes, he is not to be punished with the fixed penalty (hadd), because if it had been complete, it would have necessitated permissibility, so if it falls short, it becomes a doubt (shubha).

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on the category of prohibitions that can be entirely lifted under compulsion. The author identifies three specific items: wine, carrion, and pork. The key condition is 'ikrah mulji''—compulsion that threatens life or limb. The ruling is based on Quranic verses that except cases of necessity. The reasoning is that the prohibition of these items is tied to choice (ikhtiyar); when compulsion removes choice, the original permissibility (ibaha mutlaqa) applies. The author also distinguishes between complete compulsion, where refusal would lead to death, and incomplete compulsion. Under complete compulsion, consumption becomes permissible and even obligatory (to save life), and refusal is sinful. Under incomplete compulsion, consumption is not permissible, but if done, the hadd penalty (for drinking wine) is not applied due to the doubt created by the partial compulsion.

Key Terms
الإكراه الملجئ (al-ikraahu al-mulji'): Compulsion that forces one to act by threat of death or severe harm, leaving no meaningful choice.
الحرمة التي تحتمل السقوط أصلاً (al-hurmatu allati tahtamilu as-suqoota aslan): A prohibition that can be completely nullified due to necessity, as its basis is conditional on choice.

Definition and Core Principle of Rukhsa in Hanafi Usul
Defining rukhsa and distinguishing it from azima

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of rukhsa and identify its essential condition that the original prohibition remains.

Core Idea
Rukhsa permits an act under duress but does not remove the original prohibition.

In this passage, al-Bazdawi clarifies the concept of rukhsa—a legal concession—which is not an absolute permission but a temporary dispensation while the underlying ruling remains. Understanding this distinction is crucial for analyzing cases of coercion and necessity across the Shariah.

2
Analyze the condition 'مع قيام المحرم'
Explain what this condition means: the original prohibition is still in place; rukhsa does not repeal it.
This distinguishes rukhsa from ibaha (absolute permission).
3
Identify examples of rukhsa in the passage
List the examples given: prayer, fasting, ihram, property consumption, zina under coercion. Note that each act remains essentially prohibited but is allowed under duress.
All examples are of acts that are normally haram but become permissible due to an excuse.

Learning Objective

Core Idea

Concluding Remarks on Rukhsah and Azimah
Concluding discussion on license and resolution

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between rukhsah (license) and azimah (resolution) and apply the rule that rukhsah is conditional, not absolute permission.

Core Idea
Rukhsah is a conditional license, not absolute permission; refraining despite necessity may be virtuous.

In this concluding section of al-Bazdawi's Usul, the author summarizes the nature of rukhsah (license) in the Shariah. He clarifies that while licenses exist to address necessity, they do not turn prohibited acts into absolute permissibility. Instead, they carry conditions and consequences, such as compensation or even martyrdom for refraining.

Guided Path
1
Identify the foundational principle
The text states that the foundation of Shariah is tawhid and faith, which is not subject to annulment. This sets the context for understanding rukhsah as a substitution, not a repeal.

2
Define rukhsah and azimah
From the text, formulate definitions: rukhsah is a temporary license that substitutes the original ruling due to necessity; azimah is the original, strict ruling that applies normally.
The key distinction is that rukhsah does not abrogate azimah but provides a conditional alternative.
3
Analyze the examples
The text gives two examples: eating another's food during famine and using prohibited items during ihram. Identify that in both cases, a license (rukhsah) is granted, but with conditions: compensation for property, penalty for ihram violation.
Note the phrase 'لا إباحة مطلقة' (not absolute permission).
4
Formulate the rule
From the examples, state: Rukhsah is a conditional substitution; if used, consequences (daman, fidyah) apply; if refrained, the actor may be rewarded (martyr).
The rule is derived by reasoning (talil) from the foundational principle and the examples.
5
Distinguish from ibahah mutlaqah
Contrast rukhsah with absolute permission. The text explicitly says it is not ibahah mutlaqah; the original prohibition is merely suspended under strict conditions.
This distinction is vital for correct application of rukhsah in fiqh.

Commentary
This passage is the concluding section of al-Bazdawi's Usul al-Fiqh.
