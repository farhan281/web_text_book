Rukhsa Attached to the Condition, Not the Act
Usul rule on rukhsa attachment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the Hanafi usul principle that rukhsa (concession) is attached to the condition (sabab) rather than the act itself, and apply it to the case of a traveler fasting during Ramadan.

Core Idea
Rukhsa is attached to the cause, not the act—travel, not fasting, permits breaking the fast.

This lesson examines a key Hanafi usul distinction: whether a legal concession (rukhsa) is attached to the act itself or to the situation that triggers it. The example of a traveler in Ramadan illustrates the point. Understanding this distinction helps clarify when a concession is optional and when it becomes a strict obligation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the case
Consider a Muslim who is traveling during Ramadan. The original ruling ('azima) requires fasting, but a concession (rukhsa) allows breaking the fast.
2
Distinguish 'azima and rukhsa
'Azima is the primary strict ruling; rukhsa is a secondary ruling that lightens the obligation due to hardship. The key question is whether the rukhsa applies to the act of fasting itself or to the condition of travel.
3
Locate the attachment of the rukhsa
The text argues that the rukhsa is attached to travel (the condition), not to the act of fasting. This means that if a traveler chooses to fast, his fast is valid and counts as the Ramadan fast.
If the rukhsa were attached to the act, fasting would be impermissible or not count.
4
Consider the consequence
If the traveler fasts, the fast is valid. If he breaks it, he is not sinful and must make it up later. The rukhsa does not remove the act's validity; it merely grants permission to omit it.
5
Generalize the principle
This is a general Hanafi usul rule: a rukhsa is attached to the sabab (cause) that triggers it, not to the mahal (locus) of the act. The same principle applies elsewhere, e.g., wiping over khuffain is attached to wearing them, not to the act of wiping.

Rukhsa as a Bound Concession: The Hanafi Distinction
Defining rukhsa as concession with limits

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between rukhsa and ibaha mutlaqa by applying the Hanafi condition of strict necessity and identifying the consequence of exceeding it.

Core Idea
Rukhsa is a temporary concession for necessity, not absolute permission; exceeding it demands compensation.

This passage from the Usul of al-Bazdawi clarifies a critical distinction in Hanafi legal theory: the nature of rukhsa (concession) versus ibaha mutlaqa (absolute permissibility). Using the case of a starving person eating another's food, the author shows that rukhsa is a bound dispensation that does not remove the original prohibition's authority. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying rulings of necessity in Hanafi fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two terms: rukhsa and azima
Rukhsa is a legal concession due to necessity; azima is the original firm ruling. The passage focuses on distinguishing rukhsa from ibaha.
Azima is the general rule; rukhsa is an exception for hardship.
2
Locate the illustrative case
The example is a person compelled by calamity to eat someone else's food. The ruling is that it is a rukhsa, not ibaha mutlaqa.
The Arabic: 'حَل لتناول طعام غيره رخصة لا اباحةً مطلقة'
3
Compare the two outcomes
If one refrains and dies, they are a martyr; if they consume, they must guarantee (daman) the food's value. This shows rukhsa's limits.
The distinction between martyr and compensator is key.
4
Connect to the qiyas with ihram
The analogy with ihram prohibitions reinforces that rukhsa does not eliminate liability for exceeding necessity.
In ihram, taking a concession still requires fidyah if excess occurs.

Rukhsa in Belief vs. Actions: The 'Azima-Rukhsa Distinction
Clarifies scope of rukhsa in belief vs. actions

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the scope of rukhsa in Hanafi usul and distinguish between foundational beliefs (not subject to substitution) and actions (subject to concession with retained liability).

Core Idea
Rukhsa permits a normally prohibited act in necessity, but liability remains—it is not absolute permission.

This passage from Al-Bazdawi's Usul clarifies a key distinction: the core of faith cannot be replaced by a concession, but its performance can be relaxed in necessity. It also introduces the Hanafi principle that rukhsa does not remove liability, as illustrated by the examples of eating forbidden food under duress and violating ihram prohibitions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two categories
Recognize the passage's division between foundational beliefs (tawhid, iman) which cannot be substituted by rukhsa, and other obligations which may admit concession in performance.
Foundational beliefs are termed 'عمدة الشرع' (support of Shariah) and are immutable.
2
Distinguish 'azima from rukhsa
Define 'azima as the original, unrestricted ruling, and rukhsa as a concession that permits an otherwise prohibited act under necessity, but does not make it absolutely permissible.
Rukhsa retains the underlying obligation; it only relaxes the mode of performance.
3
Analyze the hunger example
Examine the case of a person forced by extreme hunger to eat another's property. Note that the act is permitted as a rukhsa, but if he eats, he must compensate (daman); if he refrains and dies, he is a martyr.
This shows rukhsa is not ibaha muthlaqa (absolute permission).
4
Apply the rule to the ihram example
Consider the pilgrim in ihram who, due to necessity, consumes a prohibited item. He is allowed (rukhsa) but must pay the penalty (jaza'), demonstrating the same principle.
The penalty is a form of liability that remains despite the concession.
5
Derive the Hanafi principle
Synthesize: In Hanafi usul, rukhsa does not nullify the original right or obligation; it only permits the act under duress while preserving compensation or penalty. Foundational beliefs (rights of Allah that are pillars of faith) are exempt from substitution.
This principle is crucial for understanding istihsan and the hierarchy of rulings.

However, in the performance of its requirements, rukhsa is allowed when there is genuine necessity (darura). Al-Mawhum wa al-Mutayaqqan: Acting on Doubt when Certainty is Lost
Abu Yusuf's ruling on the gift for udhiya

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-mawhum and al-mutayaqqan, and apply the Hanafi usul rule that when a certain prescribed act (mutayaqqan) is lost, acting upon the doubtful substitute (mawhum) becomes obligatory as a precaution.

Core Idea
When the certain act is lost, the doubtful substitute becomes binding out of caution.

In this passage, we encounter a core Hanafi usul principle: when the certain prescribed act (al-mutayaqqan) cannot be performed, its doubtful substitute (al-mawhum) takes effect as an obligation of precaution (ihtiyat). The case involves a gift given for the udhiya sacrifice. The certain act—the sacrifice on its appointed day—was missed. The question is whether the gift becomes a non-revocable substitute. Abu Yusuf's reasoning hinges on the interplay between certainty and doubt, a fundamental tool in Hanafi legal theory.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key terms
Locate the terms al-mutayaqqan (the certain prescribed act, here the udhiya sacrifice) and al-mawhum (the doubtful substitute, here the gift). Understand their contrasting roles.
Al-mutayaqqan is the sacrifice on its fixed day; al-mawhum is the gift considered a possible substitute.
2
Understand the scenario
A gift is given with the intention of using it for the udhiya sacrifice, but the time for the sacrifice passes. Can the donor revoke the gift? Abu Yusuf says no, because the gift has become a substitute for the missed sacrifice.
The gift is not merely a gift; it is tied to a religious obligation.
3
Apply the principle
Since the certain act (sacrifice) is lost due to its time passing, we must act on the doubtful substitute out of caution (ihtiyat). The gift therefore stands as a substitute and cannot be revoked.
This is a precautionary measure to ensure the spirit of the sacrifice is fulfilled.
4
Note the limitation
This ruling does not mean the substitute is identical to the original. Evidence: when the next year arrives, the ruling does not transfer to the sacrifice; the gift's status is temporary.
The substitute is only for that missed occasion, not a perpetual lien.

This exemplifies the usul principle of working with doubt (shubha) in the absence of certainty, and the role of caution in worship.

Qiyas to Fidyah and the Istihsan Exception in the Udhiya Gift Ruling
Qiyas and istihsan in the gift for udhiya case

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify how the analogy (qiyas) to fidyah is used and then overridden by istihsan (juristic preference) in the case of the gift for udhiya, demonstrating Hanafi usul methodology.

Core Idea
Istihsan can override qiyas when the substitute is established as an original from another aspect.

The passage not only states the ruling but also engages in a critical usul argument: a qiyas is proposed that would void the substitute gift when one becomes able to perform the original sacrifice, analogous to fidyah. However, the author explains that this analogy fails because the gift was established as an 'original' from the perspective of hospitality and the spirit of Eid. This is a textbook example of istihsan overriding qiyas, a hallmark of Hanafi usul.

1
Extract the qiyas argument
Identify the proposed analogy: the gift (substitute) should become void when one is able to perform the original sacrifice later, just as in fidyah where the ransom becomes void when the original act can be performed.

The Principle of Iqtida' (Legal Implication) in Hanafi Usul

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Iqtida' only drops conditions that are susceptible to dropping; delivery in a gift is not susceptible, so it remains required.

In this lesson, we explore the Hanafi usul principle of iqtida' (legal implication), where a statement implies a ruling not explicitly mentioned. The key example is the command to free a slave for payment, which implies a sale or gift. The scholars differ on whether this implication can dispense with the condition of physical delivery. Understanding this principle sharpens your ability to derive rulings from implied meanings.

Abu Yusuf treats it as a gift; Abu Hanifa and Muhammad treat it as a sale-like gift requiring delivery.

Commentary

Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul classifies licenses (rukhsah) into four types, two from the category of haqiqah (literal reality) and two from majaz (metaphorical extension). The author explains only the first two here. The first type is the most complete license: it occurs when an act is permitted despite the presence of the original prohibition and its ruling (i.e., the act remains unlawful in essence but is allowed due to necessity). The second type is a weaker license: the cause for the ruling is present but its effect is delayed. The Hanafi position holds that the resolve (azimah) is superior in this case, unlike al-Shafi'i who prefers the license. The text clarifies that resolve and license are complementary. All examples are drawn from Hanafi fiqh: coercion to disbelief, commanding good under threat, coercion to destroy property, extreme hunger, coerced breaking of fast, etc.

عزيمة (azimah): The original, stronger ruling without concession; e.g., fasting while traveling is azimah.
متراخ (mutarakhi): Delayed; used for a ruling that is postponed after the cause is complete.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
If an incident was common knowledge, an obscure hadith about it is likely a transmission error.

This lesson introduces a key analytical tool in Hanafi hadith criticism: the principle that when a historical incident was common knowledge among the Companions, any hadith claiming a different practice from the Prophet (peace be upon him) regarding that incident is suspect. The obscurity of the hadith, despite the fame of the incident, points to forgetfulness (sahw) on the part of the narrator. Understanding this principle allows us to differentiate between reliable and weak narrations and is part of the Hanafi method of istihsan.

Clarification can be derived from the context of the explicitly uttered words, not only from the words themselves.

In Hanafi usul, not all clarifications come from explicit statements. Some are derived from the necessary implications of what is said. This lesson introduces the concept of bayan al-darurah and delves into its first type: clarification that carries the same force as the uttered text (mantuq). Learning Objective

Core Idea
Before finding a cause, confirm the text is causable; the Hanafi path is two-step: suitability first, then distinction.

This passage from Usul al-Bazdawi presents a foundational dispute in Hanafi usul: can we assume that every ruling in the primary texts (usul) has a rational cause (illa) that can be extracted for qiyas? The text contrasts four positions, with the Hanafi view requiring a preliminary step: evidence that the text is a suitable 'witness' (shahid li al-hal) before even seeking a distinguishing indicator (dalil al-tamyiz). Understanding this layered requirement is key to Hanafi qiyas methodology.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four views
List each view briefly: (1) texts are not caused except by evidence, (2) every possible attribute is a cause unless prevented, (3) caused but need a distinguishing indicator (Shafi'i-like), (4) Hanafi: caused and witnessed except prevented, with both suitability and distinction.
The fourth view is the Hanafi one, elaborated in the passage.
2
Locate the Hanafi view and its two conditions
The Hanafi position states that rulings are caused (ma'lula) and the text is a witness (shahid) for the cause unless a preventer exists. But before that, evidence is required that the text is suitable as a witness (shahid li al-hal), and then a distinguishing indicator (dalil al-tamyiz) is needed to identify the specific cause among possible attributes.
This two-step process distinguishes Hanafis from the third view.
3

The example is gold and silver in riba: Hanafis take weight as the cause; Shafi'is do not accept weight, requiring a different cause. This illustrates how the dispute applies to actual fiqh rulings.
The example shows the thamarah al-khilaf (practical consequence) of the disagreement.
4

The first view argues that moving from text to cause is like moving from literal to metaphorical, requiring evidence. The second view compares to hadith narration: all just narrators are accepted unless disqualified. The third view says causality by all attributes blocks qiyas, so only one cause is certain but unknown, requiring a distinguishing indicator. The Hanafi response adds the prior need for suitability.
The Hanafi argument uses istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity) to keep the text as potentially causable, but it cannot prove the branch by probability alone.
5

Practice the framework: given a text (e.g., the prohibition of wine), first ask: is this text of the causable type (shahid li al-hal)? Then, what is the distinguishing indicator (e.g., intoxication) among attributes?

Commentary
This passage is a usul dispute about whether the rulings established in primary texts (usul, such as Quran and Sunnah) have known rational causes (illa) that can be extracted for analogical reasoning (qiyas). The text presents four views. The first view holds that the rulings are not known to have a cause unless evidence proves it. The second view holds that every possible attribute qualifies as a cause unless there is a preventer. The third view, attributed to al-Shafi'i, holds that a cause exists but a distinguishing indicator is necessary. The fourth view is the Hanafi position: rulings are caused and testify to the cause unless prevented, but before that one must establish that the text is suitable for being a witness (i.e., it is not a purely devotional ruling), and also a distinguishing indicator (dilalat al-tamyiz) is required. The passage then gives arguments: the first group argues that a text is binding by its wording, and moving to a cause is like moving from literal to metaphorical, requiring evidence. The second group compares it to hadith narration: since consensus is impossible, every just narrator's report is accepted unless disqualified; similarly, all attributes are valid as causes unless prevented. The third view says that causality would be pointless if every attribute were a cause; it would block qiyas, so only one cause is certain after eliminating the whole set, but it remains unknown, requiring a distinguishing indicator. The Hanafi response adds that before the indicator, we must confirm the text is not a purely devotional ruling; otherwise it could belong to the category of uncaused rulings. The original text is not negated by mere probability, but it also cannot prove the branch by probability alone—this is like istishab al-hal (presumption of continuity). The Hanafis anchor the need for evidence of suitability (shahid al-hal) before turning to the cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between apparent qiyas and istihsan based on the strength of the hidden evidence (athar) and apply the Hanafi rule to determine which prevails.

Core Idea
Istihsan overrides qiyas not by whim but because a stronger hidden evidence outweighs the apparent but weaker analogy.

The Hanafi school uniquely accepts istihsan as a source of law, but critics accuse it of being arbitrary. In this passage, al-Bazdawi clarifies that istihsan is in fact based on stronger evidence—be it a transmitted proof, consensus, necessity, or a subtler analogy. Through concrete examples, he demonstrates how the weight of the underlying effect (athar) determines the ruling, not the superficial clarity of the analogy.

Guided Path
1
Identify the competing methods

The apparent qiyas is the first intuitive analogy; istihsan provides a different result.
2
Examine the hidden evidence (athar)
Look deeper: what evidence does istihsan rely on that is not captured by the surface analogy? For the bird example, it is the pure bone and drinking method.
The athar can be a transmitted proof, a rational imperative, or a hidden qiyas.
3
Weigh the strength of evidence
Compare the permanence, logical necessity, or textual grounding of the hidden evidence against the apparent qiyas. The one with stronger effect prevails.
Al-Bazdawi analogizes to the world vs. the hereafter: the hidden is stronger because it is eternal.

Apply the principle: the stronger athar overrides the weaker qiyas. This yields the istihsan ruling as the operative legal position.
The qiyas is not invalid but is superseded due to its weaker effect.
5
Distinguish between types of istihsan

The passage then distinguishes between two types of istihsan: one based on transmitted evidence (athar), consensus, or necessity, which cannot be extended analogically; and another based on hidden qiyas, which can be extended. This is a classic Hanafi usul teaching that istihsan is not arbitrary but based on stronger concealed evidence.

القياس (al-qiyas): Analogical reasoning; extending a ruling from an original case to a new case based on shared effective cause (illa).
الأثر (al-athar): The underlying evidence or effect that determines the strength of a ruling; it can be a transmitted proof or a rational imperative.

The Fourth Type of Argumentative Shift: Inqita' and the Exception of Ibrahim
Explaining the fourth type of transition and its ruling.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the fourth type of argumentative shift, state the Hanafi ruling that it constitutes inqita', and distinguish the exception of Ibrahim (AS) on the grounds of fear of confusion.

Core Idea
Shifting to a new proof after rebuttal disconnects the argument; only fear of confusion justifies an exception.

In legal debate, once a proof is rebutted, can the arguer start a new line of reasoning for the same claim? The Hanafi school answers with a clear principle: such a shift is a disconnection (inqita') because the purpose of debate is conclusive proof. However, the story of Ibrahim (AS) appears to show a shift. This lesson unpacks the ruling and its exception.

Guided Path
1
Identify the fourth type of transition
The author describes the fourth type as moving to a completely new proof for the same ruling after the first proof has been rebutted. Some scholars (opposing) consider it good (istihsan), citing Ibrahim (AS) in Surah Al-Baqarah 2:258.
This is distinct from earlier types where the same attribute is used for another ruling.
2
State the Hanafi ruling
The correct view (al-sahih) is that such a shift is considered a disconnection (inqita') because speculation (nazar) was legislated to clarify truth conclusively; an inconclusive argument fails to clarify.
Analogous to an unavoidable rebuttal (naqd) where a new attribute cannot rescue the argument.
3
Analyze the exception (Ibrahim's story)
The Hanafi response distinguishes Ibrahim's case: his first proof was already conclusive (the tyrant's rebuttal was false), and he only moved to another proof to remove confusion from the audience, not because his argument was rebutted.
The shift was to 'another refutation' (daf'), not a new proof after rebuttal.
4
Apply the principle to legal debate
In a usul dispute, if a scholar's proof is rebutted, he cannot start a new proof from scratch. He must either defend the original proof or concede. Exception: if the first proof is already conclusive but there is fear of confusion, a clarifying shift is allowed.
This upholds the integrity of argumentation (jadal) in fiqh.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
Prolonged unconsciousness drops the obligation to make up prayers due to istihsan, even though qiyas would require it like sleep.

In this passage, the author contrasts unconsciousness (ighma) with sleep to illustrate a key usul principle: when does hardship (haraj) justify departing from the qiyas? While sleep does not generally drop obligations, unconsciousness—if prolonged—does, because extending it would cause undue hardship. This is a classic Hanafi application of istihsan overriding qiyas.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two states compared
The author compares unconsciousness (ighma) with sleep (nawm). Note how each affects the validity of acts of worship and the ability to continue (bina') in prayer. Sleep is an original state (asli) that does not always invalidate, while unconsciousness is an accidental (aradi) condition that fundamentally contradicts strength.
Both involve loss of choice and power, but their legal effects differ.

Analyze the qiyas argument
The default qiyas would treat unconsciousness like sleep: obligations (like making up prayers) are not dropped. Trace the analogy: sleep does not drop obligations, so unconsciousness should not either. This is the qiyas that would apply if there were no countervailing consideration.
Qiyas here is based on the shared feature of loss of control.
3
Identify the istihsan override
The author invokes istihsan (juristic preference) to depart from the qiyas. The key reason is that unconsciousness can extend for a long time (exceeding a day and night), causing hardship (haraj). This hardship provides a stronger basis for dropping the obligation to make up prayers.
Istihsan is a recognized source in Hanafi usul, often overriding qiyas.
4
Examine the hadith evidence
The author cites the agreed-upon hadith of a person who bathed and then became unconscious. The hadith supports the ruling that prayer obligations are dropped during prolonged unconsciousness. This combines text and reasoning (istihsan).
The hadith is used as a supporting proof for the istihsan ruling.
5
Distinguish the scope for other acts
The author notes that this ruling is specific to prayers because prolonged unconsciousness in prayer is not rare, while in fasting and zakat it is rare. Thus, the istihsan applies only where hardship is likely.

This passage shifts to slavery (riqq) as a legal incapacity that is indivisible. The key usul debate is whether emancipation (itq) can be partial. Abu Hanifa argues that ownership is divisible, so emancipating a portion produces partial effect (mukatab status), while the Sahibayn insist that freedom is indivisible, so any emancipation is total. The discussion highlights how usul principles of divisibility (tajazzu') affect fiqh rulings.

Guided Path
1
State the usul issue
The issue is whether the act of emancipation (itq) can be partial: if someone says 'you are half-free', does that make the slave half-free, or is the entire act ineffective? The debate involves the divisibility of the cause (ownership removal) and the effect (freedom).
This is a classic Hanafi internal dispute (Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn).
2
Identify Abu Hanifa's position and reasoning
Abu Hanifa holds that the partially emancipated person becomes a mukatab (slave with a contract of manumission), not fully free. His reasoning: emancipation is the removal of ownership, which is divisible, attached to a ruling (freedom) that is indivisible. Since the cause is divisible, partial removal achieves part of the cause, analogous to the limbs of wudu' being divisible (washing each limb) while the effect (permissibility of prayer) is indivisible.
Abu Hanifa uses the analogy of wudu limbs to show that divisible causes can lead to an indivisible effect.
3
Identify the Sahibayn's position and reasoning
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that emancipation is an act whose effect (freedom) is indivisible, so the act itself must be indivisible. They analogize to divorce (talaq), where the act of divorcing one-third is invalid because divorce is indivisible.
Their analogy is based on the indivisibility of the effect enforcing indivisibility of the cause.
4
Examine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: according to Abu Hanifa, a partially freed person remains a slave in many rulings (e.g., testimony) but with a path to full freedom through mukatabah. According to Sahibayn, he becomes fully free immediately and cannot be treated as a slave.
This directly impacts the legal capacity of the individual.
5
Reflect on the usul principle of tajazzu'
The dispute centers on whether a cause (sabab) can be divisible when its effect is indivisible. Abu Hanifa says yes, as seen in wudu; Sahibayn say no. The Hanafi school generally follows Abu Hanifa’s view on this issue, though later fatwa may take the other view in some contexts.
This illustrates how usul principles about causality affect fiqh.

Why Qisas is Not Inherited: The Hanafi Rationale
Explaining why qisas is not inherited based on its purpose (ta'lil).

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position on the inheritability of qisas and explain the legal reasoning (ta'lil) based on its purpose.

Core Idea
Qisas is not inherited because its purpose is vengeance for the living heirs, not benefit for the deceased.

This passage from Usul al-Bazdawi explains why the Hanafi school holds that qisas (retaliation) is not inherited like property. The author distinguishes qisas from diyah and clarifies that qisas is a personal right of the heirs, not part of the deceased's estate. Through careful analysis of the purpose (maqsad), Abu Hanifa demonstrates that qisas serves the living, while diyah fulfills the needs of the deceased.

Guided Path
1
Identify the ruling on qisas inheritance
State Abu Hanifa's ruling: qisas is not inherited. This is the conclusion that the passage justifies.
2
Pinpoint the purpose (illa) of qisas
Find the phrase that defines the purpose: 'darak al-tha'r' (satisfaction of vengeance) and protection of the heirs' lives. This purpose is directed to the living, not the deceased.
3
Apply the usul principle
A right that serves the living is not transmitted to the deceased. Since qisas benefits the heirs, it is not part of the inheritance (which accrues to the deceased).
This is a ta'lil (reasoning) based on the maqsad of the legislation.
4
Contrast with diyah
When qisas converts to diyah (due to necessity), it becomes inherited because diyah serves the deceased's needs (e.g., settling debts). This contrast reinforces the principle.
5
Draw the practical consequence
Since qisas is owned individually by each heir, if one heir forgives or executes it, the right is nullified for all. This would not happen if it were divisible property.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A contract made in jest is valid unless both parties build upon the jest – the literal offer overrides hidden collusion.

This lesson examines a key usul dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two chief students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, over the validity of contracts when the parties have engaged in jest (hazl) or prior collusion (mawadi'ah). The central question: does the apparent seriousness of the contract's wording prevail, or does hidden intention or prior agreement override it? The dispute highlights the tension between literal interpretation (zahir) and custom (urf) in Hanafi usul, and touches on concepts of istihsan and naskh. By understanding this dispute, you will grasp how different usul principles lead to divergent rulings in everyday transactions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three scenarios
The passage discusses three situations: (1) both parties agree to turn away from collusion (i'rad), (2) both build upon the collusion, (3) they are silent or disagree about collusion. Identify which scenarios are disputed.
Scenarios 1 and 2 are agreed upon; scenario 3 is disputed.
2
State Abu Hanifa's ruling and dalil
Abu Hanifa rules the contract valid in scenario 3 (both silence and disagreement). His dalil: the contract is legislated for seriousness (jidd), and jest is not textually connected to it. He applies the principle 'al-akhir nāsikh' – the later action (the contract) abrogates the prior collusion.
This is a qiyas-based argument prioritizing literal validity.
3
State the Sahibayn's ruling and dalil
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule the contract invalid in scenario 3. Their dalil: custom ('urf) is considered; the prior collusion is earlier and should be acted upon unless a text overrides it. They give precedence to the earlier intention over the literal words.
This relies on istihsan (juristic preference) based on custom.
4
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference: in a typical sale where parties later claim jest, Abu Hanifa upholds the sale; the Sahibayn invalidate it if both admit jest or if one claims jest and the other seriousness, they accept the claim of jest.
This affects commercial transactions and fatwa.
5
Analyze the underlying usul principles
Identify which principles each side uses: Abu Hanifa relies on literal meaning (zahir) and the idea that the later action abrogates; the Sahibayn rely on custom and the earlier agreement. Consider which approach is more consistent with istihsan and which with strict qiyas.
This dispute illustrates the flexibility within Hanafi usul.

Commentary
This passage discusses the effect of jest (hazl) and collusion (mawadi'ah) on the validity of contracts, particularly sales, from a Hanafi usul perspective. It presents a dispute between Imam Abu Hanifa and his two students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani (the Sahibayn). The passage begins by stating that if both parties to a contract agree to turn away from their prior collusion (i.e., they intended jest but later show seriousness), the sale is valid because the reality of the contract allows rescission. But if they actually build their contract upon the collusion (intending jest throughout), then the contract is invalid by unanimous agreement. The dispute arises when both parties are silent about their intent or disagree: Abu Hanifa holds the contract valid in both cases, prioritizing the apparent validity of the offer (the literal exchange of offer and acceptance) over any hidden collusion. The Sahibayn, however, hold that if both are silent and agree that no intent (seriousness or jest) was present, the contract is invalid; if they disagree, they accept the word of the one who claims building (collusion) and thus invalidate the contract. The text then includes a side discussion about the phrase 'fima a'lam' (as far as I know) in testimonies and acknowledgments, clarifying that Abu Yusuf considered it binding, contrary to some other opinions. The passage concludes by restating Abu Hanifa's principle: contracts are legislated for seriousness, and jest is not textually connected to them, so the contract is given precedence over collusion. Finally, it addresses a scenario where both parties are serious but have a collusive agreement on price; if they later turn away from that collusion, the price is as agreed (two thousand). This discussion illustrates a key Hanafi usul principle: the apparent meaning (zahir) of words governs contracts unless there is clear text or mutual agreement on a different intent.

Key Terms
الهزل (al-hazl): Jest or joking intention that contradicts the serious legal effect of a contract.
المواضعة (al-mawadi'ah): Prior collusion or agreement to pretend a contract (e.g., to deceive a third party or create a false impression).
الإعراض (al-i'rad): Turning away from the prior collusion or jest, i.e., showing serious intent after the contract.

Compulsive Duress (Ikrah Mulji') and Attribution of Actions
States principle and applies it via fiqh examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul rule that under compulsive duress (ikrah mulji') the act is attributed to the coercer, and distinguish it from non-compelling duress and from command by analyzing the conditions of instrumentality and ambiguity.

Core Idea
Ikrah mulji' transforms the coerced into a mere instrument; liability shifts entirely to the coercer.

This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul establishes a foundational Hanafi principle: under compulsive duress, the coerced person becomes a tool (āla) of the coercer, and all legal consequences attach to the coercer. The author supports this with concrete fiqh cases and draws a parallel to the law of command (amr) in ambiguous situations. We will unpack the rule, its proof, and its limits.

Guided Path
1
Identify the central rule
Locate the statement that the act's existence is attributed to the coercer and the coerced is removed from the chain. This is the core principle.
The phrase 'صار ابتداء وجود الفعل مضافاً إليه' is key.
2
Analyze the proof by cases
Examine the three applications: homicide under duress (qisas on coercer), accidental killing while coerced to hunt (diya and kaffara on coercer), and property destruction (coercer liable). Each illustrates the rule's effect.
Note that the coerced is completely exonerated.
3
Compare with the law of command (amr)
The author analogizes duress to a valid command. Identify the condition for a command to transfer liability: ambiguity (ishkāl) about the location or ownership. Without ambiguity, the command is invalid.
This analogy reveals the illa (effective cause) for transfer: the subordinate's lack of independent choice.
4
Distinguish exceptions
Recognize when transfer does not occur: for acts that are inherently personal (eating, intercourse) or when the locus is clear (public road). These cases show that instrumentality (āliyya) is a necessary condition.
The text also notes that killing a free man by command does not transfer; only duress always does.

Definition of Rukhsa and Application under Duress
Defines rukhsa and applies it to duress scenarios in life, property, and honor.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define rukhsa and apply the Hanafi distinction between complete and lesser duress to determine whether a concession is granted and whether hadd punishment is imposed.

Core Idea
Rukhsa permits an act under duress while the prohibition remains; the degree of duress determines the concession.

In this passage from Al-Bazdawi's Usul, we encounter the critical Hanafi concept of rukhsa – a concession that allows a normally prohibited act when a valid excuse exists, without removing the underlying prohibition. The text then applies this definition to cases of duress, distinguishing between complete threats (death, amputation) and lesser ones (imprisonment), and explores how this distinction affects the hadd punishment for a woman forced to commit zina. Understanding this distinction is essential for correctly applying the principle of rukhsa in matters of life, property, and honor.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of rukhsa
Locate the sentence that defines rukhsa: 'ما يُستباح بعذر مع قيام المحرم' (that which is permitted due to an excuse while the prohibition remains). Note that the prohibition persists; only the act is temporarily allowed.
2
Understand the distinction between azima and rukhsa
The text contrasts rukhsa with azima (the original strict ruling). While azima remains in force, rukhsa is a concession for an excuse. For example, the act of killing under duress remains prohibited in essence, but the person is excused if forced.
This is a key feature of Hanafi usul: the prohibition is not lifted, only the sin is removed for the duration of the excuse.
3
Analyze the application examples
The text provides examples: striking the heart (jinaaya), invalidating prayer, breaking fast, killing game in ihram, and consuming property under duress. For each, note that the concession is granted because the sanctity of life is superior.
The reasoning is talil (causal): the higher right (life) is preserved by allowing the lower right to be violated temporarily.
4
Differentiate complete vs lesser duress
Complete duress (threat of death/amputation) necessitates a clear concession. Lesser duress (imprisonment) creates a doubt (shubha) that prevents hadd punishment for a woman in zina, but not for a man.
This specific ruling is unique to the Hanafi school and illustrates how the degree of duress affects the legal outcome.
5
Apply the definition to a new scenario
Given a scenario of duress, ask: (1) Is the duress complete or lesser? (2) Is the right involved that of Allah or of a human? (3) Does rukhsa apply? (4) What is the consequence for punishment?
Practice with the woman-imprisonment case: lesser duress → no hadd, due to shubha.
