Classification of Time-Bound Worship: Time as Container, Condition, and Cause
Introducing the types of time-bound worship: time as container, condition, and cause.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify time-bound worship into categories where time functions as zarf, shart, and sabab, and identify the four sub-types of the first division based on attribution to parts of time.

Core Idea
Time for prayer is not just a container—it is also a condition for validity and a cause for obligation.

This lesson unpacks al-Bazdawi's classification of commanded acts (al-ma'mur bih) with respect to time. He begins by dividing worship into absolute (mutlaqa) and time-bound (muwaqqata). The focus then shifts to the first category of time-bound worship, where time serves three distinct legal functions: container, condition, and cause. Understanding these functions is essential for determining the validity and obligation of acts like prayer. The passage further breaks down this category into four sub-types based on which part of the time the act is attributed to, providing a nuanced framework for temporal analysis in usul al-fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Distinguish absolute vs. time-bound worship
Read the opening sentence: worship is two types. Absolute (mutlaqa) has no specified time, e.g., charity. Time-bound (muwaqqata) has a prescribed time. Identify which category applies to prayer.
This is the foundational dichotomy.
2
Identify the three roles of time in the first type of muwaqqata
Look for the statement: time is made a container (zarf), condition (shart), and cause (sabab). The example is prayer. Explain each function: container = act occurs within it; condition = if time passes, act is missed; cause = performing before time is invalid.
These three roles are simultaneous but distinct.
3
List the four sub-types of attribution
The passage enumerates four attributions: (1) to the first part, (2) to the part after commencement, (3) to the deficient part when time is narrow, and (4) to the entire time. Paraphrase each with prayer examples.
Each sub-type affects when the act is considered performed.
4
Recognize the second category of muwaqqata
The passage begins introducing a second division where time is a standard (mi'yar) and a cause, but it is cut off. Note that this incomplete classification is a preview.
This category may be elaborated later in the text.

Key Terms
مطلقة (mutlaqa): Absolute worship; worship that has no specific time.
مؤقتة (muwaqqata): Time-bound worship; worship that has a specific time assigned to it.

شرط (shart): Condition; the time is necessary for the act's performance; if time passes, the act is invalid.

The Effect of the Particle 'Bal' in Legal Language
Explains legal effect of 'bal' in divorce and debts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to analyze the legal effect of the Arabic particle 'bal' in statements of divorce and debt, and distinguish its treatment in reports (ikhbar) versus creations (insha') according to Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
'Bal' corrects reports but cannot undo an existing legal commitment.

The Arabic particle 'bal' is often misunderstood as simply negating the first statement. In Hanafi legal theory, its effect depends critically on whether the statement is a report (ikhbar) or a performative utterance (insha'). This lesson unpacks the rule as explained in Usul al-Bazdawi, focusing on its application in conditional divorce and acknowledgment of debts.

Guided Path
1
Identify Linguistic Function
Recognize that 'bal' is used to affirm the second statement and turn away from the first by way of correction (tadarruk). This is its core meaning in Arabic grammar.
Example: 'Zaid came to me, bal 'Amr' means the speaker corrects himself to say 'Amr came.'
2
Distinguish Reports from Creations
Determine whether the utterance is a report (ikhbar) or a performative creation (insha'). In reports, correction is possible; the second replaces the first. In creations like immediate divorce or debt acknowledgment, the first takes effect instantly and cannot be undone.
Hanafi usul: tadarruk is valid only in ikhbar.
3
Apply to Debt Acknowledgment
If someone says 'I owe a thousand dirhams, bal two thousand,' the Hanafi ruling is that he is liable for three thousand. The first commitment stands because he lacks authority to nullify it.
Contrast with Zufar's view that only two thousand is owed.
4
Apply to Immediate Divorce
If a husband says to his wife before consummation 'You are divorced once, bal twice,' only one divorce occurs. The first binds immediately; 'bal' cannot retract it.
Same applies to 'no bal twice.'
5
Apply to Conditional Divorce
In a conditional divorce before consummation, saying 'If you enter the house, you are divorced once, bal twice' results in three divorces upon entry. The second statement is treated as an independent conditional clause with its own condition.
This differs from conjunction with 'wa,' which shares the condition.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of the Arabic particle 'bal' (بل) in legal contexts, specifically in conditional divorce and debts. The author explains that 'bal' is used to affirm the second statement and turn away from the first by way of correction (tadarruk). In reports (ikhbar), correction is possible, so the second statement replaces the first. However, in creations (insha') like immediate divorce, the first takes effect instantly and cannot be retracted. The Hanafi position (opposing Zufar) holds that in debts, saying 'a thousand, bal two thousand' results in liability for three thousand because the first commitment is not voided. Similarly, in divorce before consummation, saying 'once, bal twice' results in only one divorce because the first separation occurred. But in conditional divorce (before consummation), where the condition is not yet fulfilled, saying 'once, bal twice' results in three divorces upon fulfillment because the second condition is considered an independent oath. The passage contrasts this with conjunction using 'wa' (واو), which shares the same condition. Finally, a principle is stated: when conjunctive constructions have conflicting grammatical aspects, the linguistically stronger one prevails; if equal, the closer one. An example from al-Jami' is given: 'You are divorced if you enter the house, no bal this one' for another woman is attached to the result (jaza'), not the condition, because attaching to the condition would be grammatically improper due to the lack of an emphasized pronoun.

Key Terms
بل (bal): A particle used to affirm the second statement and turn away from the first by way of correction (tadarruk).
التدارك (al-tadarruk): Correction or retraction; the intended function of 'bal' to replace the first statement with the second.
إنشاء (insha'): Performative utterance; a statement that creates a legal effect immediately upon utterance (e.g., divorce, sale, oath).
إخبار (ikhbar): Informative statement; a report that can be true or false and can be corrected.

The Letter Ba in Hanafi Usul: Meaning of Attachment
Analyzing the letter ba and its legal meaning of attachment

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state that the primary meaning of ba is attachment (ilṣāq) and apply this meaning to distinguish between valid contracts and oaths, and evaluate the Shafi'i and Maliki positions on the wiping verse.

Core Idea
In Hanafi usul, the letter ba denotes attachment (ilṣāq), not partiality or pleonasm.

Understanding the meaning of prepositions is foundational to deriving rulings from the Qur'an and Sunnah. This lesson focuses on the letter ba (ب), a key preposition in legal texts. The Hanafi school firmly asserts that ba indicates attachment (الإلصاق), and this has direct consequences for contracts, oaths, and the interpretation of the Quranic verse on wiping the head during wudu. We will unpack the Hanafi position, its proofs, and how it differs from the views of al-Shafi'i and Malik.

Guided Path
1
State the Hanafi definition of ba
Identify that the Hanafi position is that ba means attachment (ilṣāq). The author states: 'As for the letter ba, it is for attachment, according to the usage of the Arabs, and it has a specific meaning that is its literal reality.'
Memorize this as an axiom of Hanafi usul.
2
Apply the definition to a contract example
Analyze the two contract phrases: 'I bought this slave for a kurr of wheat' vs. 'I bought a kurr of wheat' (then describing it with the slave). In the first, the ba attaches the price to the principal (slave), making the sale valid. In the second, without ba, it becomes a salam contract with deferred delivery. Understand that ba transforms the price into a condition (shart) attached to the principal.
This illustrates the legal effect of ba in muamalat.
3
Apply the definition to an oath example
Examine the statement: 'If you inform me of the arrival of so-and-so, my slave is free.' The ba in 'bi qudūmih' (of his arrival) indicates that the report must be attached to the arrival, i.e., truthful. Without ba (e.g., 'If you inform me that so-and-so has arrived'), the statement includes falsehood as well. Understand the role of ba in determining the scope of oaths.
The ba restricts the object of informing to actual arrival.
4
Analyze the madhhab dispute on the wiping verse
Review the positions: Shafi'i holds ba indicates partiality (tab'īḍ) – wipe part of the head. Malik holds ba is pleonastic (ṣila) – wipe the whole head. Hanafi rejects both: ba is for attachment, so the verse means 'wipe your hands over your heads' (attach hands to heads), resulting in wiping the entire head with wet hands.
The dispute is purely linguistic; each school's ruling on wiping follows from its interpretation of ba.
5
Evaluate the Hanafi response
The Hanafi response relies on the principle that ba has a single literal meaning (attachment) and that alternatives (partiality, pleonasm) have no linguistic foundation or are only used when necessary. The author argues that repetition and homonymy do not fundamentally occur in language.
This reflects the Hanafi usul principle: al-aṣl fī al-kalām al-ḥaqīqah (the default in speech is the literal meaning).

The Particle 'fi' as a Legal Indicator: Zarf, Ambiguity, and Intention
Analyzing the particle 'fi' and its legal effects in divorce and oaths

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the particle 'fi' as a zarf marker and apply the Hanafi rule that its presence or absence in temporal expressions affects the legal outcome in divorce and oaths.

Core Idea
The particle 'fi' as a zarf: its presence creates ambiguity; its absence makes the time definite.

In this lesson, we examine the Arabic particle 'fi' and how its literal container meaning generates distinct legal rulings. The Hanafi school's treatment of 'fi' exemplifies the careful attention to linguistic precision in deriving legal effects, especially in divorce formulations. The ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) on this point demonstrates how a single particle can alter the applicability of a husband's intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the particle and its primary function
Recognize that 'fi' (في) is a preposition designating a container (zarf) in time or space. This literal meaning is the starting point for all derived legal rulings.
The zarf may be temporal, spatial, or metaphorical.
2
Distinguish between presence and omission of 'fi'
In temporal expressions like 'tomorrow' (غداً) vs. 'on tomorrow' (في غد), note the difference: without 'fi', the time is direct; with 'fi', the connection is mediated, introducing ambiguity.
This distinction is the core of the ikhtilaf.
3
Analyze Abu Hanifa's ruling
When 'fi' is omitted, divorce attaches to the entire time span, so the beginning is specified and the husband's intention to delay is not accepted. When 'fi' is present, divorce attaches to an ambiguous part, and the husband's intention clarifies the ambiguity.
The judge believes his intention in the latter case.
4
Compare with the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad consider 'fi' redundant in temporal contexts, so both formulations are equivalent. Their view simplifies but may overlook the literal zarf meaning.
The practical consequence (thamarah) is temporal: when may the husband delay?
5
Apply to other contexts
The same principle extends to oaths (fasting forever vs. in time), spatial expressions (immediate divorce unless a verb is implied), and financial expressions (ten dirhams in ten dirhams defaults to ten unless conjunction is intended).
Review the furu' in al-Ziyadat for further application.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle distinguishing a modifying explanation from a retraction in the case of an acknowledgment of price with a claim of non-receipt.

Core Idea
Claiming non‑receipt after acknowledging a price is either a modifying explanation or a retraction – the two differ based on connection.

In this passage, we encounter a dispute between Abu Hanifa and his two disciples, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad. The question is whether a buyer who acknowledges the price of a slave‑girl but claims he never received her is making a valid modifying explanation or an invalid retraction of his acknowledgment. Understanding this distinction clarifies how the Hanafi school treats statements that appear to contradict an earlier acknowledgment.

The recipient’s reaction changes the analysis.

For the two, non‑receipt is a possible attribute of the sale; it does not contradict the sale but modifies the obligation. Therefore, if connected, it is a valid modifying explanation (bayan mughayyar).

Bayan al-Dharurah: Explanation by Necessity and Its Four Types
Definition and classification of bayan al-dharurah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify each of the four types of bayan al-dharurah and apply the first type to derive an implied ruling from a text that states only one party's share.

Core Idea
Explanation by necessity conveys meaning through structure, not silence—implied shares are as binding as explicit ones.

In Hanafi usul, not every ruling is stated directly. Sometimes the speaker's need to clarify one point inevitably clarifies another. This is bayan al-dharurah—explanation forced by necessity. This lesson unpacks its four categories, focusing on the first type which holds the same weight as explicit speech. You will learn to spot how the Quran and Hanafi jurists use structure to imply shares without saying them outright.

Guided Path

State its definition: an explanation that occurs through means not originally placed for that meaning, driven by necessity.

Use the Arabic terms as anchors.

Analyze the Quranic example for type 1
Inheritance verse (4:11): the father's share is not stated but derived from the mother's specified third and the implied partnership.
The beginning of the verse implies both parents inherit; specifying the mother's third necessarily gives the father the remainder.
4

In mudarabah, stating the mudarib's share implies the rabb al-mal's share by the partnership fixed at the contract's start. Similarly, in a bequest, specifying one legatee's amount clarifies the other's.
This is istihsan, not mere silence.
5
Distinguish from other types
Note that type 2 uses the speaker's context, type 3 repels harm, type 4 ensures coherent speech. Only type 1 equals explicit speech in strength.
Focus on type 1 for this lesson; others are mentioned for completeness.

Conjunction as Definition in Presumed Constructs
usul rule on conjunction as definition

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule that conjunction (ʿaṭf) in a presumed construct serves to define the annexed term, and distinguish cases where it does not.

Core Idea
Conjunction defines a presumed term just like idafah, but not concrete nouns.

This passage from al-Pazdawi's usul work clarifies a subtle point in linguistic interpretation: when does conjunction ('atf) actually specify an ambiguous term? The key lies in whether the first element is a presumed (muqaddar) quantity or a concrete noun like 'garment' or 'horse'. Understanding this rule helps you correctly interpret financial utterances and avoid ambiguity in contracts.

Guided Path
1
Identify the phrase and check if the first element is muqaddar
Look at the expression: if the first part is a number (e.g., 'one and twenty') or a presumed quantity, it is muqaddar. Concrete nouns like 'garment' or 'horse' are not muqaddar.
A number by itself is ambiguous until specified.
2
Determine if the conjunction links quantities or concrete nouns
Conjunction linking numbers to a noun (e.g., 'one and twenty dirhams') indicates that the noun defines the whole. If linking concrete nouns (e.g., 'garment and horse'), no definition occurs.
The key is whether the first part is a presumed construct.
3
Apply the unity (ittihad) principle
Abu Yusuf held that conjunction proves unity, similar to idafah. So 'one hundred and a garment' means 'one hundred garments' because the numbers and item are considered one unit.
This only applies when the second item can be divided into the same category.
4
Recognize the exception
If the second item is a distinct entity like a slave (ʿabd), conjunction does not define because it does not share the same category as the number.
A slave is not a countable unit like dirhams or garments.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the three components of iman (asl, mulhaq, zawa'id) and explain their legal implications, including the validity of coerced faith and the ranking of worship acts.

Core Idea
Iman's essence is heart-affirmation (tasdiq), never invalidated; verbal confession (iqrar) is a pillar annexed, and its branches are ranked by the nature of blessing and intermediaries.

This passage from the Hanafi usul text breaks down iman into its original core, annexed elements, and outer branches. Guided Path

Identify the three categories
The passage states that iman has an original (asl), an annexed (mulhaq), and additions/branches (zawa'id). Recognise these as distinct levels.

Iqrar (verbal confession) was originally evidence of inner faith but has become a legal pillar (rukn) annexed to tasdiq. In worldly and afterlife rulings, it is treated as a mandatory component, but its absence due to coercion does not negate iman if tasdiq exists.
Annexed (mulhaq) shares the ruling of the original but not its essence.

The author ranks salah and then zakat among the branches of iman. The ranking considers the nature of the blessing (body vs wealth) and the presence of intermediaries (Kaaba vs poor recipient). Prayer is higher because it involves a direct blessing and an inanimate intermediary.
Iman itself is direct nearness without any intermediary, so it is highest.

سببه ، أو عدم أهليته : لا يُعدُّ جزاء. والعهدة نوعان: خالصة لا تلزم الصبي بحال. ومَشُوبةٌ يتوقف لزومها على رأي الولي، ولما كان الصبا عجزاً: صار من أسباب ولاية النظر، وقطع ولايته عن الأغيار. وأما العته بعد البلوغ : فمثل الصبا مع العقل في كل الأحكام، حتى إنه لا يمنع صحة القول والفعل، لكنه يمنع العهدة. وأما ضمان ما يستهلك من الأموال فليس بعهدة، لكنه شرع جبراً، وكونه صبياً معذوراً ، أو معتوهاً : لا ينافي عصمة المحل، ويوضع الخطاب عنه كما وُضع عن الصبي، ويولَّى عليه، ولا يلي على غيره.
The dhimma (legal capacity), and it does not contradict the ruling of the obligatory (wajib), which is reward in the afterlife if performance is possible. Do you not see that the insane person inherits and owns, and that is a form of guardianship (wilayah), except that performance is absent, so the obligation becomes non-existent based on it. For this reason we say: The insane person is held accountable for compensation of acts concerning property in full, because he is fit for its ruling as we said. So when capacity is established, this impediment becomes a cause of interdiction (hajr), and interdiction pertains to statements (his utterances become void). We said: Since his faith (iman) was not valid due to absence of its pillar, which is the contract (al-aqd), and performance as well, it was not interdiction, because absence of ruling due to absence of pillar is not from the category of interdiction. But faith is prescribed for him, so he became a believer following his parents. Likewise it is said in al-Jami': So obligation (taklif) is not valid in any respect except in rights of people (huquq al-ibad). For if the wife of an insane person becomes Muslim, Islam is presented to the guardian of the insane person to prevent injustice as much as possible. And whatever is harmful and admits of dropping is not prescribed for him. And whatever is bad (qabih) that does not admit of pardon is established for him, so that he becomes an apostate following his parents. And discrimination (al-tamyiz). As for minority (al-saghar) in its initial state, it is like insanity, because he lacks intellect. But when he attains intellect, he attains a kind of capacity for execution (ahliyyat al-ada'), but childhood is an excuse nonetheless. So what admits of dropping from the adult is dropped, and we say: The obligatory nature (fardiyyah) of faith is not dropped from him, so that if he performs it, it is obligatory, not supererogatory. Do you not see that if he believes in his childhood, rulings are incumbent upon him based on the validity of faith, and they are made following the obligatory faith. Therefore if he reaches puberty and does not repeat the testimony of faith, he is not considered an apostate. And if the first were supererogatory, it would not suffice for the obligatory. And the obligation (taklif) and imposition of performance are removed from him. In summary: we say that liability (al-uhdah) is removed from him, and his acts are valid, and he has what has no liability, because childhood is a cause of mercy, so it is made a cause of pardon for every liability that admits of pardon. Therefore he is not deprived of inheritance by killing, nor is he necessarily deprived by disbelief (kufr) or slavery (riqq), because slavery conflicts with capacity for inheritance, and likewise disbelief, because it conflicts with capacity for guardianship. And absence of right due to absence of its cause or lack of capacity is not considered a punishment. And liability is of two types: Pure, which does not bind the minor at all. And mixed, whose binding depends on the guardian's opinion. And since childhood is incapacity, it becomes a cause of guardianship of supervision (wilayat al-nazar) and cuts off his guardianship over others. As for imbecility (al-atah) after puberty, it is like minor with intelligence in all rulings, so that it does not prevent the validity of speech and action, but it prevents liability. As for compensation for what is consumed of property, it is not liability, but it is legislated as compensation (jabran). And the fact that he is a minor excused or an imbecile does not conflict with inviolability of the subject (ismat al-mahall). And address (khitab) is removed from him as it is removed from the minor. And a guardian is appointed over him, and he does not have guardianship over others.

Commentary
This passage is a ruling on legal capacity (ahliyyah) concerning the insane and the minor. The author begins by affirming that the insane person has dhimma (legal capacity) for ownership and inheritance, but not for obligations that require performance, because the obligation becomes non-existent if performance is impossible. The insane person is liable for property damages in full because he has capacity for the ruling of compensation. However, his utterances (like faith) are not valid due to missing a pillar (the contract/performance), but faith is prescribed for him derivatively through his parents. Obligation (taklif) is not valid for the insane except in rights of people (e.g., to prevent injustice). Acts that are harmful but can be dropped are not prescribed; acts that are inherently evil and do not admit pardon (like apostasy) are established, so the insane may become an apostate by following his parents. For the minor, initially lack of intellect resembles insanity. Once he acquires intellect, he has partial capacity for execution, but childhood is an excuse, so obligations that admit of dropping are dropped. However, the obligatory nature of faith is not dropped; if he performs it, it counts as fard, not nafl. This is evidenced by the fact that upon puberty he does not need to renew the shahada, showing his childhood faith was valid as fard. Liability (uhdah) is removed from the minor because childhood is a cause of mercy. He is not deprived of inheritance due to killing, nor by kufr or riqq, because those are punishments, but absence of right due to lack of cause or capacity is not punishment. Liability is two types: pure (does not bind minor at all) and mixed (depends on guardian's opinion). Since childhood is incapacity, it makes the minor subject to guardianship and cuts his own guardianship over others. Imbecility after puberty is like minority with intellect: it does not prevent validity of speech/acts but prevents liability. Compensation for consumed property is not liability but legislated as compensation (jabran). The inviolability remains, and khitab (address) is removed, and a guardian is appointed over him, not he over others.

Key Terms
العهدة (al-uhdah): Liability or obligation that can be either pure (obligatory regardless) or mixed (depends on guardian's consent) for the minor.
الصبا (al-saba): Minority, a state of legal incapacity due to lack of intellect or excuse, but once the minor attains intellect, he has partial capacity for execution.
الحجر (al-hajr): Interdiction; preventing a person from managing his own affairs due to incapacity, but here it is clarified that absence of ruling due to missing pillar is not considered hajr.
أهلية الأداء (ahliyyat al-ada'): Capacity to execute legal acts; the minor with intellect has a degree of it, but it is restricted due to minority.

Key Takeaway
In Hanafi usul, the insane and minor retain capacity for property and torts, but pure liability is never binding on the minor, while mixed liability requires guardian consent, reflecting a nuanced approach to legal capacity.
