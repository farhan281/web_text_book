The Principle of Substitution (Khalaf) in Hanafi Usul
Explains the concept of original and substitute in rights and rulings

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of substitution (khalaf) to cases such as tayammum and expiation, and evaluate the conditions under which a substitute replaces the original.

Core Idea
A substitute (khalaf) takes the place of an original (asl) only when the original is absent but has potential existence.

In this passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul, we delve into the Hanafi concept of substitution (khalaf). This principle governs how legal rulings are transferred from an original to a substitute when the original is unavailable. We will explore its application in faith, purification, and expiation, focusing on the crucial condition that the original must have the possibility of existence for a substitute to be valid. The tayammum dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is will illustrate the practical consequences of different interpretations.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Asl-Khalaf Relationship in Rights
Examine how the passage categorizes rights (Allah's rights vs. servants' rights) and identifies original and substitute in each case, e.g., in faith, tasdiq is the original, iqrar is the substitute in worldly matters.
Notice that substitution appears in multiple domains: ibadat, mu'amalat, and hudud.
2
Analyze Substitution in Tayammum
Focus on the Hanafi view that tayammum is an absolute substitute (khalaf mutlaq) vs. Shafi'i's necessary substitute (khalaf daruri). Understand the textual evidence and reasoning behind each position.
The key difference: Hanafis permit multiple prayers with one tayammum; Shafi'is restrict it to one.
3
Evaluate the Condition of Potential Existence
The passage states substitution requires the original to be absent but with the possibility of existence. Apply this to the example of yamin ghamus (false oath): no expiation because 'birr' (righteousness) cannot be performed for a past lie.
This condition is what distinguishes valid substitutes from invalid ones.
4
Apply the Principle to a New Case
Consider the case of a person who misses a prayer due to sleep. Can qada (make-up) be considered a substitute? Use the condition of potential existence to reason.
Sleep is temporary, so the original (prayer in its time) had potential existence, thus qada is valid.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul expounds the Hanafi principle of original (asl) and substitute (khalaf) across various domains of fiqh. The author first categorizes rights: some are purely for Allah (like hudud), some for humans (like qisas), and some mixed (like hadd al-qadhf). The core concept is that legal rulings often have an original and a substitute. For example, in iman (faith), the original is tasdiq (heartfelt belief) and iqrar (verbal affirmation), but in worldly matters, iqrar becomes the original substitute. Similarly, in obligations like maintenance of a minor, the original is the minor's own payment, but it is substituted by the parent's payment. In the conditions of prayer, taharah with water is the original, and tayammum is the substitute. The Hanafi school holds that tayammum is an absolute substitute (khalaf mutlaq), meaning it can be used for any number of prayers as long as the inability persists. This is contrasted with Imam al-Shafi'i, who views it as a substitute of necessity (khalaf daruri) and thus restricts its use to one obligatory prayer. In the case of two vessels (one pure, one impure) in travel, the Hanafi view is that one does not make ijtihad (tahari) but must use tayammum, because the inability to use water is established by the conflict between the vessels—thus tayammum becomes the absolute substitute. There is a further internal dispute: Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf consider the substitution to be between water and earth directly, while Muhammad and Zufar consider it between wudu (ablution) and tayammum. This difference affects the validity of a mutayammim leading a mutawaddi' in prayer. The passage also discusses a necessary substitute (khalaf daruri): when one fears losing the prayer time despite having water, tayammum becomes permissible. In the case of multiple janazas, if one performs tayammum for the first janaza, according to Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, that tayammum suffices for subsequent janazas as long as inability continues, because the substitute is absolute; Muhammad requires a new tayammum for each janaza, because he sees the substitute as tied to the specific cause (e.g., the prayer time). The general rule is that substitution is only established by a textual proof (nass) or its indication (dalalat al-nass), and its condition is that the original is currently absent but has the potential to exist in the future. If the original has no possibility of existence (like righteousness in a false oath, since one cannot make amends for a lie about a past event), then no substitute (kaffarah) is established. This principle is applied to many cases, such as the one who converts to Islam at the end of the prayer time.

Key Terms
أصل (asl): The original element or primary ruling that has a substitute (khalaf) when absent.
خلف (khalaf): A substitute that takes the place of the original (asl) when the original is absent, provided the original has potential for existence.
خلافة (khilafah): The relationship of substitution between an original and its substitute, established by text or textual indication.

Dispute on Witnesses Returning to the Wali in Case of Revived Victim
Analyzing the return of witnesses on the wali when the allegedly killed person appears alive

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi distinction between tasbib and mubashara to determine the ruling of recourse (ruju') in cases of witness liability, and evaluate the opposing view of the Sahibayn.

Core Idea
When blood is not ownable, its substitute cannot be recovered: the original determines the substitute.

In this passage, we examine a subtle usul dispute about the nature of ownership (milk) and substitution (khalaf). The scenario involves witnesses who paid diya after testifying to a murder, but the alleged victim reappears alive. The core question: can the witnesses recover the paid diya from the guardian? This dispute hinges on whether blood (dam) is legally capable of being owned — a question of the asl (original) and its badal (substitute).

Guided Path
1
Identify the core scenario
State the factual scenario: witnesses testify to murder, the accused is executed, the wali chooses to hold witnesses liable for diya, then the alleged victim appears alive. What is the legal question?
The question is whether witnesses can recover (ruju') from the wali.
2
Extract the position of Abu Yusuf and Muhammad
State their ruling: witnesses do have recourse. Then identify their reasoning: the cause of ownership (sabab al-milk) exists (transgression and guarantee), and blood is potentially ownable in Shari'a (like musk). Therefore the substitute (diya) operates when the original is inaccessible.
Note the analogy to musk: a pure substance that can be owned; its substitute can be claimed.
3
Extract Abu Hanifa's position
State his ruling: witnesses do not have recourse. Then identify his reasoning: both witnesses (by tasbib) and wali (by mubashara) are equally liable for the killing. Since the wali has no one to recover from, the witnesses cannot recover either. Also, blood cannot be owned, so the cause cannot be established, voiding the substitution.
Note the distinction from the case of witnesses of mistake (shuhud al-khata'), where recourse is allowed because the guarantee is for wealth (mal), which is ownable.
4
Analyze the response (al-jawab) to the Sahibayn's argument
The author provides a refutation: the asl (blood) is not legally ownable at all (only qisas is possible). Since the substitute mimics the original, and the original is not guaranteed if owned, the substitute is also not recoverable. Contrast with the mudabbar example where the original slave is guaranteed, so the substitute is too.
This response supports Abu Hanifa's position.

And for this reason Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, may Allah have mercy on them, said: In the case of the one testified to have been killed, if he comes alive, and the one against whom the testimony was given has been killed, and the guardian chooses to hold the witnesses liable: they (the witnesses) have recourse (return) against the guardian because the cause of ownership has been found, which is transgression and guarantee, and the guaranteed thing, which is blood, is potentially open to ownership in the Shari'a, not impossible, like the touch of musk, so the substitute (diyya) operates when the original is inaccessible. As for Abu Hanifa, may Allah have mercy on him, he said: The witnesses are destroyers in legal judgment by way of indirect causation (tasbib), and the guardian is a destroyer in reality by direct perpetration (mubashara), and they are equal in respect of liability for diya, and if the guardian does not have recourse, the witnesses also do not have recourse. Unlike the case of witnesses of mistake (shuhud al-khata'), for if they are held liable and the one testified to have been killed comes alive, they have recourse because they are not liable for destruction but for what they caused the guardian to lose; so when they are held liable, the guardian becomes a destroyer toward them because the guaranteed thing there is wealth, which is open to ownership. And the response to their (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad's) statement: The ownership of the original destroyed thing, namely blood, is not legally recognized at all and cannot be owned, so the cause cannot be established for it, and thus the substitution (khalaf) becomes void; and because the substitute imitates the original, and the original is the destroyed blood, and ownership of blood is retribution (qisas), and the original itself is not guaranteed if it were owned, so too its substitute. And in the case of the mudabbar (slave), the original is guaranteed whenever it is owned necessarily, so too its substitute.

Commentary
This passage presents a legal dispute (ikhtilaf) between Abu Hanifa and the two companions (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) regarding a specific scenario: witnesses testify that someone committed murder, and the guardian (wali of the victim) chooses to hold the witnesses liable for diya (blood money) instead of the alleged perpetrator. Later, the supposed victim appears alive. The question is: can the witnesses recover the diya they paid from the guardian? Abu Yusuf and Muhammad argue that they can recover because a valid cause of ownership (sabab al-milk) exists: the witnesses' transgression in testifying and the guarantee they paid. They claim that blood is potentially subject to ownership in Shari'a (unlike, say, an impossibility), using the analogy of musk (which can be owned, and its substitute operates when the original is not accessible). Abu Hanifa disagrees. He says both the witnesses (by indirect causation, tasbib) and the guardian (by direct act, mubashara) are equally liable for the killing; since the guardian does not have recourse against anyone, the witnesses likewise cannot have recourse against the guardian. He distinguishes the case of witnesses of accidental killing (shuhud al-khata'), where the witnesses do have recourse because they are not liable for destruction but for causing loss of wealth. The final response given in the text refutes the Sahibayn's argument: ownership of blood (the original) is not legislatively possible (only qisas is possible, which is not ownership in the same sense), so the cause (sabab) cannot be established for it, and thus the substitute (diyya) cannot stand in for it. The substitute mimics the original; the original blood is not guaranteed even if it could be owned (qisas is not a monetary guarantee), so diya is also not recoverable. In contrast, in the mudabbar example, the original slave is subject to guarantee, so its substitute is also subject to recourse.

Key Terms
تسبيب (tasbib): Indirect causation, as opposed to direct perpetration (mubashara).
مباشرة (mubashara): Direct perpetration of the act.
بدل (badal): Substitute, in this context diya standing in for blood (the original subject of ownership).

Definition of Illah (Effective Cause) as a Legal Term
Defining illah linguistically and in Shariah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-illah as a legal term, distinguishing its linguistic and Shariah meanings, and explain the Hanafi position that illah is a sign ('alam) set by the Lawgiver, not an inherent cause.

Core Idea
The Hanafi illah is a sign set by the Lawgiver, not a self-causing agent — obligation is from Allah alone.

In this passage from Usul al-Pazdawi, we encounter a foundational definition: al-illah (the effective cause). This concept is central to qiyas, but the Hanafi school insists that the illah does not cause the ruling by itself; it is a marker ('alam) designated by the Lawgiver. Understanding this definition prevents two extreme errors — the Jabariyya denial of any causal link and the Qadariyya attribution of causation to the illah itself.

Guided Path
1
Identify the linguistic meaning of illah
Illah in Arabic means 'that which changes', as illness changes a person's health. This shows that an illah is something that brings about a change in a locus.
This is the root meaning before the Shariah usage.
2
Identify the Shariah meaning
In Shariah, illah is the attribute to which the obligation of a ruling is primarily attributed. Examples: sale for ownership, marriage for permissibility, killing for retaliation.
These are common examples in fiqh.
3
Understand the Hanafi qualification
The Hanafi school emphasizes that illahs are not inherently causative. Allah alone is the true obligator. The illah is a sign ('alam) set by the Lawgiver.
This is the key theological point that distinguishes the Hanafi view from extremes.
4
Contrast with deviant views
The passage rejects two extremes: the Jabariyya who consider illahs futile, and the Qadariyya who attribute inherent causation to them. The Hanafi middle path is the correct one.
These are theological sects, not legal schools.
5
Apply the witness example
If a witness testifies to an illah (e.g., in a transaction) and then retracts, he becomes liable. This shows that in human legal dealings, the illah has causative effect, though ultimately from Allah.
This practical example demonstrates the legal consequence of treating the illah as a cause.

As for al-illah (effective cause): in language it is an expression for that which changes, and from this illness is called 'illah and the sick person 'alil, for every attribute that arises in a locus, such that the locus becomes affected (ma'lul) and its state changes accordingly, is an illah, like the wound for the wounded and the like. And in the Shariah it is an expression for that to which the obligation of a ruling is primarily attributed, such as sale for ownership, marriage for permissibility, killing for retaliation, and the like. However, the illahs of the Shariah are not inherently causative; rather, the obligator of rulings is Allah Most High, but since His imposition is hidden from us, obligation is ascribed to the illahs, so they become causative in the sight of the servants by the Lawgiver's making them so, while in the sight of the Lawgiver they are pure signs. This is like the acts of servants such as obedience: they do not inherently cause reward, but Allah Most High by His grace made them so, so the ascription to them is by His grace. Similarly, punishment is ascribed to disbelief from this perspective. As for considering them futile, as the Jabariyya said, or inherently causative, as the Qadariyya said—no. Such is the case of the illahs. The Four Categories of Legal Cause (Sabab)
Defines and classifies the concept of legal cause

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between the four categories of sabab (legal cause) as defined by Hanafi usul and apply the distinction to determine legal liability in cases of indirect causation.

Core Idea
A sabab is a pathway to a ruling; its legal effect depends on whether an intervening 'illa is attributed to it.

In Hanafi legal theory, the concept of 'legal cause' (sabab) is more nuanced than a simple cause-effect relationship. This passage from Usul al-Bazdawi introduces a critical distinction: sabab is a pathway (tariq) to a ruling, but it does not directly necessitate the ruling like an effective cause ('illa). Instead, it operates through an intermediary. The author classifies causes into four categories based on how the ruling is attributed to them, with the real cause being the focus of detailed analysis.

Guided Path
1

Identify the four divisions of sabab mentioned in the passage: (1) real cause (sabab haqiqi), (2) metaphorical cause, (3) cause resembling 'ilal, (4) cause in the sense of 'illa.
2
Define the real cause
A real cause is a pathway to a ruling without obligation or existence attributed to it; an intervening 'illa exists but is not attributed to the cause. If the 'illa is attributed, it becomes the fourth category.
3
Examine examples of real cause
Driving an animal (leading to damage) and witness testimony (leading to execution) are real causes. They are pathways, not direct acts, so they do not incur the full liability of direct action.
4

Al-Shafi'i argued that witness testimony with full intent is equivalent to direct action. The Hanafi response: the act of testimony is not killing; the killing occurs through intermediaries (judge's ruling, guardian's choice) not in the witness's control.
Hanafi: no qisas or kaffara on the witness.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul defines the legal concept of 'sabab mahd' (pure means) and distinguishes it from 'illah' (cause). The author begins by stating that a means (sabab) lacks the defining characteristic of a cause (haddu al-‘ilal), but is a pure path to it; thus qisas is not imposed on the means, as qisas is the recompense for direct perpetration. He then presents the Shafi'i position: Imam al-Shafi'i conceded this distinction but equated a cause confirmed by complete intentionality (al-sabab al-mu'akkad bil-'amd al-kamil) with direct perpetration. The Hanafi response is that testimony is not an act of killing per se; the killing results from the judge's ruling and the guardian's choice, not directly from the witness. Thus, there is no expiation (kaffarah) on the witness. The passage then explains that this type of means is in the rule of a cause because direct perpetration is attributed to it, but it remains a means for obligating the recompense of direct perpetration. When a cause entirely unattributable to the means arises, it becomes a pure means. Numerous examples follow: (1) pointing someone to steal, rob, or kill — the pointer is not a partner; (2) guiding Muslims in Dar al-Islam to a fortress in Dar al-Harb — the guide is not a partner; (3) telling a man to marry a woman stating she is free, when she is a slave — the informer is not liable for the value of the child if born; but if the informer himself arranged the marriage on that condition, he is liable as a cause. Finally, the passage addresses two exceptions: (a) a pilgrim directing to game becomes liable for the game's value, because his direction directly removes the game's security, though liability only attaches when connected to the killing; (b) a depositary's indication about the deposit makes him directly liable as a breach of trust. This text is a classic exposition of the Hanafi principle that a mere cause does not incur the same liability as a direct act, refining the conditions for legal causation.

مباشرة (mubasharah): Direct perpetration of an act; the person who directly causes the result is liable for its full consequences.

Pure Cause vs. Direct Action: Gift and Sale
A ruling illustration distinguishing pure causation from direct action.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a given act as a pure cause or an effective cause and state the Hanafi rule of liability in transactions.

Core Idea
A pure cause does not incur liability unless direct action connects it to the harm.

This lesson explores the Hanafi distinction between a pure cause (sabab mahd) and direct action (mubashara) through concrete examples from transactions. The text shows that a donor is not liable for offspring produced on a gifted slave, while a seller is liable because he becomes a guarantor. Understanding this distinction is key to mastering causation in Hanafi legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
Determine the action in question: gift, loan, or sale.
2
Classify as pure cause or effective cause
Check if the act is a pure cause (sabab mahd) or if there is a direct connection (illa). A pure cause lacks direct attribution of the harmful outcome to the actor.
3
Apply the liability rule
If pure cause, no liability. If effective cause (or direct action), liability attaches. Note the exception: seller becomes guarantor because he accepted consideration.
Consider the role of stipulation and warranty.

Commentary
This passage is a series of legal rulings illustrating the Hanafi principle that a pure cause (sabab mahd) does not incur liability unless it is connected to direct action (mubashara) or becomes an effective cause (illa). The text compares various scenarios: 

1. Marriage with condition: The husband becomes like a 'possessor of the cause' because he directly stipulated, so liability attaches. 
2. Gift of a slave-girl: If the donee produces offspring (istiwlad) and then the slave is reclaimed on grounds of defective title, the donee does not recover the child's value from the donor because the donor's gift is a pure cause—the act of producing offspring is a separate action not directly triggered by the gift. 
3. Borrower: Similar rule—no liability on the lender for reclamation of borrowed property. 
4. Buyer: Contrary—the seller is like a guarantor because he accepted a price, making him liable for latent defects. 
5. Pilgrim indicating game: The pilgrim's indication is a direct removal of safety, but liability does not attach until the killing is connected; it is like a wound whose outcome is awaited. Indication regarding property is different because property is protected by sanctity, not by being remote. 
6. Depositary pointing to deposit: This is direct betrayal, so he is liable. 
7. Hitting someone: Already covered. 
8. Giving a child a knife: If the child stabs himself, the giver is not liable because the child's action is an intervening cause. But if the knife falls from the child's hand and wounds him, the giver is liable because the fall is attributed to the act of holding. 
9. Carrying a child to danger: The carrier's clan is liable for resulting harm. 
10. Child killing or dying naturally: No liability on the usurper's clan. 
11. Carrying child on animal: If the child falls while the animal stands or walks, the carrier is liable. But if the child drives the animal, causation is cut off by the child's direct action. 
12. Telling child to climb and shake fruit: No liability because the child is the direct agent (possessor of the cause). 

Throughout, the key is whether the original act is a pure cause (sabab mahd) or becomes an effective cause (illa) through direct connection or lack of intervening independent action.

Key Terms
سبب محض (sabab mahd): A pure cause that does not of itself create liability because the harmful act is attributed to another's direct action.
مباشرة (mubashara): Direct action by a person that immediately causes the result, incurring liability.
علة (illa): An effective cause that is legally considered as directly producing the effect, like being the 'possessor of the cause' (sahib al-illa).

Pure Cause and Direct Action: Pilgrim Indication and Deposit
A ruling comparison between indication of game and property, and depositary's betrayal.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the distinction between pure cause and direct action to scenarios involving indication of game and deposit betrayal.

Core Idea
Indication that directly removes safety is a direct action, but liability awaits the killing; property indication is not direct aggression.

This lesson continues applying the pure cause vs. direct action framework to new contexts. The text contrasts a pilgrim's indication of game (which directly removes safety but defers liability) with indication of property (which is not direct aggression). It also examines a depositary's betrayal, which is direct action. These examples deepen your understanding of how causation is attributed in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the act
Identify the act in each scenario: pilgrim indicating game, depositary indicating deposit, depositary's betrayal.
2
Classify the connection
Determine if the act is a direct removal of safety (like pilgrim's indication) or not (like property indication).
Consider the nature of the object: game is protected by distance, property by sanctity.
3
Apply temporal element
For pilgrim's indication, liability is deferred until the killing occurs; it is like a wound whose outcome is awaited. For depositary, betrayal is immediate direct action.

Pure Cause and Direct Action: Harm to Children and Third Parties
A series of rulings on causation in child safety scenarios.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the distinction between pure cause and direct action to scenarios involving children, animals, and third party harm.

Core Idea
Intervening direct action by a competent person cuts off causation from a pure cause.

This lesson applies the pure cause vs. direct action principle to cases involving children and third parties. The text examines when a person is liable for harm that befalls a child after giving a knife, carrying to danger, or instructing climbing. The key factor is whether the child's own action or other intervening events break the chain of causation.

Guided Path
1
Identify the initial act
What did the person do? Give knife, carry child, instruct climbing etc.
2
Identify the intervening act or event
Is there a direct action by the child or another that intervenes? If the child acts independently (stabs himself, drives the animal), that cuts off causation.
Consider capacity: is the child capable of direct action?
3
Determine if initial act becomes effective cause
If the harm follows directly without independent intervention (e.g., child falls from hand due to holding), the initial actor is liable.
The initial actor becomes 'sahib al-illa' (possessor of the cause).

Commentary
This passage is a series of legal rulings illustrating the Hanafi principle that a pure cause (sabab mahd) does not incur liability unless it is connected to direct action (mubashara) or becomes an effective cause (illa). The text compares various scenarios: 

1. Marriage with condition: The husband becomes like a 'possessor of the cause' because he directly stipulated, so liability attaches. 
2. 3. Borrower: Similar rule—no liability on the lender for reclamation of borrowed property. 
4. Buyer: Contrary—the seller is like a guarantor because he accepted a price, making him liable for latent defects. 
5. Pilgrim indicating game: The pilgrim's indication is a direct removal of safety, but liability does not attach until the killing is connected; it is like a wound whose outcome is awaited. Indication regarding property is different because property is protected by sanctity, not by being remote. 
6. Depositary pointing to deposit: This is direct betrayal, so he is liable. 
7. Hitting someone: Already covered. 
8. Giving a child a knife: If the child stabs himself, the giver is not liable because the child's action is an intervening cause. But if the knife falls from the child's hand and wounds him, the giver is liable because the fall is attributed to the act of holding.

10. Child killing or dying naturally: No liability on the usurper's clan.

12. Telling child to climb and shake fruit: No liability because the child is the direct agent (possessor of the cause).

Real Cause (Sabab Haqiqi) and Liability in Hanafi Usul
Illustrating real cause (sabab haqiqi) through liability examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a pure cause and an intercepting cause in Hanafi liability law, and apply the rule to determine liability.

Core Idea
A cause intercepted by a direct action severs liability; otherwise the cause is treated like an effective cause.

In Hanafi usul, the concept of sabab (cause) is central to attributing liability. This lesson explores real cause (sabab haqiqi) through practical examples like giving a weapon to a child. The key principle is whether an independent direct action intercepts the causal chain, absolving the initial causer.

Guided Path
1
Identify the cause and the potential effect
Find the action or means that sets the chain in motion (e.g., giving a knife to a child). The effect is the harm that occurs.
2
Check for an intercepting direct action
Determine if the immediate agent (e.g., the child) performs an independent volitional act that directly causes the harm. If yes, that act is an intercepting cause (illa) not attributable to the original causer.
Example: child stabs himself vs. child falls and the knife cuts him.
3
Apply the liability ruling
If the intercepting action is present, the original causer is not liable. If no intercepting action, the original cause is treated as if it were the direct cause (like illa) and liability attaches.
The text gives multiple scenarios: carrying a child on an animal vs. child driving the animal.

Metaphorical Cause (Sabab Majazi) and the Dispute on Its Legal Effect
Defining metaphorical cause and analyzing the dispute over its legal effect.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi concept of metaphorical cause (sabab majazi) and its semblance (shubhah) of a real cause, and compare it with the views of Zufar and Al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
A metaphorical cause has a semblance of reality that can produce legal effects, unlike a pure metaphorical link.

Beyond direct causation, Hanafi usul recognizes a metaphorical cause in conditional divorces, oaths, and vows. This cause is not a real path to the result but has a potential that gives it a semblance (shubhah) of reality. The debate with Zufar and Al-Shafi'i highlights the practical implications, such as whether an immediate pronouncement nullifies a suspended condition.

Guided Path
1
Identify a metaphorical cause
Recognize statements like 'If you enter the house, you are divorced' or oaths by Allah. These are causes metaphorically because they are not direct means to the legal effect (divorce, expiation) but potentially lead to it.
2
Understand the Hanafi position: shubhah
Hanafis hold that such causes have a shubhah (semblance) of a real cause in legal effect. For example, an oath is prescribed for righteousness, so the penalty guarantees the act; thus the immediate pronouncement (if any) has a semblance of obligation, similar to a guarantee on usurped property.
This shubhah is enough to produce legal effects, like giving the immediate pronouncement power to nullify a suspended condition.
3
Contrast with Zufar and Al-Shafi'i
Zufar denies any shubhah; the metaphorical cause has no legal effect in itself. Al-Shafi'i treats it as a real effective cause (illa), not just a semblance.
The practical difference appears in the issue of whether an immediate divorce (tanjiz) nullifies a previously suspended divorce.
4
Apply to the tanjiz issue
According to Hanafis, if a husband says 'You are divorced' immediately, it nullifies a prior suspended divorce because the immediate pronouncement has a shubhah of obligation like a guarantee. Zufar says no nullification because no shubhah exists. Shafi'i would treat it as a real cause, leading to different rules.

Commentary
This passage is a detailed Hanafi usul al-fiqh discussion on the concept of 'sabab' (cause/means) in legal causation. It distinguishes between two types: the real cause (sabab haqiqi) and the metaphorical cause (sabab majazi). The author first illustrates real causes through examples of liability when harm is caused indirectly through a child or an animal. If the direct action of the child intervenes (e.g., the child drives the animal), the causal link is severed. This is based on the principle that a pure cause (sabab mahd) that is intercepted by an independent effective cause (illa) does not carry liability. However, if the harm is directly linked to the cause (like the child falling from the animal while being carried), the carrier's 'Aqila (tribal liability group) is liable, as the cause is considered akin to an effective cause (illa). The author then transitions to metaphorical causes, such as conditional divorces, manumissions, vows, and oaths. These are called causes metaphorically because they are not direct paths to the legal result (like divorce or expiation) but potentially lead to it. The Hanafi position is that this metaphorical cause has a semblance (shubhah) of a real cause in legal effect, meaning that it can produce legal consequences as if it were real. This is disputed by Imam Zufar, who denies any semblance. The debate is exemplified in the issue of whether an immediate pronouncement (tanjiz) nullifies a suspended (mu'allaq) statement. According to the Hanafis, the immediate pronouncement has a semblance of obligation similar to a guarantee on a usurped property (maghsoub), and if the condition (place) is lost, the semblance becomes void. Thus, the suspended effect is nullified. Al-Shafi'i, on the other hand, treats the metaphorical cause as a real effective cause (illa), leading to different rulings. The passage ends by contrasting the Hanafi view with that of Zufar.

The Description with a Semblance of Being a Cause (Shubhat al-‘Ilal)
Defining shubhat al-‘ilal and illustrating with riba al-nasi'ah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define 'wasf lahu shubhat al-‘ilal' and explain its application to riba al-nasi'ah using the Hanafi usul framework.

Core Idea
A description that alone has a semblance of a cause suffices to establish a ruling, even without completing the full ‘illah.

In Hanafi usul, a legal cause (‘illah) may be composed of multiple descriptions. Sometimes each description alone carries a semblance (shubhah) of causation, such that the ruling can be established even when only one description is present. This lesson explores that fifth category, known as 'al-wasf alladhi lahu shubhat al-‘ilal', using the example of riba al-nasi'ah (credit-based usury) where both genus and measure are effective descriptions.

Guided Path
1
Identify the compound condition
Recognize that the ruling in question depends on two effective descriptions (wusuf mughaithara). In the example, the prohibition of riba al-nasi'ah rests on both genus (jins) and measure (qadr).
2
Understand the semblance (shubhah)
Each of these descriptions, if considered alone, has a semblance of being a cause. That means it looks like a cause and can produce a legal effect, even though the full ‘illah requires both.
3
Analyze the consequence of isolation
If only one description is present (e.g., genus alone), it is not a complete sabab or ‘illah, but it still has enough similarity to a cause to establish the ruling via that semblance. This is the key feature of this category.
4
Apply to the riba example
In riba al-nasi'ah, the genus alone has a semblance of excess (shubhat al-fadl), and therefore it alone can prohibit the credit transaction. Similarly, the measure alone can do so. The ruling is established by the semblance of the cause, even though the full ‘illah is not present.
This differs from standard qiyas where the full ‘illah must exist.

Cause of the Cause ('Illat al-‘Illah) in Hanafi Usul
Definition of 'illat al-‘illah with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the concept of 'illat al-‘illah, distinguish it from a direct 'illah and a sabab, and apply it to examples in Hanafi usul.

Core Idea
A cause of a cause functions as a cause legally, but resembles a sabab when delayed.

In Hanafi legal theory (usul al-fiqh), causes are not always direct. Sometimes a cause leads to another cause, which then brings the ruling. This intermediate cause is called 'illat al-‘illah (cause of the cause). The source passage from al-Bazdawi defines it and gives examples from purchase of a relative, shooting, and tazkiyah (recommendation). This lesson will break down the concept and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Locate the opening statement: 'that which is a cause of the cause, it is a cause that resembles the reasons (asbab)'. This is the core definition.
The passage explicitly contrasts 'illat al-‘illah with both a direct cause and a sabab.
2
Analyze the first example: purchase of a relative
The purchase of a relative (who is a slave) is a cause for ownership (the relative becomes owned), and ownership in turn causes manumission (if the owner is the relative's master). Thus, purchase is an 'illat al-‘illah for manumission.
Here the ruling (manumission) is immediate upon ownership, so it is a full cause, not delayed.
3
Analyze the second example: shooting
Shooting an arrow at game is a cause for ownership, but the ruling of ownership is delayed until the game is retrieved or confirmed. Because of the delay, it resembles a sabab (occasion) more than a direct cause.
Delay is the key factor that makes it resemble a sabab.
4
Analyze the third example: tazkiyah (recommendation)
According to Abu Hanifa, recommending a seller to a buyer (tazkiyah) is like a cause of the cause. If the recommender later retracts, he becomes liable for any loss, showing that the recommendation has legal effect as an intermediate cause.
This demonstrates that an 'illat al-‘illah can carry liability even if it is not the direct cause of the contract.
5
Synthesize the principle
Summarize: An 'illat al-‘illah is a cause that produces another cause, which then produces the ruling. It is treated as a cause (with legal consequences) but when the ruling is delayed, it shares characteristics with a sabab.
This concept is crucial for proper application of qiyas when causal chains are involved.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The later of two effective qualities gets the legal attribution, even though both are necessary.

In Hanafi usul, a 'cause' (illa) can be effective in reality and law without being called a cause by name. This occurs when a ruling depends on two distinct qualities that each could bring about the effect. The later one to occur is deemed the cause for attribution, even though both are necessary. This principle helps determine legal responsibility, such as who is the manumitter of a slave.

Recognize that the ruling depends on two descriptions, each independently capable of causing the effect, e.g., ownership and kinship for manumission.

2

Establish the temporal order: the later quality is the one that happens after the other in existence.
Temporal priority is key; simultaneous occurrence may require a different rule.
3
Apply attribution to the later quality
The ruling is attributed to the later quality, making it the 'cause in ruling and meaning', even though it shares the causative role with the earlier one.
The name 'illa' is not given to either; the cause is the combination.

Learning Objective

Core Idea
A ruling's apparent cause may not be its real cause—Shari'ah often substitutes for ease.

Substitution of Cause for Effect (Iqamat al-Sabab Maqam al-Mad'uw)
Explains the principle of substituting cause for effect with two ways and three justifications

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the two ways of substituting cause for effect and identify the three justifications (daf' al-darura, ihtiyat, daf' al-haraj) in new cases.

Core Idea
The Lawgiver substitutes a visible cause for an inner meaning to ease application, via two methods and for three justifications.

After explaining the types of 'illa, Imam al-Bazdawi now discusses a key Hanafi principle: substituting an apparent cause (sabab) for the inner meaning (mad'uw) to facilitate legal rulings. He presents two ways: making the causative cause stand for the caused, and making evidence stand for the proven. This is done for reasons of necessity, precaution, or hardship.

Examine the three justifications
Justification 1: Repelling necessity/incapacity (daf' al-darura) – e.g., conditional divorce when one cannot know the inner state. Justification 2: Precaution (ihtiyat) – e.g., prohibiting incitements to unlawful acts. Travel, illness, sleep, touching, marriage are causes that are substituted for the actual effects (hardship, impurity, intercourse) for ease.
4

A report about love stands in place of love itself; the state of purity (tuhr) stands for the need (haja) in permitting divorce.
5
Recognize the unifying principle
All these substitutions aim to operationalize legal rulings using observable indicators, avoiding dependence on hidden inner states.

The Midwife's Testimony: Conditions, Consequences, and Strict Analogy
Ruling on whether a woman's testimony establishes birth for attached consequences

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that a condition (shart) requires complete proof and distinguish when subsidiary effects follow only if the condition is fully established.

Core Idea
A condition requires full proof; subsidiaries follow only when the condition is firmly established.

The testimony of a single woman (midwife) about a birth raises a classical usul question: does it establish the birth as a condition for attached legal consequences like divorce or manumission? The Sahibayn say yes when additional evidence (marital bed or pregnancy) exists, but Abu Hanifa insists on strict analogy: a condition needs complete proof, and one woman's testimony is insufficient. This lesson unpacks the reasoning and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the core issue
Determine whether the testimony of a single midwife establishes the fact of birth for the purpose of subsidiary rulings (divorce, manumission, etc.). The key is whether birth is a condition (shart) that requires complete proof.
The condition is the birth; the subsidiary effects are the suspended divorce or manumission.
2
State the Sahibayn's position and their reasoning
According to Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, when the marital bed (firash) is present or pregnancy is apparent, the birth is established by external evidence. The midwife's testimony then suffices for both birth and its subsidiaries because the consequences are subsidiary (tabi) to the birth.
They analogize to the cry of a newborn being subsidiary to birth.
3
State Abu Hanifa's position and his strict qiyas
Abu Hanifa holds that a condition (shart) like birth requires complete proof (kamil al-hujja) to be established; the midwife's testimony alone does not meet that standard. Therefore, the condition is not proven, so the subsidiaries do not follow.
He adheres to 'haqiqat al-qiyas' – strict analogy.
4
Analyze Abu Hanifa's analogy to the slave woman case
Consider a man buying a slave woman sold as a virgin. If a woman testifies she is deflowered, the buyer does not automatically return her; he only demands an oath. Similarly, the midwife's testimony does not prove birth for the purpose of rescission or other subsidiaries.
The analogy shows that a single woman's testimony is insufficient to establish a defect/condition for legal effect.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
If a husband suspends divorce on birth and only a midwife testifies, the divorce does not occur according to Abu Hanifa, but it does occur according to the Sahibayn (provided firash or pregnancy exists). This affects actual fatwa.
Later Hanafi practice often follows the Sahibayn for ease, but Abu Hanifa's position remains the strict qiyas.

Washing the Deceased Husband: Persisting Marital Ownership
Explaining the ruling on wife washing her deceased husband.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi ruling that a wife may wash her deceased husband during her 'idda and explain the rationale based on persisting ownership.

Core Idea
The husband's ownership over his wife persists after death for certain needs, allowing her to wash him.

This lesson examines a unique Hanafi ruling: a woman may wash her deceased husband during her waiting period ('idda). The passage explains this by invoking the principle of persisting ownership: the husband was the owner (malik) in the marriage, and some rights of ownership continue after death for the husband's needs. The converse—a husband washing his deceased wife—does not hold because the wife is owned, and her capacity to be owned ends at death.

Guided Path
1
State the ruling
What is the ruling regarding a wife washing her deceased husband? She may do so during her 'idda.
2
Identify the rationale
The husband is an owner (malik). His ownership remains for his needs even after death, specifically until the end of the wife's 'idda.
3
Contrast with wife's death
If the wife dies first, the husband does not wash her because she is owned (mamluka) and her capacity to be owned ends.
4
Supporting evidence
The passage notes that if any ownership remained for the wife, it would require 'idda upon him, but there is no 'idda. Also, marriage ownership is confirmed (mu'akkad) by dower, maintenance, and mahramiyya.
5
Extract the principle
Ownership can persist beyond death for the benefit of the deceased's needs.

Qisas and Inheritance: The Hanafi Position
Explaining why qisas is not inherited and when it becomes inheritable.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi position that qisas (retaliation) is not inherited, explain the rationale based on its purpose of blood vengeance, and identify when it becomes inheritable (conversion to diyah).

Core Idea
Qisas is for quenching blood vengeance of the living heirs, not for the deceased's need, so it is not inherited; only diyah is inheritable.

The final part of the passage tackles a major usul question: Is the right to qisas (retaliation for murder) inherited? The Hanafi school answers no, based on the purpose of qisas. It is not a property right but a right of vengeance belonging to the living heirs individually. This lesson examines that reasoning and the important exception: when qisas is converted to diyah (blood money), it becomes inheritable because it then serves the deceased's needs.

Guided Path
1
Identify the category
The passage classifies qisas as something 'not suitable for the deceased's need' (la yasluh li hajatihi).
2
Understand the purpose
Qisas is legislated as a punishment to quench blood vengeance (darak al-tha'r) and to secure the lives of the relatives.
3
Determine the effect

Dayanah is Preventive, Not Obligatory: The Core Principle
Usul rule defining dayanah as preventive

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi principle that dayanah is dafi'ah (preventive) not mujibah (obligatory) and apply it to cases like nafaqah and slander.

Core Idea
Dayanah repels claims but does not create new obligations — it is defensive, not transitive.

In Hanafi usul, the concept of dayanah (religious/conscientious obligation) plays a crucial role in distinguishing between what is enforced by a judge (qada) and what is left to one's conscience. This passage establishes that dayanah does not change the legal status of things; it only prevents a claim from being enforced against a person. Understanding this distinction is essential for grasping how Hanafi jurists derive rulings in matters of liability, maintenance, and punishment.

Guided Path
1
Identify the nature of dayanah
Read the first line: 'We do not make dayanah transitive.' Dayanah is not a legal cause that creates new obligations; it only deflects claims. This is the foundational claim.
The term 'muta'diyah' (transitive) indicates that dayanah does not carry over to create new legal effects like liability.
2
Analyze the wine example
The author asks: if wine were valuable, would dayanah make it valuable for liability? The answer is no: dayanah only repels the claim (daf' al-ilzam), but the value itself remains as it was originally (or not). Liability arises from the act of destruction, not from the value being established by dayanah.
This shows that dayanah does not affect the objective legal status of forbidden items.
3
Examine the nafaqah example
Maintenance (nafaqah) is legislated as a defensive measure (daf'an). The father is imprisoned for failing to provide maintenance, but not for debt. This distinction shows that dayanah is preventive, not punitive. The same action (imprisonment) is allowed for one purpose but not the other.
The qiyas with the son's maintenance versus debt illustrates that dayanah only repels a claim to force payment, but does not make the maintenance itself a primary obligation that creates liability for past non-payment.
4
Contrast with inheritance
Inheritance (mirath) is called an 'original link' (silah mubtada'ah). If dayanah were to obligate inheritance, it would become obligating (mujibah), not preventive. Therefore, inheritance is not based on dayanah; it is a separate category.
This contrast helps refine the definition: dayanah applies to obligations that arise from a defensive need, not from a primary origination.

Intra-Madhhab Dispute: Can Dayanah Preserve Original Rulings?
Intra-madhhab dispute on the scope of dayanah

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to compare the views of Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on whether dayanah can preserve the original value of wine and pig, and evaluate the argument using marriage with mahrams as a counterexample.

Core Idea
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that dayanah can preserve original rulings; Abu Hanifa disagrees, as seen with mahram marriages.

The passage introduces a dispute between Abu Hanifa (represented in the text) and his two leading students, Abu Yusuf and Muhammad al-Shaybani, regarding the scope of dayanah. While they agree on the general principle that dayanah is preventive, they differ on whether it can preserve an original ruling of permissibility or value when the evidence is weak. This lesson unpacks that disagreement and its practical implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
The dispute is whether dayanah can preserve the original ruling that wine and pig are valuable (mutaqawwim) and permissible to drink/eat (based on earlier dispensations), when the proof for that ruling becomes weak (qasur al-dalil) and only dayanah evidence remains.
The original rulings referred to are from previous sharias (or general permissibility before Islam) according to some interpretations.
2
State the Sahibayn's position
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad say: The value of wine, the permissibility of drinking it, and the value of pig were established original rulings. When the proof weakens (through dayanah), the original ruling remains. They use the principle: 'When proof falls short, the original state remains.'
They analogize to other cases where a previous ruling is presumed to continue unless changed.
3
State Abu Hanifa's implied position
The text implies Abu Hanifa (through the author's own view) disagrees: dayanah does not preserve these original rulings because wine and pig were never truly valuable in Islamic law. The author uses the example of marriage with mahrams, which was never permissible from the time of Adam. Since there was never an original permissibility, dayanah cannot preserve it.
The key difference: the Sahibayn see wine/pig as having an original permissibility; Abu Hanifa sees them as inherently prohibited.
4
Analyze the mahram counterexample
The author states that marriage with mahrams (like one's sister) was never permissible, even in Adam's time. The footnote explains that Adam married his sons to each other's twins, but not to their own sisters. This shows that original prohibitions cannot be overridden by dayanah.
This example is used to rebut the Sahibayn's argument: if their logic were correct, then mahram marriages could be preserved by dayanah, which is impossible.
5
Determine the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical difference is whether wine and pig are considered valuable (mutaqawwim) for purposes of liability (damān). If someone destroys wine or pig that was in the possession of a non-Muslim (who considers it valuable) under dayanah, is the destroyer liable? According to the Sahibayn, liability exists because the original value remains; according to Abu Hanifa, there is no liability because they are not valuable in Islamic law.
This affects cases of accidental destruction, commercial transactions, and insurance claims.

Hazl in Iqrar: Jest Invalidates Confession Absolutely
Stating that jest invalidates confession entirely

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the ruling that jest invalidates confession (iqrar) and apply the qiyas analogy from duress to jest to derive the consequence of non-ratifiability.

Core Idea
Jest destroys the truth-basis of confession, making it void and unratifiable.

In this brief but foundational passage, the author classifies legal acts and focuses on confession (iqrar). Unlike transactions that may be rescinded, confession is built on truthfulness. Jest, by indicating insincerity, destroys that foundation. We will unpack the qiyas (analogical reasoning) that links jest to duress, demonstrating why the invalidation is absolute and cannot be cured by later ratification.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Act and Its Ruling
Confession (iqrar) is the act under discussion. The ruling: jest invalidates it entirely, whether the confessed matter admits rescission or not.
Note the absolute nature: no exception for 'irrescindable' confessions like divorce.
2
Locate the Dalil (Qiyas)
The author does not cite a direct text but uses qiyas jaliy (explicit analogy). The asl (original case) is confession under duress, whose ruling (invalid, not ratifiable) is established by texts like the hadith 'رفع عن أمتي الخطأ والنسيان وما استكرهوا عليه'.
The asl ruling is taken from established usul.
3
Analyse the Illa (Effective Cause)
The illa is the lack of genuine belief or free consent. In duress, the confessor does not truly believe the content; in jest, the persona does not intend the confession to be taken seriously.
Both share the 'defect in veracity' – the confession does not reflect the confessor's true state.
4
Derive the Ruling for the Far' (New Case)
Apply the illa to jest: since jest indicates the non-existence of the reported matter, the confession is invalid (batil). Moreover, like duress, this invalidation cannot be ratified later (ijaza).
Ratification would be meaningless because the defect is in the moment of utterance.
5
Note the Thamarah (Practical Consequence)
The practical difference: a jest confession can never be enforced or validated ex post facto. A court or mufti must treat it as legally null.
This contrasts with some transactions where jest may lead to a valid act if later ratified.
