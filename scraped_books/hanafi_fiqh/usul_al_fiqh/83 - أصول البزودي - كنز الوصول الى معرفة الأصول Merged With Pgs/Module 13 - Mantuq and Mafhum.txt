Sarih and Kinayah: Explicit and Implicit Expression
Defining sarih and kinayah with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish sarih from kinayah based on the Hanafi definition and apply this distinction to legal utterances such as divorce and contract.

Core Idea
Sarih words effect legal consequences without intention; kinayah requires intention.

This passage from al-Bazdawi’s Kanz al-Wusul establishes a foundational linguistic classification in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: the distinction between explicit (sarih) and implicit (kinayah) expressions. Understanding this distinction is critical because the legal effect of an utterance often depends on whether it is sarih or kinayah. For example, in divorce, saying 'you are divorced' is sarih and effects talaq even without the husband's intention, while a kinayah phrase like 'go to your family' requires intention.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term and its lexical origin
Note that sarih literally means 'clear' or 'pure', derived from the word for palace (qasr) due to its elevation. Kinayah comes from 'kanaytu' meaning to allude or conceal.
The etymology helps remember the nature of each term.
2
Memorize the definition of sarih
Sarih is what clearly shows its intended meaning without need for context. The passage gives examples like 'you are free', 'you are divorced', 'I sold', and 'I married'.
These are all performative utterances in fiqh.
3
Memorize the definition of kinayah
Kinayah is the opposite: its intended meaning is hidden and requires context. The passage gives pronouns like 'huwa' (the pronoun of absence) as examples.
In legal context, kinayah needs intention (niyyah) to take effect.
4
Apply the distinction to a legal scenario
Take a divorce utterance: 'anti taliq' (you are divorced) is sarih, so divorce occurs even without intention. 'alhaqi bi-ahlik' (go to your family) is kinayah, so divorce only occurs if intention is proven.
This is the practical difference taught in Hanafi fiqh.

Dalalat al-Nass: Definition and Example
Defining dalalat al-nass and its example.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define dalalat al-nass and identify its key features using the Quranic example.

Core Idea
Dalalat al-nass derives rulings from linguistic meaning, not juristic reasoning.

In Hanafi usul, not every ruling comes from the exact wording of a text. Some rulings are derived from the linguistic meaning implied by the words. This lesson introduces dalalat al-nass, a key concept that distinguishes direct linguistic inference from independent reasoning (ijtihad) and analogy (qiyas). We will use the famous verse on honoring parents to illustrate how a prohibition on 'uff' extends to all forms of harm.

Guided Path
1
Locate the definition
Identify the opening statement that defines dalalat al-nass: 'what is established by the meaning of the text linguistically, not by ijtihad or istinbat'.
2
Analyze the example
Examine Quran 17:23: 'Do not say to them 'uff''. Note that 'uff' linguistically implies any form of harm or annoyance.
This is understood by any Arabic speaker, not just jurists.
3
Understand the extension
Recognize that the ruling (prohibition) extends from 'saying uff' to hitting, shouting, or any harmful act—based solely on linguistic meaning.
This extension is not qiyas; it is inherent in the language.
4
Differentiate from nass and qiyas
Contrast dalalat al-nass with nass (explicit wording) and qiyas (analogical deduction). Dalala is linguistic, not deduced by ijtihad.
The passage says: it is not called nass because it is a meaning, not an expression.
5
Confirm authority
Note that the ruling from dalala operates like nass in binding authority.
This is a key Hanafi principle: linguistic implication carries the same weight as explicit text.

Core Idea
Words with clear legal meaning (haqq, yaqin, sidq) establish acknowledgment; ambiguous words (birr, salah) do not.

This lesson examines a concrete example from Imam Muhammad's al-Jami' that illustrates how the Hanafi school classifies words based on textual clarity. In a claim of debt, not every response constitutes a valid acknowledgment. Only words that are zahir (apparent) in meaning for a financial obligation are accepted. Literal Meaning Takes Precedence Over Metaphorical Meaning
States the rule that haqiqah is preferred over majaz with three illustrative examples.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi rule that haqiqah is preferred over majaz and apply it to the examples of 'aqd, nikah, and aqra'.

Core Idea
When the literal meaning is possible, the metaphorical meaning must be discarded — the borrowed yields to the original.

In Hanafi usul, the default is to take words in their literal sense unless a clear impediment prevents it. This rule is crucial for interpreting legal texts consistently. The passage presents the principle and grounds it in linguistic reasoning, then illustrates it with classic jurisprudential terms whose meanings have been contested between madhahib.

Guided Path
1
Locate the rule statement
Find the sentence that declares the core principle: 'acting according to the literal meaning whenever possible renders the metaphorical void.'
This is the operational rule for all subsequent interpretation.
2
Grasp the rationale
The author adds a proof: 'because the borrowed does not compete with the original.' Understand that majaz is a borrowed usage, so it cannot override the original haqiqah.
This is a linguistic–usul argument, not a textual proof from Quran/Sunnah.
3
Work through the 'aqra' example
The word 'qur'' (pl. aqra') is claimed to be literal for menstruation. Trace the linguistic derivation: the root means 'gathering' (of blood). The metaphorical meaning (purity) is derived from adjacency, not from a primary sense. Transition (intiqal) also supports menstruation.
Note the inter-madhhab dispute: Shafi'is consider 'qur'' literal for purity, leading to different 'iddah rulings.
4
Analyze the remaining examples
For 'aqd, the literal is the actual contract, not mere intention. For nikah, the literal is the contract of marriage (gathering), not sexual intercourse. In each case, the haqiqah can be applied, so majaz is invalid.
These examples reinforce the rule by showing practical consequences.
5
Summarise the principle's function
The rule prevents arbitrary shifts to metaphorical readings when the literal meaning yields a valid legal result. It safeguards consistency in Hanafi ta'wil.
This is a default rule; it can be overridden by evidence that the literal meaning is impossible or leads to absurdity.

Commentary
This passage states a fundamental rule in Hanafi usul al-fiqh: when literal meaning (haqiqah) is possible, the metaphorical meaning (majaz) is invalid and cannot be applied. The reason given is that the borrowed (musta'ar, i.e., the metaphorical usage) does not compete with the original (asl, i.e., the literal meaning). The author then provides examples from Fiqh terminology to illustrate the rule. The first example is the word al-qur' (أقراء, plural of qur'). Indication from Context of Speech (دلالة سياق النظم)
Examples illustrating how context of speech alters fiqh rulings.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle of دلالة سياق النظم to distinguish between literal meaning and contextual meaning in legal speech, and identify whether a statement results in a valid legal effect (aman, wakala, iqrar) or not.

Core Idea
Context can override literal meaning: the sequential structure of speech determines legal effect, not isolated words.

This lesson explores the usul principle of 'indication from the context of speech' (دلالة سياق النظم), a key tool in Hanafi legal interpretation. The text presents several examples where the apparent meaning of words is transformed by their contextual sequence. By analyzing these cases, you will learn to identify when context overrides the literal import and how this affects fiqh rulings. This principle is critical for understanding how Hanafi jurists derive rulings from ambiguous statements.

Guided Path
1
Identify the literal meaning
Read each statement without considering context. What would each word normally mean? For example, 'You are safe' literally grants safety.
2
Examine the context (siyāq al-naẓm)
Look at the full phrase or sentence. What is the sequential structure? The second clause 'you will know what you encounter' changes the tone to a threat. Similarly, conditions like 'if you are a man' indicate doubt.
3
Determine the legal effect
Does the statement create a valid aman, wakala, or iqrar? For 'You are safe, you will know...' it does not create aman because the context negates it. For 'Descend' alone, it does. For conditional statements, they fail.
The key is whether the context is consistent with the literal meaning or contradicts it.
4
Generalize the principle
From these examples, deduce: when the context of speech (siyāq al-naẓm) clearly indicates a non-literal intent (rebuke, condition, threat), the legal effect is determined by that context, not the isolated words.
5
Apply to a new scenario
Create or analyze a new statement where context overrides literal meaning. For instance, 'I give you my car if you can fly' – the context (impossibility) shows no gift occurs.
Practice using the same logic: literal vs. contextual meaning.

The Waw Joining Sentences: Complete vs. Incomplete Clauses
Explaining when the waw joining sentences requires sharing the predicate

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a complete and incomplete sentence joined by waw and determine whether sharing of the predicate is obligatory.

Core Idea
The waw forces predicate sharing only when the second clause is incomplete.

In Arabic legal texts, the conjunction waw (and) can connect two clauses. But does the second clause always share the predicate of the first? This lesson clarifies that the key factor is whether the second clause is grammatically complete. If it is incomplete, it must share; if complete, it stands alone. This distinction has practical implications in legal formulae like divorce and debt acknowledgments.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two clauses joined by waw
Look for the waw (و) connecting two sentences. Determine the boundaries of each clause.
The waw is the conjunction 'and'.
2
Classify the second clause as complete or incomplete
A complete sentence (جملة تامة) has its own predicate and can stand alone. An incomplete sentence (جملة ناقصة) lacks a predicate or other essential element.
For example, 'and this one is divorced' (وهذه طالق) is incomplete if it doesn't specify the number of divorces.
3
Apply the sharing rule
If the second clause is incomplete, it must share the predicate of the first clause. If it is complete, it does not share; each clause has its own predicate.
Sharing means the second clause uses the same predicate (e.g., 'three times' or 'a thousand').
4

Work through the divorce example (incomplete -> shares the 'three times') and the debt example (incomplete -> shares the 'a thousand').
Also consider the conditional example where sharing is impossible due to logical necessity.
5
Handle cases where sharing is impossible
Even if the second clause is incomplete, sharing may be impossible if the predicate cannot apply to multiple subjects (e.g., 'Zaid came and Amr' – two persons cannot share one coming). In such cases, each clause has its own predicate.
This exception is due to the impossibility of sharing (استحالة الاشتراك).

The Legal Inference of the Preposition باء: Partiality vs. Completeness
Analysis of the preposition 'باء' for isti'aab and exception

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle that the preposition 'باء' indicates attachment (ilṣāq) to derive the ruling on wiping in wudhu and tayammum, and to analyze conditional exception clauses.

Core Idea
The Arabic 'باء' indicates attachment, not coverage, so it implies partiality unless overridden by a proof.

In this passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul, we dissect the preposition 'باء' as used in legal texts. The Hanafi school holds that 'باء' signifies attachment (ilṣāq) rather than comprehensive coverage, a subtlety that directly affects rulings on wiping in wudhu and tayammum, as well as the interpretation of conditional permissions. We will trace how the text derives partiality from the wudhu verse, contrasts it with the complete coverage required in tayammum due to a prophetic hadith, and then applies the same principle to a man's conditional oath.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key term and its literal meaning
Recognize that the preposition 'باء' in Arabic has a core meaning of attachment (ilṣāq). In Hanafi usul, it does not inherently imply completion or coverage; rather it connects an action to its instrument or object.
This is the foundational insight: 'باء' is for attachment, not for encompassing.
2
Analyze its use in the wudhu verse
Consider the verse 'فامسحوا برؤوسكم' (so wipe your heads). The 'باء' here attaches the wiping to the head, but it indicates the instrument of wiping, and customarily one does not wipe the entire head. Therefore, the intended meaning is partiality (tab'eed) – most of the hand or head is sufficient.
The text argues that 'باء' alone does not force isti'aab (complete coverage); partiality is the default.
3
Contrast with tayammum
In the tayammum verse 'فامسحوا بوجوهكم', the same 'باء' appears, but a well-known hadith (two strikes, one for face, one for forearms) proves that complete coverage is required. Thus, the 'باء' in that context is interpreted as redundant (ṣilah), and the ruling of isti'aab is established by the Sunnah.
The hadith overrides the default partiality; the 'باء' becomes a mere connector (ṣilah) not affecting the scope.
4
Extract the legal analogy
Tayammum is a substitute (khalaf) for wudhu. The principle is that a substitute retains the rulings of the original (al-badl lahu hukm al-mabdūl). Since wudhu requires complete coverage (of face and arms in tayammum's case), the substitute must also require completeness. This is supported by the indication of division (tanṣīf) – dividing the tayammum into two parts shows that each part must be fully covered.
Analogy (qiyas) is used: the original (wudhu) provides the ruling, and the substitute follows.
5
Apply to conditional permissions
Now apply the same 'باء' analysis to a man's statement: 'You may not leave this house except with my permission.' The 'باء' in 'except with my permission' attaches the exit to the permission. This means each exit requires a separate permission; permission is not general. In contrast, 'except that I give you permission' treats the act of permitting as the exception itself, which is of a different kind, so it is interpreted metaphorically as a limit (ghayah).
The 'باء' of attachment necessitates repetition of permission; the alternative wording avoids attachment and shifts the meaning to a limit.

The Particle 'Fi' as a Conditional: Metaphorical Extension in Hanafi Usul
Analysis of 'fi' as conditional in Hanafi usul

Learning Objective

Core Idea
The preposition 'fi' becomes a conditional particle when used metaphorically for accompaniment.

The particle 'fi' (في) is primarily a locative preposition but in legal language can be used metaphorically to indicate condition or accompaniment. This lesson examines how Hanafi usul treats such metaphorical usage, particularly in divorce (talaq) and debt (dayn) statements, and how the intended meaning affects the legal outcome.

Guided Path
1
Identify the primary meaning
Recognize that 'fi' is originally a locative preposition (zarf) indicating time or place.
Example: 'fi al-bayt' = in the house.
2
Detect metaphorical use
When 'fi' cannot function literally as a locative (e.g., before an action), it is used metaphorically (isti'ara) for accompaniment (muqarana).
The metaphorical meaning arises because the literal meaning is impossible.
3
Interpret as conditional
When 'fi' implies an action (e.g., 'dkhul' = entry), it takes on the meaning of condition (shart), equivalent to 'in' or 'upon'.
Example: 'You are divorced fi your entering the house' means divorce is conditional on entry.
4
Apply to divorce examples
Analyze statements like 'fi will of Allah' (conditional, no divorce) vs 'fi knowledge of Allah' (literal, impossible as condition, so statement is void and divorce does not occur).
The difference hinges on whether the phrase can be interpreted as a condition.
5
Apply to debt examples
In 'ten dirhams in ten dirhams', if 'fi' is meaningless, only ten are due; but if the speaker intends 'or' or 'and', the interpretation changes accordingly, leading to ten or twenty.
The intention of the speaker is considered in the absence of a literal meaning.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between sarih (explicit) and kinayah (allusive) expressions and state the Hanafi rule that sarih dispenses with intention while kinayah requires it.

Core Idea
Sarih needs no intention; kinayah needs intention to be effective.

In Islamic legal speech, words have different degrees of clarity. Some words are so clear that their legal effect is immediate, while others hide the intended meaning and require the speaker's intention to become operative. Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul presents a Hanafi analysis of the textual rulings for kaffarah (expiation) concerning feeding (it'am) and clothing (kiswa). The author opens by clarifying the linguistic nature of the verb 'it'am' (to feed): it is a transitive verb derived from the intransitive 'ta'ima' (to eat). As a general rule, when Arabic verbs become transitive by adding the hamza (as in af'ala pattern), their fundamental meaning remains; they do not take on a completely new meaning. Since the intransitive 'ta'ima' means 'to eat' (not 'to own'), the transitive 'at'ama' means 'to make someone eat' (not 'to give ownership'). Therefore, the text that prescribes feeding does not require transferring ownership of the food; rather, it requires making the poor person eat (ibaha: permission to consume). This is clear and simple. Anyone who insists that tamlik (ownership) is the intended meaning is abandoning the literal truth of the speech and forcing an unnatural interpretation. The author then mentions and refutes the opposing view (attributed to some, including al-Shafi'i) that ibaha (permission) is part of tamlik and that the text should be extended to include ownership because the needs of the poor are manifold and ownership fulfills all needs. The Hanafi response is that the text on feeding is already applied exactly; it addresses the specific need of hunger. Since food can satisfy hunger directly, the text's purpose is fulfilled by simply providing food for consumption. The case of clothing is different. The word 'kiswa' (clothing) can be a noun meaning 'garment' (with kasr of kaf) or a verbal noun (with fatha of kaf). The text uses it as a noun, meaning the physical object (a garment). For an object to become expiation, it must become owned by the expiating person and then transferred to the poor person. Therefore, the ruling for clothing is tamlik (giving ownership), not ibaha. This distinction is crucial. The author then refutes the Shafi'i analogy between feeding and clothing by pointing out fundamental differences: i'ara (lending) of clothing ends before the full benefit is realized, while ibaha in food is complete upon eating. They are opposites. Thus, the text on feeding cannot be extended to require ownership, and the text on clothing cannot be reduced to mere benefit. After establishing this, al-Bazdawi derives a corollary: since the obligation is to fulfill the needs of the poor (as indicated by the name 'miskin' which denotes need), the specific act of feeding can be done by giving one poor person ten meals over ten days, or ten poor persons in one hour, because the number of needs (hunger events) is met in both cases. This differs from clothing, where one garment suffices for a longer period. An objection is posed: if you allow feeding one person over ten days, why not allow clothing one person with ten garments over ten days? The Hanafi answer: clothing requires ownership, and ownership implies fulfilling all needs at once, so the garment fulfills the need for clothing for a duration. But if you give multiple garments to one person, the need for clothing is already met with the first, so the rest are superfluous. The minimum period for clothing is about a day (since a person needs a change of clothes daily), some scholars say. In contrast, hunger recurs daily, so feeding multiple times to one person is valid. The passage ends by citing some Hanafi teachers who allow giving all ten portions to one poor person in one day (e.g., one portion every hour), but the safer option is one day.

الكِسوة (al-kiswa): With kasr: a garment (noun); with fatha: the act of clothing (verbal noun). Here it refers to the garment itself.

The Hanafi Principle: Attribute Does Not Imply Negation
Hanafi rebuttal of Shafi'i view on attribute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school holds that an attribute (wasf) does not imply negation, and distinguish this from the Shafi'i position.

Core Idea
An attribute is at most a cause (ʿilla) for existence; it never implies negation.

In usul al‑fiqh, a recurring question is whether an attribute mentioned in a legal text (e.g., 'grazing camels') indicates that the ruling does not apply when that attribute is absent. The Shāfiʿī school generally answers yes; the Ḥanafī school says no. This lesson unpacks the Ḥanafī argument, which treats the attribute as a mere cause that establishes the ruling only when present, without implying its opposite when absent.

Guided Path
1
State the Shāfiʿī position
First, identify what Imām al‑Shāfiʿī claimed: a ruling attached to a specific attribute implies its negation when that attribute is absent, because the attribute functions like a condition (sharṭ).
The passage begins: 'that which is narrated from al-Shāfiʿī... that when a ruling is added to a specific attribute, it is evidence for its negation when that attribute is absent.'
2
State the Ḥanafī counter‑claim
The Ḥanafīs respond: 'and with us this is also false' (وَعِنْدَنَا هَذَا بَاطِلٌ أَيْضًا). The attribute does not necessitate negation.
3
Understand the distinction: condition vs. attribute vs. cause
A condition (sharṭ) suspends a pre‑existing obligation; when the condition is absent, the obligation does not exist. An attribute (waṣf), however, is not necessary for the ruling to exist—the ruling can also be established by the absolute name alone. The attribute only acts as a cause (ʿilla) that establishes the ruling when present, but does not negate it when absent.
The passage explains: the utmost degree of an attribute is that it becomes a cause (ʿilla), like 'thief' or 'adulterer' for ḥudūd punishments. A cause only establishes existence, not non‑existence.
4
Apply the principle to the Quranic examples
Qur’ān 4:23 (stepmothers) and 4:25 (believing slave women) are cited. The Shāfiʿī reading would exclude other women; the Ḥanafīs argue that the verses are not addressing the excepted cases. For instance, 'your believing slave women' does not prohibit marrying Kitābi slave women because the ruling is already established by the general name 'slave women'.
5
Note the thamarah al‑khilāf (practical difference)
The practical consequence: (1) Zakāt is not limited to grazing camels—all camels above niṣāb are subject to zakāt. (2) Marriage to a stepmother through zinā is prohibited only if she was in the husband's marriage. (3) Marriage to a Kitābi slave woman is permissible. These rulings follow from the principle that the attribute does not imply negation.

The Nature of Qasr: Original Ruling or Concession?
Hanafi vs Shafi'i dispute on the nature of qasr in travel

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school considers qasr an original ruling (asl) rather than a concession (rukhsah), and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument using the four proofs provided.

Core Idea
Qasr is the original obligation in travel, not a concession — the hadith of 'Aisha and the concept of pure dropping (isqat) prove this.

This passage from a Hanafi usul text addresses a foundational dispute: is shortening prayer (qasr) during travel an original obligation or a mere concession? The answer determines whether a traveler who prays four rak'ahs has fulfilled his duty or nullified his prayer. The author marshals two explicit and two subtle proofs to argue that qasr is the original ruling, and that the opposite view (Shafi'i) misunderstands the nature of the concession.

Guided Path
1
Identify the disputed issue
State clearly what the disagreement is about: the legal nature of qasr in travel — is it an original obligation (asl) or a concession (rukhsah)?
The dispute has a direct practical consequence (thamarah): if qasr is original, completing four rak'ahs invalidates the prayer; if a concession, it remains valid.
2
State the Hanafi position
The Hanafi school holds that qasr is the original ruling (asl) — travel is a cause that reduces the prayer to two rak'ahs, such that the traveler's obligation is exactly two rak'ahs, not four.
This is supported by the hadith of 'Aisha that prayer was originally prescribed as two rak'ahs and confirmed in travel.
3
State the Shafi'i counter-position
Imam al-Shafi'i argues that qasr is a concession (rukhsah) that does not nullify the original obligation of four rak'ahs. The traveler may choose to complete or shorten, analogous to fasting in travel where the concession is delay, not dropping.
The Shafi'i view relies on qiyas (analogy) with fasting.
4
Analyze the four Hanafi proofs
The author presents two apparent proofs (hadith of 'Aisha and the reward/punishment logic) and two hidden proofs (the concept of rukhsat al-isqat from the hadith of 'Umar, and the rational argument about servitude and choice). For each, identify the type of proof and how it establishes the Hanafi position.
The hidden proofs are especially important because they refute the Shafi'i analogy with fasting by showing qasr is a pure dropping (isqat), not a delay.
5
Determine the practical consequence (thamarah al-khilaf)
Conclude: If a traveler prays four rak'ahs, according to Hanafi the prayer is invalid (because it is an increase without justification), whereas according to Shafi'i it is valid (as the original obligation is fulfilled with an optional concession).
This shows how a usul difference translates into a fiqh ruling.

And al-Shafi'i (may Allah have mercy on him) said: it is a cause of concession, so it does not nullify the original ruling, as is said regarding the fasting person. And we have for what we have said two apparent proofs and two hidden proofs. As for the first two: one of them is that shortening is the original, and completion is an increase, as 'Aisha (may Allah be pleased with her) said: 'Prayer was prescribed as two rak'ahs, two rak'ahs; it was confirmed in travel and increased in residency.' The original cannot admit increase except by textual evidence. The second: we find that the merit of performing two rak'ahs is that if one performs them he is rewarded, and if he leaves them he is not punished, and this is the definition of supererogatory acts. As for the two hidden aspects: one of them is that this is a concession of dropping, because that is a right lifted from us, like the lifting of burdens and shackles. 'Umar (may Allah be pleased with him) said: O Messenger of Allah, why do we shorten the prayer while we are safe? The Prophet (peace be upon him) said: 'Indeed Allah has given you charity, so accept His charity.' The right of prayer upon us is a right that does not admit transfer, nor is there any monetary value in it, and His charity was a pure dropping that does not admit rejection. Do you not see that Allah's forgiveness of sins and His gift of freedom from the Fire can be rejected? This is something known by the intellects of sound nature. Unlike fasting, because the text obligated its postponement due to travel, not its dropping, so it remains obligatory, so its performance is valid, and it is established as a concession of delay, while in prayer there is a concession of dropping and abrogation, so its performance is nullified. The second: that servitude is incompatible with absolute will and complete choice, for these are attributes of Allah (majestic is His majesty). The servant only has choice regarding what benefits him, and Allah has absolute choice to do whatever He wills without any benefit returning to Him or any right binding Him.

Commentary
This passage is a Hanafi usul text discussing the legal nature of travel (safar) and its effect on the obligation of prayer. The author first defines travel as a prolonged departure, with the minimum being three days and nights (as established in fiqh). He explains that travel does not affect a person's legal capacity nor does it nullify any legal rulings; rather, it is a cause of alleviation due to inherent hardship, unlike illness which varies. The core dispute is whether shortening the prayer (qasr) in travel is an original ruling (asl) or a concession (rukhsah). The Hanafi position, attributed to Abu Hanifa and his school, is that qasr is the original obligation—prayer was initially prescribed as two rak'ahs, and this remains in travel; completion to four rak'ahs in residency is an increase that requires textual evidence. The Shafi'i view is that qasr is a concession that does not override the original obligation (azimah) of four rak'ahs, analogous to the concession for the sick or traveler who fasts. To support the Hanafi view, the author provides two apparent proofs (dalil zahir) and two hidden proofs (dalil khafi). The first apparent proof is the hadith of 'Aisha that prayer was prescribed as two rak'ahs and kept in travel but increased in residence. The second is that performing two rak'ahs is like a supererogatory act (nafl): reward for doing, no punishment for leaving. The first hidden proof is that the concession is one of dropping (isqat), like Allah's charity, not a transferable right; this is illustrated by the hadith of 'Umar. The second hidden proof is a rational argument: perfect servitude ('ubudiyya) cannot involve absolute choice—only Allah has that. The servant's choice is limited to benefit, but the original ruling in travel is a pure gift from Allah. The text contrasts this with fasting, where the concession is merely a delay, not a dropping.

Key Terms
السفر (al-safar): Prolonged departure (al-khuruj al-madid) with a minimum of three days and nights, as established in the Hanafi school.
القصر (al-qasr): Reduction of the four-rak'ah obligatory prayers to two rak'ahs during travel.
رخصة الإسقاط (rukhsat al-isqat): A concession that entirely drops a right or obligation, leaving no remaining duty.
