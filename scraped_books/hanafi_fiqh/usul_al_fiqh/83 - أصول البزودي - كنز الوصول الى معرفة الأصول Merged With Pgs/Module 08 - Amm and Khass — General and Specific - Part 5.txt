Condition in Name Only and Condition as Sign
Defining shart isman and shart 'alama with examples

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a condition as a condition in name only (shart isman) or as a sign ('alama), and apply the Hanafi position in the dispute with Zufar regarding conditional divorce.

Core Idea
The first of two combined conditions is only a name; and ihsan is a sign, not a true condition.

This lesson continues al-Bazdawi's classification with two special types: the condition that is only in name when two conditions are attached to a single ruling, and the condition that is actually a sign ('alama). The example of conditional divorce shows an intra-Hanafi dispute with Zufar, and the ihsan example clarifies why a sign does not affect causation. Understanding these distinctions is crucial for correctly applying conditions in legal reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the scenario of two conditions
When a ruling depends on two conditions (e.g., entering two houses), the first condition is only a name because the existence of the ruling is attributed to the last condition.
Example: 'If you enter house A and house B, you are divorced.'
2
Identify the role of ownership (nikah)
Ownership is required only for the validity of the consequence (divorce), not for the condition itself.
In the example, ownership (nikah) at the time of the first entry is not needed because no consequence occurs then.
3
Apply the ruling to the divorce case
If the wife enters one house after separation, then remarries and enters the second, she is divorced upon the second entry according to Abu Hanifa.
Contrary to Zufar who requires ownership at the first condition.
4
Define ihsan as a sign
Ihsan (having been in a valid marriage) does not prevent zina from being a cause; it only defines the punishment (stoning vs. flogging).
Ihsan is a sign ('alama), not a condition, because it does not delay the effect of zina.
5
Contrast sign with true condition
A true condition prevents the cause from taking effect until it occurs; a sign only identifies the ruling.
Witnesses of ihsan do not guarantee if they retract, unlike witnesses of a true condition.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul al-Bazdawi (Kanz al-Wusul) presents a classification of conditions (shurut) in Hanafi legal theory. The author begins by discussing the act of an animal: such an act is not considered to impose a ruling (e.g., liability), but it can negate pre-existing obligation. For example, if a dog veers from the direction it was sent, or an animal wanders after being sent, that behavior cuts off the liability of the sender. Then he addresses the case of digging a well: if a person digs a well and someone falls in, the digger is not liable if the victim intentionally threw himself in, because digging is a condition (shart) that substitutes for the cause ('illa) when the 'illa cannot be directly applied. Here, istihsan gives the digger the benefit of the doubt. This contrasts with a direct injurer (jarh) who cannot claim the death was due to another cause because he is the owner of the 'illa. Next, the author discusses inciting a dog: if a dog kills owned game or a person, the inciter is not liable because the dog acts by its own nature (a free action intervenes). However, if the dog kills game, the inciter is treated as if he slaughtered it himself because hunting is a form of acquisition, and liability is based on strict analogy (qiyas). Similarly, throwing fire in the road or releasing poisonous insects does not create liability if the wind or movement causes the harm, because a free action of nature intervenes. These examples are drawn from the chapter on division of causes. Then the author introduces the fourth type: condition in name only (shart isman). When a ruling depends on two conditions, the first is a condition only in name because the existence of the ruling is attributed to the last condition. The example: a man says to his wife, 'If you enter this house and this house, you are divorced,' then separates her, she enters one, he remarries her, then she enters the second. According to Abu Hanifa, she is divorced upon entering the second, contrary to Zufar who would require ownership (nikah) at the time of the first condition. The Hanafi position: ownership is only required for the validity of the consequence (the divorce), not for the condition itself. Since no divorce occurs at the first entry, ownership need not exist then. The fifth type is the condition as a sign ('alama), exemplified by ihsan (having been in a valid marriage) in the law of zina. Ihsan is not a true condition because it does not delay the effect of zina as a cause; instead, it defines the ruling (i.e., stoning vs. flogging). If the witnesses of ihsan retract, they do not guarantee (pay blood money) because ihsan is only a sign, not a cause. This contrasts with a pure condition where retraction would entail liability.

Key Terms
شرط اسم (shart isman): A condition that is only in name, not in ruling; the first of two conditions attached to a single ruling.
علامة (alama): A sign; a characteristic that indicates the ruling but does not affect its causation or existence, like ihsan for zina.

The Condition in Name Only: Shart Isman La Hukman
Defining the fourth type of condition: condition in name only.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify a condition that is a condition in name only and apply the Hanafi rule that only the last of two sequential conditions is legally effective.

Core Idea
When a ruling depends on two conditions, only the last condition carries legal effect; the first is a condition in name only.

This lesson introduces a key Hanafi usul distinction: a condition that is called a condition but has no legal effect. When a ruling is tied to two consecutive conditions, the first is a condition in name only because the legal consequence depends only on the last condition. We will parse the definition and see it applied in a divorce scenario, noting the difference with Zufar's view.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition
Read the first sentence: 'Every ruling connected to two conditions: the first of them is a condition in name, not in effect.' Understand that the legal effect is attributed to the second condition.
The key phrase is 'shart isman la hukman'.
2
Analyze the example
Work through the hypothetical: husband says 'If you enter house A and house B, you are divorced.' He divorces her, she enters A, remarries, then enters B. According to Hanafis, divorce occurs only upon entering B.
Ownership (marriage) is needed at the time of the consequence, not at the time of the first condition.
3
Understand the reasoning (talil)
The existence of a condition requires ownership only for the consequence, not for the condition itself. Since the consequence (divorce) could not occur after the first entry (no marriage), the first condition cannot be effective.
If the first condition were real, it would require ownership at that time, but that is not required.
4
Contrast with Zufar's position
Zufar holds that both conditions are equal and require ownership at each time. The Hanafi response: making ownership a condition for the first condition itself is unnecessary and leads to contradiction.
This dispute shows practical application of the definition.

Commentary
This passage defines the fourth type of condition in Hanafi usul: a condition that is a condition in name only, not in legal effect. The author states the general principle: when a ruling is tied to two conditions, the first is a condition in name only because the actual existence is attributed to the second condition. For example, if a husband says to his wife, 'If you enter this house and this house, you are divorced,' then he divorces her (separates her), she enters one house, then he remarries her, and she enters the second house—according to Hanafis, she becomes divorced upon entering the second house, contrary to Zufar. The reasoning: ownership (being a wife) is a condition for the validity of the consequence (divorce taking effect), but not for the condition itself (entry). Since no new marriage existed when the first entry occurred, ownership cannot be a condition for the first entry. The authority weight is from the matn of al-Bazdawi, part of the Hanafi usul canon.

Key Terms
شرط اسماً لا حكماً (shart isman la hukman): A condition that is called a condition only in name, but has no legal effect because the actual legal attachment is to a later condition.
الملك (al-milk): Ownership, i.e., the marital bond (nikah) that is a precondition for divorce to take effect.
الجزاء (al-jaza'): The consequence of the condition, i.e., the divorce.

Condition as a Sign (Shart 'Alamah) and Its Legal Implications
Defining the fifth type of condition: condition as a sign/marker.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify a condition as a sign (shart 'alamah) versus a true condition, and derive the resulting rules for witness testimony and retraction.

Core Idea
A sign condition identifies the ruling but does not affect the cause, altering how testimony and retraction are treated.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, conditions are classified into five types. The fifth type is the condition that is a sign (shart 'alamah)—it does not prevent the cause from being effective, but merely marks the ruling. The classic example is ihsan (marital status) in zina: the zina itself is the cause of the penalty, but whether the penalty is stoning or flogging depends on ihsan. This distinction has practical consequences for witness testimony and the liability of witnesses upon retraction. In this lesson, we will unpack this concept and see how it applies to two contested issues: testimony of women and testimony of a midwife.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of shart 'alamah
The passage defines the fifth type of condition as 'the condition that is a sign' and gives ihsan in zina as the example. A true condition prevents a cause from being effective until it exists, but ihsan does not affect the effectiveness of zina as a cause for punishment.
Contrast this with a pure condition (shart mahd) that must exist before the cause can operate.
2
Analyze the consequences of ihsan being a sign
Because ihsan is a sign, witnesses who testify to ihsan (that the accused is muhsan) do not incur liability (damān) if they retract. This differs from witnesses in pure condition cases. Additionally, ihsan can be proven by women's testimony alone, since no penalty is directly imposed; it only marks the severity.
The text explicitly says: 'for this we say that ihsan is established by the testimony of women along with men, and pure masculinity is not required'.
3
Evaluate the objection and response regarding disbelievers' testimony
An objection is raised: if disbelievers testify that a Muslim slave was emancipated, and the slave later commits zina, why isn't that testimony accepted? The response distinguishes: women's testimony is specific to the subject matter (it cannot impose a penalty), but disbelievers' testimony would cause harm to the Muslim, so it is rejected. This reinforces that only signs (not penalties) can be established by women's testimony.
The distinction hinges on the 'alamah nature: no penalty is directly attached to the sign.
4
Examine the intra-Hanafi dispute on midwife testimony
The Sahibayn (Abu Yusuf and Muhammad) accept the midwife's testimony to identify a newborn even without a marriage bed, because it is a mere determination (ta'yin) like a sign. Abu Hanifa rejects it, arguing that lineage is a hidden matter that cannot be established by testimony alone without a basis like firash.
This dispute illustrates how applying the 'alamah principle can lead to different outcomes within the school.

Hanafi rebuttal of Shafi'i on dhimmi liability

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the Hanafi position on the effect of a dhimmi's religious conviction (diyaanah) on legal liability and contrast it with the Shafi'i position, identifying the thamarah al-khilaf.

Core Idea
A dhimmi's diyaanah establishes 'ismah, protecting from both punishment and liability, not just punishment alone.

This passage addresses a classic usul dispute between the Hanafi and Shafi'i schools regarding the legal scope of a dhimmi's religious conviction (diyaanah). The context is a ruling about maintenance for an imprisoned relative, but the principle extends to all legal liabilities. Understanding this dispute is key to appreciating how the Hanafi school conceptualizes 'ismah (inviolability) and its basis in the dhimmi's own religious law.

Guided Path
1
Identify the issue
The central question: does a dhimmi's religious conviction (diyaanah) only repel corporal punishment (like hadd), or does it also affect other legal rulings such as liability for maintenance?
2
State the Shafi'i position
Imam al-Shafi'i holds that diyaanah only repels punishment (ta'arruḍ) – e.g., the dhimmi is not subject to hadd for drinking wine. Other rulings remain based on Islamic law.

State the Hanafi position

4
Examine the Hanafi rebuttal
The Hanafi response: the valuation of wealth and protection of lives fall under 'ismah, which is achieved through diyaanah. Therefore, 'ismah preserves from both punishment and liability. The Shafi'i limitation is invalidated.
5
Note the thamarah al-khilaf
The practical consequence: can a qadi order a dhimmi to pay maintenance for an imprisoned relative? Hanafi says no (liability is repelled), Shafi'i says yes.

The Third Category of Ignorance: Definition and Legal Principle
Defines third category and cites hadith evidence

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define the third category of ignorance (jahl) in Hanafi usul and explain how it can block hadd punishments, using the hadith evidence cited.

Core Idea
Ignorance in ijtihad or shubha blocks hadd; ignorance of consensus does not.

In Hanafi usul, ignorance is not a blanket excuse. The scholars distinguished three categories of ignorance. The third category is crucial because it determines when a person may be excused from punishment despite committing a prohibited act. This lesson unpacks the definition and the textual evidence from the hadith that supports leniency in cases of legitimate ijtihad or ambiguity.

Guided Path
1
Isolate the definition
Identify the precise wording of the third category: الجهل في موضع الاجتهاد الصحيح، أو في غير موضع الاجتهاد، لكن في موضع الشبهة. Note the two branches: (a) ignorance in a matter open to ijtihad, and (b) ignorance in a matter not open to ijtihad but involving shubha.
The Arabic 'موضع الاجتهاد الصحيح' means a matter where valid ijtihad is possible.
2
Examine the hadith evidence
The author cites multiple hadiths enjoining al-amr bi al-ma'ruf wa al-nahy 'an al-munkar and al-nasihah. These hadiths, though ahad, establish the general obligation of correcting wrongdoing. They form the basis for leniency: if a person acts out of ignorance in a debatable matter, they may be excused because the community is commanded to advise, not to punish immediately.
The hadiths are of zanni thubut and dalalah, consistent with the nature of the rule being a usul principle rather than a definitive text.
3
Link the hadith to the rule
Understand the wajh al-dalalah: the obligation to enjoin good and forbid evil implies that a person who errs due to ignorance should be guided, not punished with hadd. The hadiths support the idea that shubha (ambiguity) blocks punishment, as the person is still within the fold of the community’s advice and correction.
This connection is not explicit in the hadiths but is derived by the Hanafi usul method.
4
Distinguish from consensus ignorance
The text contrasts this category with the first (ignorance contrary to consensus). The hadiths do not excuse ignorance of clear, universally known prohibitions. A person who claims ignorance of the obligation of wudu, for example, cannot claim shubha.
This distinction is critical: only ignorance in areas of legitimate disagreement or ambiguity qualifies for leniency.

Applying the Rule of Ignorance: Examples in Fiqh
Applies the rule to multiple fiqh scenarios

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi rule of ignorance to the specific examples of prayer, qisas, fasting, zina, and wine drinking, distinguishing when shubha blocks punishment.

Core Idea
The same shubha blocks hadd in qisas and zina but not in cases of consensus like wudu.

The previous lesson established the principle that ignorance in matters of ijtihad or shubha can block punishment. Now we see how Hanafi jurists applied this rule to concrete scenarios. The examples range from missed prayers to homicide to wine drinking. Each example tests whether the ignorance falls within the third category or not.

Guided Path
1
Parse the prayer example
First scenario: a person prays Dhuhr without wudu, then prays 'Asr with wudu thinking Dhuhr was valid. 'Asr is invalid because his ignorance of the need for wudu is contrary to consensus. Second scenario: he makes up Dhuhr, then prays Maghrib believing 'Asr was valid. This is permissible because the order of missed prayers is a matter of ijtihad.
Contrast: ignorance of a condition (wudu) vs. ignorance of a ruling about order.
2
Analyze the qisas example
A killer with two guardians; one pardons qisas, the second kills thinking qisas is still fully due. No qisas because the killer's ignorance about the effect of one guardian's pardon is a matter of ijtihad and shubha.
The shubha here is the ambiguity about the right to qisas after partial pardon.
3
Examine the fasting example
A fasting person cups himself and then breaks his fast thinking cupping invalidated it. No kaffarah because his ignorance about cupping's effect on fasting is a matter of ijtihad (some scholars hold cupping breaks fast).
Kaffarah is like a penalty; it is dropped due to shubha.
4
Compare the zina examples
Zina with a wife's or father's slave girl: no hadd because of shubha (possibly the slave is permissible). But with a brother's slave girl: hadd applies because there is no shubha. Similarly, a harbi Muslim who drinks wine claiming ignorance: no hadd; but a dhimmi who converts and drinks: hadd applies because he already knew the prohibition.
The key is whether the ignorance is in a matter of ijtihad (ownership of slave girls) or not (wine for a former dhimmi).

Jest in Khula' with Khiyar: Abu Hanifa vs. Sahibayn
Analyzing jest's effect on khula' with option condition and full compensation

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the Hanafi positions on the effect of jest in khula' with an option condition, EXPLAIN the ikhtilaf between Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn, and APPLY the rulings to various scenarios of jest.

Core Idea
In khula', jest suspends divorce according to Abu Hanifa but the Sahibayn hold it immediate—the condition's validity is the key.

This passage from Al-Bazdawi's Usul explores how the presence of an option condition (khiyar) interacts with jest (hazl) in the context of khula' divorce. It highlights a key intra-Hanafi dispute: does jest nullify the condition, or does the condition suspend the effect of jest? Understanding this helps us grasp the Hanafi method of weighing intent against utterance.

Guided Path
1
Identify the two main positions
Find the statement from Abu Hanifa and the Sahibayn on the effect of khiyar in khula' with hazl. Note that Abu Hanifa says divorce is suspended, while Sahibayn say divorce occurs immediately.
The passage begins with 'الشرط، وقد نُصَّ عن أبي حنيفة...'
2
Understand the usul principle
Recognize that the dispute hinges on whether the condition (khiyar) is effective in khula'. For Abu Hanifa, the condition is valid and suspends the divorce; for Sahibayn, the condition is void because khula' is akin to an irrevocable divorce.
This relates to the maxim that 'al-umur bi-maqasidiha' (intent matters).
3
Analyze the scenario of jester's abandonment

The Quranic Exception and Travel Concessions
Textual analysis of Quranic exception with usul and hadith

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi usul principle of bayan to derive that travel is a valid cause for legal concession (rukhsah) from the Quranic exception 'غير باغ ولا عاد'.

Core Idea
The exception in the Quran separates rebellion from the act itself, making travel permissible for concessions.

This lesson explores a key textual analysis in Hanafi usul: how the Quranic phrase 'غير باغ ولا عاد' (Quran 2:173) is interpreted to distinguish between the prohibition of rebellion and the permissibility of travel. The author argues that travel is not inherently sinful, unlike intoxication, and therefore can serve as a basis for legal concessions. The analysis uses the Hadith evidence to reinforce that rebellion is a separate sin. By the end, you will see how this distinction is derived textually and applied in fiqh.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Quranic Text
Locate the verse and the specific phrase 'غير باغ ولا عاد' in the source. Note that the verse permits consumption of prohibitions under duress with this exception.
The full verse is Quran 2:173.
2
Distinguish the Two Meanings
Understand that the prohibition of rebellion (baghy) and aggression ('udwan) is separate from the act of travel. The text states that rebellion is a sin in itself, while travel is not.
The passage contrasts rebellion (which occurs on protected rights) with travel (which occurs on a different locus).
3
Apply the Hanafi Bayan Principle
The Hanafi principle of bayan clarifies that the exception refers to the act itself, not the person's state. Thus, the compelled person must not exceed necessity, and travel is not inherently transgressive.
This is a form of textual specification (takhsis) by context.
4
Derive the Ruling
From the separation, conclude that travel is a valid cause for rukhsah because it is not disobedience in itself, unlike intoxication which is inherently sinful and cannot justify concession.
The hadith on obedience further confirm that rebellion is a separate sin, strengthening the argument.

Commentary
This passage is a textual analysis of the Quranic exception 'غير باغ ولا عاد' in Surah al-Baqarah (2:173) and its application to the rulings of travel concessions. The author begins by distinguishing between the prohibition of rebellion (baghy) and aggression ('udwan) and the act of travel (safar) itself. He argues that the prohibition of rebellion and banditry is not inherent in travel but stems from a separate meaning — namely, transgression against authority and society. Travel is a different locus (mahall); it is an act that occurs on a place other than the protected rights of life and property. Therefore, travel can be a cause (sabab) for legal concessions (rukhsah) because it is not inherently sinful. The author contrasts travel with intoxication (sukr), which is disobedience in itself and thus cannot serve as a basis for concession. He then analyzes the Quranic verse, clarifying that the exception 'غير باغ ولا عاد' refers to the act itself — i.e., the compelled person must not exceed what sustains his life. The wording of the verse supports this interpretation. Finally, he notes that the rulings of travel are numerous and ends with 'والله أعلم'. The footnote (1) provides supporting hadith on the obligation of obedience to rulers and the prohibition of rebellion, reinforcing the point that rebellion is a separate sin from travel. These hadith are from authentic sources and serve as proofs for the mainstream Hanafi position on the necessity of obedience unless commanded to sin.

Key Terms
باغ (baghi): One who rebels against legitimate authority or transgresses beyond necessity.
عاد (aadi): One who aggresses and exceeds limits, especially in consuming prohibitions under duress.
رخصة (rukhsah): A concession granted due to necessity or valid cause, such as travel, that permits what is normally prohibited.
