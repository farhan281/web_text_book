Editorial Methodology in Usul al-Bazdawi
Editorial preface describing methodology and manuscripts

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the editor's methodology for producing a critical edition of Usul al-Bazdawi, including his use of manuscripts and integration of takhrij.

Core Idea
A critical edition of a foundational usul text requires careful manuscript comparison and integration of supplementary works like takhrij.

When you open a modern printed edition of a classical Hanafi usul text, you are seeing the result of an editor's careful work. This lesson examines the editorial preface of Usul al-Bazdawi, where the editor describes his two main goals: establishing a correct text from manuscripts and making it typographically clear. He also explains why he did not add extensive commentary, and how he combined the original text with the hadith takhrij of Qasim ibn Qutlubugha. Understanding this process helps us appreciate the reliability of the sources we rely on for istinbat.

Guided Path
1
Identify the editor's two main goals
Read the first paragraph: the editor states his purpose is to establish the correct text from manuscripts and to serve it typographically so it is bright, clear, and accessible.
2
Understand why he omitted extensive commentary
The editor notes the extreme difficulty of the book and explains that brief marginal notes are insufficient. He directs students to full commentaries like those of al-Babarti, al-Sighnaqi, and al-Bukhari.
He only added rare signatory notes marked with his name 'Sa'id'.
3
Observe how he integrated the takhrij
He combined al-Bazdawi's Usul with the hadith takhrij by Qasim ibn Qutlubugha into one volume, placing each hadith reference at its appropriate place in the original text.
This is described as 'setting the gem in the ring'.
4
Examine the manuscript descriptions
The editor describes two key manuscripts: one from 651 AH (Paris, 200 folios, few annotations) and one from 692 AH (Damascus, 270 folios, richly annotated with red headings and full diacritics). Note their provenance and features.
These manuscripts show the diversity of textual transmission.

Commentary
This passage is an editorial introduction from the modern edition of al-Bazdawi's Usul al-Fiqh. The editor explains his twofold purpose: first, to establish a corrected text from manuscript sources, and second, to produce a typographically clear edition that is easy to read. He notes that he did not write extensive marginal commentary because the difficulty of the original requires full annotated commentary, and he directs students to the existing commentaries by al-Babarti, al-Sighnaqi, and 'Ala' al-Din al-Bukhari. However, he did add small signatory notes marked with his name 'Sa'id' as a way to identify his own brief clarifications. A key feature of his edition is that he integrated the hadith takhrij (source attribution) by Qasim ibn Qutlubugha directly into the text, placing each hadith reference at its appropriate location within al-Bazdawi's work. He describes this as combining the original (asl) and the subsidiary (far') beautifully. The editor then provides a detailed description of two important manuscripts he used: the first is an old copy from 651 AH (1253 CE) housed in the National Library of Paris, described as excellent but with few marginal notes; the second is a magnificent royal copy from 692 AH (1293 CE) copied in Damascus by Mahmud al-Samarqandi, with extensive annotations, red headings, and full diacritics. This manuscript later became part of a waqf endowment by the mother of Sultan Selim III. The editor acknowledges a colleague who provided him with images of these manuscripts. The passage is a standard editorial preface, not a legal text, and provides background on the textual transmission of al-Bazdawi's Usul.

Key Terms
تخريج (takhrij): The process of attributing hadiths to their original sources and evaluating their chains of transmission.
أصول البزدوي (Usul al-Bazdawi): The famous Hanafi usul al-fiqh work by Fakhr al-Islam 'Ali al-Bazdawi, titled 'Kanz al-Wusul ila Ma'rifat al-Usul'.

The Methodology of Takhrij in Hanafi Usul: The Contribution of Allama Qasim
Description of Qasim's takhrij methodology

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain the methodology of Allama Qasim in takhrij of al-Bazdawi's Usul and identify the role of such takhrij works in Hanafi usul studies.

Core Idea
Takhrij traces hadiths to their sources and grades them, strengthening the evidential basis of usul arguments.

Allama Qasim ibn Qutlubugha wrote a pioneering takhrij of the hadiths and athar cited in al-Bazdawi's foundational Usul al-Fiqh. This lesson unpacks his methodology as described in his introduction and elsewhere, showing how he systematically verified the prophetic and companion reports that underpin Hanafi usul principles. Understanding his approach reveals how later scholars consolidated the proof structure of the madhhab.

Guided Path
1
Identify the need for takhrij in usul
Usul al-fiqh texts often cite hadiths and athar as evidence. Takhrij verifies their sources and authenticity, ensuring the proof is sound. Qasim's work fills this gap for al-Bazdawi's text.
2
Describe Qasim's two-step method
For hadiths explicitly cited by al-Bazdawi, Qasim relied on that citation. For others, he turned to the commentary of Abd al-Aziz al-Bukhari (Kashf al-Asrar) to identify the report.
This shows a hierarchy of reliance: first the matn, then the primary sharh.
3
Recognize the chronological and scholarly context
Qasim completed the work in 859 AH at age 57, indicating his maturity. He noted he was the first to undertake this takhrij, highlighting its originality.
This places the work in the 9th century, a period of consolidation in Hanafi scholarship.
4
Understand the structural arrangement
Qasim followed al-Bazdawi's chapter order. For each chapter heading, he extracted and traced every hadith or athar mentioned in that chapter.
This systematic organization made the work user-friendly for students of al-Bazdawi.
5
Appreciate the significance
Qasim's takhrij bridges hadith sciences and usul al-fiqh, showing that usul arguments are grounded in verified transmissions. It also demonstrates the collaborative nature of Hanafi scholarship.
Similar works exist for other usul texts, but Qasim's was pioneering for al-Bazdawi.

Commentary
This passage explains the methodology of Allama Qasim (likely Qasim ibn Qutlubugha) in his work of takhrij (identifying and grading) the hadiths and athar (reports from Companions) that appear in Al-Bazdawi's foundational Hanafi usul al-fiqh text. Qasim states his approach in the introduction: he relies on al-Bazdawi's own citations where explicit, and for those not explicitly cited, he relies on the commentary of Abd al-Aziz al-Bukhari (Kashf al-Asrar). He authored the book in 859 AH at age 57. He also mentions his book in his biographical work Taj al-Tarajim, noting his pioneering role in this takhrij. He arranged the work according to al-Bazdawi's chapter order, and for each chapter heading he extracts all relevant hadiths and athar. This systematic approach was a significant contribution to Hanafi usul studies.

The Three Components of Fiqh: Knowledge, Mastery, Action
Defining fiqh and its components with proofs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the three components of fiqh and explain how each is supported by scriptural evidence.

Core Idea
Fiqh is not merely knowledge; it is knowledge mastered and acted upon.

This lesson examines the Hanafi usul definition of fiqh. Unlike a purely academic definition, the text insists that fiqh requires practical action. By exploring the Quranic term 'hikmah', the linguistic root of 'fiqh', and a prophetic hadith, the author builds a composite definition.

Guided Path
1
Identify the three components
The passage divides fiqh into knowledge of what is prescribed, mastery of that knowledge, and action. This is the core definition.
2
Examine the Quranic proof linking fiqh to hikmah
Allah calls Sharia knowledge 'hikmah' in verse 2:269. Ibn Abbas interprets hikmah as knowledge of halal and haram. This connects fiqh to wisdom, which in language means knowledge and action.
The editor provides a chain for this interpretation.
3
Analyze the linguistic derivation and the poet's usage
The word 'fiqh' is derived from precision in understanding combined with action, as illustrated by the bedouin poet calling a physician 'faqih'.
The poet's use shows that a faqih is one who knows and acts on that knowledge.
4
Apply the definition to the concept of a faqih
One who possesses all three is a faqih mutlaq; partial possession yields a limited faqih.
5
Support from the hadith and the verse on indhar
The Prophet praised those who understand the beginning and end, halal and haram. The verse on tafaqquh emphasizes warning others, which combines knowledge and calling to action.
Indhar (warning) here means inviting to knowledge and action.

The Requirement (Muqtada) and Its Signs

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the muqtada in a given text, apply its three conditions, and distinguish it from idmar.

Core Idea
Muqtada is what the text requires for its own validity — without it, the text fails.

This passage from Al-Bazdawi's Kanz al-Wusul introduces a key concept in Hanafi usul: the 'muqtada' (implied requirement). Unlike ordinary implicit references (idmar), a muqtada is a logical necessity for the correctness of the text itself. Understanding this distinction is crucial for correct istinbat, as muqtada carries the same binding force as an explicit statement.

Guided Path
1
Identify the definition of muqtada
Locate the author's definition: muqtada is what the text requires for its correctness. The text says: 'فإن ذلك أمر اقتضاه النص لصحة ما تناوله' (that is a matter required by the text for the correctness of what it covers).
Muqtada is not explicitly stated; it is logically inferred.
2
Understand the sign (علامة) of muqtada
The sign has three conditions: (1) the mentioned thing becomes valid through it, (2) it is not nullified when it appears, (3) it is suitable for the intended purpose. These conditions ensure that the muqtada is genuinely necessary.
If any condition fails, the implication is not muqtada.
3
Contrast muqtada with idmar
Using the Quranic example 'Ask the village' (Yusuf 82), the 'people' are not muqtada because they are not necessary for the text's correctness; the command to ask is valid even without specifying sentient beings. Instead, it is idmar (implicit reference).
The key difference: muqtada is necessary for the text's validity; idmar is not.
4
Apply the concept to the example of freeing a slave
The command to free a slave for expiation (kaffarah) necessarily implies ownership. Without ownership, the command cannot be fulfilled. Thus, ownership is a muqtada.
This example confirms that muqtada is treated as if explicitly stated.
5
Summarize the legal effect
The muqtada is attached to the text via the requirement and has the same binding force as an explicit textual ruling. It must be acted upon.
This is why Hanafi usul treats muqtada as a source of obligation.

Time in Hanafi usul is not merely a container; it also functions as cause, condition, or standard depending on the act.

The First Two Sentence Types in Hanafi Usul: Consequential and Dependent
Defining first two sentence types: consequential and dependent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the first two types of sentences (consequential and dependent) in Hanafi usul al‑fiqh, explain their legal effect, and apply the definitions to textual examples.

Core Idea
A consequential sentence binds the ruling exclusively to its cause; a dependent sentence requires context for validity.

In Hanafi usul, legal sentences are classified into four types based on how they relate to their context. This lesson focuses on the first two: the consequential sentence (جملة جزائية), which is linked by the particle 'fa' and restricted to its specific cause, and the dependent sentence (جملة غير مستقلة), which is not self-standing and gains meaning only through context. Understanding these categories helps clarify the scope of legal rulings derived from prophetic actions and statements, especially in matters like acknowledgment (iqrār).

Guided Path
1
Identify the 'fa' of consequence
In a text containing 'fa' (ف) that connects a result to a preceding action, the sentence is considered a consequential sentence (جزاء). The 'fa' marks the result as specific to that cause.
Examples: 'saha fa-sajada' (forgot and prostrated) and 'zana fa-rujima' (committed adultery and was stoned).
2
Apply the rule of specificity
Because the 'fa' indicates consequence, the ruling (e.g., prostration, stoning) applies only to the specific cause mentioned. It cannot be generalized to other contexts without additional evidence.
Contrast with general commands that apply broadly.
3
Recognize dependent sentences
Sentences that are not independent are those that rely on a preceding question or context for their meaning. The examples given are response particles: 'bala', 'na'am', and 'ajal' used in reply to a question.
These are not complete independent statements; they gain legal force through the question.
4
Determine legal effect of dependent sentences
In Hanafi law, a response like 'bala' to a negative question is treated as an acknowledgment (iqrar). Similarly, 'na'am' to a positive question and 'ajal' (covering both) also constitute iqrar when used in interrogation context.
Even when used without a question, they may retain this effect by implication.

Refuting Objections to Mutawatir
Refutation of false traditions and status of denying mutawatir

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to critique common objections against mutawatir reports and state the Hanafi position that denying mutawatir leads to disbelief.

Core Idea
Objections based on isolated reports or speculative narratives fail against mutawatir; rejecting such certain knowledge is kufr.

In this continuation, al-Bazdawi addresses specific false traditions that might be used to cast doubt on the principle of tawatur: Zoroastrian reports, a fabricated story about a king, Jewish reports, and the crucifixion narrative. He demonstrates that each objection is based on weak transmission or inattentiveness, and therefore does not undermine the certainty of mutawatir. He concludes that denying a mutawatir report constitutes disbelief (kufr). The passage then transitions to the second category of reports: mashhur.

Guided Path
1
Identify the objections
List the four objections mentioned: Zoroastrian reports (all illusion), horse-leg story (fabricated in king's court), Jewish reports (ahad from seven people), and the crucifixion (speculative substitution).
These are examples of non-mutawatir reports that some might elevate to challenge tawatur.
2
Analyze each objection's weakness
For each, explain why it does not meet the standard of tawatur: Zoroastrian reports are imaginal; the horse story was witnessed only by the king's retinue; Jewish reports are ahad; the crucifixion is based on a Quranic verse (4:157) that allows possibility of substitution, but such reports are from hostile sources.
The common flaw: they lack the necessary conditions of tawatur (large number, justice, etc.).
3
Apply the principle of mutawatir overriding doubts
The author states that these aspects are invalidated by the mutawatir. This means that once a report is established as mutawatir, any contradictory reports are automatically rejected because they lack certainty.
This is a key usul principle: the certain (qati') takes precedence over the speculative (zanni).
4
Conclude the status of denying mutawatir
The passage explicitly says: 'فصار منكر المتواتر ومخالفه كافراً بالله العظيم' (the denier and opponent of the mutawatir becomes a disbeliever in God Almighty). Understand that this is because rejecting certain knowledge from God is equivalent to rejecting revelation.
This shows the high epistemological value of mutawatir in Hanafi thought.
5
Transition to mashhur
Notice the final sentence: 'مع المرتبة الثانية فهو المشهور، وهذا: باب المشهور من الأخبار' (along with the second rank, it is the mashhur. And this is the chapter of mashhur reports). This introduces the next topic.
Mashhur is below mutawatir but above ahad; it does not yield certainty but is widely transmitted.

Definition of Intellect and its Role in Report Acceptance
Definition of intellect and its types, with ruling on child's report

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi definition of 'aql, classify its types (deficient and complete), and explain why the report of a child is not a proof (hujja).

Core Idea
A report is only a proof if the reporter has complete intellect — the child's deficient intellect disqualifies his testimony.

In this passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul, we examine the first condition for accepting a report: the intellect ('aql) of the reporter. The author defines intellect as a light and divides it into deficient and complete. He then argues that only complete intellect, marked by puberty, makes a report a hujja. This lesson unpacks that definition and its implications.

Guided Path
1
Identify the term being defined
The author defines 'aql (intellect) as a condition for accepting a report. Recognize that this is a definition lesson.
2
Understand the definition of 'aql
'Aql is a spiritual light that enables the heart to perceive after sensory perception ends. It is known through a person's choices that lead to beneficial outcomes.
This definition emphasizes that intellect is not merely rational capacity but a divinely granted light.
3
Distinguish the two types of intellect
There is deficient intellect (qasir) found in children, and complete intellect (kamil) found in adults. The deficiency is indicated by the fact that the child's intellect increases over time, showing initial deficiency.
The absolute (mutlaq) intellect refers to its perfect form.
4
Apply the ruling: child's report is not a hujja
Since Shari'ah attaches obligations to the minimum degree of complete intellect (marked by puberty), a child lacks that. Moreover, the Shari'ah does not make a child a guardian even in worldly affairs, so in religious matters it is even more appropriate to reject his report. The same applies to the insane.
This is an implicit qiyas: if not guardian in dunya, then not guardian in deen.

Precedence of Certainty When Permission Precedes Prohibition
Principles of conflicting evidence and hadith authentication

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to apply the Hanafi principle that when a permission precedes a prohibition, the certain state (al-mutayaqqan) takes precedence, and identify the scope of this rule by distinguishing between the original creation (asl al-wad') and the fatrah period.

Core Idea
Certainty in permissibility is not overridden by a later prohibition unless the prohibition is itself certain.

This passage from Usul al-Fiqh addresses a critical question: when we have an earlier evidence permitting something and a later evidence prohibiting it, which prevails? The Hanafi answer prioritizes the state of certainty (al-mutayaqqan) — the original permissibility — because what is certain cannot be removed by doubt. However, the principle is carefully scoped: it applies only to the interval between prophets (fatrah) before our Shariah, not to the original creation where humans were never without divine guidance. The author illustrates the rule with the example of eating dabb (lizard) and spends considerable effort defending the hadith that prohibits it, demonstrating the rigorous method of hadith authentication in the Hanafi school.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Ruling Principle
The author states: 'فكان المتيقن أولى' (the certain is more appropriate). This is the core rule: when permission precedes prohibition, we hold onto the certain state of permissibility.
This principle is based on the maxim 'al-yaqin la yazulu bi al-shakk' (certainty is not removed by doubt).
2
Understand the Scope of the Principle
The author clarifies that this rule is built on the view that permissibility (ibaha) is the original state (asl). However, Hanafis do not accept this for all time because humans were never left without divine guidance. It applies only to the period of fatrah (interval between prophets) before our Shariah.
This shows how Hanafi usul restricts a general principle based on theological premises.
3
Apply the Rule to the Case of Dabb
The example given is the hadith prohibiting dabb. The original state for actions before the Shariah was permissibility (during fatrah). A later prohibition (hazir) from the Prophet is considered. According to the rule, the certain (original permissibility) should take precedence unless the prohibition is established with certainty.
The author then analyzes the authenticity of the prohibition hadith to determine whether it can override that certainty.
4
Evaluate the Hadith Authentication
The author devotes a detailed section to defending the hadith's chain and providing supporting evidences. This demonstrates the method of verifying ahadith for deriving rulings in the Hanafi school.
Note the tools: jarh wa ta'dil analysis, shawahid (supporting narrations), and rebuttal of critics.
5
Determine the Thamarah (Practical Consequence)
If the prohibition hadith is considered valid, then eating dabb is prohibited. If not, the original permissibility stands. The Hanafi school generally holds dabb to be permissible, indicating the hadith is not considered strong enough to override the certainty of ibaha.
Thus the rule of certainty operates: a doubtful prohibition (zanni al-thubut) does not overturn certainty.

Commentary

Key Terms
المبيح (al-mubih): The textual evidence that permits an action.
الحاظر (al-hazir): The textual evidence that prohibits an action.

The Four Types of Objection (Mumana'a) in Legal Reasoning
Defines and exemplifies the four categories of objection in qiyas

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify and categorize each of the four types of objection (mumana'a) in Hanafi legal reasoning and explain how each invalidates an opponent's argument.

Core Idea
Objection in legal debate must be one of four categories: challenge the proof, the description, the conditions, or the legal effect.

In Hanafi usul al-fiqh, legal debate (nazar) is built on the idea that the questioner (sā'il) is a denier (munkir) who must object within defined boundaries. Al-Bazdawi's chapter on objection (mumāna‘a) provides the essential toolkit for contesting an opponent's qiyas. Knowing these four categories equips you to dismantle unsound arguments and strengthen your own reasoning.

Guided Path
1
Understand the foundational role of objection
Recognize that mumana'a is 'the foundation of consideration' (asas al-nazar). The questioner is a denier and must not exceed the bounds of denial.
This sets the mindset for structured debate.
2
Identify objection to the proof itself
This occurs when the opponent relies on an invalid proof, such as reasoning by negation (nafy) or a defective analogy (tard). The example given is al-Shafi'i's argument that marriage is not property, so it does not require two male witnesses.
Hanafis reject negation as a valid proof.
3
Identify objection to the description (wasf)
Here, the description claimed as the cause (illa) is disputed. Examples: a minor's empowerment over consumption, prohibition on the day of Nahr, and al-Shafi'i's description of the false oath as 'binding'.
The opponent may admit the existence of the description but dispute its validity or applicability.
4
Identify objection to the conditions of the cause
The opponent can object to a condition that is agreed upon by consensus but is missing in the branch or original. Example: al-Shafi'i's analogy of spot salam to the price in a sale, where the condition of not changing the text ruling is violated.
Conditions like 'not altering the text' must be maintained.
5
Identify objection to the meaning that makes it a proof
Mere description without legal effect (athar) is not a proof. The opponent must show the effect. Example: a depository claiming return—his statement is accepted despite being a claimant in form.
Denial is considered in meaning, not mere form.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul defines the chapter on objection (mumana'a), which is the foundation of legal reasoning (nazar) because the questioner (sā'il) in a debate is essentially a denier (munkir) and must not exceed the bounds of denial. The author enumerates four types of objection:

1. Objection to the proof itself (mumana'a fi nafs al-hujja): Some people rely on invalid proofs. Example: al-Shafi'i argued that marriage is not property, so it cannot be established by the testimony of two women with one man (as required for property transactions). The Hanafi response is that using negation as a proof (al-ihtijāj bi al-nafy) and reasoning by it is invalid. Similarly, one who argues by 'tard' (a defective type of analogy) is also refuted.

2. Objection to the description (wasf) that is claimed as the cause (illa): The opponent may admit the description but dispute its existence or validity. Examples: (a) In the case of a minor's deposit, we say the minor is 'empowered over consumption'—this description is contested. (b) For fasting on the day of Nahr (10th Dhul Hijjah), we argue that the prohibition indicates nullification/abrogation (tahqīq), but the opponent (Shafi'i) holds that prohibition of a legal act does not imply abrogation. (c) al-Shafi'i said the false oath (yamin al-ghamus) is 'binding' (ma'qūda)—this description is also contested.

3. Objection to the conditions (shurūt) of the cause: The Hanafi scholar must object to any condition that is agreed upon by consensus (shart bi al-ijmā') and is missing in either the branch or the original. Example: al-Shafi'i argued that spot salam (salam al-hal) is one of the two countervalues in a sale ('iwaḍ), so it can be either spot or deferred like the price. The Hanafi response: A condition for valid reasoning is that it does not change the ruling of the text (naṣṣ) and that the original is not deviated from analogy by its ruling. Here, the original (sale) deviates from analogy because it allows deferred price, but the branch (salam) has a specific text; thus the condition is not met.

4. Objection to the meaning (ma'nā) by which the description becomes a proof: Mere description without legal effect (athar) is not a proof. The opponent must demonstrate the effect. The way of the denier is denial in meaning, not form. Example: A depository (mūda') who claims he has returned the deposit: his statement is accepted (because of the legal effect of trustworthiness), even though he is a claimant in form. This illustrates that the denial is substantial, not merely formal.

The passage emphasizes that objection is legitimate within these four categories and is fundamental to juristic debate, especially in the Hanafi tradition's rigorous approach to qiyas.

Key Terms
الممانعة (al-mumāna‘a): Objection or denial in legal debate, specifically the act of challenging the opponent's proof, its cause, its conditions, or its legal effect.
الطرد (al-ṭard): A defective type of analogy that merely correlates cause and effect without proper justification; invalid as a proof.
اليمين الغموس (yamīn al-ghamūs): A false oath sworn knowingly, considered grave; in the example, al-Shafi'i described it as 'binding' (ma‘qūda) while Hanafis object to that description.
السلم الحال (al-salam al-ḥāl): A spot salam contract (forward sale with immediate payment and deferred delivery); al-Shafi'i compares it to the price in a sale, but Hanafis object to the analogy.

Types of Mu'arada: Al-Qalb (Inversion) as a Contradictory Objection
Defining qalb and its neutralization

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to define al-qalb as a type of mu'arada, distinguish it from pure contradiction, and explain the Hanafi method of neutralizing it by demonstrating equivalence (nazir).

Core Idea
Al-qalb inverts cause and ruling; Hanafi usul neutralizes it if items are equivalent.

In this passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul, we explore the types of objections (mu'arada) that a questioner can raise after being refused. The key distinction is between objections that contain a contradiction (munaqada) and those that are pure. The most important contradictory objection is al-qalb (inversion), where the opponent swaps cause and effect. The Hanafi school provides a sophisticated method to counter such inversions by showing either that the two items are truly equivalent (naziran) and can mutually support each other, or that they are not symmetrical, so the inversion fails. Understanding this is crucial for mastering Hanafi argumentation.

Guided Path
1
Define mu'arada and its two types
Identify the types of objection: one containing opposition (munaqada) and one pure. Recognize that qalb belongs to the first type.
Mu'arada is the questioner's last resort after refusal.
2
Understand al-qalb as inversion
Grasp that qalb linguistically means to turn something upside down. In argumentation, it means making the effect into the cause and the cause into the effect, creating a circularity.
This applies when the reasoning is by the ruling itself, not by a pure description.
3
Analyze the examples of qalb
Examine the two given examples: (1) the analogy that disbelievers are flogged and stoned like Muslims, and (2) the analogy that recitation is obligatory in all rak'ahs like bowing and prostration. See how the opponent inverts the reasoning.
These examples illustrate how qalb can be attempted.
4
Learn the Hanafi response (mukhlis)
Understand the Hanafi exit: rephrase the argument as two-way inference (istidlal) where the thing is evidence for its parallel and vice versa, provided they are truly equivalent (naziran).
The text gives examples: vow vs. commencement, guardianship over property vs. self.
5
Apply the condition for valid exit
Check the condition: the two items must be naziran (equal). In the flogging/stoning example, they are not equal because they differ in conditions (e.g., thayyib). Similarly, recitation is an additional pillar unlike bowing. Thus, the qalb is neutralized because the parallel is false.
This is the key: without true equivalence, qalb fails.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul introduces the chapter on mu'arada (contradiction/objection) in legal argumentation. The author states that after a questioner is refused, his only recourse is to present a mu'arada. This is divided into two types: one that contains munaqada (opposition/inconsistency) and one that is pure (free of opposition). The type with opposition is called al-qalb (inversion). Qalb has two linguistic meanings, but here it refers to inverting the relationship between 'illa (cause) and hukm (ruling): the opponent takes the ruling that was originally the effect and treats it as the cause for the original cause, thereby creating a contradiction. For example, if someone argues that a ruling is proven by a certain cause, the opponent may invert and argue that the cause is proven by the ruling, which leads to circularity and invalidates the original analogy (qiyas). However, the author notes that this inversion does not work if the reasoning is based on a pure description (al-wasf al-mahd) rather than the ruling itself. Two examples are given: the analogy that disbelievers should be flogged and stoned like Muslims, and the analogy that recitation in prayer should be obligatory in the last two rak'ahs like bowing and prostration. The Hanafi response ("we said") counters the inversion by rephrasing the argument as a dua (four-leg) analogy: the two items are mutually evidence for each other if they are truly equivalent (naziran). For instance, the commitment by vow and by commencement are two parallels; each can be evidence for the other. Similarly, guardianship over property and over self are parallel for both thayyib and virgin. However, flogging and stoning are not parallel because they differ in conditions (e.g., thayyib is required for stoning). Likewise, recitation is not equivalent to bowing and prostration because recitation is a pillar additional to other pillars. Thus, the Hanafi school rescues its analogy from the inversion objection by demonstrating that the two cases are not symmetrical.

القلب (al-qalb): A type of mu'arada that inverts the relationship between cause and ruling, creating an opposition (munaqada).
المناقضة (al-munaqada): A contradiction or inconsistency that arises from the inversion of cause and effect.

The Linguistic and Legal Definition of Tarjih
Definition of tarjih and chapter outline

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the linguistic and legal definition of tarjih, identify its essential elements (mumathala, ta'arud, and the attribute of preference), and outline the four topics of the chapter.

Core Idea
Tarjih is a slight preference that resolves conflict between two equivalent proofs without destroying their equivalency.

This passage opens the chapter on tarjih in al-Bazdawi's Usul. After establishing that conflicting proofs require resolution, the author defines tarjih linguistically and legally, using the analogy of a balance scale. Understanding this definition is critical because tarjih is the second line of defense after other methods of eliminating contradiction, such as naskh or jama'.

Guided Path
1
Identify the chapter structure
The author lists four topics: definition of tarjih, aspects of tarjih, way out when aspects conflict, and invalid aspects. Note these for overall orientation.
This structure will be expanded in subsequent sections.
2
Analyze the linguistic definition
Tarjih is defined as 'superiority of one of two equivalents (mumathala) in an attribute (sifah).' The two proofs must be equivalent; the attribute tips the balance without breaking the equivalence.
The term 'mumathala' (equivalency) is crucial: only when proofs are equal does tarjih apply.
3
Examine the balance analogy
Imagine a scale with two equal pans (the two equivalent proofs). Adding a small weight like a dāniq (half a dirham) to one pan tips it – this is tarjih. Adding a large weight like six or seven dirhams destroys the equivalence, so it is not tarjih.
The added attribute must be 'subsidiary' (tabi'), not overwhelming.
4
Derive the legal meaning
Legally, tarjih works the same: an additional attribute (like specificity, authenticity, etc.) gives preference to one proof over its equivalent. The attribute must not negate the original equivalency.
Example from the text: a small surplus in weight when repaying debts is allowed, but not a large excess.
5
Note the opposite: tafṣīf (fraud)
The opposite of tarjih is tafṣīf (fraud), which involves a deficiency in weight or measure. This confirms that tarjih is about adding a slight preference, not reducing the other side.
Both tarjih and tafṣīf operate by an attribute that does not destroy the original equivalency.

The Hanafi Principle: Coercion Removes Consent, Not Choice
Defining the effect of coercion on choice vs consent.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state the Hanafi distinction between choice (ikhtiyar) and consent (rida) under coercion, and apply it to distinguish coerced acts from jest and error.

Core Idea
Coercion corrupts choice but never eliminates it; it only removes consent.

In this passage from Usul al-Bazdawi, the author clarifies a foundational Hanafi principle: coercion does not destroy a person's capacity to choose, but it does remove their legal consent. This distinction is pivotal in determining the validity of coerced acts. Understanding where coercion sits relative to jest (hazl) and error (khata) sharpens our grasp of how the Hanafi school balances individual responsibility with external duress.

Guided Path

The passage explicitly states that coercion eliminates consent (rida) in both the ruling (hukm) and the cause (sabab), but not choice (ikhtiyar).

Because choice remains (though corrupted), valid choice can override corrupt choice. Coerced acts can be valid if the corrupt choice is treated as non-existent in the presence of valid choice.

The Hanafi Theory of Instrument (Āla) and Categorization of Coerced Acts
Explaining the instrument concept and categorization of coerced acts.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain how the Hanafi school attributes coerced acts to the coercer through the concept of instrument (āla) and categorize actions/statements based on their amenability to such attribution.

Core Idea
The coerced becomes an instrument of the coercer for actions that can be physically transferred; statements remain with the speaker.

The second part of the passage builds on the principle that choice remains under coercion by introducing the concept of the coerced person as an instrument (āla). The author then categorizes all transactions into statements and actions, and within actions, those that can be attributed to the coercer and those that cannot. Understanding this categorization is essential for determining who bears legal responsibility in coerced scenarios.

Guided Path
1
Understand the instrument concept
When the corrupt choice is treated as non-existent, the coerced becomes like one without choice, acting as an instrument of the coercer for certain acts—meaning the act is attributed to the coercer, not the coerced.
This only applies to acts that 'admit' being an instrument, i.e., actions that can be physically performed by one person on behalf of another.
2
Identify which acts admit instrumentality
The passage distinguishes statements (always remain with the speaker) and actions (some like statements, some can be attributed to the coercer). Actions that involve physical force (e.g., striking) can be attributed; statements like divorce cannot.
3
Apply the two types of coercion
Complete coercion (kāmil) corrupts choice and compels (ilja'); partial (qāṣir) only removes consent without compelling. The instrument concept is relevant when choice is fully overridden.
4
Note the four categories of legal inviolabilities (hurmat)
The passage lists four types of inviolabilities: absolute, removable, concessionary, etc. These affect permissibility under coercion but not the core attribution rule.
5
Summarize the rule
Coercion never changes the underlying ruling or the locus of the crime; it only changes attribution by making the coerced an instrument when possible.
If not possible, the ruling remains with the coerced.
