The Obligation of Following the Companions (Taqlid al-Sahabi)
Statement of the rule of taqlid al-sahabi with three scholarly positions.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to STATE the Hanafi rule on taqlid al-sahabi and DISTINGUISH the positions of al-Barda'i, al-Karkhi, and al-Shafi'i.

Core Idea
Following a Companion is obligatory and overrides qiyas in the Hanafi school.

This lesson introduces the principle of taqlid al-sahabi—the obligation to follow the Companions' rulings. The Hanafi school elevates Companion statements above qiyas, but there is disagreement among Hanafi scholars on its scope. We will examine the main positions and the rationale behind them.

Guided Path
1
Identify the key claim
Abu Sa'id al-Barda'i states that taqlid al-sahabi is wajib and qiyas is abandoned for it. This is the classic Hanafi position.
Al-Barda'i represents the majority of early Hanafis.
2
Note the internal Hanafi ikhtilaf
Al-Karkhi limits the obligation to matters beyond the reach of qiyas. Compare the scope of both views.
Al-Karkhi's view allows qiyas when a companion ruling exists but qiyas can also reach it.
3
Identify the opposing madhhab view
Al-Shafi'i rejects taqlid al-sahabi entirely, though some of his followers make exceptions for the Rightly-Guided Caliphs.
This highlights the distinctiveness of Hanafi usul.
4
Understand the epistemological basis
Companion reports are considered stronger than qiyas because of their direct knowledge of revelation and proximity to the Prophet.
The obligation is rooted in the Companions' authority as primary transmitters.

Chapter on following the Companions of the Prophet, peace be upon him, and emulating them, may Allah be pleased with them. Abu Sa'id al-Barda'i, may Allah have mercy on him, said: Following a Companion is obligatory, and qiyas is abandoned for it. He said: And upon this we found our masters, may Allah have mercy on them. Al-Karkhi, may Allah have mercy on him, said: Following him is not obligatory except in what cannot be known by qiyas. Al-Shafi'i, may Allah have mercy on him, said: Not one of them is to be followed. And among them are those who made a distinction in following, so they followed the Rightly-Guided Caliphs and their likes, may Allah be pleased with them. And the practice of our masters, may Allah have mercy on them, differed in this chapter: Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, may Allah have mercy on them, said: Informing of the amount of capital in salam is not a condition, and it has been reported from Ibn Umar, may Allah be pleased with them both, the contrary. Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, may Allah have mercy on them, said: Regarding the pregnant woman, she is divorced three times according to the sunna, and it has been reported from Jabir and Ibn Mas'ud, may Allah be pleased with them both, the contrary. And Abu Yusuf and Muhammad, may Allah have mercy on them, said: Regarding the common laborer, he is a guarantor, and they narrated that from Ali, may Allah be pleased with him. And Abu Hanifa, may Allah have mercy on him, opposed that by opinion. And the practice of our masters agreed upon following in what is not comprehensible by qiyas. They said regarding the minimum of menstruation: it is three days, and its maximum: ten days, and they narrated.

Commentary
This passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul al-Fiqh addresses the authority of the statements and actions of the Companions (taqlid al-sahabi), a key source of law in the Hanafi school. The text opens by presenting three positions: al-Barda'i holds it obligatory and that it overrides qiyas; al-Karkhi limits it to matters beyond the reach of qiyas; al-Shafi'i rejects it entirely, though some of his followers make exceptions for the Rightly-Guided Caliphs. The author then notes that the Hanafi masters (ashabuna) differed in practice, and gives three examples showing the interplay of qiyas and taqlid. First, in salam (advance payment for goods), Abu Yusuf and Muhammad do not require specifying the capital, while Abu Hanifa does, citing Ibn Umar's report. Second, for divorcing a pregnant woman, Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf allow three divorces as sunna, but reports from Jabir and Ibn Mas'ud indicate only one. Third, the common laborer (ajir mushtarak) is considered a guarantor by Abu Yusuf and Muhammad based on a report from Ali, but Abu Hanifa opposes by analogy (ra'y). Finally, the passage notes agreement on following tradition in matters not reachable by qiyas, as with the menstruation period set at 3-10 days based on reports. The examples demonstrate that Hanafi usul prioritizes companion reports over qiyas when they exist, though ijtihad remains in ambiguous cases.

Key Terms
تقليد الصحابي (taqlīd al-ṣaḥābī): Following the opinion or practice of a Companion of the Prophet in legal matters.
القياس (al-qiyās): Analogical reasoning used to derive rulings not explicitly stated in primary sources.
السلم (al-salam): A sale contract with advance payment for future delivery of specified goods.

Taqlid al-Sahabi in Practice: Companion Reports and Qiyas
Application examples of taqlid al-sahabi in Hanafi fiqh, showing varying treatment of companion reports.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to APPLY the principle of taqlid al-sahabi to specific fiqh examples and EVALUATE how Hanafi masters treated companion reports in relation to qiyas.

Core Idea
Hanafi masters sometimes followed companion reports over qiyas, but not always, showing ijtihad in weighing evidence.

This lesson examines four concrete applications from the passage where the Hanafi masters relied on Companion reports or chose not to. These examples illustrate that taqlid al-sahabi is not a mechanical rule; it requires evaluating the report's applicability and strength relative to other evidences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the four examples
The passage gives examples from salam (advance payment), talaq of a pregnant woman, ajir mushtarak (common laborer), and the duration of menstruation.
In the first three, the masters differed; in the fourth, they agreed.
2
Examine the salam example
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad hold that informing the capital amount is not a condition, mentioning a companion report from Ibn Umar to the contrary. They do not follow that report.
This shows that not every companion report overrides their ijtihad.
3
Examine the divorce example
Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf rule that a pregnant woman can be divorced three times, despite reports from Jabir and Ibn Mas'ud stating otherwise.
Again, they do not follow these reports, possibly because their own view is based on stronger evidence.
4
Examine the ajir mushtarak example
Abu Yusuf and Muhammad rule the ajir mushtarak is a guarantor, citing a report from Ali. Abu Hanifa opposes by qiyas.
Here, the companion report is followed by the Sahibayn, but Abu Hanifa prioritizes qiyas.
5
Examine the menstruation example
All masters agree that the minimum is three days and maximum ten days, based on companion reports. This is a matter beyond the reach of qiyas.
When qiyas cannot operate, they unanimously follow the reports.

Can a Tabi'i Be Followed? The Hanafi Dispute
Ruling on taqlid of Tabi'un and intra-Hanafi dispute

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to identify the two Hanafi positions on taqlid of a Tabi'i and apply the historical proofs (Shuraih and Masruq) to evaluate each position.

Core Idea
A Tabi'i whose fatwa was accepted by Companions can be followed, but some Hanafis disagree—historical examples settle the dispute.

This lesson examines a nuanced intra-Hanafi dispute: whether a Follower (Tabi'i) who issued fatwas during the Companions' era may be followed (taqlid) in the same way as a Companion. The source passage presents two opinions within the Hanafi school and supports the first with two historical incidents: Shuraih opposing Ali and Masruq opposing Ibn Abbas. Understanding this debate sharpens your awareness of how evidence from Companion practice (qawl sahabi) is weighted in Hanafi usul.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Legal Issue
Pinpoint the precise question: Is it valid to make taqlid of a Tabi'i who reached the level of fatwa and rivaled the Companions in opinion? The passage distinguishes between two cases of Tabi'un.
The key phrase is 'إذا ظهرت فتواه في زمن الصحابة'.
2
Isolate the Two Hanafi Opinions
State the first opinion (some of our shaykhs): the Tabi'i is like a Companion in this chapter, so taqlid is valid. State the second opinion (other shaykhs): taqlid is invalid because the Tabi'i lacks the possibility of tawqif.
The dispute is purely within the Hanafi madhhab.
3
Examine the Proofs (Dalil) for the First Opinion
Read the two historical examples: (1) Shuraih openly contradicted Ali regarding the rejection of al-Hasan's testimony, and Ali addressed him with 'Say, O black-eyed slave,' indicating acceptance. (2) Masruq opposed Ibn Abbas on the vow to sacrifice a son, and Ibn Abbas later adopted Masruq's fatwa.
These examples demonstrate the Companions' recognition of Tabi'un's ijtihad.
4
Assess the Second Opinion's Basis and the Tarjih
The second opinion rests on the notion that Tabi'un cannot have tawqif (direct divine instruction). However, the passage favors the first opinion because of the clear historical evidence. Note the thamarah al-khilaf: practical permissibility of taqlid.
Tarjih mechanism: explicit historical incidents outweigh a theoretical objection.
5
Apply the Ruling to a New Scenario
Consider a Tabi'i like Sa'id ibn al-Musayyib, who was a young Tabi'i during the later Companion era. If he issued fatwas and Companions did not object, does the first opinion extend to him? Reflect on the condition 'appeared in their time and was acknowledged as a rival'.
This tests the scope of the ruling.

Commentary
This passage is a usul al-fiqh discussion on the authority of the statements of the Companions and the Followers (Tabi'un) within the Hanafi school. It first addresses when the Companions disagree: their statements collectively contain the truth, and none is invalidated by mere conflict because their disagreements are based on ijtihad, not on textual evidence that would determine the matter. Hence, their differing opinions are treated like conflicting analogies requiring tarjih; if tarjih is impossible, the mujtahid may choose any, but only one is correct, and the rest require specific evidence to be acted upon later. Then the text turns to the Follower: if a Tabi'i did not issue fatwas during the Companions' era, he is like any other later imam, and taqlid of him is not allowed. However, if his fatwa appeared in the Companions' time and he was acknowledged by them as an opponent, then according to some Hanafi scholars (like some of our shaykhs), he is treated like a Companion in this respect, meaning his opinions carry weight and may be followed; but others hold that taqlid of him is still invalid because he lacks the possibility of direct tawqif (divine instruction) that the Companions possessed. The preferred opinion (the first) is supported by two historical proofs: Shuraih's opposition to Ali, where Ali did not reprimand him but addressed him as 'O black-eyed slave' (a term of familiarity), and Masruq's opposition to Ibn Abbas on the vow to sacrifice a son, after which Ibn Abbas adopted Masruq's ruling. These show that the Companions accepted the Tabi'un's independent ijtihad, thus their opinions can be regarded as authoritative.

تقليد (taqlid): اتباع قول المجتهد دون معرفة دليله، والمراد هنا اتباع قول التابعي أو الصحابي
