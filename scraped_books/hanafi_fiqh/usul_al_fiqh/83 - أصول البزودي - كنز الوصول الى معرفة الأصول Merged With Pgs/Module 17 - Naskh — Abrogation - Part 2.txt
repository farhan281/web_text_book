Addition of a Qualification: Naskh or Takhsis?
Hanafi argument that adding a qayd to mutlaq is naskh, not takhsis

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to explain why the Hanafi school considers adding a qualification to a mutlaq as naskh, and evaluate the Shafi'i counter-argument.

Core Idea
Adding a condition to an absolute text replaces the original ruling — it is abrogation, not mere specification.

In this passage from al-Bazdawi's Usul, a fundamental dispute between the Hanafis and Shafi'is is examined: when a text introduces a qualification (qayd) to an absolute (mutlaq) ruling, does that create a new ruling (naskh) or merely specify the existing one (takhsis)? The Hanafi school argues that the nature of the ruling changes entirely, while al-Shafi'i maintains that the original ruling remains confirmed. This lesson unpacks the proof and its practical consequences.

Guided Path
1
Identify the dispute
State the exact issue: does adding a qayd (e.g., 'believing' to 'slave') to a mutlaq constitute naskh or takhsis?
2
Summarize the Shafi'i position
Present al-Shafi'i's argument: the qualification confirms the original ruling (e.g., flogging remains with stoning), so it is takhsis, not naskh.
3
Analyze the Hanafi proof
Follow the Hanafi definition of naskh: expiration of one ruling and commencement of another. When mutlaq becomes muqayyad, it becomes a different ruling because absoluteness and restriction are opposites.
Notice the analogy with parts of an illa or hadd.
4
Examine the Hanafi response to Shafi'i
The Hanafi rebuttal: takhsis only removes individuals from a general term, but a qayd introduces a new element not covered by the original text. Thus, it is new legislation.
5
Apply to practical examples
Use the fiqh examples given: recitation of Fatiha, tawaf purification, and the small amount of muthallath. See how the Hanafi ruling follows from the principle.

Al-Shafi'i, may Allah have mercy on him, said: It is specification (takhsis), not abrogation (naskh). That is like the addition of stoning to flogging, and the addition of the qualification of faith in the expiation of an oath and dhihar. He said because the slave is general regarding the disbeliever and the believer, so specification fits it. Indeed, abrogation is alteration, and in the qualification of faith there is confirmation, not alteration. Likewise in the condition of stoning there is confirmation of flogging, not alteration, so it is not abrogation. And it is not a condition that the addition is specification necessarily, rather it is not abrogation in any case. And our proof: that abrogation is the expiration of the duration of a ruling and the beginning of another ruling. The absolute text necessitates acting upon its absoluteness. When it becomes qualified, it becomes something else because restriction and absoluteness are opposites that do not combine. When this is other than the first, there is no avoiding saying the first has ended and the second has begun. This is because whenever it becomes qualified, the absolute becomes part of it, and what is part of something has the ruling of the whole, like part of the effective cause (illa) and part of the punishment (hadd), such that the testimony of a slanderer is not invalidated by part of the hadd according to us because it is not [effective as] a hadd. So it is established that this is abrogation, like abrogating its entirety. As for specification: it is a disposal of the wording by clarifying that some individuals of the generality are not intended by the wording while the wording otherwise covers them, and the qualification is not covered by the absolute. Do you not see that absoluteness denotes non-existence and restriction denotes existence, so it becomes an establishment of a text [?] by analogy or by a solitary report? And because when a specified individual is no longer intended, the remaining [individuals] remain established by that very wording, so it is not abrogation. And when the qualification of faith is established, the believing slave is not established by that first text through its wording, but by this qualification. So it is a new establishment. And the evidence of specification is for removal, not for establishment. And it is not problematic that when stoning is attached to flogging, flogging no longer remains a [complete] punishment. For this reason we do not consider recitation of al-Fatiha as obligatory because it is an addition, and we do not consider ritual purification in circumambulation as a condition because it is an addition. And for this reason Abu Hanifa and Abu Yusuf, may Allah have mercy on them, said: The small amount of a three-fold [intoxicant? literally 'muthallath'] is not forbidden because it is part of the intoxicant, and part of the illa does not have the ruling of the illa. Likewise, the major impure and the minor impure do not use a small quantity of water according to us because it is part of the purifier, so it is not a complete purifier. And because the evidence of abrogation, if it had come alongside, would have been contradictory, and the qualification contradicts the absolute, like other types of abrogation. An analogy to this principle is disagreement of witnesses regarding the amount of the price: the sale is not established because the increase over the price makes the first part of it, and it has become a whole from one perspective, so they become two different things, and what is part does not have the ruling of the whole. And Allah knows best. And what is connected to the sections of the Sunan: the chapter on the actions of the Prophet, peace be upon him.

Commentary
This passage presents the Hanafi argument against al-Shafi'i's view that adding a qualification (qayd) to a mutlaq text is merely specification (takhsis). The author, al-Bazdawi, first states al-Shafi'i's position: additions like stoning to the flogging penalty for zina, or the condition of faith in the freeing of a slave for expiation, are takhsis because the original word 'slave' includes both believers and disbelievers, so specifying to believers is just removing some individuals; abrogation would be a change, but here the original ruling is confirmed. The Hanafi response defines naskh as the expiration of one ruling and the commencement of another. When a mutlaq becomes muqayyad, the ruling changes because absoluteness and restriction are opposites. The original ruling ends and a new one begins. They argue that a part (the qualified ruling) has the same force as the whole, like part of a cause or part of a hadd penalty — it is not just a remainder. Specification, on the other hand, only removes some individuals from the general term while the rest remain under the same text. In the case of a qayd, the qualified individual (e.g., believing slave) is not covered by the original text at all; the original text only covered the absolute slave. Therefore, the qualification establishes a new ruling, which is naskh. Examples from Hanafi fiqh illustrate this: recitation of Fatiha is not obligatory in prayer because it is an addition (in the Shafi'i view it is obligatory based on a hadith that adds it? But the Hanafi view is that it is not obligatory because the addition would be naskh and requires mutawatir evidence). Similarly, purification (taharah) is not a condition for tawaf according to Hanafis because the condition would be an addition to the original ruling. Also, a small amount of 'muthallath' (a date drink) is not forbidden because it is only part of the intoxicant. The passage ends by linking this principle to transaction law: if witnesses disagree on the amount of the price, the sale is not established because the increase turns the first amount into a part, and part does not have the ruling of the whole. This lesson is a classic usul dispute between Hanafis and Shafi'is on whether adding a condition is naskh or takhsis.

Key Terms
نَسخ (naskh): The expiration of the duration of a legal ruling and the commencement of another, replacing the first.
تخصيص (takhsīṣ): A disposal of the wording by clarifying that some individuals of a general term are not intended, while others remain under the same text.
قيد (qayd): A qualification added to an absolute (mutlaq) text, such as 'believing' to 'slave' or 'stoning' to 'flogging'.

Ignorance in Dar al-Harb and at Initial Revelation: A Valid Excuse
Usul rule: Ignorance in dar al-harb and at initial revelation as a valid excuse

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to state and apply the Hanafi rule that ignorance of a ruling is an excuse when the divine address has not reached the person, whether due to location (dar al-harb) or timing (initial revelation).

Core Idea
Ignorance is an excuse when the proof has not reached the person.

This lesson addresses the fourth division of ignorance (jahl) in Hanafi usul: ignorance in dar al-harb for a Muslim who has not emigrated, and ignorance at the time of a ruling's initial revelation. The key principle is that obligation (taklif) is contingent on the person being aware of the divine address. We examine the rationale and the textual evidence provided by the author, along with a nuanced critique from the footnote.

The key condition is non-receipt of the ruling.
2
Understand the rationale
The divine address (khitab) is obscure (khafi) to such a person; he is not negligent, so ignorance is a valid excuse.
Negligence (taqsir) is absent; the fault lies in the hiddenness of the proof, not the person.

Examine the textual proofs
The author cites the Quba and wine prohibition stories and verses from al-Baqarah and al-Ma'idah. Study these proofs to see how they support the rule.
These examples show that the Sahaba were excused until the ruling reached them.

Commentary
This passage presents the fourth division of ignorance (jahl) according to the author: ignorance in dar al-harb (the abode of war) for a Muslim who has not emigrated to Muslim lands. Such a person is excused from complying with newly revealed legal rulings because the divine address (khitab) is obscure to him due to his location. The obligation does not become binding until the ruling reaches him. The basis is that he is not negligent; the fault lies in the hiddenness of the proof. Similarly, when a ruling is first revealed, those who have not yet been informed of it are excused. The author cites two well-known cases: the story of the people of Quba who continued to pray towards Jerusalem after the qibla was changed to the Kaaba until they were informed, and the story of the companions who died before the prohibition of wine. The verse from al-Baqarah (2:143) was revealed affirming the validity of their earlier prayers. The footnote (likely from a later commentator) notes that the author's citation of the verse is contested by a hadith from Ibn 'Abbas that gives a different context, and similarly for the wine verse. The main point is that ignorance of a ruling that has not reached a person is a valid excuse.

Matters Not Admitting Annulment: The Hanafi Classification of Hazl
Categorizing legal acts that are not subject to annulment due to hazl

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to classify legal acts into three categories based on their susceptibility to annulment by hazl (jest), and apply the Hanafi rule for each category.

Core Idea
Legal acts are classified by their relationship to property; acts devoid of property are always binding in jest, while acts with subsidiary property require nuanced analysis.

This passage from Al-Bazdawi's Usul presents a foundational Hanafi taxonomy of legal acts that cannot be annulled by jest (hazl). You will learn the three categories, the underlying rationale, and the specific rulings for acts like divorce, manumission, and marriage. The classification helps determine when a jestful utterance produces binding legal effects.

Guided Path
1
Identify the Three Categories
The passage divides acts into three types: (1) acts with no property at all, (2) acts where property is subsidiary, and (3) acts where property is intended. Understand the basis of this division.
Memorize these three as the core framework.
2
Analyze Category 1: No Property
List the examples: divorce, manumission, pardon of qisas, oath, vow. Explain why they are valid in jest using the hadith and rational reasoning (the jester voluntarily chooses the cause).
The hadith 'Three things... their jest is serious' is the primary dalil.
3
Analyze Category 2: Property as Subsidiary
Focus on marriage as the example. Distinguish between jest about the origin (invalidates the contract) and jest about the dower amount (depends on agreement). Note the contrast with sale where an invalid condition voids the contract.
Marriage is less susceptible to invalidation by conditions than sale.
4
Analyze Category 3: Property as Intended
The passage begins this category but does not complete it. In principle, contracts like sale where property is the main object are subject to annulment if jested, unless the jest is about a subsidiary term.
This category is implied by the classification structure.

(2) acts where property is subsidiary, like marriage (the dower is subsidiary to the marriage contract) — here the ruling varies: jest about the origin (the marriage itself) invalidates the contract, but jest about the dower amount leads to different outcomes depending on whether the parties agree on a prior understanding. (3) acts where property is intended (not detailed here, but presumably sales and similar). The hadith and rational reasoning show that hazl does not affect these acts because the jester voluntarily initiated the cause. For marriage specifically, an invalid condition (like jest about the dower) does not invalidate the contract, unlike a sale where it would. This shows the Hanafi principle that marriage is less susceptible to invalidation by conditions.

Key Terms
الهزل (al-hazl): Jest or joking, which may or may not affect legal acts depending on the nature of the act.
الطلاق (al-talaq): Divorce, a non-proprietary act that is valid even if uttered in jest.
العتاق (al-i'taq): Manumission of a slave, also valid in jest.
ما لا يحتمل النقض (ma la yahtamil al-naqd): Matters that do not admit of annulment — legal acts that remain valid even if performed in jest, and cannot be undone by subsequent denial.

Rukhsa al-Isqat: The Travel Prayer Concession as Complete Removal
Arguing that the concession for shortening prayer is a removal of obligation, not a mere delay.

Learning Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to distinguish between a rukhsa of isqat and a rukhsa of ta'khir using Hanafi usul principles, and apply the concept of ikhtiyar muqayyad bi al-rifq to determine whether a ruling involves a true choice.

Core Idea
The prayer concession during travel is a rukhsa isqat; the traveller must shorten and has no valid choice to complete.

This passage from Usul al-Bazdawi argues that the concession to shorten prayer during travel is a complete removal of the obligation to pray four rak'ahs, not merely a permission to postpone. It uses the hadith of Umar (RA) and a sophisticated argument about choice (ikhtiyar) and benefit (rifq) to prove this. Understanding this distinction is crucial because it determines whether the traveller has an option to complete the prayer or must shorten.

Commentary
This passage is an argument establishing that the concession for shortening the travelling prayer is a rukhsa of isqat (removal) rather than a concession of delay. The author presents two 'khafiyyan' (subtle) aspects. First, he argues that the concession completely removes the obligation of full prayer during travel, like the removal of burdens (isr wa aghlal) from the community. He supports this with the hadith of Umar (RA) questioning why they shorten in safety, and the Prophet's (PBUH) response that Allah has given a charity (sadaqa) which must be accepted. The author explains that the prayer is a right that cannot be owned or monetized; accepting the charity means accepting removal without option to reject. This is contrasted with fasting, where the text orders delay (ta'khir) not removal, so fasting remains obligatory and can be performed later. Second, the author argues that perfect choice is an attribute of Allah, not of servants. Servants only have choice that brings benefit (rifq). If the traveller were given a choice between shortening and completing, that would not actually benefit him (since shortening is easier), so such choice would be an attribute of lordship (rububiyya), not servitude. The author then provides analogies: the oath-breaker is given choice among expiations for his benefit; but in cases where choice does not benefit (like choosing a larger amount without benefit), no choice is given. Examples include a mudabbar slave whose crime value is far less than the diya, or an emancipated slave whose crime value is less than the arsh—no choice is given. Conversely, in slave crimes where keeping the slave or paying ransom may be beneficial, choice exists. Applying this, in prayer there is no benefit in choosing the longer form, so no choice should exist; the traveller must shorten. The author refutes a possible objection that extra reward could be a benefit by stating that reward depends on excellence of obedience, not length.

Key Terms
رخصة الإسقاط (rukhsat al-isqat): A concession that completely removes the obligation (like prayer during travel) as opposed to merely delaying it.
اختيار (ikhtiyar): Choice given to a servant, which must entail some benefit (rifq) to be considered legitimate; otherwise it would be an attribute of Allah alone.
رفق (rifq): Benefit or ease that justifies the granting of a choice; the underlying rationale for giving options in legal rulings.
